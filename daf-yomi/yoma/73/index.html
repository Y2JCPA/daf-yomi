⚔️ The Priest Anointed for War: Eight Garments?

Rav Dimi
The priest anointed for war wears all eight garments of the High Priest — he is like a High Priest in all respects regarding his dress.
Baraita
He serves neither in four garments like a common priest, nor in eight garments like a High Priest — implying a middle category.
Abaye
Would you make him like a non-priest?! He wears eight garments — the baraita only excludes him from the Yom Kippur eight-garment service.
Ravin
The eight-garment rule was stated only regarding his consulting the Urim VeTummim, not for regular Temple service.
Resolution The war priest shares five explicit Torah rules with the High Priest (mourning restrictions) but does not inherit his father's position — his primary role is consulting the Urim VeTummim on behalf of the king.

👔 Three Levels of Priestly Status

High Priest

  • Eight garments in all service
  • Bull brought for unwitting sins
  • Does not rend clothing publicly — only from below
  • Offers sacrifices as acute mourner
  • Ten unique halakhot apply
  • Son inherits his position

Priest Anointed for War

  • Eight garments (Urim VeTummim context)
  • Five Torah-explicit mourning rules apply
  • Does NOT inherit father's position
  • Purpose: consult Urim VeTummim for king
  • No Bull for unwitting sins
  • Steps down when original HP is restored

Common Priest

  • Four garments only
  • Mourns publicly — rends from above
  • Cannot offer sacrifices as acute mourner
  • No unique mourning restrictions
  • Inherits father's priestly position
  • No bull for individual unwitting sin

🔮 How to Consult the Urim VeTummim

Positioning: The questioner stands facing the High Priest; the High Priest turns his face toward the Divine Presence (Shekhinah)
↓
Low Voice: The question is asked quietly — audible only to the asker, as implied by "by the judgment of the Urim" (Numbers 27:21)
↓
One Question at a Time: Do not ask two matters simultaneously; wait for an answer before asking again — even if asked simultaneously, only one is answered
↓
The Answer: The High Priest reads the Divine response and transmits it — e.g., "Thus says God: Go up and succeed"
↓
Rabbi Yehuda's View: He need not say 'Go up' and 'succeed' separately — the entire answer comes at once in one utterance
↓
Emergency Exception: If urgency demands, two questions may be asked together and answered together — as David did at Keilah (I Samuel 23)

✨ How Do the Breastplate Letters Provide Answers?

Twelve tribal names are engraved on the breastplate's twelve stones
↓
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Letters of the answer protrude from the stones and the High Priest reads them
Reish Lakish
Letters join together before the High Priest's eyes to form the answer
↓
Problem: The letter tzadi (צ) does not appear in any of the twelve tribes' names!
↓
Rav Shmuel bar Yitzḥak: The names of Abraham (אברהם), Isaac (יצחק), and Jacob (יעקב) were also engraved — supplying all missing letters
↓
Even so: The High Priest must have Divine Spirit resting upon him to correctly arrange and read the letters — the letters alone do not suffice

⚖️ Is a Half-Measure Forbidden by Torah Law?

Rabbi Yoḥanan
A half-measure is prohibited by Torah law — the Torah forbids even a minute amount. The Mishna's use of 'prohibited' (אסור) refers to any amount, even sub-threshold.
Reish Lakish
A half-measure is only rabbinically prohibited. Torah punishment requires a full measure. The Mishna says 'prohibited' because there is no punishment — not because Torah forbids it.
Gemara Challenge
But another Mishna holds one liable for swearing to eat a half-measure of forbidden food! If it's already rabbinically forbidden, an oath cannot take effect — ein shevua chal al issur!
Reish Lakish
That Mishna is where one swears NOT to eat a half-measure. Per the Rabbis, since there is no prior Torah prohibition on that amount, the oath creates a new prohibition and liability.
Key Principle — אין שבועה חלה על איסור An oath cannot take effect upon something already forbidden (Torah level). Reish Lakish: since a half-measure is only rabbinically forbidden, an oath adding to it does take effect. Rabbi Yoḥanan: it is Torah-forbidden, so the mishna's language of 'prohibited' signals Torah-level stringency.
