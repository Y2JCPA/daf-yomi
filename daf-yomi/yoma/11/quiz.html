5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why did the Gate of Nicanor require a mezuza while all other eastern Temple gates did not?
A. It was the most ornate gate and commanded special honor
B. The Chamber of Parhedrin, the High Priest's residence during Yom Kippur week, was located just inside it
C. Nicanor himself was a prominent donor who requested a mezuza
D. It was the only Temple gate with two complete doorposts
Correct: B — The Chamber of Parhedrin — where the High Priest resided during his week of preparation before Yom Kippur — was located just inside the Gate of Nicanor. This residential function created a genuine mezuza obligation for that specific gate.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Abaye rejected Rav Safra's explanation for why no mezuza was on Meḥoza's gates. What was Abaye's actual reason?
A. The gates were too tall to reach for affixing a mezuza
B. Meḥoza did not have a sufficient Jewish majority to obligate its gates
C. Danger — gentiles in the mixed city would suspect Jews of using mezuzot for sorcery
D. The local rabbis of Meḥoza had formally exempted the city's gates
Correct: C — Abaye argued that the real reason was danger: since gentiles also lived in Meḥoza, they might suspect Jews of using the publicly displayed mezuzot for witchcraft. This suspicion could lead to persecution, as illustrated by the near-execution of a mezuza examiner in Tzippori.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Kahana's interpretation, what principle does 'your house' teach about mezuza?
A. Only houses you personally built are obligated
B. Only houses designated for residence are obligated — excluding storehouses, barns, and woodsheds
C. Only houses with two complete doorposts are obligated
D. Only houses in which both men and women dwell are obligated
Correct: B — Rav Kahana derives that 'your house' means a place designated for residence, thereby excluding functional work structures like storehouses for hay, cattle barns, woodsheds, and general storerooms, which are not residential in character.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
The Gemara asks why a synagogue needs a mezuza given the phrase 'your house.' What is the answer?
A. A synagogue is considered each member's personal house of prayer
B. The reward verse immediately following — 'so that your days be numerous' — implies broad inclusion of all structures where people dwell or gather
C. A separate rabbinic decree explicitly obligated synagogues
D. The word 'houses' is in the plural, which includes communal buildings
Correct: B — The Gemara answers that immediately following the mezuza commandment is the reward verse 'so that your days be numerous' — this juxtaposition implies the obligation extends broadly. One could not exclude a synagogue and still expect to receive the promised reward of long life.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Can a synagogue become impure with the leprosy impurity (nega'im) according to the Gemara?
A. No — all authorities agree synagogues are definitively exempt
B. Yes — all authorities agree synagogues are definitively obligated
C. It is subject to a tannaitic dispute: Rabbi Meir obligates it, the Rabbis exempt it
D. Only synagogues outside of Israel can become impure with nega'im
Correct: C — The Gemara reconciles contradictory baraitot by identifying a tannaitic dispute: Rabbi Meir holds that synagogues can become impure with leprosy-impurity, while the Rabbis disagree. An alternative resolution distinguishes between synagogues that contain a residential dwelling and those that do not.
