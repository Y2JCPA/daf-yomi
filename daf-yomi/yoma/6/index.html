🏠 The High Priest Leaves Home

לִשְׁכַּת פַּרְהֶדְרִין
Central Question After digressing into inauguration garments, the Gemara returns to the mishna: Why is the High Priest moved to the Chamber of Parhedrin seven days before Yom Kippur — and why can't his wife join him? The answer involves separating him from potential niddah (menstrual) impurity, leading to a deep dispute about retroactive defilement, when a ba'al niddah immerses, and whether tumat met (corpse impurity) is fully permitted or merely overridden in public Temple service.

🎗️ Belt of High Priest vs. Common Priest — and Dressing Order

Questioner
The verse 'And you will gird them with belts' implies Aaron and his sons were girded simultaneously — contradicting the view that Aaron was dressed first!
Respondent
That verse teaches something else: the belt of the High Priest (כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל) is not the same as the belt of the common priest. The High Priest's belt is of fine blue, purple, and linen — uniquely his.
Questioner
If the belts are identical, why does the Torah write both 'he girded him' (singular) AND 'he girded them' (plural)?
Respondent
Learn from the double language: Moses dressed Aaron first, then dressed his sons — the sequence was deliberate, not simultaneous.
Conclusion The dual verses prove Moses dressed Aaron before his sons, and the High Priest's belt is distinct from the common priest's belt.

🏠 Why Is the High Priest Removed From His Home?

Mishna: High Priest moves to Lishkat Parhedrin seven days before Yom Kippur
↓
Gemara asks: Why must he leave his wife — why not simply let her join him?
↓
Baraita (Rabbi Yehuda): Lest his wife become a niddah — then he would be forbidden to remain close to her
↓
Gemara objects: Would a High Priest knowingly have relations with a wife of uncertain status? That assumes wickedness!
↓
Revised concern: Lest she become a niddah and retroactively defile him — even past relations could be backdated as impure (per Rabbi Akiva's 24-hour rule)

⚖️ Rabbi Akiva vs. The Rabbis — Retroactive Niddah Impurity

Rabbi Akiva

  • A niddah who finds blood retroactively imparts impurity up to 24 hours back
  • The husband who was with her during that window is retroactively impure
  • This fully explains the mishna's concern: High Priest may have been defiled before even realizing it
  • Stricter position — maximally protective of Yom Kippur purity

The Rabbis

  • Dispute Rabbi Akiva's retroactive impurity for extended periods
  • BUT agree when blood is found immediately after inspection (nearly no time lapse)
  • Rav Ḥisda: The High Priest's wife would be inspected regularly — so any blood found is effectively immediate
  • Even according to the Rabbis, the mishna's concern is valid

⚰️ Tumat Met in Public Temple Service: Permitted or Overridden?

Rav Naḥman
Corpse impurity is fully permitted in cases involving the public (טֻמְאָה הֻתְּרָה בְּצִבּוּר). The Torah permits the service — we do not need to seek out pure priests as alternatives.
Rav Sheshet
No — corpse impurity is merely overridden (דְּחוּיָה), not permitted. When pure priests are available in that day's family unit, they must serve; the impure ones stand aside.
Ravina
Either way, we cannot sequester the High Priest from corpse impurity — it pervades public spaces and cannot practically be avoided the way niddah impurity can.
Rav Naḥman
Even if 'overridden': when an entire patrilineal family unit is impure, all serve together. Only when pure and impure are mixed do the pure priests take priority.
Key Hebrew Distinction הֻתְּרָה = the prohibition is removed entirely for the public | דְּחוּיָה = the prohibition is merely pushed aside when unavoidable — pure priests must still serve if present
