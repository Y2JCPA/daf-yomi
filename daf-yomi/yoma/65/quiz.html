QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav, when Pair #1 is designated, one goat is lost, Pair #2 is designated, and then the lost goat is found — which goat is ultimately offered?
A. The recovered goat of Pair #1
B. The remaining (unused) goat of Pair #1
C. The first goat chosen in Pair #2's lottery
D. Either goat from Pair #2 at the priest's discretion
Correct: B — Rav follows Rabbi Yosei: the mitzva should be performed with the first animal. Since one goat of Pair #1 was used, the remaining goat of Pair #1 is offered. The second pair was only a conditional designation from the start.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does Beit Rabbi Yannai derive from 'shall be stood alive before the Lord' (Lev. 16:10)?
A. The scapegoat must survive the entire journey to the wilderness
B. Both goats must be alive at the time the inner blood is sprinkled
C. The lottery must be performed while both goats stand before the High Priest
D. The High Priest must stand before the scapegoat during confession
Correct: B — Beit Rabbi Yannai derives that both goats must be alive through the sprinkling of the blood inside. If the scapegoat dies first, the blood of the inner goat can no longer be sprinkled and must instead be spilled — the two goats are interdependent.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rabbi Zeira, why are leftover Yom Kippur animals left to die rather than to graze?
A. A rabbinic decree protects against the High Priest dying during the year
B. Communal offerings may not carry over from one year to the next
C. The lottery designation does not establish eligibility across years
D. A sin-offering whose owners have atoned must be left to die
Correct: C — Rabbi Zeira holds that the lottery is year-specific. A goat designated for Azazel in one year's lottery cannot serve the following year. Since it cannot be used, it is left to die. This is distinct from the rabbinic-decree explanation.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In the Shekalim parallel case, if lost city shekels are found after replacement coins were already donated, what happens to both sets?
A. The found coins replace the donated ones; donated coins are returned to the donors
B. Both sets are sanctified, but neither counts toward the following year's obligation
C. The found coins are returned to the donors since the Temple is already funded
D. Per all opinions, both sets count toward the following year's obligation
Correct: B — Both sets remain sanctified — they were dedicated to the Temple and cannot be reclaimed. However, they do not count toward next year's shekel obligation. Rabbi Yehuda uniquely holds that one year's obligation can be fulfilled the following year.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rabbi Yehuda rules that if the blood of the inner goat spills before sprinkling, the scapegoat must be left to die. Which principle underlies this?
A. Any defect in the Yom Kippur service invalidates all subsequent steps
B. The two goats are interdependent — each requires the other to remain valid
C. The scapegoat cannot be sent before a complete blood service is performed
D. Both B and C
Correct: D — According to Rabbi Yehuda, the two goats form one unified mitzva. The scapegoat ritual requires a valid blood service, and the blood service requires a living scapegoat. They are halakhically linked in both directions — neither can stand without the other.
