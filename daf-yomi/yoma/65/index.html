🐐 The Fate of Extra Yom Kippur Goats

Rav
The remaining goat of the first pair is sacrificed (following Rabbi Yosei — mitzva with the first animal). The second pair's extra goat is left to graze (Rabbis) or die (Rabbi Yehuda). The second pair was designated conditionally from the start.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
The remaining goat of the second pair is sacrificed. The leftover goat of the first pair — which was disqualified when its partner was lost — is left to graze (Rabbis) or die (Rabbi Yehuda).
Core Tension If Rav holds the first pair's goat is offered, the second pair's leftover goat was never truly disqualified. Rava resolves this: per Rabbi Yosei, the second designation was conditional from the outset — it was always a mere contingency, not a true sanctification.

🔗 Why Does the Scapegoat's Death Require Spilling the Blood?

Objection
If the scapegoat dies, why must the blood of the inner goat be spilled? The lottery was already performed — that is the essential act of the scapegoat! The mitzva is complete!
Beit Rabbi Yannai
The verse states: 'shall be stood alive before the Lord' (Lev. 16:10). Both goats must be alive at the moment the inner blood is sprinkled. If the scapegoat has died, the blood of the inner goat can no longer be sprinkled — it must be spilled.
📜 Key Verse — Vayikra 16:10 וְהֶעֱמִיד חַי לִפְנֵי ה׳ — 'Shall be stood alive before the Lord.' The scapegoat's life is required not merely at the lottery, but throughout the entire sprinkling service inside.

💀 Why Are Yom Kippur Animals Left to Die, Not Graze?

Rav Tavi / Rabbi Yoshiya

  • Communal offerings cannot carry over year to year
  • Verse: 'This is the burnt-offering for every New Month' — each year's obligations are distinct
  • Problem: Applies only to communal offerings, not to the High Priest's personal bull

Rabbi Zeira

  • The lottery designation does not carry over from one year to the next
  • A goat designated by lottery one year is ineligible the following year
  • Problem: The bull is not chosen by lottery — what disqualifies it?

Rabbinic Decree (Gemara's Conclusion)

  • Bull: Decree due to concern the High Priest may die during the year, making it a sin-offering whose owners have died
  • Goat: Decree due to concern a sin-offering's year will pass — a goat unused across Yom Kippur is disqualified
  • Gemara: Not difficult — per Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, the goat could be redeemed

💰 Lost Temple Shekels — A Parallel to Yom Kippur Replacements

מסכת שקלים ב:א — שָׁלְחוּ שִׁקְלֵיהֶם וְנִגְנְבוּ אוֹ שֶׁאָבְדוּ
💰 Parallel: The Shekalim Case A city sent shekels to the Temple; they were stolen or lost. If the Temple treasury had already collected the year's funds: the messengers bear the loss and the city need not donate again. If not yet collected: the messengers take an oath and the city donates again. If the lost coins are found later, both sets remain sanctified — but neither counts toward next year's obligation.

This parallel illuminates why extra Yom Kippur animals cannot simply roll over: Rabbi Yehuda holds that obligations of one year can be fulfilled the following year; the Rabbis disagree. The dispute directly affects whether replacement animals are left to die or to graze.
