👘 Were Sacred Garments Worn During the Lottery?

Rav Naḥman
The mishna describes priests removing non-sacred garments and then donning sacred ones — the lottery preceded the wearing of priestly vestments.
Rav Sheshet
The mishna describes priests already wearing sacred garments — proven by the baraita: the lottery was held in the Chamber of Hewn Stone while priests were in priestly vestments.
Gemara
Rav Sheshet's reading is supported: the priests dressed from the feet upward (trousers first) to avoid improper exposure — this implies they were actively donning sacred garments, not non-sacred ones.
Halakhic Conclusion The lottery was conducted while priests wore sacred garments. They dressed from the ground upward: trousers, tunic, belt, and finally the mitre — maintaining dignity throughout.

🏛️ Abaye's Proof: The Chamber of Hewn Stone

Chamber of Hewn Stone
Built like a large basilica (basileki)
Lottery held in its eastern section; Elder sits in west
↓
❌ If entirely non-sacred
The lottery in the east would not fulfill: "In the House of God we walked with the throng" — the lottery must occur within sacred space
❌ If entirely sacred
The Elder could not sit in the west — chambers fully in sacred space cannot have openings toward the non-sacred area (Mishnah rule)
✓ Abaye's Conclusion
Half in the sacred area, half in the non-sacred area — two doorways, one to each zone, resolving both constraints

🩸 Who Collects the Blood Between Slaughter and Sprinkling?

Initial Logic
The slaughterer collects the blood — the mishna assigns slaughter and sprinkling to separate priests, so the slaughterer must receive the blood in a vessel after cutting the animal.
Counter-argument
If the slaughterer collects, then when a non-priest slaughters (permitted!), we would need 13 priests at the laver — but Ben Katin made only 12 spigots, one per priest. A contradiction!
Proof from Ben Katin
Ben Katin replaced the laver's original two spouts with exactly 12 spigots — one for each of 12 priests. This proves there are never 13 participants; the sprinkler must be the collector.
Rav Aḥa b. Rava
Confirmed by the mishna's explicit sequence: 'The slaughterer slaughtered, the collector collected, the sprinkler sprinkled' — three distinct acts, with the collector being the sprinkler-to-be.
Conclusion The sprinkler collects the blood — proven by Ben Katin's 12 spigots and the mishna's three-stage sequence. There are always exactly 12 priests at the laver.

🐂 Three Views on the Order of Carrying Limbs to the Altar

Ben Azzai / Rabbi Yehoshua

  • Order follows how the animal walks
  • Front limbs carried up first
  • Right side precedes left
  • Mimics natural locomotion
  • The living form guides the offering

Rabbi Akiva (= Tanna of Mishna)

  • Order follows how it was cut up
  • Head + right leg first
  • Two forelegs second
  • Tail + hindquarters third
  • Follows the anatomy of skinning

Rabbi Yosei HaGelili

  • Breast and neck go first
  • Based on Ezekiel 24:4:
  • "Every good piece, thigh and shoulder"
  • Finest, meatiest cuts take precedence
  • Rava: same principle, different anatomy reading

🦴 Why the Head and Right Leg Ascend Together

ראש וכרע — ראש שיש בו עצמות (יומא כ"ה ב׳)
Rava's Explanation The head has much bone — because the head is largely bone and relatively low in meat, the right leg (fleshier) accompanies it to the altar so the offering is not diminished in honor.

The head always goes first: Derived from the verse about the burnt offering — just as the priest performs rites with the right hand, the right side of the animal takes precedence. The head leads because it is the most dignified part of the creature.

All opinions agree: the head is sacrificed first. The debate concerns only the subsequent order of the remaining limbs.
