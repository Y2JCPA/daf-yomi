⚖️ The Source of the High Priest's 7-Day Sequestering

Rabbi Yoḥanan
The High Priest's 7-day sequestering is derived from the inauguration of the Mishkan — just as Aaron and his sons were sequestered 7 days before the inaugural service (Lev. 8), so too the High Priest before Yom Kippur.
Reish Lakish
But at the inauguration sprinkling was with blood, while Yom Kippur uses water — they cannot be compared! Rather, derive it from Sinai, where Moses waited in the cloud before receiving the Torah.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
That is not difficult — Rabbi Ḥiyya taught: Water replaced blood. The verse says 'as has been done this day… to make atonement' — linking Yom Kippur directly to the inauguration.
Reish Lakish
A baraita supports my view: 'Moses ascended in the cloud, was covered in the cloud, and was sanctified in the cloud — in order to receive the Torah for Israel.' This is the paradigm for the High Priest's preparation.
Gemara's Conclusion A baraita was found supporting each opinion. Ultimately, the Gemara concludes that Rabbi Yoḥanan holds all inauguration details are indispensable — failure to perform any detail invalidates the inauguration itself.

🔗 Two Sources for the High Priest's Sequestering

Rabbi Yoḥanan: From the Inauguration

  • Aaron and his sons sequestered 7 days before Mishkan's inauguration (Lev. 8:33)
  • High Priest similarly sequestered 7 days before Yom Kippur
  • Rabbi Ḥiyya: water sprinkling on Yom Kippur replaced blood sprinkling
  • Verse: 'As has been done this day… to make atonement' links the two events
  • Implication: every detail of the inauguration is legally indispensable

Reish Lakish: From Sinai

  • Moses ascended, was covered, and was sanctified in the cloud before receiving Torah
  • Baraita: Moses' cloud-sequestering is the model for the High Priest's preparation
  • Sinai had 6 days before the call — raises question about deriving 7 days
  • Parallel: two Torah scholars (students of Moses) transmit the service — excluding Sadducees
  • Supported by a full baraita explicitly linking Sinai to Yom Kippur preparation

👂 Who Heard God Call Moses? Sinai vs. Tent of Meeting

Rabbi Elazar
When God said 'And He called to Moses' at Sinai, all of Israel was standing there and heard. The Divine voice went to the ends of the world — this was a public calling.
Objection from Verse
But it is written: 'Moses went into the Tent of Meeting… he heard the voice speaking to him from above the Ark cover' — this implies the voice reached only Moses, not others in the camp!
School of Rabbi Yishmael
Verbal analogy (gezeirah shavah): 'Moses came into the cloud' parallels 'Israel went into the sea on dry land.' Just as the sea split miraculously for Israel, the cloud split to allow Moses private entry — the voice was contained within.
Additional Teaching — Rabbi Matya ben Ḥarash God's calling served not just to summon Moses, but to inspire awe in him — so the Torah would be received with reverence, trembling, and quaking, as befits Divine communication.

🗣️ Torah Etiquette: Calling Before Speaking

מלמד שלא ידבר אדם לחבירו אלא אם כן קראו תחילה — ויקרא רבה א׳
וַיִּקְרָא אֶל מֹשֶׁה וַיְדַבֵּר ה׳ אֵלָיו 'And He called unto Moses, and the Lord spoke unto him' (Leviticus 1:1)

Why does the verse mention calling before speaking? God could have simply spoken — yet He called first.

The Torah teaches derech eretz (proper conduct): a person should never address another abruptly without first signaling their intention. Even God modeled this with Moses.

Rabbi Matya ben Ḥarash adds a second dimension: the calling was meant to instill awe and reverence in Moses, so the Torah would be transmitted in a spirit of trembling and gravity — not casual conversation.
