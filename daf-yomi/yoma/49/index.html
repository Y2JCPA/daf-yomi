📜 Can a Second High Priest Continue the First's Rite?

Rabbi Ḥanina
"With a bull" means with his own bull — the newly appointed High Priest must slaughter a new bull. He cannot enter with blood his predecessor already slaughtered.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
"With a bull" means simply with bull blood — the new High Priest may enter with the blood already taken from the first priest's slaughter, since the animal itself is unchanged.
Core Question Does בְּפַר ('with a bull') require personal slaughter, or does it merely specify the correct animal type? This dispute governs whether ritual acts can be 'inherited' by a successor.

🔄 Rav Sheshet's Reversal on Carrying Blood

Rav Ḥisda
Carrying the blood pan between the Ulam (antechamber) and the outer altar is not an essential rite — if done by a non-priest it is not disqualified.
Rav Sheshet (initially)
I raise a dilemma about this very case — I was unaware of the baraita and therefore ruled without it, implicitly against its ruling.
Rav Sheshet (later)
Now I object to Rav Ḥisda from that same baraita! The baraita explicitly lists carrying among disqualified rites — proving even this transport requires a qualified priest.
The Twist Rav Sheshet first ruled unknowingly against the baraita, then — upon learning it — used the same baraita as his weapon against Rav Ḥisda's position.

⚗️ The Hardest Rite: Incense in the Holy of Holies

1. High Priest enters Kodesh HaKodashim carrying the coal pan and the incense ladle simultaneously
↓
2. He places the coal pan on the ground between the staves of the Ark, without setting down the ladle
↓
3. He grips the front edge of the ladle with both arms pressed against his body — an awkward, constrained posture
↓
4. He scoops the incense directly into his cupped palms from the ladle — using no secondary vessel, relying purely on manual dexterity
↓
5. He places the incense on the coals; the fragrant smoke rises and fills the chamber — the atonement rite is complete
↓
"This is the most difficult sacrificial rite in the entire Temple service" — more demanding even than pinching or taking handfuls at the altar

✋ High Priest Dies Mid-Rite: Two Parallel Dilemmas

🫙 Incense Already Scooped

  • First HP scoops the handful of incense, then dies
  • Can the second HP enter with that same handful?
  • Rav Huna b. Rav Yehoshua: No — any new scoop will inevitably differ in amount
  • Rav Pappa's dilemma: What if another priest scooped and placed it into the HP's hands?
  • Outcome: Teiku — the dilemma stands unresolved

🐂 Bull Already Slaughtered

  • First HP slaughters the bull, then dies
  • Can the second HP enter with that blood?
  • Rabbi Ḥanina: No — 'with a bull' means his own personal slaughter
  • Rabbi Yoḥanan: Yes — 'with a bull' only specifies the species, not ownership
  • Outcome: Disputed — no definitive Talmudic ruling given

🌿 Tangent: Cress Juice & Rabbi Ḥanina's Medical Wisdom

בְּפַר — 'with a bull' (Leviticus 16:3) — the pivot phrase of the entire sugya
Shabbat Medicine Digression Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi asked Rabbi Ḥanina whether one may grind and drink cress on Shabbat as a remedy. The Gemara probes the question: if there is danger to life, grinding is obviously permitted; if there is no danger, it's obviously forbidden — so what was actually being asked? Answer: The question was whether any medicinal danger justified invoking pikuach nefesh for cress specifically. Rabbi Ḥanina's authority was established: "No man ever consulted me about a mule-kick wound and survived" — corrected by the Gemara to mean the wound itself never healed, not that the patient invariably died. His long life (103+ years) was attributed to his great Torah merit.
