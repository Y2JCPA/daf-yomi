🌊 Crossing Water: Sandals on Shabbat

Gemara
On Yom Kippur, crossing water barefoot is fine — shoes are banned anyway. But on Shabbat, how does one cross a river?
Gemara
Shoes may be worn on Shabbat if tied tightly. But sandals don't fit snugly — they might slip off in the water. What then?
Rav Riḥumi
I personally saw Ravina cross a river wearing sandals on Shabbat — it is permitted!
Rafram
Ravina, you should have worn sandals to the Exilarch's lecture rather than skipping it entirely!
Ravina
There was a pool on the road. I hold one should not cross water in sandals — so I could not come without compromising my practice.
Takeaway Ravina's personal stringency was to avoid sandals when crossing water — even though he crossed a river in sandals in another context. He applied situational judgment.

🪵 Cork Sandals on Yom Kippur — Permitted?

Dilemma: Are cork sandals forbidden on Yom Kippur as shoes?
↓
Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Naḥmani testifies: "I saw Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi go out in cork sandals on Yom Kippur"
↓
Challenge (Rami bar Ḥama)
Prosthetic leg = shoe (R. Meir). If even a prosthetic counts, doesn't cork too?
Rava's Response
Rags on a prosthetic don't make it a garment. Only shoes are forbidden — other coverings are not.
↓
Ruling: Cork sandals are permitted — they are not standard shoes

❄️ Cooling Off on Yom Kippur

Prohibited

  • Sitting on dripping-wet clay (Yehuda bar Gerogarot / R. Yehoshua ben Levi: only if dripping)
  • Placing a full silver cup against the body (Rav Pappa)
  • Ceramic vessel — even if not full, may be prohibited (absorbs and releases cold)

Permitted

  • Placing a baby (yanuka) next to the body — Rabba's practice
  • Using an empty silver cup — Rava's practice
  • Damp but not dripping clay
  • Tisha B'Av: soaking cloth before; using residual moisture the next day

Ceramic — Special Rule

  • Ceramic absorbs and re-releases coldness
  • Even an empty ceramic vessel may be forbidden
  • Stricter standard than silver

🦿 Prosthetic Leg: Shabbat Debate → Yom Kippur Ruling

Mishnah: An amputee may go out with a wooden prosthetic on Shabbat — it is like a shoe (Rabbi Meir). Rabbi Yosei prohibits it.
↓
Baraita: If padded with rags, Rabbi Meir deems it impure (treating it as a garment). Rabbi Yosei deems it pure.
↓
Rava's challenge: If the prosthetic isn't a garment on its own, do rags transform it into one? Only shoes are banned — other coverings are not.
↓
Rava's resolution: All agree the prosthetic IS a shoe. On Shabbat, Rabbi Yosei decrees against it — lest the wearer remove and carry it in a public domain.
↓
Yom Kippur result: Since a prosthetic leg = shoe per Rava, it is forbidden on Yom Kippur under the standard shoe prohibition.
