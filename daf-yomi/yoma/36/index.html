🐂 Yom Kippur Bull: Confession & Ritual Positioning

High Priest positions bull between the Entrance Hall (Ulam) and the altar, head south, face west
↓
Performs semikha: places both hands on the bull's head, standing east of the animal
↓
Recites vidui (confession): chatati — aviti — pashati (sin → wrongdoing → rebellion)
↓
Atonement is achieved through words — proven via gezera shava linking bull to the scapegoat
↓
Bull is then slaughtered; atonement for priest and his household is complete

🧭 Where Is 'the North'?

R. Elazar b. R. Shimon
North = from the northern wall of the altar to the wall of the Temple courtyard — plus the area directly opposite the altar. Only a defined strip qualifies.
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
North = the entire Temple courtyard. Any location within the courtyard is valid for slaughtering offerings that require the north.
Gemara
Even if the mishna follows Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi — who permits the entire courtyard — the bull is placed specifically between the Entrance Hall and the altar due to the High Priest's weakness on Yom Kippur. He should not have to walk long distances.
Resolution The mishna is compatible with both opinions; the specific positioning is a practical concession to the High Priest's physical strain on the fast day.

📜 Confession Formula: Which Order?

Rabbi Meir

  • Order: aviti — pashati — chatati
  • Wrongdoing → Rebellion → Sin
  • Derived from Leviticus 16:21 (scapegoat verse)
  • Begins with the more severe transgressions
  • A person who led prayers using this order was corrected by Rabba

The Rabbis (Halakha)

  • Order: chatati — aviti — pashati
  • Sin → Wrongdoing → Rebellion
  • Follows David's prayer sequence in Psalms 106:6
  • Begins with lighter (unwitting) sins, escalates to rebellions
  • ✓ Rav rules: halakha follows the Rabbis

🔗 Proving Atonement Is Through Words

וְכִפֶּר בַּעֲדוֹ וּבְעַד בֵּיתוֹ — ויקרא ט״ז:ו׳
Gezera Shava — Verbal Analogy Question: How do we know that the bull's atonement (Lev. 16:6) is achieved through words — i.e., verbal confession?

Proof 1 — Gezera Shava: The word כִּפֶּר (kipper / atonement) appears both by the bull (16:6) and the scapegoat (16:10). Just as the scapegoat achieves atonement through spoken confession, so too does the bull.

Proof 2 — Order of Verse: Leviticus 16:11 mentions atonement before the word slaughter — proving atonement must precede and be separate from the act of sacrifice, i.e., it is achieved through words alone.
