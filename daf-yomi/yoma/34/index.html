⏰ When Is the Morning Incense Burned?

Mishna
Morning incense was burned between the receiving/sprinkling of the blood and the burning of the limbs on the altar.
Gemara Challenge
But Abaye taught that incense comes after the limbs are already placed on the altar! This seems to contradict the Mishna's sequence.
Resolution
The Mishna follows the Rabbis, not Abaye's view. Moreover, the Mishna is not describing the full sequence — it says only that incense was burned before the limbs were actually burned, not before they were placed.
Key Distinction
Placing limbs on the altar ramp ≠ burning them. Incense comes after placement but before ignition — both accounts are consistent.
Halakhic Precision The Talmud carefully distinguishes between 'taking limbs up to the ramp' and 'burning the limbs' — a distinction that resolves the apparent contradiction between Abaye and the Mishna.

👘 High Priest's Linen Garments on Yom Kippur

☀️ Morning Service

  • Origin: Pelusium, Egypt
  • Value: 12 maneh (1,200 dinars/zuz)
  • Renowned for extraordinary fineness
  • Worn for morning Tamid and Yom Kippur Avodah
  • Higher value reflects the prominence of the morning service

🌆 Afternoon Service

  • Origin: India
  • Value: 8 maneh (800 dinars/zuz)
  • Also fine linen, but less costly than morning garments
  • Worn for the afternoon Tamid service
  • Second immersion occurs between the two garment changes

💧 Heating Water for the High Priest's Immersion

Requirement: The High Priest must immerse five times and sanctify hands and feet ten times on Yom Kippur — in cold water each time.
↓
Accommodation: If the High Priest was old or delicate, hot water was prepared and mixed into the cold mikveh to temper its chill.
↓
Rabbi Yehuda's method: Iron blocks were heated on erev Yom Kippur (the day before) and then cast into the cold water on Yom Kippur itself.
↓
Problem raised: Doesn't the iron continue to heat the water on Yom Kippur itself, constituting forbidden labor (melachah)?
↓
Abaye's resolution: Rabbi Yehuda prohibits unintentional acts only for full Torah-level prohibitions. Heating water via iron is only a rabbinic restriction — so the unintentional continued heating on Yom Kippur is permitted.
