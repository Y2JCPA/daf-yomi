QUESTION 1 OF 5
How many total strands made up each thread of the Tabernacle curtain, and how is this number derived?
A. 6 strands — one color, single ply
B. 24 strands — 4 colors × 6 strands each
C. 28 strands — 4 colors × 6 strands plus 4 gold strands
D. 12 strands — 4 colors × 3 strands each
Correct: B — The curtain used four colored threads (white, purple, scarlet, sky-blue), each spun from 6 strands, giving 24 total. The 28-strand count applies to the breastplate and ephod, which added one gold strand per color.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Which of the three Temple crowns did Rabbi Yoḥanan say was available to any Jew who wished to take it?
A. Crown of the Altar — symbolizing the Priesthood
B. Crown of the Table — symbolizing Royalty
C. Crown of the Ark — symbolizing the Torah
D. All three were equally available
Correct: C — The Crown of the Ark represents the Torah, which — unlike the hereditary priesthood or Davidic kingship — is available to every Jew. This makes it the greatest of the three crowns.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What did Rava derive from the verse 'From within and from without you shall cover it' regarding the gold plating of the Ark?
A. The Ark needed to be inspected from both sides before Yom Kippur
B. The priests who carried the Ark had to purify themselves inside and out
C. Any Torah scholar whose inner character does not match his outward appearance is no scholar at all
D. The Ark's gold plating was purely decorative and held no halakhic significance
Correct: C — Rava read the verse homiletically: just as the Ark was covered with gold both inside and out, a Torah scholar must possess integrity that matches within and without. Abaye adds that such a mismatched person is called 'loathsome.'
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In Rabbi Yannai's parable, what do the 'courtyard' and the 'gate' each represent?
A. Courtyard = Torah study; Gate = fear of Heaven
B. Courtyard = fear of Heaven; Gate = Torah study
C. Courtyard = the Temple; Gate = the priestly vestments
D. Courtyard = good deeds; Gate = prayer
Correct: B — Rabbi Yannai taught that fear of Heaven is the courtyard — the essential foundation — and Torah study is the gate that leads into it. Building a gate without a courtyard (studying Torah without fear of Heaven) is senseless.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How did the Sages resolve the apparent contradiction between 'the staves shall not be removed' and the necessity of carrying the Ark with those very staves?
A. The staves could be fully removed only during transport between encampments
B. Only the High Priest was permitted to remove and reinsert the staves
C. The staves may shift or be repositioned but must never be completely extracted from the rings
D. The prohibition applied only when the Ark was in the Holy of Holies
Correct: C — The baraita clarifies that 'shall not be removed' prohibits full extraction from the rings — the staves may move or shift position during carrying, but permanent detachment is forbidden at all times.
