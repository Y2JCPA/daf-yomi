QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Shimon, when does the frontplate (tzitz) effect acceptance for impure offerings?
A. Only when physically worn on the High Priest's forehead during the service
B. Only during the specific moment of sprinkling the blood
C. Even when not on his forehead, as long as it is intact and fit
D. Only when both the High Priest and the frontplate are ritually pure
Correct: C — 'Always upon his forehead' means continuous awareness, not contact. Rabbi Shimon holds a whole, fit frontplate in the Sanctuary effects acceptance even when not worn — derived from the word 'always' (תָּמִיד), contrasted with 'on his forehead' which sets the location when it is worn.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the key proof the Gemara brings that Rabbi Yosei holds immersion at its appointed time IS a mitzva?
A. He rules that an impure person must immerse even on Yom Kippur itself
B. One with God's name written on flesh may enter water to immerse at the onset of Shabbat
C. He requires the High Priest to immerse five times during the Yom Kippur service
D. He equates sprinkling with immersion in all Temple-related cases
Correct: B — A baraita teaches that one who has God's name written on his flesh may not normally bathe, yet may enter water to immerse at Shabbat's onset. This shows that timely immersion (a mitzva) overrides the usual prohibition — proving Rabbi Yosei values immersion at its appointed time.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does Rava rule that we sprinkle purification water on the High Priest all seven days before Yom Kippur?
A. Because the High Priest is certainly impure from contact with the public
B. Because the calendar — not human action — fixes the seven days, making Days 3 and 7 of any impurity uncertain
C. To satisfy both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosei's positions simultaneously
D. Because the baraita explicitly mandates seven-day sprinkling for the High Priest
Correct: B — Rava explains: for an ordinary person, we know when they became impure, so we sprinkle only on Days 3 and 7. But the High Priest's seven-day period is determined by the calendar (Yom Kippur falls on a fixed date). Since we cannot know when during that period impurity might have occurred, we sprinkle all seven days to be certain.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What was the original name of the Chamber of Parhedrin, and what did the name change signify?
A. Chamber of Hewn Stone — it relocated to a different part of the Temple Mount
B. Chamber of Vessels — it was repurposed from storing the High Priest's garments
C. Chamber of Balvatei — its renaming reflected the decline in worthiness of High Priests
D. Chamber of the Sanhedrin — jurisdiction over the High Priest was transferred
Correct: C — During the era of Shimon HaTzaddik, righteous High Priests who served for life occupied it — it was called the Chamber of Balvatei, a dignified term. When the priesthood began to be sold to unworthy individuals who served only temporarily, the chamber was renamed Parhedrin, connoting temporary or purchased officeholders.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What distinguishes the purpose of sequestering the High Priest before Yom Kippur from sequestering the priest who burns the red heifer?
A. The High Priest's sequestration is for ritual purity; the red heifer priest's is for sanctity
B. The High Priest's sequestration is for sanctity and awe; the red heifer priest's is for ritual purity
C. Both are sequestered for identical reasons — only the duration differs
D. The red heifer priest is sequestered to avoid the public; the High Priest is not restricted from visitors
Correct: B — The Gemara explicitly states: the High Priest's sequestration is 'for the purpose of sanctity' — to inspire reverence before entering the Holy of Holies. His colleagues read Scripture before him to maintain this state of mind. The red heifer priest's sequestration, by contrast, is to achieve the required ritual purity (taharah) necessary for performing the burning.
