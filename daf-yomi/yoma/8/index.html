🏅 Frontplate (Tzitz): Continuous Wearing or Continuous Awareness?

Rabbi Yehuda
The High Priest must continuously wear the frontplate on his forehead. If removed, it no longer effects acceptance — just as phylacteries require actual contact with the body.
Rabbi Shimon
The frontplate effects acceptance even when not worn, as long as it is intact and fit. 'Always upon his forehead' means he must not be distracted from its awareness — not literal continuous contact.
Gemara
Challenge to Rabbi Shimon: The verse also says 'on his forehead' — doesn't that imply physical location is required?
Rabbi Shimon
That verse establishes where it should be placed when worn. But an intact frontplate in the Sanctuary still effects acceptance for impure offerings even if currently off the forehead.
Core Dispute Rabbi Yehuda: acceptance requires actual wearing. Rabbi Shimon: the frontplate's existence and fitness suffices — acceptance is independent of physical contact with the forehead.

⚖️ Rabbi Meir vs. Rabbi Yosei: Public Impurity & Sprinkling

Rabbi Meir

  • Impurity is overridden in cases involving the public
  • Sprinkling purification water is still required — one sprinkles and then immerses
  • Immersion at appointed time IS a mitzva
  • Sprinkling = Immersion: if immersion at appointed time is a mitzva, so too is sprinkling at its appointed time

Rabbi Yosei

  • Impurity is fully permitted in public cases — not just overridden
  • If fully permitted — why require sprinkling at all?
  • Immersion at appointed time IS a mitzva (proven from the baraita about God's name on flesh)
  • Sprinkling ≠ Immersion: the two obligations cannot be equated

💧 When Must the High Priest Receive Sprinkling?

High Priest may have contracted corpse-impurity at some unknown point
↓
Purification requires sprinkling on Day 3 and Day 7 of impurity — other days have no effect
↓
Normal Case
We know when impurity began — sprinkle on correct days only
High Priest / Yom Kippur
Calendar fixes the seven days — we cannot identify which days are Day 3 and Day 7 of any potential impurity
↓
Rava's ruling: Since the period is determined by the calendar (not by us), we sprinkle the High Priest all seven days to ensure Days 3 and 7 are covered

🌊 Is Immersion at the Appointed Time a Mitzva?

First Tanna
Not a mitzva. The impure person must wait until the correct day — immersing earlier has no effect. The timing is governed by the type of impurity, not by any affirmative obligation to immerse precisely at sunset.
Rabbi Yosei
Immersion at its appointed time IS a mitzva. Proof: one who has God's name written on his flesh normally may not bathe, yet may enter water to immerse at Shabbat's onset — the mitzva of timely immersion overrides the normal prohibition.
Gemara
This baraita reveals that Rabbi Yosei does hold immersion at its appointed time is a mitzva — yet he still does not equate sprinkling with immersion. The two obligations remain fundamentally distinct.
Resolution Rabbi Ḥanina the deputy High Priest does not equate sprinkling with immersion. For the red heifer priest, sprinkling all seven days is a rabbinic decree — not because of the mitzva of timely sprinkling.

🚪 The High Priest's Sequestration: From Balvatei to Parhedrin

Seven days before Yom Kippur: The High Priest leaves his home and is transferred to a special chamber adjacent to the Temple
↓
Originally called Chamber of Balvatei (בַּלְוָטֵי) — a term of dignity and honor — used in the era of Shimon HaTzaddik and other righteous High Priests
↓
When the High Priesthood began to be sold or granted to unworthy individuals, the chamber's name changed to reflect the diminished stature of its occupants
↓
Renamed Chamber of Parhedrin — a term associated with temporary officeholders, reflecting that High Priests no longer served for life
↓
Purpose: Sanctity and awe — to prepare the High Priest spiritually and fill him with reverence before entering the Holy of Holies on Yom Kippur. His colleagues would read before him from Scriptures to maintain this state.
