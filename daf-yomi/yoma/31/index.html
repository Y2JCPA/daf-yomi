🪣 Do Interpositions Invalidate the HP's Immersion?

Abaye
Do interpositions that normally invalidate immersion also apply to the High Priest's immersion — which is only ancillary and serves as a reminder? Perhaps we should be lenient?
Rav Yosef
All ordinances the Sages instituted were established according to the same rules as Torah law — so yes, interpositions invalidate even this reminder-immersion. No leniency.
Gemara
But raise the dilemma further — even according to ben Zoma, who is stringent about immersion, and even according to the Rabbis, who do not require all people to immerse. The question is not resolved.
📌 Unresolved Question Whether rabbinic immersion standards track Torah standards exactly remains a live dispute — the Gemara leaves the dilemma standing (תֵּיקוּ).

👍 Does Partial Body Entry Count as Entry?

Question (Abaye → Rav Yosef): If a person enters a forbidden space with only part of their body, is it legally considered entry?
↓
Rav Yosef: The thumbs of the metzora (leper) will prove this!
↓
The Leper's Thumbs
The metzora extends only his thumbs (and big toes) into the Temple courtyard — yet this is accepted as valid entry for the purification rite
Legal Conclusion
Partial body entry counts as full entry — extending thumbs into a space = being considered present in that space

⚖️ Rabbi Meir vs. Rabbis: How Many Sanctifications?

Rabbi Meir

  • Each removal of garments requires its own sanctification
  • Each donning of garments requires its own sanctification
  • Result: 2 sanctifications per transition
  • Total count exceeds ten
  • Therefore the mishna's count may not follow Rabbi Meir
  • Rav Pappa: Mishna counts only between-service sanctifications

The Rabbis

  • Torah juxtaposes garment removal next to donning
  • Just as donning = sanctify while already dressed…
  • …so removal = sanctify while still wearing the garment
  • One sanctification covers both the removal and the next donning
  • Total = exactly ten — precisely matches the mishna
  • Mishna is straightforwardly in accordance with the Rabbis

🌡️ Accommodation for an Elderly or Delicate High Priest

אִסְטְנִיס (istenis) — from Greek: a person of delicate constitution requiring special physical accommodation
Special Dispensation — אִסְטְנִיס If the High Priest was old or an istenis (a delicate, physically sensitive person) who could not bear immersion in cold water, they would heat water on Yom Kippur eve — before the fast began — and pour it into the cold mikveh water to warm it sufficiently. This avoided any Yom Kippur prohibition while honoring the priest's health needs. The immersion remained fully valid.
