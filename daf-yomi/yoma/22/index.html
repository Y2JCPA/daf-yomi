🏛️ From Race to Lottery: Ash-Removal Reform

Original practice: Any priest who wished could remove the ashes — no lottery, no race
↓
Many priests arrived: Competition led to a footrace up the ramp; first within 4 cubits of the altar top wins
↓
The incident: Two priests tied; one shoved the other off the ramp, breaking his leg
↓
Court reformed the practice: Lottery instituted — priests extend fingers; appointee counts to a number
↓
Rav Ashi resolves: Actually two separate ordinances — first when many priests showed up, second after the race turned dangerous

⚖️ Were There One or Two Lottery Ordinances?

Gemara
The mishna implies the lottery was instituted solely because of the dangerous incident — the broken leg. So there was only one reason for the reform?
Rav Ashi
No — two separate ordinances were issued. First, when the Sages saw that many priests were coming, they instituted a lottery. Then, when the race itself became dangerous, they issued a second ordinance.
Gemara
But the mishna only mentions the injury as the cause. Doesn't that suggest a single ordinance?
Rav Ashi
The mishna records the most memorable and dramatic cause. Both ordinances existed; the injury simply overshadowed the earlier, less dramatic reason in the tradition.
Takeaway A mishna may cite the most vivid of multiple legislative reasons without listing them all

📏 The Four Cubits Question — Rav Pappa's Dilemma

Rule: First priest to reach within 4 cubits of the top of the altar wins the right to remove the ashes
↓
From where are these 4 cubits measured?
Not from the bottom
The base (יְסוֹד) is only 1 cubit — not 4 cubits from the ground
Not from the middle
No clear marker midway up the ramp — the law would be undefined
From the top
Must be the 4 cubits adjacent to the altar itself — but does this include or exclude the base and ledge?
↓
Rav Pappa's unresolved dilemma: Are the base cubit (יְסוֹד) and ledge cubit (סוֹבֵב) included in the 4, or excluded? — תֵּיקוּ

👑 Saul and David — Contrasting Royal Legacies

Saul

  • Flawless, unblemished lineage — no ancestry fault
  • Like a one-year-old at coronation: pure, unstained by sin
  • Forewent his royal honor at Gibeah — inappropriate humility for a king
  • Showed unwarranted mercy to Agag of Amalek — contravened Divine command
  • Dynasty did NOT continue — perfection left no room for Divine mercy

David

  • Mixed ancestry — lineage included Ruth the Moabite
  • Two major sins: causing Uriah's death + inciting the census
  • Bathsheba sin: punished personally (fasting, mourning) — settled
  • Mephibosheth slander: punished — estate restored, not kept
  • Dynasty continued — imperfect lineage, sins cleansed through punishment

⚔️ Saul Strove with God over Amalek

אַל תִּתְצַדַּק הַרְבֵּה — Do not be overly righteous (Kohelet 7:16)
Rabbi Mani on I Samuel 15:5 'And Saul strove in the valley' — Rabbi Mani: Saul argued with God about the command to destroy Amalek

His reasoning: If the people sinned, why kill the animals? If the adults sinned, why kill the children?

A Divine Voice responded: "Do not be overly righteous" (Kohelet 7:16) — even compassionate reasoning cannot override an explicit Divine command

Rav Huna: How little does one who has the support of his Lord need to worry — yet Saul, despite his purity, lost his kingdom over this single act of misplaced mercy
