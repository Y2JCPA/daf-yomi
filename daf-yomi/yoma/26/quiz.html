QUESTION 1 OF 5
Who was eligible to participate in the third daily lottery for burning the incense?
A. Any Kohen who had not yet served that day
B. Only Kohanim who had never burned the incense before
C. Only the most senior Kohanim in the Temple
D. Any Kohen, with preference given to newcomers
Correct: B — The Tosefta teaches that no priest ever burned the incense twice — a new priest was always found. The third lottery was exclusively for Kohanim who had never performed this service, because it was said to bring wealth, making it unfair to allow repeat participants.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Abaye explains why the Torah's blessing of wealth applies to incense but not burnt-offerings, even though both appear in Deut. 33:10. What is his reasoning?
A. Incense is intrinsically holier than burnt-offerings
B. Burnt-offerings do not require the same physical effort
C. Incense is infrequent while burnt-offerings are frequent
D. The blessing only applies to the service performed first in the verse
Correct: C — Abaye distinguishes by frequency: burnt-offerings are brought many times daily (obligatory and voluntary), while incense is offered only twice a day in a fixed amount. The Torah's special blessing of wealth attaches to the rare, infrequent service, not to the common one.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak, when IS a new afternoon lottery held (as opposed to the weekday rule)?
A. On Yom Kippur, when all service is especially sacred
B. On Rosh Chodesh, when additional offerings are brought
C. On Shabbat, when the outgoing priestly rotation is replaced by an incoming one
D. Whenever the morning priest becomes ritually impure
Correct: C — Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak resolves the contradiction between Rabbi Yochanan and the baraita: on weekdays, the morning priest keeps his privilege in the afternoon (no new lottery). But on Shabbat, priestly mishmarot rotate — the outgoing rotation serves in the morning, the incoming one in the afternoon — requiring a fresh lottery.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How many priests participate in the daily afternoon (mincha) offering, and what makes it different from the basic morning offering?
A. 9 priests — identical to the morning offering
B. 10 priests — one extra added for the afternoon wine libation
C. 11 priests — 9 plus 2 who bring the two logs for the altar fire
D. 13 priests — with the full complement of libation and meal-offering priests
Correct: C — The daily afternoon offering requires 11 priests: the standard 9 who perform the animal sacrifice plus 2 additional priests who carry the two logs placed on the altar fire. Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai derives from a change to plural verb form in the Torah that the logs must be brought by two separate priests.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What do individual offerings and communal offerings share in common regarding flaying and cutting, according to the Gemara?
A. Both require exactly the same number of priests to be assigned
B. Both must be performed exclusively by ordained Kohanim
C. Both may be performed by non-priests (Israelites)
D. Both require a special lottery before the service can begin
Correct: C — Ḥizkiya and the Gemara teach that flaying and cutting are equal for individual and communal offerings: in both cases, these tasks may be performed by non-priests. They are not classified as avodah (Temple service) requiring priestly status, and this is derived from a scriptural comparison between the two types of offerings.
