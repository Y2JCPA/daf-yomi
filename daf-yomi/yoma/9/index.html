⚖️ First Temple vs. Second Temple Generations

First Temple Era

  • Committed idolatry, sexual immorality, bloodshed
  • Sins were open and unconcealed
  • End of punishment was revealed by prophets
  • Wicked — yet they trusted in God
  • Baseless hatred limited to princes only

Second Temple Era

  • Observed Torah, mitzvot, and gemilut chasadim
  • Did NOT commit the three cardinal sins
  • Sins concealed — end of punishment unrevealed
  • Pervaded by שִׂנְאַת חִנָּם — baseless hatred
  • Temple still unrebuilt — judgment persists today

💬 First or Second Generation — Who Was Greater?

Rabbi Yoḥanan
The fingernails of the former (First Temple generation) are preferable to the belly of the latter. Despite their sins, the First Temple generation was spiritually superior.
Reish Lakish
On the contrary! The Second Temple generation was superior — even under subjugation by foreign kingdoms, they were engaged in Torah study!
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Their very Torah study proves my point — they had Torah available yet still fell to baseless hatred. This makes their failure all the more inexcusable.
Rabbi Elazar
Look to the Temple — if it has been restored as it was for our predecessors, then the latter were greater. Since it has not been rebuilt, this testifies against them.
Bottom Line Sinat chinam is so severe that the Temple remains unrebuilt — the very absence of restoration is the verdict

שִׂנְאַת חִנָּם שְׁקוּלָה כְּנֶגֶד שָׁלֹשׁ עֲבֵירוֹת — Baseless hatred equals the three cardinal sins
💔 The Weight of Sinat Chinam The Second Temple generation observed Torah, mitzvot, and gemilut chasadim — and yet baseless hatred among them was equal in weight to all three cardinal sins of the First Temple combined. Because their sin was concealed rather than exposed, their punishment has no revealed end. Rabbi Elazar teaches: the unrebuilt Temple is itself the ongoing testimony. When Reish Lakish swam in the Jordan and Rabba bar bar Hana helped him out, Reish Lakish rebuked him — the Babylonians' failure to make aliyah en masse is itself a symptom of this incomplete redemption.
