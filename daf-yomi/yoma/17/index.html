🏛️ Where Was the Chamber of the Lambs?

Contradiction: Tractate Middot vs. other mishnayot — Chamber of the Lambs in different corners
↓
Rav Adda's Answer
Chamber was set back from the corner — not literally at the corner of the Hall of the Hearth
Rav Huna's Answer
Each tanna envisions the circuit in opposite directions — same chamber, different reference point
↓
Both resolutions converge: Chamber was closer to the southwest corner — contradiction resolved

📚 Tractate Middot — Whose Opinion?

Gemara
Must one not conclude from this that the mishnayot in tractate Middot are in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov?
Conclusion
Indeed — conclude from it that this is so. The mishnayot in Middot follow Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov throughout.
Rav Adda b. Rav Yitzḥak
The Chamber of the Lambs was removed from the corner — it was not actually situated at the corner of the Hall of the Hearth but was located at some distance from it.
Gemara
In any event, it is reasonable that this chamber was closer to the southwest corner than to the northwest corner — and we can demonstrate this from the structure of the contradictions themselves.
Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov His accounts of the Temple are regarded as precise and authoritative. The Talmud frequently invokes his rulings in Middot as the definitive architectural description of the Temple.

🧭 Rav Huna's Resolution — Two Directions

How can two mishnayot about the shewbread describe the Chamber of the Lambs as being in different corners?
↓
Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua: Each tanna begins his circuit from the same point but travels in opposite directions
↓
Tractate Middot
Envisions chambers via the right:
South → East → North → West
Shewbread Mishnah
Envisions chambers via the left:
South → West → North → East
↓
The same chamber appears in a different positional relationship depending on which direction the tanna envisions traveling — the contradiction is only apparent

👑 The High Priest's Two Privileges

Privilege 1 — Sacrificing First

  • High Priest may sacrifice any portion he chooses before other priests
  • If another priest brings an offering on the same day, the High Priest has priority
  • He may select which offering he wants to bring first
  • This reflects his supreme status in Temple service

Privilege 2 — Eating First

  • High Priest may eat any priestly portion he chooses first
  • He declares: 'This sin-offering, I am eating'
  • Or: 'This guilt-offering, I am eating'
  • He may even take one whole loaf of the two Shavuot loaves
  • Or one loaf from the twelve loaves of showbread

🍞 The Baraita's Internal Contradiction

ClauseRuling on Shavuot LoavesUnderlying Opinion
First clauseHigh Priest takes one loaf of the two Shavuot loavesRabbi Yehuda HaNasi — H.P. takes half of all communal offerings (= one whole loaf of two)
Middle clauseHigh Priest takes one loaf of the twelve showbread loavesThe Rabbis — H.P. takes half only when items come in pairs; otherwise one from the set
Last clauseHigh Priest takes half of the two loaves (= one whole loaf)Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi again — consistent with the first clause
The ProblemFirst and last match; middle clause seems to follow a different opinionApparent self-contradiction — one baraita, two opinions?

אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: רֵישָׁא וּמְצִיעָתָא — דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים, וּבִשְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם מוֹדוּ חֲכָמִים לְרַבִּי שֶׁאֵין רָאוּי לִיתֵּן לוֹ פְּרוּסָה
⚖️ Abaye's Resolution The first clause and the middle clause of the baraita both follow the opinion of the Rabbis. The Rabbis hold that the High Priest takes half of communal offerings divided in two — but they concede with regard to the two Shavuot loaves: dividing a loaf would leave only a piece (פְּרוּסָה), and it is inappropriate to present the High Priest with a mere broken piece. Therefore even the Rabbis agree he receives one whole loaf — and the last clause is consistent with this, not a contradiction.
