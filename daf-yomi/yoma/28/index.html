🌅 From Temple Procedure to Patriarchal Torah

Rava
If arranging the two logs is a complete Temple service worthy of the death penalty for a non-priest, it needs its own dedicated lottery!
Gemara
But burning of limbs and fats is also a complete service — yet it has no separate lottery. So completeness alone cannot be the criterion!
Resolution
Arranging logs is a nighttime service; burning limbs is a daytime service — they belong to entirely different categories.
Rav
Abraham our Patriarch fulfilled the entire Torah before it was given — even rabbinic ordinances — as it says: "My mitzvot, My statutes, and My Torahs" (Gen. 26:5).
Daf Theme Two intertwined questions: What makes a Temple service 'complete' enough to demand its own lottery? And how far back does Torah observance truly reach — all the way to Abraham?

🎲 Rava vs. the Gemara — Logs and the Lottery System

Rav Asi / R' Yoḥanan
A non-priest who arranges the two logs on the altar is liable to the death penalty. This means it is a full, complete Temple service.
Rava
Then it should require a separate, dedicated lottery — not bundled with other tasks. Every complete service deserves its own lot!
Gemara
It does appear in a lottery — the baraita confirms it. Rava must mean it should have a lottery entirely by itself, not grouped with other services.
Gemara (counter)
But then what of the burning of limbs and fats? That too is a complete service for which a non-priest is liable — yet it has no dedicated lottery!
Final Answer
Logs = nighttime service. Burning limbs = daytime service. These are fundamentally separate realms — the Mishna's mention of slaughter only teaches that nighttime performance is irreparable.
Key Distinction Nighttime and daytime services are separate categories. A nighttime service (logs) cannot be compared to a daytime one (burning limbs) for lottery purposes.

🗣️ Three Tannaitic Formulas for Announcing Dawn

Rabbi Yishmael

  • Formula: "The light flashed"
  • Indicates the first flash of dawn light
  • Minimal brightness threshold
  • Earliest possible announcement of day

Rabbi Akiva

  • Formula: "The light has risen"
  • Brighter than a mere flash
  • Fuller illumination required
  • Later threshold — more light visible

R' Yehuda ben Beteira

  • Formula: "The people have gone to work"
  • Gemara objects: this is very bright!
  • Workers leave only in full daylight
  • Much later than either preceding opinion

🕍 Mincha Time — Derived from Abraham's Prayer

Rav Safra: The time for Mincha begins when walls begin to blacken from shade — when the descending sun casts shadows on the walls
↓
Source: Abraham prayed in the afternoon — "Abraham rose early to the place where he had stood" (Gen. 19:27). 'Standing' alludes to the Amida prayer.
↓
Rav Yosef Objects
Can we derive halakha from Abraham? He lived before the Torah was given! His conduct has no legal force.
Rava Responds
The Tanna already derived halakha from Abraham — citing him as a precedent for reshit. Rav Yosef's principle is already contradicted.
↓
Rava's further difficulty: A Mishna implies mincha is offered before 6.5 hours on Passover-eve Shabbat — but Rav Safra says walls blacken at 6.5 hours. Perhaps Temple walls were tapered and cast shadows earlier than ordinary walls.
