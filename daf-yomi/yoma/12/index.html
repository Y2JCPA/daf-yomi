🏛️ Was Jerusalem Divided Among the Tribes?

Rabbis
Jerusalem was not divided among the tribes — it belongs to all of Israel. Homeowners never charged rent because they held no private title. Guests kept the hides of their Passover offerings as payment.
Rabbi Yehuda
Jerusalem was divided among the tribes — Judah and Benjamin each own their portion. Private ownership existed, so houses there can contract leprosy-impurity (tzara'at habayit).
Rabbis
Therefore synagogues in large cities — which attract all of Israel — are public property. No single owner means the verse 'land of your possession' (Lev. 14:34) does not apply.
Rabbi Yehuda
Village synagogues can contract impurity because they are owned by local residents — identifiable, private owners. The verse applies.
📌 Practical Consequence Whether Jerusalem was tribally divided determines if its houses and synagogues can contract tzara'at — and underlies the entire debate about priestly jurisdiction over the Temple precincts.

🔵 The Temple Straddles Two Tribes

Tribe of Judah

  • 🏔️ The entire Temple Mount (Har HaBayit)
  • 🏛️ The Temple building (Heikhal)
  • 🔥 The Altar (Mizbe'ach)
  • ⛺ The Temple Courtyard (Azarah) — except the Chamber of Hewn Stone
  • 🗝️ The majority of the sacred precincts

Tribe of Benjamin

  • 🏠 The Chamber of Hewn Stone (Lishkat HaGazit) — Sanhedrin headquarters
  • 🚪 A strip of Benjamin's land 'swallowed' into Judah's section
  • 🙏 Verse: "Ever does he protect him and he rests between his shoulders" (Deut. 33:12)
  • ⚖️ Like one who cannot abide something between his shoulders — Benjamin is nestled within Judah

👔 The Priestly Belt: Linen or Kilayim?

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
The belt of the common priest is kilayim — a mixture of wool and linen. This matches the elaborate Tabernacle descriptions and distinguishes the common priest's belt from the High Priest's Yom Kippur belt.
Rabbis
The belt of the common priest is made entirely of linen — the same material as the High Priest's belt on Yom Kippur. There is no kilayim in the regular priestly garments.
Ravin (clarification)
All agree: The High Priest's Yom Kippur belt = pure linen (explicit in Torah). The dispute concerns only the common priest's belt throughout the year.
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
Proof from Lev. 6:3 — "And the priest shall put on his linen garment" — the term יִלְבַּשׁ (shall put on) broadens the application of linen garments, supporting the Rabbis' view.
❌ Rabbi Dosa Rejected Rabbi Dosa claimed the High Priest's Yom Kippur linen garments could later be used by a common priest. This is rejected: garments used for the most sacred service should not descend to ordinary service — a matter of honor for the Temple.

🔄 How the Replacement High Priest is Initiated

Triggering Event: The High Priest becomes ritually impure or otherwise disqualified before Yom Kippur
↓
Designation: Another priest is appointed as replacement High Priest — but how do we identify him?
↓
Rav Adda bar Ahava: He is recognizable by his linen belt — the belt distinguishes High Priest (linen) from common priest (different material)
↓
Abaye's proposal: Before Yom Kippur service, the replacement dons all eight garments of the High Priest and turns over a limb on the altar — a formal initiation act
↓
Rejection of Abaye: The preliminary act is superfluous — his very performance of the Yom Kippur service (valid only for a High Priest) constitutes his elevation
↓
Conclusion: No pre-initiation ritual is required; the Yom Kippur service itself establishes his status as High Priest
