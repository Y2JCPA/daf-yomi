🔥 Hirhurei Aveira — Thoughts of Sin Are Worse

הִרְהוּרֵי עֲבֵירָה קָשִׁים מֵעֲבֵירָה
Opening Principle The daf opens with a striking moral-philosophical claim: hirhurei aveira (thoughts of transgression) are worse than the transgression itself. Three parallel mnemonics drive home the point: the smell of roasting meat is more enticing than eating it; the heat at summer's end is more oppressive than midsummer; and a winter fever burns more powerfully than a summer fever — because a cold oven requires greater heat to warm than a hot one.

🔥 Three Analogies: The 'Worse Than' Principle

Smell of Roasting Meat

  • Thoughts of sin worse than sin itself
  • Aroma more enticing than actually eating
  • Unchecked desire amplifies the pull
  • The imagination exceeds reality

Heat at Summer's End

  • End-of-summer heat is harsher than midsummer
  • Body already depleted by prolonged exposure
  • Accumulated wear makes the heat worse
  • Later stage of suffering exceeds the peak

Winter Fever (Cold Oven)

  • Winter fever more powerful than summer fever
  • Mnemonic: a cold oven needs greater heat
  • A cold body requires more force to raise temperature
  • Low baseline amplifies the intensity of the fever

🦌 Esther as Hind of the Dawn — Miracles and Scripture

Rabbi Zeira
Why is Esther likened to a hind (אַיֶּלֶת הַשַּׁחַר)? Just as a hind's womb is narrow and she is beloved to her mate anew at each encounter — so Esther found renewed miraculous favor before Ahasuerus at every appearance.
Gemara Question
But isn't there the miracle of Hanukkah, which was performed many years after Purim? The verse implies Esther was the final great miracle — how can that be reconciled?
First Answer
Additional miracles were performed after Purim — but Purim was the last miracle for which permission was granted to record in Scripture. Hanukkah's miracle was never written into Tanakh. Thus Esther marks the end of scriptural miracles.
Second Answer
Alternatively: Those who hold permission was not granted for Esther's scroll at all explain differently — the analogy to dawn's gradual spreading light describes how her salvation emerged slowly, not that she ends all miracles.
Scriptural Source "For the leader, about the hind of the dawn" (Ps. 22:1) — Rabbi Abbahu: Just as dawn's light spreads and grows gradually, so Esther's salvation unfolded incrementally before Ahasuerus.

🌄 The Dawn Incident: Slaughtering the Tamid Too Early

Incident: In the Temple, priests mistook the moon's brightness for dawn and slaughtered the daily offering (tamid) before dawn
↓
Rest of the Year
Could have waited for true dawn — yet confusion happened. Institution: An appointed priest now announces dawn's arrival from a high point in the Temple.
Yom Kippur
The appointed priest ascends to the roof of the Beit HaParvah. When he sees dawn, he announces it so the Kohen Gadol may immerse at exactly the right moment for the Avodah.
↓
Ruling (Father of Rabbi Avin): Any offering slaughtered, pinched, or whose handful was removed before dawn is disqualified — not merely premature — and must be burned

🍞 Rava vs. Rabbi Zeira: Resolving the Shewbread Contradiction

Father of Rabbi Avin
Baraita: Night service is not merely premature — it disqualifies. Even a handful of meal-offering removed at night cannot be restored. The offering must be burned.
Rabbi Zeira
Objection from Mishna: If a priest arranged the shewbread on Sunday (after Shabbat ends), leave it on the table until the following Shabbat. This implies night arrangement is only premature — not disqualifying!
Rava
Rabbi Zeira raises the objection well. And the father of Rabbi Avin cites a received baraita — neither position can simply be dismissed. The contradiction must be resolved.
Ravina
Resolution: Shewbread is only consecrated when Shabbat day arrives. Arranging it on Sunday night precedes consecration entirely — there is no 'night service' in the disqualifying sense. The baraita and mishna address different stages.
Temple Purity Protocol (Mishna) Anyone who covers his legs (defecates) in the Temple must immerse afterward. Anyone who urinates must sanctify hands and feet from the laver (כִּיּוֹר) — two levels of purification for two degrees of bodily function.
