QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Ami, why do hands require sanctification after brushing urine drops?
A. Urine is a primary source of ritual impurity requiring full immersion
B. This is a unique Temple enactment with no logical basis
C. One uses one's hands to brush urine drops, so the hands themselves must be washed
D. Only the right hand requires washing; the left is exempt
Correct: C — Rabbi Ami teaches that since one brushes urine drops with his hands, those hands require sanctification — the direct use of the hands in this act is what triggers the washing requirement.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why is excrement in its anatomical place (the anus) different from excrement on one's skin regarding Shema?
A. Excrement on skin is always visible; excrement in place is always hidden
B. Excrement in its place is a greater disgrace — the body itself is considered impure
C. Excrement on skin prohibits Shema; excrement in its place permits it
D. Rav Huna holds both cases equally prohibit reciting Shema
Correct: B — The Gemara distinguishes: excrement in its anatomical place is a greater disgrace — the body itself is rendered impure — unlike excrement merely on the skin, where Rav Huna permits Shema recitation.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the core practical halakhic difference between Ben Zoma and Rabbi Yehuda regarding immersion before Temple service?
A. Ben Zoma requires immersion only for the High Priest; Rabbi Yehuda requires it for all kohanim
B. Ben Zoma holds failure to immerse violates a positive Torah commandment and may desecrate service; Rabbi Yehuda holds there is no violation
C. Ben Zoma requires immersion in sacred Temple waters; Rabbi Yehuda permits any valid mikveh
D. Ben Zoma holds the service is always valid regardless; Rabbi Yehuda holds it is always desecrated
Correct: B — Ben Zoma views the immersion as a Torah-level obligation — omitting it violates a positive mitzva and may desecrate the service. Rabbi Yehuda views it as a rabbinic reminder tool — no Torah violation occurs if omitted.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does a leper who immersed on the evening before his eighth day NOT need to immerse again in the morning?
A. The eighth-day immersion applies only upon actual Temple entry, not in the morning
B. Lepers are categorically exempt from the general immersion requirement
C. His prior evening immersion already fulfilled the reminder function for forgotten impurity
D. Rabbi Yehuda ruled that lepers follow a different purification law than kohanim
Correct: C — The baraita explains: since he already immersed the previous evening, that immersion purified him and reminded him of any old impurity. The entire purpose of the morning immersion is thus already fulfilled.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
When re-entering a meal, what distinguishes the rules for washing before drinking vs. washing before eating?
A. Before drinking: wash outside; before eating: wash inside in public view
B. Before drinking: wash both hands; before eating: wash only the brushing hand
C. Before drinking: must wash hands visibly inside in public; before eating: may wash outside and then enter
D. No distinction — one must always wash both hands publicly before returning to the table
Correct: C — Rav Ḥisda rules: when re-entering to drink, one must wash inside where others can see (public appearance of cleanliness required). When re-entering to eat, washing outside before entering is acceptable — eating demands greater care, making prior washing sufficient.
