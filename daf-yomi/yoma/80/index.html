🥚 Why Does Impurity Get a Different Measure?

Baraita
All Torah eating measures are an olive-bulk (כזית) — the verse uses the same language throughout. But the impurity measure is different because the verse changed its expression.
Rabbi Abbahu citing Rabbi Elazar
The verse says okhel ye'akhel — a doubled eating expression. This means food eaten at one time. The esophagus holds at most one chicken egg — so the measure is an egg-bulk (כביצה).
Gemara
Perhaps it refers to the giant egg of the bar yokhani bird, which is enormous?
Rabbi Abbahu
Im tafasta merubeh lo tafasta; im tafasta mu'at tafasta — if you grasped much, you grasped nothing; if you grasped little, you grasped something. We use a regular chicken egg.
Key Principle The doubled verb okhel ye'akhel signals a departure from the standard olive-bulk, yielding an egg-bulk for impurity — the maximum a human esophagus can hold in one swallow.

🫒 Torah Eating Measures Side by Side

Olive-Bulk (כזית)

  • Default for ALL Torah eating prohibitions
  • Forbidden fat, blood, non-kosher food
  • Liability for sin-offering in most cases
  • Smaller than an egg — stricter threshold

Large Date-Bulk (ככותבת)

  • Specific to Yom Kippur eating prohibition
  • Larger than olive-bulk — more lenient
  • Derived from the special affliction context
  • All foods combine toward this measure

Egg-Bulk (כביצה)

  • Specific to ritual impurity of foods
  • Derived from okhel ye'akhel (doubled expression)
  • Based on esophagus capacity
  • Larger than olive, smaller than bar yokhani egg

📝 Rabbi Elazar: Record What You Ate

One eats forbidden fat unwittingly — must eventually bring a sin-offering
↓
Rabbi Elazar: Write down the exact amount eaten, lest a future court changes the measure
↓
Future court INCREASES minimum
If he ate less than the new larger minimum → no offering required. His written record proves he is now exempt.
Future court DECREASES minimum
A smaller threshold cannot retroactively obligate him — his sin was committed under old law. Writing is still precautionary.

🤔 Rabbi Zeira's Cascade of Objections

Rabbi Zeira — Objection 1
Why is eating measured universally (large date for everyone) while drinking varies by individual cheek size? The asymmetry is unexplained.
Rabbi Zeira — Objection 2
Is Og King of Bashan really satisfied by a date-bulk of food? If giants are not satisfied, eating should also be individualized like drinking.
Rabbi Zeira — Objection 3
If the eating measure is about settling one's mind, then fatty meat (satisfying) vs grapevine shoots (not satisfying) should yield different measures.
Rava — Additional Objection
Yom Kippur eating liability = date-bulk; but rendering impure via eating = egg-bulk. These are different measures, so foods eaten can't combine across both standards.
Status These objections are left unresolved — the Gemara records the tensions between universal and personalized halachic measures without a final reconciliation.

🍖 Combining Foods, Overeating, and Sinai Tradition

§ Salt Combines with Meat (Rav Pappa): If one ate meat with salt on it, the salt and meat combine toward the date-bulk measure — even though salt alone is not considered food for Yom Kippur purposes.
↓
Overeating Exemption (Reish Lakish): One who eats in an excessive manner — forcing himself to eat when already full — is exempt from Yom Kippur liability. Eating beyond satiation is not halachic 'eating.'
↓
Practical case: One who overate on Erev Yom Kippur and was still full when the fast began — any eating during the fast is considered beyond satiation and does not obligate.
↓
Parallel — Teruma (Rabbi Yirmeya citing Reish Lakish): A non-priest who ate teruma excessively pays the principal but NOT the added fifth — excessive eating is legally diminished eating.
↓
§ Halakha l'Moshe miSinai (Rabbi Yoḥanan): All measures and punishments are oral tradition from Sinai. Even though punishments appear in the Torah, their specific measures and thresholds are transmitted orally.
