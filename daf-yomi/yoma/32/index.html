🛁 Five Immersions, Ten Sanctifications

The High Priest performs 5 immersions and 10 sanctifications of hands and feet on Yom Kippur
↓
Each immersion corresponds to a change of garments between golden and white vestments — five transitions total
↓
Two sanctifications accompany each immersion: one before removing old garments, one after donning new ones
↓
Rav Ḥisda: if performed in the Torah's written order, the tradition of 5 + 10 is confirmed by counting

📜 Deriving the Five Immersions — Three Approaches

Rabbi Yehuda
Derived from the verses describing garment removal and donning — each garment change implies a required immersion before the next set.
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
Derived from: 'He shall bathe his body in water and put them on' (Lev. 16:24) — bathing and donning are paired, implying immersion before each set of garments.
School of Rabbi Yishmael
A fortiori: If a priest entering the Temple for ordinary service requires sanctification without immersion, certainly the High Priest changing into sacred garments on Yom Kippur requires full immersion!
Rav Ḥisda
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's view is an independent position — distinct from Rabbi Meir (who requires more sanctifications) and the Rabbis (who require fewer).
Consensus All opinions agree on 5 immersions and 10 sanctifications — the debate concerns only the scriptural derivation.

👘 Golden Garments vs. White Garments

Golden Garments (בִּגְדֵי זָהָב)

  • Worn for communal/public offerings
  • Daily morning and afternoon tamid
  • Ram guilt-offering and musaf
  • Eight garments total
  • Used throughout the year as well

White Garments (בִּגְדֵי לָבָן)

  • Worn for entering the Holy of Holies
  • Bull and goat atonement service
  • Sending away the scapegoat (Azazel)
  • Four fine linen garments only
  • Used exclusively on Yom Kippur

🔄 Why Two Sanctifications Per Immersion?

The High Priest transitions between two services
↓
1st Sanctification
Sanctifies hands and feet before removing current garments — prepares to conclude service
2nd Sanctification
Sanctifies hands and feet after donning new garments — prepares to begin next service
↓
Source: 'And Aaron shall come…and he shall remove the linen garments…and he shall wash his flesh…and put on his garments' — two distinct sanctification points implied

🔪 What Does Keratzo Mean? The Mishna states the High Priest keratzo the sheep of the daily morning offering. Ulla: keratzo means 'killing/slaughtering.' Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak confirms via Rav Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish: valid slaughter requires cutting the majority of both organs — the windpipe and the gullet. The completion of the slaughter is a Temple service and cannot be performed by a non-priest.
