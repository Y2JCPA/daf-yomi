QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Ashi, what does the verse 'With a bull Aaron shall come into the sacred place' (Lev. 16:3) prove?
A. The live bull must accompany Aaron into the Holy of Holies
B. Blood is legally called 'the bull' — Aaron enters bearing the bull's blood
C. Aaron may only enter the sacred place after purchasing the bull personally
D. The bull must be slaughtered immediately upon Aaron's entrance
Correct: B — Rav Ashi argues that Aaron cannot bring a live bull into the Holy of Holies — he brings its blood. Therefore the verse uses 'bull' to mean the bull's blood, proving that blood is legally classified as part of 'the bull.'
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the final halakhic principle determining which offerings override Shabbat and ritual impurity?
A. All communal offerings override; no individual offerings ever do
B. Only Yom Kippur offerings override due to their unique sanctity
C. Any offering with a fixed time overrides; any offering without a fixed time does not
D. Only blood-service offerings override; meal-offerings and burnt-offerings do not
Correct: C — The Gemara rejects 'communal vs. individual' as the criterion — R. Ya'akov shows communal offerings that do NOT override. The final principle: fixed-time offerings (like Yom Kippur's) override Shabbat and impurity; offerings with no fixed time do not.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Rava's final answer to Abaye's challenge regarding the second set of Yom Kippur bulls all dying?
A. The baraita refers to the bull for an unwitting communal sin, not the HP's bull
B. The HP's bull is a communal offering and communal sin-offerings are not left to die
C. The HP's bull is a partners' sin-offering — partners' offerings are not left to die while partners live
D. The second bull never gains full sanctity because it was consecrated conditionally
Correct: C — Rava ultimately categorizes the HP's bull as a 'sin-offering of partners' (קָרְבַּן שֻׁתָּפִין). Since Israel are the beneficiaries and co-owners, and since some partners (Israel) are always alive, the replacement bull cannot be left to die — so the entire second set must die when the first is found.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
When Rabbi Elazar wonders whether substitution (temurah) applies to the Yom Kippur bull, which principle does Rabbi Yoḥanan provide to address part of the dilemma?
A. We follow the consecrator (the High Priest), so it is individual and substitution applies
B. We follow the one who seeks atonement (Israel), so Israel is the owner — making it communal
C. The High Priest's stated intention at the time of consecration is the sole legal factor
D. Since the offering overrides Shabbat, it must be communal and substitution is impossible
Correct: B — Rabbi Abbahu citing Rabbi Yoḥanan: we follow the one who seeks atonement, not the mere consecrator. One who consecrates an animal for another's atonement — the beneficiary (Israel) is the legal owner. This means the HP's bull belongs to Israel, not just to the HP.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rav Sheshet argues that the offering in the baraita — which overrides Shabbat and can have a substitute — is NOT the Yom Kippur bull. What does he say it is instead?
A. The goat sent to Azazel on Yom Kippur
B. The communal bull for an unwitting sin brought by the Sanhedrin
C. The ram of Aaron brought as a burnt-offering on Yom Kippur
D. The tamid (daily) offering which has a fixed twice-daily schedule
Correct: C — Rav Sheshet identifies the offering as Aaron's personal ram (עֵגֶל of Aaron → his ram-burnt-offering on Yom Kippur, Lev. 16:3). The bull cannot be the subject because the Gemara notes the bull has far more overriding restrictions than merely Shabbat and impurity — making it a poor fit for the baraita.
