🩸 Does Blood Count as 'The Bull'? (50a)

R. Yitzḥak Nappaḥa
Objection! 'Remove the entire bull outside the camp' (Lev. 4:12) — the bull is only removed after slaughter AND burning of fats. The blood was already sprinkled beforehand, proving blood is NOT part of the remaining bull.
R. Ami
Correct — that verse proves the bull exists independently of its blood. Blood already served its purpose at sprinkling; calling blood 'the bull' is a stretch.
Rav Ashi
I side with those who say blood IS called 'the bull.' Proof: 'With a bull Aaron shall come into the sacred place' (Lev. 16:3). He cannot bring in the live bull — he brings in the blood. So blood = bull!
Counter-position
That verse means Aaron became qualified to enter the sacred place by acquiring and consecrating a bull from his own personal funds — not by carrying its blood inside.
Crux of the Debate "With a bull Aaron shall come in" (Lev. 16:3) — does 'bull' refer to the living animal, its blood, or the act of purchasing it?

⚖️ Which Offerings Override Shabbat & Impurity? (50a)

R. Meir's Position

  • Communal sacrifices → override Shabbat & impurity
  • Individual sacrifices → override neither
  • Yom Kippur bull = individual → does NOT override
  • High Priest's wafer meal-offering = individual → does NOT override
  • Challenged by R. Ya'akov with counter-examples

R. Ya'akov's Rebuttal

  • Bull for unwitting communal sin — communal yet does NOT override
  • Goats for communal idolatry — communal yet do NOT override
  • Festival goats — communal yet do NOT override
  • ∴ 'Communal' cannot be the operative criterion!
  • There must be a different distinguishing principle

Final Accepted Principle

  • ✅ Fixed time (זְמַן קָבוּעַ) → overrides Shabbat & impurity
  • ❌ No fixed time → overrides neither
  • Applies equally to individual AND communal offerings
  • Yom Kippur offerings: fixed annual time → override
  • Bull for unwitting sin: no fixed time → does NOT override

💀 The Lost Yom Kippur Bull — Abaye vs. Rava (50a–b)

Abaye's Challenge: A baraita teaches — if the Yom Kippur bull was lost, a replacement was set aside, and the first was then found: all animals of the second set must die.
This implies the bull is communal — not individual as R. Meir says!
↓
Rava (first answer): The baraita refers to a bull for an unwitting communal sin — not the High Priest's Yom Kippur bull at all.
↓
Abaye refutes: The baraita explicitly states 'of Yom Kippur' — it must be the High Priest's bull!
↓
Rava's Revised Answer ✓
It is a sin-offering of partners (קָרְבַּן שֻׁתָּפִין). High Priest atones for all Israel → Israel are co-owners. A partners' sin-offering is never left to die because some partners still live.
Why not 'communal'?
A tribe can bring a communal sin-offering for communal error. Partners cannot bring one for a partners' error. The HP's bull is closer to 'partners' than 'communal.'

🔄 Rabbi Elazar's Dilemma: Can One Make a Substitute? (50b)

תְּמוּרָה — substitution: if one says 'this unconsecrated animal shall replace my consecrated one,' both become sacred (Lev. 27:10)
Rabbi Elazar Asks According to Rabbi Meir — who holds the Yom Kippur bull is an individual offering — can the High Priest perform temurah (substitution) for it? Option A: Follow the consecrator (the High Priest who paid) → it is individual → his substitution takes effect Option B: Follow the beneficiary (all Israel who seek atonement) → it is communal → no substitution Resolution (R. Abbahu citing R. Yoḥanan): We follow the one who seeks atonement. One who consecrates an animal for another's atonement — the beneficiary is the legal owner. Deeper dilemma remains: Do fellow priests achieve atonement through the essence of the offering (like the High Priest) — or only through the High Priest's verbal confession?
