⚖️ The Chatzi Shiur Debate

Rabbi Yochanan
A half-measure is prohibited by Torah law. Though one receives no punishment for eating it, the act itself is Torah-forbidden — the verse's language implies even partial eating violates the prohibition.
Reish Lakish
A half-measure is permitted by Torah law. Only a full shiur triggers a Torah prohibition. Any cited verse is merely an asmakhta — rabbinic support, not a true Torah source.
Why It Matters If chatzi shiur is Torah-prohibited, swearing not to eat renders even the smallest amount forbidden under oath law. If only rabbinic, that oath logic may not apply — and offerings for violations differ accordingly.

⚖️ Challenges to Reish Lakish from the Baraita

Rabbi Yochanan (objection)
Proof from forbidden fat (chelev):
A baraita derives from a verse that only items punishable by karet are included in that prohibition — implying the verse itself is the source of the law, making a half-measure Torah-forbidden.
Reish Lakish (response)
The verse is only a support (asmakhta):
If it were a true Torah source, the verse would have no need to separately mention karet. Its citation as a proof-text merely hangs a rabbinic ruling on a scriptural hook.
Gemara Endorses Reish Lakish's Reading It is reasonable to say the baraita cites the verse as support only — for if it were a Torah prohibition, why would the verse need to specify karet as the distinguishing factor?

🍞 Gezerah Shavah: What Does 'Affliction' Mean?

Yom Kippur — Source Verse

  • "You shall afflict your souls" (Leviticus 16:29)
  • What type of affliction is required?
  • The verse alone does not specify fasting
  • School of Rabbi Yishmael: derive meaning from parallel usage elsewhere
  • Conclusion: affliction on Yom Kippur = withholding food

Manna in the Desert — Parallel Verse

  • "He afflicted you and caused you to hunger, and fed you with manna" (Deut. 8:3)
  • Affliction here is explicitly linked to hunger
  • This verse anchors the gezerah shavah
  • Just as desert affliction = hunger, so too Yom Kippur
  • Challenge rejected: 'affliction' in Gen. 31:50 (Laban) means marital relations, not hunger — not comparable

👁️ Manna, Blindness, and the Satisfaction of Eating

Verse: "Who feeds you manna in the desert which your fathers did not know" (Deut. 8:3) — in what sense did the fathers not know the manna?
↓
Rav Yosef teaches: From here there is an allusion that blind people eat but are not fully satisfied — because they cannot see their food. Sight contributes to the pleasure and satisfaction of eating.
↓
The manna fell in a manner inaccessible to blind people's sight. Their fathers, who included blind individuals, therefore did not fully "know" (experience) the manna's satisfaction.
↓
Abaye's practical ruling: A blind person should not eat alone but together with a sighted person — so that through their companion's sight, they benefit from greater satisfaction.
↓
Implication for Yom Kippur: Full satisfaction from eating depends partly on vision. This enriches our understanding of what constitutes genuine physical affliction — and what genuine eating means.
