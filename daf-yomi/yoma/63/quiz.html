QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Ḥisda, why is one exempt for slaughtering the Yom Kippur goats outside the Temple before the doors of the Sanctuary were opened?
A. The lots had not yet been drawn, so the goats had no designation at all
B. Lacking the opening of the doors is comparable to lacking an action, so the offering was not yet ready
C. The High Priest had not yet recited the morning prayers over the goats
D. Before sunrise, all Temple service is considered void
Correct: B — Rav Ḥisda teaches that opening the Temple doors is a prerequisite for the Yom Kippur service. Lacking this is legally comparable to lacking a required act, meaning the offering is not yet fit — and slaughtering it outside incurs no liability.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the key difference between Rav Dimi's and Ravin's versions of Rabbi Yoḥanan's ruling about a Paschal lamb slaughtered outside during the rest of the year?
A. Rav Dimi says liable only not for its own sake; Ravin says liable only for its own sake
B. Rav Dimi says exempt either way; Ravin says liable either way
C. Both agree on the ruling but attribute it to different Tannaim
D. Rav Dimi applies the rule only on Passover eve; Ravin applies it year-round
Correct: B — These are two contradictory Babylonian transmissions of the same ruling: Rav Dimi holds Rabbi Yoḥanan exempted in all cases; Ravin holds Rabbi Yoḥanan obligated in all cases. Each version requires its own independent resolution.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why is a zav who sacrifices his sin-offering outside the Temple before completing his purification exempt from liability?
A. A zav's offering is of lesser sanctity and the outside prohibition does not apply to it
B. The sin-offering of a zav can never be sacrificed inside the Temple under any circumstances
C. Since the offering is not yet fit to be brought inside the Temple, it is considered 'lacking time' and he is exempt
D. The Rabbis granted a special leniency for those who are ritually impure
Correct: C — A zav must count seven clean days before his offering can be accepted inside the Temple. Until then, the offering cannot fulfill its halakhic purpose inside — it is 'lacking time by its owner.' Since it is unfit inside, slaughtering it outside carries no liability.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What does Ḥanan the Egyptian say must happen if the scapegoat dies after the blood of the God-goat has already been sprinkled on Yom Kippur?
A. The Yom Kippur service is complete and no further action is needed
B. Only a new scapegoat must be found, without repeating the lottery
C. A new lottery must be conducted with a new pair of goats
D. The sprinkled blood is retroactively rejected and the entire atonement is void
Correct: C — Ḥanan the Egyptian holds that the two Yom Kippur goats are bound together as a pair. Even if the God-goat's blood has already been sprinkled, if the scapegoat dies, a complete new pair must be brought and a new lottery must be held.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yirmeya's principle, why might one who slaughters a Paschal lamb outside the Temple 'not for its own sake' still be exempt?
A. A Paschal lamb always retains its full sanctity regardless of the slaughterer's intent
B. Uprooting an offering's designation outside the Temple is not considered a valid uprooting, so it never becomes a peace-offering
C. The intent to change designation must be declared verbally before the act of slaughter
D. Outside slaughter can only change an offering's status with explicit rabbinic authorization
Correct: B — Rabbi Yirmeya rules that slaughtering outside the Temple cannot transform the Paschal lamb into a peace-offering — 'uprooting outside is not called uprooting.' Since the lamb remains a Paschal offering (unfit off-season), there is no valid offering being brought outside and no liability.
