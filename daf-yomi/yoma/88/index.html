⚖️ Is Immersion at Its Proper Time a Mitzva?

Rabbis
Immersion at its proper time is not a mitzva. Therefore, one who sees an emission on Yom Kippur may immerse even after ne'ila — and if he has no reed available, he may wait and immerse the following day.
Rabbi Yosei
Immersion at its proper time is a mitzva. If he has already prayed ne'ila before noticing the emission, there is no longer any purpose in immersing on Yom Kippur itself — so it is prohibited.
Gemara
The 'Rabbi Yosei' in the baraita is actually Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda — a different sage — who holds that immersion need only occur by the end of the day, not necessarily before ne'ila. This resolves the contradiction between the two baraitot.
Resolution The dispute turns on whether tevila bizmanah (טְבִילָה בִּזְמַנָּהּ) is itself a mitzva. The Rabbis say no — permitting delay to the next day. Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda says yes — requiring same-day immersion by nightfall.

🌙 Does Ne'ila Exempt the Evening Prayer?

Baraita
One who prays ne'ila must still recite the evening Amida and insert havdala into Atah Ḥonen — the blessing of wisdom. Ne'ila does not exempt one from Maariv.
Logical Proof
If ne'ila did exempt Maariv, there would be no place to recite havdala — since ne'ila is an abridged prayer without an Atah Ḥonen slot. The requirement to insert havdala there proves Maariv remains obligatory.
הַבְדָּלָה — Havdala Havdala is inserted into Atah Ḥonen because that blessing concerns wisdom and distinction (הַבְדָּלָה = separation) — just as God separates holy from mundane, Shabbat/Yom Tov from the weekday.

📊 Three Positions on Tevila Bizmanah (טְבִילָה בִּזְמַנָּהּ)

Rabbis

  • Tevila bizmanah is NOT a mitzva
  • May delay immersion until the day after Yom Kippur
  • If no reed available on Yom Kippur, wait until tomorrow
  • Immersion after ne'ila is still permitted on Yom Kippur
  • Lenient approach — timing has no intrinsic halakhic weight

Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda

  • Tevila bizmanah IS a mitzva
  • Immersion must occur by end of the same day
  • After ne'ila: no prayers remain → no purpose → prohibited
  • Sufficient if performed before nightfall on the same day
  • Stringent — immersion timing carries halakhic significance

Baraita (Reed Case)

  • God's name written in ink on one's flesh
  • May not wash or anoint — would erase holy name
  • Must hold a reed between the teeth to immerse
  • The rabbis disagree: must he wait, or immerse with reed?
  • Cited to probe whether timely immersion demands effort/sacrifice

⚠️ Spiritual Portent: Emission on Yom Kippur

כָּל הָרוֹאֶה קֶרִי בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים יִדְאַג כָּל הַשָּׁנָה כֻּלָּהּ — וְאִם שָׁהָה וְחָיָה, זְכֻיּוֹתָיו עָמְדוּ לוֹ
School of Rabbi Yishmael — בֵּית מִדְרָשׁוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל One who sees a seminal emission on Yom Kippur should be worried the entire year — it may be a divine sign that he and his fast were rejected above. However, if he survives the year, he can be assured that his accumulated merits and good deeds protected him. By contrast, a tanna taught before Rav Naḥman: such a person's sins are forgiven — a more comforting tradition. The Gemara juxtaposes these teachings without explicit resolution, leaving the spiritual tension intact.
