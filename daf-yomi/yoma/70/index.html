📖 Why Read 'And on the Tenth' by Heart?

Gemara
Why doesn't the High Priest simply furl the first Torah scroll to the Numbers portion and read from it?
Answer
Furling the scroll before the congregation is disrespectful — they would have to wait in silence while he searches.
Gemara
Then let a second Torah scroll be brought, already furled to that portion!
Rav Huna bar Yehuda
People might mistakenly think the second scroll was brought because a flaw was discovered in the first scroll.
Counter (Gemara)
But on Tevet-Shabbat-Hanukkah, three men read from three scrolls — are we not concerned about flaw suspicion there?
Resolution
Three men, three scrolls: no suspicion (each reader deserves his own scroll). One man, two scrolls: flaw is suspected.
Conclusion The Numbers portion is read by heart — a unique permitted exception — to avoid both disrespect to the congregation and false suspicion against the scroll.

⚔️ Sacrifice Order: Rabbi Eliezer vs Rabbi Akiva

Rabbi Eliezer

  • Follows written Torah order: Leviticus before Numbers
  • First performs all Leviticus 16 atonement services
  • Then sacrifices additional Musaf offerings of Numbers 29
  • Seven lambs and bull of burnt-offering offered after Leviticus services
  • Source: order of books — Leviticus precedes Numbers
  • Clarified by: school of Shmuel (resolves ambiguities in Mishna)

Rabbi Akiva

  • Groups offerings by time of day, not by book order
  • Morning: bull of Yom Kippur burnt-offering + seven lambs (with daily offering)
  • Then: Yom Kippur atonement service from Leviticus
  • Then: outside goat sin-offering from Numbers
  • Verse: 'Besides the morning burnt-offering you shall sacrifice these' (Num 28:23)
  • Clarified by: Tosefta (which adds detail absent from Mishna)

🐑 When Are the Seven Lambs Sacrificed?

Seven Lambs on Yom Kippur
Tannaim dispute the timing of their sacrifice
↓
Rabbi Akiva (Tosefta)
All 7 lambs with morning daily offering — before any Yom Kippur service begins
Rabbi Yehuda (b. name of Akiva)
1 lamb with morning offering → perform Yom Kippur service → 6 lambs with afternoon offering
Rabbi Elazar b. R. Shimon
6 lambs in morning (lest negligent later) → perform service → 1 lamb in afternoon
↓
All agree: Only one ram for the people (not two)
Per Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi — 'one' (אחד) = singular, and must be from the choicest stock
