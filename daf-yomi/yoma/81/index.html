⚖️ Do Food and Drink Combine on Yom Kippur?

Rabbi Yehoshua
Food and drink do NOT combine — each is measured independently toward the liability threshold
Rabbis
Items of different measures combine for ritual impurity — perhaps food and drink similarly combine for Yom Kippur liability
Rav Naḥman
Even the Rabbis only spoke of combining for ritual impurity (טומאה), not Yom Kippur — they would also agree food and drink do not combine for fasting
Reish Lakish
No — the mishna follows Rabbi Yehoshua; the Rabbis genuinely dispute this and hold that food and drink DO combine on Yom Kippur
Mishna's Ruling The mishna follows Rabbi Yehoshua: food and drink are counted separately; half a measure of each does not combine to form liability

⚠️ Why Is There No Explicit Warning Against Eating on Yom Kippur?

וְעִנִּיתֶם אֶת נַפְשֹׁתֵיכֶם — 'You shall afflict your souls' (Leviticus 23:32)
Reish Lakish's Question The Torah never states 'Do not eat on Yom Kippur.' Instead it uses positive language: 'You shall afflict your souls.' Reish Lakish asks: why was it impossible for the Torah to write an explicit negative prohibition? The Gemara explores every proposed formulation and finds each one flawed — either creating too many overlapping prohibitions or being misread as a positive commandment rather than a prohibition.

💬 Proposed Solutions to the Warning Problem

Reish Lakish
The Torah cannot write 'Do not eat' — this would be imprecise, since affliction encompasses more than eating
Rav Hoshaya
Objection! Let the Torah write: 'Guard yourself lest you not be afflicted' — that would be explicit and precise
Gemara
Rejected: 'Guard yourself,' 'lest,' and 'not' each independently count as a prohibition — this would create three redundant negative commandments
Rav Beivai bar Abaye
Then let the Torah write: 'Guard yourself in the mitzva of affliction' — avoiding the triple-prohibition problem entirely
Gemara
Rejected: 'Guard yourself' attached to a positive mitzva reads as a positive commandment — the prohibition character would be lost
Conclusion No workable negative formulation exists; therefore the Torah used the positive 'afflict yourselves,' and the prohibition is derived indirectly

↓

🕍 Tosefet Yom Kippur — Adding Time to the Fast

Source Verse: 'On the ninth day of the month at evening, from evening until evening, you shall rest on your Shabbat' (Leviticus 23:32)
↓
Problem: Yom Kippur is the 10th of Tishrei — why does the verse say 'ninth day'?
↓
Ḥiyya bar Rav's Teaching: The verse mentions the ninth to teach that one begins fasting while it is still the ninth — adding time before Yom Kippur begins at nightfall
↓
Adding at the End: 'From evening until evening' teaches that one also extends beyond the technical end of Yom Kippur — the fast continues past nightfall on the 10th
↓
Principle of Tosefet: One must add from the mundane (חול) onto the holy (קודש) — this obligation applies both at the start and the conclusion of Yom Kippur
