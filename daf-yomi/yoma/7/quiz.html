QUESTION 1 OF 5
What is the practical difference between Rav Sheshet's position (hutrah) and Rav Naḥman's position (dechuya) regarding communal impurity?
A. Rav Sheshet permits impurity only on Yom Kippur; Rav Naḥman permits it year-round
B. Rav Sheshet says impurity is fully permitted so purity need not be sought; Rav Naḥman says a pure replacement should be used if available
C. Rav Sheshet requires the tzitz to atone; Rav Naḥman says no atonement is needed
D. There is no practical difference — the dispute is purely theoretical
Correct: B — The key practical difference: under hutrah (Rav Sheshet) the prohibition simply does not apply, so priests need not seek purity. Under dechuya (Rav Naḥman) impurity is tolerated but purity is always preferable — a pure substitute should replace an impure offering when possible.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does Rav Naḥman deflect the Tosefta challenge about communal meal-offerings being replaced when impure?
A. He reinterprets 'impure' in the Tosefta to mean merely questionably impure
B. He concedes and limits his view to Yom Kippur offerings only
C. He argues the Tosefta's 'bulls' refer to the atonement bull for communal idolatry — an individual-style offering, not a standard communal sacrifice
D. He argues the Tosefta contradicts itself and is therefore unreliable
Correct: C — Rav Naḥman explains that the Tosefta's case involves the bull brought when the entire community sins with idolatry unwittingly (par he'elem davar shel tzibur) — though technically communal in scope, it functions as an individual atonement offering and therefore prefers purity, unlike standard communal festival sacrifices.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Rabbi Shimon challenges Rabbi Yehuda by citing the Yom Kippur Avodah. What is Rabbi Yehuda's response?
A. He admits that the tzitz works even when not worn, conceding the point
B. He argues that Yom Kippur is unique because impurity is hutrah for the public, making the tzitz unnecessary — not proof that physical presence is irrelevant
C. He argues the High Priest does in fact wear the tzitz during part of the Yom Kippur service
D. He argues Rabbi Shimon's proof is based on a minority opinion in the Tosefta
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda responds that Yom Kippur is a special case where communal impurity is fully overridden (hutrah), making the tzitz's atoning power simply unnecessary that day. The absence of the tzitz on Yom Kippur therefore cannot prove that the tzitz works without physical presence under normal circumstances.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Abaye, what is the exact scenario where Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon disagree about the tzitz?
A. When the tzitz is physically broken and shattered
B. When the tzitz is worn by someone other than the High Priest
C. When the tzitz has slipped from the forehead but remains fully intact
D. When the High Priest is performing a service outside the Temple
Correct: C — Abaye clarifies that both sages agree a broken tzitz cannot effect acceptance. Their dispute is specifically about an intact tzitz that has slipped out of position on the High Priest's forehead — Rabbi Yehuda says it no longer works; Rabbi Shimon says it still does as long as the priest is mindful.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How do Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon each interpret the word תָּמִיד (tamid / 'always') in the verse about the tzitz?
A. Rabbi Yehuda: it must be worn every day of the year; Rabbi Shimon: it is optional on most days
B. Rabbi Yehuda: the priest must always be conscious of wearing it; Rabbi Shimon: the tzitz always effects acceptance when the priest is mindful, regardless of position
C. Both agree: the tzitz must physically be present at all times during Temple service
D. Rabbi Yehuda: 'always' modifies the atonement, not the placement; Rabbi Shimon: 'always' is a leniency for impurity only
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda uses 'tamid' to derive that the High Priest must always maintain mental awareness that he is wearing the tzitz — a requirement of mindfulness during service. Rabbi Shimon uses the same word to teach that the tzitz always possesses its atoning power so long as the priest is aware of it, even when not on the forehead.
