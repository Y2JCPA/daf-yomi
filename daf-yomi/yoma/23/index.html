⚔️ Must a Torah Scholar React to Insults?

Tanna Kamma
One who does NOT avenge himself or bear a grudge like a snake when insulted is NOT considered a Torah scholar — upholding Torah's honor demands a strong reaction to disgrace.
Baraita
Those who are insulted but do not insult back, who are shamed but do not respond — about them Scripture says: 'Those who love Him are as the sun rising in its might' (Judges 5:31).
Resolution
A scholar may keep resentment in his heart (so the offender knows the insult registered), but must NOT act on it or verbally remind the offender — a middle path between passivity and retaliation.
📌 Key Distinction Vengeance = acting on the hurt. Bearing a grudge = reminding someone of past refusal while currently helping them. Both prohibited (Lev. 19:18) — but inner feelings alone are permitted.

🖐️ The Lottery Finger-Counting Procedure

Priests gather at the courtyard for the morning lottery to assign the ash-removal service
↓
Each priest extends one or two fingers — the middle finger and thumb are forbidden to prevent deceptive counting
↓
Rav Ḥisda's ruling: A healthy priest extends one finger; a sick priest who cannot separate his fingers extends two — but both count as one in the tally
↓
The appointed official counts around the circle to a pre-announced number — the priest on whom the count lands wins the service
↓
Ben Beivai was in charge of the pakia — a whip-like implement (Rav Pappa: like a whip of myrtle) used to rouse priests for their duties

🩸 Two Temple Tragedies Side by Side

The Broken Leg (Mishna)

  • Two priests tied racing up the altar ramp
  • One shoved the other — his leg was broken
  • No bloodshed; injury only
  • Lottery instituted for the full ramp ascent
  • Running still allowed within the final four cubits

The Stabbing (Tosefta)

  • Two priests still raced within the final four cubits
  • One stabbed the other with a knife — the priest died
  • Father: 'My son is still convulsing — knife is ritually pure!'
  • Rabbi Tzadok delivered lamentation on the Entrance Hall steps
  • Lottery extended to the final four cubits — running abolished

שִׁמְעוּ אַחֵינוּ בֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל — Hear us, our brothers, House of Israel (תוספתא יומא)
🕍 Rabbi Tzadok's Lamentation at the Temple Standing on the steps of the Ulam (Entrance Hall), Rabbi Tzadok cried out: 'Hear me, brothers of the house of Israel! Behold what the verse says regarding a slain body found in the field — the elders and priests must wash their hands and declare: Our hands did not shed this blood!' Jerusalem, however, does not bring the eglah arufah — because Jerusalem was not apportioned to any single tribe. The dying priest's father rushed in and declared: 'May my son's death be an atonement for you! He is still convulsing — the knife is not yet defiled!' This teaches: the ritual purity of Temple vessels mattered so greatly that it overrode even grief for a dying son.
