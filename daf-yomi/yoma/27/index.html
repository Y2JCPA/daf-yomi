🪵 Is the Wood Verse Needed for Itself or as an Exclusion?

Abaye
Since Lev. 1:5 already establishes that blood collection onward is priestly, why does the Torah then write 'sons of Aaron shall put fire on the altar'? That verse should now be superfluous — use it to derive that flaying may be done by non-priests!
Gemara
The wood verse is still needed for its own sake. One might think: since placing wood does not itself atone (atonement comes from the blood), perhaps a non-priest may arrange the logs. The verse is required to teach that this too is priestly.
Abaye
Then we cannot derive the permissibility of flaying from the wood verse. We need a different source entirely for that ruling.
Gemara
Therefore we learn from Lev. 1:9 — 'the priest shall burn all of it' — which is superfluous since burning was already taught. This extra verse comes to exclude flaying and cutting from the priestly requirement.
Hermeneutic Principle A verse needed for its own law cannot simultaneously serve as an exclusion for other services. Each derivation must have an independent textual source.

⚖️ Priestly vs. Non-Priestly Temple Services

Priests Only — כֹּהֲנִים

  • 🩸 Collecting and sprinkling blood (Lev. 1:5)
  • 🪵 Arranging the two logs on the altar
  • 🦴 Laying out the animal's limbs on the altar
  • 🏃 Carrying limbs to the altar ramp (Lev. 1:13)
  • 🌅 Daily woodpile arrangement — requires morning lottery

Non-Priests Permitted — זָרִים

  • ✂️ Flaying (skinning) the animal
  • 🗡️ Cutting the animal into pieces
  • 🌙 Burning limbs and fats (continues overnight)
  • 📋 Preparatory acts prior to blood collection
  • ⚠️ Note: slaughter requires a priest (excluded elsewhere)

🌙 Rabbi Zeira Objects to Rabbi Yoḥanan (Amud B)

R. Yoḥanan
A non-priest who arranges the woodpile on the altar incurs the death penalty by Heaven, and furthermore the woodpile he arranged is invalid.
R. Zeira
Strong objection! Name me one service that is valid when performed at night and yet is prohibited for a non-priest. Wood arrangement is valid even at night — so it cannot be a genuine priestly service carrying a death penalty!
Gemara
What about burning the limbs and fats? That continues at night and yet is prohibited for non-priests!
R. Zeira
Burning of limbs is not a nighttime service — it merely continues into the night. Ash removal is also not truly nocturnal; it merely begins from dawn. My principle stands.
Rava
Then let us revise: Rabbi Yoḥanan meant specifically the arrangement of the two logs (the main woodpile), which is a distinct priestly act — and a non-priest performing it incurs death.
Revised Ruling Rabbi Yoḥanan's death penalty applies specifically to arranging the two logs (מַעֲרָכָה גְּדוֹלָה), not general wood placement. This specific arrangement is a priestly daytime service.

🎲 Rava's Criterion: Which Services Require the Morning Lottery?

The Question: Which Temple services require assignment by the priestly lottery (גּוֹרָל)?
↓
Rava's Rule: A service requires a lottery if it is (1) a daytime service AND (2) a non-priest performing it incurs the death penalty
↓
Slaughter: Daytime ✓ · Priest-only ✓ → Requires lottery
↓
Burning limbs & fats: Nighttime service ✗ → Does NOT require lottery, even though priest-only
↓
Two-log arrangement: Daytime ✓ · Priest-only ✓ → Requires lottery — included in the morning draw
↓
Objection (Mar Zutra): The mishna's lottery list begins with 'slaughter' — implying wood arrangement precedes the lottery altogether!
↓
Resolution: The mishna emphasizes slaughter because it is irreparable if done at night. Wood arrangement done at night can be redone; slaughter cannot be undone.
