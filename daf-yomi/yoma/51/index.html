🚪 One Curtain or Two Between the Sanctuaries?

Rabbi Yosei
There was only one curtain in the Temple, as in the Tabernacle. The verse references one parochet — there is no basis for adding a second.
Rabbis
There were two curtains, one cubit apart, in the Second Temple. The Tabernacle had one, but the Temple required two due to its permanent stone wall construction.
Gemara
Rabbi Yosei is saying well to the Rabbis. The Rabbis respond: in the Tabernacle there was one curtain; in the Temple, two curtains compensated for the uncertainty of which side the wall-thickness belonged to.
The Mishna (51b) The High Priest walked westward until he reached the area between the two curtains — a space of one cubit. Rabbi Yosei disputes: there was only one curtain.

🚶 Which Side Did the High Priest Walk?

Rabbi Yehuda — South Side

  • Walked on the south side of the Sanctuary
  • Passed between the inner altar and the candelabrum (menorah)
  • Consistent with two curtains whose entrance faced south
  • The northern entrance was blocked by the curtain arrangement

Rabbi Meir — North Side

  • Walked on the north side of the Sanctuary
  • Passed between the table (shewbread) and the candelabrum
  • Consistent with Rabbi Yosei: one curtain, entrance on north
  • The table was arranged so as not to block his path — debate whether N-S or E-W

🐑 Does the Second Pesach Override Ritual Impurity?

Is the Second Pesach (14 Iyar) classified as an individual or a communal offering?
↓
Individual Offering
Does NOT override Shabbat or ritual impurity — cannot serve as proof for communal override
Communal Offering
DOES override Shabbat and ritual impurity — valid basis for Rav Sheshet's baraita proof
↓
Rav Huna b. Rav Yehoshua: this tanna holds the Second Pesach does override impurity — treated as communal
↓
Rabbi Yehuda's support: "According to all the statute of the Paschal offering they shall keep it" (Num. 9:12) — even brought in a state of ritual impurity

תמורת כהן גדול — בשור או באיל?
🐂 Rav Sheshet: The Offering Is the Ram, Not the Bull The baraita under discussion concerns temurah (substitution) for the High Priest's Yom Kippur animal. Rav Sheshet argues the offering is not his bull but his personal ram. The bull raises complex questions — the other priests gain atonement through it, possibly creating joint ownership. A jointly-owned animal has different rules for substitution. The ram, by contrast, is solely the High Priest's own, making substitution straightforward. This interpretation avoids the difficult question of whether other priests are full partners in the bull.
