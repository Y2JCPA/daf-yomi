Sotah 23 — Halakhic Distinctions: Kohen vs. Bat Kohen, Man vs. Woman

CORE THEME The Mishna catalogues halakhic differences between a kohen and a bat kohen, and between a man and a woman. The Gemara then traces each distinction to its biblical source and resolves the complex problem of what happens to the meal-offering of a sota married to a kohen.

Kohen vs. Bat Kohen: Four Key Differences

Bat Kohen 👩

  • Her mincha is eaten by kohanim
  • Can become disqualified from teruma through forbidden relations
  • May become tamei from a corpse
  • May not eat from kodshei kodashim

Kohen 👨‍🦳

  • His mincha is not eaten — entirely burned
  • Does not become desacralized through forbidden relations
  • May not become tamei from a corpse (except 7 relatives)
  • May eat from kodshei kodashim
SOURCE Lev. 6:11 — "Every male among the children of Aaron may eat of it" — males, not daughters.

Her Share
As an Israelite-origin woman — her portion should be eaten
His Share
As a kohen — his portion must be burned entirely
↓
Solution: The handful (kometz) is sacrificed by itself
↓
The remainder — neither eaten nor burned as offering
RESOLUTION The remainder is scattered on the place of the ashes (beit hadeshen) — per R' Elazar b'R' Shimon.

How to Dispose of the Remainder?

Problem
If we burn the remainder on the altar, we violate "lo taktiru" — the prohibition against burning non-designated parts as an offering!
Yehuda b. R' Shimon b. Pazi
Burn it as firewood, not as an offering — this is permitted per R' Eliezer.
R' Eliezer
"For a pleasing aroma you may not burn them, but you may burn them as firewood on the altar."
Chakhamim
We disagree with R' Eliezer! You cannot burn the remainder on the altar even as firewood.
R' Elazar b'R' Shimon
The remainder is scattered on the beit hadeshen (ash heap) — not eaten, not burned on the altar.
Gemara
Even the Rabbis agree with R' Elazar b'R' Shimon in this case, since the handful was removed from the offering.
DISTINCTION The Rabbis only disagree regarding a kohen's sin-offering mincha (which should be burned entirely without kemitza). Here, where kemitza was performed, all agree the remainder goes to the ash heap.
