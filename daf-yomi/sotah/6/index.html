Sotah 6 — Levirate Marriage, Teruma Prohibitions & the Meal-Offering

DAF OVERVIEW The daf continues the discussion of why a sota cannot enter levirate marriage (yibum), with Rav Yosef, Rava, and Abaye debating through verse interpretation and kal va'chomer reasoning. The Mishna then lists women who may not eat teruma due to suspected adultery. The Gemara explores whether the bitter water evaluates a woman when witnesses exist overseas, and analyzes the status of the meal-offering when witnesses emerge at different stages of the Temple procedure.
3 / 9

Why Can't a Sota Do Yibum? — 'Another Man'

Rav Yosef
The verse calls a man who marries a divorcée suspected of adultery 'another man' (Deut. 24:2) — implying he is not the peer of the first husband. The first removed an evil woman; the second brought one in.
Abaye
If so, even if she married a second husband who died childless without any adultery, she shouldn't do yibum with his brother — since the verse calls him 'another man'!
Rav Yosef
With the second husband, she remained with a good name — she did not engage in illicit behavior during that marriage. Therefore yibum with his brother is permitted.
KEY VERSE Deuteronomy 24:2 — 'And she departs out of his house and goes and becomes another man's wife' — this 'other' implies inferiority, not just sequence.

Rava's Kal Va'chomer — Sota & Yibum

Rava's Reasoning:
A sota becomes forbidden to her own husband (who was permitted)
↓
Then certainly she remains forbidden to the husband's brother (who was already forbidden)!
↓
Abaye challenges: A High Priest who betrothed a widow and died — shouldn't yibum be forbidden to his common-priest brother by the same logic?
Refuted
She was always forbidden to the High Priest — she didn't become forbidden through marriage
Better Challenge
A priest's wife who was raped — can she do yibum with a ḥalal brother?
↓
Answer: A raped woman is permitted to an Israelite, and a ḥalal has Israelite status for marriage — so the kal va'chomer doesn't apply
PRINCIPLE The kal va'chomer only works when the woman becomes newly forbidden to a husband who was genuinely permitted — and remains forbidden to all categories of men.

Mishna: Women Prohibited from Teruma

SituationWhy Prohibited from Teruma
She says: 'I am defiled to you'Self-admission of adultery
Witnesses testify she is defiledTestimony establishes adultery
She says: 'I will not drink'Refusal implies guilt
Husband does not want to make her drinkHe chooses not to test her; suspicion remains
Husband had relations with her on the way to the TempleWater becomes ineffective due to his prohibited act
PRACTICAL IMPACT In each case, the suspected adultery is never resolved — so a wife of a kohen loses her right to eat teruma.

Overseas Witnesses & the Bitter Water

סוטה שיש לה עדים במדינת הים — אין מי המרים בודקין אותה
RAV SHESHET'S TEACHING Rav Amram quotes Rav Sheshet: If there are witnesses overseas who can testify that the sota committed adultery, the bitter water does not evaluate her. The water's miraculous test works only when there is no possibility of proving guilt through human testimony. This has major implications for the status of her meal-offering.

When Did the Witnesses Come? — Meal-Offering Status

Before Sanctification

  • Witnesses testify before offering placed in service vessel
  • Offering was consecrated in error
  • Should be transferred to non-sacred status (redeemed)
  • Like all offerings sanctified by mistake

After Sanctification

  • Witnesses testify after offering placed in service vessel
  • At time of sanctification — was it valid?
  • If water evaluates despite witnesses: properly sanctified → burned
  • If water doesn't evaluate: sanctified in error → should be redeemed
RAV'S PROOF Since the Mishna says the meal-offering is burned (not redeemed), Rav argues the water does evaluate even when witnesses exist — otherwise the offering was never properly sanctified.
