Does the Bitter Water Evaluate the Paramour Too?

Gemara
The mishna says the water evaluates him — who is 'him'? The husband or the paramour (bo'el)?
Gemara
It can't mean the husband — if he sinned similarly, the water doesn't even evaluate her (Numbers 5:31)!
R' Akiva
The superfluous vav in 'and the water shall enter' teaches that the paramour is also punished by the water.
R' Yehuda HaNasi
I derive the paramour's punishment from 'belly to swell and thigh to fall away' — referring to the paramour's body.
Key Principle The bitter waters don't just test the woman — they also reach the paramour, wherever he may be.

Who Does 'Him' Refer To in the Mishna?

Gemara
The mishna says the water evaluates 'him' — could this mean the husband?
Baraita
'The man shall be clear of iniquity' (Num. 5:31) — if the husband sinned, the water doesn't evaluate her at all!
Gemara
So 'him' must refer to the paramour (bo'el). But why isn't the paramour named explicitly?
Gemara
Since the first clause uses 'her' (direct object), it parallels with 'him' — no need to specify. The latter clause names both explicitly.
Resolution The entire mishna refers to the paramour. The first clause uses shorthand; the latter clause names him explicitly.

R' Akiva vs R' Yehuda HaNasi: Source of the Paramour's Punishment

R' Akiva

  • Derives from the superfluous vav prefix
  • 'Ve-nitma'a' — the 'and' teaches an additional prohibition
  • 'U-va'u' — the 'and' teaches the paramour is evaluated too
  • Each vav doubles the three verses → 6 derivations

R' Yehuda HaNasi (Rebbi)

  • Derives from repetition of full phrases in two verses
  • 'Nitma'a' in Num. 5:14 and again in 5:29
  • Does not expound the prefix vav
  • Only 3 derivations — all for the woman herself
Proof Since Rebbi uses repetition of full phrases, by contrast R' Akiva must derive his halakhot from the vav.

R' Akiva's Six Derivations from Three Verses

VerseFor Her (Woman)For Him (Paramour)
Num. 5:24 — 'And the water shall enter'Command (צִוּוּי) — God empowers water to punish herVav → Command for him — water also punishes paramour
Num. 5:27 — 'And the water shall enter and become bitter'Execution (קִיּוּם) — punishment takes effectVav → Execution for him — his punishment also takes effect
Num. 5:22 — 'And the water that causes the curse'Knowledge (הוֹדָעָה) — priest informs her of consequencesVav → Knowledge for him — awareness of his punishment
Rebbi's View R' Yehuda HaNasi reads only 3 derivations (command, execution, knowledge) — all for the woman. He derives the paramour's punishment from 'belly to swell and thigh to fall away.'

Safek Tumah: Uncertain Impurity Derived from Sotah

The sotah case establishes the model for uncertain ritual impurity (ספק טומאה)
↓
What conditions from sotah apply to all uncertain impurity?
Condition 1
Private Domain
Just as sotah secluded herself privately, uncertain impurity applies only in a reshut ha-yachid
Condition 2
Awareness (Da'at)
Just as sotah can be asked about what happened, impurity applies only to an entity with yesh bo da'at lisha'el
Principle Safek tumah in a reshut ha-rabbim (public domain) is deemed pure — unlike a private domain where it is impure.
