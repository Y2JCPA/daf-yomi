When Does the Handful Permit the Remainder?

R' Chanina
The remainder is permitted when the fire takes hold of the handful — i.e., when it ignites.
R' Yochanan
It is permitted only when the fire consumes most of the handful.
Gemara
Each amora understands the baraita's phrase "after the handful is sacrificed" in accordance with his own opinion.
Practical Difference Priests must wait to eat the remainder — the question is how long. R' Chanina permits earlier (ignition), R' Yochanan requires more complete burning.

Three Unique Meal-Offerings Compared

Sota Offering

  • ❌ No oil or frankincense
  • 🌾 Brought from barley
  • 🫘 Brought as unsifted flour (kemach)
  • Different in both respects

Sinner's Offering

  • ❌ No oil or frankincense
  • 🌾 Brought from wheat
  • ✨ Brought as fine flour (solet)
  • Different in one respect

Omer Offering

  • ✅ Requires oil and frankincense
  • 🌾 Brought from barley
  • 🔨 Brought as groats (grisot)
  • Different in one respect
Key Insight The sota offering is uniquely degraded in both categories — neither wheat/fine flour nor oil/frankincense — reflecting the severity of the suspected act.

R' Shimon: Why the Sinner's Offering Lacks Quality

A sinner brings a meal-offering (Lev. 5:11)
↓
Shouldn't it require oil & frankincense so the sinner doesn't gain financially?
↓
No — so that his offering not be of superior quality
↓
Same logic for sin-offerings (ḥelev, etc.) — no fine flour or libations
↓
Leper's Offering
Does require libations — leprosy itself already atoned; offering only permits eating sacrificial food
Nazirite's Sin-Offering
Does not require libations — per R' Elazar HaKappar, the nazirite is also a sinner for denying himself wine
R' Shimon's Principle Two competing concerns: (1) a sinner shouldn't profit, (2) a sinner's offering shouldn't be beautified. The Torah balances both.

Why Is the Sota's Offering Animal Food?

R' Meir
She fed her paramour delicacies from around the world — therefore her offering is animal food (barley).
R' Gamliel
That works for a rich sota — but what about a poor one who can't afford delicacies?
R' Gamliel
Rather: her actions were the actions of an animal — so her offering is animal food.
R' Gamliel
Scribes, permit me — I will explain this as a ḥomer (decorative wreath / allegory).
Note Rabban Gamliel's explanation applies universally to all sotot regardless of wealth, making it the stronger reasoning.

The Earthenware Vessel: Must It Be New?

PositionReasoningImplications
R' Yishmael: Must be newVerbal analogy (gezeira shava) — 'vessel' (sota) = 'vessel' (leper). Just as running water is unused, the vessel must be unused.Basin water must also be spring water (like the leper's running water)
Rabbis: Need not be newNo gezeira shava applies; the verse simply says 'earthen vessel'Basin water may be any water, not specifically spring water
Rava: Even if not new…The vessel's exterior must not be blackened — it must be clear like the water itselfIf blackened and re-fired to white — unresolved dilemma (teiku-like)
Source Numbers 5:17 — "And the priest shall take holy water in an earthen vessel" — the Torah's language drives all three positions.
