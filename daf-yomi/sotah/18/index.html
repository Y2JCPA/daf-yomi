The Two Oaths: When Are They Administered?

Rav
Two oaths are mentioned in the Torah — one is administered before the scroll is erased, and one after.
Rava
Objects: Both oaths appear in the Torah before any mention of erasure! Rather, both are before drinking — one with a curse and one without.
Rav Amram (quoting Rav)
Oath with curse: 'I adjure you that you were not defiled — and if you were, all these curses shall come upon you.'
Rava
That's insufficient — the curse and oath are separate statements! Say: 'I adjure you that if you were defiled, all curses come upon you.'
Rav Ashi
Still insufficient — there's a curse but no oath she wasn't defiled! Must say: 'I adjure you that you were NOT defiled, and that IF you were, all curses come upon you.'
RESOLUTION Rav Ashi's formula integrates both oath and curse into a single statement — the oath includes the denial AND the conditional curse.

Mishna: What Does 'Amen, Amen' Cover?

Chachamim (Tanna Kamma)

  • Amen on the curse + Amen on the oath
  • Amen: not with this man + Amen: not with another man
  • Amen: not while betrothed
  • Amen: not while married
  • Amen: not while awaiting yavam
  • Amen: not while married to yavam
  • Amen: I was not defiled; if I was — may curses come

Rabbi Meir

  • Amen: I was not defiled in the past
  • Amen: I will not become defiled in the future
  • Baraita clarifies: NOT that the water judges her future now
  • Rather: if she becomes defiled later, the water she drank NOW will destabilize her THEN
KEY PRINCIPLE The double 'Amen' dramatically expands the scope of the sota's acceptance — covering multiple relationships, time periods, and both oath and curse.

Widow Awaiting Yavam (שומרת יבם) Who Committed Adultery

Rav Hamnuna
A shomeret yavam who committed adultery is forbidden to her yavam. Proof: the Mishna includes her oath period as 'awaiting yavam' — she must become forbidden for this to be stipulated!
Eretz Yisrael (Sages of the West)
The halakha is NOT in accordance with Rav Hamnuna. She does not become forbidden to the yavam.
Gemara
Then whose opinion does the Mishna follow? It must be Rabbi Akiva, who holds that kiddushin doesn't take effect even for ordinary lavim (prohibitions).
Gemara (explains)
R' Akiva equates ordinary Torah prohibitions with karet prohibitions for personal status — so a shomeret yavam who commits adultery is treated like a married woman who does.
DISPUTE The Mishna's inclusion of shomeret yavam in the amen formula follows Rabbi Akiva's strict position — not the majority view from Eretz Yisrael.
