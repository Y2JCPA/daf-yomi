Sacred Tongue vs. Any Language

Mishnah
Some recitations may be said in any language (Shema, Amidah, Grace after Meals, oaths), while others must be said only in the sacred tongue (first fruits, ḥalitza, blessings and curses).
Gemara
The Gemara derives each ruling from specific verses, then challenges: Can one really pray in any language? R' Yoḥanan says angels don't understand Aramaic!
Resolution
R' Yoḥanan's concern applies to individual prayer (which needs angelic support). Communal prayer goes directly to God — any language works.
Key Principle The language of sacred speech depends on whether the ritual demands precise formulation (Hebrew) or heartfelt understanding (any language).

Any Language vs. Sacred Tongue Only

Any Language

  • Shema — derived from 'Hear' (שמע): in any language you hear/understand
  • Amidah — prayer is a request for mercy, in whatever way one desires
  • Grace after Meals — 'bless': in any language you bless
  • Oath of testimony — 'heard the voice': any language one hears
  • Oath on a deposit — derived via gezeirah shavah from oath of testimony

Sacred Tongue Only

  • First fruits (Bikkurim) — 'and you shall speak and say' linked to Levites
  • Ḥalitza — 'and she shall speak and say' / 'kakha'
  • Blessings and curses — Levites: 'with a loud voice' linked to Moses at Sinai
  • Priestly Benediction — 'So (ko) shall you bless'
  • Sotah — specific recitation formula required
Pattern Rituals requiring precise Biblical formulas must be in Hebrew; rituals focused on personal understanding or petition may use any language.

Can One Pray in Aramaic?

Rav Yehuda
A person should never request his needs in Aramaic.
R' Yoḥanan
Because the ministering angels do not understand Aramaic (אין מלאכי השרת מכירין בלשון ארמי), and they won't attend to that person's prayer.
Gemara
But the Mishnah says the Amidah may be in any language! How can these be reconciled?
Resolution
R' Yoḥanan refers to individual prayer (needs angels). The Mishnah refers to communal prayer (goes directly to Hashem).
Practical Halakhah An individual praying alone should avoid Aramaic; a congregation praying together may use any language they understand.

Do Angels Really Not Know Aramaic?

Challenge: A Bat Kol (Divine Voice) spoke in Aramaic from the Holy of Holies!
↓
Case 1: Yoḥanan the High Priest heard: 'The youth at Antokhya have been victorious'
Case 2: Shimon HaTzaddik heard: 'Gaskalgas (Caligula) has been killed'
↓
Answer 1
The Bat Kol is different — its purpose is to communicate, so it uses any language
Answer 2
It was the angel Gabriel, who knows all 70 languages (as he taught them to Yosef)
Note Gabriel is unique among angels in knowing all 70 languages — the other ministering angels do not know Aramaic.

Gezeirah Shavah: How 'Sacred Tongue' Is Derived

RitualKey PhraseLinked ToConclusion
First Fruits (Bikkurim)"And you shall speak and say" (Deut. 26:5)Levites: "shall speak and say" (Deut. 27:14)Hebrew only
Blessings & Curses (Levites)"With a loud voice" (Deut. 27:14)Moses: "voice" at Sinai (Ex. 19:19)Hebrew only
Ḥalitza (Rabbis' view)"And she shall speak and say" (Deut. 25:9)Levites via 'speak and say'Hebrew only
Ḥalitza (R' Yehuda)"Kakha" — 'so' (Deut. 25:9)Implies: precisely as writtenHebrew only
Priestly Blessing (R' Yehuda)"Ko" — 'so' (Num. 6:23)Implies: precisely this formulationHebrew only
R' Yehuda's Rule Wherever the Torah uses 'ko,' 'kakha,' or the language of 'speaking and saying,' it refers exclusively to the sacred tongue.

Where Are Mount Gerizim and Mount Ebal?

R' Yehuda
"Beyond the Jordan" means farther west. "Oaks of Moreh" = Shechem (derived from Genesis 12:6). Mount Gerizim and Mount Ebal are near Shechem.
R' Elazar b'R' Yosei
"Beyond the Jordan" means near the Jordan. Deut. 27:4 says 'when you cross the Jordan, set up these stones on Mount Ebal' — implying they are close to the crossing point, not at distant Shechem.
R' Elazar b'R' Yosei
Further proof: Shechem is in Hivite land, not Canaanite; it's among mountains, not the Arabah (plain); and you can't see Gilgal from Shechem!
R' Eliezer b. Yaakov
The verse doesn't establish their location at all — it shows the people the route into Canaan: go by roads, through settled areas, across plains.
Samaritan Dispute R' Elazar told the Samaritans: 'We both agree oaks of Moreh = Shechem, but we derive it by gezeirah shavah. You, who don't use gezeirah shavah — how do you know? You must have forged your Torah!'
