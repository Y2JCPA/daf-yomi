How Long Is the 'Measure of Seclusion'?

Baraita
The measure of seclusion = time for defilement = time for intercourse = time for initial stage of intercourse
R' Yishmael
Time to circle a palm tree on foot
R' Eliezer
Time to mix a cup of wine (quarter-log)
R' Yehoshua
Time to drink that cup of wine
Ben Azzai
Time to roast an egg
R' Akiva
Time to swallow that egg
Peleimu
Time for a woman to reach into a basket and take a loaf of bread
Key Principle Nine Sages offer practical examples of the minimal time for seclusion — each a brief, everyday action

Why Are Three Definitions Needed?

If only "time for defilement"
↓
Might include time for appeasement (persuasion)
↓
So the baraita adds: "time for intercourse" — excluding appeasement
↓
If only "time for intercourse"
↓
Might mean completion of the act
↓
So the baraita adds: "initial stage of intercourse" (ha'ara'ah)
Conclusion All three terms are necessary: each excludes a potential misunderstanding about the duration

Circling vs. Returning of the Palm Tree

Gemara
Baraita 1: R' Yishmael says circling a palm tree. R' Eliezer disagrees.
Baraita 2: R' Eliezer himself says returning of a palm tree. Contradiction!
Abaye
Circling = walking around the trunk on foot (longer).
Returning = a palm branch blown by wind snapping back (shorter). Two different measures!
Rav Ashi
This 'returning' — is it one snap back? Or does it include the time until the branch fully settles?
Gemara
Teiku — the question stands unresolved
Resolution Abaye resolves the contradiction: R' Eliezer's two statements refer to different physical phenomena — walking around a tree vs. a branch reverting after wind

Ben Azzai's Knowledge

סוד ה׳ ליראיו
How Did Ben Azzai Know? R' Yoḥanan says each Sage estimated based on his own experience. But Ben Azzai never married!

Three answers:
1. He was married and later separated
2. He heard from his teacher
3. "The counsel of the Lord is with them that fear Him" (Psalms 25:14) — those who fear God receive knowledge beyond personal experience

Proverbs 6:26 — Two Interpretations

Rav Avira (in name of R' Ami / R' Asi)

  • "On account of a harlot → a loaf of bread"
  • One who eats bread without washing hands
  • is as if he engaged with a harlot
  • The verse connects bread to illicit behavior

Rava's Reinterpretation

  • The verse's phrasing is inverted from this reading
  • Should say: "On account of a loaf → a harlot"
  • Rather: one who sins with a harlot
  • will eventually be reduced to begging for bread
Note Both readings derive moral lessons from the same verse, but in opposite causal directions
