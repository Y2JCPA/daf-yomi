Who Drinks the Sota Water?

Woman suspected of adultery (sota)
↓
Is the marriage itself permitted?
❌ Marriage Prohibited
Neither drinks nor collects ketubah
(e.g., pregnant/nursing wife of another — R' Meir)
✅ Marriage Permitted
Drinks or forfeits ketubah
(e.g., wife of mamzer, convert, aylonit per Rabbis)
Key Principle The sota ritual generally applies only where the underlying marriage is halakhically valid and permitted. Where the marriage itself is prohibited, the woman neither drinks nor collects her ketubah.

The Aylonit Dispute — Can She Drink?

Rabbis (Tosefta)
An aylonit (sexually underdeveloped woman) can drink the sota water if the marriage is permitted.
R' Shimon b. Elazar
An aylonit neither drinks nor collects her ketubah!
R' Shimon b. Elazar
The verse says "she shall conceive seed" (Numbers 5:28) — the ritual applies only to one capable of bearing children.
Rav Naḥman
I agree with R' Shimon b. Elazar. The aylonit cannot drink.
Gemara
The Tosefta explicitly says she can drink — a conclusive refutation of Rav Naḥman!
Rav Naḥman
It is a tannaitic dispute, and I follow R' Shimon b. Elazar's opinion.
Key Verse "וְנִקְּתָה וְנִזְרְעָה זָרַע" — 'And she shall be cleared, and shall conceive seed' (Numbers 5:28). Does this limit sota to those who can bear children, or does it promise a blessing to the innocent?

"She Shall Conceive Seed" — R' Akiva vs. R' Yishmael

R' Akiva

  • If the sota was barren and found innocent...
  • She is remembered and will now conceive
  • The verse promises a new blessing of fertility
  • Reading: "She shall conceive seed" = she will become pregnant

R' Yishmael

  • If that were true, barren women would deliberately seclude themselves to get the blessing!
  • Virtuous barren women who don't seclude themselves would lose out
  • Rather: if she had painful births → easy births
  • If she had girls → boys; short → tall; dark → fair
R' Yishmael's Logic The Torah cannot create a system where sinful behavior (seclusion) leads to greater reward than righteous behavior. The blessing must be an improvement, not a new miracle.

Warning About a Gentile — Rav Hamnuna's Ruling

Objection
"And she was defiled" appears twice — once for husband, once for paramour. Sota applies only where she becomes newly forbidden to the paramour. But a Jewish woman is already forbidden to a gentile!
Rav Hamnuna
One can issue a warning with regard to a gentile. The sota ritual still applies.
Objection
And if a gentile has relations with a bat kohen, she should not be disqualified from teruma — "should be unto a strange man" implies only one eligible for betrothal (kiddushin)!
Rav Hamnuna
He does disqualify her — as R' Yoḥanan teaches from the verse about widowhood and divorce.
R' Yoḥanan / R' Yishmael
A bat kohen may eat teruma again only if her marriage could end in widowhood or divorce — a gentile's union cannot, so she is permanently disqualified.
Derivation "She returns to her father's house... she may eat of her father's bread" (Lev. 22:13) — only from a relationship that ends in widowhood or divorce (i.e., a valid Jewish marriage). Relations with a gentile permanently disqualify.
