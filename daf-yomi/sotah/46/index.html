Sotah 46 — Eglah Arufah: Disqualifications, Ritual, & Accompaniment

Does a blemish disqualify the eglah arufah?
↓
A fortiori from red heifer suggests yes
↓
The word "bah" (בה) excludes: blemish disqualifies only the red heifer
↓
Valley restrictions: Land may not be worked or sown
↓
Elders wash hands and declare: We did not neglect this person
↓
R' Meir: Accompaniment (לוויה) is obligatory — its reward is without measure
Core Theme The Gemara traces how precise Torah language — especially the word "bah" — limits legal inferences between the eglah arufah, red heifer, and sacred offerings, then transitions to the communal obligation of caring for travelers.

Blemish, Labor & Age: Three Categories Compared

Red Heifer (פרה אדומה)

  • ❌ Blemish disqualifies
  • ❌ ANY labor disqualifies (even placing a bundle of sacks)
  • ✅ Not disqualified by age

Eglah Arufah (עגלה ערופה)

  • ✅ Blemish does NOT disqualify
  • ❌ Labor disqualifies only if it pulls/moves a burden
  • ❌ Disqualified by age (must be under 2 years)

Sacred Offerings (קדשים)

  • ❌ Blemish disqualifies
  • ✅ Labor does NOT disqualify
  • ❌ Some offerings disqualified by age
Key Insight No single category shares all its disqualifications with another — the word "bah" in each context creates precise legal boundaries.

How Does 'Bah' Block the A Fortiori?

Gemara
A kal va-chomer: If the red heifer — not disqualified by age — is disqualified by blemish, surely the eglah arufah — which IS disqualified by age — should also be disqualified by blemish!
Answer
The verse says "bah" (בה) — 'wherein has no blemish' — it alone. Blemish disqualifies only the red heifer, not the eglah arufah.
Gemara
But 'bah' also appears regarding the eglah arufah: 'that has not been worked bah.' So labor beyond pulling a yoke shouldn't disqualify the red heifer either!
Answer
A gezeira shava (verbal analogy) of 'yoke'–'yoke' teaches that all labor disqualifies the red heifer. The 'bah' of the eglah arufah is needed to exclude sacred offerings from labor disqualification.
Legal Principle The same word — "bah" — serves different exclusionary functions in each context: limiting blemish to the red heifer and limiting labor-disqualification away from sacred offerings.

Red Heifer
Immediately disqualified

Eglah Arufah
Not disqualified until the heifer pulls and moves the burden

'Neither Worked Nor Sown' — Past or Future?

R' Yoshiya
The verse 'which may be neither worked nor sown' refers to the past — the valley must be a place that was never previously worked or sown.
R' Yonatan
It refers to the future — after the ritual, the land is permanently prohibited from being worked or sown.
Rava
Resolution: Everyone agrees the land is prohibited for the future. They disagree only about whether the valley must also have been unworked in the past.
R' Yoshiya
The word 'which' (אשר) indicates the past tense.
R' Yonatan
The word 'which' is a term of amplification (ריבוי), not a past-tense indicator.
Practical Difference According to R' Yoshiya, the elders must find a valley never before cultivated. According to R' Yonatan, any rough valley qualifies — only future use is restricted.

What Is Permitted vs. Prohibited in the Eglah Arufah Valley?

ActivityStatusReason
Sowing❌ ProhibitedExplicitly stated in the verse
Plowing / working the soil❌ ProhibitedDerived from 'neither worked' — labor on the land itself
Combing flax✅ PermittedNot labor performed on the land itself
Cutting stones✅ PermittedNot labor performed on the land itself
Hermeneutical Key The word 'which' (אשר) functions as an amplification (ריבוי), preventing the verse from being read as a generalization-and-detail (כלל ופרט), which would have limited the prohibition to sowing alone.
