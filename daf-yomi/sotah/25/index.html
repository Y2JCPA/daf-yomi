↓
Does she forfeit her ketubah automatically?
↓
No Warning Needed
She knows her actions are prohibited — awareness suffices
Warning Required
She deserves a chance to repent (תשובה) before forfeiting
Resolution The Gemara concludes from the mishna about court-issued warnings that forewarning is indeed required.

Is Forewarning Required for Forfeiting Ketubah?

The Dilemma
A woman who violates precepts (עוברת על דת) — is forewarning from her husband required to cause her to forfeit her ketubah?
Position A
No warning needed — she knows her actions are prohibited, so she has already forfeited her rights.
Position B
Warning is required — perhaps she wishes to repent (לחזור בתשובה), and the warning reminds her to do so.
Stakes This determines whether a husband must formally warn his wife before she loses her ketubah payment upon divorce.

Why Didn't Other Sages Use Rav Ḥanina's Proof?

Court warns wife of a deaf-mute, imbecile, or prisoner
↓
Is this case generalizable to all wives?
↓
Not Generalizable
These wives have no fear of their husbands (incapacitated/absent), so warning is uniquely needed here
Generalizable
If even these women need warning, all women who violate precepts need warning
Key Distinction Other Sages believed the case of an incapacitated husband is unique — the wife has no natural deterrent, so extra warning is needed. The Gemara ultimately rejects this distinction.

Can a Husband Keep a Wife Who Violates Precepts?

Position A
The Torah made divorce dependent on the husband's objection — if he doesn't object, he may keep her.
Position B
The Torah itself objects to her behavior — it is as though he objected, regardless of his wishes.
Proof
The court warns on behalf of incapacitated husbands — but perhaps the husband wouldn't want the court to warn her?
Response
Ordinarily, since she violates precepts, it is amenable to the husband to divorce her — the court acts under this assumption.
Implication The Gemara does not fully resolve this dilemma, but suggests that the normal presumption is that a husband would not want to remain with a wife who violates precepts of modesty.

Who Can Drink the Bitter Water? — Tanna Kamma vs. Rabbi Elazar

Tanna Kamma

  • Ailonit (sexually underdeveloped) — cannot drink
  • Elderly woman — cannot drink
  • Barren woman — cannot drink
  • Reason: 'She shall conceive seed' — only applies to those who can bear children

Rabbi Elazar

  • Ailonit — cannot drink (all agree)
  • Elderly woman — can drink
  • Barren woman — can drink
  • Reason: Husband can marry another wife to procreate — these are permitted marriages
Rav Naḥman / Rabba bar Avuh The dispute is only about barren and elderly women. Regarding an ailonit, all agree she cannot drink — as the verse says 'and shall conceive seed' (Numbers 5:28).
