Sotah 30 — Degrees of Impurity & The Song at the Sea

DAF OVERVIEW The Gemara concludes its analysis of ritual impurity degrees, marshaling five Tanna'im who all agree that second-degree impurity cannot create third-degree in non-sacred food. The discussion then shifts to the ḥalla separation dispute and its multiple interpretations, the nature of Shabbat boundaries, and finally a beautiful aggadic section on how the Jewish people sang the Shirah at the Sea — with three distinct models of communal song.

Five Tanna'im: Second-Degree Can't Impart Third to Non-Sacred

TannaSource / Proof
R' MeirMishna Para 11:5 — permits second tithe to one requiring rabbinic immersion, showing non-sacred stops at second-degree
R' YoseiDerived fourth-degree for kodesh — had non-sacred taken third-degree, he'd have found fifth-degree for kodesh
R' YehoshuaMishna Teharot 2:2 — third-degree impurity exists only in non-sacred prepared at teruma purity, not ordinary food
R' ElazarMishna Teharot 2:7 — first-degree in non-sacred only disqualifies one level (i.e., second-degree is the limit)
R' EliezerMishna Ḥalla 2:8 — permits pure dough next to impure, implying second-degree dough can't transmit to non-sacred
KEY PRINCIPLE Rav (via R' Asi): All five authorities agree — שני אין עושה שלישי בחולין — second-degree impurity cannot create third-degree in ordinary non-sacred food.

R' Yoḥanan's Puzzle: Why Didn't R' Yosei Use a Kal va-Ḥomer?

R' Yoḥanan
If food touched by a tevul yom has third-degree impurity (from second-degree contact), and that second-degree item is itself forbidden even for non-sacred food…
R' Yoḥanan
Then shouldn't a kal va-ḥomer prove fourth-degree for sacrificial food — without needing R' Yosei's separate derivation?
Gemara
Perhaps refute: a tevul yom was previously an av ha-tumah (primary source), so it's uniquely stringent?
Gemara
But R' Yosei already proved fourth-degree from meḥusar kippurim — who was also a primary source — and didn't reject it on those grounds!
R' Yoḥanan
Conclusion: R' Yosei disagrees with Abba Shaul on the premise. I cannot fully explain R' Yosei's reasoning.
INSIGHT R' Yoḥanan honestly admits he cannot reconstruct R' Yosei's full reasoning — a rare moment of intellectual humility in the Gemara.

The Ḥalla Separation Dispute — R' Eliezer vs. the Rabbis

A person has ritually pure dough and ritually impure dough and wants to separate ḥalla
↓
Can one place pure dough in the middle (even an egg-bulk) to separate ḥalla from pure on behalf of impure?
R' Eliezer — Permits
The middle dough contracts second-degree from impure dough, but cannot transmit third-degree to the pure batch
Rabbis — Prohibit
Multiple possible reasons: second-degree can transmit third, or untithed dough is like ḥalla, or one may not cause impurity to non-sacred food in Eretz Yisrael
RAV MARI B. RAV KAHANA Offers three alternative explanations of the dispute — the Rabbis may prohibit even if they agree second-degree can't create third-degree in non-sacred food.
6 / 9

Three Readings of the Ḥalla Dispute

Reading 1

  • Core dispute: Can second-degree impart third to non-sacred?
  • R' Eliezer: No → permits
  • Rabbis: Yes → prohibit

Reading 2 (Rav Mari)

  • All agree second-degree can't impart third to non-sacred
  • Dispute: Is untithed dough treated like ḥalla?
  • Rabbis: Yes — can contract third-degree
  • R' Eliezer: No — remains non-sacred

Reading 3

  • All agree on impurity rules
  • Dispute: May one cause impurity to non-sacred food in Eretz Yisrael?
  • R' Eliezer: Permitted
  • Rabbis: Prohibited
METHODOLOGY The Gemara demonstrates how a single halakhic dispute can be understood through multiple independent disagreements — each reading preserves different shared assumptions.

Shabbat Boundaries (Teḥum Shabbat): Torah or Rabbinic?

R' Akiva
The contradictory verses about Levite city land teach the 2,000-cubit Shabbat boundary — this is a Torah law (d'oraita).
R' Eliezer b. R' Yosei HaGelili
The verses refer to different types of surrounding land for Levite cities — the Shabbat boundary is only rabbinic (d'rabbanan).
PRACTICAL IMPACT If teḥumin are Torah law, violations are far more severe. If rabbinic, there is more room for leniency in edge cases.
