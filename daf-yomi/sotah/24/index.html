Husband Dies Before Wife Drinks

Beit Shammai
She collects her ketubah and does not drink. Since the husband died, the ritual cannot proceed — but she should not be penalized.
Beit Hillel
She either drinks or does not collect her ketubah. The suspicion remains unresolved — she cannot simply collect without clearing her name.
LOGIC Beit Hillel maintains the binary: a suspected woman must either prove innocence through the ritual or forfeit her ketubah. Beit Shammai treats the husband's death as an external circumstance that should not harm her.

Pregnant/Nursing & Barren Women — Can They Drink?

Pregnant/Nursing from Another Man

  • R' Meir: Neither drinks nor collects — marriage is prohibited (must wait 24 months)
  • Rabbis: He can separate and remarry her later — marriage is considered permitted, so she drinks

Ailonit / Elderly / Barren

  • Rabbis: Neither drinks nor collects — marrying a woman who can't bear children violates 'be fruitful and multiply'
  • R' Elazar: He can marry another woman to fulfill the mitzvah — so this marriage is permitted, and she drinks
PATTERN Both disputes hinge on the same question: Is the marriage considered prohibited (blocking sotah) or merely irregular but still valid?

Excluding Betrothed & Yevama — Biblical Sources

R' Yoshiya
"While under your husband" (Num. 5:19) — excludes a betrothed woman who doesn't yet live with her husband. "The wife of any man" (Num. 5:12) — includes a yevama.
R' Yonatan
"While under your husband" — excludes the yevama, whose bond comes from the brother. "This is the law of jealousy… under her husband" (Num. 5:29) — separately excludes a betrothed woman.
Gemara
R' Yonatan holds the betrothed bond is stronger — it's his own betrothal, and adultery carries stoning. The yevama's bond is weaker — from the brother, and violation carries only lashes.
KEY DISTINCTION Both agree that neither betrothed women nor yevamot drink — they disagree on which verse excludes which category, reflecting different views of the relative strength of these bonds.
