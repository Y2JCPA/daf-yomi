Nedarim 27 — Force Majeure, Partial Dissolution & Permitted Deception

A vow is made but circumstances change or prevent fulfillment
↓
Partial Dissolution
R' Akiva: If part of the vow is dissolved, the entire vow is dissolved
Ones (Force Majeure)
Illness, sick child, or impassable river — vow is not binding
Asmakhta
Conditional obligation — valid only in eminent court + no ones
Key Theme The Torah exempts those impeded by forces beyond their control — but the boundaries of this exemption are fiercely debated.

Partial Dissolution of Vows — R' Akiva vs. the Rabbis

Case
A man vows that a basket of figs is forbidden. He discovers it contains benot shuaḥ (white figs) and says: Had I known, I wouldn't have vowed.
Rabbis (Before R' Akiva)
The benot shuaḥ are permitted, but the basket and remaining figs are still forbidden.
R' Akiva
נדר שהותר מקצתו הותר כולו — A vow dissolved partially is dissolved completely. All the produce is permitted!
Rabba
R' Akiva's rule applies when the person would have reformulated the vow — 'black and white figs forbidden, benot shuaḥ permitted.'
Rava
Even the Rabbis agree in Rabba's case. R' Akiva's innovation is when he says: 'The whole basket forbidden, benot shuaḥ permitted' — even then, all is dissolved.
Principle R' Akiva holds that once part of a vow collapses, the entire structure of the vow falls apart — it cannot stand in partial form.

Mishna — Ones: Circumstances Beyond One's Control

נדרי אונסין — חלה הוא, או שחלה בנו, או שעכבו נהר
אונס If one's friend vowed that he should eat with him, and he became sick, or his son became sick, or a river blocked his path — these are vows impeded by ones. They are not binding and do not require dissolution.

Court Documents & Missed Deadlines — Rav Huna vs. Rava

Case
A litigant deposits his winning documents in court and says: 'If I don't return within 30 days, these documents are void.' He is impeded by ones and cannot return.
Rav Huna
His documents are void. He set a condition and failed to meet it — the reason doesn't matter.
Rava
He is a victim of ones! The Torah itself exempts such a person: 'But to the maiden you shall do nothing' (Deut. 22:26). His documents remain valid.
Rava
And the Mishna confirms: ones exempts even from vows, not just capital punishment.
Gemara
But what about the get case — a man says 'this is your divorce if I don't return in 12 months' and dies? Death is ultimate ones, yet the get is valid!
Gemara
Different: had he known he'd die, he would have finalized the get immediately. He wanted her freed from yibum. But a litigant never intended to forfeit his rights.
Distinction Ones exempts when the person would NOT have wanted the outcome. Where the person's deeper intent aligns with the result (as in the get), ones doesn't help.

The Ferry Case & Foreseeable Ones

A man gives a get to agents: 'If I don't return in 30 days, deliver it.'
↓
He arrives on day 30 but the ferry is on the other side of the river
↓
He shouts: 'See that I have arrived!'
↓
Is this considered ones?
↓
Shmuel's Ruling
This is NOT an arrival. The get is valid.
Reasoning
A ferry is foreseeable ones (אונס דשכיח). He should have anticipated it and stipulated explicitly.
Rule Ones that is common and foreseeable does not exempt — one must anticipate and stipulate for such circumstances in advance.
