Nedarim 16 — Oaths, Vows & Their Limits

חומר בשבועות מבנדרים, וחומר בנדרים מבשבועות
CORE THEME The Gemara resolves contradictions in the mishna's attribution, explores the ambiguity of the phrase she'okhal (that I will eat), and establishes the fundamental distinction: oaths are more stringent regarding matters without substance, while vows are more stringent because they can override mitzvot.
3 / 9

Whose Mishna Is It?

Gemara
The mishna must follow Rabbi Meir — R' Yehuda doesn't differentiate between 'korban' and 'ha-korban'; both are invalid without 'ke-korban.'
Challenge
But the latter clause says 'that which I will not eat is not a korban' permits the food — yet R' Meir forbids 'lekorban'!
Resolution
No contradiction: that mishna says lekorban (for an offering) — effective. Our mishna says la lekorban (not for an offering) — ineffective.
KEY DISTINCTION Lekorban (לקרבן) = the vow takes effect. La lekorban (לא לקרבן) = the vow does not take effect.

The Ambiguity of 'She'okhal' (שאוכל)

Person says: 'An oath she'okhal lekha'
(an oath that I will eat of yours)
↓
Context: Pressured to eat, said 'I WILL eat'
She'okhal = I will eat
The oath affirms he will eat
Context: Refused to eat, said 'I will NOT eat'
She'okhal = I will NOT eat
The oath reinforces his refusal
ABAYE'S PRINCIPLE The same phrase she'okhal carries two opposite meanings — context of the speaker's prior statements determines the intent.
5 / 9

Abaye vs. Rav Ashi on 'She'okhal'

Abaye

  • She'okhal has two meanings based on context
  • If importuned to eat → 'I will eat'
  • If refusing to eat → 'I will NOT eat'
  • Reads the mishna as written: she'okhal

Rav Ashi

  • The mishna actually means she'i okhal (that I will not eat)
  • Speaker blundered in pronunciation
  • Mishna teaches: despite garbled wording, intent was clear
  • Rejects Abaye because 'she'lo okhal' is also ambiguous
WHY RAV ASHI REJECTS ABAYE If context determines meaning, then 'she'lo okhal' (I will not eat) could also mean the opposite when someone was pressured to eat — making the rule unreliable.

Stringencies: Oaths vs. Vows

FeatureOaths (שבועות)Vows (נדרים)
Matters without substance (דבר שאין בו ממש)✅ Take effect — e.g., 'I won't sleep/speak/walk'❌ Do not take effect (or only by rabbinic law)
Overriding mitzvot❌ Cannot override — 'lo yachel devaro' applies only to his own word, not God's✅ Can override — one may not use an item forbidden to him, even for a mitzva
MechanismOath binds the personVow renders the object forbidden
Example: Sukka'I swear not to sit in sukka' — oath void'Dwelling in sukka is konam' — sukka forbidden to him
FUNDAMENTAL DISTINCTION An oath is a personal commitment that cannot contradict a prior divine obligation. A vow creates a prohibition on the object itself, which then cannot be used even for a mitzva.
7 / 9

How Do Vows Override Mitzvot?

Gemara
The verse says 'he shall not profane his word' — his word he may not profane, but he may profane it for God's matters. So oaths can't override mitzvot.
Gemara
But the same verse also says 'or swears an oath...he shall not profane his word' — this should apply to vows too!
Abaye
A vow works differently: saying 'the benefit of sukka is forbidden to me' renders the sukka a forbidden object — one cannot be fed forbidden items, even for a mitzva.
Rava
Were mitzvot given for benefit? No! Rather, the vow says 'dwelling in a sukka is konam for me' — it prohibits the action, not the benefit.
RAVA'S OBJECTION Mitzvot were not given for personal enjoyment (מצוות לאו ליהנות ניתנו). So Abaye's framing of 'benefit' is incorrect — the vow must target the act itself.
