Lost Items, Vows & Forbidden Benefit

Mishna
Where one takes payment for returning a lost item, the benefit goes to hekdesh (Temple treasury).
R' Ami / R' Asi (Version 1)
One says: even if the owner's property is forbidden to the finder, he still returns it. The other says: only if the finder's property is forbidden to the owner.
R' Ami / R' Asi (Version 2)
One says: only where the owner's property is forbidden to the finder — the peruta of Rav Yosef is uncommon. The other: even if the finder's property is forbidden to the owner, returning what is already his is permitted.
Gemara
The second version's strict opinion cannot explain why payment goes to hekdesh — it is difficult (kashya).
Core Issue When a neder creates forbidden benefit between two parties, can the mitzvah of returning lost property still be fulfilled, and what happens to any payment involved?

Two Directions of the Neder Ban

Owner's Property → Forbidden to Finder

  • The finder may not benefit from the owner's property
  • Returning the item is a mitzvah obligation — not personal benefit
  • If the finder accepts payment, that payment is the owner's property — forbidden!
  • Payment must go to hekdesh

Finder's Property → Forbidden to Owner

  • The owner may not benefit from the finder's property
  • Returning the item is giving back what already belongs to the owner
  • Dispute: is the act of returning itself a 'benefit'?
  • If finder refuses payment, owner keeping it = benefit from finder
Key Distinction The direction of the neder determines who is restricted and whether payment to hekdesh is needed.

Version 1: R' Ami & R' Asi on Returning Lost Items

Opinion A
Even if the owner's property is forbidden to the finder, he returns it — returning a lost item is a mitzvah, not personal benefit.
Opinion B
If the owner's property is forbidden to the finder, he may not return it. The mishna only addresses where the finder's property is forbidden to the owner.
Gemara
According to Opinion B, the mishna's case is: the finder refuses payment. If the owner keeps the payment, he benefits from the finder — so it goes to hekdesh.
Resolution Opinion B explains hekdesh is needed because the finder waives compensation, effectively benefiting the owner from the finder's property.

Version 2: The Peruta of Rav Yosef

Permitted to return it.

Finder's property → Owner
Dispute:
One view: Forbidden — returning benefits the owner.
Other view: Permitted — he returns what is already the owner's.
Peruta of Rav Yosef The small benefit a finder gains (exemption from other obligations while busy returning) is too uncommon to be considered a real 'benefit' from the owner.

'My Loaf Is Konam for You' — Then Given as a Gift

R' Ḥiyya bar Avin
If one says 'My loaf is konam for you' and then gives it as a gift — is it still forbidden? Perhaps 'my loaf' means only while it's in my possession?
Rava
It is obvious that it remains forbidden. The emphasis is on 'for you' — the loaf is like hekdesh for that person regardless of who holds it.
R' Ḥiyya bar Avin
But then what does 'my loaf' come to exclude? Doesn't it exclude a case where the other person stole it — and likewise a gift?
Rava
No. 'My loaf' excludes where he invited him to eat before the vow — that portion is already his. But a gift after the vow? Still forbidden.
Rava's Ruling The phrase 'for you' creates an absolute prohibition on the recipient. 'My loaf' only excludes what was already given before the vow.
