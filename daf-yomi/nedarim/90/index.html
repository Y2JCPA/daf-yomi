Nedarim 90: Dissolving Vows & Women's Divorce Claims

DAF OVERVIEW The daf explores whether vow dissolution (by a ḥakham) follows the same rules as vow nullification (by a husband) regarding vows not yet in effect. It then transitions to a pivotal Mishna listing three types of women originally entitled to divorce with their ketubah, and how the Sages retracted those rulings to prevent manipulation.

Can Dissolution Apply Before a Vow Takes Effect?

Rav Pappi
The dispute between Rabbi Natan and the Rabbis is about nullification only. But regarding dissolution by a ḥakham — all agree it works only after the vow takes effect.
Rav Pappi
As it says: "He shall not profane his word" (Num. 30:3) — implying there must already be a "word" (an existing vow) for the ḥakham to dissolve.
Ravina / Mareimar
The dispute is about nullification only — agreed. But regarding dissolution, all agree a ḥakham can dissolve even before the vow takes effect!
Ravina / Mareimar
"He shall not profane his word" — meaning there was only speech, not yet action, and still the ḥakham may dissolve it.
Gemara
Ravina's version is conclusively refuted from the baraita about naziriteship — dissolution must wait until the vow takes effect.
KEY DISPUTE Both Rav Pappi and Ravina agree the Rabbi Natan / Rabbis dispute is about nullification. They disagree about dissolution — but Ravina's lenient view is refuted.

Sequential Dissolution of Linked Vows

A person declares: "The property of X is konam for me"
↓
Then adds: "I am a nazirite for when I request dissolution of this vow"
↓
Which vow must be dissolved first?
↓
Step 1
First dissolve the konam vow on property
Step 2
Only then dissolve the naziriteship vow (which now exists)
PROOF This baraita proves that dissolution requires the vow to already be in effect — the naziriteship vow is triggered only once dissolution of the first vow is requested.

Three Women: Original Ruling vs. Retraction

Priest's wife: "I am defiled to you" (claims rape)Divorced with ketubahMust bring proof of her claim
"Heaven is between me and you" (husband is impotent)Divorced with ketubahCourt deals with it by way of request
"I am removed from the Jews" (vow against intercourse)Divorced with ketubahHusband nullifies his part; vow remains for all other Jews
REASON FOR RETRACTION The Sages feared that a woman might cast her eyes on another man and use these claims to force a divorce while keeping her ketubah payment.

Can the Priest's Claiming Wife Eat Teruma?

Rav Sheshet — Permitted

  • She may partake of teruma
  • Reason: so she not cast aspersions on her children
  • If barred from teruma, people assume her sons are unfit for priesthood

Rava — Forbidden

  • She may not partake of teruma
  • She can eat non-sacred food (ḥullin) instead
  • Her claim should be taken into account regarding teruma
CONCESSION Rava says Rav Sheshet concedes: if the wife was widowed or divorced, she may not eat teruma — people will assume the rape happened after marriage ended, so no aspersion on children.

Rav Aḥa bar Rav Huna's Clever Solution

🗣️ A man vowed that all benefit from the world is forbidden to him
↓
👕 He could no longer wear clothes (benefit from the world)
↓
🧱 Rav Aḥa smeared him with clay to protect from the elements
↓
🏛️ Brought him before Rav Ḥisda to dissolve the vow
↓
🌟 Rava praised: "Who is wise enough if not Rav Aḥa bar Rav Huna!"
STRATEGY Rav Aḥa held that dissolution requires the vow to take effect first. He let it take effect, protected the man without forbidden benefit, then sought dissolution from a ḥakham.
