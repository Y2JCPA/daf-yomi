QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does R' Yehuda prefer a nedavah (voluntary gift offering) over a neder (vow)?
A. A nedavah is more spiritually meritorious than a neder
B. A nedavah can be consecrated and offered immediately, avoiding the risk of delay and transgression
C. A neder requires a more expensive animal
D. The Torah explicitly prohibits making a neder
Correct: B — R' Yehuda holds that a person can bring his lamb to the Temple courtyard, consecrate it on the spot, lean on it, and slaughter it immediately — eliminating the risk of delayed fulfillment that comes with a neder (obligation-first vow).
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to R' Elazar HaKappar, why is a nazirite called a 'sinner'?
A. Because he will inevitably become ritually impure
B. Because naziriteship is a form of idol worship
C. Because he caused himself unnecessary suffering by abstaining from wine
D. Because he is attempting to appear more righteous than others
Correct: C — R' Elazar HaKappar interprets 'he sinned against the soul' (Numbers 6:11) as meaning the nazirite sinned by denying himself wine. He derives a kal va-chomer: if abstaining from wine alone makes one a sinner, one who fasts from everything is even more so.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the practical reason Reish Lakish gives for why the Sages created substitute vow-terms?
A. To make vows accessible to people who don't speak Hebrew
B. To prevent people from accidentally saying God's name in vain when formulating a vow
C. To distinguish between different levels of vow severity
D. To allow vows to be annulled more easily
Correct: B — Reish Lakish explains: if one says 'korban,' he might say 'korban la-Hashem,' which risks saying 'la-Hashem' first and then changing his mind — uttering God's name in vain. Substitute terms avoid this chain of risk entirely.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does the Gemara attempt to connect the R' Yochanan / Reish Lakish debate to a Tannaitic dispute?
A. Through a disagreement between R' Akiva and R' Yishmael about vow language
B. Through the Beit Shammai / Beit Hillel dispute about whether substitutes of substitutes are valid
C. Through a dispute about whether the Torah was given in Hebrew or Aramaic
D. Through R' Meir and R' Yehuda's views on intent in vows
Correct: B — The Gemara suggests: Beit Shammai (who validate substitutes of substitutes) may hold like R' Yochanan that substitutes are foreign words (so variants exist), while Beit Hillel (who invalidate them) may hold like Reish Lakish that they are Sage-invented (so only the specific coined terms work). The Gemara rejects this, showing both positions can align with either Tanna.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the status of the term 'kinma' when used in a vow context?
A. It is a valid substitute for konam according to all opinions
B. It is invalid according to all opinions because it refers to cinnamon
C. It remains unresolved (teiku) — it could mean konam or cinnamon (kineman besem)
D. Shmuel rules it has no legal effect
Correct: C — Ravina asked Rav Ashi whether 'kinma' means konam (making it a valid vow-substitute) or refers to kineman besem (sweet cinnamon, Exodus 30:23) with no vow intent. The Gemara leaves this question unresolved (teiku).
