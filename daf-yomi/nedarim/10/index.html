Nedarim 10: Substitute Language & the Power of Words

R' Yehuda: Best to volunteer (nodev) and pay — not to vow (noder)
↓
Why? A vow risks delay & transgression. A nedavah can be consecrated & offered immediately
↓
Mishna: Konam, ḥerek, nazik, shevuta = valid substitute terms (kinuyim) for vows
↓
R' Yochanan
Substitutes are foreign-language equivalents of korban, ḥerem, nazir, shevua
Reish Lakish
Substitutes are invented by the Sages to avoid saying God's name in vain
Core Theme The Sages carefully crafted vow-language to protect the sanctity of God's Name while preserving the legal force of personal commitments.

Vowing vs. Volunteering: R' Yehuda's Position

Gemara
The Mishna says R' Yehuda holds: Better to vow (noder) and pay. Does he really encourage vows?
Gemara (Answer)
Emend the text: R' Yehuda means one who volunteers a gift offering (nodev nedavah) and pays — not one who takes a neder
Gemara
But even a nedavah risks a stumbling block — what if he delays fulfilling it?
R' Yehuda
A person brings his lamb to the Temple courtyard, consecrates it there, leans on it, and slaughters it immediately — no delay, no stumbling block
Key Distinction Neder = 'It is incumbent upon me to bring an offering' (obligation first, animal later → risk of delay). Nedavah = designating a specific animal (can be done on the spot).

Is a Nazirite a Sinner? Three Opinions Agree

Shimon HaTzaddik

  • Refused to eat a nazirite's guilt-offering (Nedarim 9b)
  • Viewed naziriteship as leading to regret and sin

R' Shimon

  • Early pious men did NOT volunteer naziriteship
  • Nazir is called a sinner: 'he sinned against the soul' (Num. 6:11)
  • They brought burnt/peace/thanks-offerings instead

R' Elazar HaKappar

  • The nazirite sinned by denying himself wine
  • Kal va-chomer: if abstaining from wine = sinner, one who fasts from everything is even more so a sinner
  • Verse applies even to a pure nazirite (impure one = repeated the sin)
Abaye's Summary Shimon HaTzaddik, R' Shimon, and R' Elazar HaKappar all agree: a nazirite is called a sinner because unnecessary self-denial is itself a transgression.

Mishna: Substitute Terms (Kinuyim) for Vow Language

CategoryReal TermSubstitutes
OfferingsKorban (קרבן)Konam, Konaḥ, Konas
DedicationḤerem (חרם)Ḥerek, Ḥerekh, Ḥeref
NaziriteshipNazir (נזיר)Nazik, Naziaḥ, Paziaḥ
OathsShevua (שבועה)Shevuta, Shekuka, Mohi
Halakha Using any of these substitute terms has the same legal force as using the original term — the vow, dedication, naziriteship, or oath takes full effect.

What Are These Substitute Terms?

R' Yochanan
Substitutes are words from foreign languages — other nations' terms for korban, ḥerem, nazir, and shevua
Reish Lakish
They are terms the Sages invented (badu) for taking vows — like Jeroboam who 'devised (bada) in his own heart' (I Kings 12:33)
Gemara
According to Reish Lakish: why did the Sages create substitutes?
Reish Lakish
So one won't say 'korban' → which might lead to 'korban la-Hashem' → which might lead to saying 'la-Hashem' first and then changing his mind — uttering God's name in vain
Proof Text Leviticus 1:2 says 'korban la-Hashem' — offering first, then God's name — teaching us to always mention the offering before invoking Hashem.

Why Say 'Offering' Before 'To the Lord'?

A person wants to dedicate an offering
↓
❌ Wrong Order
Says 'La-Hashem…' first → changes mind → doesn't say 'korban' → God's name spoken in vain!
✅ Correct Order
Says 'Korban la-Hashem' → offering mentioned first → even if he stops, God's name was not uttered in vain
↓
Kal va-chomer: If even one who intended to say God's name upon an offering must be careful, how much more so one who says it with no purpose at all
R' Shimon (Baraita) The Torah's word order in 'korban la-Hashem' (Lev. 1:2) teaches: always mention the offering first, then God's name — to protect against saying Hashem's name in vain.
