Nullifying a Betrothed Woman's Vows

A betrothed young woman (na'arah me'orasa) takes a vow
↓
Who has authority to nullify?
General Rule
Her father and her current husband must act together
Key Principle
If she has not yet entered her own jurisdiction, even after 100 betrothals, father + final husband nullify
Mishna Principle As long as a young woman has never left her father's jurisdiction through nissu'in (marriage) or reaching majority, her father and last betrothed nullify her vows jointly.

The Mishna's Rule: Multiple Betrothals

💍 A betrothed young woman (na'arah me'orasa) takes a vow
↓
📋 She is divorced on the same day
↓
💍 She is betrothed again — even to 100 men in one day
↓
👨‍👧 Her father and final betrothed nullify her vows together
↓
🔑 Condition: She never entered her own jurisdiction (reshut atzma)
Key Principle The Mishna establishes that vow-nullification authority passes to each new betrothed, as long as the woman remains under her father's jurisdiction.

Shmuel's Scriptural Source

Gemara
From where do we derive that the final betrothed can nullify vows disclosed to the first betrothed?
Shmuel
The verse states: "And if she be to a husband and her vows are upon her" (Numbers 30:7–9) — "upon her" indicates vows already existing upon her.
Gemara
Perhaps this only applies to vows not discerned by the first betrothed?
Shmuel
The phrase "aleha" (upon her) is superfluous — it teaches that all vows, even those known to the earlier betrothed, can be nullified by the final one.
Derivation The seemingly redundant word aleha (upon her) expands the final betrothed's nullification power to include vows that the previous betrothed already knew about.

The Baraita: Father Nullified, Husband Died

A betrothed young woman takes a vow
↓
Father hears and nullifies his portion
↓
Husband dies before hearing the vow
↓
She is betrothed again on the same day
↓
Father + new husband nullify together
Supporting Shmuel This baraita accords with Shmuel's ruling: the new betrothed steps into the prior one's role, even regarding vows the first betrothed never heard.

Beit Shammai vs. Beit Hillel: Nature of Nullification

Beit Shammai

  • Each party (father/husband) severs (kotzetz) his own portion of the vow
  • If the betrothed nullified and then died, his portion is fully removed
  • The father can then nullify his own portion independently
  • Vows disclosed to the betrothed — authority reverts to the father upon death

Beit Hillel

  • Nullification only weakens (merafeh) the vow, not sever it
  • Father cannot nullify alone after betrothed's death
  • Father must act together with a new betrothed
  • Accords with Shmuel: final betrothed can nullify prior vows
R' Natan R' Natan identifies the dispute in the baraita: Beit Shammai say the father can nullify alone (severing), while Beit Hillel require a new betrothed to act jointly with the father (weakening).
