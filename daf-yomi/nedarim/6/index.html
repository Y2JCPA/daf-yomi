Can Incomplete Statements Create Legal Obligations?

Abaye
Even ambiguous intimations (yadot she'einan mochikhot) are valid — unclear hints still create vows.
Gemara
A baraita says 'upon me' creates a vow — implying without 'upon me' it does not. This refutes Abaye!
Abaye
Without 'upon me,' it's not even an intimation — he could mean ownerless property or charity.
Gemara
A second baraita: 'This is a sin-offering' without 'my' says nothing. Conclusive refutation!
Abaye
That baraita follows Rabbi Yehuda, who rejects ambiguous intimations.
Gemara
But you said your view works even according to Rabbi Yehuda!
Abaye
I retract that claim. Rabbi Yehuda does reject ambiguous intimations.
OUTCOME Abaye's position is weakened through multiple refutations. The Gemara then pivots to Rav Pappa's new dilemmas about intimation in betrothal and pe'a.

The Core Dispute: What Kind of Intimation Is Required?

Abaye

  • Ambiguous intimations (yadot she'einan mochikhot) ARE valid
  • Even unclear hints create binding vows
  • Initially claimed this works even per R' Yehuda
  • Later retracted the R' Yehuda claim

Rava

  • Only obvious intimations (yadot mochikhot) are valid
  • Ambiguous hints do NOT create vows
  • Claims this is true even per the Rabbis
  • Rabbis only relaxed the rule for gittin (divorce)
KEY TERMS Yad Mokhakhat = obvious intimation, clearly refers to a vow. Yad she'einah Mokhakhat = ambiguous intimation, could mean something else entirely.

"Upon Me" vs. "It Is Hereby" — What Happens?

Person points to an object and speaks
↓
What did he say?
"It is upon me" / "This is hereby upon me"
Forbidden to him like an offering — clear intimation of a personal vow (neder)
"It is hereby" (without 'upon me')
Abaye: Not even an intimation — could mean hefker (ownerless) or tzedakah (charity)
Gemara's challenge: Both parties forbidden — perhaps he meant hekdesh (consecrated property)
DISTINCTION The phrase 'alai' (upon me) clarifies that the speaker is creating a personal prohibition. Without it, the statement is open to multiple interpretations.

The Sin-Offering Refutation

Baraita
If one says 'This is a sin-offering' or 'This is a guilt-offering' — even if he owes one — he has said nothing.
Baraita
But if he says 'This is MY sin-offering' or 'MY guilt-offering' — and he owes one — it takes effect.
Gemara
This is a conclusive refutation (teyuvta) of Abaye — ambiguous intimations clearly don't work!
Abaye
This baraita follows Rabbi Yehuda, not the Rabbis.
Gemara
But you yourself said your view works even according to Rabbi Yehuda!
Abaye
I retract that claim (hadar bei).
CRITICAL POINT The word 'sheli' (my) transforms an ambiguous statement into an obvious intimation — connecting the offering to the speaker's personal obligation.

Rav Pappa's Dilemma: Intimation for Betrothal (Kiddushin)

A man says to Woman A: 'You are hereby betrothed to me'
↓
He then turns to Woman B and says: 'And you' (ve'at)
↓
What did he mean?
Betrothal intended
He meant: 'And you too are betrothed to me' — kiddushin takes effect for Woman B
No betrothal
He meant: 'And you see that I am betrothing this woman' — no kiddushin for Woman B
UNRESOLVED The Gemara notes that Rav Pappa once asked Abaye whether Shmuel held ambiguous intimations valid for kiddushin — but this doesn't prove Rav Pappa's own view. He was questioning Shmuel's position, not stating his own.
