QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the Gemara consider the 'upon me' baraita a challenge to Abaye?
A. Because it implies that without 'upon me,' the statement is not a valid vow — suggesting ambiguous intimations fail
B. Because 'upon me' is a type of explicit vow, not an intimation at all
C. Because the baraita only discusses sin-offerings, not ordinary vows
D. Because Abaye never discussed the phrase 'upon me'
Correct: A — The baraita specifically states 'upon me' creates a vow, implying that without these clarifying words (i.e., with only an ambiguous intimation), no vow is created — directly challenging Abaye's position that ambiguous intimations are valid.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does Abaye defend against the argument that 'It is hereby' (without 'upon me') should still be a valid ambiguous intimation?
A. He says the Rabbis disagree with the baraita
B. He argues it's not even an intimation — the speaker could mean ownerless property or charity
C. He claims the baraita is discussing a different type of vow
D. He says ambiguous intimations only apply to offerings, not vows
Correct: B — Abaye argues that 'It is hereby' without 'upon me' is not merely an ambiguous intimation — it's not an intimation at all, because the speaker could reasonably mean hefker (ownerless) or tzedakah (charity), not a neder.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What does Abaye retract (hadar bei) in this sugya?
A. His entire position that ambiguous intimations are valid
B. His claim that ambiguous intimations are valid even according to Rabbi Yehuda
C. His view that obvious intimations are required for offerings
D. His agreement with Rava about gittin
Correct: B — Abaye retracts only his claim that his position works even according to Rabbi Yehuda. He still maintains that ambiguous intimations are valid according to the Rabbis, but concedes that Rabbi Yehuda requires obvious intimations.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In Rav Pappa's betrothal dilemma, why is the case specifically where the man says 'And you' (ve'at) rather than 'And you too' (ve'at nami)?
A. Because 'And you too' would be too long a phrase for betrothal
B. Because 'And you too' is obviously betrothal — the dilemma only exists with the shorter, ambiguous phrase
C. Because 'And you' is the standard formula for kiddushin
D. Because Rav Pappa was testing whether any speech at all is needed
Correct: B — The Gemara clarifies that 'And you too' clearly extends the betrothal and is not a dilemma. The question only arises with the ambiguous 'And you,' which could mean 'you too are betrothed' or merely 'you are a witness to this betrothal.'
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the significance of pe'a being juxtaposed to offerings in the Torah for Rav Pappa's dilemma?
A. It proves pe'a must be brought to the Temple
B. If the juxtaposition imports all offering rules, then intimation would apply to pe'a; if it only teaches bal te'akher, it would not
C. It means pe'a can only be designated on Shabbat
D. It establishes that pe'a requires a minyan of ten
Correct: B — The crux of the dilemma is whether the hekesh (juxtaposition) between pe'a and offerings is broad — importing all rules including intimation — or narrow, teaching only the specific rule of bal te'akher (the prohibition against delaying fulfillment of one's obligation).
