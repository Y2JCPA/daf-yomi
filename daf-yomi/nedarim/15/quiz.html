Nedarim 15 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Gemara, why does Rav Yehuda distinguish between a condition (tenai) and a direct prohibition (issur)?
A. A person is careful with a direct prohibition but not with a mere condition
B. A condition requires witnesses but a prohibition does not
C. A direct prohibition is only rabbinic while a condition is biblical
D. Conditions only apply to vows while prohibitions apply to oaths
Correct: A — The Gemara explains that people are careful (zahir) about direct prohibitions but are NOT careful about conditions. This is why Rav Yehuda is concerned about retroactive violation when the second-day act is a condition, but not when it is a direct prohibition.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why can't the mishna's case of 'sleeping is konam for me' be taken literally?
A. Sleep is not a physical action
B. Vows (nedarim) only apply to things with actual substance, and sleep has no substance
C. One cannot make a vow about oneself, only about objects
D. The word 'konam' only applies to food items
Correct: B — The Gemara cites a baraita: oaths apply to things with and without substance, but vows apply ONLY to things with substance (davar sheyesh bo mamash). Since sleep lacks substance, the vow cannot apply directly — hence the reinterpretation to 'konam for my eyes.'
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does R' Yochanan's ruling about swearing not to sleep for three days impact the discussion?
A. It proves that sleep vows are always invalid
B. It shows the vow on sleep must have a limited timeframe to be valid
C. It demonstrates that oaths and vows are identical
D. It proves that all conditional vows are void
Correct: B — R' Yochanan rules that an oath not to sleep for three days is a vain oath because it's physically impossible. The Gemara uses this to show that a sleep vow with no time limit would also be meaningless — forcing the mishna to be reinterpreted as a conditional, time-limited case.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to the Gemara, how can a husband vow against conjugal relations if the Torah obligates him to provide them?
A. The vow overrides the Torah obligation entirely
B. He vows against the PLEASURE he derives, not against the act itself
C. He can only do so with his wife's explicit consent
D. He must get a beit din to annul the Torah obligation first
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that the mishna must refer to him saying 'the pleasure I derive from intimacy is forbidden to me.' He is obligated to perform the act, but not obligated to experience pleasure — so the vow can attach to the pleasure, effectively prohibiting the act.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is Ravina's novel approach to explaining 'sleeping is konam for me'?
A. The vow is completely invalid and carries no consequence
B. The vow doesn't take effect by Torah law, but violating it is a rabbinic transgression of lo yachel
C. The vow takes effect because the Rabbis gave sleep the status of a physical substance
D. The vow is valid because konam is a special category that applies to intangibles
Correct: B — Ravina says the vow literally does NOT take effect by Torah law (since sleep lacks substance). However, the Sages instituted a rabbinic-level prohibition of lo yachel — similar to the rule that customs treated as prohibitions cannot be violated, as this would be like profaning one's word.
