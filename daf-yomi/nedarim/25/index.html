Whose Understanding Governs an Oath?

Gemara
When one swears he saw as many people as left Egypt, doesn't he mean it by his own understanding — perhaps referring to ants (shumshemanei)?
Rav Ashi
When one takes an oath, it is based on our understanding — that of an ordinary person. We assume he means people, not ants.
Gemara
But doesn't a person take an oath according to his own understanding? A baraita teaches that courts must explicitly warn against private stipulations!
Gemara
The very need for the warning implies that without it, an oath would follow one's private intent!
Key Question If oaths naturally follow the court's understanding, why must the court explicitly warn against private stipulations (tenai she'b'libo)?

The Hollow Cane of Rava — A Case of Deception

💰 A creditor demands repayment; the borrower claims he already paid
↓
⚖️ Rava orders the borrower to take an oath that he repaid
↓
🪄 The borrower hides the coins inside a hollow cane
↓
🤝 He hands the cane to the creditor: 'Hold this while I swear on the Torah'
↓
📖 He swears truthfully: 'I returned all the money that was in my possession'
↓
😡 The creditor breaks the cane in anger — coins spill out!
↓
⚠️ Technically true, but deeply deceptive — this is why courts warn: 'according to OUR understanding'
Resolution The court's warning about 'our understanding' is meant to prevent deception through actions (like the cane trick), not necessarily through private definitions of words.

Moses' Oath to Israel — Closing Every Loophole

Moses administers an oath to accept the Torah
'Not according to your understanding, but according to mine and the Omnipresent's'
↓
Why was this warning needed?
What loophole might Israel exploit?
↓
Gemara's Suggestion
They might call an idol 'God' and claim they fulfilled the oath
Gemara's Rejection
Idols are called 'gods' in the Torah itself (Exodus 12:12) — not a private stipulation
↓
Why not just say 'accept the mitzvot'?
↓
'Mitzvot'
Could mean commandments of a king
'All Mitzvot'
Could mean just tzitzit, which equals all mitzvot
'Torah'
Could mean only the Written Torah
'Torahs'
Still doesn't cover everything…
Takeaway Every possible formulation of the oath has a potential loophole — Moses therefore had to specify: 'according to MY understanding.'

Principle Vows made based on mistaken facts or unintentional circumstances are treated as nidrei shoggin — unintentional vows that are automatically dissolved.

The Figs Dispute: Beit Shammai vs. Beit Hillel

Beit Shammai

  • Father and brother: permitted to eat
  • Others in the group: prohibited
  • The vow holds for those he intended to exclude
  • Only the unintentional portion is dissolved

Beit Hillel

  • Everyone is permitted to eat — both family and others
  • Connected to R' Akiva's principle below
  • A partially dissolved vow is entirely dissolved
  • Since the vow was unintentional for some, it falls for all
Rabba's Clarification Both agree that if he'd say 'all forbidden except father,' others remain prohibited. They disagree when he'd say 'so-and-so is forbidden, father is permitted' — naming each person individually.
