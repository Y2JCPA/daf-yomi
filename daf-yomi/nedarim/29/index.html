Can Sanctity Depart on Its Own?

Rav Hamnuna
Challenges Ulla: Where did the sanctity of the saplings go? Sanctity can't just vanish — like a wife doesn't leave marriage without a get!
Rava
Distinguishes: Kedushat damim (value-sanctity) can lapse on its own when a condition is fulfilled. Kedushat ha-guf (inherent sanctity) requires an action to remove it.
Abaye
Challenges from a baraita: An ox consecrated as a burnt-offering for 30 days becomes a peace-offering automatically — that's inherent sanctity departing with nothing done!
Gemara
Resolves: The baraita may refer to value-sanctity, not inherent sanctity. But the two-clause structure of the baraita suggests both types can lapse.
Rav Pappa
Defends Bar Padda: The peace-offering sanctity takes effect now and doesn't depart — the burnt-offering sanctity then takes effect after 30 days, like delayed betrothal.
Core Question Does conditional sanctity — whether of value or of the object itself — automatically lapse when the condition is met, or must an active process of redemption remove it?

Rav Hamnuna Challenges Ulla

Rav Hamnuna
Where did the sanctity of the saplings go? How can consecrated items become non-sacred without redemption?
Rav Hamnuna
Analogy: If a man says 'Today you are my wife, tomorrow you are not' — does she exit marriage without a get? Obviously not!
Rava
You're comparing two different things! Kedushat damim (value-sanctity) is not the same as kedushat ha-guf (inherent sanctity).
Rava
Value-sanctity departs on its own when the condition is fulfilled — the saplings are cut, sanctity lifts. But inherent sanctity (like a wife) requires an action to remove.
Key Distinction Kedushat damim — sanctity of monetary value, can lapse conditionally.
Kedushat ha-guf — sanctity of the object itself, requires active removal.

Two Types of Sanctity

Kedushat Damim (Value-Sanctity)

  • Object's monetary value is consecrated
  • Used to purchase offerings
  • Can depart when a condition is fulfilled
  • Example: Saplings consecrated until cut down
  • No redemption needed if conditional

Kedushat HaGuf (Inherent Sanctity)

  • The object itself is holy
  • Directly placed on the altar
  • According to Rava: requires action to remove
  • Example: Animal designated as burnt-offering
  • Analogous to betrothal — needs a get
Rava's Position The saplings in the mishna had only value-sanctity, so their consecration could lapse automatically when cut. A betrothed woman has inherent sanctity — she needs a bill of divorce.

Abaye's Challenge: The Ox Baraita

A man says: 'This ox is a burnt-offering for 30 days, after 30 days a peace-offering'
↓
For 30 days → the ox has the status of a burnt-offering (olah)
↓
After 30 days → it automatically becomes a peace-offering (shelamim)
↓
But this is kedushat ha-guf! The inherent sanctity departed with nothing done!
Gemara's Answer
Perhaps the baraita refers to kedushat damim — he consecrated the ox's value for purchasing offerings, not the animal itself
Problem
The second clause of the baraita seems to imply inherent sanctity — why teach two clauses if both are value-sanctity?
Abaye's Point If inherent sanctity can shift from burnt-offering to peace-offering without any action, Rava's distinction collapses.

Rav Pappa Defends Bar Padda

Gemara
This baraita should refute Bar Padda, who holds sanctity doesn't depart on its own and trees require redemption!
Rav Pappa
Bar Padda can reinterpret: When he says 'from now — peace-offering', that sanctity takes effect immediately and never departs.
Rav Pappa
The burnt-offering sanctity then takes effect after 30 days — like betrothal that takes effect later. No sanctity actually departs!
Gemara
But what's the novelty? If he said it takes effect after 30 days, obviously it does!
Rav Pappa
The novelty: even if he retracted within 30 days, the consecration still takes effect — he cannot undo his declaration.
Bar Padda's Defense The baraita doesn't show sanctity departing — it shows new sanctity taking effect at a predetermined time, like delayed betrothal with money given now.
