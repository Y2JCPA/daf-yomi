5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
What is Rava's key distinction in response to Rav Hamnuna's challenge?
A. Between sanctity of the Temple and sanctity of the land
B. Between kedushat damim (value-sanctity) and kedushat ha-guf (inherent sanctity)
C. Between voluntary and obligatory offerings
D. Between sanctity imposed by the Torah and sanctity imposed by the Rabbis
Correct: B — Rava distinguishes between kedushat damim — where the monetary value is consecrated and can lapse when a condition is met — and kedushat ha-guf — where the object itself is holy and requires an action (like redemption or a get) to remove the sanctity.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the Gemara argue that the two clauses of the baraita (burnt→peace and peace→burnt) must address different types of sanctity?
A. Because the Torah requires two witnesses for each type
B. Because if both were kedushat damim, the second clause would be redundant via kal va-chomer
C. Because Bar Padda demanded it
D. Because one clause is from the Mishnah and one from a Baraita
Correct: B — If both clauses referred to value-sanctity, and we already know stringent sanctity (olah) can lapse to become lenient sanctity (shelamim), then certainly lenient can lapse to become stringent — making the second clause unnecessary. Two clauses suggest one addresses inherent sanctity.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rav Pappa reinterpret the baraita to defend Bar Padda's position?
A. He says the baraita is not authoritative
B. He argues the peace-offering sanctity takes effect now and the burnt-offering takes effect after 30 days — no sanctity ever departs
C. He claims the ox was never truly consecrated
D. He says both sanctities exist simultaneously on the ox
Correct: B — Rav Pappa explains that when one says 'from now — peace-offering,' that sanctity takes immediate effect. The burnt-offering sanctity then takes effect after 30 days as a new consecration, not as a replacement. No sanctity actually departs on its own.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What does the principle 'amirato la-Gavoha k'mesirato l'hedyot' mean, and why is it invoked here?
A. God's words to man are like a binding contract — it explains why the Torah is authoritative
B. A verbal declaration to God has the legal force of a physical transfer to a person — it explains why one cannot retract consecration within 30 days
C. Speaking in the Temple is equivalent to bringing an offering — it explains how prayer replaced sacrifice
D. A promise between people is as binding as an oath to God — it explains the laws of nedarim
Correct: B — This principle means that a mere verbal declaration of consecration to God is legally equivalent to a completed physical act of acquisition between people. It is invoked to explain why, even according to the opinion that a woman can retract betrothal within 30 days, one cannot retract consecration — the verbal declaration already completed the act.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In the betrothal analogy, what is the novelty (chiddush) that the baraita teaches according to Rav Pappa's interpretation?
A. That betrothal money must be silver coins
B. That consecration takes effect after 30 days even if the person retracted during that period
C. That a woman can refuse betrothal at any time
D. That one must formally redeem consecrated animals before the 30 days
Correct: B — According to Rav Pappa, the baraita's novelty is that even if the person retracted his consecration within the 30-day period, the burnt-offering sanctity still takes effect after 30 days. Unlike betrothal (where retraction is debated), consecration cannot be retracted because a declaration to God is immediately binding.
