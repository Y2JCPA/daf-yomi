Nedarim 33: How Far Does a Food-Benefit Vow Reach?

A person vows: "Benefit from your food is forbidden to me"
↓
Reish Lakish: This includes any benefit associated with food
↓
Rava: Even benefit that leads to preparation of food is forbidden
↓
Clearly Forbidden
Sack, donkey, basket for bringing produce
Unresolved (Teiku)
Horse to ride to feast? Ring to appear wealthy? Walking on his land?
Key Theme The Gemara progressively expands the scope of a food-benefit vow, then tests its limits with increasingly indirect connections to food.
3 / 9

How Broadly Does a Food-Benefit Vow Extend?

Gemara
He vowed against food — a sieve and strainer aren't food!
Reish Lakish
The vow was: "Benefit from your food (hana'at ma'akholkha) is forbidden to me" — this includes any benefit associated with food.
Gemara
That should only mean he can't chew the other's wheat and put it on a wound — direct food-benefit!
Rava
The vow was: "Benefit that leads to preparation of your food" — even preparatory tools are included.
Rav Pappa
This means borrowing a sack, donkey, or basket to bring produce is forbidden.
Progression Each Amora expands the vow's scope: food itself → food-associated benefit → benefit leading to food preparation.

Rav Pappa's Unresolved Dilemma

Can he borrow a horse to ride to a feast?
↓
Can he borrow a ring to appear wealthy at a feast?
↓
Looking wealthy → served first or served better food
↓
Is this benefit that leads to food?

Proof Attempt
Mishna permits lending garments & rings → even to be seen with?
Refuted
Mishna refers to not being seen with them — listed only to parallel the first clause
Teiku Rav Pappa's dilemma — whether indirect social benefits at a feast count as food-benefit — remains unresolved.

Paying Another's Debt: Benefit or Just Preventing Harm?

Rav Hoshaya: Opinion of Ḥanan

  • Repaying debt = driving away a lion (preventing harm)
  • Not actually giving him anything new
  • Follows Ḥanan: one who pays another's debt without being asked loses his money
  • Establishes mishna per individual Tanna (Ḥanan)

Rava: Everyone Agrees

  • Mishna deals with a loan where creditor said: repay only if you choose
  • Repaying such a loan isn't truly repaying a debt
  • No need to rely on Ḥanan's minority view
  • Establishes mishna per consensus opinion
Driving Away the Lion A foundational concept: preventing someone's loss is not the same as giving them a benefit. This permits the vow-maker to repay the other's debts.

Ḥanan vs. Sons of the High Priests

Mishna Setup
A husband went overseas. Someone voluntarily supported his wife. Can he demand reimbursement?
Ḥanan
He lost his money. The husband never asked him to pay — he has no claim.
Sons of High Priests
The supporter takes an oath on how much he spent and is reimbursed by the husband.
R' Dosa ben Harkinas
I agree with the Sons of the High Priests — the supporter is reimbursed.
R' Yochanan b. Zakkai
Ḥanan is correct — he placed his money on the antler of a deer (keren ha-tzvi), a risky venture with no guaranteed return.
Relevance If paying another's debt creates no obligation (Ḥanan), then it's merely preventing harm — not giving benefit — and is permitted under a vow.
