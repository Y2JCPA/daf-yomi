Is Divorce Like Silence or Ratification? And Can Vows Be Nullified Unheard?

Gemara
The unresolved dilemma: When a betrothed man divorces his fiancée after hearing her vow, is it like silence (preserving the father's ability to nullify) or like ratification (blocking further nullification)?
Baraita
Multiple proofs are attempted from baraitot and mishnayot — but each is rejected. The dilemma remains unresolved.
Mishna
The practice of Torah scholars: fathers and husbands would preemptively declare all vows nullified before the woman enters a new jurisdiction.
Rami bar Ḥama
Can a husband nullify a vow without hearing it? Does the Torah's mention of 'hearing' require actual knowledge of a specific vow?
Core Theme Two major questions dominate this daf: the legal effect of divorce on existing vows, and whether nullification requires actual awareness of a specific vow.

Proof from the Baraita: When Does Authority Revert to the Father?

Husband dies — when does father regain authority to nullify?
↓
Father Can Nullify
Husband never heard the vow
Husband heard & nullified
Husband heard, was silent, died same day
Father Cannot Nullify
Husband heard & ratified
Husband heard, was silent, died next day
↓
Neither clause mentions divorce — so no proof either way!
Key Point The baraita's omission of divorce from both clauses means it cannot resolve whether divorce is like silence or ratification. Each clause could explain the omission differently.

Attempted Proofs: Is Divorce Like Silence or Ratification?

Gemara (Proof 1)
From the first clause of the baraita — divorce isn't listed among cases where father regains authority. So divorce must be like ratification!
Gemara (Rejection)
But the second clause also omits divorce from cases where father cannot nullify — suggesting divorce is like silence!
Gemara (Proof 2)
From Mishna 71a: She vowed while betrothed, was divorced, re-betrothed — the new husband can nullify. So divorce must be like silence!
Gemara (Rejection)
Perhaps the first husband never heard the vow. The mishna may deal only with that case.
Gemara (Proof 3)
From Mishna 89a: She vowed, he divorced and remarried her same day — cannot nullify. So divorce is like ratification!
Gemara (Rejection)
Perhaps that mishna discusses a married woman — and a husband simply can't nullify vows from before marriage. Not because of ratification!
Result Unresolved (תיקו-like) — The Gemara cannot determine from any source whether divorce functions as silence or ratification regarding vows.

Divorce as Silence vs. Divorce as Ratification

Divorce = Silence

  • Vow remains in its original state — neither confirmed nor denied
  • Father could still nullify (with a new betrothed)
  • New husband can participate in nullification
  • Supported by Mishna 71a (if read precisely)

Divorce = Ratification

  • Divorce effectively confirms the vow permanently
  • Father loses ability to nullify
  • No future husband can undo the vow
  • Supported by Mishna 89a (if read precisely)
Practical Difference If a betrothed man hears her vow and then divorces her, can her father (with a new betrothed) still nullify? The answer depends on this unresolved question.

Mishna: The Practice of Torah Scholars

מנהג נדרים שלפני שתצא מרשותו... כל נדרים שנדרת בתוך ביתי הרי הן מופרין
מנהג תלמידי חכמים Before a daughter leaves her father's house for marriage, the father would say: 'All vows you vowed in my house are hereby nullified.'

Before a bride enters her husband's jurisdiction, the husband would say: 'All vows you vowed before entering my jurisdiction are hereby nullified.'

This was necessary because once she enters his domain, he cannot nullify her prior vows.

Can a Husband Nullify Vows Without Hearing Them?

Rami bar Ḥama
The Torah says 'her husband hears it' (Numbers 30:8). Does nullification require actual hearing of a specific vow?
Rava
The Mishna says the father declares preemptive nullification without hearing specific vows — so hearing must not be required!
Gemara
Rejection: The father means 'when I will hear each vow, then it is nullified.' It's a conditional statement, not a blanket nullification.
Gemara
Then why say it now? Answer: It is the practice of a Torah scholar to prompt his daughter to tell him about her vows, which he can then nullify.
R' Eliezer
From Mishna 75a: A husband says 'all vows until I return are nullified' — R' Eliezer says they are nullified! He never heard them!
Gemara
Rejection: He means 'when I hear each vow, it's nullified.' He states it now in case he's preoccupied later and forgets to nullify.
Note Every proof that nullification works without hearing is reinterpreted as a conditional: 'when I hear it, then it takes effect.' The question remains open.
