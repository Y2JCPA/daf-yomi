Who Is the Tanna of the Four Vows?

R' Abba bar Memel → R' Ami
You told us in the name of R' Yehuda Nesia: The tanna is R' Yehuda in the name of R' Tarfon — naziriteship requires explicitness of intent (פירוש). Similarly, bargaining vows lack genuine intent.
Rava
You can say it follows even the Rabbanan! The Mishna says 'both want' (present tense) — they were satisfied with three dinars from the outset. They never intended to vow at all.
R' Yehuda / R' Tarfon
Two people who wagered on naziriteship — neither becomes a nazir, because naziriteship requires clear, certain intent.
PRINCIPLE The Mishna's four vows may be invalid either because vows require explicit intent (R' Yehuda) or because these parties never intended to vow at all (Rava).

Seller vows: 'I won't sell for less than a sela'
Buyer vows: 'I won't pay more than a shekel'
↓
Is this merely encouragement (זירוז) or a real vow?
Exaggeration?
Like 'a drop of cold water' — people speak in exaggerated terms
Firm position?
Each party specified an exact price — maybe they meant it literally
↓
Ravina challenges: the cold water case is different — the righteous say little and do much
↓
תיקו — The dilemma remains unresolved
UNRESOLVED When a buyer and seller each specify exact prices in vow form, we cannot determine if it's exaggeration or genuine intent.

The Cold Water Case: Exaggerated Speech in Vows

What He Said

  • Entering your house is konam for me
  • A drop of cold water is konam for me
  • Seems to forbid even the tiniest amount

What He Meant

  • He was refusing a full meal invitation
  • He may enter the house and drink cold water
  • A person speaks in exaggerated terms (לשון הבאי)

Ravina's Challenge

  • The host offered 'a drop' meaning a full meal
  • 'The righteous say little and do much'
  • So the vow refers to the meal, not literally water
  • This logic may not apply to buyer-seller vows
CONCEPT לשון הבאי — Exaggerated language. People often use extreme formulations without literal intent, and the halakha recognizes this.

Do the Four Vows Need a Halakhic Authority?

Rav Asi (via Rav Yehuda)
These four vows still require a request to a halakhic authority (שאלה לחכם) to dissolve them.
Shmuel
The Mishna says the Sages dissolved them — meaning they are already dissolved! Why would they need further authority?
Rav Yosef's Version of Rav Asi
A חכם can only dissolve vows that are similar to these four — i.e., vows not truly intended as vows, plus vows made by mistake (נדרי שגגות).
IMPLICATION Rav Yosef's version implies a חכם cannot dissolve a vow based on regret alone (חרטה) — he must find a concrete opening (פתח).

Approaches to Dissolving Vows: Finding an Opening

SageQuestion Asked to the VowerBasis for Dissolution
Rav HunaIs your heart still upon you? (לבך עלך?)Simple regret — if desire has changed, vow is dissolved
Rabba bar Rav HunaHad 10 people appeased you, would you have vowed?Hypothetical appeasement as an opening
R' Yehuda (Baraita)Is this desire still upon you?Same as Rav Huna — regret-based dissolution
R' Yishmael b'R' YoseiHad 10 people appeased you at the time?Same as Rabba bar Rav Huna — hypothetical opening
R' AsiDo you have regret?Direct regret — dissolved when vower confirms
R' ElazarDid you truly want to vow?Vower: 'If they hadn't angered me, I wouldn't have' — emotional opening
R' YochananHad you known your neighbors would speak of your daughter…Unforeseen consequence as opening
DISPUTE The core question: Can a חכם dissolve a vow based on regret (חרטה) alone, or must he find a specific opening (פתח) — a factor the vower didn't consider?
