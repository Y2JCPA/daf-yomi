Who Is the Mishna Discussing? — 'I Am Defiled to You'

Woman says: "I am defiled to you" (טמאה אני לך)
↓
Wife of an Israelite?
If willingly — no ketubah right
If by force — she's not forbidden to him!
Wife of a Priest?
If willingly — no ketubah right
If by force — forbidden but entitled to ketubah ✓
↓
Conclusion: Mishna refers to a priest's wife raped by force — she collects her ketubah
Rava's Question Answered Like one whose field was flooded — the husband's priestly status is the 'natural disaster' forcing the divorce, so she receives her ketubah payment.

If a Woman Claims 'You Divorced Me' — Is She Believed?

Rav Hamnuna
She is believed. A woman would not be brazen enough to lie directly to her husband's face about something he knows to be true or false.
Rava
She is not believed. When life under her husband is difficult (קשה לה תותיה דגברא), she would be brazen enough to lie to escape the marriage.
Rav Hamnuna
Regarding 'I am defiled' — even the version that says she's not believed is because the husband can't verify her claim. But divorce? He knows the truth, so she wouldn't dare lie.
Rava
Regarding 'I am defiled' — even the version that says she is believed is only because she demeans herself by claiming rape. But a divorce claim costs her no embarrassment.
Core Dispute Is the presumption of 'a woman would not be brazen in her husband's presence' (אינה מעיזה פניה בפני בעלה) strong enough to establish credibility?

Three Types of Claims — Credibility Analysis

"I Am Defiled" (טמאה אני לך)

  • Self-demeaning claim (rape)
  • Husband cannot verify independently
  • First version: Believed
  • Ultimate version: Not believed — suspected of ulterior motive

"Heaven Is Between Us" (שמים ביני לבינך)

  • Intimate claim about husband's virility
  • Husband partially knows the truth
  • Believed per Rava: too ashamed to specify falsely
  • Not believed per ultimate version: she may rationalize lying

"You Divorced Me" (גירשתני)

  • No self-demeaning element
  • Husband fully knows the truth
  • Rav Hamnuna: Believed — she wouldn't dare lie to his face
  • Rava: Not believed — marital hardship motivates brazenness
Key Variable Each claim differs in (1) whether the husband can verify it, and (2) whether the woman demeans herself by making it — these factors determine credibility.

Rav Nahman: Casting Eyes on Another Man

👩 Wife washes husband's hands every morning after intimacy — an established routine
↓
🌅 One morning she brings water, but husband says: 'We did not have relations last night'
↓
😰 She responds: 'Then perhaps one of the gentile aloe merchants (אהלויי) is to blame!'
↓
⚖️ Rav Nahman rules: She has cast her eyes on another man — her words are unreliable
↓
👩‍⚖️ Second case: Wife complains husband hurt her during relations — he says they had none
↓
🛢️ She blames gentile oil merchants (נפתויי) — same ruling: cast her eyes on another
Pattern In both cases the woman volunteers a claim of assault unprompted, then quickly redirects blame to specific men — revealing she has a particular person in mind, undermining her credibility.
