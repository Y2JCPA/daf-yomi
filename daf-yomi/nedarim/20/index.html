Four Reinterpreted Vows (Mishna)

Vow as StatedClaimed Reinterpretation
"This is ḥerem (dedication) to me""I meant ḥermo shel yam — a fishing net"
"This is like a korban (offering)""I meant offerings/gifts to kings, not to God"
"I am atzmi (myself) an offering""I meant etzem — a bone I set aside to pretend-vow with"
"Benefit from me is konam for my wife""I meant my first wife, whom I already divorced"
Key Principle In each case, the speaker claims the vow's key word had a non-sacred, everyday meaning — so the vow never took effect at all.

R' Meir vs. the Rabbis: Treating Ambiguous Vows

R' Meir
These vows do not need dissolution for a scholar. But if an ignoramus asks for dissolution, we punish him and treat stringently — the vow stands.
Rabbis
We are not stringent. Instead, we broach dissolution by suggesting a different extenuation, and teach him not to vow this way again.
Rav Yehuda
R' Meir's ruling distinguishes: a Torah scholar knows these vows don't take effect, so no dissolution is needed. An am ha'aretz who asks — he is treated stringently.
Rav Yosef
Since the Sages say the authority does not attend to him prematurely, a court that dissolves the vow immediately is not acting properly.
Rav Aḥa bar Yaakov
A halakhic authority who dissolves the vow prematurely should be excommunicated.
Punishment Defined An ignoramus nazirite who violated his naziriteship must first observe it for the same number of days he was lax (R' Yehuda). R' Yosei: for a long naziriteship, 30 days suffices.

↓

The Virtue of Shame (Busha)

שֶׁלֹּא תֶחֱטָאוּ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהַבּוּשָׁה מְבִיאָה לִידֵי יִרְאַת חֵטְא
Baraita — Exodus 20:17 "That His fear may be upon your faces" — this refers to shame (blushing). "That you not sin" — shame leads to fear of sin.

🔑 It is a good sign that a person experiences shame.

Others say: One who feels shame will not quickly sin. One who lacks the capacity for shame — his forefathers did not stand at Mount Sinai.

R' Yochanan ben Dehavai vs. the Rabbis: Marital Intimacy

R' Yochanan ben Dehavai
The ministering angels told me: Lameness, muteness, deafness, and blindness in children result from specific improper conduct during marital relations.
R' Yochanan
That is the statement of ben Dehavai. But the Rabbis said: the halakha is NOT in accordance with him. Whatever a man wishes to do with his wife, he may do.
Ameimar
The 'ministering angels' he mentions are actually the Sages — an honorary title. If they were real angels, we'd have to follow them!
Rabbi (Yehuda HaNasi)
To a woman who complained her husband 'overturned the table': The Torah permitted him — what can I do for you?
Rav
To a similar complaint: How is this different from a fish (binnita) one may eat any way he wishes?
Halakha The Rabbis' permissive ruling is the accepted halakha: marital intimacy is compared to meat from a butcher or fish — it may be prepared in any manner.
