Vows Under Duress: When May One Mislead?

Mishna
One may take vows to tax collectors and robbers that produce belongs to the king — even if it does not.
Shmuel
But dina d'malkhuta dina — the law of the kingdom is the law! Why permit evasion?
Rav Kahana / Shmuel
The mishna refers to a tax collector with no fixed amount — he collects arbitrarily.
School of R' Yannai
Or: a collector who appointed himself — not authorized by the kingdom.
Beit Shammai
One may vow but not take an oath. Don't initiate vows. Don't add beyond what is compelled.
Beit Hillel
One may even take an oath, initiate a vow, and add beyond what is compelled.
Key Principle Under duress from illegitimate authorities, the Sages permitted vows with mental stipulations to protect one's property.

Dina D'Malkhuta Dina

דינא דמלכותא דינא
Shmuel's Challenge Shmuel taught: The law of the kingdom is the law (דינא דמלכותא דינא). If Jews must obey state laws, how can the Mishna permit misleading tax collectors with vows?

Two answers resolve this:
• Rav Kahana/Shmuel: The collector has no fixed amount — he taxes arbitrarily
• School of R' Yannai: The collector is self-appointed, not authorized by the kingdom

Beit Shammai vs. Beit Hillel: Vows to Extortionists

Beit Shammai

  • May vow — but NOT take an oath
  • May NOT initiate a vow to the robber
  • May vow only about what the robber compels
  • Adding beyond what is compelled — the addition is BINDING

Beit Hillel

  • May even take an oath
  • May initiate a vow to the robber
  • May vow even about what he is NOT compelled
  • Even additions beyond what is compelled — all is PERMITTED
Example Robber demands: 'Say konam for your wife.' He adds 'and my children.' Beit Shammai: wife permitted, children forbidden. Beit Hillel: all permitted.

Problem
Normally, devarim shebalev (unspoken thoughts) are not significant
Resolution
Under duress (ones), mental stipulations ARE valid
Rav Amram / Rav The key innovation: mental stipulations that normally have no legal force become effective when one acts under compulsion.

Resolving the Contradiction: Mishna vs. Baraita

Rav Huna
A baraita teaches: Beit Shammai say one may not initiate with an oath. This implies one MAY initiate with a vow.
Rav Huna
But the Mishna says Beit Shammai prohibit initiating even with a vow! A contradiction!
Gemara
The Mishna taught about vows to show Beit Shammai's strictness — they forbid even initiating a vow.
Gemara
The baraita taught about oaths to show Beit Hillel's leniency — they permit even initiating an oath.
Rav Ashi
Alternative: The baraita does not refer to robbers/tax collectors at all — it addresses a different case entirely.
Resolution Each source highlights the far-reaching nature of one side: the Mishna shows how strict Beit Shammai are; the baraita shows how lenient Beit Hillel are.

Why 'Redemption' Language? The Gemara asks: why not say 'consecrated / not consecrated'? Because the latter clause requires 'not subject to redemption' — so the Mishna uses uniform language throughout.
