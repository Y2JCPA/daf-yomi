The Logic of Vow Language: Negative Inference & Hidden Meanings

Gemara
What does laḥullin mean? It means la (not) ḥullin (non-sacred) — i.e., it should be sacred like an offering.
Challenge
Whose opinion is the mishna? Rabbi Meir doesn't hold you can infer a positive from a negative — as shown by his rule on double conditions.
Gemara
It must be Rabbi Yehuda. But wait — the latter clause is Rabbi Yehuda, so the first clause can't also be his!
Resolution
The entire mishna is Rabbi Yehuda: 'Like Jerusalem' = valid vow, but 'Jerusalem' alone = meaningless.
Core Issue The Gemara wrestles with whether vow formulas that imply meaning through negation are valid — and which Tanna's logic governs the mishna.

Decoding 'Laḥullin' — The Prefix That Changes Everything

Person says food is laḥullin
↓
How do we read this word?
Reading 1: ḥullin
Non-sacred — no vow, food remains permitted
Reading 2: la-ḥullin
NOT non-sacred = sacred like an offering — vow takes effect!
Key Insight The prefix la- (לא) negates 'non-sacred,' creating a double negative that results in a positive vow of consecration.

Whose Mishna Is It? The Attribution Puzzle

Gemara
The mishna says laḥullin is a valid vow. This requires inferring a positive from a negative (מכלל לאו אתה שומע הן).
R' Meir
I require double conditions — like Bnei Gad and Reuven. You cannot infer a positive from a negative. So the mishna isn't mine.
Gemara
Then it's Rabbi Yehuda? But the latter clause explicitly says 'Rabbi Yehuda says...' — implying the first clause is someone else!
Resolution
The entire mishna is R' Yehuda. The latter clause merely clarifies his own position: 'Like Jerusalem' works, but 'Jerusalem' alone does not.
Bnei Gad Paradigm Moshe stated both outcomes: if they fight → land; if not → no land. R' Meir requires this double formulation for all conditions.

The Baraita's Cases: Ḥullin vs Laḥullin

Saying 'Ḥullin'

  • "Non-sacred" / "The non-sacred" / "Like the non-sacred"
  • Combined with 'that I eat' or 'that I don't eat'
  • Result: No vow — food permitted
  • Calling something non-sacred doesn't consecrate it

Saying 'Laḥullin'

  • "That which I eat of yours is laḥullin"
  • Means: NOT non-sacred → sacred = forbidden
  • "That which I do NOT eat is laḥullin"
  • Result varies by Tanna — key dispute below
Critical Distinction The la- prefix transforms a neutral statement into a vow. Without it, declaring something 'ḥullin' has zero vow effect.

Rav Ashi vs the Baraita Tanna: Resolving the Contradiction

Problem
The baraita says 'That which I will not eat is laḥullin' = permitted. But R' Meir's mishna (13a) says 'lakorban' in the same construction = forbidden!
R' Abba
R' Meir reads lakorban as 'le-korban' — for an offering, therefore I won't eat yours. The same logic should make laḥullin forbidden!
Tanna of Baraita
I agree with R' Meir that you can't infer positive from negative. But I disagree on lakorban — it doesn't mean 'for an offering.' Vows must be explicit.
Rav Ashi
Different resolution: The baraita means 'as non-sacred' (permitted). The mishna means 'should not be non-sacred' (forbidden). Different words, different results!
Rav Ashi's Elegance Rather than attributing mixed opinions, Rav Ashi distinguishes the cases textually: ke-ḥullin (as non-sacred) vs la-ḥullin (not non-sacred).
