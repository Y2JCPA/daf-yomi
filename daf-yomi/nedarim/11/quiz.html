Nedarim 11 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
What does the term 'laḥullin' mean according to the Gemara's interpretation?
A. It is non-sacred (permitted)
B. It is not non-sacred — i.e., consecrated like an offering
C. It is partially sacred
D. It refers to Jerusalem offerings specifically
Correct: B — The Gemara reads 'laḥullin' as 'la' (not) + 'ḥullin' (non-sacred), creating a double negative that means the item should be treated as sacred like an offering.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why can't the first clause of the mishna be attributed to Rabbi Meir?
A. Rabbi Meir doesn't believe vows can take effect at all
B. Rabbi Meir holds you cannot infer a positive from a negative statement
C. Rabbi Meir only accepts vows made in the Temple
D. Rabbi Meir requires witnesses for all vows
Correct: B — Rabbi Meir requires double conditions (like Bnei Gad and Reuven) and holds that from a negative statement alone you cannot infer the positive — so 'not non-sacred' wouldn't automatically mean 'sacred' for him.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rav Ashi resolve the contradiction between the baraita (food permitted) and R' Meir's mishna (food forbidden)?
A. He says the baraita is a later forgery
B. He distinguishes between saying 'as non-sacred' (no vow) and 'not non-sacred' (vow)
C. He says both cases result in the food being forbidden
D. He attributes them to different historical periods
Correct: B — Rav Ashi explains the baraita uses 'ke-ḥullin' (as/like non-sacred — no vow), while the mishna uses 'la-ḥullin' (not non-sacred — effective vow). The different wording yields different results.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In Rami bar Ḥama's dilemma about peace-offerings after blood sprinkling, what is the core question?
A. Whether peace-offerings can be eaten at all
B. Whether the vow attaches to the item's original forbidden status or its current permitted status
C. Whether blood sprinkling is valid after sunset
D. Whether one can vow using the name of any offering
Correct: B — After blood sprinkling, peace-offering meat becomes permitted. The dilemma is whether associating a vow with this meat references its earlier forbidden status (making the vow effective) or its current permitted status (making it meaningless).
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to the Gemara's resolution, why is the entire mishna attributed to Rabbi Yehuda despite his name appearing only in the latter clause?
A. The latter clause is a later addition by a different editor
B. The latter clause clarifies his own ruling: 'like Jerusalem' works but 'Jerusalem' alone is meaningless
C. Rabbi Yehuda retracted his earlier position
D. The Gemara actually attributes the mishna to Rabbi Meir
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that R' Yehuda authored both parts: the first clause establishes that 'like Jerusalem' is a valid vow, and the latter clause (in his name) adds the qualification that saying just 'Jerusalem' without comparison has no vow effect.
