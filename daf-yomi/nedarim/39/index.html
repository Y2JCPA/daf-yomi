Nedarim 39 — Bikur Cholim: Obligations & Origins

Gemara
The mishna says a visitor may stand but not sit with the ill person. Why does this distinction matter?
Shmuel
The visitor's property is forbidden to the ill. In places where one pays for sitting but not standing, sitting provides forbidden benefit.
Ulla
The ill person's property is forbidden to the visitor, but existential needs were excluded. Visiting while standing qualifies; sitting does not.
Reish Lakish
The Torah itself alludes to bikur cholim: "If these men die the common death of all men and be visited..." (Numbers 16:29).
DAF THEME This daf resolves the standing/sitting distinction for visiting the ill under vows, then explores the Torah origins of bikur cholim through the Korah narrative and aggadic teachings about creation.

The Core Question: Standing vs. Sitting

Gemara
If the visitor's property is forbidden to the ill — even sitting should be permitted (no benefit flows to the ill).
Gemara
If the ill person's property is forbidden to the visitor — even standing should be prohibited (he benefits from entering the house).
Shmuel
The visitor's property is forbidden to the ill. In places where one takes payment for sitting but not standing, sitting spares the ill person expense — that is forbidden benefit.
Gemara
This also teaches: even where one pays for bikur cholim, payment is proper for sitting but not for standing.
SHMUEL'S PRINCIPLE The forbidden benefit is economic: sitting saves the ill person the cost of hiring someone to sit with him.

Ulla's Alternative Explanation

The ill person's property is forbidden to the visitor
↓
But the ill person excluded existential needs from the vow
↓
Is visiting the ill an existential need?
Standing
Yes — this is how one fulfills the mitzva. Permitted.
Sitting
No — one can visit while standing; sitting is unnecessary. Prohibited.
KEY DISTINCTION According to Ulla, the exclusion of existential needs applies only to the minimum needed — standing suffices for bikur cholim.

Shmuel vs. Ulla — The Son's Illness

Shmuel

  • Visitor's property forbidden to the ill
  • Mishna deals with this case
  • Baraita (son's illness) deals with Ulla's case
  • Two separate scenarios — no contradiction

Ulla

  • Ill person's property forbidden to the visitor
  • Existential needs excluded from the vow
  • Exclusion covers HIS illness, not his SON's
  • When son is ill: inquire in marketplace only
BARAITA CHALLENGE If the person himself is ill — the visitor may enter. If his son is ill — the visitor may only inquire in the marketplace. Ulla explains this cleanly; Shmuel must split the cases.

Torah Source for Bikur Cholim

אִם כְּמוֹת כׇּל הָאָדָם יְמוּתוּן אֵלֶּה וּפְקֻדַּת כׇּל הָאָדָם יִפָּקֵד עֲלֵיהֶם
REISH LAKISH Reish Lakish finds a Torah allusion to visiting the ill in Numbers 16:29: "If these men die the common death of all men, and be visited after the visitation of all men..."

Rava explains: Moses said — if Korah's congregation dies naturally, becoming ill and confined to their beds with people coming to visit them, then people will say "the Lord has not sent me." The verse presupposes that visiting the ill is the normal practice for all people.
