How May a Sage Dissolve a Vow?

R' Eliezer
A Sage may broach dissolution by asking: Had you known your vow would shame your parents, would you have vowed?
Rabbis
This approach is forbidden — parental honor is too broad an extenuation.
R' Tzadok
If anything, broach dissolution via honor of God — but then no vow would ever stand!
Rabbis
We concede to R' Eliezer when the vow directly involves the parent — there the extenuation fits the specific vow.
R' Eliezer
A Sage may also broach dissolution via a new circumstance (nolad) — e.g., the forbidden person became a sofer.
Rabbis
New circumstances are not valid grounds for dissolution.
Key Issue The Mishna debates which forms of regret a halakhic authority may suggest to the vow-taker to dissolve the neder.

R' Eliezer vs. Rabbis: Grounds for Dissolution

R' Eliezer

  • May broach dissolution via honor of parents (kibud av va'em)
  • May broach dissolution via new circumstances (nolad)
  • Examples: forbidden person became a sofer, or is marrying off his son

Rabbis

  • Prohibit parental honor as a general opening
  • Prohibit new circumstances as grounds
  • Exception: Concede when the vow directly involves the parent
R' Tzadok's Challenge If parental honor works, then honor of God should work for ALL vows — rendering every vow dissolvable and meaningless.

Abaye vs. Rava: 'If So, There Are No Vows'

Abaye
It means: vows would not be dissolved properly — the person may claim regret only because he won't publicly admit he'd dishonor God, without truly regretting the vow.
Rava
It means: there would be no requests for dissolution at all — people would assume all vows are automatically void, and never approach a Sage.
Practical Difference Abaye worries about insincere regret; Rava worries about people ignoring the process entirely.

When the Rabbis Concede to R' Eliezer

Does the vow directly involve the parent?
Yes — between him & parent
Rabbis concede: may broach dissolution via parental honor
No — general vow
Rabbis prohibit using parental honor as an opening
Abaye's Logic When someone vows against a parent, he has already shown he's not concerned for their honor — so claiming regret via their honor is sincere, not mere embarrassment.

Nolad — New Circumstances: The Case of Moses

🤝 Moses vows to Yitro not to leave Midian
↓
💀 God tells Moses: 'All the men who sought your life are dead' (Exodus 4:19)
↓
🔓 Moses returns to Egypt — his vow is effectively dissolved
↓
📖 R' Eliezer: This proves a Sage may dissolve vows based on new circumstances!
↓
❌ Rabbis: Dathan & Abiram didn't actually die — they lost their wealth and status
↓
💡 Reish Lakish: 'Dead' means they lost property — not a true nolad
R' Yochanan The 'men' in Exodus 4:19 were Dathan and Abiram — the same troublemakers from the rebellion of Korah, proving they were still alive.
