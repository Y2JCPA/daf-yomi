Nedarim 2: The Power of Words

כל כנויי נדרים כנדרים
KEY THEME The opening Mishna establishes that substitute terms (kinuyim) and incomplete statements (yadot) carry full legal force for vows, dedications, oaths, and nazirite vows. The Gemara then analyzes why this Mishna lists all four categories and why it explains intimations before substitutes — revealing a consistent Mishnaic literary style.

Vows vs. Oaths: A Fundamental Distinction

Neder (Vow)

  • Operates on the object
  • "This loaf is forbidden to me like a korban"
  • The item becomes prohibited
  • Parallel to consecrating an offering
  • Written in Torah alongside oaths (Bamidbar 30:3)

Shevua (Oath)

  • Operates on the person
  • "I swear I will not eat this loaf"
  • The person is prohibited from acting
  • One forbids himself, not the object
  • Listed after vows in the same verse
WHY IT MATTERS This distinction explains why the Mishna separates vows from oaths: vows and dedications both forbid objects, so they are taught together, while oaths forbid the person from acting.

Why Does Nedarim's Mishna List All Four Categories?

Questioner
Tractate Nazir only teaches about nazirite vows. Why does Nedarim teach all four — vows, dedications, oaths, and nazirite vows?
Gemara
Because vows and oaths appear together in the Torah (Bamidbar 30:3), the Mishna had to teach both.
Gemara
And once it taught two categories, it went ahead and taught all of them.
Questioner
Then why not teach oaths immediately after vows, since they're paired in the verse?
Gemara
After teaching vows (which forbid objects), the Mishna teaches dedications — which also forbid objects. Oaths, which forbid the person, come last.
LOGIC The Mishna groups by functional similarity (object-prohibition together) rather than by scriptural proximity.

The Mishna's Literary Style: Explain the Last Topic First

📜 Mishna states TWO principles: substitutes (kinuyim) then intimations (yadot)
↓
❓ Gemara asks: Why does it immediately explain yadot instead of kinuyim?
↓
📐 Answer: The Mishna's style is to explain the CONCLUDING topic first
↓
🕯️ Proof 1: Shabbat 20b — "With what may one light / not light?" → explains 'not light' first
↓
🫕 Proof 2: Shabbat 47b — "In what may one insulate / not insulate?" → explains 'not insulate' first
↓
👩 Proof 3: Shabbat 57a — "With what may a woman go out / not go out?" → explains 'not go out' first
↓
⚔️ Challenge: Bava Batra 108a and other Mishnayot explain the OPENING topic first!
OPEN QUESTION The Gemara brings multiple counter-examples from Yevamot, Menachot, and Bekhorot where the Mishna explains its first topic first — the literary rule is not universal.
