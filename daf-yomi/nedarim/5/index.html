What Counts as a Valid Vow Expression?

Shmuel
If one says 'That which I eat of yours' or 'That which I taste of yours', he is prohibited from eating. But 'I am avowed from you' means only: I am not speaking with you.
Gemara
Does Shmuel then hold that ambiguous intimations are not intimations?
Gemara
Yes — Shmuel follows Rabbi Yehuda, who requires explicit language even in a bill of divorce.
Abaye
Ambiguous intimations ARE valid — even unclear expressions can create a vow.
Rava
Ambiguous intimations are NOT valid — the Torah juxtaposes intimations with naziriteship, requiring distinct articulation.
Core Issue Must vow language explicitly state its intent, or can context and implication suffice to create a binding obligation?

'I am separated from you' (מופרש אני ממך)I am not doing business with youNot a food/benefit vow at all

R' Yosei b. R' Ḥanina — Original vs. Revised Reading

One person says a vow formula to another
↓
Which expression was used?
'I am avowed TO you' (לך)
Both are prohibited from deriving benefit from each other
'I am avowed FROM you' (ממך)
Only the vow-maker is prohibited; the other is permitted
Revision The Gemara revises R' Yosei b. R' Ḥanina's teaching: originally both prepositions ('to' and 'from') were said to prohibit both parties, but the corrected version distinguishes between them.

Challenge from the Mishna (47b) Against Shmuel

Mishna
'I am to you like hekdesh' — the addressee is prohibited from the vow-maker's property, but not vice versa.
Gemara
But according to Shmuel, both should be prohibited! How do we reconcile?
Gemara
The mishna refers to a case where he specified: 'And you are not like hekdesh for me.'
Gemara
But the mishna's final clause — 'I am to you AND you are to me' — proves that an unspecified vow does NOT prohibit both!
Gemara
This refutes Shmuel's original reading. His opinion must be restated.
Logical Force The final clause of the mishna — requiring BOTH directions to be stated — proves that a one-directional vow only binds in one direction, contradicting Shmuel's original position.

Ambiguous Intimations (ידות שאינן מוכיחות)

Abaye — Valid

  • Ambiguous expressions do create valid vows
  • Even Rabbi Yehuda only requires explicit language for a get (bill of divorce)
  • Divorce requires full severance (כריתות) — vows do not
  • Context can supply the missing clarity

Rava — Not Valid

  • Ambiguous expressions do not create valid vows
  • Source: Numbers 6:2 — 'nazir lehazir'
  • Torah juxtaposes intimations with naziriteship itself
  • Just as naziriteship needs distinct articulation, so do its intimations
  • Even the Rabbis may agree outside of divorce contexts
Note Neither Abaye nor Rava simply follows one Tanna — each argues his position could work even according to the opposing Tanna's view on divorce documents.

Rava's Scriptural Derivation

נֶדֶר נָזִיר לְהַזִּיר לַה׳ — מַקִּישׁ יָדוֹת לִנְזִירוּת: מָה נְזִירוּת בְּפֵירוּשׁ, אַף יָדוֹת בְּפֵירוּשׁ
Source Verse Numbers 6:2 — 'נזיר להזיר לה׳' ('The vow of a nazirite, to consecrate himself to the Lord')

The doubled expression 'nazir lehazir' teaches that intimations (derived from the extra word) must share the same quality as naziriteship itself — namely, distinct, unambiguous articulation. An unclear hint that could mean many things is not sufficient.
