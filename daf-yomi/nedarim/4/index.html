How Does 'Bal Te'acher' Apply to a Nazir?

A person vows to become a nazir
↓
How can the prohibition against delaying (bal te'acher) apply?
↓
Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov
Delays leaving a cemetery — prevents naziriteship of purity
Rav Aḥa b. Rav Ika
Delays shaving at the end of his nazirite term
Mar Zutra b. Rav Mari
Delays bringing his offerings after completing the term
Key Principle The Torah juxtaposes vows and naziriteship (Numbers 30) to teach that bal te'acher applies to all aspects of nazirite obligations.

Nazir in a Cemetery: When Does Bal Te'acher Apply?

Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov
If one vows naziriteship while in a cemetery, and delays leaving and purifying himself — he violates bal te'acher.
Gemara Challenges
This works according to the view that naziriteship doesn't apply immediately in a cemetery. But Mar bar Rav Ashi says all agree it applies immediately!
Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov
Even so — he delays a naziriteship of ritual purity (נזירות טהרה). He must fulfill a pure term, and remaining impure delays that.
Rav Ashi
By this logic, a nazir who intentionally renders himself impure also violates bal te'acher — he prevents completing naziriteship in purity!
Distinction Even though naziriteship takes effect immediately in the cemetery, the obligation to complete a pure term still creates liability for delay.

Three Explanations of Bal Te'acher for a Nazir

Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov

  • Delays leaving the cemetery
  • Prevents naziriteship of ritual purity
  • Applies even if naziriteship took effect immediately

Rav Aḥa b. Rav Ika

  • Delays shaving (תגלחת) at end of term
  • Applies according to ALL opinions
  • Even if shaving is not indispensable — it remains a mitzvah

Mar Zutra b. Rav Mari

  • Delays bringing offerings (קרבנות)
  • Burnt-offering, sin-offering, and peace-offering
  • Derived separately from Deut. 23:22
Note The Gemara explores multiple avenues because the juxtaposition of vows and naziriteship must teach something beyond what is already derived from other verses.

Why Is the Nazirite Sin-Offering a 'Novelty'?

The nazirite's sin-offering (חטאת נזיר) cannot be accepted through a vow
↓
But the same is true of a sin-offering for forbidden fat — and bal te'acher still applies there!
↓
Sin-offering for fat
Comes for atonement (כפרה) — so delaying it is clearly a violation
Nazirite sin-offering
Does NOT come for atonement — so you might think bal te'acher doesn't apply
↓
Contrast with sin-offering of a woman after childbirth: also not for atonement, but it permits her to eat consecrated food
↓
The nazirite's sin-offering neither atones nor permits — a true novelty requiring a separate derivation
Conclusion The juxtaposition of vows and naziriteship is needed because the nazirite sin-offering is unique: it doesn't atone and doesn't permit, so we couldn't derive bal te'acher from other offerings.

Nullifying a Daughter's or Wife's Nazirite Vow

Baraita
Just as a father may nullify his daughter's vows and a husband his wife's — so too with nazirite vows, derived from the juxtaposition.
Gemara
Why do we need the juxtaposition? We could derive this through a 'What do we find' (מה מצינו) analogy from regular vows!
Gemara Answers
Regular vows have no time limit. But naziriteship has a set duration (30 days). You might think a father/husband cannot nullify a time-bound obligation.
Gemara
Therefore the juxtaposition teaches that nullification applies even to nazirite vows despite their time limit.
Halacha A father can nullify his minor daughter's nazirite vow, and a husband can nullify his wife's nazirite vow — just as with ordinary vows.
