Nedarim 65: Dissolving Vows — Presence, Conditions & Torah Claims

A person is prohibited by vow from benefiting another
↓
Can the vow be dissolved?
Presence Rule
Must dissolve in presence of the affected party (Rav Naḥman — Moses/Yitro)
Changed Conditions
R' Meir: If stated reason no longer applies (dog died, father repented), vow dissolves
Torah Claims
Broach with Torah prohibitions: vengeance, grudge, love your neighbor
Core Principle Dissolution of vows requires either the presence of the affected party, a change in the vow's underlying conditions, or awareness of Torah prohibitions violated.

Must Vows Be Dissolved in the Affected Party's Presence?

Baraita
One prohibited by vow from benefiting another — they dissolve the vow only in the presence of the subject of the vow.
Rav Naḥman
Source: God told Moshe 'Go, return to Egypt' (Exodus 4:19) — meaning: In Midian you vowed to Yitro, go dissolve your vow in Midian, in his presence.
Rav Naḥman
Where did Moshe vow? 'Vayo'el Moshe' (Exodus 2:21) — vayo'el relates to ala (oath), as in Ezekiel 17:13.
Gemara
Another proof: Zedekiah swore to Nebuchadnezzar — the Sanhedrin dissolved it without Nebuchadnezzar present, and they were punished for this error.
Key Rule The dissolution of a vow or oath must take place in the presence of the person affected by it — derived from Moshe's oath to Yitro.

R' Meir's Cases: Looks Like a New Situation, But Isn't

R' Meir's View

  • Vowed not to marry a woman because her father is evil → father died or repented
  • Vowed not to enter a house because of a bad dog or snake → dog/snake died
  • These appear to be new situations but are NOT — they can still be used to broach dissolution

Rabbis' View

  • The Rabbis do NOT concede to R' Meir
  • They maintain these qualify as new situations (nolad)
  • Cannot use such changed circumstances to broach dissolution
Dispute R' Meir holds that when the stated reason for a vow ceases to exist, the vow dissolves — but the Rabbis disagree.

How Does R' Meir's Ruling Work? Two Explanations

Gemara
Question: If the dog died, that is a new situation! How can R' Meir say it isn't?
Rav Huna
The vower is like one who makes his vow dependent on a condition (תולה נדרו בדבר). He explicitly stated the reason — so the vow only applies as long as the dog is alive.
R' Yoḥanan
We tell him: The dog had already died (or the father had already repented) before the vow — making it a mistaken vow (נדר טעות) from the outset.
R' Abba
Objection to R' Yoḥanan: A later Mishna (66a) already teaches mistaken vows (ugly/beautiful, black/white). Why teach the same concept twice?
Gemara
Indeed, this is difficult (קשיא) for R' Yoḥanan.
Key Distinction Rav Huna: conditional vow (תולה בדבר) — dissolved when condition ends. R' Yoḥanan: mistaken vow (נדר טעות) — never took effect. R' Abba's objection to R' Yoḥanan stands unresolved.

Torah Prohibitions Used to Broach Dissolution (R' Meir)

ProhibitionSourceHow It Applies
Do not take vengeance (לא תקום)Leviticus 19:18Prohibiting benefit from someone is a form of vengeance
Do not bear a grudge (לא תטור)Leviticus 19:18Maintaining a vow against someone harbors resentment
Do not hate your brother (לא תשנא)Leviticus 19:17The vow creates enmity in the heart
Love your neighbor as yourself (ואהבת לרעך)Leviticus 19:18The vow violates the fundamental duty of love
Your brother shall live with you (וחי אחיך)Leviticus 25:36If the subject is poor, the vow prevents providing support
R' Meir's Position The court may ask: 'Had you known your vow would violate these Torah commandments, would you still have vowed?' If he says no — the vow is dissolved.
