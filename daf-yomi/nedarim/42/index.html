When Does a Vow on Property Expire?

Rav & Shmuel
Whether one says "this property" or "my property" — the prohibition remains even after it leaves his possession (e.g., Shevi'it makes produce ownerless)
R' Yochanan & Reish Lakish
If one says "my property" — the prohibition applies only while it remains in his possession. Once ownerless in Shevi'it, the produce is permitted.
Gemara Resolution
They actually don't disagree! Rav & Shmuel discuss "this property"; R' Yochanan & Reish Lakish discuss "my property". Different cases, consistent rulings.
🔑 Key Principle The precise language of a vow — "this property" (נכסים אלו) vs. "my property" (נכסי) — determines whether the prohibition survives a change in ownership.

Rav & Shmuel vs. R' Yochanan & Reish Lakish

Rav & Shmuel
If he vowed before Shevi'it: "Benefit from this property is forbidden" — the other may not enter the field nor eat overhanging produce, even when Shevi'it arrives.
R' Yochanan & Reish Lakish
If he vowed before Shevi'it: "Benefit from my property is forbidden" — when Shevi'it arrives, he still may not enter the field, but may eat overhanging produce.
Rav & Shmuel
If he vowed during Shevi'it: he may not enter the field but may eat overhanging produce, since produce was already ownerless at time of vow.
R' Yochanan & Reish Lakish
Once produce becomes hefker in Shevi'it, the phrase "my property" no longer applies to it — so the prohibition lapses.
⚖️ Initial Understanding The Gemara initially assumes these Amoraim disagree on whether a person can render an item forbidden beyond his possession (אדם אוסר דבר שברשותו לכשיצא מרשותו).

Can a Prohibition Outlast Ownership?

Person vows: "Benefit from my property is forbidden to you"
↓
Does the vow persist after property leaves his possession?
"This property" (נכסים אלו)
Yes — prohibition attached to the item itself, not to ownership. Remains forbidden.
"My property" (נכסי)
No — "my" implies while in my possession. Once ownerless (Shevi'it), prohibition lapses.
📌 Resolution The Gemara concludes Rav/Shmuel and R' Yochanan/Reish Lakish do not disagree — each pair addresses a different formulation of the vow.

"This Property" vs. "My Property" — Final Ruling

נכסים אלו — "This Property"

  • Prohibition attached to the object
  • Remains forbidden even after ownership changes
  • Like saying: "Entering this house is konam"
  • Rav & Shmuel's case

נכסי — "My Property"

  • Prohibition attached to the owner's possession
  • Lapses once item leaves his ownership
  • Like saying: "Entering your house is konam"
  • R' Yochanan & Reish Lakish's case
📖 Proof The Mishna on 46a distinguishes: "your field" — permitted if sold. "This field" — forbidden even if sold. This confirms the distinction.

Why Can't He Enter the Field in Shevi'it?

Gemara
If produce is ownerless in Shevi'it, the land should also be ownerless — the Torah permits everyone to enter fields during Shevi'it!
Ulla
The Mishna refers to fruit trees standing on the borders of the field. Since he can reach the produce without entering, he has no need to enter.
R' Shimon ben Elyakim
Even if trees are in the middle of the field — he still may not enter! This is a rabbinic decree lest he remain standing there longer than necessary for eating.
🌳 Practical Rule Even though Shevi'it land is technically ownerless, the Rabbis restricted entry to prevent the forbidden party from lingering in the field beyond what eating requires.
