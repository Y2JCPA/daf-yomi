Are Vows of Exhortation Binding?

R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov
Nidrei ziruz (vows of exhortation) are not real vows — they are merely tools of social persuasion
The Rabbis
Vows of exhortation are binding vows — one cannot dismiss a vow simply because it was used to encourage
Gemara
Multiple proofs are brought from mishnayot to show the Rabbis disagree — each is deflected
Rav Huna & Rav Adda bar Ahava
Halakha follows R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov — vows of exhortation are not binding
Key Principle A vow made solely to persuade someone socially (e.g., to accept a gift) is not a true vow — unless language reveals genuine intent

Proof #1: 'Take a Gift for Your Son'

Mishna
One vows: 'Benefiting from you is konam if you don't accept a kor of wheat and two barrels of wine for your son'
Gemara
The recipient can dissolve the vow by saying 'This is my honor — to refrain.' But if he doesn't say this, it's a real vow!
Gemara (Challenge)
If R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov is right, this should be a neder ziruz automatically — proving the Rabbis disagree!
Gemara (Answer)
Even R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov agrees here — the vower said: 'I am not a dog, that I benefit from you and you don't benefit from me.' This shows genuine intent.
Distinction When the vower's language reveals personal dignity at stake ('I am not a dog'), it's no longer mere exhortation — it's a real vow

Proof #2: 'Give My Son a Gift'

One says: 'Benefiting from me is konam for you if you don't give my son a kor of wheat and two barrels of wine'
↓
R' Meir
The vow is valid — no benefit until the gift is given
Rabbis
He can dissolve it by saying: 'I consider it as though I received the gift'
↓
Challenge: If he must say this formula, doesn't that prove it's a real vow — against R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov?
↓
Answer: He said 'I am not a king' — showing genuine reciprocity, not mere encouragement
Pattern Each proof follows the same structure: infer from the Mishna that dissolution is needed → challenge R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov → deflect by showing genuine vow intent

The Decisive Proof: Lodging Invitation Gone Wrong

R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov

  • One says: 'Konam if you don't lodge with me, eat hot bread, drink hot water'
  • The friend becomes irritated at being pressured
  • R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov: Still nidrei ziruz — not binding
  • Even irritation doesn't transform exhortation into a real vow

The Rabbis

  • 'The Rabbis did not concede to him' — even in earlier simpler cases
  • If the friend is irritated, the vow clearly has coercive force
  • Vows of exhortation ARE binding vows
  • This proves the Rabbis fundamentally disagree
Conclusion The Gemara concludes: The Rabbis DO disagree with R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov on nidrei ziruz. But the halakha follows R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov (Rav Huna, Rav Adda bar Ahava).

Mishna: Nidrei Havai — Vows of Exaggeration

📜 New Mishna category: Nidrei havai — vows using obvious hyperbole
↓
👥 'Konam if I didn't see as many people on this road as those who ascended from Egypt'
↓
🐍 'Konam if I didn't see a snake as large as the beam of an olive press'
↓
✅ These are clearly exaggerations — no one takes them literally
↓
⚖️ A Sage taught: Nidrei havai are permitted, but oaths of exaggeration are forbidden
Key Distinction Oaths (shevuot) are more severe than vows (nedarim) — one does not swear lightly, so exaggerated oaths are still taken seriously
