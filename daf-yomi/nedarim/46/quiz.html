Nedarim 46 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Rabbis, what is the status of a jointly owned courtyard when both partners vowed not to benefit from each other?
A. Both may enter freely since they each own a portion
B. Both are prohibited from entering the courtyard
C. Only the one who vowed first is prohibited
D. They alternate days of use
Correct: B — The Rabbis hold that since the courtyard is jointly owned, neither partner can enter without benefiting from the other's portion. R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov disagrees and permits each to claim they enter only their own portion.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the court force the vow-taker to sell his portion of the courtyard?
A. Because the other partner has a larger share
B. Because he created the problem by taking the vow, so he must resolve it
C. Because the court always favors the wealthier party
D. Because selling is always required after a vow
Correct: B — The Gemara reasons that since the vow-taker brought the prohibition upon himself, the court forces him to sell his portion so the other partner is not harmed. This also proves the Mishna discusses a self-imposed vow.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Yosef's corrected version of Ze'eiri, when does the dispute between the Rabbis and R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov apply?
A. Only when the courtyard can be divided into 4 square cubits per partner
B. Only when the courtyard cannot be divided into 4 square cubits per partner
C. Only when there are more than two partners
D. Only when the vow was made in court
Correct: B — Rav Yosef corrected Ze'eiri's statement: the dispute is where the courtyard CANNOT be divided. If it CAN be divided, all agree it is forbidden, since entering may mean using the other's definable portion.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the difference between saying 'Your house is konam for me' and 'This house is konam for me'?
A. There is no practical difference
B. 'Your house' is a stronger prohibition
C. 'Your house' is tied to the owner — if he sells, it's permitted; 'This house' is tied to the property — it remains forbidden
D. 'This house' only applies during the owner's lifetime
Correct: C — Possessive language ('your house') links the vow to the owner's relationship with the property. If he sells or dies, the vow no longer applies. Demonstrative language ('this house') attaches the vow to the physical property itself, regardless of ownership changes.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Abaye, when is a vow-bound person permitted to use a bathhouse owned by the one who imposed the vow?
A. When the owner receives less than one-quarter of the profits
B. When the owner receives less than one-half of the profits
C. Only when the owner receives a flat annual rental fee (taska) with no profit share
D. It is never permitted
Correct: C — Abaye holds that any share of profits — even less than one-quarter — constitutes a 'right to profits from usage' that makes it forbidden. It is permitted only when the owner has completely forfeited all profit participation and receives only a fixed annual rental fee (taska).
