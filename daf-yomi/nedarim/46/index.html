Shared Property Under Vow Restrictions

Mishna
If both partners vowed not to benefit from each other, both are prohibited from entering the shared courtyard.
R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov
Each can say: 'I enter my own portion, not yours.'
Rabbis
Neither may enter — the courtyard is jointly owned and benefit cannot be divided.
Gemara
What if only one partner imposed the vow on the other — is the forbidden party also an ones (beyond his control)?
Core Issue Can jointly owned property be conceptually divided so each partner 'uses only their own portion,' or is shared ownership inherently indivisible?

Mutual vs. One-Sided Vow in Shared Courtyard

Both Vowed Against Each Other

  • Rabbis: Both prohibited from entering courtyard
  • R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov: Each may enter 'their own portion'
  • All agree: Cannot set up mill, oven, or raise chickens

One Partner Prohibited

  • The prohibited partner may not enter the courtyard
  • Court forces the vow-taker to sell his portion
  • Gemara: Only if he imposed the vow on himself — since he caused the problem, he must fix it
Key Distinction If the other person imposed the vow, the forbidden party is an ones (victim of circumstance) — forcing him to sell may be unjust.

Ze'eiri vs. Rav Yosef: Does Courtyard Size Matter?

Ze'eiri (via Rabba)
The dispute is only where the courtyard can be divided (4 amos² each). If it cannot be divided — all agree it's permitted.
Rav Yosef
But a synagogue cannot be divided, yet the Mishna (48a) says both are prohibited from using communal property!
Rav Yosef (correcting)
Ze'eiri must have meant the opposite: the dispute is where it cannot be divided. If it can be divided — all agree it's forbidden.
Rav Huna
The halakha follows R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov.
R' Elazar
The halakha follows R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov.
Resolution When a courtyard is large enough to divide, entering it means potentially using the other's portion — all agree it's forbidden. The dispute is only over indivisible shared space.

Marketplace Person Entering a Partner's Courtyard

A person from the marketplace (shuk) is forbidden by vow from benefiting from one courtyard partner
↓
May he enter the shared courtyard?
Rabbis
No — the courtyard partly belongs to the one from whom he may not benefit
R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov
Yes — he can say: 'I enter the other partner's portion, not yours'
Principle R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov consistently holds that shared ownership can be conceptually divided for vow purposes.

'Your House' vs. 'This House' — Konam on Property

Vow LanguageOwner Dies or SellsStatus
Your house is konam for me to enterHouse passes to new owner✅ Permitted — vow was on the person's ownership
This house is konam for me to enterHouse passes to new owner❌ Forbidden — vow was on the specific property
Your field is konam for me to purchaseField sold to another✅ Permitted
This field is konam for me to purchaseField sold to another❌ Forbidden
Language Matters Possessive language (beitekha — 'your house') ties the vow to the owner. Demonstrative language (bayit zeh — 'this house') ties the vow to the object itself.

Leased Bathhouse or Olive Press

The vow-imposer owns a bathhouse or olive press leased to the public
↓
Does the owner retain a right to profits from usage (tovas hana'ah)?
Yes — retains usage rights
❌ Forbidden for the vow-bound party to use it
No — fully leased out
✅ Permitted — owner has no current benefit stake
↓
What counts as retaining usage rights?
Rav Naḥman
Receives ½, ⅓, or ¼ of profits = forbidden. Less than that = permitted.
Abaye
Even less than ¼ of profits = forbidden. Permitted only with a flat annual fee (taska).
Practical Difference According to Abaye, the only way it's permitted is if the owner receives a fixed annual rental fee (taska) with no share in profits whatsoever.
