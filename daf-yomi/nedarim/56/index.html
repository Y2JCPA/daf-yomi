Does 'House' Include the Upper Story?

R' Meir
One who vows that a house is forbidden may still enter the upper story (aliyya) — it is not included in 'house.'
Rabbis
The upper story is included in 'house,' so entry to the aliyya is also prohibited.
Both
If one vows the upper story is forbidden, the ground floor is permitted — the house is not included in the aliyya.
PRINCIPLE The direction matters: 'house' may include the upper story, but 'upper story' never includes the ground floor.

Leprosy Verse: Who Needs 'In the House'?

Leviticus 14:35: 'a plague in the house' — includes gallery and upper story
↓
Rav Ḥisda
This must follow R' Meir — the Rabbis already include the upper story in 'house,' so the verse would be unnecessary.
Abaye
Even the Rabbis need the verse — you might think only what is attached to the ground counts as 'house' for leprosy (Lev. 14:34).
ABAYE'S INSIGHT Even if the Rabbis include an aliyya in 'house' for vows, leprosy law requires attachment to the ground — so a special verse is needed.

What Is a Dargash? Three Opinions

Ulla

  • A 'bed of good fortune' (שרירא טבא)
  • Placed in the home as a fortuitous omen
  • Not designated for sleeping
  • Challenged: does it have loops (karvitin)?

Rav Taḥalifa

  • A leather bed
  • Reported by Ravin from Eretz Yisrael
  • Defined by material, not function

R' Yirmeya (Revised)

  • Bed: straps inserted through holes in the frame
  • Dargash: straps go through loops (karvitin) attached to the frame
  • The loops can be loosened and it collapses
HALAKHA R' Yehoshua ben Levi rules that the halakha follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: a mourner loosens the loops of the dargash rather than overturning it.

Mourner's Obligation: Overturning Beds

🪦 A mourner must overturn all beds in the house — not just the one slept in
↓
🛏️ Exception: A bed designated exclusively for vessels (not sleeping) need not be overturned
↓
✨ Similarly, Ulla's 'bed of fortune' need not be overturned — it's not for sleeping
↓
🔗 Dargash: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says one loosens the loops (karvitin) and it collapses on its own
↓
📏 A bed whose two posts (nakliteha) protrude and cannot be overturned — stand it on its side (R' Yehuda HaNasi)
KEY DISTINCTION The dargash's unique loop-and-strap construction means it cannot simply be flipped — it requires a different method of mourning observance.
