"Until the Rain" vs. "Until the Rains"

Objection
Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says rains falling 7 consecutive days count as the first and second rainfall — implying the vow depends on actual rain, not the expected date!
R' Zeira
"Until the rain" (singular) = the expected date of the second rainfall period.
Resolution
R' Shimon ben Gamliel's ruling applies to one who said "until the rains" (plural, עד הגשמים) — that depends on actual rainfall.
R' Zeira
But "until the rain" (singular, עד הגשם) depends on the calendar date when rain is expected. My ruling stands!
Key Distinction עד הגשם (singular) = expected date  |  עד הגשמים (plural) = actual rainfall.

Vows Until Holidays: Intent Follows Custom

R' Yehuda: Until Passover

  • "Wine is konam until Passover"
  • Vow expires at the night of Passover
  • Reason: people drink wine at the Seder
  • He didn't intend to miss the four cups

R' Yehuda: Until Yom Kippur

  • "Meat is konam until Yom Kippur"
  • Vow expires at erev Yom Kippur
  • Reason: people eat meat at the pre-fast meal
  • He didn't intend to miss the se'uda mafseket

R' Yosei b'R' Yehuda: Until Shabbat

  • "Garlic is konam until Shabbat"
  • Vow expires at erev Shabbat
  • Reason: garlic is customarily eaten on Friday
  • He didn't intend to miss the Shabbat preparation
Principle We interpret vow duration by the vower's presumed intent — he would not have vowed to prevent himself from fulfilling a mitzvah or customary practice.

Dissolving Gift-Conditional Vows

Case 1
Reuven to Shimon: "Benefit from you is konam for me if you don't take a kor of wheat and two barrels of wine for your son."
Sages
Shimon can dissolve it himself — he says: "This is my honor, that I decline the gift." The vow was only made for his honor.
Case 2
Reuven to Shimon: "Benefit from me is konam for you if you don't give my son a kor of wheat and two barrels of wine."
R' Meir
Shimon is prohibited until he gives the gifts — the vow is binding.
Rabbis
Even here, Reuven can dissolve his own vow by saying: "I consider it as though I received the gift."
R' Meir vs. Rabbis R' Meir holds the vow is strictly binding. The Rabbis allow self-dissolution when the underlying purpose of the vow can be satisfied without literal fulfillment.
