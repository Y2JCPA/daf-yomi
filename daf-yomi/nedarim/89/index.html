Vow Nullification: Jurisdiction & Timing

A married woman takes a vow while under her husband's jurisdiction
↓
Does the vow's effect depend on her marital status?
No — time-based vow
Nullification follows the time the vow was taken (all agree)
Yes — marriage-dependent
R' Yishmael: follows time of fulfillment
R' Akiva: follows time of pronouncement
Core Principle The key question is whether nullification power is determined by the moment the vow is spoken or the moment it takes effect.

Mishna: Husband's Power to Nullify

Wife vows: "I am a nazirite after 30 days" while married
↓
Husband nullifies the vow
↓
She is widowed or divorced within 30 days
↓
The vow remains nullified — his nullification stands
Reverse Case If she vowed and was divorced the same day, even if he remarries her that day, he cannot nullify — she entered her own jurisdiction (reshut atzmah).

R' Yishmael vs. R' Akiva: Widow's Pre-Marriage Vow

Baraita
A widow/divorcée says: "I am a nazirite for when I marry" — then she marries. Can the husband nullify?
R' Yishmael
He CAN nullify. The vow takes effect during marriage, so the husband has jurisdiction over its fulfillment.
R' Akiva
He CANNOT nullify. The vow was pronounced when she was independent — that's what counts.
Baraita
A married woman says: "I am a nazirite for when I divorce" — then she divorces. Can the husband nullify?
R' Yishmael
He CANNOT nullify. The vow takes effect when she is divorced — beyond his jurisdiction.
R' Akiva
He CAN nullify. She pronounced the vow while married — he had jurisdiction at that moment.
Mnemonic — י״ל ל״י Yod-Lamed, Lamed-Yod: Yafer, Lo Yafer; Lo Yafer, Yafer — tracking R' Yishmael's positions across the two cases.

What Determines Nullification Power?

R' Yishmael — Time of Fulfillment

  • Derives from: "Every vow of a widow…shall be upheld" (Num. 30:10)
  • The practical application (kiyyum) of the vow must be during widowhood/divorce
  • Nullification depends on her status when the vow takes effect
  • Abaye: Yishmael agrees on time-based vows — only disagrees on marriage-dependent vows

R' Akiva — Time of Pronouncement

  • Derives from: "With which she has bound her soul" (Num. 30:10)
  • The binding (issur) of the vow — the act of vowing — determines jurisdiction
  • Nullification depends on her status when the vow was spoken
  • Rav Ḥisda: Our mishna follows R' Akiva's opinion
Abaye's Reconciliation Even R' Yishmael agrees the mishna is correct: a vow dependent on days (not marriage) follows the time of pronouncement, since the vow has no inherent connection to marital status.

Conditional Vows: R' Natan vs. the Rabbis

Mishna
A wife says: "Benefit from your father is konam for me if I prepare food for you." The husband can nullify.
R' Natan
He cannot nullify. The condition hasn't been fulfilled yet — she must cook for him (her obligation), and then she's forbidden from her father-in-law.
Rabbis
He can nullify even conditional vows. The vow afflicts her — she'd lose access to her relatives.
R' Natan
Same logic for: "Intercourse with me is forbidden to all Jews if I have relations with you." She must fulfill her marital duty — the consequence falls on others.
Rabbis
He can nullify this too — it is a vow of self-affliction (innui nefesh).
Key Dispute R' Natan holds a husband cannot nullify a vow whose prohibition hasn't yet taken effect. The Rabbis hold he can, since the potential affliction is already bound to her.
