Nedarim 13: What Can Anchor a Vow?

Core Question: Can a person create a neder by associating an item with something forbidden by the Torah itself (not by a vow)?
↓
Permits (Excludes Bechor)
"When a man takes a vow" — only items forbidden by means of a vow can anchor new vows. Bechor is sacred from birth.
Forbids (Includes Bechor)
"To the Lord" — this phrase includes items forbidden by Torah law as valid vow-anchors.
KEY PRINCIPLE The effectiveness of a vow depends on whether the item used as a reference point was itself made forbidden through a human act of consecration or is inherently sacred by Torah law.

Can You Vow by a Firstborn Animal?

Setup
A person places meat of a bechor (firstborn) next to regular meat and says: "This meat shall be like the bechor meat."
Tanna (Permits)
The verse says "When a man takes a vow" — he must vow with an item forbidden by means of a vow. A bechor is forbidden by Torah law, not by a vow!
Tanna (Forbids)
The verse says "To the Lord" — this includes items forbidden by Torah law as valid vow-anchors.
Gemara
Both agree he refers to the bechor before blood-sprinkling (when it's still forbidden). The dispute is whether Torah-forbidden items can anchor vows.
CONTEXT A bechor (firstborn kosher animal) is sacred from birth — its meat is forbidden until the blood is sprinkled on the altar.

Sin-Offering vs. Firstborn: Why the Difference?

Chatat / Asham (Included)

  • The owner designates a specific animal
  • Forbidden status comes from human act of consecration
  • Similar to a vow — person creates the prohibition
  • "To the Lord" includes these offerings

Bechor / Firstborn (Excluded)

  • Sacred from its mother's womb
  • Forbidden status is automatic by Torah law
  • No human act creates the prohibition
  • Even R' Yehuda HaNasi's mitzva to consecrate it doesn't change its nature
R' YEHUDA HANASI There is a mitzva to verbally consecrate a firstborn (Deut. 15:19), but if the owner fails to do so, it is still consecrated — proving the sanctity is inherent, not vow-created.

Korban Formulation Variations

Formula UsedExampleEffect (with "that I eat of yours")
A lamb (imra)"Imra that I eat of yours"R' Meir: Forbidden / R' Yehuda: Permitted
For a lamb (le'imra)"Le'imra that I eat of yours"R' Meir: Forbidden / R' Yehuda: Permitted
Like a lamb (ke'imra)"Ke'imra that I eat of yours"Both: Forbidden
Enclosures / Wood / Fires"Like the enclosures that I eat…"Forbidden — all reference Temple items
The Altar / Sanctuary / Jerusalem"Like Jerusalem that I eat…"Forbidden — sacred items anchor the vow
R' MEIR vs. R' YEHUDA R' Meir treats all three formulations (a/for/like) as valid vow language. R' Yehuda requires the explicit comparison word "like" (כ-) to create a vow.

"That Which I Will NOT Eat" — Positive from Negative?

Person says: "That which I will not eat of yours shall be lekorban."
↓
Does this imply that what he does eat is forbidden (like a korban)?
↓
R' Meir — Forbidden
Read as: "It shall be lekorban, therefore I will not eat yours." He stated the reason, not a limitation.
Baraita — Permitted
Read as: "La (not) le'imra" — what I don't eat is not-a-lamb. Cannot infer positive from negative.
↓
R' Abba's resolution: R' Meir's case uses lekorban ("it shall be an offering"), while the baraita uses la le'imra ("not a lamb") — different formulations, no contradiction.
PRINCIPLE מכלל לאו אי אתה שומע הן — From a negative statement you cannot infer a positive one. R' Meir agrees with this principle; the resolution turns on how the statement is parsed.

Vows on Speech and Actions — Substance Required?

Mishna
If one says: "Konam for my mouth to speak with you" or "for my hand to work with you" — it is forbidden.
Baraita
Vows only take effect on davar sheyeish bo mamash (a matter of substance) — unlike oaths, which apply even to non-physical matters!
Gemara
Contradiction! Speaking and walking are actions, not physical items. How can a vow apply?
Rav Yehuda
He said: "My mouth shall be forbidden regarding its speech." The vow attaches to the limb (a physical thing), not to the action.
Gemara
The mishna's language confirms this: it says "for my mouth to speak" — not "that which I speak" — the vow is on the mouth itself.
OATHS vs. VOWS Oaths apply to actions (substance or not). Vows require substance — Rav Yehuda shows the vow attaches to the limb, satisfying this requirement.
