Nedarim 14: Invalid Vows & Their Consequences

A person makes a vow associating an item with something forbidden by Torah law (not by vow)
↓
Does the vow take effect?
By Torah Law
No — 'a vow to the Lord' requires association with an item forbidden by vow (Num. 30:3)
By Rabbinic Law
Depends — Dissolution may be required to prevent taking vows lightly
KEY PRINCIPLE A vow (neder) only takes effect when one associates an item with something forbidden by means of a vow, not with something inherently forbidden by Torah law.

Why Does the Mishna Mention Non-Sacred Food (Ḥullin)?

Gemara
The mishna says vows using ḥullin (non-sacred), pig meat, or idol worship don't take effect. But the ḥullin ruling was already stated on 10b!
Initial Answer
It is superfluous — but since the mishna teaches pig meat and idol worship, it tangentially includes ḥullin as well.
Ravina
The mishna lists ḥullin to teach that it requires no request to a halakhic authority for dissolution — it's completely void.
Gemara
But the latter clause about 'like my mother' shows dissolution is needed there — implying the first clause already doesn't need it! Ravina's reading is rejected.
CONCLUSION The mention of ḥullin in the mishna is simply tangential to the other cases — it adds no new legal ruling.

Source: Why Torah-Forbidden Associations Don't Create Vows

"When a man takes a vow to the Lord" (Numbers 30:3)
↓
A vow must associate an item with something forbidden by means of a vow (neder)
↓
But the verse continues: "to bind his soul with a bond (issar)" — implying association with something asur (forbidden) by Torah?
↓
That phrase teaches a different halakha (baraita on 12a):
Issar = bond
A vow that extends an existing vow-prohibition to another item takes effect
Not Torah prohibition
An association with an item forbidden by Torah law itself does not take effect
BIBLICAL SOURCE Numbers 30:3 — Both clauses are needed: one teaches that vows require association with vow-created prohibitions; the other teaches that layered vow-extensions (issar) work.

Abaye vs. Rava: Dissolution for Invalid Vows

Baraita
If a man says to his wife: 'You are like the flesh of my mother / sister / orlah / kilayim' — he has said nothing.
Gemara
But the mishna says 'You are like my mother' requires dissolution! Contradiction!
Abaye
'Said nothing' means by Torah law. But by rabbinic law, he still needs dissolution from a halakhic authority.
Rava
The baraita refers to Torah scholars who know the vow is void. The mishna refers to an ignoramus (am ha'aretz) — rabbis required dissolution lest they take vows lightly.
PROOF FOR RAVA Rav Naḥman states: A Torah scholar who vows by a Torah scroll does not need dissolution — confirming Rava's scholar/ignoramus distinction.

Vowing by a Torah Scroll: Three Cases

Formula UsedHolding Scroll?Vow Takes Effect?
'By this Torah scroll'Either❌ No — the scroll itself is not a valid neder object
'By what is written in it'In his hands✅ Yes — he means the names of God
'By what is written in it'On the ground❌ No — he likely thinks the parchment is blank
'By it and what is written in it'On the ground✅ Yes — specifying contents shows awareness of God's name
'By it and what is written in it'In his hands✅ Yes — obviously effective
RAV NAḤMAN'S KEY INSIGHT When the scroll is on the ground, a person naturally assumes it might be blank parchment. Only explicit mention of 'what is written in it' shows intent to invoke God's name.

Mishna: Vows on Bodily Actions

Actions That Can Be Prohibited by Konam

  • 😴 Sleeping is konam for me
  • 🗣️ Speaking is konam for me
  • 🚶 Walking is konam for me
  • 💑 Intimacy with you is konam (said to wife)

Consequence of Violation

  • Violates "He shall not profane his word" (Numbers 30:3)
  • The vow takes effect — these are valid nedarim
  • Unlike vows on Torah-forbidden items, these create real prohibitions
  • The konam formula treats the action as if it were an offering
NOTE Even though sleeping, speaking, and walking are not tangible objects, the konam formula effectively prohibits them — the person who violates such a vow transgresses a biblical prohibition.
