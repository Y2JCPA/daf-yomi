Gemara: What Does 'Shabbat Observers' Mean?

Gemara
If the vow means those who actually observe Shabbat, why single out Samaritans? Even some gentiles observe Shabbat!
Gemara
Rather, it means those commanded about Shabbat. The Samaritans are true converts (geirei emet) and are commanded.
Gemara
But then for 'ascending to Jerusalem' — Samaritans are also commanded to ascend! Why are they excluded?
Abaye
The vow refers to those commanded AND who actually perform. For Shabbat — Samaritans do both. For Jerusalem — they are commanded but do not perform, going to Gerizim instead.
Abaye
And gentiles who keep Shabbat? They perform but are not commanded — so they are also excluded from the vow.
ABAYE'S FRAMEWORK Three categories: Commanded & perform (included) · Commanded but don't perform (excluded) · Perform but not commanded (excluded).

Commercial Transactions Under Vows

"Jewish property is forbidden TO ME"

  • May BUY from a Jew at MORE than market price
  • May SELL to a Jew at LESS than market price
  • He ensures HE receives no benefit
  • The Jew gets the better deal each time

"My property is forbidden TO JEWS"

  • May BUY from a Jew at LESS than market price
  • May SELL to a Jew at MORE than market price
  • He ensures the JEW receives no benefit
  • But people won't agree — they always lose!
KEY INSIGHT The vow-taker adjusts prices to ensure the forbidden party never benefits. But when it's always the other person who loses, "they do not listen to him" — no one will trade at a perpetual disadvantage.

Shmuel's Rule: Who Benefits More — Buyer or Seller?

Shmuel
One who takes a vessel from a craftsman to examine and it breaks — he is liable, like a borrower. All the benefit is the buyer's.
Gemara
But our Mishna lets the vow-taker sell at less than market price — implying the seller also benefits! If only the buyer benefits, why adjust?
Resolution
The Mishna deals with an average sale — neither side clearly benefits more. Shmuel's case is a keen sale (high-demand item) where the buyer benefits more.
Rav Naḥman
A middleman (safseira) who couldn't sell a donkey and it broke on the return — he must pay!
Rava
But a baraita says on the return trip he's exempt — only a paid bailee!
Rav Naḥman
A middleman's return is like the trip there — he'd still sell it at the owner's door! It's not truly 'returned' until delivered back.
DISTINCTION Gift-sender whose gift is rejected → paid bailee on return (could stop trying). Middleman → still a borrower on return (would still sell if opportunity arose).
