Vows on Cooked Foods: What's Forbidden?

A person vows: 'Cooked foods are forbidden to me'
↓
Our Tanna
Permitted: roasted & boiled
Only 'cooked' (בישול) is forbidden
R' Yoshiya
Forbidden: even roasted
Roasting = cooking (II Chron. 35:13)
↓
Resolution: Both follow language of people — different locales had different usage
Key Principle Vows follow the language of everyday people (lashon bnei adam), and local dialect determines their scope.

Mishna: Vow Formulations & Their Scope

'Food cooked in a dish is forbidden'Food boiled in a dishOther preparations
'What enters a dish is konam, I won't taste it'Anything cooked in a dishNothing — broadest scope
Pattern Adding 'konam… I won't taste it' expands the vow's scope, but the specific formulation still determines its limits.

Does Roasting Count as 'Cooking'?

R' Yoshiya
One who vows against cooked foods is forbidden roasted foods too.
R' Yoshiya
Proof: 'Va-yevashlu ha-pesach ba-esh' — 'They cooked the Pesach offering in fire' (II Chron. 35:13). Roasting is called cooking!
Gemara
Do they disagree whether vows follow Torah language vs. people's language?
Gemara
No. Both agree vows follow people's language. In R' Yoshiya's locale, even roasting was called 'cooking.'
Gemara
The verse is merely an asmakhta (allusion), not a formal proof — as he said: 'there is no proof, only an allusion.'
Takeaway Local dialect governs vows. The same word can have different scope in different communities.

Loose vs. Thick: What Is a 'Cooked Food'?

Vow: 'Tavshil (cooked food) is konam, I won't taste it'
↓
Abaye: A tavshil = something eaten with bread
↓
Loose dish
Eaten with bread as accompaniment
→ Forbidden
Thick dish
Eaten on its own, not with bread
→ Permitted
↓
Baraita support: 'Soft gourds (hiteriyyot) with which the sick eat their bread' — confirms the bread criterion
Abaye's Definition A 'cooked food' (tavshil) is specifically a side dish eaten to accompany bread. Thick porridge stands alone and doesn't qualify.

Gourds: Beneficial or Dangerous?

Baraita: Gourds for the Sick

  • Soft gourds (hiteriyyot) — the sick eat bread with them
  • Rava bar Ulla: the innards (luliva) of gourds are beneficial
  • Rava: 'the sick' = Sages, weakened by Torah study
  • Gourd innards best with chard (R' Yehuda)

R' Yirmeya's Doctor: Gourds Kill

  • Doctor sees a gourd in R' Yirmeya's house
  • Calls it an 'angel of death' and refuses to treat him
  • Resolution: hard gourds are harmful
  • Or: the gourd itself is harmful, only the innards help
Secret Recipe Flax seed innards with kutaḥ — but don't tell an am ha'aretz, lest they uproot flax just for the seeds!

Babylonian Porridge Customs

R' Zeira
Babylonians are foolish — they eat bread with bread! Thick porridge with bread is bread with bread.
Rav Ḥisda
The people of Huzal asked: wheat porridge with wheat bread, or wheat porridge with barley bread?
Rava
I eat my bread with ḥasisei — toasted barley porridge.
Rav Huna
Rav said: porridge with one finger is tasty. With two — more so. With three — best of all!
Rav
For porridge, travel 1 parasang. For ox meat, travel 3 parasangs. And spit out gourd and porridge even before King Shapur — they burn like lead!
Halachic Note For Babylonians, even thick porridge counts as a 'cooked food' since they eat it with bread — so vows have broader scope there.
