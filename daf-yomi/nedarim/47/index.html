Nedarim 47: Ownership, Replacements & Mutual Vows

Core Question:
Does a konam on property persist after ownership changes?
↓
"Your house"
Vow follows the owner — prohibition lifts when sold/inherited
"This house"
Vow follows the object — prohibition persists regardless of owner
↓
Avimi's Dilemma: What if the owner said "This house is konam for you" — can he forbid it beyond his ownership?
↓
Rava: Yes — proven from inheritance case where father forbids benefit "in lifetime and after death"
Key Principle A person can render an item in his possession forbidden even for a time after it leaves his possession.
3 / 9

"Your House" vs. "This House" — Mishna's Distinction

"Entering YOUR House is Konam"

  • Vow is tied to the person (owner)
  • Owner dies → prohibition lifted
  • Owner sells → prohibition lifted
  • The relationship to the owner defines the vow

"Entering THIS House is Konam"

  • Vow is tied to the object (the house itself)
  • Owner dies → still forbidden
  • Owner sells → still forbidden
  • The physical house retains the prohibition
Language Matters The precision of vow language — "your" vs. "this" — determines whether a prohibition is personal or attached to the object itself.

Can an Owner Forbid Property Beyond His Ownership?

Avimi
If the owner says "Entering this house is konam for you," and then dies or sells it — what is the halakha?
Avimi
Can a person render an item in his possession forbidden even after it will leave his possession?
Rava
Proof from a Mishna (Bava Kamma 108b): A father says "Benefiting from me is konam for you" — if he dies, the son still inherits.
Rava
But if the father explicitly says "in my lifetime and after my death" — the son does not inherit.
Rava
This proves a person can forbid his property even for after it leaves his possession!
Gemara
Conclude from the mishna that this is indeed so.
Resolution A person can extend a prohibition on his property beyond his own period of ownership — proven from the father-son inheritance case.

Rami bar Ḥama's Dilemma: Replacements of Forbidden Produce

Mishna (57a): "This produce is konam upon me" — forbidden from replacements and growths
↓
Rami bar Ḥama asks:
What if one says "This produce is konam for so-and-so"?
Are replacements forbidden to that other person?
↓
Maybe Permitted
For oneself: can forbid another's produce on himself → can extend to what doesn't yet exist.
For another: cannot forbid another's produce on that person → cannot extend to replacements.
Maybe Forbidden
Replacements are like growths (giduleihen) — they derive from the original forbidden item.
If so, no difference between self and other.
Core Logic The dilemma hinges on why replacements of forbidden produce are forbidden — is it an extension of vow power, or an inherent quality of derivation?

Attempted Proofs: Are Replacements Like Growths?

R' Aḥa bar Minyumi
Proof from a baraita: A man vows "Benefiting from me is konam for you" to his wife — she may borrow money and creditors collect from him.
R' Aḥa bar Minyumi
She benefits only indirectly! This proves replacements are not like growths — they are permitted.
Rava
No proof! Perhaps replacements are forbidden l'khatchila (ab initio), but b'dieved (after the fact) it stands.
Rava
Since the wife has no other means of support, this is considered b'dieved — permitted out of necessity.
Gemara
Second proof: Betrothal with money from sold orla fruit is valid — replacements are permitted!
Rava
Same answer: orla replacement is also only valid b'dieved. The dilemma remains unresolved.
Unresolved — Teiku-like Both proofs are deflected with the l'khatchila/b'dieved distinction. The Gemara does not reach a final resolution on whether replacements equal growths for vows affecting another person.

Mishna: Mutual Vow Formulations

Vow FormulaWho Said ItWho Is ForbiddenFrom Whose Property

"You are konam to me"Vow-takerThe other person is forbiddenFrom the vow-taker's property
"I to you and you to me"Vow-takerBoth are forbiddenFrom each other's property
Important Exception Even when both are mutually forbidden, they may still benefit from communal property — items belonging to olei Bavel (those who ascended from Babylonia), i.e., shared national assets.
