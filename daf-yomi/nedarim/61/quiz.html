Nedarim 61 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
If someone vows 'this year' during a leap year, how long does the vow last?
A. 12 months (follow majority of years)
B. 13 months (the full actual year)
C. Until Rosh Hashanah regardless of month count
D. Only until Adar I
Correct: B — The Gemara concludes that 'this year' means until the actual end of the calendar year, even if it includes an intercalated 13th month.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to the Rabbanan, why is the Jubilee Year included in a vow taken 'for this Jubilee cycle'?
A. Because the 50th year begins the next cycle
B. Because the 50th year is the conclusion of the previous cycle
C. Because Jubilee has no effect on vow counting
D. Because the Torah explicitly states this rule
Correct: B — The Rabbanan hold that the 50th year concludes the outgoing Jubilee cycle and does not count as year one of the next cycle. Therefore it is part of — and included in — the current cycle's vow.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why must the attributions of R' Meir and R' Yosei in our mishna be reversed?
A. Because of a contradictory Tosefta about Shabbat vows
B. Because Kiddushin 64b shows R' Meir considers all uncertain meanings, contradicting our mishna
C. Because a later amora overruled the original mishna
D. Because R' Yosei never lived at the same time as R' Meir
Correct: B — In Kiddushin 64b, R' Meir accounts for all possible meanings of ambiguous language (broad interpretation) while R' Yosei takes the narrowest reading. Our mishna's attributions conflict with this, so Rav (via R' Chanina bar Avdimi) says they must be reversed.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the practical difference between 'until it arrives' and 'until it will be' for an UNFIXED occasion like the grain harvest?
A. 'Until it arrives' ends earlier
B. 'Until it will be' extends until the harvest ends
C. There is no difference — both mean until the harvest begins
D. 'Until it arrives' includes Shabbat and 'until it will be' does not
Correct: C — For unfixed occasions, both expressions are treated identically — the vow is in effect only until the occasion arrives. The distinction between the two phrases only matters for fixed occasions.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to the first Tanna, why are grapes NOT included in 'kayitz' (summer produce)?
A. Grapes ripen in autumn, not summer
B. Grapes are not plucked by hand but with tools
C. Grapes are a liquid product, not a solid fruit
D. Grapes were not grown in the Land of Israel during the Temple period
Correct: B — The word kayitz relates to katzatz (cutting/plucking by hand). Since figs are hand-plucked while grapes typically require tools, only figs qualify as kayitz produce. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagrees, noting that ripe grapes can also be hand-plucked.
