Two Categories of Communal Property

Property of Olei Bavel (עולי בבל)

  • Belongs to all of Israel
  • Temple Mount (הר הבית)
  • Temple Courtyards (עזרות)
  • Water cistern in the middle of the road
  • Vow-bound persons may use these

Property of the City (אותה העיר)

  • Belongs to city residents jointly
  • City square (רחבה)
  • Bathhouse (מרחץ)
  • Synagogue (בית הכנסת)
  • Torah ark and Torah scrolls
  • Vow-bound persons are forbidden
Distinction National property is too broadly owned for any individual's share to matter; city property is considered joint ownership among residents.

Writing Portions to the Nasi: Dispute on Procedure

R' Yehuda
One may write his portion to the Nasi or to a common person (hedyot).
R' Yehuda
The difference: Writing to the Nasi does not require formal conferral of possession (kinyan). Writing to a commoner does.
Rabbanan
Both require formal conferral of possession — Nasi and commoner alike.
Rabbanan
They mentioned the Nasi only לדבר בהווה — to speak of the common situation.
R' Yehuda
The Galileans need not write at all — their forefathers already did! They were kanteranin (quarrelsome) and often vowed against each other.
Baraita R' Yehuda explains: The Galileans were quarrelsome and frequently took vows of prohibition against each other, so their ancestors preemptively wrote their communal portions to the Nasi.

The Beit Horon Incident

👨‍👦 A father vowed not to benefit from his son
↓
💒 The son was marrying off his own son and wanted his father at the wedding
↓
🎁 Son told a friend: 'The courtyard and meal are a gift to you — but only so my father can come and eat'
↓
🏛️ The friend said: 'If they're truly mine, I consecrate them to Heaven!'
↓
😠 Son: 'I didn't give them so you could consecrate them!'
↓
🎯 Friend: 'You gave them only to circumvent the vow — the sin would be on my head!'
↓
📜 Ruling: Any gift not absolute enough to be consecrated by the recipient is not a gift
Principle כל מתנה שאינה שאם הקדישה מקודשת אינה מתנה — The litmus test for a valid gift: could the recipient consecrate it?

Rava's Analysis: When Does Explicit Intent Invalidate a Gift?

Person gives a gift to bypass a vow.
What did the giver say?
Version 1 (Lenient)
'The gifts are before you only so that my father should come'
→ Forbidden (explicit condition)

'They should be before you that my father should come'
→ Permitted (leaves it to recipient's judgment)
Version 2 (Strict)
Even saying 'They are before you, my father should come and eat'
→ Forbidden

Because his ultimate actions prove his initial intent was not a true gift
Rava's Reasoning The stricter version holds that even casual mention of the purpose reveals that the giver never truly relinquished ownership — actions prove intent (סוף מעשה מוכיח על תחילתו).

The Flax Sheaves Case: Acquisition to Transfer (קנה על מנת להקנות)

Setup
A father vowed his son can't benefit from him. But if the grandson becomes a Torah scholar, the father wants him to inherit. He gave property to the son only to pass to the grandson.
Sages of Pumbedita
This is kinyan al m'nat l'haknot — acquire on condition you transfer to another. The son does not acquire. The transfer fails.
Rav Nachman
The son does acquire! Just like acquisition by sudar (cloth) — it's given only to transfer ownership of something else, yet it works.
Rav Ashi
Two problems with the analogy:
1) The cloth recipient could keep the cloth if he seized it
2) With sudar, acquisition is immediate. Here, the grandson acquires only when he becomes a scholar — by then the 'cloth has returned to its owner.'
Core Dispute Can property be effectively transferred through an intermediary who never truly owns it? Rav Nachman says yes; the Sages of Pumbedita and Rav Ashi say no.
