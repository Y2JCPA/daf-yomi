Proof from Nazir: 'And I' as Ratification

Gemara
A woman vows to be a nazirite. Her husband hears and says 'And I' — taking a matching vow upon himself.
Gemara
The Mishna in Nazir (20b) rules: He can no longer nullify her vow.
Questioner
But he only ratified it for one moment — the time until he took his own vow. Why can't he nullify it after that?
Questioner
This proves: Even one hour of ratification is permanent!
Gemara
Rejected. The Tanna holds that saying 'And I' is equivalent to saying 'It is ratified for you forever.'
Gemara
So this proof doesn't resolve Dilemma 3. All questions remain teyku.
Key Distinction Saying 'And I' to match a wife's vow is not merely temporary ratification — it is understood as full, permanent endorsement of her vow.

Mishna: Father's Power vs. Husband's Power

Father's Advantage

  • If the husband dies, authority reverts to the father
  • Father can nullify her vows on his own again
  • Source: 'hayo tihyeh' — doubled verb implies two betrothals
  • Applies even to vows the deceased husband already heard

Husband's Advantage

  • If the father dies, authority does NOT revert to husband
  • Husband cannot nullify alone — vow stands
  • But: husband can nullify vows of an adult wife
  • Father's authority ends at her majority (bagrus)
Mishna Principle The father-daughter bond in vow authority is stronger in youth; the husband-wife bond is stronger in adulthood. Each has a domain where their power exceeds the other's.

Rabba's Reading The seemingly redundant doubling 'hayo tihyeh' teaches that when a betrothed dies, the father's authority is fully restored — as if the betrothal never occurred.

Death of Father vs. Death of Husband — Step by Step

👨‍👧 A na'arah (young woman) is betrothed — father and husband must jointly nullify vows
↓
💀 Scenario A: Father dies → husband does NOT gain sole authority
↓
🔒 Vow remains in force — no one can nullify it alone
↓
💀 Scenario B: Husband (betrothed) dies → father regains sole authority
↓
✅ Father nullifies on his own, as before the betrothal
↓
💍 If she becomes betrothed again — father and new husband must act jointly once more
Asymmetry The father's authority is primary and enduring — it can be restored. The husband's authority is secondary and contingent — it cannot stand alone without the father.
