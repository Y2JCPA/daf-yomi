Nedarim 51: Language Precision in Vows

Person makes a vow about a category of food
↓
Does the vow use generic language or expansive language ('konam… I will not taste')?
Generic
Prohibited only from the most common item in that category
Expansive (konam)
Prohibited from all items in that category
Core Principle The scope of a vow depends on common usage of language (leshon bnei adam) versus explicit expansive formulation.

Bar Kappara's Etymologies at the Wedding

Bar Kappara
What is the meaning of to'eva (abomination)?
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
Offers various explanations — all refuted
Bar Kappara
First — have your wife pour me wine. Then arise and dance for me!
Bar Kappara
To'e ata bah — 'You are straying after it,' i.e., after an atypical mate
Bar Kappara
Tevel (perversion with an animal) = tevalin yesh bah? — 'Does it have any spice?' What is different about this act?
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
And what is zimma (lewdness)?
Bar Kappara
Zo ma hi? — 'What is she?' The man is confused about his wives' relationships
Result Ben Elasa, Rabbi's wealthy son-in-law, could not tolerate this humiliation of Rabbi and left the wedding with his wife.

Remutza Gourd: What Is It?

Shmuel

  • A type of gourd from Karkuza
  • Called kara karkuzai
  • Does not cook well
  • A distinct species of gourd

Rav Ashi

  • A gourd insulated in embers (remetz)
  • Not a species — a preparation method
  • REFUTED by baraita on kilayim
  • Baraita lists remutza as a distinct type subject to diverse-kinds prohibition
Conclusion Rav Ashi is conclusively refuted — the remutza gourd is a species, not a cooking method. One who vows against 'cooked food' may eat it since it doesn't cook well.

Dish (Kedeira) vs. Stewpot (Ilpas) Vows

Person vows against food from a cooking vessel
↓
Which vessel did the person specify?
"Enters a dish"
Forbidden from food in a dish AND a stewpot
(food passes through the dish first)
"Enters a stewpot"
Forbidden from stewpot food only
(permitted to eat dish-only food)
↓
What if he said 'cooked in a dish'?
"Cooked in a dish"
Permitted to eat stewpot food — referred only to dish cooking
"Cooked in a stewpot"
Permitted to eat dish food — referred only to stewpot cooking
Key Since food is first cooked in a dish then transferred to a stewpot, vowing against 'what enters a dish' covers both vessels — but not vice versa.

Dag vs. Daga — Large Fish or Small Fish?

R' Shimon b. Elazar
Dag (דג) = large fish forbidden, small permitted
Daga (דגה) = small fish forbidden, large permitted
Rav Pappa
Proof: 'The Lord prepared a great dag' (Jonah 2:1) — dag means large!
Gemara
But the next verse says Jonah prayed from the daga! Also: 'The daga in the river died' (Exodus 7:21) — did only small fish die?
Gemara
Resolution: Biblically, daga can mean both large and small. But in common speech (leshon bnei adam), daga refers only to small fish.
Principle Vows follow the language of people (leshon bnei adam), not biblical usage — so daga in a vow means only small fish.
