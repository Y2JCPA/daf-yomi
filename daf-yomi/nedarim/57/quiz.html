QUESTION 1 OF 5
What is the practical difference between saying "This produce is konam UPON me" vs. "This produce is konam FOR me, I won't eat it"?
A. The first forbids replacements and growths; the second permits them
B. The first is a Torah-level vow; the second is only rabbinic
C. The first applies to eating and benefit; the second only to eating
D. There is no practical difference — both formulations are equally strict
Correct: A — The Mishna teaches that 'konam upon me' creates an intrinsic prohibition on the item, extending to replacements and growths. 'Konam for me, I won't eat' is a personal restriction — only the original item is forbidden.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why are onions treated more strictly than wheat regarding growths from a prohibited item?
A. Onions are more valuable and therefore carry stricter rules
B. Onion seeds do not cease to exist — the original prohibited item persists through growth
C. The rabbis imposed a special decree on root vegetables
D. Onions absorb more sanctity from the soil than grains do
Correct: B — The Mishna distinguishes between items whose seeds cease (like grain — the seed is consumed) and items whose seeds don't cease (like onions — the original bulb persists). Since the prohibited item remains, even growths of growths are forbidden.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does R' Yirmeya challenge R' Yitzchak Nappacha's resolution of the Sabbatical onion dilemma?
A. R' Yitzchak cited a source that was later retracted
B. R' Yitzchak relied on one Sage (R' Yannai) against two dissenting Sages (R' Yochanan and R' Yonatan)
C. R' Yitzchak confused teruma with Sabbatical Year law
D. R' Yirmeya held that the dilemma was purely theoretical
Correct: B — R' Yirmeya objected that R' Yitzchak was following R' Yannai alone, while both R' Yochanan (orla vine case) and R' Yonatan (kilayim onion case) held that prohibited items are NOT neutralized by excess growth.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In the case of "What you prepare, I won't eat until Pesach" — if the wife prepares food before Pesach, what is the husband's status after Pesach?
A. He is permanently forbidden from that food
B. He is permitted — the time limit of the vow has expired
C. He must wait 30 days after Pesach
D. He needs the food to be replaced before he can eat it
Correct: B — When the time phrase 'until Pesach' modifies the prohibition's duration (not the items), the vow expires at Pesach. Food prepared before Pesach becomes permitted after Pesach passes.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does the Gemara reject the proof from the litra of tithed onions to resolve the Sabbatical onion dilemma?
A. The litra case involves a rabbinic obligation, not a Torah-level one
B. The litra case is a stringency (requiring tithing), which cannot prove a leniency (permitting Shevi'it)
C. The litra case deals with teruma, not ma'aser
D. The onions in the litra case were planted in a different manner
Correct: B — The Gemara distinguishes: requiring tithing on the entire crop is a stringency. You cannot use a stringent ruling to prove that growths neutralize a prohibition in a lenient direction (like permitting Sabbatical-Year produce).
