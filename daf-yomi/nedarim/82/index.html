Nedarim 82: The Boundaries of a Husband's Power to Nullify Vows

Gemara
A woman who says 'I am removed from the Jews' — her husband nullifies only his part. She remains forbidden to all other men.
Gemara
If this were an affliction vow, nullification would apply to everyone. So it must be a vow affecting the relationship between him and her.
Rav Huna
Our entire chapter follows Rabbi Yosei, who defines affliction more restrictively.
Shmuel (citing Levi)
A husband can nullify all affliction vows, except where she says: 'Benefit from me is konam for so-and-so' — that's entirely between her and another.
Core Issue The Gemara clarifies which vows a husband can nullify, distinguishing between innui nefesh (affliction) and devarim she-beinav le-veinah (matters between him and her), and whether the mishna follows R' Yosei or the Rabbis.

Two Categories of Vows a Husband Can Nullify

Innui Nefesh (Affliction)

  • Vows that cause the wife physical deprivation
  • Nullification applies to everyone — not just the husband
  • Example: 'Produce of this country is konam for me' (if no alternative)
  • Shmuel: husband can nullify all such vows

Beinav le-Veinah (Between Him & Her)

  • Vows affecting the marital relationship specifically
  • Nullification applies only with respect to the husband
  • Example: 'I am removed from the Jews'
  • If divorced, the vow remains in force toward others
Key Distinction The category determines the scope of nullification — affliction vows are fully nullified, while 'between him and her' vows are nullified only regarding the husband.

'I Am Removed from the Jews' — Proving the Category

Woman vows: 'I am removed from the Jews' (forbids marital relations with all men)
↓
Husband nullifies — but only his part. She remains forbidden to all other Jews.
↓
If this were innui nefesh, nullification would cover everyone. Why is she still forbidden to others?
↓
Conclusion
This vow is classified as beinav le-veinah — he nullifies only regarding himself
Limitation
Per Rabbis, this proof only works for R' Yosei's opinion, since Rav Huna says the chapter follows R' Yosei

Shmuel (citing Levi): Benefit Vows — 'From Me' vs. 'For Me'

Shmuel / Levi
A husband can nullify all affliction vows, except: 'Benefit from me is konam for so-and-so' — this is entirely between her and another person.
Shmuel / Levi
But if she says: 'Benefit from so-and-so is konam for me' — he can nullify it, because she may one day need that person's help. This is affliction.
Gemara (Objection)
But the mishna says: 'Produce of this country is konam for me' — he cannot nullify! She forbade benefit from others' produce, yet he can't nullify?
Rav Yosef
The mishna means she said: 'that you bring' — she only forbade produce her husband brings. She can still get it herself or from others.
Principle The direction of the vow matters: forbidding her benefit to others is not the husband's domain, but forbidding others' benefit to her is affliction he can nullify.

Produce & Storekeeper Vows — Resolving the Objections

Vow FormulationCan Husband Nullify?Reason
'Produce of this country is konam for me'NoHe can bring produce from another country — no affliction
'Produce that you bring from this country'NoShe can get it herself or from others — no affliction
'Produce of this storekeeper is konam for me'No (per R' Yosei)Other storekeepers exist — not considered affliction
Same vow, but husband can only buy from that storekeeperYesWithout this store, she would be deprived — this is affliction
'Benefit from so-and-so is konam for me'Yes (per Shmuel)She may need that person someday — potential affliction
Resolution The Gemara ultimately concludes: the first clause of the mishna refers to a case where the woman forbids all produce from that source (even what she brings herself), and the mishna follows R' Yosei, while Shmuel follows the Rabbis.

Two Loaves: Can Partial Affliction Extend Nullification?

Shmuel
If a woman vows that two loaves are forbidden to her — one fine-quality (abstaining = deprivation) and one poor-quality (abstaining ≠ deprivation) — since he can nullify for the fine loaf, he can also nullify for the poor loaf.
R' Yochanan
He can nullify only for the fine loaf that causes actual deprivation. He cannot nullify for the poor loaf — it does not constitute affliction.
Rav Asi
(Second version) I posed this as a dilemma to R' Yochanan: What is the halakha regarding the two loaves?
R' Yochanan
He nullifies only regarding the loaf that would cause deprivation, not the other. Each item in the vow is assessed independently.
Dispute Shmuel: Nullification of part extends to the whole vow. R' Yochanan: Each component is evaluated separately — only items causing affliction can be nullified.
