Nedarim 43: Navigating Prohibited Benefit

Core Question: When benefit is forbidden between two people, what transactions are prohibited and what workarounds exist?
↓
Direct Dealings
Lending, borrowing, buying, selling — all prohibited (some by decree)
Indirect Methods
Via shopkeeper, laborers, third party, or hefker declaration
Hefker Dispute
Rabbanan: Permitted
R' Yosei: Prohibited — artifice like Beit Choron
Key Theme The Gemara explores the boundaries between genuine workarounds and prohibited artifice when navigating vows of forbidden benefit.
3 / 9

Why Is Borrowing FROM Him Also Prohibited?

Gemara's Question
Granted, the muddar (one from whom benefit is forbidden) can't lend — that benefits the other. But why can't he borrow? How does the borrower benefit the lender?
Gemara's Question
And even if borrowing money benefits the lender (preserving coin value), and buying helps the seller — why can't he borrow an item? He returns the same item!
R' Yosei b'R' Chanina
The Mishna refers to where both vowed against each other (שניהם נדרו). So naturally neither can lend or borrow.
Abaye
Even with a one-sided vow, the Sages decreed against borrowing from him — lest he come to lend to him in return. Reciprocity is common.
Principle Abaye: All the Mishna's prohibitions on the muddar (borrowing money, items, purchasing) are rabbinic decrees — a fence lest he come to benefit the prohibited party.

Konam on Plowing: Intent Determines Scope

Person asks to borrow a cow. Owner refuses: "It's not available."
Borrower vows: "Plowing my field with this cow is konam forever!"
↓
Does this person typically plow his own field himself?
Yes — Plows Himself
Prohibited for him to plow with that cow.
✅ Others may plow his field with it.
No — Others Plow for Him
Prohibited for him AND everyone else to plow his field with that cow.
His intent was to forbid all benefit of plowing with this cow.
Key Distinction The scope of the vow follows the person's typical manner (דרכו) — it reveals whether he meant to prohibit only himself or all benefit from that action.

Workaround: The Shopkeeper & Laborer Method

🚫 The muddar (one for whom benefit is forbidden) has no food / needs his house built / field harvested
↓
🗣️ The madir (one from whom benefit is forbidden) goes to the shopkeeper or laborers
↓
💬 He says: "So-and-so vowed that benefit from me is forbidden — I don't know what to do"
↓
🍞 The shopkeeper gives food / laborers do the work for the muddar
↓
💰 The shopkeeper/laborers then collect payment from the madir who spoke to them
↓
✅ This is permitted — the benefit comes from the third party, not directly from the madir
Note The madir never directly instructs or hires the shopkeeper — he merely states his predicament. The shopkeeper acts on his own understanding.

Traveling Together: Two Methods of Providing Food

Method 1: Third Party

  • Another person is present on the road
  • Madir gives food to the third party as a gift
  • Third party may then give it to the muddar
  • ✅ Permitted — food no longer belongs to the madir
  • Accepted by all opinions

Method 2: Hefker (Ownerless)

  • No third party available
  • Madir places food on a rock or fence
  • Declares: "These are hefker for anyone"
  • Muddar takes the food
  • ⚠️ R' Yosei prohibits this method
Dispute The Rabbanan permit the hefker method. R' Yosei considers it a form of artifice — but what is his precise reasoning?

Why Does R' Yosei Prohibit the Hefker Method?

R' Yochanan
R' Yosei holds hefker is like a gift (הפקר כמתנה) — ownership doesn't leave the original owner until someone acquires it. So the muddar benefits from the madir's property!
R' Abba
Objection! R' Yosei himself distinguishes: if hefker preceded the vow — it's permitted! If hefker = gift, it shouldn't matter which came first — the item is still in the owner's possession either way.
R' Abba
He answers his own objection: When hefker predates the vow, the intent of the vower excludes already-ownerless items. The distinction relates to vow interpretation, not hefker mechanics.
Rava
Another objection from the shechiv mera case: property gifted partially leaves the giver's possession even before the recipient acquires it — contradicting R' Yochanan's principle!
Rava
Rather, R' Yosei's reason is a rabbinic decree due to the incident at Beit Choron (מעשה דבית חורון) — where someone used artifice with a gift to circumvent a vow, and the Sages invalidated it.
Resolution Rava: R' Yosei doesn't hold hefker = gift conceptually. Rather, declaring food hefker for a specific person is transparent artifice, prohibited by decree after the Beit Choron incident.
