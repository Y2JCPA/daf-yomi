From Torah Text to Binding Intimations

Torah writes 'nazir lehazir' — a doubled term (Numbers 6:2)
↓
Derive: Intimations of nazirite vows = nazirite vows
Substitutes for nazirite vows = nazirite vows
↓
Nazirite vows are juxtaposed to general vows in the same verse
Nazirite → Vows
Intimations of general vows are binding like vows
Vows → Nazirite

Core Mechanism The doubled expression 'nazir lehazir' is the textual hook for the entire halakhic framework of intimations (yadot), which the Gemara then extends to all vows through juxtaposition (hekkesh).

Why Does the Mishna List Substitutes Before Intimations?

Questioner
If the Tanna cherishes intimations (since they're derived by exegesis), why not teach them first?
Gemara
The Tanna begins with substitutes (kinuyim) because they are written in the Torah — vows are valid in any language.
Questioner
That works if substitutes are foreign-language terms. But if they are terms the Sages invented, they're novel too!
Gemara
The Mishna is actually incomplete (ḥassura meḥasra) — once you insert the missing phrase, you can place intimations first: 'All intimations are like vows, all substitutes are like vows…'
Key Point The Gemara resolves the ordering problem by declaring the Mishna text incomplete and reconstructing it with intimations listed first.

The 'Language of Men' Debate

Torah Spoke in Language of Men

  • 'Lindor neder' is just normal speech — no exegetical meaning
  • Intimations derived from JUXTAPOSITION to nazirite vows
  • 'Nazir lehazir' teaches intimations of nazirite vows
  • Alternative: derives from 'all that proceeds from his mouth' (Num. 30:3)

Torah Does NOT Speak in Language of Men

  • 'Lindor neder' is a MEANINGFUL doubled term
  • Derives intimations of general vows DIRECTLY from this phrase
  • 'Nazir lehazir' teaches naziriteship stacks upon naziriteship
  • Juxtaposition teaches additional halakhot (profanation, delay, nullification)
Practical Difference Both sides agree intimations are binding — the debate is about which verse teaches it and what role the juxtaposition plays.

How Does 'He Shall Not Profane' Apply to a Nazirite?

Questioner
Once someone says 'I am a nazirite,' he's bound immediately. If he eats grapes, he violates 'he shall not eat.' If he drinks wine, 'he shall not drink.' When does profanation ever apply?
Rava
It applies in addition to the specific prohibition. If he drinks wine, he violates two prohibitions: 'he shall not drink' AND 'he shall not profane his word.'
Questioner
And 'You shall not delay' — when does that apply? He's a nazirite immediately!
Rava
When he says: 'I will not depart this world until I become a nazirite.' Since he doesn't know when he'll die, he's effectively a nazirite right now — like a man who gives his wife a bill of divorce effective one hour before his death.
Rava's Principle Since we must say 'perhaps he is about to die at any moment,' the naziriteship takes effect immediately and any delay constitutes a violation.
