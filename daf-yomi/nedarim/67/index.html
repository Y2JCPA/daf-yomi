Nedarim 67: Partnership in Nullification

CORE PRINCIPLE A betrothed young woman's (נערה מאורסה) vows can only be nullified through the joint action of both her father and her betrothed husband — neither can act alone. The Gemara derives this from Numbers 30:7–9 and systematically rejects alternatives.

Why Does the Mishna Repeat Itself?

Gemara
The mishna says: If the father nullified but the husband did not, or vice versa, the vow stands. Isn't this just restating the first clause — 'her father and husband nullify her vows'?
Answer
The repetition is necessary — the first clause could mean either her father or her husband can nullify. The second clause clarifies: both must act together.
Gemara
And why add 'needless to say if one ratified (קיים) the vow'? If mere inaction prevents nullification, ratification is obvious!
Answer
It addresses a case where one ratified, then sought dissolution (שאלה) of the ratification from a sage. You might think the vow is now gone — so the mishna teaches: both must still actively nullify.
CHIDDUSH Even if ratification is dissolved by a halakhic authority, the vow is not automatically nullified — active joint nullification is still required.

Biblical Verses: Betrothed vs. Married

Verse (Numbers 30)Applies ToWhat It Teaches
"If she be to a husband" (30:7–9)Betrothed woman (מאורסה)Father and husband jointly nullify her vows
"If she vowed in her husband's house" (30:11)Married woman (נשואה)Husband alone nullifies her vows
"In her father's house in her youth" (30:4–6)Unbetrothed daughterFather alone nullifies her vows
DERIVATION Rabba: Since 30:11 covers married women, the earlier verses (30:7–9) must refer to a betrothed woman — hence the joint nullification rule.

Father Alone vs. Husband Alone vs. Together

Father Alone

  • Can nullify unbetrothed daughter's vows
  • Cannot nullify betrothed daughter's vows alone
  • His ratification blocks husband from nullifying

Betrothed Husband

  • Cannot nullify alone — needs father's partnership
  • Cannot nullify vows predating the betrothal
  • Weaker authority than a fully married husband

Both Together

  • Joint nullification required for betrothed woman
  • Either one's ratification blocks the other
  • Dissolution of ratification does not equal nullification
PRINCIPLE The betrothed occupies a middle status — still in her father's domain but now connected to a husband, requiring dual consent.

Can the Betrothed Nullify Alone?

Gemara
Perhaps the betrothed can nullify alone, and the father is mentioned only to teach that his ratification blocks nullification?
Gemara
If so, why does the Torah write 'If she vowed in her husband's house' (30:11)? A married husband's power could be derived a fortiori (קל וחומר) — if a betrothed nullifies alone even with a father present, surely a husband can when the father has no authority!
Gemara
Perhaps 30:11 teaches that a husband cannot nullify pre-betrothal vows?
Gemara
That would imply a betrothed CAN nullify pre-betrothal vows — but a fully married man has greater authority! This is unreasonable.
Conclusion
Therefore: The betrothed cannot nullify alone — his power exists only through partnership with the father.
QAL VA'CHOMER The kal va'chomer (a fortiori) argument proves the betrothed cannot act independently — otherwise the married husband's verse would be superfluous.
