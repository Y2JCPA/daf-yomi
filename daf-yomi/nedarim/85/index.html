Thief of Untithed Produce: Who Is Penalized?

R' Yehuda HaNasi
The thief is penalized — he must repay the full value of all the untithed produce, including the teruma and tithes portion.
R' Yosei b.R. Yehuda
The owner is penalized — he receives only the value of the non-sacred portion. This encourages him to separate tithes promptly.
Gemara
Both agree that tovat hana'ah (benefit of discretion) has no monetary value. The dispute is about whom the Sages chose to penalize.
R' Yehuda HaNasi
Penalize the thief so he won't steal again — even though the owner had no monetary rights in the teruma.
R' Yosei b.R. Yehuda
Penalize the owner so he won't delay separating tithes — had he acted promptly, the gifts wouldn't have been stolen.
Key Principle The penalty's target depends on whom the Sages wish to incentivize: deterring theft vs. encouraging prompt tithing.

Rava's Resolution: Teruma Is Different

תרומה שאני — דכיון דאתא לאסורה עלייהו, שוויה כעפרא
Rava When a person forbids all priests from benefiting from him, the teruma — which only priests can eat — becomes like mere dust. Since it is now forbidden to every possible consumer, the owner has effectively abandoned it. Therefore priests who take it derive no benefit from him, and there is no violation of the vow.

Mishna: Wife's Vow on Her Labor

Cannot Be Nullified

  • "I won't produce for my father"
  • "I won't produce for your father"
  • "I won't produce for my/your brother"
  • Does not affect husband-wife relationship
  • Husband has no authority to nullify

Automatically Void

  • "I won't produce for you (husband)"
  • She is obligated to work for him per ketubah
  • First Tanna: No nullification needed
  • Vow cannot override existing obligation

Should Still Nullify

  • R' Akiva: She may produce surplus beyond her obligation — vow applies to the excess
  • R' Yochanan b. Nuri: He may divorce her — then the vow takes full effect and he can never remarry her
Halakha Shmuel rules like R' Yochanan ben Nuri: the husband should nullify the vow as a precaution against future divorce.

Resolving Shmuel: Konamot vs. Hekdesh

Rav Yosef
Konamot are different from hekdesh. Since a person can prohibit another's produce to himself via konam (which he cannot do with hekdesh), he can also prohibit future entities.
Abaye
That logic doesn't hold! Granted, one can prohibit another's produce to himself. But here the wife prohibits her own future produce to another person (her husband). She has jurisdiction over neither the produce (it doesn't exist) nor the person.
Rav Huna b.R. Yehoshua
The case is where she says: 'My hands are consecrated to the One Who made them.' Her hands already exist — so there is no problem of consecrating a future entity!
Gemara
But her hands are pledged to her husband! How can she consecrate them?
Rav Huna b.R. Yehoshua
She stipulated: the vow takes effect when I become divorced. At that point, her hands are no longer pledged to him.
Resolution By formulating the vow as 'my hands are consecrated — effective upon divorce,' both problems are solved: the object exists now, and it won't be pledged to the husband when the vow activates.
