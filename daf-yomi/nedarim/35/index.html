Konam on Cow or Spade — When Does it Expire?

Questioner
A man says: 'Every cow I own other than this one is konam for you.' He owns other cows — they are forbidden during his lifetime.
Baraita
If the one who vowed dies, or the cow is given as a gift to the subject of the vow — it is permitted.
Rav Aḥa b"R Ika
The gift case means it was given by a third party — the owner sold it to someone else, who then gave it to the subject of the vow.
Rav Ashi
The language confirms this: it says 'that was given to him' (passive) — not 'that he gave it to him' (active). A third party gave the gift.
Principle A konam on property is tied to the owner's possession. Once property leaves the owner — via death or transfer to a third party — the prohibition lifts.

Me'ilah (Misuse of Consecrated Property) in Konamot

Rava's Dilemma to Rav Naḥman
Does me'ilah apply to items forbidden by konam?
↓
Rav Naḥman: Learn from Mishna 33a — benefit from returning a lost item falls to hekdesh.
Konam = like consecrated property → me'ilah applies
↓
This is a Tannaitic dispute:
R' Meir
Me'ilah applies
If loaf is konam 'to all' → whoever eats it commits me'ilah.
If konam 'to me' → only the one who vowed commits me'ilah.
Rabbanan
No me'ilah in konamot — neither for the one who vowed nor for others.
Nafka Mina According to R' Meir, a universal konam ('to all') has the full status of hekdesh, including redemption. A personal konam ('to me') does not allow redemption.

Who Commits Me'ilah When a Forbidden Loaf Is Gifted?

Rav Aḥa b"R Avya
If one says 'My loaf is konam for you' and gives it as a gift — who commits me'ilah?
Rav Aḥa b"R Avya
The giver? But the loaf isn't forbidden to him! The receiver? He can say: 'I wanted a permitted item, not a forbidden one!'
Rav Ashi
The receiver commits me'ilah when he uses the loaf. The rule is: anyone who uses consecrated property thinking it is non-sacred commits me'ilah.
Key Rule Me'ilah occurs at the moment of use, not receipt. The receiver's mistaken belief that the item is permitted is precisely the condition for me'ilah — unwitting use of the sacred.

Mishna: What a Mudar Hana'ah May Do for the Subject of the Vow

Permitted

  • Separate terumah and tithes (with owner's consent)
  • Sacrifice bird-nests of zavim, zavot, and women after childbirth
  • Sacrifice sin-offerings and guilt-offerings
  • Teach midrash, halakhot, and aggadot
  • Teach the vower's children Bible

Forbidden

  • Teach the vower himself Bible
  • Provide direct financial benefit
  • Act as the vower's personal agent for offerings (disputed)
Why Bible Is Different Teaching Bible provides direct livelihood benefit (a paid profession), while midrash, halakhot, and aggadot may not be taught for pay.

Are Priests Our Agents or Agents of Heaven?

If Priests Are Our AgentsIf Priests Are Agents of Heaven
They act on our behalf — like a shaliachThey serve God directly — we are incidental
A mudar hana'ah cannot have the priest sacrifice for him — the priest provides him benefitA mudar hana'ah can have the priest sacrifice for him — no human agency involved
Requires the owner's knowledge and intentCan function without the owner's da'at
Proof from Mishna seems to refute this......but the Gemara deflects the proof
Resolution? The Gemara does not resolve the dilemma. The Mishna's cases (zavim, zavot, women after childbirth) are all mechusar kaparah — offerings that don't require the owner's intent — so they prove nothing about general priestly agency.
