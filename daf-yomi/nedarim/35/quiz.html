QUESTION 1 OF 5
If a man vows his cow is konam for another person, and then a third party gives that cow to the subject of the vow, what is the status of the cow?
A. Forbidden — the konam follows the object permanently
B. Permitted — the konam lifted when it left the owner's possession
C. Forbidden — unless the third party also made a kinyan
D. Depends on whether the owner intended to circumvent the vow
Correct: B — Rav Aḥa son of Rav Ika explains that when the property leaves the owner's possession (via a third party), the konam prohibition no longer applies. The Gemara confirms this from the passive language of the baraita.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to the Rabbanan (Sages), what is the status of me'ilah (misuse of consecrated property) in konamot?
A. Me'ilah applies fully, just like hekdesh
B. Me'ilah applies only to universal konamot ('to all')
C. There is no liability for me'ilah in konamot at all
D. Me'ilah applies only to the one who made the vow
Correct: C — The Rabbanan hold that there is no liability for misuse of consecrated property in cases of konamot — neither for universal vows nor personal vows. R' Meir disagrees and holds me'ilah does apply.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does the Mishna specifically list bird-offerings of zavim, zavot, and women after childbirth, rather than simply saying 'he may sacrifice offerings for him'?
A. These are the only offerings a mudar hana'ah may bring
B. These are offerings of mechusar kaparah which don't require the owner's intent
C. Bird-offerings are cheaper and thus less of a benefit
D. These are the only offerings where women are involved
Correct: B — R' Yoḥanan teaches that offerings of those lacking atonement (mechusar kaparah) don't require the owner's knowledge and intent. Since no agency is needed, even if priests are 'our agents,' they can still sacrifice these specific offerings for someone from whom they are forbidden to benefit.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
When someone receives a loaf that is konam for them and unknowingly eats it, who is liable for me'ilah according to Rav Ashi?
A. The giver, because he caused the transgression
B. The receiver, because me'ilah occurs through unwitting use of the sacred
C. Both are liable equally
D. Neither is liable since the receiver did not intend to accept a forbidden item
Correct: B — Rav Ashi rules that the receiver is liable for me'ilah at the moment he uses the loaf. The principle of me'ilah specifically applies when someone uses consecrated property thinking it is non-sacred — which is exactly the receiver's situation.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to R' Yehuda, why does a husband bring offerings on behalf of his wife who is an imbecile (shotah)?
A. Because she cannot form intent for the offering
B. Because the Torah specifically commands husbands to bring their wives' offerings
C. Because he writes in the ketubah that her financial obligations are upon him
D. Because a shotah is exempt from all mitzvot
Correct: C — R' Yehuda teaches that the husband writes in the ketubah: 'All your financial obligations are upon me.' This includes his wife's obligatory offerings, regardless of her halakhic competence — even bringing a wealthy person's offering if he is wealthy.
