Does a Vow Reference an Item's Original or Current Status?

Core Question
When one vows 'like this offering' after the sprinkling of blood, does the vow attach to the original forbidden status or the current permitted status?
Proof Attempts
The Gemara brings multiple proofs — from notar/piggul, the neder issar baraita, and the teruma of thanks-offering loaves — each refuted in turn.
Rava
The mishna's mention of notar and piggul proves one refers to the original forbidden status of the offering.
Rav Huna b. Rav Natan
Perhaps the mishna speaks of a burnt-offering (olah), whose meat remains forbidden even after blood-sprinkling.
KEY ISSUE The question is never fully resolved on this daf — every proof is deflected, leaving the matter open.

Notar & Piggul: Original or Current Status?

Rava
Notar and piggul apply after sprinkling of blood, when me'ilah (misuse of consecrated property) no longer applies. Yet the vow takes effect — proving one references the original forbidden status!
Rav Huna b. Rav Natan
Perhaps we're speaking of notar of a burnt-offering (olah). Since olah meat may never be eaten, me'ilah still applies even after sprinkling.
Rava
If so, the mishna should have explicitly stated it refers to a burnt-offering!
Gemara
The tanna uses the style of lo tzricha — it's obvious for olah meat; the novelty is that even notar/piggul of an olah creates a valid vow, lest you think he meant the issur of notar itself.
LO TZRICHA A common Talmudic teaching style: 'It is not necessary to state X; the novelty is Y.'

The Neder Issar — Vow of Prohibition

A person says: 'I will not eat meat today like the day my father died'
↓
Shmuel's condition: He must have been obligated by a prior vow on that referenced day
↓
Gemara's Attempted Proof
It was a Sunday — many permitted Sundays passed — yet the vow works. He must reference the original status!
Gemara's Refutation
Shmuel meant he was continuously obligated every anniversary. So the vow references the current status of the day.
EXAMPLES The baraita lists: day father/teacher died, Fast of Gedaliah (Tzom Gedaliah), or the day one saw Jerusalem destroyed.

Teruma of Aaron vs. Teruma of Thanks-Offering

Teruma of Aaron (Ḥalla/Teruma)

  • Forbidden to non-priests by Torah law
  • Considered an issur Torah, not an issur neder
  • Declaring food 'like teruma of Aaron' → PERMITTED
  • Cannot base a vow on a Torah prohibition

Teruma of Thanks-Offering Loaves

  • Designated only after sprinkling of blood
  • Ravina argues: vow after sprinkling must reference original status
  • Gemara refutes: perhaps the mishna's 'his teruma' includes this case
  • Alternative: teruma can be separated during kneading, before sprinkling
RAVINA'S PROOF — DEFLECTED The Gemara shows the thanks-offering teruma can be designated before blood-sprinkling (during kneading), so no proof about original vs. current status.

How Can Teruma Be Separated During Kneading?

🍞 Ideally, 40 loaves are baked for the thanks-offering (10 of each of 4 types)
↓
📏 Rav Tovi bar Kisna / Shmuel: If one baked only 4 loaves (1 per type), he fulfills his obligation
↓
❓ Problem: He must give teruma (1 complete loaf per type) to the kohen — but he only has 1 of each!
↓
🫓 Solution: He separates teruma during kneading — taking 1 piece of dough from each type
↓
🔥 He then bakes the remaining dough into 4 loaves
↓
✅ Since teruma was separated before sprinkling, no proof about original vs. current status
HALACHA The verse (Leviticus 7:14) requires: one complete loaf from each type — not a slice, and not from one type for another.
