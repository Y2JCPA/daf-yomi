Nedarim 88 — Gifts, Acquisition & a Wife's Legal Hand

DAF OVERVIEW This daf transitions from the exegetical debate on blind persons and exile to a Mishna about circumventing a vow against one's son-in-law when giving a gift to one's daughter. The Gemara explores whether a wife has independent acquisition rights or whether her hand is like her husband's hand, with major implications for eiruv law and stipulated gifts. The daf closes with a Mishna on the binding force of a widow's or divorcee's vows.
3 / 9

"Without Seeing" — Include or Exclude the Blind?

Rava
There is no contradiction — each Tanna derives from the context of the verse differently.
R' Yehuda
"Into the forest" already includes a blind person (he can enter a forest). So "without seeing" must exclude him from exile.
R' Meir
"Without knowledge" already excludes a blind person (he cannot know who stands nearby). So "without seeing" must include him in exile.
KEY PRINCIPLE When a phrase seems redundant, it must teach the opposite of what was already derived — either inclusion or exclusion.

Mishna: Giving a Gift Despite a Vow Against the Son-in-Law

Father vowed that his son-in-law may not benefit from him
↓
But he wants to give money to his daughter
↓
Problem: Whatever a wife acquires belongs to her husband
↓
Solution: Say — "This gift is given on condition your husband has no rights to it; only what you pick up and place in your mouth"
STIPULATION The father must explicitly restrict the gift so the son-in-law cannot acquire it through his wife.
5 / 9

Rav vs. Shmuel: "Do As You Please"

Rav

  • The Mishna's rule applies only if father said: "what you pick up and place in your mouth"
  • If he said "do as you please" — stipulation fails
  • Husband acquires the money

Shmuel

  • Even if the father said "do as you please"
  • The husband still does not acquire
  • The stipulation against the husband's rights remains effective
CORE DISPUTE Does a broad stipulation ("do as you please") effectively block the husband's automatic acquisition of his wife's gifts?

R' Zeira Challenges Rav: Is a Wife's Hand Like Her Husband's?

R' Zeira
Rav follows R' Meir who says a wife's hand is like her husband's — she has no independent acquisition.
R' Zeira
But the Mishna in Eiruvin (anonymous = R' Meir) says a wife can acquire eiruv food from her husband on behalf of others!
Rava
R' Meir concedes for eiruv: since she acquires for others, not herself, it leaves the husband's domain.
DISTINCTION Acquiring for oneself vs. acquiring on behalf of others — the latter is effective even under R' Meir's principle.

Who Can & Cannot Acquire Eiruv on Behalf of Others?

Can Acquire ✓Cannot Acquire ✗
Adult son or daughterMinor son or daughter
Hebrew slave or maidservantCanaanite slave or maidservant
Wife (per Mishna Eiruvin)Wife (per Baraita — Ravina's challenge)
RESOLUTION — RAV ASHI The Mishna in Eiruvin refers to a wife who owns her own courtyard in the alleyway — she acquires the eiruv for herself via her courtyard, and thereby for others too.
