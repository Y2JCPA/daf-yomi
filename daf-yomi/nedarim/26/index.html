When Is a Partially Dissolved Vow Fully Dissolved?

Rabba
The dispute is when one says: 'So-and-so and so-and-so are forbidden, and father is permitted.' But if one says 'All of you except father,' all agree only father is excluded.
Rava
The opposite! All agree that 'so-and-so and so-and-so forbidden, father permitted' releases everyone. The dispute is only when one says 'All of you except father.'
Beit Shammai
Follow Rabbi Meir: Hold by the first expression. 'All of you are forbidden' stands — 'except father' merely clarifies father's exclusion.
Beit Hillel
Follow Rabbi Yosei: The conclusion of one's statement is primary. Altering the formulation to exclude father means the vow is partially dissolved — and thus fully dissolved.
Core Principle נדר שהותר מקצתו הותר כולו — A vow that is partially dissolved is dissolved completely (Rabbi Akiva's rule, per Beit Hillel).

Rabba vs Rava: Where Is the Dispute?

Rabba's View

  • All agree: 'All of you except father' → only father is permitted
  • Dispute: 'So-and-so and so-and-so forbidden, father permitted'
  • Beit Shammai (R' Meir): First expression governs — all forbidden
  • Beit Hillel (R' Yosei): Partial dissolution → all permitted

Rava's View

  • All agree: 'So-and-so and so-and-so forbidden, father permitted' → all permitted
  • Dispute: 'All of you except father'
  • Beit Shammai (R' Meir): First expression — 'all of you' — stands
  • Beit Hillel (R' Yosei): Partial dissolution → all permitted
Key Difference The two Amoraim agree on the underlying logic (R' Meir = first expression, R' Yosei = conclusion), but disagree about which formulation triggers the dispute.

Rav Pappa Challenges Rava from Rabbi Akiva's Mishna

Rav Pappa
Rabbi Akiva's mishna distinguishes: If one says 'the property of all of you is konam' and one is permitted → all are permitted. But if he says 'this one and that one' and the last is permitted → only the last is permitted!
Rav Pappa
According to Rabba, this works: the first clause = 'this one and that one' (Beit Hillel dispute), the latter clause = 'all of you except one' (all agree). But Rava — you say all agree when one says 'this one and that one' that everyone is permitted. Then who disagrees with Rabbi Akiva in the latter clause?
Rava
And does it work for Rabba either? If the latter clause is 'from all of you' — who is 'first' and who is 'last'? You can't distinguish first from last in a general statement about everyone!
Rava's Counter Rava turns the objection around: the concept of 'first' and 'last' only makes sense if the people are individually enumerated, not collectively addressed.

Rava's Reinterpretation of Rabbi Akiva's Mishna

First clause: He said 'From all of you'
↓
One person's prohibition dissolved
↓
Partial dissolution → All permitted (Beit Hillel / R' Akiva)
↓
Latter clause: He linked them: 'So-and-so like so-and-so, and so-and-so like so-and-so'
↓
First in Chain Permitted
All linked after him are also permitted
Last in Chain Permitted
Only the last is permitted; those before remain forbidden
Middle Permitted
From him and below = permitted; from him and above = forbidden
Proof A baraita supports this: if the middle one in the chain is permitted, those mentioned after him are permitted and those before him remain forbidden — confirming the linked-chain model.

The Linked Chain of Prohibitions

🔗 Vow-maker says: 'Reuven is forbidden to partake'
↓
🔗 'Shimon should be forbidden like Reuven'
↓
🔗 'Levi should be forbidden like Shimon'
↓
🔗 'Yehuda should be forbidden like Levi'
↓
✅ If Shimon's prohibition is dissolved → Levi and Yehuda are also permitted
↓
🚫 But Reuven (mentioned before Shimon) remains forbidden
Principle In a linked chain, dissolving one link breaks the chain downstream but leaves the upstream links intact.

The Kuferi Onion Incident

ElementDetail
Original Vow'Onions are konam for me because they are bad for the heart'
New InformationThe kuferi (כופרי) onion is actually good for the heart
RulingRabbi Meir dissolved the vow for all types of onions
Objection (Rav Adda bar Ahava)If he said 'all onions except kuferi' — shouldn't only kuferi be permitted?
ResolutionHe said: 'Such-and-such onion forbidden, kuferi permitted' — the 'this one and that one' formulation
ResultAll agree: when one enumerates individuals and excludes one, all are permitted
🧅 Why It Matters This case is explained as the formulation where all opinions agree that everything is permitted — even Rabbi Meir, who normally follows the first expression.
