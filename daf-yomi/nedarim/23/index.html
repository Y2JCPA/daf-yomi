Nedarim 23: Dissolving Vows & Preemptive Annulment

Problem: A person has made a vow that needs dissolution
↓
After the Fact
Sages search for an opening (petaḥ) — a fact the vower didn't consider
Preemptive
On Rosh HaShana, declare: 'All my future vows are void'
↓
Key Condition: The opening must not be nolad (a truly new, unforeseeable situation)
↓
Vow is dissolved — but methods should not be publicized lest people treat vows lightly
Key Theme The Gemara balances the need to release people from burdensome vows with the concern that vows retain their seriousness.

The Sages Struggle to Dissolve a Vow

🧑‍⚖️ A man comes before the Sages to dissolve his vow
↓
❓ They ask: 'Did you vow knowing this fact?' — He says: 'Yes'
↓
☀️➡️🌥️ They move from sun to shade and shade to sun, unable to find an opening
↓
💡 Botnit asks: 'Would you have vowed knowing the Sages would be so troubled?'
↓
🚫 He says 'No' — and they dissolve it based on his regret over troubling the Sages
Insight The physical detail of moving sun-to-shade became itself the basis for dissolution — the vower's concern for the Sages' discomfort served as a valid opening.

The Launderer Incident: Is This Nolad?

Narrative
R' Yishmael b. R' Yosei came to dissolve a vow. Every opening the Sages proposed, he answered 'Yes, I knew that.'
Narrative
A launderer, frustrated by the delay, struck R' Yishmael with his tool.
R' Yishmael
Had I known the launderer would hit me, I would not have vowed! — Vow dissolved.
Rav Aḥa of Difti
This is nolad — a new, unforeseeable situation! We learned one may not use nolad to dissolve vows.
Ravina
It is not nolad. It is common to find appikurei (heretics) who trouble the Sages. Being attacked was foreseeable in general, even if not from this specific launderer.
Principle A situation is not considered nolad (new) if the general category of event was foreseeable, even if the specific instance was not.

Abaye's Vow About His Daughter's Marriage

Abaye's wife has a daughter from a previous marriage
↓
Abaye says: Marry my relative
Wife says: Marry my relative
↓
Abaye vows: 'Benefit from me is forbidden to you if you defy me'
↓
Wife defies him and marries the daughter to her relative
↓
Rav Yosef asks: 'Had you known she would defy you, would you have vowed?'
↓
Abaye: 'No' — Vow dissolved. He intended only a threat, not a real prohibition.
Tosefta Precedent A parallel case: A man vowed to prohibit his wife if she ascended to Jerusalem for the Festival. She defied him. R' Yosei dissolved the vow with the same reasoning.

Preemptive Annulment: Abaye vs. Rava

Abaye

  • The Rosh HaShana declaration works if he does NOT remember it at vow time
  • If he remembers the stipulation and still vows, he has uprooted the stipulation
  • Emend the Mishna: 'provided he does NOT remember'

Rava

  • Keep the Mishna as stated — it works even if he DOES remember
  • Key: He made a general stipulation but doesn't know which vows it covers
  • If he says 'I vow per my original intention' → vow is void
  • If he simply vows without referencing the stipulation → vow stands
Practical Difference According to Rava, the annulment works only if the vower actively invokes the original stipulation at the time of the vow. Merely remembering it is not enough — he must explicitly connect the vow to it.

Rava's Warning: Don't Teach This Publicly

תנא מסתים לה ולא אמר לה בהדיא... ואת אמרת לה בפירקא?
🤫 Concealed Teaching Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana planned to teach the method of preemptive vow annulment at the public Festival lecture.

Rava objected: 'The Tanna of our Mishna intentionally conceals this teaching — even though Torah scholars study it — so that the public will not treat vows lightly (zilzul bi-nedarim). And you want to teach it publicly?!'

This tension — between making halakhic tools accessible and preserving the gravity of vows — underlies the entire sugya.
