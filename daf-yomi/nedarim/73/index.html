Can a Husband Nullify Without Hearing the Vow?

Gemara
If a husband appoints a steward (shali'ach) to nullify — why not just nullify when he hears?
Answer
He reasons: Perhaps I will be preoccupied (tarud) at that moment and will forget to nullify.
Gemara
The question of nullification without hearing is left unresolved (teiku).
KEY POINT The Gemara cannot determine whether hearing is a formal requirement or merely the typical circumstance of nullification.

Can a Deaf Man Nullify His Wife's Vows?

Rami bar Ḥama asks: What about a cheresh (deaf husband)?
↓
R' Zeira's Principle:
If flour is suitable for mingling → mingling not indispensable
If flour is not suitable → mingling is indispensable
↓
Perhaps
Hearing is impossible for a deaf man → stricter standard applies → Cannot nullify
Or Perhaps
"Her husband hears" is not truly indispensable → Can nullify
↓
Rava resolves: Baraita — "Her husband hears it" excludes the wife of a deaf man
PRINCIPLE R' Zeira's rule: When a precondition is merely absent, it may be waived. When it is structurally impossible, it becomes indispensable.

Nullifying Vows for Two Wives Simultaneously

Sota Parallel

  • Two sota women are NOT given the bitter waters together
  • First Tanna: Each becomes emboldened (gas) by the other's presence
  • R' Yehuda: "He shall make HER drink" — singular, one at a time

Vow Nullification

  • Ravina applies the same logic from sota
  • "Disallows HER" (Numbers 30:9) — singular language
  • A husband cannot nullify vows for two wives simultaneously
METHOD Ravina uses a gezeirah shavah-style analogy: just as "her" in sota means one woman, "her" in vow nullification means one wife at a time.

Mishna: When Can a Betrothed Husband Nullify?

CategoryWaiting PeriodAfter Period Ends
Virgin (na'arah)12 months from betrothalHusband obligated to support her
Widow30 days from betrothalHusband obligated to support her
R' Eliezer—Husband can nullify vows alone (like full marriage)
Rabbis (Chakhamim)—Cannot nullify until she enters his jurisdiction (chuppah)
DISPUTE R' Eliezer ties nullification power to the obligation of support (mezonot). The Rabbis tie it to actual entry into the marriage.

Rabba and Abaye: Why Does R' Eliezer Rule This Way?

Rabba
R' Eliezer follows the initial mishna (Ketubot): once 12 months pass, she partakes of his food — treated as married.
Abaye
Not necessarily! The initial mishna only permits rabbinic-level teruma. Vows are Torah law — you can't compare!
Abaye
Perhaps R' Eliezer's reason is Rav Pineḥas in the name of Rava: a wife vows contingent on her husband's consent since he supports her.
Abaye
So R' Eliezer may grant nullification power over vows but still prohibit teruma — even rabbinic teruma!
ABAYE'S CHALLENGE You cannot assume R' Eliezer equates vow-nullification with teruma eligibility. His reason for vows may be entirely different — rooted in the wife's implicit consent.
