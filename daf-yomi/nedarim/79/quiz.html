Nedarim 79 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Gemara's conclusion, what happens if a husband is silent about his wife's vow in order to annoy her?
A. The vow is ratified and he can no longer nullify it
B. He can nullify it later since he didn't intend to ratify
C. The vow is suspended until he explicitly responds
D. A sage must decide whether the vow is valid
Correct: A — R' Chanina held that silence-to-annoy allowed later nullification, but this was conclusively refuted (teyuvta) through three separate objections. The conclusion is that any silence ratifies the vow.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the key difference between ratification and nullification regarding mental intent?
A. Neither can be performed mentally
B. Both can be performed mentally
C. Ratification in the heart is valid; nullification in the heart is not
D. Nullification in the heart is valid; ratification in the heart is not
Correct: C — The baraita teaches that if a husband ratified a vow in his heart, it is ratified. But if he nullified it in his heart, it is not nullified. This asymmetry favors ratification.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to the final answer in the Gemara, what is the practical difference between nullifying a vow of affliction and nullifying a vow affecting the marital relationship?
A. Affliction vows require a sage's approval; relationship vows do not
B. Affliction vows are nullified for the husband and all others; relationship vows only for the husband
C. Relationship vows can be nullified mentally; affliction vows cannot
D. Affliction vows are nullified temporarily; relationship vows permanently
Correct: B — The Gemara concludes that affliction vows are nullified universally — for the husband and any future husband. Relationship vows are nullified only with respect to the current husband; if she remarries, the vow takes effect with the new husband.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does Rava's objection from the Friday-before-Shabbat mishna refute R' Chanina?
A. Because Shabbat is holier than vows and overrides them
B. Because if silence-to-annoy allowed later nullification, refraining on Shabbat should also allow it — but it doesn't
C. Because vows cannot be made on Shabbat
D. Because the husband explicitly ratified the vow by accepting Shabbat
Correct: B — If R' Chanina were correct that silence-to-annoy doesn't count as ratification, then a husband who was silent out of respect for Shabbat (not intending to ratify) should be able to nullify after Shabbat. The mishna says he cannot, refuting R' Chanina.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yosei, when can a husband nullify his wife's vow prohibiting herself from a specific storekeeper's produce?
A. Always, since any food prohibition is affliction
B. Never, since she can obtain food elsewhere
C. Only if that storekeeper is his sole source of sustenance
D. Only if a sage determines the vow involves affliction
Correct: C — Rabbi Yosei holds that a vow is only one of affliction if the deprivation is inescapable. If the husband can obtain sustenance only from that particular storekeeper, then the vow truly causes affliction and can be nullified.
