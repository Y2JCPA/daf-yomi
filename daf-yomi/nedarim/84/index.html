Nedarim 84: Vows Against 'People' — Scope & Exceptions

A woman vows: "Benefit from me is forbidden to all people"
↓
Is her husband included in "people"?
Rav Naḥman: No
Husband is not generally included — but context matters
Rava's Challenge
Mishna implies husband is included (he must nullify his part)
↓
Even under such a vow, she may benefit from leket, shikḥah, pe'ah — matanot aniyyim
↓
What about poor man's tithe (ma'aser ani)?
Mishna: Omits it
Owner has tovas hana'ah (benefit of discretion) → forbidden
Baraita: Permits it
No discretion → she may take it freely
KEY CONCEPT Tovas hana'ah (טובת הנאה) — the benefit of choosing who receives your gifts — determines whether taking constitutes 'benefit from people.'
3 / 9

Is a Husband Included in 'People'?

Rava
Objection! The mishna says if a woman says "I am removed from the Jews," her husband must nullify his part — implying he IS included!
Rav Naḥman
Generally a husband is not included in 'people.' But here it's different — she clearly intends to include him.
Rava
How do you know her intent?
Rav Naḥman
She means to forbid what is permitted to her — intercourse with her husband. Relations with other men are already forbidden!
Gemara
If husband is included → he nullifies his part only (neder affecting relationship). If not → it's a neder of innui (affliction) and he nullifies entirely.
PRINCIPLE A vow is interpreted to apply to what the person could meaningfully prohibit — not what is already forbidden.
4 / 9

What Can She Benefit From Under the Vow?

✅ Permitted (Mishna)

  • Leket (gleanings) — fallen stalks during harvest
  • Shikḥah (forgotten sheaves) — sheaves left behind
  • Pe'ah (corner of field) — unharvested corner for the poor

❓ Disputed

  • Ma'aser Ani (poor man's tithe)
  • Mishna omits it → possibly forbidden
  • Baraita includes it → possibly permitted

❌ Forbidden

  • Any gift where the giver has tovas hana'ah
  • Benefit from any specific person
  • Anything that constitutes receiving from 'people'
WHY PERMITTED? Leket, shikḥah, and pe'ah have no owner discretion — any poor person may take them. No individual 'person' confers the benefit.

Poor Man's Tithe of Demai — R' Eliezer vs. Rabbis

Rav Yosef
The contradiction between mishna and baraita reflects a tannaitic dispute. The baraita follows Rabbi Eliezer; the mishna follows the Rabbis.
R' Eliezer
One need not designate ma'aser ani of demai by name. The owner has no benefit of discretion → the vow-taker may take it.
Rabbis
One must designate ma'aser ani by name — the owner does have discretion → it's forbidden to the vow-taker.
Rav Yosef
R' Eliezer holds the doubt about demai does not render produce tevel → no discretion. The Rabbis hold it does → owner has discretion.
DEMAI Demai (דמאי) — doubtfully tithed produce purchased from an am ha'aretz (one not careful about tithes). Its uncertain status drives this entire dispute.
6 / 9

Abaye's Challenge to Rav Yosef

Abaye: Perhaps everyone agrees demai uncertainty does render produce tevel!
↓
The dispute is about why an am ha'aretz would or wouldn't separate ma'aser ani
R' Eliezer's Logic
An am ha'aretz can declare his property ownerless (hefker), become 'poor,' and take the tithe himself — no real loss → he is assumed to have separated it
Rabbis' Logic
A person does not ordinarily declare property ownerless — he fears someone else will acquire it → amei ha'aretz are suspected of not tithing
↓
Abaye's objection: Rav Yosef's proof linking the disputes is inconclusive
ABAYE'S POINT Even if both sides agree demai is potentially tevel, they can still disagree about whether amei ha'aretz actually separate ma'aser ani — based on behavioral assumptions.
