QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does Rav Naḥman say that the case of 'I am removed from the Jews' is different from a general vow against 'people'?
A. Because it uses the word 'Jews' instead of 'people'
B. Because she clearly intends to forbid what is actually permitted to her — intercourse with her husband
C. Because the husband always nullifies all vows automatically
D. Because the phrase 'removed from the Jews' is a special legal formula
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman argues that while a husband is generally not included in 'people,' here her intent is clear — she wants to forbid something permitted (relations with her husband), not what is already forbidden (relations with others).
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the key factor that determines whether a poor person who vowed against benefit from people may take a particular gift?
A. Whether the gift is mentioned in the Torah
B. Whether the gift has kedushah (sanctity)
C. Whether the giver has tovas hana'ah (benefit of discretion) in choosing the recipient
D. Whether the poor person verbally requested the gift
Correct: C — The central principle of this daf is that if the giver has discretion over who receives the gift (tovas hana'ah), then taking it counts as benefit 'from a person.' Without such discretion, there is no identifiable benefactor.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rava resolve the contradiction between the mishna (which omits poor man's tithe) and the baraita (which permits it)?
A. He says the baraita is not authoritative
B. He distinguishes between tithe distributed in the house (owner has discretion) and at the threshing floor (no discretion)
C. He says both are referring to the same case but the baraita is more lenient
D. He says the mishna discusses a Torah-level vow while the baraita discusses a rabbinic vow
Correct: B — Rava elegantly resolves the contradiction without positing a tannaitic dispute: the mishna refers to ma'aser ani distributed at home ('you shall give' — discretion exists), while the baraita refers to distribution at the threshing floor ('you shall leave' — no discretion).
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Abaye, why does Rabbi Eliezer hold that amei ha'aretz are NOT suspected of failing to separate poor man's tithe?
A. Because poor man's tithe has no sanctity
B. Because an am ha'aretz can declare his property ownerless, become poor, and take the tithe himself — suffering no loss
C. Because the rabbis enacted a special leniency for demai
D. Because poor man's tithe is only a rabbinic obligation
Correct: B — Abaye explains R' Eliezer's reasoning: since an am ha'aretz could theoretically declare his property hefker (ownerless), become classified as poor, and reclaim the tithe — he loses nothing by separating it, so he is assumed to have done so.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the practical difference between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Rabbi Yosei son of Rabbi Yehuda regarding a thief who steals untithed produce?
A. R' Yehuda HaNasi says he is exempt; R' Yosei says he must pay
B. R' Yehuda HaNasi says he pays full value; R' Yosei says he pays only the value of the non-sacred (ḥullin) portion
C. R' Yehuda HaNasi says he returns the produce; R' Yosei says he pays money
D. R' Yehuda HaNasi says he pays double; R' Yosei says he pays the principal only
Correct: B — R' Yehuda HaNasi holds tovas hana'ah has monetary value, so the thief pays the full value of the tevel (including the terumah/tithe portions). R' Yosei b'R' Yehuda holds it has no monetary value, so the thief pays only for the ḥullin portion.
