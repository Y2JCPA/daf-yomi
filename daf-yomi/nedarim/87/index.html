Nedarim 87: Tearing, Timing, and Splitting Vows

DAF OVERVIEW This daf explores mistaken identity in keri'ah (garment-tearing) to illuminate when a husband's vow-nullification applies to the wrong person. It then establishes the landmark principle of toch kdei dibbur — that retracting within the time of a short greeting counts as continuous speech — with four famous exceptions. The Mishnah and Gemara then debate whether a compound vow can be partially upheld or nullified, and what level of knowledge a husband needs before the clock starts on his right to nullify.

Was the report specific (named a particular person)?
Specific report was wrong
Told 'your father died' — actually his son died
Has NOT fulfilled his obligation
Non-specific report
Told 'a relative died' — assumed father, was actually son
HAS fulfilled his obligation
PARALLEL Just as keri'ah for a specifically-named wrong person is invalid, a husband who nullifies a vow thinking it was his daughter's — when it was his wife's — has not effectively nullified it.

Rav Ashi's Alternative: Toch Kdei Dibbur

Rav Ashi
The baraitot about keri'ah don't contradict each other based on specific vs. non-specific reports.
Rav Ashi
Rather, the distinction is timing: Was the error discovered within toch kdei dibbur (the time to say shalom alekha rabbi)?
R' Shimon b. Pazi
In the name of R' Yehoshua b. Levi citing Bar Kappara: If one tore for a person who fainted (thinking they died), and the person then died within toch kdei dibbur — he has fulfilled his obligation.
Rav Ashi
If the person died after toch kdei dibbur — he has not fulfilled it, and must tear again.
KEY PRINCIPLE Toch kdei dibbur ke-dibbur dami — a retraction within this brief window is treated as continuous speech.

Four Exceptions to Toch Kdei Dibbur

ExceptionWhy No Retraction?
Blasphemy (megadef)The severity of cursing God's name cannot be undone
Idol worship (avodah zarah)Verbal acceptance of an idol as one's god takes immediate effect
Betrothal (kiddushin)Marriage creates an immediate legal bond
Divorce (gerushin)A get dissolves the marriage instantly
HALAKHAH The Gemara concludes: toch kdei dibbur ke-dibbur dami applies in ALL areas of halakhah except these four cases.

Partial Nullification of a Compound Vow

R' Yishmael
If she vowed: 'Figs and grapes are konam for me' — this is one vow. If the husband upheld regarding figs, the entire vow is upheld. If he nullified regarding figs, it's not nullified until he also nullifies regarding grapes.
R' Akiva
The verse says yekimennu / yeferennu — read as yakim mimmennu / yafer mimmennu — 'he may uphold part of it / nullify part of it.' Partial nullification works!
R' Yishmael
Is it actually written yafer mimmennu with a mem? No! The text only supports partial upholding, not partial nullification.
R' Akiva
The verse juxtaposes nullification to upholding — they must work the same way. Just as upholding is partial, so too nullification.
MISHNAH RULE The Mishnah follows R' Yishmael: a compound vow is a single unit — partial nullification is insufficient.

One Vow vs. Two Vows: The Formulation Matters

One Compound Vow

  • "Tasting these figs and grapes is konam for me"
  • Single declaration covering multiple items
  • Upholding part = upholding ALL
  • Must nullify ALL parts to nullify
  • Treated as one indivisible unit

Two Separate Vows

  • "Tasting a fig and tasting a grape are konam"
  • Repeats the key word for each item
  • Each item is an independent vow
  • Can uphold one and nullify the other
  • Rava: follows R' Shimon's logic on oaths
RAVA'S SOURCE R' Shimon holds that one is not liable for multiple false oaths in one statement unless he states a separate oath-formula for each — same logic applies here to separate vow-formulas.
