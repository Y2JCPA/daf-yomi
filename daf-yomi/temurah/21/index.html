🩸 The Cooked Blood Debate

Ze'eiri
Blood that one cooked: one does not transgress a prohibition by drinking it — it was disqualified from the altar and thus excluded from the blood prohibition.
Abaye
Objection from a baraita! Blood curdled by the sun — one is liable. Even sun-curdled blood, though disqualified from the altar, remains prohibited!
Rava
Not difficult: Ze'eiri speaks of blood curdled by fire — fully disqualified. The baraita speaks of blood curdled by the sun — still resembles liquid blood.
Abaye
Perhaps Ze'eiri discusses inner sin offering blood (disqualified from its specific altar), while the baraita addresses outer sin offering blood — still fit for the external altar.
Rava's Resolution Even inner sin offering blood carries liability — since blood corresponding to it is fit for placement on the external altar (Rav Pappa: therefore both types are forbidden).

⚖️ Blood Liability: Inner vs. Outer Sin Offerings

Outer Sin Offering Blood

  • Sprinkled on the external altar in the Temple courtyard
  • Congealed blood: liability for eating it — Rabbi Ḥisda
  • Verse: "And the priest shall take..." (Lev. 4:25) implies ongoing fitness
  • Blood remains suitable even when congealed

Inner Sin Offering Blood

  • Placed inside the Temple on the golden altar
  • Ze'eiri: fire-curdled — no prohibition
  • Rava: still liable — corresponding blood is fit for external altar
  • Rav Pappa: both types carry full liability

📜 Who Provides Salt for a Private Meal Offering?

Argument A
Compare salt to frankincense: both accompany the meal offering in one vessel — so the individual brings salt from home, just as he brings his own frankincense.
Argument B
Compare salt to wood: both apply to all offerings, not just meal offerings — so the Temple provides both from public funds, just as it provides wood.
Baraita
"An everlasting covenant of salt" (Num. 18:19) parallels "an everlasting covenant" of shewbread (Lev. 24:8). Just as shewbread comes from Temple funds — so too salt.
Ruling The Temple provides salt for all offerings from public funds. One who vows a meal offering need not bring his own salt — the Temple covenant covers it.
