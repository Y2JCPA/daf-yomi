🧂 Deriving Communal Salt & Wood

Salt: The Merciful One granted salt rights to Israelites — those obligated in half-shekel donations that fund the chamber
↓
Question: For wood, why is it obvious it must come from communal supplies?
↓
Baraita: One might have thought an individual could donate wood — a verse teaches otherwise
↓
Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua: Just as the altar was built solely for divine service and never used for mundane purposes, so too the wood must be virgin — never used by an ordinary person
↓
Conclusion: Both salt and wood derive from the communal fund supplied by the chamber

🪵 Araunah's Threshing Sledges

מוֹרִיגִים — לוּחַ הַדַּיִשׁ עִם שִׁינֵּי בַּרְזֶל
Challenge: Old Wood on the Altar? The Gemara challenges Rabbi Elazar: Is previously-used wood really unfit? Araunah offered David his morigim as wood for a burnt offering on the altar — yet this wood had clearly been used before! What are morigim? Ulla: a turbal bed. Rav Yehuda clarifies: a threshing board with sharp iron teeth on its underside, dragged over grain to separate kernels from stalks. Resolution: Royal donations or exceptional circumstances may override this rule — or the wood had not yet been put to mundane use.

🤲 Mishna: Intermingled Kometz — Fit or Unfit?

Tanna Kamma (Rabbis) — All Fit

  • Kometz + another Israelite's kometz ✓
  • Kometz + meal offering of priests ✓
  • Kometz + anointed High Priest's meal offering ✓
  • Kometz + meal offering accompanying libations ✓
  • All cases: the mixture may be burned on the altar

Rabbi Yehuda — Two Cases Unfit

  • Kometz + anointed priest's offering ✗
  • Kometz + meal offering of libations ✗
  • Reason: these are entirely consumed on the altar
  • The mixture's entire 'norm' is for the fire
  • Unfit — the mixture cannot be properly separated

⚖️ One Verse, Two Rulings — Rabbi Yoḥanan's Insight

Rabbi Yoḥanan
Remarkably, both the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda derive their opposing rulings from the same verse — the Yom Kippur blood rite, where bull's blood and goat's blood are combined after separate sprinklings.
The Rabbis
We learn: items that ascend the altar do not nullify one another — even if they are different types (bull ≠ goat). This is the controlling criterion.
Rabbi Yehuda
We learn: min b'mino — a substance mixed with the same type — is never nullified. Bull and goat blood are both 'blood of sacrificial offerings,' i.e., the same type.
Gemara Challenge
Perhaps both criteria are required simultaneously — same type AND altar-bound — before ruling no nullification? The Gemara rejects this: the verse's phrasing indicates each criterion operates independently.
The Source Verse (Leviticus 16) On Yom Kippur, the blood of the bull and the blood of the goat are each sprinkled separately, then combined — teaching they do not nullify each other in the mixture

🔥 When Is a Sacrificial Mixture Nullified?

Two substances are mixed together in a Temple context
↓
Do both items ascend the altar?
Rabbis: YES → Not nullified
Rabbis: NO → Standard rules (majority nullifies minority)
Are they the same type (min b'mino)?
Rabbi Yehuda: YES → Not nullified
Rabbi Yehuda: NO → Standard nullification applies
↓
Practical result: Kometz + kometz → fit under both views. Kometz + entirely-burned offering → fit per Rabbis, unfit per Rabbi Yehuda
