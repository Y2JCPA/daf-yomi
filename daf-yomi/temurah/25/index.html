⚔️ What Does the Tzitz Atone For?

Rabbi Zeira
Why not say the tzitz atones for offerings that left the courtyard? Their general prohibition was permitted — communal offerings are valid even if taken outside!
Abaye
The verse says 'that they may be accepted before the Lord' — the tzitz atones only for a sin whose general leniency occurs before the Lord, i.e., inside the Temple.
Rabbi Ile'a
Then why not say the tzitz atones for left-hand service? Its prohibition was permitted on Yom Kippur when the High Priest used his left hand for the incense!
Abaye
The verse says 'Aaron shall bear the sin committed with the sacred items' — tzitz atones for a sin done to the offering itself, not a procedural defect in service performance.
Rav Ashi
Also: hakodashim is plural — the tzitz atones for sins involving multiple holy items, not a single procedural act performed on one offering at a time.
Core Rule Tzitz atones for impurity of the offering itself, not procedural violations — and only where permitted 'before the Lord' inside the Temple.

⚖️ Tzitz Acceptance: Included vs. Excluded

✅ Tzitz Effects Acceptance

  • Handful of meal offering that became impure
  • Blood that became impure — sprinkled on altar
  • Flesh and fat of offering rendered impure in the Temple
  • Impure offering brought by a Jewish individual
  • Impurity contracted unwittingly (all agree)

❌ Tzitz Does NOT Effect Acceptance

  • Offering that left the Temple courtyard (yotzei)
  • Left-hand service — procedural, not impurity of offering
  • Blemished animal — 'it shall not be accepted' (Lev. 22:23)
  • Offerings of non-Jews — 'for them' = Israel only
  • Piggul and notar (invalid through intent/delay)

📜 Eating Sacred Meat While Impure 'Every one that is ritually pure may eat of it' (Lev. 7:19) — one who is impure eating what is permitted to the pure is liable for the prohibition.

But if meat is already disqualified — left the courtyard, became notar — it is not permitted even to the pure. Therefore an impure person eating it is not liable for the impurity prohibition, since the standard of 'permitted to the pure' is never met.

'That belong to the Lord' — extends the impurity prohibition to include notar and meat that left the courtyard, confirming liability still applies to them.
