QUESTION 1 OF 5
What happens when a priest performs the kemitza (handful removal) of a regular voluntary meal offering 'not for its own sake' (shelo lishmah)?
A. The offering is completely disqualified and must be discarded
B. The offering is valid and fully fulfills the owner's obligation
C. The offering is valid but does not fulfill the owner's obligation
D. The remainder is forbidden to the priests
Correct: C — The Mishna rules: most meal offerings performed shelo lishmah remain valid (priests may eat the remainder), but they do not satisfy the owner's obligation — he must bring another offering.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is Rabba's resolution of the apparent contradiction in Rabbi Shimon's rulings about shelo lishmah?
A. 'Disqualified' refers to change of name; 'valid' refers to change of sanctity
B. 'Disqualified' refers to change of sanctity; 'valid' refers to change of name
C. Rabbi Shimon's two rulings address different stages of the offering ritual
D. Rabbi Shimon retracted his first ruling in favor of the second
Correct: B — Rabba resolves: when a rite is performed for a different category of offering (change of sanctity, e.g., sinner's offering for a voluntary), it is disqualified. When it is performed for the same category under a different name (change of name, e.g., one voluntary for another), it is valid but does not fulfill the obligation.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why, according to Rabbi Shimon, are slaughtered offerings NOT disqualified by shelo lishmah while certain meal offerings are?
A. The Torah explicitly exempts slaughtered offerings from intent requirements
B. Slaughtered offerings share identical modes of sacrifice — wrong intent is not externally detectable
C. Slaughtered offerings have a stricter standard requiring only the moment of slaughter to have proper intent
D. Meal offerings require more kavvanah because they are brought by individuals, not the community
Correct: B — Rabbi Shimon: meal offerings have distinct modes of preparation (e.g., sinner's offering has no oil or frankincense), so performing a sinner's rite with intent for a voluntary offering is 'recognizably false.' Slaughtered offerings all share identical slaughter, blood collection, and sprinkling — wrong intent is imperceptible and cannot disqualify.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What verse does the Gemara cite to prove that a valid-but-shelo-lishmah meal offering cannot simply be replaced by the owner?
A. Leviticus 2:1 — 'When a person brings a meal offering to God'
B. Leviticus 6:7 — 'This is the law of the meal offering'
C. Numbers 5:15 — 'He shall bring her offering'
D. Deuteronomy 23:24 — 'That which has gone out of your lips you shall observe and do'
Correct: D — Deut. 23:24 teaches that a vowed gift offering must be fulfilled as promised. The Gemara derives: since the original shelo-lishmah offering remains standing as a valid halakhic offering, the owner cannot deviate from it — which proves the offering is still legally binding even though it failed to fulfill his obligation.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is Abaye's challenge to Rabba's 'recognizably false intent' distinction?
A. Rabbi Shimon never used the language of 'recognizably false' intent in his original ruling
B. If disqualification is derived from comparison to sin/guilt offerings via biblical analogy, why should detectability matter at all?
C. The Mishna explicitly rules that all shelo lishmah, whether detectable or not, is equally disqualifying
D. Rabba's distinction contradicts the ruling about bird offerings at the end of the daf
Correct: B — Abaye argues: since disqualification through improper intent is derived from the Torah's comparison of meal offerings to sin and guilt offerings — not from independent logic about detectability — why should it matter whether the wrong intent is visible or not? Rabba responds that Rabbi Shimon's reasoning is based on svara (independent logic), not solely the biblical analogy.
