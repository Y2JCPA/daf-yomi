⚖️ Shelo Lishmah — Meal Offerings vs. Slaughtered Offerings

🌾 Meal Offerings

  • Different modes of preparation for each type
  • Sinner's offering: no oil or frankincense
  • Jealousy offering: no oil or frankincense
  • Wrong intent is recognizably false — mode of prep betrays the intent
  • Therefore: shelo lishmah can disqualify certain types

🐂 Slaughtered Offerings

  • One manner of shechita (slaughter) for all offerings
  • One manner of blood collection for all
  • One manner of blood sprinkling for all
  • Wrong intent is not recognizable — no external difference
  • Therefore: shelo lishmah does NOT disqualify at all

🧠 Resolving Rabbi Shimon's Apparent Contradiction

Rabbi Shimon (Ruling 1)
A sinner's meal offering performed shelo lishmah is DISQUALIFIED — just like a sin offering slaughtered not for its sake
Rabbi Shimon (Ruling 2)
A voluntary meal offering performed for the sake of another type is VALID — like a guilt offering: valid but does not fulfill the owner's obligation
Talmud
Contradiction! Why does the sinner's offering get disqualified but the voluntary does not?
Rabba
No contradiction: 'disqualified' = change of SANCTITY (e.g., sinner's offering → voluntary). 'Valid but unfulfilled' = change of NAME (e.g., one voluntary offering → another voluntary)
Abaye
The Torah derives disqualification by comparing meal offerings to sin/guilt offerings — so why should detectability matter? Wrong intent is wrong intent!
Rabba
Rabbi Shimon's reasoning is not purely from analogy — it is based on svara (logic): the mode of preparation PROVES the intent. Only undetectably wrong intent can disqualify, since the rite itself shows no contradiction
Core Principle מוּכְחָא מִילְּתָא — 'The matter is self-evident': only intent that is not contradicted by the visible rite can disqualify an offering (Rabbi Shimon's view)

📜 Verse Proof — Why the Valid-but-Shelo-Lishmah Offering Cannot Be Replaced

מוֹצָא שְׂפָתֶיךָ תִּשְׁמֹר וְעָשִׂיתָ כַּאֲשֶׁר נָדַרְתָּ לַה' אֱלֹקֶיךָ נְדָבָה — דברים כג:כד
Deuteronomy 23:24 The Gemara proves: once a meal offering has been validly performed — even shelo lishmah — the owner may NOT deviate from it by substituting another. The offering remains bound by its original vow. This proves the original offering is still halakhically standing and valid, even though it failed to fulfill the owner's obligation. The owner must bring an additional offering to satisfy his vow.
