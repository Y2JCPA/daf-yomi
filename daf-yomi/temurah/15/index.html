🔗 The Permitting Relationship — Who Can Render Whom Piggul

Core Rule: Item A can render Item B piggul only if A's rite permits B to be consumed or offered
↓
Thanks Offering → Loaves
Blood of offering permits the loaves
∴ Offering renders loaves piggul ✅
Loaves do NOT permit offering
∴ Loaves cannot render offering piggul ✗
Shavuot Lambs → Two Loaves
Waving lambs permits the loaves
∴ Lambs render loaves piggul ✅
Loaves do NOT permit the lambs
∴ Loaves cannot render lambs piggul ✗
Animal Offering → Libations
Blood of offering permits the libations
∴ Offering renders libations piggul ✅
Libations do NOT permit offering
∴ Libations cannot render offering piggul ✗

🏛️ Closing the Tzitz Dispute — Multiple Proofs

Rav Huna b. Rav Natan
Can the Rabbi Yehuda / Rabbis dispute really be about the tzitz? Rabbi Yehuda himself agrees the frontplate works for items that ascend the altar when impure!
Rav Ashi
Proof from the Paschal offering brought in communal impurity — the tzitz cannot be the subject of their dispute.
Ravina
Further proof from the two loaves and shewbread: if the dispute were about the tzitz, Rabbi Yehuda should say both loaves are burned — but he doesn't say that!
Gemara Conclusion
All three proofs converge: the Rabbi Yehuda / Rabbis dispute is definitively NOT about whether the tzitz effects acceptance for impure items.
📜 Baraita Support If a bowl of frankincense accompanying the shewbread became impure, Rabbi Yehuda says both bowls must be removed — proving his concern is something other than the tzitz.

🍞 Piggul Asymmetry — Two Parallel Cases in the Mishna

Thanks Offering (תּוֹדָה) + Loaves

  • Offering accompanied by 40 loaves (4 types × 10)
  • ✅ Slaughtering offering with improper future-eating intent → loaves become piggul
  • ❌ Improper intent regarding the loaves alone → offering is NOT piggul
  • Reason: blood of offering permits the loaves to be eaten
  • The loaves have no rite that permits the offering

Shavuot Lambs + Two Loaves (שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם)

  • Two lambs waved together with two leavened loaves
  • ✅ Slaughtering lambs with improper intent → loaves become piggul
  • ❌ Improper intent regarding the loaves alone → lambs are NOT piggul
  • Reason: the lambs' waving rite permits the loaves
  • Mishna needed for both cases — neither is obvious from the other

🤔 Rabbi Elazar's Dilemma — Combining Intent Across Items

Rabbi Elazar
What if one slaughters the thanks offering intending to eat an olive-bulk from the offering AND an olive-bulk from its loaves the next day — are the loaves rendered piggul by this combined intent?
Rabbi Elazar
I'm not asking whether the offering becomes piggul — clearly yes. I ask: can the offering-intent combine with the loaves-intent to make the loaves piggul too?
Rav
Yes — even in this case, the loaves ARE rendered piggul. The thanks offering is not rendered piggul by the loaves-intent, but the loaves ARE piggul via the combined intent.
Gemara Challenge
A fortiori objection: if intent about the loaves alone cannot render the offering piggul, how can combined intent with the offering render the loaves piggul?
Gemara Resolution
We reject this a fortiori — the baraita about planting seeds in a vineyard shows we cannot transfer severity across different prohibition categories.
🌱 Baraita Analogy Hemp/arum in a vineyard = Torah prohibition; other seeds = rabbinic only. The Sages prohibited the seeds but not the grapes — proving a fortiori reasoning doesn't always hold across categories.

🐑 Rabbi Abba's Version — Piggul Between Two Lambs

מַחְשְׁבַת פִּגּוּל — intent to consume a sacrifice outside its designated time or beyond its permitted boundaries, rendering it forbidden under karet
Rabbi Abba's Teaching Rabbi Elazar's dilemma can also be framed for the Shavuot lambs themselves: if one slaughters Lamb A intending to eat an olive-bulk from Lamb A and an olive-bulk from Lamb B the next day — is Lamb B rendered piggul by this combined intent? Rav's answer (citing a mishna from 16a): If one slaughtered one lamb intending to eat it the next day, that lamb is piggul and the other is fit. If he slaughtered one lamb intending to eat the other lamb the next day — both are fit (no piggul at all). This ruling resolves Rabbi Elazar's dilemma about the combined-intent scenario.
