QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the thanks offering render its accompanying loaves piggul, but the loaves do not render the offering piggul?
A. The loaves are physically attached to the offering
B. The blood of the offering permits the loaves to be consumed, but the loaves have no rite that permits the offering
C. The offering is more sacred than the loaves
D. The priest consecrates the offering before the loaves
Correct: B — The Gemara's principle is that only the item whose rite permits another can render it piggul. The blood of the thanks offering permits the loaves — so intent during slaughter extends to them. The loaves have no such permitting rite over the offering.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Rabbi Elazar asks Rav about combining intent: olive-bulk from the offering and olive-bulk from the loaves the next day. What did Rav rule?
A. Neither the offering nor the loaves become piggul
B. Both the offering and the loaves become piggul
C. The loaves become piggul but the offering does not
D. The offering becomes piggul but the loaves do not
Correct: C — Rav ruled that the combined intent renders the loaves piggul (since the offering's blood permits them), but the offering itself is not rendered piggul merely by the intent regarding the loaves, since the loaves' rite does not permit the offering.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does the Gemara reject the a fortiori argument against Rav's ruling on piggul of the loaves?
A. By citing a verse that explicitly overrides the inference
B. By citing a baraita where a Torah-level prohibition does not transfer its severity to a rabbinic-level case
C. By arguing that a fortiori reasoning never applies to Temple law
D. By showing that the loaves are more stringent than the offering
Correct: B — The baraita about planting seeds shows that when hemp/arum (biblical) were planted in a vineyard, the Sages prohibited the seeds but not the grapes — demonstrating we cannot always transfer severity via a fortiori when the categories differ (Torah vs. rabbinic prohibition).
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is Rabbi Meir's reasoning that one is liable for eating piggul libations of an animal offering?
A. The libations sanctify the offering when poured upon it
B. The libations are brought on the same day as the offering
C. The blood of the offering permits the libations to be brought on the altar, so piggul intent during slaughter extends to them
D. The priest consecrates both the offering and libations with a single declaration
Correct: C — Rabbi Meir holds that the blood of the offering permits the libations to be offered. Since the offering's rite permits the libations, improper intent (piggul) during slaughter renders the libations piggul — and one is liable for eating them.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How do the Rabbis challenge Rabbi Meir's position that libations are fixed to a specific offering at the time of its slaughter?
A. They argue that libations come from a different owner than the offering
B. They note that one may bring an offering today and bring its libations up to ten days later with a different animal
C. They say the blood is already sprinkled before the libations arrive at the altar
D. They argue that libations are a rabbinic enactment and cannot be affected by piggul
Correct: B — The Rabbis point out that since libations can be used with a different animal offering up to ten days later, they are clearly not permanently fixed to any particular offering — undermining Rabbi Meir's claim that they become fixed at the moment of slaughter.
