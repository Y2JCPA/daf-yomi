🔥 When Does Partial Piggul Intent Invalidate an Offering?

Rav
The dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis applies only when one rite was silent and the other had piggul intent. If the handful was placed with piggul intent and the frankincense in silence — all agree it is piggul.
Shmuel
The dispute applies even when both rites were performed with piggul intent. Rabbi Meir says piggul; the Rabbis disagree. The 'silence' condition is not a prerequisite for the dispute.
Rabbi Ḥanina
The baraita supports Rav: it refers to two priests — one burned the handful with piggul intent, the other burned the frankincense in silence. The Rabbis only dispute when one rite was performed in silence.
Rav Ashi
The High Priest had piggul intent during the 1st and 3rd sets of Yom Kippur blood presentations and was silent during the 2nd set — alternating intent and silence, consistent with Rav's framework.
Core Question Does piggul intent during only part of a permitting factor (e.g., only the handful, not the frankincense) render the entire offering piggul — and does it matter whether the other part was done in deliberate silence or also with intent?

⚖️ Rabbi Meir vs. The Rabbis — Piggul with Partial Permitting Factors

Rabbi Meir

  • Piggul intent during any part of the permitting factors renders the offering piggul
  • Burning only the handful with piggul intent: piggul
  • Principle: performing part of a rite with intent counts as performing the whole rite
  • Applies to meal offerings, Yom Kippur blood presentations, and other multi-stage sacrifices
  • Liable to karet for eating such an offering

The Rabbis

  • Piggul intent must occur during all permitting factors to render the offering piggul
  • If only the handful had piggul intent and frankincense was silent: not piggul
  • Exception agreed upon: meal offering of a sinner / sota — no frankincense, so handful alone suffices
  • Partial permitting factor with intent is insufficient for invalidation
  • No liability to karet for eating

Scenario A: Handful placed in silence (no piggul intent) → Frankincense placed with piggul intent to eat remainder next day
↓
→ Dispute applies here. Rabbi Meir: piggul. Rabbis: not piggul. This is the only case where they disagree according to Rav.
↓
Scenario B: Handful placed with piggul intent → Frankincense placed in silence
↓
→ All agree: piggul! He performed the primary rite with intent — this is the manner of one who renders an offering piggul. No dispute.
↓
Rabbi Ḥanina's resolution: The challenging baraita refers to two priests (a first burned the handful with intent, a second burned the frankincense in silence) — consistent with Rav's rule about silence

🫙 Piggul Intent During Conveying (הולכה) of the Handful

Reish Lakish
Conveying the handful alone does NOT create piggul. Conveying is only part of the permitting factors — there is also the conveying of the frankincense. Both must have piggul intent. Law of conveying = law of burning.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Conveying the handful IS like removing the handful for piggul purposes. Just as intent during removal of only the handful renders it piggul (handful is the sole permitting factor for removal), so too for conveying the handful alone.
Rava
Rabbi Yoḥanan holds: any rite that does not itself permit the offering (like conveying) — even if performed with piggul intent for only one of the permitting factors (the handful) — is sufficient to render it piggul.
Abaye
Objection: The mishna rules that slaughtering one of the two Shavuot lambs with piggul intent regarding the two loaves does NOT render them piggul — even though slaughter is also a non-permitting rite!
Rava's Resolution The slaughter of the lambs sanctifies the two loaves, making it a permitting act in a different sense. Conveying is truly non-permitting in every respect — hence Rabbi Yoḥanan's ruling applies differently there.

🌱 Burning a Sesame-Seed Amount with Piggul Intent

Priest burns a sesame-seed amount (less than an olive-bulk) of handful + frankincense, intending to eat a sesame-seed of the remainder next day
↓
Opinion: Piggul
The manner of this offering's consumption is in small portions — so sesame-seed intent is valid piggul intent. Its burning too is in such a manner.
Opinion: Not Piggul
Piggul requires intent regarding an olive-bulk (minimum for eating liability). A sesame-seed is too small — the intent is legally void.
↓
Gemara's conclusion: Both opinions could be held by any of the tannaim (R. Meir, Rabbis, R. Yehuda HaNasi). The sesame-seed question is independent of the partial permitting factor dispute — it cannot be attributed to any one position with certainty.
