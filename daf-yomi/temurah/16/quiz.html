QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav, in which specific scenario do Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis dispute whether partial piggul intent renders a meal offering piggul?
A. When both the handful and frankincense were placed with piggul intent
B. When the handful was placed in silence and the frankincense with piggul intent
C. When both the handful and frankincense were placed entirely in silence
D. When the handful was placed with piggul intent and the frankincense in silence
Correct: B — Rav says the dispute applies when the handful was placed in silence and the frankincense with piggul intent. The reverse case (handful with intent, frankincense in silence) is agreed by all to be piggul, since he performed the primary rite with intent.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In which special case do the Rabbis concede to Rabbi Meir that piggul intent during only the handful renders the offering piggul?
A. A communal meal offering brought on Shabbat
B. A voluntary meal offering accompanying an animal sacrifice
C. The meal offering of a sinner or the sota's jealousy offering
D. Any meal offering where the frankincense was lost
Correct: C — The Rabbis concede regarding the meal offering of a sinner and the sota's jealousy offering because these have no frankincense — the handful is the sole permitting factor. Intent during the only permitting factor is sufficient even for the Rabbis.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does the Gemara reconcile the three baraitot that give different counts (43, 47, 48) for Yom Kippur blood presentations?
A. 43 is the correct count; 47 and 48 reflect later rabbinic additions
B. 43 vs. 47 reflects different counting of outer altar horn sprinklings; 47 vs. 48 is a tannaitic dispute (Ben Azzai adds one)
C. All three counts refer to different High Priests performing the service
D. 43 counts bull only; 47 adds goat; 48 adds the special Yom Kippur incense
Correct: B — The Gemara resolves: 43 and 47 differ in how they count the outer golden altar sprinklings (separately or grouped). 47 vs. 48 is a tannaitic dispute — 48 follows Ben Azzai, who includes one additional presentation not counted by the Sages.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the dispute between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish regarding piggul intent during the conveying (הולכה) of the handful?
A. Reish Lakish: conveying creates piggul; Rabbi Yoḥanan: it never creates piggul
B. Rabbi Yoḥanan: conveying the handful alone creates piggul; Reish Lakish: both handful and frankincense must be conveyed with intent
C. Both agree conveying creates piggul but dispute whether frankincense conveying is also needed
D. Both agree conveying does not create piggul independently under any circumstances
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that intent during conveying of the handful alone renders the offering piggul (analogous to removal, where the handful is the sole permitting factor). Reish Lakish holds conveying is part of a joint permitting factor — both handful and frankincense conveyings must have piggul intent.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
If a priest burns only a sesame-seed amount of handful and frankincense with intent to eat a sesame-seed of the remainder the next day, what does the Gemara conclude?
A. All agree it is piggul, since even tiny amounts count for burning
B. All agree it is not piggul, since a sesame-seed is below the minimum measure for eating
C. It is disputed, but the views cannot be definitively attributed to any known tannaitic position
D. It follows Rabbi Meir's position that any partial burning with intent renders the offering piggul
Correct: C — The Gemara concludes that both the 'piggul' and 'not piggul' positions could logically be held by any of the tannaim (Rabbi Meir, the Rabbis, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi). The sesame-seed question is independent of the partial permitting factor dispute and cannot be definitively assigned to one tannaitic view.
