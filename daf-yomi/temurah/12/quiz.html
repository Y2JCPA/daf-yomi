QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the mishna, what is required for the permitting factor to be considered 'sacrificed in accordance with its mitzva,' enabling piggul to apply?
A. The priest must verbally declare his intent before each rite
B. All four rites must be performed without any improper area-intent disqualifying the permitting factor
C. The remainder must measure at least two olive-bulks at the time of burning
D. The meal offering must be a voluntary offering, not an obligatory one
Correct: B — The permitting factor is 'sacrificed per its mitzva' when the rites are not disqualified by prior improper intent (such as outside-area intent). For example, performing the first three rites in silence and only expressing piggul intent at the burning still allows piggul to apply.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the basis of Rav Huna's position that a lacking remainder cannot be rendered piggul?
A. A lacking remainder has already been declared ownerless and exits Temple law
B. The lack is intrinsic to the remainder itself — an internal disqualification — unlike 'leaving' the courtyard, which is external
C. Rabbi Akiva explicitly rules that piggul requires a complete, whole remainder
D. The burning of the handful is only effective when the remainder is present in full at the altar
Correct: B — Rav Huna distinguishes between external disqualifications (like 'leaving' the courtyard) — where Rabbi Akiva says sprinkling is still effective — and internal disqualifications (like the remainder lacking its measure). The internal lack blocks piggul from taking effect.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Rava's initial proof that a lacking remainder CAN become piggul, and why does Abaye reject it?
A. Rava cites a baraita about shewbread; Abaye shows the baraita is ambiguous and doesn't address lacking remainders
B. Rava cites Rabbi Ḥiyya's omission of 'or an olive-bulk'; Abaye says the omission reflects Rabbi Elazar's requirement of a full handful — a stylistic parallel, not a lacking remainder
C. Rava cites the meal offering of a sinner; Abaye notes sinners' offerings never have frankincense so the proof is inapplicable
D. Rava derives it from Rabbi Yehuda's rule about time-intent preceding area-intent; Abaye says this rule is about order, not quantity
Correct: B — Rava's proof: Rabbi Ḥiyya omits 'or an olive-bulk' regarding the remainder, implying the remainder is already sub-measure yet piggul applies. Abaye's rebuttal: Rabbi Ḥiyya follows Rabbi Elazar who requires the full handful burned; unable to write 'an olive-bulk of its handful,' he symmetrically omits it for the remainder too.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
The Gemara asks why Rabbi Ḥiyya's mishna says 'to burn its handful' rather than 'to burn its handful and its frankincense.' What is the answer?
A. Rabbi Ḥiyya holds that frankincense has no role in the piggul process
B. The frankincense is burned separately by a different priest and cannot receive piggul intent
C. Rabbi Ḥiyya's mishna discusses specifically the meal offering of a sinner, which has no frankincense
D. Frankincense is only relevant for peace offerings, not meal offerings
Correct: C — The meal offering of a sinner (מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא) contains no frankincense — only the handful. Since the tanna chose to illustrate the law with this offering, only the handful is mentioned. The Gemara accepts this as a valid reason for the specific phrasing.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
The mishna rules that intent to eat half an olive-bulk of remainder and intent to burn half an olive-bulk of the handful do NOT combine to create piggul. What underlying principle does the Gemara derive from this?
A. Half-measures can never be combined for any purpose in Temple law
B. Consumption by a person (eating the remainder) and consumption by the altar (burning the handful) are categorically distinct and do not join together
C. The remainder and the handful are two separate items and may never be measured jointly
D. Piggul only applies when the improper intent concerns the same object throughout all four rites
Correct: B — The mishna's ruling reflects the principle that eating (by a person) and burning (on the altar) are fundamentally different modes of consumption. Two half-measures of different types therefore cannot combine into a full olive-bulk to trigger piggul — unlike two halves of the same type (both eating or both burning), which do combine.
