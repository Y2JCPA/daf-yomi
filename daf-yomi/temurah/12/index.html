⚔️ Central Dispute: Does Piggul Apply to a Lacking Remainder?

Rav Huna
Even per Rabbi Akiva — who says sprinkling blood is effective for meat that 'left' — burning the handful does NOT render a lacking remainder piggul. A lack is intrinsic to the remainder itself, disqualifying it from within. It is unlike 'leaving,' which is an external factor.
Rava
Even per Rabbi Eliezer — who says sprinkling is not effective for meat that left — burning the handful DOES render a lacking remainder piggul. A lack in measure is like 'leaving': both are external disqualifications that do not bar piggul intent from taking hold.
The Core Dilemma If the remainder of a meal offering became lacking (less than an olive-bulk) between the removal of the handful and its burning on the altar — can piggul intent expressed during the burning still render that diminished remainder as piggul, with karet liability for one who eats it?

🌾 The Four Rites That Can Create Piggul

1. Removing the handful — קְמִיצָה
Taking the kometz from the meal offering in the Temple courtyard
↓
2. Placing in the service vessel — נְתִינָה בִּכְלִי
Transferring the handful into a sanctified Temple vessel
↓
3. Conveying to the altar — הוֹלָכָה
Carrying the vessel with the handful to the altar
↓
4. Burning on the altar — הַקְטָרָה
Placing the handful on the altar fire — the permitting act
↓
If any one of these rites is performed with intent to eat the remainder or burn the handful the next day → the offering becomes piggul (פִּגּוּל) and one who eats of it is liable to karet

⚖️ Permitting Factor: Sacrificed Per Its Mitzva vs. Not

✅ Sacrificed Per Its Mitzva → Piggul Applies

  • Removed handful in silence (no disqualifying intent)
  • Placed in vessel in silence
  • Conveyed in silence
  • Burned on altar with improper time-intent
  • → Permitting factor properly done up to the burning
  • → Offering IS piggul; karet applies for eating remainder

❌ Not Sacrificed Per Its Mitzva → Disqualified, Not Piggul

  • Removed handful with intent to eat remainder outside its designated area
  • OR: intent to burn the handful or frankincense outside
  • → This prior intent disqualifies the permitting factor
  • → Offering is pasul (פָּסוּל) — unfit
  • → NOT piggul; no karet liability

🔬 Rava's Proof from Rabbi Ḥiyya & Abaye's Rebuttal

Rava
Rabbi Ḥiyya's version of the mishna omits 'or an olive-bulk' when listing intent about consuming the remainder. Why? Because his mishna discusses a case where the remainder already became lacking — yet piggul still applies! This proves a lacking remainder can be rendered piggul.
Abaye
No proof! Rabbi Ḥiyya's mishna follows Rabbi Elazar, who requires the full handful to be burned (not just an olive-bulk of it). Since he can't write 'or an olive-bulk of its handful,' he also omits 'or an olive-bulk' from the remainder clause — for stylistic symmetry only, not because the remainder is lacking.
Gemara's Challenge to Abaye — and the Answer If Rabbi Ḥiyya follows Rabbi Elazar, he should have written 'to burn its handful AND its frankincense outside' — not just 'its handful.' Answer: He is discussing the meal offering of a sinner (מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא), which has no frankincense. Only the handful permits it — so only the handful is mentioned.

🔄 Rava's Position — Stated, Challenged, and Retracted

Rava's original claim: burning the handful renders even a lacking remainder piggul
↓
Proof #1: Rabbi Ḥiyya's mishna
Omits 'olive-bulk' → remainder is already lacking → yet piggul applies
Proof #2: Shewbread baraita
Loaves that remain inside Temple are fit even if one left → sprinkling is effective → lacking = external disqualification
↓
Abaye refutes both proofs — Proof #1: stylistic omission per Rabbi Elazar. Proof #2: baraita never says remaining loaves are fit; the frontplate doesn't help loaves that left.
↓
Rava retracts: his original statement about lacking remainders was only tentative — he withdraws the claim
