QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabba, why does a meal offering removed not for its own sake still fulfill the owner's obligation?
A. Because the verse 'And this is the law of the meal offering' unifies all meal offerings under one law
B. Because all meal offerings look alike, so the false intent is unrecognizable to observers
C. Because intent regarding the vessel type is always considered inconsequential
D. Because Rabbi Shimon holds that no meal offering is ever disqualified by improper intent
Correct: B — Rabba's principle is that unrecognizable false intent does not disqualify an offering. Since all meal offerings appear similar in preparation, an observer cannot detect that the priest had improper intent, and the offering remains valid.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Which verse does Rava cite as the basis for his ruling that a meal offering removed not for its own sake is valid?
A. "And slaughter it for a sin offering" (Leviticus 4:33)
B. "Who sacrifices the blood of the peace offering" (Leviticus 7:33)
C. "And this is the law of the meal offering" (Leviticus 6:7)
D. "Command Aaron and his sons, saying" (Leviticus 6:2)
Correct: C — Rava derives from the phrase 'And this is the law [torat] of the meal offering' (Lev. 6:7) that there is one unified law governing all meal offerings. Intent for any meal offering therefore constitutes acceptable intent.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
The Gemara challenges Rabba with the case of a goat sin offering slaughtered for the sake of a guilt offering. What is the force of the challenge?
A. A guilt offering can also be brought as a goat, so there is actually no visible difference
B. A goat (dark hair) and a guilt-offering ram (white wool) are clearly distinguishable — the false intent IS recognizable, yet according to Rabba the offering should be invalid
C. The Gemara argues both offerings are brought for the same type of sin
D. A goat sin offering is communal, not individual, making its identity ambiguous
Correct: B — The challenge is that a goat and a guilt-offering ram are obviously different (species, wool color) — the false intent is plainly recognizable. By Rabba's own principle, this should disqualify the offering, yet the baraita seems to indicate otherwise, creating a difficulty for Rabba.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rav Ashi, when a priest removes a handful from a pan meal offering while intending a deep-pan meal offering, why is the offering valid?
A. Because the verse 'And this is the law of the meal offering' applies equally to both pan and deep-pan offerings
B. Because the owner did not specify which type of meal offering he intended to bring
C. Because the priest's intent was only about the vessel type, and intent regarding the vessel is halakhically inconsequential
D. Because a pan and a deep-pan meal offering are physically indistinguishable once prepared
Correct: C — Rav Ashi's reasoning is that the priest's wrong intent concerned only the vessel (pan vs. deep-pan), not the essential identity of the offering. Since vessel-type intent is inconsequential, the offering's essential character as a meal offering is unaffected and it remains valid.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does Rabba rule that a Paschal offering slaughtered for the sake of a guilt offering is valid, even though one animal is in its first year and the other in its second?
A. Because both the Paschal offering and a guilt offering can be brought from sheep of any age
B. Because guilt offerings are always brought from third-year animals, making the age difference even greater and less relevant
C. Because the year-age difference between animals is 'not on people's minds' — a large first-year and a small second-year animal can appear the same age
D. Because the Paschal offering's unique timing (eve of Passover) makes its identity evident regardless of the priest's intent
Correct: C — Rabba explains that the age difference between a first-year and a second-year animal is not obvious to observers — relative size variation means the two animals can look the same age. The false intent is therefore not recognizable, and the offering remains valid.
