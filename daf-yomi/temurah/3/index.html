🔀 Three Amoraim Resolve One Contradiction

The Contradiction: Two baraitot quote Rabbi Shimon — one says a meal offering not for its own sake fulfills the obligation; the other implies it does not.
↓
Rabba: Unrecognizable false intent does not disqualify. Meal offerings look alike, so the priest's wrong intent cannot be detected → offering is valid.
↓
Rava: The verse "And this is the law of the meal offering" (Lev. 6:7) establishes one unified law for all meal offerings → any meal-offering intent counts as proper intent.
↓
Rav Ashi: The priest's intent was only about the vessel type (pan vs. deep-pan). Intent regarding the vessel is halakhically inconsequential — the offering's essential identity is unchanged.
↓
Consensus: All three rule the meal offering valid and the owner's obligation fulfilled — they differ on why, with significant consequences for other cases.

⚔️ Challenging Rabba: Is the False Intent Really Unrecognizable?

Gemara
A burnt offering slaughtered for the sake of a sin offering should be valid — it is male, and a sin offering of an individual is always female. Isn't that difference obvious?
Rabba
A female lamb's tail makes its sex hard to discern — an observer might think it is male, intended as a burnt offering. The difference is not recognizable.
Gemara
Fine, but what about a female goat sin offering? Its sex is plainly obvious — surely the false intent is recognizable there!
Gemara
And a Paschal offering (first-year) slaughtered for a guilt offering (second-year)? The age difference should be visible!
Rabba
The age difference is not on people's minds — a large first-year animal and a small second-year animal can look alike. This is not a recognizable difference.
Rabba's Principle Only intent that is truly and obviously recognizable from the offering's physical appearance disqualifies it

⚖️ Three Amoraim — Same Ruling, Different Reasoning

Rabba

  • Meal offering valid: all meal offerings look alike
  • Unrecognizable intent → does not disqualify
  • Recognizable intent → disqualifies
  • Challenged by: goat vs. ram, obvious sex
  • Source: logical inference from appearances

Rava

  • Meal offering valid: verse unifies all meal offerings
  • "And this is the law of the meal offering" (Lev. 6:7)
  • One law = any meal-offering intent is acceptable
  • Sin offering for wrong category: valid per R. Shimon
  • Source: scriptural derivation

Rav Ashi

  • Meal offering valid: vessel intent is inconsequential
  • Pan vs. deep-pan = difference only in vessel type
  • The offering's essential identity is unchanged
  • Challenged by: dry vs. oil-mixed (Rav Aḥa b. Rava)
  • Source: conceptual distinction between types of intent

📜 Rava's Verse: One Law Unifies All Meal Offerings

וְזֹאת תּוֹרַת הַמִּנְחָה — תּוֹרָה אַחַת לְכָל הַמְּנָחוֹת
Leviticus 6:7 — וְזֹאת תּוֹרַת הַמִּנְחָה "And this is the law [torat] of the meal offering" — Rava derives from the singular word torat that there is one unified law for all meal offerings. Therefore a priest who removes a handful from a pan meal offering intending it for a deep-pan meal offering has not acted improperly — any meal-offering intent is acceptable intent, and the owner fulfills his obligation. Rava extends this: even a sin offering brought for the wrong category of sin is valid according to Rabbi Shimon, by analogy.
