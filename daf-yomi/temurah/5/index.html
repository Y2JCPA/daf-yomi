🌾 The Omer Handful: Valid or Disqualified?

Rav
If the handful of the omer meal offering was removed not for its own sake, the offering is disqualified entirely.
Reish Lakish
The offering is valid. The handful is burned on the altar. However, the remainder may not be consumed until a new, valid omer is brought.
Rav Adda bar Ahava
Explanation: An offering is not considered 'whose time has not yet arrived' if it is to be brought on that very day — so sacrificing this handful violates no prohibition.
Rav Pappa
True reason: Reish Lakish holds that the illumination of the eastern horizon already permits the new crop — so by the time of sacrifice, the prohibition has already lifted.
Core Issue The dispute turns on when the new crop becomes permitted and whether wrong intent can disqualify the uniquely time-bound omer offering.

⏰ Challenges to the 'Same-Day' Principle

Rav Adda bar Ahava
An offering is not considered 'whose time has not yet arrived' if it is to be brought on that same day.
Rav Adda b. Rav Yitzchak
Objection from baraita: A metzora (leper) who has not yet brought his atonement — bird offerings for a zav can come from new crop even before the omer, proving same-day offerings remain restricted in some contexts.
Rav Sheshet
Further objection: If oil is placed on the leper's thumb before the blood of his guilt offering, the placement must be repeated. Why, if the same-day rule removes the 'time not arrived' restriction?
Rav Pappa
Response: The leper's laws are different. The verse says זֹאת תִּהְיֶה — 'this shall be' (Lev. 14:2) — indicating the prescribed order must remain exactly as established, regardless of other principles.
Hebrew Key זֹאת תִּהְיֶה תּוֹרַת הַמְּצֹרָע — 'This shall be the law of the leper' (Leviticus 14:2). The word תִּהְיֶה ('shall be') pegs the leper's rite to its original fixed order permanently.

🕊️ Birds vs. Meal Offerings — Contrasting Halakhot

🐦 Birds — Unique Halakhot

  • A zav, zava, or yoledet must bring atonement offering before their other birds are valid
  • Pinching the nape (melikah): forbidden for non-sacred birds, yet permitted as the method of slaughter for sacred birds
  • Two birds of a metzora may come from the new crop before the omer
  • No service vessel, waving, or oil required

🌾 Meal Offerings — Unique Halakhot

  • Must be placed in a service vessel (כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת)
  • Require waving (tenufah) and bringing (hagashah) to the altar corner
  • Require oil and frankincense
  • General prohibition on new crop was permitted through the omer sacrifice
  • A handful burned validly even when remainder awaits a new omer

🐄 Why Can't We Derive Tereifa's Disqualification by A Fortiori?

Starting point: A verse is needed — 'bring your offering from the herd' (Lev. 1:3) — to teach that a tereifa (torn animal) is unfit for the altar.
↓
Proposed a fortiori: A blemished animal (permitted to a layperson for eating) is disqualified from the altar — surely a tereifa (forbidden to all) should also be disqualified!
↓
Refutation 1 — fat and blood: These are forbidden to laypeople yet are offered on the altar. So 'forbidden to laypeople' does not automatically mean 'unfit for the altar.'
↓
Counter-proof — bird pinching: Pinching a non-sacred bird's nape is forbidden and renders it a carcass, yet pinching is the valid method for sacred birds. So the a fortiori still does not work.
↓
Refutation 2 — pinching's sanctity: Pinching is only forbidden because of sanctification — before sanctification, it was not prohibited. But a tereifa was already forbidden before consecration.
↓
Conclusion: The a fortiori inference cannot be completed without a counter-proof. Four Sages each supply a different counter-proof, and each is refuted in turn. The verse remains necessary.
