📜 Writing a Torah Scroll: Core Halakhot of Daf 30

One may finish writing a Torah scroll (or individual book) anywhere in the column — even mid-column or mid-line
↓
The last 8 verses (describing Moses' death) must be read by a single reader and may not be divided
↓
Each sheet must contain 3–8 columns; sheets at the scroll's end may have even one line
↓
Margins: lower = 1 tefaḥ, upper = ¾ tefaḥ, between columns = 2 finger-widths; lines and words have their own spacing rules
↓
If God's Name is omitted: scrape the next word, suspend it above the line, then write the Name in its proper place — dispute on whether this is permitted

✍️ Who Wrote the Last 8 Verses of the Torah?

Rabbi Yehuda
Moses wrote the entire Torah, including the final verses describing his own death — he wrote them with tears, in anguish and grief.
Rabbi Shimon
Is it possible the Torah scroll was ever missing even one letter? Rather, God dictated and Moses wrote tearfully up to the account of his death. From that point, the Holy One dictated and Moses wrote with a sealed, unclear intent — not fully understanding what he transcribed.
Gemara (inference)
Rav rules that the last 8 verses are read by one person. This fits Rabbi Shimon — since Moses wrote them like the rest of the Torah, we do not split one author's continuous section between two readers.
The Triggering Verse "And Moses the servant of God died there" (Deut. 34:5) — could Moses have written this himself while alive?

📐 Sheet & Column Rules for a Torah Scroll

Column width minimum: Space for ~30 letters — the width of the word למשפחותיהם
↓
Max columns per sheet: 8 — more makes each column resemble a narrow letter/missive
↓
Min columns per sheet: 3 — fewer looks improper and unbalanced
↓
Sheet with 9 columns: Split as 4 + 5, not 3 + 6 — keep sheets as equal as possible
↓
End of scroll: Final sheet may have only 1 column, 1 verse, or even 1 line — minimum does not apply
↓
Where to finish: Rav — anywhere in the column; Rav Ashi — specifically in the last line of the column

🖊️ Scribe Omits God's Name — What Happens Next?

Scribe omits God's Name (שם ה׳) and writes the next word(s) by mistake
↓
Scrape off the word(s) written where the Name should have been
↓
Suspend the scraped word(s) above the line — held in place
↓
Rabbi Yosei / Rav (Rav Ḥananel)
Write God's Name in the now-empty space below.
Halakha: suspension is valid
Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar (R. Meir)
Do not suspend the Name in any manner — begin the entire scroll again
↓
Rabbi Shimon Shezuri (middle view): May suspend the entire Name — but never just part of the Name

⚖️ Three Rulings on God's Name & Rabbi Shimon Shezuri

Rav Ḥananel citing Rav

  • Halakha: suspend God's Name above the line
  • Follows Rabbi Yosei
  • Scraped text goes above; Name fills the gap below
  • The scroll remains valid

Rabba bar bar Ḥana citing R. Yitzḥak bar Shmuel

  • Halakha: do NOT suspend God's Name
  • Aligned with R. Shimon ben Elazar (citing R. Meir)
  • Suspension is categorically forbidden
  • Restart the scroll entirely

Rabbi Ḥanina (via Ravin bar Ḥinnana / Ulla)

  • Halakha follows Rabbi Shimon Shezuri
  • But which ruling of his? Gemara is uncertain
  • Candidates: get (divorce), demai teruma, cowpea plants, a chest, or wine
  • Each candidate is then shown to already have a ruling — Gemara left unresolved
