QUESTION 1 OF 5
On what basis is the meal offering of jealousy disqualified when performed not for its sake?
A. The verse 'This is the law of the meal offering' unifies all meal offerings under one strict rule
B. A verbal analogy (gezeirah shava) on the word 'iniquity' (avon) links it to the sin offering
C. The word 'it' (hu) appears in the verse about the jealousy offering
D. A direct prohibition in Numbers 5 forbids improper jealousy offerings
Correct: B — The Gemara derives the disqualification via gezeirah shava: 'iniquity' (avon) appears in Numbers 5:15 for the jealousy offering and in Leviticus 10:17 for the sin offering — linking their laws of 'not for their sake.'
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why doesn't the gezeirah shava on 'iniquity' extend to disqualify guilt offerings as well?
A. Guilt offerings have no connection to sin offerings in the Torah
B. The Torah explicitly states that guilt offerings are exempt from this rule
C. The guilt offering verse uses 'avono' (his iniquity) — a different grammatical form that breaks the analogy
D. Guilt offerings are communal, not individual, so the analogy doesn't apply
Correct: C — One derives a gezeirah shava from 'avon' to 'avon,' but not from 'avono' (his iniquity) to 'avon.' The school of Rabbi Yishmael demonstrates that different forms of the same root word break the verbal analogy.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Huna citing Rav, what does the phrase 'It is a guilt offering' (Lev. 7:5) actually teach?
A. That a guilt offering slaughtered not for its sake is fully disqualified
B. That guilt offerings are subject to the same rules as sin offerings
C. That a guilt offering whose owner died or whose sin was atoned must graze, and if slaughtered as a burnt offering afterward it is valid
D. That guilt offerings can never be substituted with other animals
Correct: C — Rav Huna citing Rav: 'It is a guilt offering' teaches that when a guilt offering's purpose lapses (owner died or sin atoned), it is left to graze. If it is then slaughtered as a burnt offering, it is valid — 'it' teaches its ongoing sanctity.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why is the Omer excluded from the mishna's rule that offerings not for their sake are merely unfulfilled?
A. The Omer is a sin offering and follows the strict sin-offering rules
B. The Omer is both a communal offering and has a fixed time — the mishna's leniency applies only to individual, non-time-fixed offerings
C. A specific verse in Leviticus 23 disqualifies the Omer when not for its sake
D. The Omer is offered by the High Priest alone, who faces stricter standards
Correct: B — The Gemara limits the mishna's rule to individual offerings with no fixed time. The Omer fails on both counts: it is communal (on behalf of all Israel) and time-fixed (16th of Nisan) — so it is disqualified, not merely unfulfilled.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rabbi Yirmeya explains why nazirite and leper guilt offerings are disqualified when not for their sake. What is his principle?
A. The Torah treats guilt offerings more strictly than sin offerings in all cases
B. Offerings that 'render fit' (machshir) are treated more strictly than those that merely 'atone' — they must be performed precisely for their purpose
C. Any offering linked to impurity is automatically disqualified when performed incorrectly
D. The nazirite and leper are in a special category requiring double-precision in all their offerings
Correct: B — Rabbi Yirmeya: the Torah is more stringent for offerings that 'render fit' (machshir) — enabling the nazirite/leper to re-enter the camp and partake of offerings — than for those that merely atone for sin. Their purpose is so specific that mislabeling defeats it entirely.
