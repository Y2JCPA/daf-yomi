🏛️ The Mezuza Format Dispute: Open or Closed?

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar
Leave space above and below the text. Begin Vehaya im shamoa on the line following the end of the first passage — this is the open (פתוחה) manner.
Rav (via Rav Ḥananel)
The halakha follows Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar — but this refers only to the spacing above and below, not necessarily the open-passage format.
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
It is a mitzva ab initio to write in the closed (סתומה) manner. But if one wrote in the open manner — it is permitted after the fact.
Abaye
Rav holds one must observe established customs! Just as with ḥalitza — even if Elijah came and ruled otherwise, we do not deviate from entrenched practice.
Practical Ruling Closed (סתומה) is preferred ab initio. Open (פתוחה) is valid post facto. Either way, proper spacing above and below the text is required per Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar.

📋 Parchment Rules: Three Sacred Objects Compared

Torah Scroll (ספר תורה)

  • Written on klaf (hair side)
  • Requires scoring (ruled lines)
  • Must copy from a written text
  • Passages in closed format
  • Highest sanctity — cannot be reduced

Phylacteries (תפילין)

  • Written on klaf, not dokhsostos
  • Does NOT require scoring
  • May be written without copying from text
  • Written on hair side of parchment
  • Greater sanctity than mezuza

Mezuza (מזוזה)

  • Written on klaf (hair side)
  • Requires scoring (unlike phylacteries)
  • May be written without copying from text
  • Closed format preferred; open valid post facto
  • Lesser sanctity than phylacteries

🚪 Mezuza Validity: What Makes It Fit or Unfit?

Proper parchment (klaf), hair side — written with sacred intent (לשמה): ✅ Valid foundation
↓
Written on dokhsostos (flesh-side leather): ❌ Unfit for phylacteries; disputed for mezuza — some say unfit, others permit
↓
Written as a missive (like a casual letter, without precision): ❌ Unfit — sacred documents require deliberate, careful writing לשמה
↓
Hung on a stick in the doorway rather than affixed to the post: ❌ Unfit — the verse demands 'written upon the doorposts'
↓
Placed outside the airspace of the entrance: ❌ Invalid — must be within the entrance airspace ('upon your gates')
↓
Affixed to the doorpost within the entrance airspace, written with scoring: ✅ Fully valid mezuza

🛏️ Torah Scroll on a Bed — Permitted to Sit?

Rav Huna (practice)
When I wished to sit on my bed that had a Torah scroll on it, I overturned a jug and placed the Torah upon it — elevating it and honoring it above where I would sit.
Rabbi Yoḥanan (via Rabba bar bar Ḥana)
It is permitted to sit on a bed upon which a Torah scroll rests. If someone whispers to you that it is forbidden — there is no substance to his words.
Unresolved Tension Rav Huna's stringent personal practice directly contradicts Rabbi Yoḥanan's explicit lenient ruling. The Gemara notes the disagreement — Rav Huna's behavior reflects a pious stringency beyond the letter of the law.
