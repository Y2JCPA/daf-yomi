📜 Torah Scroll Tear: Can It Be Sewn?

Torah scroll parchment has a tear
↓
Tear spans 2 lines or fewer
✅ May sew the parchment — scroll remains fit
Tear spans 3 lines or more
❌ Cannot sew — scroll is unfit for use
↓
Parchment processed with gall
Labeled "new" — can be sewn to other gall-processed sheets
Parchment not processed with gall
Labeled "old" — cannot be joined to new parchment by sewing

⚖️ Tevel Mixed With Tithed Produce

R. Shimon Shezuri
My untithed produce (tevel) became mixed with a larger quantity of tithed produce. I came to Rabbi Tarfon for a ruling on what to do.
R. Tarfon
By Torah law the minority is nullified in the majority — but by rabbinic law it is not. You must separate tithes from the mixed produce.
Gemara
Why not tell him: Go take produce from a gentile — which is Torah-exempt — and separate tithes from it on behalf of the tevel mixture?
R. Tarfon
Gentile produce is itself obligated by rabbinic law — so one cannot use it to exempt other tevel. Or: one cannot separate tithes from one rabbinic obligation on behalf of another.
Two Versions One tradition has R. Tarfon never suggesting the gentile produce solution; another says he did suggest it but it was rejected — reflecting a dispute about the rabbinic status of gentile produce

📋 Mezuza Writing Formats Compared

Two-by-Two Format

  • Exactly two words on every line
  • ✅ Fit — ruled valid by Rav via Rav Ḥananel
  • Uniform, column-like structure
  • The baseline valid format

Mixed Format

  • Two words on one line, three on the next (alternating)
  • ⚠️ Raised as dilemma before the Sages
  • Rabba bar bar Ḥana / R. Yoḥanan: also fit
  • Not universally confirmed — debated

Final Line Special Rule

  • Last two words: al ha'aretz (upon the earth)
  • Rav Ḥisda: write these alone on the final line
  • Dispute: placed at start or end of the line?
  • Hinges on how Deut. 11:21 is expounded

🔄 Rolling and Writing the Mezuza

Valid layout confirmed: Rav rules a mezuza written two words per line (two-by-two) is fit for use
↓
Rav Ḥisda: The final two words — al ha'aretz — are written alone on the last line; dispute whether at line's start or end
↓
Rav Huna's rolling practice (observed by Rabbi Ḥelbo): Roll from eḥad toward shema — i.e., left to right
↓
Result: when the mezuza is placed in its case, Shema (the opening word) faces outward and is seen first upon unrolling
↓
Rabbi Meir's practice: He wrote the mezuza on dokhsostos (inner hide layer, not outer parchment) and formed it like a Torah scroll column
