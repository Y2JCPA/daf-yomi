🩸 The Machshava Dispute: Intent to Leave Blood Until Tomorrow

Rabbi Yehuda
If one intends to leave the blood overnight, all agree the offering is unfit — intent to do something inherently disqualifying is equivalent to doing it.
Rabbis
The offering is fit. Improper intent only invalidates when it involves an act of consuming (piggul). Merely leaving blood is not such an act.
Rabbi Eliezer
The offering is unfit even with intent to merely leave the blood. Any improper thought during a sacrificial rite — even short of consumption — renders it invalid.
Rabbi Elazar (Amora)
Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis disagree even when the intent is only to leave the blood. This is a broader dispute than Rabbi Yehuda initially framed it.
Resolution via Aggadah The story of Yosef the Babylonian confirms: Rabbi Yehuda taught that all agree the offering is unfit when one intends to leave the blood overnight — and Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua's tearful reversal validated this position.

📜 The Story of Yosef the Babylonian

אַשְׁרֵיכֶם תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה חֲבִיבִין עֲלֵיכֶם בְּיוֹתֵר
Aggadic Interlude — Love of Torah Yosef the Babylonian had learned from Rabbi Yehuda that intending to leave blood overnight renders an offering unfit. He then posed the same question to Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua three times — evening, morning, and evening again — receiving each time the ruling: "the offering is fit."

After being pressed a final time, Rabbi Elazar declared: "It seems to me my halakhot were not accurate until now." Yosef replied: "My teacher, yes — the offering is fit, as you said."

Upon hearing this, Rabbi Elazar's eyes streamed with tears:
"Happy are you, Torah scholars, for whom matters of Torah are exceedingly dear!"

Yosef then clarified: "You have returned to me that which I had lost" — meaning that Rabbi Elazar's reversal confirmed the original ruling (unfit) that Yosef had learned from Rabbi Yehuda.

🧑‍🍳 Who May Pour the Oil — Priest or Non-Priest?

Rabbis
A non-priest may pour the oil. The verse assigns 'bringing to Aaron's sons' only to the later priestly rites — removal of the handful and burning. Pouring is a preparatory step, not listed among priestly-exclusive acts.
Rabbi Shimon
Pouring oil is a priestly rite. Any priest who denies the validity of the meal offering rites — including pouring — loses his share in priestly gifts. The verse 'Aaron's sons' covers all these rites.
Rav Naḥman
Not difficult: Rabbi Shimon speaks of a priest's meal offering (no handful removal), the Mishna of an Israelite's meal offering. Different contexts — different rules apply.
Rava
But the priest's meal offering derives its pouring-obligation from the Israelite's — so if non-priests may pour in the source case, the same must be true in the derived case. The distinction collapses.
Alternate Version A second version of the dispute distinguishes between meal offerings where a handful is removed vs. those where it is not — yielding the same back-and-forth between Rav Naḥman and Rava.

🧂 Meal Offering Types: Differing Rules for Pouring

Israelite's Meal Offering

  • Handful (kometz) is removed by priest
  • Pouring of oil: explicitly stated in Torah
  • Rabbis: non-priest may pour oil — valid
  • Priestly exclusivity begins at handful removal
  • Remainder (shirayim) eaten by priests

Priest's Meal Offering

  • No handful — entire offering burned on altar
  • Pouring of oil: derived from Israelite's offering
  • Rabbi Shimon: pouring is exclusively priestly
  • Rava: derived rules follow the source's rules
  • No priestly gifts — nothing remains to be eaten

What the Baraita Lists as Meal Offering Rites

  • Pouring of oil (יְצִיקָה)
  • Mixing (בְּלִילָה)
  • Breaking (פְּתִיתָה)
  • Salting (מְלִיחָה)
  • Waving (תְּנוּפָה)
  • Bringing near altar (הַגָּשָׁה)
  • Removal of handful (קְמִיצָה)
  • Burning the handful (הַקְטָרָה)
