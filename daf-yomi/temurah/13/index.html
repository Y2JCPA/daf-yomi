⚖️ Rabbi Yosei vs. Rabbis: Frankincense Intent and Piggul

Rabbis
If the priest removes the handful intending to burn the frankincense the next day — it is piggul! Frankincense is a permitting factor for the meal offering, so improper intent applies fully.
Rabbi Yosei
The frankincense is not the meal offering's own preclusion. A permitting factor cannot render another permitting factor piggul. The offering is unfit — but there is no karet liability.
Rabbis
The rule that 'a permitting factor doesn't render another piggul' only applies when they are entirely different in kind. Frankincense is intrinsic to the meal offering — piggul applies!
Rabbi Yosei
Frankincense does not prevent the remainder of the meal offering from being eaten — only the handful does. Therefore frankincense is not the offering's preclusion, and its intent cannot create full piggul.
Practical Difference Rabbis: intent re frankincense = piggul + karet. Rabbi Yosei: offering is unfit but partaking incurs no karet.

מנחות יג.
🤔 Abaye's Redundancy Challenge The mishna rules that intent to consume half an olive-bulk and intent to burn half an olive-bulk do not combine into a full measure of piggul intent. Abaye asks: this could be derived from an earlier mishna!

The Gemara answers: the new mishna is needed for a case where one intended in accordance with the typical manner for one half, and contrary to typical manner for the other — a nuanced combination not covered before. Despite surface similarity, the two cases are legally distinct.

⚔️ Why Say 'Rabbi Yosei Concedes'?

Question
Why does the mishna awkwardly say 'Rabbi Yosei concedes' in the case of handful-intent? Just state the law directly: removing the handful intending to burn it the next day = piggul!
Answer
The tanna must use 'concedes' to set up the contrast with the latter clause — where one intends to burn the frankincense next day, and Rabbi Yosei says it is unfit but NOT piggul. Without 'concedes,' his leniency in the harder case would be unexplained.
Mishna Structure Case 1 (handful intent): Rabbi Yosei concedes = piggul. Case 2 (frankincense intent): Rabbi Yosei disagrees = unfit only. The juxtaposition clarifies his reasoning.

🧑‍🍳 Rabbi Yannai: Non-Priest Collecting Frankincense

Rabbi Yannai rules: If a non-priest collects the frankincense from a meal offering — the act is invalid and the meal offering is disqualified.
↓
Rabbi Yirmeya's reason: Frankincense collection is analogous to removal of the handful — an explicitly priestly rite. A non-priest cannot perform it any more than he can remove the handful.
↓
Rav Mari's proof from 12a mishna: The mishna lists placing the handful in a vessel as a piggul-generating rite — implying it is a significant priestly service, comparable to collecting blood in a bowl.
↓
Gemara rejects: Placement in a vessel appears in the list only because it is indispensable (you cannot offer the handful without it), not because it parallels blood collection — so no proof from here.
↓
Final reasoning accepted: Both blood collection and frankincense collection involve vessel sanctification of a permitting factor — this structural parallel makes them equivalent, mandatory priestly rites.

🍞 Piggul Intent on One of the Two Shavuot Loaves

Rav Huna

  • Rabbi Yosei holds: piggul intent regarding one loaf does NOT render the other loaf piggul
  • Analogy: Disqualifying intent ≤ ritual impurity — impurity of one limb never spreads to the whole offering
  • Each loaf is an independent unit for piggul liability
  • The two lambs that accompany the loaves are treated in parallel

Rav Naḥman (objecting)

  • Baraita (Rabbis): no karet liability unless intent encompasses an olive-bulk from both loaves
  • This implies both loaves form a single unit — intent on one alone cannot suffice
  • Rav Huna's position reflects only Rabbi Yosei, not the majority Rabbinic view
  • The baraita is best read as the Rabbis' opinion, not Rabbi Yosei's
