🏺 Central Question: Do Service Vessels Sanctify Without Intent?

Problem: If a priest returns the handful to the service vessel, does the vessel re-sanctify (and thereby disqualify) the handful?
↓
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Vessels only sanctify with intent — returning the handful without intent does not sanctify it
Challenge
But Reish Lakish raised this as an unresolved dilemma — implying it was never definitively answered
↓
Rav Amram's Solution: Return the handful to a heaped bowl (levisa) — the handful rests above the rim, technically outside the vessel
↓
Counter: Once the handful was removed, a furrow formed — returning it places it inside the vessel again!

🗣️ Service Vessels: Sanctification With or Without Intent?

Rabbi Yoḥanan
Service vessels sanctify items placed in them only when placed there with specific intent. Returning the handful without intent causes no re-sanctification.
Reish Lakish
But I raised this very issue as an unresolved dilemma before you! If you had already ruled, why did I still have the doubt?
Rav Amram
Even if vessels sanctify without intent, we can return the handful to a heaped bowl (levisa) where it rests above the rim — outside the vessel entirely.
Gemara
If the offering was heaped, how was the handful removed in the first place? And once removed, a furrow forms — returning it places it inside the vessel once again!
Core Principle The debate hinges on whether service vessel sanctification requires intent (כוונה) — with practical consequences for every step of the meal offering service.

👨‍🏫 Avimi Teaches Rav Naḥman: Proper Handful Procedure

Scene: Rav Naḥman meets Avimi returning from Rav Ḥisda's study hall and asks: How does one properly remove a handful from a meal offering?
↓
Step 1 — Removal: Avimi pointed to a raised vessel (held off the ground). The handful must be removed from a vessel that is elevated, not resting on the ground.
↓
Step 2 — Sanctification: Avimi pointed to a vessel resting on the ground and said: The priest places the handful into this vessel to sanctify it.
↓
Rav Naḥman's Objection: This requires three priests — one to raise the vessel for removal, one to sanctify, and one to actually remove the handful!
↓
Avimi's Response: "And let it require even three priests!" — Multiple priests for a single rite poses no halakhic problem.
↓
Rav Naḥman's Counter from the Mishna (12a): The mishna lists all rites of a meal offering and never mentions raising the vessel — implying it is not required.

📖 The Story of Avimi and Rav Ḥisda — Humility in Torah

איעקר מיניה מסכת מנחות — "tractate Menaḥot was uprooted from him"
Role Reversal Rav Ḥisda said: "I absorbed many blows (kulfei) from Avimi — he would mock me when I questioned him." This implies Avimi was Rav Ḥisda's teacher. Yet the Gemara also says Rabbi Avimi "learned in the study hall of Rav Ḥisda" — as if Rav Ḥisda were the teacher! Resolution: Avimi was originally the teacher, but tractate Menaḥot was 'uprooted' from him — he forgot it entirely. So Avimi humbled himself and came before his own student, Rav Ḥisda, to relearn what he had lost. When Rav Naḥman encountered Avimi, he was returning from this very session. Lesson: Even a great Sage must humble himself to relearn — even from his own student.

⬆️ Rava's Two Dilemmas: Vessel Resting on the Ground

Removing the Handful

  • Rava: Obviously permitted
  • Proof: The frankincense bowls rest on the Table of the shewbread — the Table is not held off the ground
  • Rav Sheshet: Supported this ruling from the mishna about the shewbread replacement
  • Conclusion: Valid to remove a handful from a vessel resting on the ground

Sanctifying the Handful

  • Rava: Unresolved dilemma
  • If derived from meal offering sanctification → may require raising off ground
  • If derived from blood collection laws → ground-level placement may be valid
  • Gemara challenges: Rava's own ruling on blood suggests he derives from blood — yet he still left this unresolved
  • Conclusion: The dilemma stands (תיקו)
