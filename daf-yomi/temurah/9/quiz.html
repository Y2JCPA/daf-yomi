Temurah 9 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Reish Lakish, why is mixing the oil of a meal offering outside the Temple courtyard valid?
A. The Torah explicitly permits post-handful rites to be performed anywhere
B. After the handful is removed, the remaining rites are merely a priestly mitzva, not obligatory Temple service
C. Oil mixing never required the Temple courtyard even before handful removal
D. The verse about mixing applies only to the Tabernacle, not the permanent Temple
Correct: B — Reish Lakish derives from the verse 'he shall pour oil upon it and put frankincense' that pouring and mixing after handful-removal are only a priestly mitzva. Since they are not full Temple service, the courtyard location requirement does not apply.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
If a meal offering lacks its full measure of flour before the handful is removed, what does Rabbi Yoḥanan rule?
A. The offering is immediately and permanently disqualified
B. The priest removes the handful anyway and the lack is overlooked
C. The owner may bring additional flour from home to refill the missing portion
D. The Temple storehouse supplies the missing flour at no cost to the owner
Correct: C — Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that the handful has not yet 'established' the offering — therefore additions remain permitted. The owner refills from home. Reish Lakish disagrees, ruling it disqualified from the moment it was found lacking.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What baraita proof does Rabbi Yoḥanan cite to show that Rabbi Yehoshua permits burning the handful even when only part of the remainder survives?
A. A burnt offering: if half an olive-bulk of flesh and half of fat remain (totaling one olive-bulk), the blood may still be sprinkled
B. A guilt offering: the priest burns the handful even if the entire remainder was lost to fire
C. The leper's log: oil may be poured even when the log is only partially filled
D. Shewbread: the frankincense may be burned even after the bread crumbles to dust
Correct: A — The baraita states that for a burnt offering, even partial remnants combining to an olive-bulk permit sprinkling the blood. This proves Rabbi Yehoshua validates rites when any portion of the remainder survives — supporting Rabbi Yoḥanan's position.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rabbi Zeira derive that removing the handful with the left hand disqualifies a meal offering?
A. Directly from the phrase 'the priest shall take' — 'take' always implies the dominant (right) hand
B. From an analogy to the High Priest's Yom Kippur service, where the right hand is always used
C. From four mentions of 'left hand' in the leper purification passage — restriction after restriction amplifies, proving right hand is the general rule
D. From the verse 'the right hand of the Lord is exalted,' applied homiletically to all Temple service
Correct: C — Rabbi Zeira applies the principle that restriction after restriction (ribbui achar ribbui) amplifies. The four mentions of 'left hand' in Leviticus 14 mark the leper rite as a special exception, thereby establishing that all other Temple rites — including handful removal — require the right hand.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rav Malkiyu argues that two verses in Leviticus 2 together refute Rabbi Yoḥanan on both disputes. How does the Gemara ultimately respond?
A. The Gemara accepts the refutation — Rabbi Yoḥanan's rulings are overturned by the two verses
B. The Gemara says one verse teaches when refilling is permitted (pre-handful lacking), and the other teaches the handful may be burned on any surviving portion — supporting Rabbi Yoḥanan, not refuting him
C. The Gemara rules that both verses address only intentional shortfall, not accidental lacking
D. The Gemara says the verses apply only to communal meal offerings, not individual ones
Correct: B — The Gemara rejects Rav Malkiyu's refutation by reinterpreting the verses in Rabbi Yoḥanan's favor: the first verse permits refilling a lacking offering before the handful (Rabbi Yoḥanan's lenient view), and the second verse permits burning the handful on whatever remainder survives — both supporting, not undermining, Rabbi Yoḥanan.
