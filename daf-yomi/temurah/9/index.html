⚖️ Three Disputes: Rabbi Yoḥanan vs. Reish Lakish

Reish Lakish
Mixing oil outside the courtyard: Valid. After the handful is removed, the remaining rites are merely a priestly mitzva — not obligatory Temple service. Location is therefore unrestricted.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Mixing oil outside: Disqualified. Even if priesthood is not required, the meal offering must still be performed inside the Temple courtyard — the service-vessel requirement implies location sanctity.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Offering lacks flour before handful: Owner refills from home. The handful has not yet 'established' the offering, so additions remain possible.
Reish Lakish
Offering lacks flour before handful: Disqualified immediately. A lacking measure invalidates the offering from the outset — no refilling permitted.
Core Question Do post-handful rites require full Temple service conditions (inside courtyard, service vessel), or are they merely priestly mitzvot valid even outside?

🫗 Dispute 1: Mixing Oil Outside the Courtyard

Reish Lakish
Valid. The verse 'he shall pour oil and put frankincense' teaches that pouring and mixing after handful-removal are a mitzva of the priesthood alone — not binding Temple service. Therefore location is irrelevant.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Disqualified. Granted, a non-priest may perform these rites. But a service vessel is still required — and using a service vessel demands the sanctity of the Temple courtyard. Outside = invalid.
Key Distinction Both agree that priesthood is not strictly required post-handful. They disagree whether the location requirement (inside courtyard) survives independently of the priesthood requirement.

🌾 Dispute 2 & 3: Lacking Meal Offering — When Does It Matter?

Meal offering lacks its full measure of flour
↓
⬅ Before Handful Removed
Rabbi Yoḥanan: Owner refills from home — handful not yet established

Reish Lakish: Disqualified — lacking measure invalidates immediately
After Handful Removed ➡
Rabbi Yoḥanan: Burn the handful — any remaining portion of the remainder is sufficient

Reish Lakish: Remainder disqualified — handful may not be burned on its account
↓
Proof text (Nega'im 14:10): Log of oil for a leper — lacking before priest pours → priest refills; lacking after priest pours → disqualified. Rabbi Yoḥanan uses this to support both his rulings.

📜 Who Does Rabbi Yehoshua Support? Two Readings

Reish Lakish's Reading of Rabbi Yehoshua

  • Rabbi Yehoshua: if remainder is entirely destroyed, the handful cannot be burned
  • Therefore: a 'lacking' remainder is likewise disqualified
  • Verse 'from the meal offering' = from an intact meal offering only
  • Shewbread baraita: bread broke before detachment → frankincense cannot be burned
  • Conclusion: Reish Lakish follows the plain meaning of Rabbi Yehoshua

Rabbi Yoḥanan's Reading of Rabbi Yehoshua

  • Baraita: 'any offering with even a small remainder' → blood may still be sprinkled
  • Verse 'from the meal offering' = from a meal offering already whole at time of handful
  • Shewbread baraita follows only Rabbi Eliezer (not Rabbi Yehoshua)
  • Rabbi Eliezer: even if bread burned entirely, frankincense may be burned
  • Proof: baraita specifies bread 'broke' — if following Eliezer, why mention breaking at all?

✋ Deriving That the Left Hand Disqualifies Handful Removal

Mishna ruling: A priest who removes the handful (kometz) with his left hand has disqualified the meal offering
↓
Rabbi Zeira's source: Derived from the leper purification passage (Leviticus 14) — the Torah specifies 'left hand' four times in that section
↓
Four mentions: Two for oil poured onto the priest's own left palm; two for oil applied to the leper's left thumb and toe
↓
Hermeneutical principle: Ribbui achar ribbui — restriction after restriction amplifies. The leper passage restricts to 'left' as a special exception, implying that generally right hand is required
↓
Challenge 1: Perhaps conclude the opposite — just as the leper uses the left hand, so too Temple rites generally use the left?
Answer: Four mentions of 'left' in one passage signal exception, not the rule
↓
Rabbi Yirmeya's challenge: The verse 'upon the thumb of his right hand' in the same leper passage — what additional teaching does this verse supply, given the left-hand derivation?
