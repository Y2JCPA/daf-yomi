🔥 Does Burning Render Burning Piggul?

Rava
Burning DOES render burning piggul. The Mishna (12a) treats burning like removal, placement, and conveying — in all of these, whether one's intent was to eat the remainder OR burn the frankincense the next day, the offering is piggul.
Gemara (rejection)
No — removal/placement/conveying are permitting acts (matirim) for the offering, so any improper intent during them renders piggul. The burning of the frankincense is not a permitting act, so intent to burn it does not create piggul.
Rav Ḥisda citing Rav
Burning does NOT render burning piggul. The handful is the permitting factor for the remainder — only intent during the handful's burning about consuming the remainder creates piggul. The frankincense permits nothing.
The Core Question If a priest burns the handful with intent to burn the frankincense the next day — is the meal offering rendered piggul (invalid with karet liability)?

⚖️ Rava vs. Rav Ḥisda: Can Burning Create Burning Piggul?

Rava
From the Mishna (12a): the rule applies equally to removal, placement, conveying, and burning of the handful. Just as intent to eat the remainder OR burn the frankincense next day during removal renders piggul — so too during burning!
Gemara (rejects Rava)
Those three acts all permit the offering — therefore any improper intent during them (whether regarding the remainder or the frankincense) is piggul-creating intent. Burning is different: it is not a permitting act, so intent to burn the frankincense (also non-permitting) cannot generate piggul.
Rav Menashya bar Gadda (citing Rav Ḥisda citing Rav)
Rabbi Meir's piggul rule applies only where the intent concerned the remainder during its permitting act — the handful. But frankincense is NOT the permitting factor for the remainder, so intent about it during burning is legally inert.
Accepted Ruling Rav and Rav Ḥisda: burning does NOT render burning piggul. Rav Ya'akov bar Idi attempted to bring support from the Passover lamb mishna, but the Gemara rejects it — each animal stands independently.

🤝 Rav Hamnuna's Teaching — Rabbi Ḥanina's Novelty

Setup: Rav Hamnuna says: Rabbi Ḥanina taught me something equivalent to all my other learning combined — a significant ḥiddush
↓
The Case: A priest burns the handful with two simultaneous intents: (1) to burn the frankincense the next day, AND (2) to eat the remainder the next day
↓
Why this is a question: Intent #1 (frankincense) alone would NOT render piggul per Rav. Does the combined intent still work? Does one invalid intent cancel the valid one?
↓
Rav Adda bar Ahava's resolution: Rabbi Ḥanina holds burning does NOT render burning piggul — frankincense intent alone fails. But the remainder intent (a proper piggul-type intent) DOES render it piggul, even when combined with the non-operative intent.
↓
The Ḥiddush: Mixed improper intent — one valid piggul-type + one non-operative type — the valid intent takes full effect. The offering is piggul.

📜 Mishna: Four Scenarios of Cross-Type Improper Intent

Priest removes the handful with improper intent about time or place
↓
Intent to CONSUME non-edible
E.g., eat the handful (which is burned) after its time
Intent to BURN non-burnable
E.g., burn the remainder (eaten by priests) after its time
Intent to CONSUME edible item
E.g., eat the remainder after its time — standard piggul case
Intent to BURN burnable item
E.g., burn the frankincense after its time
↓
Rabbi Eliezer: All four categories render the offering unfit (pasul) — based on he'akhol ye'akhel equating cross-type intent. Not piggul (no karet), but invalidated.
↓
Rabbis: Only same-type intent (consume edible / burn burnable) renders piggul. Cross-type intent (columns 1 & 2) has no legal effect — offering is fully permitted.

📖 The Verse Dispute: he'akhol ye'akhel (Lev. 7:18)

Rabbi Eliezer

  • he'akhol = infinitive → human consumption
  • ye'akhel = imperfect → burning on altar
  • Two different verb forms = equating cross-type intent
  • Intent to consume a burned item = like intent to consume an eaten item
  • Result: cross-type intent renders the offering unfit (pasul)
  • Liability: unfit by Torah law; disputed whether karet applies
  • Rabbi Eliezer: the mixed form proves the Torah meant to equate them

The Rabbis

  • Agree the two forms exist but derive differently
  • The Torah uses 'eating' language for altar-burning throughout
  • This simply shows 'consumption' and 'burning' are interchangeable terms
  • OR: repetition teaches that piggul requires intent involving an olive-bulk (kezayit)
  • Just as eating requires an olive-bulk minimum, so does piggul-intent
  • Result: cross-type intent has no legal effect at all
  • Only same-type improper intent beyond the time renders piggul

📋 Temurah 17 — Summary Map

Does burning the handful with intent to burn frankincense next day = piggul?
↓
Rav / Rav Ḥisda: NO — frankincense is not a matir; only intent about the remainder during the handful's burning creates piggul (accepted ruling)
Rava: YES — burning is listed alongside the other acts in the Mishna (rejected: those acts are matirim; burning is not)
↓
Rav Hamnuna's Ḥiddush: Combined intent (frankincense + remainder next day) → piggul, because the valid intent (remainder) takes effect even when paired with inoperative intent (frankincense)
↓
Mishna — Rabbi Eliezer: Intent to consume non-edible OR burn non-burnable after its time → offering is pasul (unfit, no karet). Derived from he'akhol ye'akhel equating cross-type intent.
Mishna — Rabbis: Cross-type intent has no legal effect. he'akhol ye'akhel teaches only the olive-bulk measure for piggul, not cross-type equation. Offering remains fully valid.
