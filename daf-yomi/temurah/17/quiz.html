QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the accepted ruling of Rav and Rav Ḥisda, if a priest burns the handful with intent to burn the frankincense the next day, what is the status of the meal offering?
A. Piggul — the priest is liable for karet
B. Not piggul — the frankincense is not the permitting factor for the remainder
C. Piggul according to Rabbi Meir but valid according to the Rabbis
D. Unfit (pasul) but not piggul — no karet liability
Correct: B — Rav Ḥisda citing Rav rules the offering is NOT piggul. The handful is the permitting factor (matir) for the remainder. Piggul requires improper intent about the remainder during its permitting act. The frankincense permits nothing, so intent about it during burning cannot create piggul.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the ḥiddush (novelty) in Rav Hamnuna's teaching from Rabbi Ḥanina about combined intent?
A. If a priest has two simultaneous intents, neither one takes legal effect
B. Intent to burn the frankincense alone is sufficient to render piggul
C. When intent to eat the remainder next day is combined with intent to burn the frankincense, the offering is piggul due to the valid intent
D. The frankincense serves as a second permitting factor alongside the handful
Correct: C — Per Rav Adda bar Ahava's resolution: Rabbi Ḥanina holds burning does not render burning piggul, so frankincense-intent alone fails. But the added intent to eat the remainder next day is a proper piggul-creating intent, and it takes effect even when combined with the non-operative intent about the frankincense.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
In the Mishna, how does Rabbi Eliezer rule regarding a priest who removes the handful with intent to consume, after its time, an item not typically consumed (like the handful itself)?
A. The offering is fully valid — only consuming an edible item creates piggul
B. The offering is piggul and the priest is liable for karet
C. The offering is unfit (pasul) but not piggul — no karet liability
D. The ruling depends on whether the frankincense was also burned improperly
Correct: C — Rabbi Eliezer rules the offering is pasul (unfit) but not piggul — there is no karet liability. He derives from he'akhol ye'akhel that cross-type intent (consuming non-edible portions) invalidates the offering, but it does not reach the status of piggul with its karet penalty.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rabbi Eliezer interpret the doubled expression he'akhol ye'akhel in Leviticus 7:18?
A. The repetition establishes that two witnesses are needed to impose karet for piggul
B. He'akhol (infinitive) refers to human eating; ye'akhel (imperfect) refers to altar burning — equating cross-type intent
C. The doubled form teaches that piggul requires intent involving at least an olive-bulk
D. The verse teaches that only intent expressed verbally (not mentally) renders piggul
Correct: B — Rabbi Eliezer reads he'akhol as a reference to human consumption and ye'akhel as a reference to burning on the altar. By using two distinct verb forms, the Torah equates improper intent regarding an item-for-eating with intent regarding an item-for-burning, thereby invalidating the offering when cross-type intent occurs.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Elazar in the baraita regarding Rabbi Eliezer's view?
A. Rabbi Yehuda holds that cross-type intent renders piggul; Rabbi Elazar holds it only renders the offering unfit
B. Rabbi Yehuda holds that leaving blood for the next day is unfit per Rabbi Eliezer with no karet; Rabbi Elazar adds that even in that case Rabbi Eliezer says unfit while Rabbis say valid — the dispute is over karet liability
C. Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Elazar disagree over whether the Rabbis' view is correct
D. Rabbi Yehuda limits the baraita to meal offerings; Rabbi Elazar extends it to blood offerings
Correct: B — The Gemara concludes that both Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Elazar agree on the invalidation in the case of leaving blood for the next day per Rabbi Eliezer. Their dispute is over karet: Rabbi Yehuda implies no karet; Rabbi Elazar holds that even in this boundary case Rabbi Eliezer says unfit while the Rabbis say valid, suggesting they dispute whether karet applies to cross-type intent scenarios.
