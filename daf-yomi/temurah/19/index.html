🔤 Two Hermeneutical Principles at Stake

Rabbis
A verse is interpreted as referring to both the matter preceding it and the matter following it — 'with his finger' applies to both the preceding pouring and the following waving.
R. Shimon
The vav (וְ) conjunction links the verse to the preceding matter only — 'And he shall bring it to Aaron's sons, the priests' (Lev 2:2) ties the priest requirement back to the preceding act of pouring the oil.
Core Verse "And he shall pour oil upon it and put frankincense upon it. And he shall bring it to Aaron's sons, the priests" (Lev 2:1–2) — does 'the priests' govern pouring (preceding) or only the handful (following)?

🧩 Does Vav Add to the Previous Matter?

R. Shimon
Yes — vav links 'the priests' backward to oil-pouring. Performing that rite without a priest invalidates the offering. This is the plain meaning of the conjunction.
Gemara Challenge
But Lev 1:5 states: 'And he shall slaughter the bull … and Aaron's sons the priests shall bring the blood' — does vav make slaughter require a priest too?
R. Shimon
No — the prior verse describes the owner placing hands, and the owner slaughters. Since blood-sprinkling (priestly) cannot be derived from that owner-chain, we cannot extend vav to slaughter.
Gemara
Why not derive: just as placing hands is owner-only, so too slaughter is owner-only? — Because blood-sprinkling follows, and an owner cannot sprinkle; deriving the possible from the impossible fails.
Principle Where the chain of owner-acts makes extension impossible, the vav conjunction does not impose a new priestly requirement.

📜 Rav's Rule: Law + Statute = Indispensable Rite

Rav's Core Principle: Wherever the Torah writes both law (תּוֹרָה) and statute (חֻקָּה) for a sacrificial rite, their co-occurrence signals that failure to perform the rite invalidates the offering.
↓
Refinement 1: Even where only one term appears, if the other is found nearby in context, Rav's rule still applies — the Gemara resolves challenges by locating the missing term in adjacent verses.
↓
Refinement 2: When 'statute' is written with regard to eating the offering (e.g., 'statute forever' for shewbread), it governs the entire offering — all rites are thereby indispensable.
↓
Exception — Meal Offering: Even though 'statute forever' is written for the meal offering's eating (Lev 6:11), only the rites repeated in the verses are indispensable — 'of its groats, and of its oil' (Lev 2:16) limits the scope.
↓
Result: The groats and oil are indispensable; other elements (e.g., frankincense placement details) are not invalidating, despite the general statutory language.

⚔️ Rav vs Shmuel: Repeated Rites in the Meal Offering

Rav

  • Every meal offering rite repeated in the Torah is indispensable
  • 'His handful' (Lev 2:2) + 'with his hand' (Lev 6:8) = hand is mentioned twice → using the hand, not a vessel, is indispensable
  • Aaron's eighth-day service ('he filled his hand') proves for all generations that a vessel is invalid
  • Derives permanent halakha from Aaron's service — it is not merely a one-time event

Shmuel

  • Agrees: repeated rites in the verses are certainly indispensable
  • Disputes Rav's source: Aaron's eighth-day service was הוֹרָאַת שָׁעָה — a one-time temporary situation
  • One does not derive a halakha for all generations from a temporary event
  • The twelve repetitions of the princes' offerings — that is sufficient to derive a permanent rule, since each prince separately confirms the same rite

🏛️ Locating the Meal Offering on the Altar

Priest must bring the handful to the altar — but where exactly?
↓
Verse 1: 'In front of the altar'
Suggests the southern face of the altar — the side facing outward
Verse 2: 'Before the Lord'
Suggests the western face — oriented toward the Sanctuary entrance
↓
R. Eliezer's Principle: Any time two options exist, choose the one that fulfills both verses simultaneously — neither alone is sufficient
↓
✅ Southwestern Corner — satisfies 'in front of the altar' (south) and 'before the Lord' (west) at once
↓
Rav Ashi: R. Eliezer holds the altar stood in the northern part of the courtyard — its western face therefore faced the Sanctuary, making 'before the Lord' = west
