When Is a Nazirite Released From His Vows?

Rabbi Eliezer
"After that the nazirite may drink wine" means after ALL actions — offerings, shaving, and waving must all be completed.
The Rabbis
"After" means after a single action — once one offering is sacrificed, the nazirite may drink wine even without shaving.
Rav
The priest's lack of waving (tenufah) precludes the release of the nazirite's prohibitions.
Rabbi Shimon
Once the blood of one offering has been sprinkled, the nazirite is permitted to drink wine and contract corpse-impurity.
Core Dispute Does "after that" (Numbers 6:20) refer to completion of the entire nazirite ritual, or just a single component?

When May a Nazirite Drink Wine?

Rabbi Eliezer
"And after that the nazirite may drink wine" (Numbers 6:20) — after all the actions: sacrificing all offerings and shaving.
The Rabbis
"After" means after a single action. He may drink wine after sacrificing one offering, even before shaving.
Gemara
The Rabbis derive this by gezeirah shavah (verbal analogy): "after" in v. 20 parallels "after" in v. 19, which refers to a single action — shaving.
Rabbi Shimon
Once the blood of one offering is sprinkled, the nazirite may drink wine and contract corpse-impurity.
Gezeirah Shavah The word "after" (אחר) appears in both verses 19 and 20, linking them: just as v. 19 means after one act (shaving), v. 20 means after one act (one offering).

The Bald Nazirite: Must He Pass a Razor?

Beit Shammai

  • He need not pass a razor over his head
  • Version 1 (Ravina): He has no remedy — can never complete naziriteship
  • Version 2 (R' Avina): He has a remedy — razor is not required at all

Beit Hillel

  • He must pass a razor over his head
  • Version 1 (Ravina): Passing razor fulfills the mitzvah even without hair
  • Version 2 (R' Avina): He must shave; if he cannot, he has no remedy
Two Versions Ravina and Rabbi Avina give opposite readings of the Beit Shammai/Beit Hillel dispute regarding the bald nazirite.

Rabbi Pedat's Unifying Principle

בית שמאי ורבי אליעזר אמרו דבר אחד
Key Insight Rabbi Pedat said: Beit Shammai and Rabbi Eliezer follow the same principle — if the Torah mandates a ritual and it cannot be performed precisely as delineated, the person has no remedy and cannot fulfill the obligation.

Example: A leper missing a thumb cannot receive the blood/oil placement (Leviticus 14:14). Rabbi Eliezer says he can never attain purity. Rabbi Shimon says place it on the spot. The Rabbis say use the left thumb.
