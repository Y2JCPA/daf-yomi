Woman's Nazirite Vow Nullified by Husband — What Happens to Her Offerings?

Woman vows naziriteship and separates animals for offerings
↓
Husband nullifies her vow
↓
Whose animal was it?
His Animal
Goes out to graze among the flock — loses sanctity entirely
Her Animal
Sin-offering → left to die
Burnt-offering → sacrificed as olah
Peace-offering → sacrificed, eaten one day, no bread
Key Principle Once the husband nullifies the vow, the woman no longer needs these offerings — this changes whether the husband bears any obligation for them.

Mishna: Fate of the Separated Animals

Animal Was His

  • Goes out to graze among the flock
  • Treated as non-consecrated
  • Waits until it gets a blemish
  • Then may be used for any purpose

Animal Was Hers

  • Sin-offering (chatat) → left to die
  • Burnt-offering (olah) → sacrificed on altar
  • Peace-offering (shelamim) → sacrificed
  • Eaten for ONE day only (not two)
  • Does NOT require bread (loaves/wafers)
Why the Difference? If the animal was his, after nullification there's no valid vow to sustain the consecration. If hers, each offering type follows its own rule for what to do with an ownerless consecrated animal.

Who Is the Tanna? — Husband's Obligation to Fund Offerings

Rav Ḥisda
The mishna follows the Rabbis — a husband is not indebted to his wife for her offerings.
Rav Ḥisda
If it were R' Yehuda, the husband's animal would stay consecrated — because he's obligated to provide her offerings!
R' Yehuda
(Baraita) A husband brings the offering of a rich man on behalf of his wife, even for obligations from before their marriage.
R' Yehuda
He wrote in the ketubah: "I accept all claims of guarantee upon me from beforehand."
Rava
You can even say it's R' Yehuda! He's only obligated for what she needs. After nullification, she doesn't need it anymore.
Two Versions The Gemara presents an alternative version where Rav Ḥisda attributes the mishna to R' Yehuda (not the Rabbis), applying the same logic that obligation only exists for necessary offerings.

Allocated Funds — What Happens to Consecrated Money?

Offering TypeFate of MoneyMisuse Liability (Me'ilah)
Sin-offering (Chatat)Cast into the Dead Sea (destroyed)No benefit allowed, but no me'ilah liability
Burnt-offering (Olah)Used to bring a burnt-offeringFull me'ilah liability applies
Peace-offering (Shelamim)Used to bring a peace-offeringEaten for one day, no bread required
Unallocated fundsAll go to communal gift offerings (nedavah)Treated as voluntary communal donations
Allocated vs. Unallocated If she hadn't specified which coins go to which offering, all funds default to communal nedavah offerings. Only when funds are designated do the specific rules per offering type apply.

How Does a Married Woman Own Property?

Mishna says: "If the animal was hers…"
↓
But a married woman's acquisitions belong to her husband!
How can she own an animal?
Rav Pappa
She saved from her dough — ate less and used the savings to buy the animal
Alternative
Another person gave it to her on condition that the husband has no rights to it
Halakhic Background The general rule is mah she-kantah ishah kantah ba'alah — what a wife acquires, her husband acquires. These two scenarios are exceptions.

The Four Nazirite Rams That Don't Require Bread

🍞 Normally, a nazirite's peace-offering (ram) is accompanied by loaves and wafers
↓
👩 Hers — Woman whose husband nullified her nazirite vow
↓
👦 His — Father vowed son as nazirite, but the son (or relatives) objected
↓
⚰️ After Death — Owner died before bringing the offering
↓
✅ After Atonement — Owner already gained atonement through a different offering
↓
💡 Common logic: in all four cases, the offering is no longer fit for its original atonement purpose
Shmuel to Avuh bar Ihi Shmuel urgently demanded: "You shall not sit on your feet until you explain this baraita to me!" — emphasizing the importance of understanding these four exceptions.
