Nazir 22: The Chain Reaction of Linked Vows

נדרה באשה נזירות ושמעה חברתה ואמרה ואני — והפר לה בעלה של ראשונה: היא מותרת וחברתה אסורה
CORE QUESTION When a woman vows naziriteship and another person links their vow to hers, what happens if the first vow is nullified by the husband? Does the linked vow survive — and does the answer depend on whether nullification severs (cuts going forward) or uproots (erases retroactively)?
3 / 9

Bird Offerings After Nullification

Baraita
A woman who vowed naziriteship, became ritually impure, and then her husband nullified — she brings the bird sin-offering but not the bird burnt-offering.
Gemara (challenge)
If the husband severs (cuts forward), she should also bring the burnt-offering — she was a nazirite when impure!
Gemara (counter)
But if he uproots entirely, she shouldn't bring the sin-offering either!
Resolution
She indeed should not bring the sin-offering. This baraita follows R' Elazar HaKappar — the sin-offering atones for the vow itself, not the impurity.
KEY OPINION R' Elazar HaKappar: A nazirite is called a sinner for depriving himself of wine. The sin-offering atones for taking the vow, not for impurity.

The Nazirite as Sinner

ומה זה שלא ציער עצמו אלא מן היין נקרא חוטא — המצער עצמו מכל דבר על אחת כמה וכמה
R' ELAZAR HAKAPPAR "And make atonement for him, for that he sinned by reason of the soul" (Numbers 6:11)

With which soul did the nazirite sin? Because he deprived himself of wine, he is called a sinner.

Kal va-chomer: If one who deprives himself only of wine is a sinner, one who deprives himself of everything through fasting — all the more so!

Two Women: Linked Nazirite Vows

Woman A vows naziriteship
↓
Woman B hears and says: "And I" (ואני)
↓
Husband of Woman A nullifies her vow
Tanna Kamma
Woman A: permitted
Woman B: prohibited
(Husband severs, not uproots)
R' Shimon
If B said "I am like you" — both permitted
(Vow was fully dependent)
DISTINCTION "And I" = independent link (survives nullification). "I am like you" = fully dependent (dissolved with the original).

Rami bar Ḥama's Analogy: Peace-Offering Flesh

Fundamental State

  • Associate with the object's original status
  • Peace-offering flesh is forbidden before blood-sprinkling
  • Woman's vow was active before nullification
  • → Vow takes effect / Second woman is bound

Eventual Permitted State

  • Associate with the object's final status
  • Peace-offering flesh is permitted after sprinkling
  • Woman's vow was nullified eventually
  • → Vow ineffective / Second woman is free
GEMARA'S DISTINCTION The cases may not be identical: peace-offering flesh retains some sanctity even when permitted, but a nullified vow has no vow at all.

Husband Says 'And I' — Can He Nullify?

Mishna
A woman vowed naziriteship. Her husband said "And I" — he cannot nullify her vow.
Gemara (inference)
If 'And I' meant linking to the fundamental state only, he could nullify hers and keep his own!
Gemara (conclusion?)
So it must mean he links to all matters — nullifying hers would nullify his own. That's why he can't.
Gemara (rejection)
No — saying "And I" is equivalent to saying "It is upheld for you" (kiyum). He must first get a sage to dissolve his upholding, then he can nullify.
PRACTICAL RULE A husband who joins his wife's vow implicitly upholds it. He needs a halakhic authority to dissolve the upholding before he can nullify.
