QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Beit Shammai, why is the person a nazirite when he makes an absurd vow involving a cow?
A. Because a person does not utter a statement for naught — 'I am a nazirite' is extracted as binding
B. Because the cow is a valid nazirite offering
C. Because meat and wine are always paired in halacha
D. Because the cow standing fulfills a biblical condition
Correct: A — Beit Shammai's core principle is that a person doesn't speak for naught. They extract the declaration 'I am a nazirite' and disregard the nonsensical part about refraining from the cow's flesh.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the Gemara say all three cases (figs, cow, door) are necessary?
A. Each case involves a different type of nazirite offering
B. Each eliminates a reason to limit the ruling — figs resemble grapes, meat pairs with wine, but a door has no food connection at all
C. They represent three different time periods in Jewish law
D. Each case was taught by a different Tanna
Correct: B — Without all three, one might limit Beit Shammai's ruling: figs resemble grapes, meat pairs with wine. The door case proves the principle applies even to something completely unrelated to food or drink.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
In Rava's final explanation, what is the core dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel?
A. Whether cows can be nazirite offerings
B. Whether the vow was stated in proper Hebrew
C. Whether 'it stood' means by his hand (intent) or simply that it rose (literal condition)
D. Whether one can become a nazirite conditionally at all
Correct: C — Beit Shammai interpret his intent as wanting to stand it by his own hand (which he failed to do, so the naziriteship takes effect). Beit Hillel say his intent was simply that the cow was prone — and now it stood, so the condition is fulfilled and no naziriteship applies.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What difficulty does the Gemara raise against Rava's first explanation (that he dedicated the cow as a nazirite offering)?
A. A cow cannot be slaughtered for an offering
B. A door cannot be an offering — so the parallel case wouldn't work
C. Beit Hillel would agree in that case
D. The mishna doesn't mention offerings
Correct: B — If the explanation is that he declared the cow as his nazirite offering (thereby accepting naziriteship), the parallel case of the door fails — a door cannot possibly be an offering.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to R' Yehuda, what do Beit Shammai actually hold in this mishna?
A. The person is a full nazirite with all restrictions
B. The person must bring a sin-offering for a false vow
C. The cow becomes forbidden to him as an offering (neder), but he is not a nazirite
D. The person must observe naziriteship for 30 days as a penalty
Correct: C — R' Yehuda limits Beit Shammai's ruling: the vow only makes the cow forbidden to him like an offering (a standard neder/prohibition), but even Beit Shammai concede this does not constitute actual naziriteship.
