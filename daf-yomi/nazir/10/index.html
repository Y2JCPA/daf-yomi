Can a Vow Tied to a Cow or a Door Create Naziriteship?

Mishna
If one said: 'This cow said: I am a nazirite if I stand up' — or: 'This door says: I am a nazirite if I am opened'...
Beit Shammai
He is a nazirite. A person does not utter a statement for naught — the words 'I am a nazirite' are binding.
Beit Hillel
He is not a nazirite. The entire statement is nonsensical and cannot create a vow.
R' Yehuda
Even Beit Shammai only meant he vowed the cow forbidden as an offering — not that he actually became a nazirite.
Core Question When someone makes an absurd conditional statement that includes nazirite language, do we extract the binding element or dismiss the whole thing?

The Mishna's Absurd Vow Scenarios

Beit Shammai

  • He IS a nazirite
  • A person doesn't speak for naught
  • We extract 'I am a nazirite' from the statement
  • The rest ('refrain from its flesh') is disregarded

Beit Hillel

  • He is NOT a nazirite
  • The vow is tied to nonsensical conditions
  • Naziriteship requires vowing from vine products
  • Cow flesh and doors have nothing to do with naziriteship
R' Yehuda's Qualification Even Beit Shammai only meant the cow becomes forbidden as an offering — they concede it is not actually a vow of naziriteship.

Rami bar Ḥama's Explanation: The Prone Cow

Gemara
Does a cow speak?! What does the mishna mean?
Rami bar Ḥama
A prone cow was before him. He tried to make it stand and failed. He said: 'This cow thinks it won't stand — I am a nazirite and will refrain from its flesh if it stands on its own.' Then it stood.
Gemara
But didn't Beit Shammai already rule on this same principle in the previous mishna about figs?
Rava
It is normal for the Sages to cite two or three examples from different cases, even when they reflect the same principle.
Key Principle Beit Shammai extract the nazirite declaration; Beit Hillel view the entire statement — including its absurd condition — as a single unit.

Why Are All Three Cases Necessary?

Case 1: Dried figs & cakes
↓
Maybe Beit Shammai only rule 'nazirite' because figs can be confused with grapes?
↓
Case 2: Cow's flesh (meat)
↓
Maybe only because meat pairs with wine, so wine was on his mind?
↓
Case 3: A door
↓
Even something completely unrelated to food — Beit Shammai still say nazirite
Conclusion Each case eliminates a potential limitation. Together they prove Beit Shammai's rule applies universally — and Beit Hillel disagree in all cases.

ScholarScenarioDifficulty Raised

Note Each explanation is challenged, pushing toward Rava's final reading that centers on the cow standing contrary to his expectation.

Rava's Final Explanation: Step by Step

🐄 A prone cow lies before him, refusing to stand
↓
🗣️ He declares: 'I am a nazirite and will refrain from wine if it does NOT stand — since I will force it up!'
↓
💪 He tries to force the cow to stand — but fails
↓
🐄 The cow then stands up ON ITS OWN
↓
⚖️ Beit Shammai: His intent was to stand it by HIS hand — he didn't, so the naziriteship takes effect
↓
⚖️ Beit Hillel: His intent was that it was prone — now it stood. Condition fulfilled, no naziriteship
Dispute Focus The core disagreement: do we follow the literal condition (it stood = condition met) or the underlying intent (he meant to stand it himself)?
