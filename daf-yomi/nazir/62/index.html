Ambiguous Intimations of Naziriteship (Yados)

Gemara
The verse "when a man or woman shall clearly utter" (Num. 6:2) — naziriteship is already juxtaposed to vows. Why is it needed?
Answer
To include yados — ambiguous, partial expressions of a nazirite vow.
Abaye
Ambiguous intimations are valid intimations — the vow takes effect.
Rava
Ambiguous intimations are not valid — the vow does not take effect.
Gemara
If we follow Rava, what does the verse teach?
R' Tarfon
If people vowed naziriteship as a wager on an uncertain event, none are nazirites — naziriteship requires explicit, definitive enunciation.
Nafka Mina If you say 'I am like a nazir if X happens' as a bet — R' Tarfon says you're not a nazir; the Rabbis disagree.

Wife vs. Slave: Vow Nullification (Mishna)

Wife (Isha)

  • Husband can nullify her vows (hafarah)
  • Nullification is permanent
  • Even if later divorced or widowed, the vow stays nullified
  • True legal annulment of the vow itself

Slave (Eved)

  • Master cannot nullify (meifer) his vows
  • Can only force (kofeh) the slave to violate
  • If slave is emancipated, he must complete his naziriteship
  • The vow itself was never annulled — only suspended
Stringency The Mishna says slaves have a greater stringency — their vows survive, merely suppressed by the master's authority.

Why Can a Master Force Only Naziriteship?

Gemara
The verse says "to bind his soul with a bond" — only one whose soul is in his own possession. This excludes a slave. But this verse is about vows generally!
Rav Sheshet
If a slave vowed not to eat one cluster of grapes: the vow only prohibits that cluster. The master has no reason to insist on that cluster. But naziriteship prohibits all grapes — weakening the slave.
Gemara
What if that's the only cluster available? Then the slave is weakened either way!
Rava
Case of a grape seed (ḥartzanah): a vow on one seed doesn't weaken the slave. But naziriteship prohibits all grape products, which does.
Gemara
What if the only thing before him is the grape seed?
Abaye
The distinction isn't about forcing — a slave's other vows and oaths never take effect at all. Only naziriteship takes effect and requires the master to actively force violation.
Resolution Abaye reinterprets the baraita: the master must force only naziriteship because other vows/oaths are simply void for a slave.
