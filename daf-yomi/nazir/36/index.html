Do Permitted & Forbidden Substances Combine?

Rav Dimi / R' Yochanan
Permitted and forbidden substances do not combine — except for a nazirite
Abaye
But teruma mixtures prove they do combine elsewhere! One is flogged for eating an olive-bulk of teruma soup!
Rav Dimi
That case involves eating a full olive-bulk of teruma within the time to eat a half-loaf — not actual combining
Abaye
Then why are the Rabbis lenient about Babylonian kutaḥ on Passover?
Rav Dimi
Kutaḥ is too pungent — one cannot consume an olive-bulk of leaven that quickly
Core Issue When forbidden food is diluted by permitted food, does the mixture carry Torah-level liability?

Ze'eiri: Leaven on the Altar

Leviticus 2:11 — "kol se'or v'khol devash"
(any leaven, any honey)
↓
Word "kol" implies leaven even in a mixture is forbidden on the altar
↓
Whose view? Rabbi Eliezer — who derives from "kol" in Exodus 12:20 that mixed leaven is forbidden on Passover too
↓
Ze'eiri's Innovation
Excludes Abaye's position that one is liable for less than an olive-bulk of leaven on the altar
Ze'eiri Teaches
Less than an olive-bulk alone is not punishable — liability comes from combining permitted + forbidden to reach an olive-bulk
Key Dispute Ze'eiri uses 'kol' to establish combining — not to create liability for sub-olive-bulk amounts

Abaye Challenges: The Teruma Soup Case

Mishna (Tevul Yom 2:3)
Thick soup of teruma + garlic/oil of ḥullin: if a tevul yom touches it → all disqualified
Mishna
Thick soup of ḥullin + garlic/oil of teruma: if a tevul yom touches it → only the spot touched is disqualified
Abaye
Why is even the touched spot disqualified? The base is non-sacred!
R' Yochanan (via Rabba b. bar Ḥana)
Because a non-priest is flogged for eating an olive-bulk of the soup — teruma mixed in creates Torah liability
Abaye
This proves permitted combines with forbidden beyond the nazirite case!
Teruma Principle Anything into which teruma is mixed is considered teruma by Torah law — R' Yochanan

Rav Dimi's Defense: Combining vs. Olive-Bulk in Time

Permitted Combines with Forbidden

  • Less than olive-bulk of issur + heter = olive-bulk
  • The mixture itself is treated as forbidden
  • Applies ONLY to nazirite (per R' Yochanan)
  • Derived from 'kol' in the nazirite verse

Olive-Bulk in Time of Half-Loaf

  • Full olive-bulk of issur present in the mixture
  • Consumed within k'dei akhilat pras
  • Liability is for the forbidden substance alone
  • This explains the teruma soup case — no 'combining' needed
Rav Dimi's Distinction The teruma soup case doesn't prove combining — it proves one eats enough pure teruma fast enough to be liable

The Babylonian Kutaḥ Problem

שריף לה — שטר לה
🥣 Kutaḥ Abaye's challenge: If eating an olive-bulk in k'dei akhilat pras is Torah law, why do the Rabbis permit Babylonian kutaḥ (which contains bread) on Passover?

Rav Dimi's answer:
• If one swallows kutaḥ straight (shareif) — this is abnormal; no one eats pungent dip as food. No liability for anomalous behavior.
• If one dips food into kutaḥ (shatar) — one uses only a tiny amount per dip. One cannot consume an olive-bulk of leaven within the required timeframe.

Rav Dimi's Final Answer Spice teruma is only rabbinic (d'rabbanan) — Torah teruma applies only to grain, wine, and oil. The Sages are lenient with rabbinic teruma.
