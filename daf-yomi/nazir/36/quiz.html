QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Ze'eiri, what does the word 'kol' in Leviticus 2:11 teach about offering leaven on the altar?
A. One is liable even for leaven mixed with permitted substances
B. One is liable only for pure leaven
C. One is liable for less than an olive-bulk of pure leaven
D. The prohibition applies only to honey, not leaven
Correct: A — Ze'eiri derives from 'kol' that permitted and forbidden substances combine — meaning leaven in a mixture triggers liability on the altar, following Rabbi Eliezer's hermeneutic approach.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does Rav Dimi explain the teruma soup case without relying on 'permitted combines with forbidden'?
A. The soup is entirely teruma by rabbinic decree
B. One consumes a full olive-bulk of teruma within the time to eat a half-loaf of bread
C. The garlic and oil nullify the teruma
D. Tevul yom disqualification is only rabbinic
Correct: B — Rav Dimi argues the soup contains enough teruma that one eating it will consume an olive-bulk of actual teruma within k'dei akhilat pras — the forbidden substance alone reaches the required amount, no combining needed.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does Rav Dimi say Babylonian kutaḥ on Passover poses no Torah-level concern?
A. Kutaḥ contains no actual leaven
B. The leaven in kutaḥ is nullified (batel b'shishim)
C. Eating it straight is abnormal, and dipping yields too little leaven per bite
D. Kutaḥ is only rabbinically forbidden
Correct: C — Rav Dimi gives two reasons: swallowing pungent kutaḥ straight (shareif) is abnormal conduct for which there's no liability, and dipping food into it (shatar) uses so little that one cannot consume an olive-bulk of leaven in the required timeframe.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rav Dimi dismiss Abaye's objection from the spice mortars case?
A. He says the case is hypothetical and never happens
B. He argues the ruling follows Rabbi Eliezer only
C. He explains that teruma separated from spices is only rabbinic, not Torah-level
D. He says the double-doubt principle overrides Torah law
Correct: C — Rav Dimi explains that Torah-level teruma applies only to grain, wine, and oil. Teruma from spices is d'rabbanan, and the Sages are lenient with rabbinic teruma — so no Torah-level concern arises.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the core distinction R' Yochanan / Rav Dimi maintains throughout the sugya?
A. Torah prohibitions are stricter than rabbinic ones
B. Nazirite law is entirely unique with no parallels
C. Combining permitted with forbidden to reach an olive-bulk is different from consuming a full olive-bulk of issur within a timeframe
D. All mixtures are forbidden by Torah law
Correct: C — The entire sugya hinges on this distinction: 'combining' (tzeruf) means sub-olive-bulk forbidden + permitted = olive-bulk (unique to nazir), while 'olive-bulk in pras-time' means a full olive-bulk of the forbidden substance itself is consumed, requiring no combining principle.
