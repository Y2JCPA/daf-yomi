"I Am a Nazirite from Dried Figs"

Mishna
One says: "I am hereby a nazirite and will refrain from dried figs and fig cakes." What is the law?
Beit Shammai
He is a nazirite. The addition about figs is insignificant — only grape products are prohibited to a nazirite.
Beit Hillel
He is not a nazirite. He did not properly accept naziriteship upon himself.
R' Yehuda
Even Beit Shammai only meant when he said figs are forbidden like an offering — it's a vow on figs, not naziriteship.
Core Issue When someone combines a valid nazirite declaration with an impossible restriction (figs instead of grapes), do we follow the first clause or the last?

The Underlying Tannaitic Principles

Beit Shammai → R' Meir

  • A person does not utter a statement for naught
  • "I am a nazirite" — immediately effective
  • "From figs" = he misspoke, intended real naziriteship
  • First clause stands independently

Beit Hillel → R' Yosei / R' Shimon

  • A person is held to the conclusion of his statement
  • "From figs" shows he never intended grape-based naziriteship
  • This is a vow with its inherent opening (petach)
  • Not donated in the typical manner → void
Key Contrast R' Meir preserves the vow by ignoring the impossible clause; R' Yosei uses it to reveal the speaker's true intent.

Beit Shammai's Logic: Step by Step

Person says: "I am hereby a nazirite"
↓
Naziriteship takes effect immediately
(a person doesn't speak for naught)
↓
He then adds: "from dried figs"
↓
Is this a built-in opening to dissolve the vow?
No — Beit Shammai
Naziriteship = consecrated property.
One cannot request dissolution of consecrated vows.
He remains a full nazirite.
Yes — Beit Hillel
The ending clause reveals his intent.
He never meant real naziriteship.
He is not a nazirite.
Neder vs. Hekdesh Beit Shammai equate naziriteship with consecrated property (hekdesh), which cannot be dissolved by a halakhic authority.

R' Natan's Baraita — Two Versions

R' Natan (Version 1)
Beit Shammai: Figs are forbidden by vow AND he is a nazirite.
Beit Hillel: Figs are forbidden by vow but he is not a nazirite.
R' Natan (Version 2)
Beit Shammai: Figs are forbidden by vow but he is not a nazirite.
Beit Hillel: Figs are not forbidden and he is not a nazirite.
Gemara
Version 1: Beit Shammai follow R' Meir (nazirite) + R' Yehuda (fig vow). Beit Hillel follow R' Yosei (fig vow only).
Gemara
Version 2: Beit Shammai follow R' Yehuda (fig vow, no naziriteship). Beit Hillel follow R' Shimon (nothing at all).
Our Mishna The standard mishna does not match R' Natan's version, as R' Natan gives the figs independent vow-status in at least one reading of Beit Hillel.

Who Authored the Menachot Mishna?

Chizkiyya
The mishna follows Beit Shammai: just as they say a nazirite from figs is still a nazirite (we follow the first clause), so too a barley meal-offering becomes wheat.
R' Yochanan
Even Beit Hillel agree — the mishna speaks of one who later clarifies: "Had I known barley was invalid, I would have said wheat." This is an error, not a condition.
Chizkiyya
If so, only barley works (since omer/sotah use barley — plausible mistake). But lentils? No meal-offering uses lentils — clearly no intent to donate.
Gemara
But wait — figs are equally foreign to naziriteship, yet Beit Shammai say he's a nazirite! Chizkiyya retracted his position.
Rava's Proof If the mishna were Beit Shammai, it should teach the greater novelty of lentils, not just barley. Since it teaches barley specifically, it must not follow Beit Shammai.
