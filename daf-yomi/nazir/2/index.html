How Does One Become a Nazirite?

A person wants to take a nazirite vow (נזירות)
↓
Direct Vow
"I am a nazir" — explicit declaration
Substitutes (כינויים)
"I am a nazik, nazi'aḥ, or pazi'aḥ" — alternate words
Intimations (ידות)
"I will be" or "I will be beautiful" — implied intent
Key Principle All three methods — direct vows, substitutes, and intimations — create binding nazirite obligations

Why Is Tractate Nazir in Seder Nashim?

מה גרם לאשה לסור — יין
The Gemara's Answer The Torah discusses divorce: "because he has found some unseemly matter about her" (Deut. 24:1).

What caused the woman to transgress? Wine.

Therefore: Anyone who sees a sota in her disgrace should abstain from wine — linking Nazir to Sota and Gittin in Seder Nashim.

Substitutes (כינויים) vs. Intimations (ידות)

Substitutes (כינויים)

  • Alternate words for 'nazir'
  • "I am a nazik, nazi'aḥ, pazi'aḥ"
  • Explicitly mentioned in Torah-based halakha
  • Listed second in Mishna's examples

Intimations (ידות)

  • Incomplete statements implying nazir intent
  • "I will be" / "I will be beautiful"
  • Derived from exposition of verses
  • Listed first in Mishna's examples (though mentioned second in opening)
Why? Since intimations are derived from verse exposition and not explicit in Torah, they are dear to the tanna — so he explains them first

Why Does the Mishna Explain Intimations Before Substitutes?

Gemara
The tanna began with substitutes — why does he explain intimations first?
Rava
The Mishna is incomplete (חסורי מחסרא) — it should read: substitutes are like vows, and similarly intimations are like vows
Gemara
But still — explain substitutes first since they're mentioned first!
Gemara
The Mishna's style: explain the last topic first — like Shabbat 20b (lamp fuels), 47b (insulation), 57a (women's ornaments)
Challenge
But Shabbat 51b (animals) and Bava Batra 108a (inheritance) explain the first topic first!
Resolution
The tanna uses both styles depending on context — here, intimations are dear to him because they derive from verse exposition
Principle When the tanna begins, he starts with the main Torah halakha. When he explains, he favors topics derived from exposition.

"I Will Be" (אהא) — When Is It a Nazirite Vow?

A person says: "I will be" (אהא)
↓
Was a nazirite passing before him?
Yes ✓
His intent is clear — he is a nazirite
(Shmuel)
No ✗
Perhaps he means "I will be fasting" — not a nazirite
Shmuel's Principle Ambiguous intimations that provide no evidence of intent are not binding vows — context is required
7 / 9

Challenges to Shmuel's Rule

Gemara
Even with a nazirite passing — maybe he means "I will buy that nazirite's offerings" to exempt him?
Shmuel
The case is where he said in his heart that he accepts naziriteship upon himself
Gemara
If so, isn't it obvious he's a nazirite?
Shmuel
You might think his mouth and heart must be identical — that he must articulate clearly. The Mishna teaches: since his words can be interpreted as nazir, and that was his intent, he is a nazirite.
פיו ולבו שוין We do not require one's spoken words to perfectly match inner intent — interpretable speech plus genuine intention suffices
