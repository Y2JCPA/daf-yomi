Nazirite Discovers Impurity: Known vs. Unknown

A nazirite shaved to conclude his naziriteship
↓
He then discovers he was impure from a corpse during his term
↓
Known Impurity (טומאה ידועה)
Someone in the world knew about the corpse → Negates entire naziriteship
Tumat Tehom (טומאת התהום)
No one anywhere knew about the corpse → Does NOT negate naziriteship
KEY RULE If discovered before shaving, he negates in either case — known or unknown impurity.

Mishna: Known Impurity vs. Tumat Tehom

Known Impurity (טומאה ידועה)

  • Corpse floating openly at the mouth of a cave
  • Someone in the world was aware of it
  • Discovered after shaving → negates entire naziriteship
  • Discovered before shaving → also negates

Tumat Tehom (טומאת התהום)

  • Corpse sunk into the ground, completely hidden
  • No person anywhere knew about it
  • Discovered after shaving → does NOT negate
  • Discovered before shaving → DOES negate
CAVE EXAMPLE Descended to immerse → corpse floating = known. Corpse buried in floor = tumat tehom.

Scriptural Source for the Known/Unknown Distinction

R' Eliezer
From Numbers 6:9 — 'And if any man shall die beside him' (עליו). The emphasis on 'beside him' means: it must be clear to him that he became impure.
Reish Lakish
From Numbers 9:10 — 'impure due to a corpse or on a road far away.' Impurity is juxtaposed with 'road' — just as a road is in the open, so too the impurity must be in the open.
Gemara
Problem with R' Eliezer: The Tosefta says if even one person at the end of the earth knows, it's not tumat tehom. But R' Eliezer's verse focuses on what he knows — what does a distant person's knowledge matter?
Gemara
Further problem: A corpse across the road renders one impure for teruma but pure for naziriteship — this doesn't fit neatly with either verse derivation.
Resolution
The halakha of tumat tehom is actually a received tradition (הלכה). The verses are cited merely as support (אסמכתא), not as actual sources.
CONCLUSION The distinction between known and unknown impurity is a halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai — a tradition from Sinai, not derived from any verse.

Rami bar Chama's Dilemma: When Does Knowledge Matter?

A nazirite became impure during his term
↓
He only discovered the impurity after his full term ended but before shaving
↓
Follow the Knowledge?
Knowledge came after the term → perhaps he does not negate
Follow the Impurity?
Actual impurity was during the term → perhaps he negates
↓
Rava resolves: The mishna says 'before shaving he negates in either case' — this must include after the full term. He negates.
FOLLOW-UP Does he negate the entire term or just seven days? The mishna doesn't distinguish — indicating he negates only seven days.

Chazaka: Presumptive Status Governs

🧑 A person has a presumptive status (חזקה) — either pure or impure
↓
🏊 Descended to immerse (was impure) → found a hidden corpse → remains impure
↓
❄️ Descended to cool off (was pure) → found a hidden corpse → remains pure
↓
📏 Principle: דבר שבחזקת טומאה — טמא, דבר שבחזקת טהרה — טהור
↓
⚖️ 'There is a basis for the matter' — we follow established status unless definitively overturned
MISHNA'S RULE Chazaka is the tiebreaker: when impurity is uncertain, we rely on the person's prior established status.
