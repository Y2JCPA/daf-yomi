Nazir 32: Vows, Errors & Dissolution

DAF OVERVIEW This daf explores the interplay between erroneous vows and their consequences. The Gemara asks whose opinion the mishna follows regarding counting naziriteship days after transgression, examines what happens to consecrated animals when vows are dissolved, and recounts Nachum the Mede's error in dissolving nazirite vows after the Temple's destruction. The daf concludes with a mishna about conditional nazirite vows made about an approaching stranger, with a four-way dispute between Beit Shammai, Beit Hillel, Rabbi Tarfon, and Rabbi Shimon.

Transgressing Naziriteship: How Many Days Must Be Re-Observed?

Rabbis
A nazirite who transgressed (e.g., drank wine) must observe the exact number of days he failed to keep — only then can he seek dissolution or bring offerings.
R' Yosei
Thirty days is enough. He only needs to add extra days of observance if he transgressed for 30 days or more.
Gemara
The mishna says he counts from the time he vowed — this fits neither opinion perfectly!
Gemara
Resolution: The mishna can fit R' Yosei (referring to a short naziriteship under 30 days) or the Rabbis (emending: he counts like from the time he vowed).
KEY POINT The Gemara reconciles the mishna with both opinions through careful reading — either limiting the case or emending the text.

Dissolved Vows: What Happens to the Animal?

A nazirite separates an animal for his offering
↓
He requests dissolution from a chakham (halakhic authority)
↓
The vow is dissolved
↓
The animal goes out to graze among the flock — it is non-sacred
↓
R' Yirmeya's inference: Even Beit Hillel agree — when the initial consecration is uprooted, any substitute is also uprooted
R' YIRMEYA From Beit Shammai we learn about Beit Hillel: substitution only works when the original consecration still stands. If the underlying vow is dissolved, both original and substitute lose sanctity.

Animal Tithe: Error vs. Intentional Mislabeling

PositionError (called 9th 'Tenth')Intentional (knowingly mislabeled)
Rav Naḥman✅ Consecrated❌ Not consecrated
Rav Ḥisda & Rabba bar Rav Huna✅ Consecrated✅ All the more so consecrated
Rava's challenge to Rav NaḥmanIf error alone consecrates, Beit Hillel should have answered Beit Shammai: tithe is unique because intent doesn't consecrate — you can't learn other consecrations from it!
RAVA'S ARGUMENT If Rav Naḥman is correct that intentional mislabeling doesn't consecrate in tithe, Beit Hillel had an obvious rebuttal to Beit Shammai — yet they were silent. This silence suggests Rav Ḥisda's view is more compelling.

The Error of Nachum the Mede

🚶 Nazirites ascend from the exile to bring their offerings in Jerusalem
↓
🏚️ They arrive and find the Temple destroyed
↓
📜 Nachum the Mede asks: 'Had you known the Temple would be destroyed, would you have vowed?' They say: 'No!'
↓
🔓 He dissolves their vows based on this 'new situation' (nolad)
↓
❌ The Rabbis overrule him: Vows taken BEFORE the destruction cannot be dissolved — there was no error at the time of the vow
↓
✅ Only vows taken AFTER the destruction are invalid — those were genuinely based on a false assumption
CRITICAL DISTINCTION A new situation that arises AFTER a vow cannot retroactively dissolve it. Only a pre-existing error at the time of the vow invalidates it.

Can Vows Be Dissolved Through a 'New Situation' (Nolad)?

R' Eliezer
A halakhic authority may broach dissolution by raising a nolad — a new situation the person didn't anticipate when vowing.
Rabbis
They may not dissolve vows based on a nolad.
Rabba
The Rabbis overwhelmed R' Eliezer until he retracted and accepted their view — as evidenced by his silence in our mishna.
Rava
Even the Rabbis agree: you can broach dissolution through conditions resembling a nolad — e.g., 'If someone had told you the Temple would be destroyed, would you have vowed?'
Rav Yosef
Jeremiah 7:4 mentions 'Sanctuary' three times — hinting at three Temples, implying the second would be destroyed. They should have anticipated this!
Abaye
Daniel 9:24 gives a timeline of 490 years — they could have calculated when! But the Gemara responds: they didn't know the exact day.
RAVA'S MIDDLE PATH While a true nolad cannot dissolve a vow, presenting a hypothetical scenario that existed as a possibility at the time of the vow — 'What if someone had told you...' — is a valid method of dissolution.
