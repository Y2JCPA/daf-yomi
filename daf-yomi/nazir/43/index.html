How Can a Nazir Be Liable Twice?

Gemara
When entering a house with a corpse, one's hand enters first — he's already impure before his body enters! How can impurity and enclosure be simultaneous?
R' Elazar
If he inserts his hand first: liable only for impurity, not enclosure. Only if his whole body enters at once — both liabilities.
Gemara
But his nose would enter before his body!
Rava
He enters standing upright so his head doesn't enter first. Then impurity and enclosure happen simultaneously.
Gemara
But his toes would still enter first!
Problem No matter how one enters, some body part enters first — creating a sequence rather than simultaneity.

Solutions for Simultaneous Liability

How can impurity + enclosure occur at the exact same moment?
↓
Rav Pappa
Enters in a chest/box/cabinet (holds 40+ se'a, blocks impurity). Someone removes the lid — both liabilities attach at once.
Mar bar Rav Ashi
Enters house while occupant is alive. The person dies while the nazir is sitting inside — both attach simultaneously.
Key Insight Only scenarios where the nazir is already fully inside when impurity begins can produce truly simultaneous liability.

When Does Corpse Impurity Begin?

First Tanna (Leviticus 21:4)

  • Source: "He shall not become impure… to profane himself"
  • A goses (dying person) is included in the prohibition
  • Impurity prohibition may apply even before actual death
  • "When they die" teaches: no impurity from leprosy/zav of relatives

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi (Numbers 6:7)

  • Source: "…for his father or mother… when they die"
  • A goses does NOT impart impurity
  • Only actual death triggers the prohibition
  • Same verse also teaches: impurity only from corpses, not from tzara'at/zav
Reish Lakish The practical difference between the two derivations is the status of a goses (dying person) — does proximity to a dying person violate the impurity prohibition?

Must a Father Be 'Whole' for Priestly Impurity?

Rav (via Rav Ḥisda)
If a priest's father's head was severed, the son may not become impure for him. The verse says "for his father" — when he is whole, not lacking.
Rav Hamnuna
If bandits in the Valley of Aravot severed his father's head — you'd say his son the priest can't bury him?!
Rav Ḥisda
That's a met mitzva! If a priest must become impure for strangers with no one to bury them, kal va-chomer for his own father!
Gemara
But his son is with him — so there IS someone to bury him. That's not a met mitzva!
Gemara
Since they were traveling on the road, he is considered like one with no one to bury him.
Principle A met mitzva (unattended corpse) overrides even the nazir's and priest's impurity restrictions — the wholeness requirement does not apply.
