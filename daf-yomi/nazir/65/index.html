From Found Corpse to Graveyard Status

Single corpse found lying in usual manner
↓
May remove corpse + surrounding earth (teḥum)
↓
Examine 20 cubits — find more corpses?
No more found
Not a graveyard — area is pure
3 found (all new)
Graveyard established — examine further 20 cubits
Key Rule Only three previously unknown corpses buried in Jewish manner establish a graveyard. Known corpses, killed persons, or non-standard burials do not count.

What Doesn't Count Toward a Graveyard

ExclusionReason
Already known corpse (מת שהוכר)Must be newly discovered
Killed person (הרוג)May have been buried at site of death
Buried sitting (יושב)Not Jewish burial custom
Head between thighsNot the usual Jewish manner
Incomplete body (missing vital part)No surrounding earth or graveyard status
Corpses facing opposite directionsJewish cemeteries align all bodies same direction
Mix of known + newly foundCannot combine — must be all one type
Rav Yehuda's Reasoning Each exclusion is derived from precise language in the Mishna: 'found' excludes known; 'corpse' excludes killed; 'lying' excludes sitting; 'in the usual manner' excludes abnormal positions.

R' Yeshevav vs. R' Akiva: Mixing Known and Found Corpses

R' Yeshevav
I found two known corpses and one newly discovered — together they form a graveyard!
R' Akiva
All your toil is in vain. A graveyard requires either three known or three newly found — you cannot combine them.
R' Yeshevav
But there are three bodies here in one location!
R' Akiva
The known corpses were intentionally placed there. Only three unexpected discoveries together raise the concern of an ancient cemetery.
Halakha R' Akiva's view prevails: mixed categories of corpses (some known, some found) do not combine to establish graveyard status.

Two Definitions of 'Surrounding Earth' (עפר תבוסה)

Ben Azzai (via R' Yoḥanan)

  • Take all loose earth near the corpse
  • Dig into virgin ground beneath
  • To a depth of three fingerbreadths
  • Assumes blood/moisture absorbed into soil

R' Elazar b. R' Tzadok (Baraita)

  • Take wood chips (possible coffin remnants)
  • Take lumps of earth (possible absorbed blood)
  • Discard items certainly unrelated (stones)
  • Set aside uncertain items
  • Remaining items combine to majority of skeleton, ¼ kav bones, or full ladle of dust
Source Rav Yehuda derives the concept from Genesis 47:30 — Jacob's instruction to Joseph: 'Carry me out from Egypt' implies taking the surrounding earth with the remains.

Rava's Dilemma: Sequential Discovery of Corpses

Find corpse #1 → examine → remove it
↓
Find corpse #2 nearby → examine → remove it
↓
Find corpse #3 — now it's a graveyard!
First Version of Rava
Do not move #3. But also do not return #1 and #2 — they stay where reburied.
Second Version of Rava
Since permission was granted to move #1 and #2, one may move all three.
Reish Lakish "They found a pretext and deemed Eretz Yisrael pure." The Sages were lenient to avoid multiplying uncertain impurity in the Holy Land.

Uncertainty in Leprous Sores (נגעים)

לטהרו או לטמאו — פתח הכתוב בטהרה תחילה
Principle Any uncertain case of leprosy is initially deemed pure. Source: "To pronounce it pure or to pronounce it impure" (Leviticus 13:59) — since the verse opens with purity, uncertainty defaults to purity.

R' Yehoshua ruled 'keiha' (כהה) on the dispute about whether baheret or white hair came first. Rav Yehuda explains: he pronounced it unsolvable — and therefore pure.
