Nazir 4 — Oaths, Samson, and the Nature of Vows

מִיַּיִן וְשֵׁכָר יַזִּיר — He shall abstain from wine and strong drink (Numbers 6:3)
CORE THEME This daf explores how naziriteship interacts with prior oaths, what the extra phrase 'strong drink' (שֵׁכָר) teaches us, the unique status of a nazirite like Samson, and whether one can create vows by extending the sanctity of items not originally consecrated by vow.
3 / 9

Oath to Drink Wine, Then a Nazirite Vow

Rava
If one swore 'I will drink wine' and then said 'I am a nazirite' — the naziriteship supersedes the oath.
Gemara
Even though drinking wine is now a mitzvah (fulfilling his oath), the nazirite prohibition overrides it.
Rabbis
We learn from 'strong drink' (שֵׁכָר) two things: one becomes a full nazirite even accepting one prohibition, and wine is forbidden even when it's a mitzvah.
R' Shimon
No — 'strong drink' teaches a gezera shava to the Temple: just as only wine is forbidden to a nazirite, only wine is forbidden to priests serving in the Temple.
KEY PRINCIPLE Naziriteship can override a prior oath — the Torah's mention of 'strong drink' is the source for this rule according to the Rabbis.

What Does 'Strong Drink' (שֵׁכָר) Teach?

Rabbis

  • Teaches TWO halakhot from one phrase
  • 1) Full nazirite even from accepting ONE prohibition
  • 2) Wine forbidden even when drinking is a mitzvah
  • Combines with 'wine' for dual teaching

R' Shimon

  • Teaches a gezera shava to Temple service
  • Just as nazirite: only WINE is forbidden
  • So too priests: only wine, not other drinks
  • Excludes R' Yehuda's view that figs/honey/milk make priest liable
R' YEHUDA'S VIEW R' Yehuda holds that even dried figs from Ke'ila, honey, or milk — anything dulling the senses — renders a priest liable if he serves in the Temple.

R' Shimon: Prohibition Cannot Layer on Prohibition

R' Shimon's principle:
אין איסור חל על איסור
A second prohibition doesn't take effect on top of an existing one
↓
Example: One who eats neveilah (carcass) on Yom Kippur — exempt from karet for Yom Kippur eating
↓
Problem: If one swore not to drink wine, how can nazirite prohibition also apply?
↓
Answer: 'Strong drink' is a special Torah inclusion — for naziriteship, a second prohibition does take effect
EXCEPTION Even though R' Shimon normally holds issur doesn't layer on issur, the Torah's extra word 'strong drink' creates a special exception for naziriteship.

Mishna: Ways to Invoke a Samson Naziriteship

StatementWhy It's Needed
"I am like Samson"Might refer to a different person named Samson
"Like the son of Manoah"Could be another person with that name
"Like the husband of Delilah"Clearly identifies the biblical figure
"Like the one who tore off the doors of Gaza"Unmistakable reference to Judges 16
"Like the one whose eyes were gouged by the Philistines"No ambiguity — this is the biblical Samson
TALMUDIC LOGIC Each additional identification is needed because the previous one could theoretically refer to someone else. Only unique details confirm the biblical Samson.

R' Yehuda vs. R' Shimon: Can One Become a 'Samson Nazirite'?

R' Yehuda
A nazirite like Samson may become impure from a corpse ab initio — just as Samson himself became impure.
R' Shimon
One who says 'I am a nazirite like Samson' has said nothing — Samson never vowed to be a nazirite. His status came from an angel's instruction to his mother.
Gemara
Our Mishna says 'if he becomes impure' — implying it's prohibited ab initio. That contradicts R' Yehuda!
Gemara
Answer: The Mishna IS R' Yehuda. It uses 'if he becomes impure' only to match the parallel language used for the permanent nazirite.
CORE DISPUTE R' Yehuda: Samson naziriteship is valid, with lenient impurity rules. R' Shimon: It's impossible — you can't vow based on someone who never vowed.
