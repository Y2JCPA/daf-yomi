Erroneous Consecration: Does a Mistake Invalidate Hekdesh?

Mishna
If one said: 'The black bull that emerges first is consecrated' — and a white bull emerged first…
Beit Shammai
It is consecrated. We derive initial consecration from temurah (substitution) — just as substitution takes effect even in error, so does hekdesh.
Beit Hillel
It is not consecrated. Temurah works in error only because there is already a consecrated animal. Initial consecration cannot descend upon an item in error.
Rav Pappa
Even Beit Shammai agree erroneous hekdesh doesn't take effect. They interpret: he meant 'the first black bull among my black bulls' — even if a white bull preceded it.
Abaye
The mishna deals with one standing at noon, speaking of past events: 'The black bull that emerged first…' — and people told him a white one emerged first.
Core Dispute Does hekdesh b'ta'ut (erroneous consecration) take effect? Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel disagree, and the Gemara offers multiple reinterpretations of Beit Shammai's position.

The Gemara Challenges Beit Shammai

Gemara
If Beit Shammai derive from temurah: one who says 'this animal is a substitute at midday' — it doesn't take effect until midday arrives. The temurah follows his explicit statement.
Gemara
So too here — the consecration should only take effect if a black bull actually emerges first! Why does Beit Shammai say the white bull is consecrated?
Rav Pappa
Beit Shammai concede that hekdesh doesn't take effect against one's explicit words. The man meant: 'the first among my black bulls to emerge' — even if a white bull came out before it.
Gemara
But what if he has only one black bull?
Rav Pappa
The case requires him to own two or three black bulls. He consecrated the first black bull to emerge, regardless of white bulls preceding it.
Beit Hillel's Response If he meant 'the first black bull,' he should have said: 'The first black bull that emerges.' His wording implies the black bull must be the first of any kind to emerge.

Two Reinterpretations of the Mishna

Rav Pappa

  • Man speaks in the morning about the future
  • He owns multiple black bulls
  • He means: 'the first of my black bulls to emerge'
  • White bull emerging first is irrelevant
  • Called 'erroneous' because his wording was misleading
  • Even Beit Shammai agree true error invalidates hekdesh

Abaye

  • Man speaks at noon about the past
  • 'The black bull that emerged first is consecrated'
  • People tell him: a white bull actually emerged first
  • He says: 'Had I known, I wouldn't have said black'
  • True erroneous consecration — a genuine mistake
  • Gemara emends all three cases to past tense
Rava from Barnish Challenged Rav Pappa: If the first black bull was consecrated as intended, how is this 'erroneous'? Answer: The wording was erroneous, even if the result matched his intent.

The Challenge from Nazirite Vows

הקדש בטעות — hekdesh b'ta'ut
Key Proof The Gemara cites a mishna (31b): If people in a group each vow naziriteship based on contradictory conditions about a passerby's identity, Beit Shammai say they are all nazirites — even though some vows must be erroneous. This proves Beit Shammai do hold erroneous consecration is valid! The Sages resolve: Beit Shammai's general rule is that erroneous consecration works, but in this mishna about bulls, Rav Pappa's specific reinterpretation applies.
