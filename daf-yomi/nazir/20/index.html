Queen Helene: Whose Opinion Does R' Yehuda Follow?

Gemara
Does R' Yehuda hold she became impure after 30 days in Eretz Yisrael, following Beit Shammai?
Gemara
Or did she not become impure, and he follows Beit Hillel — requiring 7 additional years?
Proof
R' Yehuda says 14 years total. If Beit Shammai, it should be 14 years and 30 days!
R' Yehuda
Citing R' Eliezer: "This is the law of the nazirite" (Num. 6:13) — impurity at completion yields only 30 days, not 7 years.
Conclusion
R' Yehuda follows Beit Hillel: she observed 7 extra years, was not impure, total = 14 years.
Resolution A baraita confirms: R' Yehuda in the name of R' Eliezer cannot be aligned with Beit Shammai, as impurity at the end of 30 days would require only 30 more days — not a full 7 years.

Two Sets of Witnesses: 2 Terms vs. 5 Terms

Beit Shammai

  • Testimony is divided (מחולקת)
  • Witnesses contradict each other
  • Both testimonies are rejected entirely
  • Result: No naziriteship at all

Beit Hillel

  • 2 terms are included in 5 (בכלל)
  • No true contradiction exists
  • Unanimous agreement on at least 2 terms
  • Result: He observes 2 terms
R' Yishmael b. R' Yochanan b. Beroka Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel actually agree when there are two sets of witnesses. They only disagree when one pair of witnesses gives conflicting counts.

Rav's Principle: Counting as Contradiction

One witness says: He counted 1, 2
Other witness says: He counted 3, 4, 5
↓
Do the counts overlap or conflict?
Rav (Babylonia)
Counting = contradiction
Testimony is divided and rejected
Sages of Eretz Yisrael
No contradiction in counting — smaller number is included in the larger one
Key Distinction Rav holds: when each witness heard specific sequential counts, the testimonies directly contradict. The Western sages disagree — sequential counting still allows inclusion.

Chain Nazirite Vows: 'And I' (ואני)

1️⃣ First person declares: "I am hereby a nazirite" (הריני נזיר)
↓
2️⃣ Second person hears and says: "And I" (ואני) — becomes a nazirite
↓
3️⃣ Third person says: "And I" — also becomes a nazirite
↓
⬆️ If the first vow is dissolved → all are dissolved (chain collapses)
↓
⬇️ If the last vow is dissolved → only the last is dissolved
↓
📝 Alternative formula: "My mouth is like his mouth, my hair like his hair" — also valid
Principle Each subsequent vow depends on those before it. Dissolving the foundation collapses the chain; dissolving the top leaves the foundation intact.

ScenarioCan Husband Nullify Wife's Vow?Husband's Vow Status
He vows, she says 'And I'✅ Yes — he can nullify her vowHis vow remains intact
She vows, he says 'And I'❌ No — nullifying hers would nullify hisHis depends on hers
He says: 'I am a nazirite, and you' — she says Amen✅ Yes — he imposed it on herHis vow remains intact
She says: 'I am a nazirite, and you' — he says Amen❌ No — nullifying hers dissolves hisHis depends on hers
Rule A husband cannot nullify his wife's vow if doing so would effectively nullify his own — since a man cannot nullify his own nazirite vow through this mechanism.
