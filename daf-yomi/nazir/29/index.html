Why Can a Father Impose Naziriteship on His Son?

R' Yochanan
It is a halakha l'Moshe mi'Sinai — a tradition transmitted to Moses from Sinai specifically about naziriteship
Reish Lakish
It is for the purpose of chinuch (education) — to train the son in observing mitzvot
Gemara
The Gemara raises six challenges against Reish Lakish from the mishna's specific rules, and Reish Lakish answers each one
R' Yehuda HaNasi
A father can vow until the son develops two pubic hairs (physical maturity)
R' Yosei b'R' Yehuda
A father can vow only until the son reaches the age of vows (mental maturity)
Core Debate Is a father's power to impose naziriteship on his son rooted in divine tradition (halakha l'Moshe mi'Sinai) or in educational obligation (chinuch)? Each source yields different halakhic consequences.

The Fundamental Dispute: Source of the Father's Power

R' Yochanan
A father's ability to impose naziriteship on his son is a halakha l'Moshe mi'Sinai — a tradition from Sinai with Torah-level authority
R' Yosei b'R' Chanina
Reish Lakish says it is in order to educate him in mitzvot (chinuch) — a rabbinic-level obligation
Gemara
These two explanations produce different halakhic outcomes in multiple areas. The Gemara systematically tests Reish Lakish's view.
Why It Matters If it's halakha l'Moshe mi'Sinai, it has Torah-level force and its rules cannot be questioned. If it's chinuch, it's rabbinic, and must be logically consistent with educational principles.

Six Challenges to Reish Lakish's Chinuch Theory

Challenge 1: Why only a son and not a daughter?
↓
✅ A father is obligated to educate his son but not his daughter
↓
Challenge 2: Why only naziriteship and not other vows?
↓
✅ The mishna uses lo zu af zu style: even naziriteship where there is deprivation (no wine, no shaving), the father can impose it
↓
Challenge 3: Why can relatives object if it's educational?
↓
✅ Naziriteship is optional, not essential education — the child's suffering outweighs the benefit
Pattern Each challenge tests whether Reish Lakish's educational rationale can explain the mishna's specific limitations. He successfully answers each one.

Challenges 4–6: Ritual Consequences

IssueR' Yochanan (Halakha)Reish Lakish (Chinuch)
Rounding the head at completion of naziriteshipTorah law (halakha) overrides the prohibitionRounding the entire head is only rabbinic — so rabbinic chinuch overrides it
Bringing an offering in the TempleThe offering is a Torah obligation — legitimate sacrificeProhibition against non-sacred animals in Temple is only rabbinic — chinuch overrides it
Bird sin-offering killed by pinching (melikah)Torah-level nazirite — proper offering, permitted to eatA baraita challenges this: for a female minor it involves two rabbinic prohibitions — too many to override
Key Principle Reish Lakish consistently holds that the prohibitions at stake are themselves only rabbinic, allowing the rabbinic obligation of chinuch to override them.

Until When Can a Father Vow for His Son?

R' Yehuda HaNasi

  • Until the son develops two pubic hairs
  • Physical maturity is the cutoff
  • Even after the son reaches the 'age of vows,' the father can still impose naziriteship
  • Possibly supports: halakha l'Moshe mi'Sinai (Torah-level power overrides rabbinic age-of-vows status)

R' Yosei b'R' Yehuda

  • Until the son reaches the age of vows (~year before maturity)
  • Mental maturity / understanding is the cutoff
  • Once the son can make his own vows, the father loses his power
  • Possibly supports: chinuch rationale (once capable, no more parental override)
Note The Gemara shows this dispute does not necessarily map onto the R' Yochanan vs. Reish Lakish debate — multiple explanations are offered for both positions.

Can We Link the Tanna'im to the Amora'im?

Attempt: R' Yehuda HaNasi = R' Yochanan (halakha)
R' Yosei b'R' Yehuda = Reish Lakish (chinuch)
↓
Rejected — Option 1
Both hold halakha l'Moshe mi'Sinai. They disagree whether the age-of-vows status is Torah or rabbinic law
Rejected — Option 2
Both hold chinuch. They disagree whether chinuch can override the age-of-vows status (both rabbinic)
Conclusion The Gemara shows there is no necessary connection between this tannaitic dispute and the amoraic debate about the source of a father's nazirite vow.
