How Impurity Laws are Derived for Nazirite & Kohen Gadol

Numbers 6:7 says nazirite may not defile for relatives "when they die"
↓
R' Yehuda HaNasi: Only death impurity is forbidden — not tzara'at or zav impurity
↓
"His mother" is extra in the High Priest's verse — available for gezeira shava
↓
Nazirite
May not defile for death of parents, but may for their tzara'at/zav
Kohen Gadol
Same rule derived via verbal analogy of "his mother"
Core Principle The gezeira shava of "his mother" links nazirite and High Priest, teaching that both are forbidden only from corpse impurity — not from impurity of the living.

R' Yehuda HaNasi's Gezeira Shava

R' Yehuda HaNasi
The verse says "when they die" — only death impurity is forbidden to a nazirite. He may become tamei for their tzara'at or zav emissions.
Gemara
But how do we know the same applies to a Kohen Gadol?
R' Yehuda HaNasi
"His mother" is unnecessary for the Kohen Gadol — it's derived by kal va-chomer from an ordinary kohen. So the phrase is free for a gezeira shava.
Gemara
Just as "his mother" by nazirite limits the prohibition to death impurity, so too by Kohen Gadol — he may become tamei for their tzara'at or zav.
Key Mechanism A superfluous word in one verse becomes 'free' to create a verbal analogy (gezeira shava) with the same word in another verse.

Deriving the Nazirite's Obligation for Met Mitzva

Numbers 6:6 — "He shall not come near a dead body (nefesh met)"
↓
Does nefesh include animal carcasses?
Tanna Kamma
The double term nefesh met specifies: a human corpse only
R' Yishmael
"Shall not come near" = only bodies that impart ohel (tent) impurity = human corpses
↓
Since the general prohibition already covers all people, "for his father or mother" is now available to teach:
↓
Only for relatives he may not defile — but he must defile for a met mitzva
Result Both a nazirite and a Kohen Gadol must set aside their sanctity to bury an unattended corpse (met mitzva).

Nazirite vs. Kohen Gadol: Why We Need a Verse

Kohen Gadol

  • Sanctity is permanent (lifelong)
  • Does not bring an offering for impurity
  • Must become tamei for met mitzva

Nazirite

  • Sanctity is temporary (term-limited)
  • Does bring an offering for impurity
  • Kal va-chomer from Kohen Gadol fails — needs a verse
Why the Kal Va-Chomer Fails Since a nazirite must bring a korban for becoming tamei (an extra consequence), one cannot simply infer from the Kohen Gadol. A dedicated verse is needed.

What Each Relative Mentioned in Numbers 6:7 Teaches

PhraseWhat It Teaches
"For his father"He may not become tamei for his father — and kal va-chomer for other relatives
"For his mother"Gezeira shava with Kohen Gadol (R' Yehuda HaNasi): only death-impurity is forbidden, not tzara'at/zav
"For his brother"He may not defile for his brother — but he must defile for a met mitzva
"For his sister"Even when going to bring korban Pesach or perform milah, he must stop for a met mitzva
Exegetical Economy Each seemingly redundant relative in the verse is 'free' to teach a distinct halakha — nothing in the Torah is superfluous.

Rabbi Akiva's Alternative Reading

R' Akiva
"Nefesh" (body) refers to distant people (non-relatives). "Met" (dead) refers to close relatives.
R' Akiva
"Father or mother" → may not defile, but must for met mitzva. "Brother" → even a Kohen Gadol who is also a nazirite must defile for met mitzva.
R' Akiva
"Sister" → even one going to korban Pesach or brit milah must stop to bury a met mitzva.
Gemara
But how does R' Akiva derive R' Yehuda HaNasi's gezeira shava about non-corpse impurity?
Gemara
Since a Kohen Gadol-nazirite must defile for met mitzva, so too a plain Kohen Gadol — the gezeira shava becomes unnecessary.
R' Akiva's Innovation By reading 'brother' as referring to a Kohen Gadol-nazirite, R' Akiva derives additional laws without needing the gezeira shava.
