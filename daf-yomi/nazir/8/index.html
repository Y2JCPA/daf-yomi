How Many Naziriteships? Interpreting Vague Vows

A person makes a nazirite vow using a comparison ('like the hair of my head,' 'capacity of the basket,' 'days of the solar year')
↓
Did the person use the plural term neziruyot (naziriteships)?
Yes — Plural
All agree: Multiple distinct terms of naziriteship, shaves every 30 days
No — Singular
Dispute: Tanna Kamma says many terms; Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi says one long term
Core Issue The phrasing of a nazirite vow determines whether one accepts many short naziriteships (shaving every 30 days) or one impossibly long term (never shaving).

Mishna: Vow Formulations and Their Consequences

Vow FormulationResult
"I am a nazirite like the hair of my head"Nazirite forever — shaves every 30 days (Tanna Kamma) or never (Rabbi)
"Naziriteships upon me like the hair of my head"All agree: distinct terms, shaves every 30 days
"Nazirite like the capacity of the basket"If unspecified — view basket as full of mustard seeds; nazirite for life
"Nazirite from here to place X"Count days of walking; minimum 30 days
"Nazirite like the days of the solar year"365 consecutive naziriteships
Key Principle Ambiguous nazirite vows are interpreted stringently — the smallest unit of measurement (mustard seeds, individual days) maximizes the obligation.

One Long Term or Many Short Terms?

Tanna Kamma
One who says 'I am a nazirite like the hair of my head' accepts a separate term for each hair. He shaves every 30 days and begins the next term.
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
He has accepted one long term of naziriteship lasting as many days as there are hairs. He does not shave every 30 days.
Tanna Kamma
The practical difference? Under my view he brings offerings every 30 days. Under Rabbi's view — never, because he can never complete the single term.
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
Only one who explicitly says neziruyot (plural — 'naziriteships') accepts distinct terms. Singular language = one term.
Hinge Point The dispute turns on whether singular language ('I am a nazirite like X') implies one extended obligation or many sequential ones.

The Basket Dilemma: Why Mustard Seeds?

One says: 'I am a nazirite like the capacity of the basket' without specifying intent
↓
What fills the basket? Cucumbers (few) or mustard seeds (countless)?
Cucumbers/Gourds
Fewer items → fewer naziriteships → remedy exists
Mustard Seeds
Countless items → nazirite for life
↓
The Mishna rules: view as mustard seeds — the stringent interpretation prevails
Gemara's Question Why not be lenient and assume cucumbers? This launches the Gemara's extended analysis of how to handle uncertainty in nazirite vows.

Resolving the Basket: Chizkiyya vs. R' Yochanan

Chizkiyya
The mishna follows R' Shimon, who holds that a person willingly places himself in a stringent uncertainty. Since the basket's contents are ambiguous, we assume mustard seeds.
R' Yochanan
Even R' Yehuda (who is lenient on uncertain naziriteship) agrees here! The pile-of-grain case is different — there, naziriteship may never begin. Here, it certainly begins.
Chizkiyya
R' Shimon's rule: uncertain naziriteship → act stringently. R' Yehuda's rule: uncertain naziriteship → act leniently.
R' Yochanan
The key distinction is whether naziriteship certainly takes effect. If it does, even R' Yehuda agrees you cannot remove it while uncertainty remains about its duration.
R' Yochanan's Distinction Uncertain whether naziriteship begins at all → lenient. Certain it began but uncertain how long → stringent, even for R' Yehuda.

Does R' Yehuda Hold Like Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi?

Evidence He Agrees

  • R' Yochanan says R' Yehuda views the basket vow as ONE long term (like Rabbi)
  • This explains why mustard seeds — since it's one term, you can't count down to a smaller number
  • Singular language = one extended naziriteship

Evidence He Disagrees

  • R' Yehuda says the 365-naziriteship man 'completed' them — implying multiple terms
  • R' Yehuda: 'like the piles of figs' = many naziriteships, not one
  • Gemara resolves: R' Yehuda agrees with Rabbi in ONE case, disagrees in another
Resolution R' Yehuda agrees: singular language ('I am a nazirite like X') = one long term. But disagrees: 'like the NUMBER of X' = many distinct terms, unlike Rabbi who requires the explicit plural 'naziriteships.'
