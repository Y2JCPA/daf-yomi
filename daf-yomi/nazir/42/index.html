Rubo K'Kulo: Is Majority Like the Whole?

Rav Aḥa b. Rav Ika
If a nazirite leaves two hairs unshaved, he has done nothing. This proves rubo k'kulo — the majority of an entity is considered like all of it — applies by Torah law.
R' Yosei b. R' Ḥanina
Objection! The verse 'On the seventh day he shall shave it' refers to an impure nazirite, not a pure one. You can't derive a general rule from this!
Sages of Eretz Yisrael
They laughed at this: Where does an impure nazirite's shaving rule come from? From the pure nazirite! So let the pure nazirite equally derive from the impure — leaving 2 hairs = nothing.
Key Principle The Torah specifies 'he shall shave it' to teach that in this case ALL must be removed — implying that generally, majority suffices (rubo k'kulo).

Abaye & Rava's Shaving Dilemmas

Nazirite shaves but leaves 2 hairs — has done nothing
↓
What happens next?
Abaye's Dilemma
Hair regrows, then he shaves again — this time including those 2 hairs. Is his obligation fulfilled?
Rava's Dilemma
He shaves one of the 2 remaining hairs, and the other falls out on its own. Fulfilled?
↓
Rav Aḥa of Difti corrects: What if one hair fell out first, then he shaved the remaining one?
↓
Ravina: No fulfillment — even though no hair remains, he failed to shave it all properly
Unresolved These dilemmas (teiku) explore whether shaving can be completed in stages or must be done in a single comprehensive act.

Nazirite Hair Care: What's Permitted?

Permitted ✓

  • Shampooing (ḥofef) the head
  • Separating (mefaspes) hairs manually
  • Reason: Hair loss is unintentional (davar she'eino mitkaven)

Prohibited ✗

  • Combing hair with a comb
  • Reason: One who combs intends to remove stray hairs
  • Shampooing with earth (R' Yishmael) — causes hair to fall out
Rabba The entire mishna follows R' Shimon (unintentional acts are permitted). Combing is prohibited because the person intends to remove strays — making it an intentional act, not an unintentional one.

Corpse Impurity: One Lashing or Many?

Rabba / Rav Huna
Numbers 6:7 includes all forms of corpse impurity (contact, carrying, tent). 'He shall not come near' (6:6) adds a separate prohibition for entering an enclosure. But impurity + impurity again = one set of lashes only.
Rav Yosef
By God! Rav Huna actually holds he's liable even for impurity upon impurity! A nazirite standing in a cemetery who touches another corpse — he is liable again!
Abaye
Objection from a baraita: A priest carrying a corpse who touches another — not liable. 'He shall not profane' excludes one already profaned.
Rav Yosef
But our mishna says: if warned each time — liable for each impurity! The baraita and mishna must address different situations.
Resolution The baraita (exempt) refers to concurrent contact (tum'ah b'chiburin) — still touching the first corpse. The mishna (liable) refers to sequential contact — separated from the first before touching the second.
