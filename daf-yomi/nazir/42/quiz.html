QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Aḥa b. Rav Ika, what principle does the nazirite shaving rule prove?
A. A positive mitzva overrides a prohibition
B. Majority of an entity is like the whole (rubo k'kulo)
C. An unintentional act is permitted
D. One who is already impure cannot become impure again
Correct: B — Rav Aḥa derives from the nazirite shaving law — where leaving 2 hairs invalidates the shaving — that generally, the majority of something is considered like the entirety. The Torah had to specify complete shaving precisely because the default rule is that majority suffices.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Rabba say the entire mishna about shampooing and combing follows R' Shimon?
A. R' Shimon permits all hair care for a nazirite
B. Combing involves intent to remove stray hairs, making it an intentional act even for R' Shimon
C. The Rabbis agree with R' Shimon on this specific case
D. R' Shimon prohibits both shampooing and combing
Correct: B — Rabba explains that even R' Shimon, who permits unintentional acts, considers combing intentional — because anyone who combs intends to remove loose/stray hairs. Thus the prohibition on combing is consistent with R' Shimon's framework.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
In the resolution between the baraita (priest exempt) and the mishna (nazirite liable for repeated impurity), what is the key distinction?
A. Priests and nazirites have different Torah prohibitions
B. The baraita refers to concurrent contact; the mishna to sequential contact
C. The baraita is rabbinic law; the mishna is Torah law
D. The priest was carrying the corpse; the nazirite was in a cemetery
Correct: B — The Gemara resolves the contradiction: the baraita (exempt) discusses concurrent impurity (tum'ah b'chiburin) — still touching the first corpse when touching the second. The mishna (liable) discusses sequential impurity — he separated from the first corpse before becoming impure again.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is Ravina's ruling about a nazirite who shaved all but two hairs, one fell out, and he then shaved the remaining one?
A. He has fulfilled the obligation since no hair remains
B. He has not fulfilled the mitzva of shaving, despite no hair remaining
C. He must wait for hair to regrow and shave again completely
D. He has partially fulfilled his obligation
Correct: B — Ravina rules that even though no hair remains on the head, there is no fulfillment of the mitzva of shaving — he failed to shave it all on the first attempt, and on the second attempt he shaved less than the required amount.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to R' Yannai, tum'ah b'chiburin (concurrent contact impurity) from person-to-person contact applies to which areas of law?
A. Nazirite law and Paschal offering
B. All areas of ritual purity
C. Teruma and consecrated foods only
D. Priestly purity and nazirite law
Correct: C — R' Yannai teaches that person-to-person concurrent contact impurity (where someone touches a person who is touching a corpse) is a rabbinic-level impurity that applies only to teruma and consecrated foods — not to nazirite law or the Paschal offering. Direct person-to-corpse concurrent contact is a separate, Torah-level category.
