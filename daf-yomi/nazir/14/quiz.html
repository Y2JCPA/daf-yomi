QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Tosefta, if one says 'I am a nazir after 20 days, and a nazir from now for 100 days,' how does he count?
A. 20 days (first), then 30 days (second), then 80 days (remainder of first)
B. 30 days (second), then 100 days (first) — totaling 130 days
C. 100 days (first) immediately, ignoring the second vow entirely
D. 20 days (first), then 100 days (restarted first), then 30 days (second)
Correct: A — The Tosefta teaches he counts 20 days for the first naziriteship, interrupts for the full 30-day second term, then completes the remaining 80 days of the first term.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does R' Yochanan hold that impurity during the son's naziriteship negates the personal naziriteship days too?
A. Because the son's naziriteship is more stringent than the personal one
B. Because he views both periods as one long continuous naziriteship
C. Because the Torah requires all naziriteship to restart upon any impurity
D. Because the personal naziriteship was never properly accepted
Correct: B — R' Yochanan holds that the paused personal naziriteship and the son's naziriteship form one long, continuous period of naziriteship, so impurity during any part negates the whole.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why was it necessary for the Gemara to teach the R' Yochanan/Reish Lakish dispute in BOTH the son's naziriteship case and the leprosy case?
A. Because the halakha differs between the two cases
B. To show that R' Yochanan eventually conceded to Reish Lakish
C. Because from either case alone, one might think the other disputant concedes in the other scenario
D. Because leprosy and naziriteship have identical Torah sources
Correct: C — From the son's case alone, one might think R' Yochanan is strict only when both periods involve naziriteship. From the leprosy case alone, one might think Reish Lakish is lenient only because leprosy is fundamentally different. Both cases are needed to show each holds his position universally.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In Rav Chisda's case of a nazirite who became impure after the sprinkling of blood but before shaving, what does 'no remedy' mean according to the Gemara's conclusion?
A. He can never become pure again
B. He must observe an entirely new term of naziriteship
C. He cannot fulfill the mitzva of shaving his naziriteship in purity
D. He is permanently forbidden from drinking wine
Correct: C — According to the Rabbis, shaving is not indispensable to completing naziriteship, so his term is technically over. The 'no remedy' means specifically that he can never fulfill the mitzva of shaving in purity — he can't bring a second set of offerings for the same term.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to R' Yosi b. R' Chanina, why is a completed nazirite (who hasn't brought offerings) flogged for corpse impurity but NOT for shaving or wine?
A. Because corpse impurity is a more severe prohibition than shaving or wine
B. Because the verse uses the word 'all' (כל) to extend only the impurity prohibition beyond the term
C. Because shaving and wine become permitted immediately upon completion of the term
D. Because offerings are only required for corpse impurity, not for shaving or wine
Correct: B — The verse states 'ALL the days he has consecrated himself unto the Lord, he shall not come near a dead body' (Numbers 6:6). The extra word 'all' extends the corpse-impurity prohibition to include days after the term's completion. No similar extension exists for shaving or wine.
