🔥 The Central Dispute: Does Burning Render Burning Piggul?

Rava / Sharp People of Pumbedita
Burning DOES render burning piggul. A mishna (12a) equates burning with removal, placement, and conveying — just as improper intent during those acts creates piggul regardless of item, so too with burning the handful intending to burn frankincense the next day.
Rav Ḥisda citing Rav
Burning does NOT render burning piggul. Rabbi Meir's rule applies only when one has improper intent during the sacrifice of the handful — the handful's permitting factor for the remainder. But frankincense is NOT the permitting factor for the handful, so a different rule applies.
Rav Adda bar Ahava's Resolution Both views are correct in their respective domains: burning alone (re: frankincense) doesn't create piggul, but if one ALSO had improper intent regarding the remainder, the two intents combine and the offering IS piggul.

🗣️ Rav Menashya bar Gadda Before Abaye: The Rav Ḥisda Tradition

Rava (and Pumbedita scholars)
Burning renders burning piggul. The mishna lists burning alongside removal and conveying — these are all equivalent piggul-creating acts. Whether intent was to consume the remainder OR to burn the frankincense the next day, the result is piggul.
Rav Menashya bar Gadda (Rav Ḥisda / Rav)
Burning does NOT render burning piggul. Rabbi Meir's piggul rule requires intent concerning the item's own matir (permitting factor). The handful permits the remainder — so intent about the remainder creates piggul. But frankincense does not permit the handful, so intent about frankincense cannot create piggul for the handful.
Abaye Confirms the Tradition Abaye asked: Did Rav Ḥisda say this in Rav's name? Rav Menashya confirmed: Yes. The Gemara then cites the explicit tradition: 'Rav Ḥisda says that Rav says: Burning does not render burning piggul.'

🔀 Rabbi Ḥanina's Novel Ruling — Rav Hamnuna's Greatest Teaching

Priest burns the handful
with TWO simultaneous intents
↓
Intent A
Burn the frankincense the next day
(frankincense ≠ permitting factor)
→ By itself: not piggul
Intent B
Consume the remainder the next day
(remainder = what handful permits)
→ By itself: piggul
↓
Combined Result: Piggul!
Even though Intent A alone would not create piggul, when combined with Intent B (which would), the offering is fully piggul.
— Rav Adda bar Ahava explaining Rabbi Ḥanina

📜 Mishna: Four Categories of Improper Intent

Eat | Not Usually Eaten

  • Example: intent to eat the handful beyond its time
  • Handful: not normally consumed by humans
  • Rabbi Eliezer: Offering is UNFIT (פסול)
  • Rabbis: Offering remains VALID
  • ⚠️ Not full piggul — no karet liability

Burn | Not Usually Burned

  • Example: intent to burn the remainder beyond its time
  • Remainder: not normally burned on altar
  • Rabbi Eliezer: Offering is UNFIT (פסול)
  • Rabbis: Offering remains VALID
  • ⚠️ Not full piggul — no karet liability

Eat | Usually Eaten

  • Example: intent to eat the remainder beyond its time
  • Remainder: normally eaten by priests
  • All agree: PIGGUL (פיגול)
  • ✡️ Karet liability applies
  • Classic piggul scenario

Burn | Usually Burned

  • Example: intent to burn frankincense beyond its time
  • Frankincense: normally burned on altar
  • All agree: PIGGUL (פיגול)
  • ✡️ Karet liability applies
  • Classic piggul scenario

📖 Rabbi Eliezer's Derivation from Doubled Language

הֵאָכֹל יֵאָכֵל — ויקרא ז:יח
הֵאָכֹל יֵאָכֵל — 'It Shall Surely Be Eaten' (Vayikra 7:18) The Torah uses two different grammatical forms of 'eat': he'akhol (infinitive) and yokhal (imperfect). Rabbi Eliezer: one form refers to human eating, the other to altar burning — the Torah thereby equates them. Just as improper intent to eat-what-is-eaten renders the offering unfit, so too intent to eat-what-is-burned (or burn-what-is-eaten) renders it unfit.

The Rabbis respond: The doubled language teaches only that piggul intent requires a minimum measure — an olive-bulk (כזית) — the same minimum as for actual eating. Cross-category intent creates no consequence.

Rabbi Eliezer's counter: If the verse meant only to teach the olive-bulk, it could have repeated the identical form (he'akhol he'akhol or yokhal yokhal). The fact that two different forms are used proves a different law is intended.
