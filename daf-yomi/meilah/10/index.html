⚖️ Finger or Priesthood — What Mandates the Right Hand?

Reish Lakish
Any place in the Torah where finger or priesthood is mentioned, the right hand is required — one condition alone suffices.
Rava
Agreed — if either finger or priesthood is written, only the right hand may be used for that rite.
Abaye
But priesthood is written for conveying burnt-offering limbs to the altar ramp — yet the right hand is not required there!
Gemara
The rule applies only to rites indispensable to atonement. Conveying limbs is not such a rite, so it is excluded.
Rabbi Shimon
I require both finger and priesthood to be written — priesthood alone is never sufficient to mandate the right hand.
Core Dispute All agree: rites essential to atonement mandate the right hand. The dispute is whether finger, priesthood, or both are needed as the triggering condition.

🫙 Why Two Verses for Leper Oil Placement?

Goal: Priest places oil on leper's right thumb and big toe during purification
↓
Only 'upon the blood' (Lev. 14:17)
I would think: oil is placed only when blood is still present on the thumb/toe. If wiped away — oil cannot be placed.
Only 'upon the place of the blood' (Lev. 14:28)
I would think: oil is placed only when blood was already wiped away. If blood still remains — oil cannot be placed.
↓
Both verses together: Oil is placed whether the blood of the guilt offering is still present or has already been wiped away — neither case excludes the oil.

📜 Verbal Analogies Rava Derives from the Leper Passage

ויקרא יד:יז — עַל דַּם הָאָשָׁם
גְּזֵרָה שָׁוָה — Gezerah Shavah Foot (Lev. 14:17, wealthy leper) ↔ foot in the yavam passage → teaches a connection between leper purification release and the ḥalitzah procedure of levirate marriage.

Ear (Lev. 14:17, wealthy leper) ↔ ear in the Canaanite slave passage → teaches that the ear-boring of a Hebrew slave who wishes to remain is performed on the right ear.

Left hand (Lev. 14:26, poor leper) → Rav Sheisha son of Rav Idi: this disqualifies the priest's right hand from holding the log of oil for purification rites.

💰 Wealthy Leper vs. Poor Leper — Purification Rites

Wealthy Leper (Lev. 14:14–18)

  • Brings a full guilt offering (ram)
  • Oil placed 'upon the blood of the guilt offering'
  • Three logs of oil used
  • Priest sprinkles oil before God seven times
  • Right ear, right thumb, right big toe anointed
  • Remaining oil poured on the leper's head

Poor Leper (Lev. 14:21–29)

  • Brings a lamb (lesser guilt offering)
  • Oil placed 'upon the place of the blood'
  • One log of oil used
  • Priest pours oil into his own left hand
  • Same body parts anointed (right ear/thumb/toe)
  • Extra verse disqualifies priest's right hand from holding the log

🌾 Source for Right Hand in Handful Removal

Gemara
The verbal analogy from the leper's 'hand' teaches that removal of the handful (קמיצה) from a meal offering must be performed with the right hand.
Objection
But Rabbi Yehuda son of Rabbi Ḥiyya already derives this! He compares the meal offering to a sin offering and guilt offering — all of which are performed with the right hand.
Gemara
The verbal analogy is specifically needed for the meal offering of a sinner. Since Rabbi Shimon says it requires no intention (לשמה), one might think the right-hand requirement is also waived. The analogy teaches: no — right hand is still required.
Gemara
A second derivation covers sanctification of the handful — placing it into a second service vessel — which must also be performed with the right hand.
Mishna Resumes The daf closes as the mishna returns to its case: if a priest removed the handful and a stone or grain of salt came out in his hand along with the flour...
