⚖️ Rabbi Shimon's Rationale — Two Possibilities

Rabba
Rabbi Shimon validates cross-purpose removal because the offerings physically intermingled in the vessel — the handful from one became bound up in the other, so the removal is valid.
Rava
Rabbi Shimon validates it because of the verse "And this is the law of the meal offering" (Lev. 6:7) — one unified law covers all meal offerings, regardless of which was intended.
Rav Ashi
There is a further distinction: one who removes a handful from an offering prepared in one vessel for the sake of a different vessel is treated differently than removal within the same vessel.
Rav Asi
I find none of these resolutions satisfactory — Rav Hoshaya's dilemma remains unresolved. The practical halakha depends on which rationale Rabbi Shimon actually holds.
Why It Matters If Rabbi Shimon's reason is intermingling, only same-vessel cross-purpose removal is valid. If his reason is the unified law verse, even cross-vessel removal would be valid — a major practical difference.

📜 The Word 'It' (הִיא / הוּא) — Two Different Functions

Sin Offering — 'It' Disqualifies

  • Verse: "It is a sin offering" (Lev. 4:24)
  • The word it teaches: only this offering category is disqualified by wrong intent — not all offerings
  • ❌ Disqualified if slaughtered not for its own sake
  • Source from which the meal offering of a sinner's disqualification is derived

Guilt Offering — 'It' Redeems

  • Verse: "It is a guilt offering" (Lev. 7:5)
  • The word it is NOT used to disqualify wrong intent here
  • ✅ Remains fit even if slaughtered not for its own sake
  • Word it teaches instead: if the owner died or already achieved atonement, the designated animal must be left to pasture until it develops a blemish

🌾 The Omer Offering — Special Disqualification

עֹמֶר — קרבן ציבור שיש לו זמן קבוע ובא לְהַתִּיר אֶת הֶחָדָשׁ
Rav's Ruling (Amud B) The omer meal offering (communal barley offering, 16 Nisan) is disqualified if the handful is removed not for its own sake — despite the mishna's rule applying only to individual offerings.

Three reasons make it stringent: (1) it is a communal offering, (2) it has a fixed time (unlike individual meal offerings), and (3) it is a matir — it comes to permit the new grain crop for all Israel. Any offering that 'renders fit' or 'permits' demands proper intent and is disqualified when that intent is lacking.

🏥 Nazirite/Leper Guilt Offerings — 'Render Fit' vs. 'Atone'

Question
The mishna (Zevahim 2a) says ALL slaughtered offerings are fit if slaughtered not for their sake — yet the guilt offerings of a nazirite and leper are disqualified. Why? They are guilt offerings!
Rabbi Yirmeya
The Torah distinguishes offerings that atone from those that render fit (מַכְשִׁירִין). The nazirite and leper's guilt offerings render the person fit to re-enter the community and partake of sacred food — a higher standard applies.
R. Yehuda b. R. Shimon b. Pazi
Objection! There are 'render fit' offerings that function even after death — a nazirite who dies leaves undesignated funds that become communal gift offerings. Those still function without the nazirite ever being rendered fit!
Rav Pappa
Rabbi Yirmeya means: there is no fixed living process of rendering fit that continues after death. The nazirite/leper's guilt offering has a set, active path to fitness — that fixed process is what demands proper intent.
Principle Offerings with a fixed, living manner of rendering someone fit (מַכְשִׁירִין בְּדֶרֶךְ קְבוּעָה) require proper intent and are disqualified when slaughtered otherwise — unlike standard atonement offerings.
