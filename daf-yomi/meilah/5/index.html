🌾 Core Dispute: Omer Handful Removed Not For Its Own Sake

Rav
The omer meal offering is disqualified. A handful removed not for its own sake invalidates the entire offering — it may not be burned upon the altar.
Reish Lakish
The handful is valid and burned, but another omer must be brought — because the remainder may not be consumed by priests until a properly-intentioned omer is offered.
Rav Adda bar Ahava
Defending Reish Lakish: An offering is not considered 'whose time has not yet arrived' if it is to be brought on that very same day — so the new-crop prohibition is no obstacle.
Rav Pappa
Reish Lakish's deeper reasoning: The illumination of the eastern horizon itself permits the new crop — the handful was always intrinsically fit from the moment of dawn.
Practical Result According to Reish Lakish: burn the handful validly; bring a second omer before the priests consume the remainder. The offering is not voided — only the consumption right is delayed.

⏰ Navigating the 'Whose Time Has Not Yet Arrived' Problem

Omer handful removed not for its own sake
Can it be burned on the altar before the new crop is permitted?
↓
❌ Problem
Before omer is offered, new crop is forbidden. Does that make this handful an offering 'whose time has not yet arrived'? If so — invalid!
✅ Rav Adda bar Ahava
Not considered 'untimely' if the offering will be permitted on the same day. The crop becomes permitted later today — so the handful is not premature.
↓
Rav Adda son of Rav Yitzḥak objects: The baraita contrasts birds and meal offerings — meal offerings' general prohibition was permitted. This implies a prohibition existed — contradicting the 'same-day' principle!
↓
Resolution: Since the handful was fit from dawn, sacrificing it is not a case of 'a prohibition being permitted' — it was always permitted. Rav Pappa then reveals: Reish Lakish holds dawn itself permits the new crop.

🦶 Leper Ritual Order — Rav Sheshet and Rav Pappa Clash

Rav Sheshet
Objection: If oil is placed on the leper's thumb and toe before the blood of the guilt offering, the priest must repeat it. But if the same-day principle applies, why? The guilt-offering blood will come later today!
Rav Pappa
Answer: Leper halakhot are uniquely fixed — the verse states זֹאת תִּהְיֶה תּוֹרַת הַמְּצֹרָע (Lev. 14:2). 'Shall be' (תִּהְיֶה) signals the order must remain exactly as prescribed.
Rav Pappa (self-challenge)
But then if the verse fixes all leper procedures — why does slaughtering the sin offering before the guilt offering disqualify it? That ordering violation should also be covered by תִּהְיֶה!
Rav Pappa (resolution)
The תִּהְיֶה applies only to the placement procedures (blood and oil on thumb/toe), not to the sequencing of different sacrifices. The leper-sacrifice order is derived elsewhere, not from this verse.
Key Verse זֹאת תִּהְיֶה תּוֹרַת הַמְּצֹרָע — 'This shall be the law of the leper' (Leviticus 14:2). The word תִּהְיֶה ('shall be') indicates the ritual must remain in its prescribed state.

🐦 Unique Halakhot: Birds vs. Meal Offerings

Birds Only (לֹא בִּמְנָחוֹת)

  • Offered via melikah — pinching nape of neck, not slaughter with a knife
  • A zav, zavah, or yoledet who has not yet brought atonement: their bird offering is disqualified if brought out of order
  • Non-sacred bird killed by pinching = forbidden carcass — but sanctity permitted the same act when consecrated
  • Do not require a service vessel, waving, or placement at altar corner

Meal Offerings Only (לֹא בָּעוֹפוֹת)

  • Must be placed in a service vessel (כְּלִי שָׁרֵת) — no vessel, no valid service
  • Require waving (תְּנוּפָה) and bringing to the corner of the altar
  • General prohibition (e.g., new crop) was permitted when brought as meal offering — demonstrating unique leniency
  • Involve handful removal (קְמִיצָה) with its attendant intent requirements

🐑 The Tereifa Debate: Why a Verse Is Indispensable

Starting point: Torah writes 'from the herd' (Lev. 1:2) — the Sages derive that a tereifa (fatally injured animal) is unfit as an offering
↓
A fortiori attempt: A blemished animal is permitted to ordinary people yet unfit for sacrifice — surely a tereifa, forbidden even to ordinary people, is unfit a fortiori!
↓
Refutation: Fat and blood — forbidden to ordinary people, yet offered on the altar! So 'forbidden to people' does not prevent altar use.
↓
Counter-refutation: Fat/blood come from a permitted animal. Bird melikah — entirely forbidden, yet birds are offered this way. Each proposed proof is successively refuted.
↓
Four amoraim propose different 'responses': Rav (omer), Reish Lakish (incense), Mar b. Ravina (Shabbat), Rav Adda bar Abba (diverse kinds) — each showing a case where something forbidden to people is permitted for the Most High, thereby blocking the a fortiori.
↓
Conclusion: Every proposed 'response' is itself refuted. The baraita's point stands: a verse is absolutely required to exclude the tereifa — the a fortiori inference alone cannot establish it.
