QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Reish Lakish, what is the practical result when a handful of the omer is removed not for its own sake?
A. The omer is entirely disqualified and the handful must be discarded
B. The handful is burned validly, but a second omer must be brought before the priests consume the remainder
C. The handful is burned and the remainder is consumed as normal
D. The priest must remove a new handful with proper intent before any burning occurs
Correct: B — Reish Lakish holds the handful is valid and may be burned on the altar. However, since the remainder's consumption depends on a properly-intentioned omer, the priests cannot eat it until another omer is brought with correct intent.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is Rav Adda bar Ahava's principle about offerings 'whose time has not yet arrived'?
A. Any offering that involves a prohibition is automatically disqualified
B. An offering is not considered untimely if it will become permitted on that same day
C. Only the omer offering is exempt from the untimely-offering prohibition
D. An offering is untimely only if brought more than twenty-four hours early
Correct: B — Rav Adda bar Ahava teaches that if the prohibition associated with an offering will be lifted later on the very same day, the offering is not classified as 'one whose time has not yet arrived' — making the omer handful valid even before the new crop is officially permitted to the public.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does Rav Pappa say a verse (תִּהְיֶה) is needed specifically for leper halakhot?
A. Leper impurity is more severe than other forms of impurity
B. The word תִּהְיֶה indicates the ritual order must remain exactly as prescribed
C. The leper must repeat all rituals performed out of order, regardless of circumstances
D. The leper's guilt offering is uniquely classified as a conditional freewill offering
Correct: B — Rav Pappa derives from תִּהְיֶה ('shall be') in Leviticus 14:2 that the leper's placement procedures are locked in their prescribed order. If the priest places oil before blood on the thumb and toe, the oil placement must be repeated — even though the same-day principle would otherwise apply.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Which halakha is unique to meal offerings and does NOT apply to bird offerings?
A. Disqualification if brought by one who has not yet brought an atonement offering (zav, zavah)
B. Slaughter performed by pinching the nape of the neck (melikah)
C. Requirement to be placed in a service vessel and waved (tenufah)
D. The general prohibition was permitted when the animal became consecrated
Correct: C — The baraita specifies that meal offerings uniquely require placement in a service vessel (כְּלִי שָׁרֵת), waving (תְּנוּפָה), and bringing to the altar corner. These procedures have no parallel in bird offerings. Melikah, by contrast, is unique to birds.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why is an explicit verse necessary to exclude the tereifa from altar sacrifice, rather than a simple a fortiori inference?
A. A fortiori inferences are never used in the laws of sacrifices
B. The a fortiori argument is successfully refuted by cases where something forbidden to ordinary people is permitted for the altar
C. A tereifa is not actually more stringently forbidden than a blemished animal
D. The verse is needed only to define which injuries render an animal a tereifa
Correct: B — The Gemara demonstrates that the a fortiori inference ('a blemished animal permitted to people but unfit for offering — surely a tereifa forbidden to people is unfit!') can be countered by examples of things forbidden to ordinary people yet permitted for the altar (omer new crop, incense, Shabbat labor, diverse kinds). Each proposed counter-example is discussed, ultimately proving the verse is essential.
