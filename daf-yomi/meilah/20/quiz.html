5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav, what is the principle that makes a sacrificial rite indispensable (me'akev)?
A. The rite involves direct contact with the altar
B. The rite is mentioned in both the Torah and the Mishnah
C. The rite is repeated in the biblical verses
D. The rite concerns items that are burned on the altar
Correct: C — Rav holds that only rites explicitly repeated in the biblical verses are indispensable. Since he views the two salt verses as not a simple repetition, an unsalted offering remains fit — consistent with the Mishnah's ruling.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the Gemara exclude blood from the requirement to be salted?
A. Blood is never brought near the altar at all
B. A specific verse explicitly exempts blood from salting
C. Blood is not burned on the altar and does not share the handful's key characteristics
D. Blood was excluded by rabbinic decree after the Temple period
Correct: C — The klal-prat-klal derivation includes only items comparable to the handful: burned on the altar AND requiring other items (like wood). Blood is sprinkled, not burned, and lacks these traits — so it is excluded.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What hermeneutical principle does the Gemara use to determine the scope of the salting obligation?
A. Gezerah shava (verbal analogy between two verses)
B. Klal-prat-klal (generalization, detail, generalization)
C. Kal va-chomer (argument from the less to the more stringent)
D. Hekesh (textual juxtaposition)
Correct: B — The two broad verses bracket the specific detail of the handful, forming a klal-prat-klal. This classical hermeneutical principle limits inclusion to items similar to the detail — burned on the altar and requiring other items.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What characteristic do burnt offering limbs share with the handful of a meal offering that leads the Gemara to require salting for limbs?
A. Both are eaten by the priests after the sacrifice
B. Both render the rest of the offering permitted, and both require wood, are subject to me'ila, and require other items
C. Both are brought only on festivals
D. Both come from grain-based offerings
Correct: B — The Gemara provides a mnemonic of shared traits: limbs require wood for burning, are subject to misuse (me'ila), are prohibited while impure, and other items come as requirements — all parallels to the meal offering's handful.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What does the Gemara derive from the superfluous word 'korban' in Leviticus 2:1?
A. Meal offerings may be brought by non-priests
B. One may voluntarily donate wood as an offering to be burned on the altar
C. The frankincense must be brought in a sacred vessel
D. Salt must be applied before the offering reaches the altar
Correct: B — The baraita on Amud B derives from the extra word 'korban' that one may voluntarily donate wood for burning on the altar. Rava adds that such wood may even require removal of a portion, analogous to a meal offering's handful.
