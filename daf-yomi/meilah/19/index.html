⚖️ Two Hermeneutical Disputes of Menachot 19

Rabbi Shimon
The 'vav' (and) in 'And he shall bring it to Aaron's sons, the priests' adds the bringing to the preceding act — pouring the oil. The verse expands to both what precedes and follows.
Rabbis
'With his finger' applies to both what precedes and what follows — but not because of the vav. We interpret the verse as referring to both surrounding matters on independent grounds.
Rav
Any sacrificial rite for which both 'law' (חוק) and 'statute' (חוקה) appear is indispensable — failure to perform it invalidates the offering entirely.
Shmuel
One cannot derive a rule for all generations from Aaron's eighth-day service — that was a one-time inauguration event, not a binding precedent.
Core Question When does a Torah verse's structure — its conjunctions, repetitions, and 'law/statute' terminology — signal that a sacrificial rite is absolutely indispensable (מעכב)?

📜 Does Vav Expand a Verse's Scope?

Gemara (Challenge)
Does Rabbi Shimon truly hold that a verse is interpreted by both what precedes and succeeds it? A baraita seems to contradict this!
Rabbi Shimon
The term 'hand' is not stated with regard to collecting the blood — only placement. Therefore the preceding-and-succeeding principle does not apply there. My reasoning is about the vav, not that general rule.
Abaye
Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis dispute whether a verse is interpreted by both preceding and succeeding matters. That is the true root of their disagreement throughout.
Gemara (Resolution)
Rabbi Shimon's actual reasoning: 'And he shall bring it to Aaron's sons, the priests' (Lev. 2:2) — the vav adds the act of bringing to the preceding act of pouring the oil, making both performed by priests.
Verse at Issue ויצק עליה שמן ונתן עליה לבנה והביאה אל בני אהרן הכהנים — 'He shall pour oil upon it and put frankincense upon it, and bring it to Aaron's sons, the priests' (Leviticus 2:1–2)

⚖️ Rav's Principle: Law and Statute

Rav's Rule: When both law (חוק) and statute (חוקה) appear for a sacrificial rite — that rite is indispensable (מעכב)
↓
Only 'Law' written
Nazirite offering, Thanks offering (Toda), Leper offering — are these invalidated if a rite is missing?
Only 'Statute' written
Yom Kippur rites — 'a statute for you forever' — are the two goats indispensable?
Both terms written
Rites are definitely invalidated — this is Rav's confirmed rule
↓
Gemara's Resolution: Even one term teaches indispensability in its context; Rav means that wherever either appears for a sacrificial rite, the absence of that rite invalidates

📿 The Mnemonic: Nun-Tav-Tzadi; Yod-Kuf-Mem-Lamed

נ — Nazirite (Nazir): 'This is the law of the nazirite' (Numbers 6:21) — only 'law' written, yet the Gemara must explain whether his rites are indispensable
↓
ת — Thanks Offering (Toda): 'This is the law of the sacrifice of peace offerings' (Lev. 7:11) — only 'law'; four loaf-types are required and their omission invalidates
↓
צ — Leper (Metzora): 'This shall be the law of the leper' (Lev. 14:2) — four species used in purification; the Gemara addresses whether all four are indispensable
↓
י — Yom Kippur: 'It shall be a statute for you forever' (Lev. 16:29) — only 'statute'; the two goats must match each other and are mutually indispensable
↓
ק/מ/ל — Other Offerings: General 'law' language appears for many offerings, yet not all rites invalidate — Rav's principle must be applied carefully to each specific case

🔄 Rav vs Shmuel: Do Repeated Verses Signal Indispensability?

Rav

  • Every rite of the meal offering repeated in the Torah is repeated to teach that its absence invalidates the offering
  • 'His handful' (Lev. 2:2) and 'with his hand' (Lev. 6:8) are two separate verses = indispensable use of hand, not vessel
  • The repetition in Aaron's eighth-day service (Lev. 9:17) teaches this rule for all generations

Shmuel

  • Aaron's eighth-day service was a one-time inauguration event — one cannot derive a rule for all generations from a temporary situation
  • Repetition alone does not automatically make a rite indispensable; context must be examined
  • The Nesi'im (princes) offerings are different — repeated 12 times, once per prince, making derivation for all generations justified
