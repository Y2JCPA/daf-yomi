⚖️ Three Amoraim Resolve the Contradiction

Contradiction: Two baraitot report conflicting opinions of Rabbi Shimon — does a meal offering whose handful was removed not for its own sake fulfill the owner's obligation?
↓
Rabba
Recognizably false intent disqualifies; when the mode of preparation proves the offering's identity, owner fulfills obligation
Rava
"One law" for all meal offerings (Lev. 6:7) — removing a handful for any meal offering always fulfills the obligation
Rav Ashi
Intent regarding vessel type only is inconsequential — only substantive intent (different offering category) disqualifies
↓
All three agree: Slaughtered offerings, each governed by specific individual laws, ARE disqualified by improper intent

🔍 Is the False Intent Recognizable? Challenges to Rabba

Gemara
A bird sin offering whose blood was sprinkled below the red line for the sake of a burnt offering should effect acceptance — the actions performed prove it is a sin offering!
Rabba
No — because people might say: perhaps it IS a sin offering and the blood was already properly sprinkled below the line. The false intent is not recognizable to observers.
Gemara
But what about a goat sin offering slaughtered for a guilt offering? A guilt offering is a RAM with white wool; a goat has black hair. The species difference is entirely obvious!
Rabba
This case poses a genuine difficulty. Similarly, a calf or bull slaughtered for a Paschal or guilt offering — these animals cannot be mistaken for either offering.
Gemara
And for a Paschal offering (1st year lamb) for a guilt offering (2nd year ram) — is the age difference not recognizable?
Rabba
The difference between a first-year and second-year animal is NOT on people's minds, as an animal in its first year can appear as large as one in its second year.
Rabba's Core Rule When the mode of preparation proves the offering's identity, the owner fulfills the obligation. When the false intent IS clearly recognizable (different species, sex), it disqualifies.

📊 Three Amoraim — Three Resolutions

Rabba

  • Recognizably false intent → disqualifies
  • Non-recognizable false intent → fulfills obligation
  • Mode of preparation as objective proof of identity
  • Bird offerings not discussed by Rabbi Shimon at all
  • Difficulty: goat vs. ram difference is clearly visible

Rava

  • Source: Lev. 6:7 — "this is the LAW of the meal offering"
  • Singular 'law' = ONE law for ALL meal offerings
  • Handful removed for any meal offering → fulfills obligation
  • Slaughtered offerings each have specific individual laws
  • Sin offering for fat vs. blood atonement: Rabbis disqualify, R. Shimon validates

Rav Ashi

  • Intent regarding vessel type is inconsequential
  • Pan → deep-pan: mere vessel difference, not substantive
  • Substantive intent (dry vs. oil-mixed) DOES disqualify
  • Slaughtered offerings: even manner of slaughter is identical, yet each has distinct laws
  • Challenge: dry vs. oil-mixed cannot be excused as vessel-intent

📜 Rava's Verse and Rav Ashi's Rejoinder

זֹאת תּוֹרַת הַמִּנְחָה — חֲדָא תּוֹרָה לְכָל הַמְּנָחוֹת (ויקרא ו:ז)
וְזֹאת תּוֹרַת הַמִּנְחָה Rava (Lev. 6:7): "And this is the law of the meal offering" — the definite article signals ONE law for all meal offerings. Removing a handful from any meal offering for the sake of another always fulfills the obligation. Slaughtered offerings lack this unifying verse, so each stands alone.

Rav Aḥa challenges Rav Ashi: If vessel intent is inconsequential, why does removing a handful from a dry meal offering for the sake of an oil-mixed meal offering disqualify? Rav Ashi: That difference is in the substance of the offering itself, not merely the vessel — and such intent is indeed consequential.

The Contradiction: Baraita A — R. Shimon: meal offering not for its own sake is valid. Baraita B — R. Shimon: meal offering not for its own sake is invalid.
