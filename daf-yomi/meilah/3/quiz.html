5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabba, when does improper intent NOT disqualify a meal offering or sacrifice?
A. When the priest announces his corrected intent before sprinkling the blood
B. When the false intent is not recognizable to observers from the actions performed
C. When the owner was unaware of the priest's improper intent
D. When the offering belongs to the most sacred order
Correct: B — Rabba's key principle: only recognizably false intent disqualifies. When the mode of preparation does not visibly reveal improper intent to observers, the offering remains valid and fulfills the owner's obligation.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What biblical source does Rava cite to rule that removing a handful for a different meal offering still fulfills the owner's obligation?
A. "And he shall slaughter it for a sin offering" (Leviticus 4:33)
B. "Who sacrifices the blood of the peace offering" (Leviticus 7:33)
C. "And this is the law of the meal offering" (Leviticus 6:7)
D. "A fire offering of pleasing aroma to the Lord" (Leviticus 1:9)
Correct: C — Rava derives from the definite singular form in Lev. 6:7 that there is ONE law for all meal offerings. This unifying verse means removing a handful for the sake of any meal offering fulfills the obligation — unlike slaughtered offerings which each have specific individual laws.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Ashi, why does removing a handful from a pan meal offering for the sake of a deep-pan meal offering NOT disqualify?
A. Because both offerings require the same amount of oil
B. Because the priest corrected his intent before completing the rite
C. Because intent regarding the type of vessel is inconsequential
D. Because Rabbi Shimon exempts all meal offering mistakes from disqualification
Correct: C — Rav Ashi's resolution: the pan vs. deep-pan difference is merely a difference in the vessel used, and intention concerning the vessel type is inconsequential. Only intent directed at a substantively different offering category (e.g., dry vs. oil-mixed) disqualifies.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Which case poses the most serious challenge to Rabba's position about recognizable false intent?
A. A Paschal offering (first year) slaughtered for a guilt offering (second year)
B. A bird sin offering whose blood was sprinkled above the red line
C. A goat sin offering slaughtered for a guilt offering
D. A pan meal offering handful removed for a deep-pan meal offering
Correct: C — The goat sin offering vs. guilt offering case is most damaging to Rabba: a guilt offering is a RAM with distinctly white wool, while a goat has obviously black hair. This species and color difference is unambiguously recognizable, making Rabba's rule of 'non-recognizable intent' difficult to apply here.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What does Rabbi Shimon mean when he says meal offerings are 'not similar to slaughtered offerings'?
A. Meal offerings are intrinsically more holy than slaughtered offerings
B. Meal offerings do not require priestly intent, while slaughtered offerings do
C. Meal offerings are not similar to MOST slaughtered offerings, but are similar to the few whose preparation rites resemble one another
D. Meal offerings are only valid when brought by laypeople, unlike slaughtered offerings
Correct: C — Rabbi Shimon clarifies that meal offerings are not similar to MOST slaughtered offerings. There are, however, certain slaughtered offerings whose preparation rites are similar to each other — and regarding those, the same leniency could apply.
