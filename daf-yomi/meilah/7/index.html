🫙 The Handful: Removal, Return, and the Sanctification Problem

Priest removes the kometz (handful) from a meal offering inside a service vessel
↓
The handful is disqualified. Can the priest return it to the vessel without further sanctifying it?
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Vessels sanctify only with intent — since priest does not intend re-sanctification, returning is safe
Challenge (Reish Lakish)
But you never resolved whether vessels sanctify without intent — this proves nothing!
↓
Rav Amram: Return it to a heaped bowl (levisa) — handful sits above the rim, not inside the vessel
↓
Objection: Removing the handful creates a furrow — so returning it places it back inside the vessel, re-sanctifying it anyway

🫙 Do Service Vessels Sanctify Without Intent?

Rabbi Yoḥanan
Service vessels sanctify items placed in them only with specific intent. The priest returning a handful does not intend for the vessel to re-sanctify it — so it is not sanctified and no further disqualification occurs.
Reish Lakish
But I raised this very question as an unresolved dilemma before you! You cannot derive from this case that vessels require intent — the dilemma was never settled.
Rav Amram
Even if vessels sanctify without intent, one can return the handful to a heaped bowl (levisa) — the handful rests above the rim and is technically outside the vessel.
The Gemara
Two objections: (1) If the bowl is heaped, how was the handful removed initially? (2) Removal creates a furrow — returning the handful drops it into the furrow, which is inside the vessel!
Core Tension Every proposed solution for safely returning a disqualified handful back to the meal offering runs into a technical objection — the service vessel will re-sanctify it regardless.

🔄 Avimi & Rav Ḥisda — Demonstrating Proper Meal Offering Service

Background: Avimi was a senior Amora who forgot tractate Menaḥot. He came to the study hall of Rav Ḥisda — his own student — to review it. Rav Ḥisda had previously suffered mockery from Avimi over these laws.
↓
Question 1 (Rav Naḥman): How does one properly remove a handful? Avimi pointed to a vessel resting on the ground — implying removal from a ground-level vessel is valid.
↓
Question 2 (Rav Naḥman): How does one sanctify meal offerings? Avimi again pointed to a ground-level vessel. Rav Naḥman objected: the vessel must be raised off the ground first!
↓
Avimi's Full Method — Three Priests Required:
① A priest lifts the vessel off the ground
② A second priest sanctifies the meal offering in the raised vessel
③ A third priest removes the kometz
↓
Rav Naḥman's challenge: The mishna (12a) lists all rites of a meal offering with improper intent — but never mentions raising the vessel. This omission proves raising is not required!
↓
Avimi's rebuttal: That mishna only lists the order of sacrificial rites — not every procedural requirement. Absence from that list proves nothing.

⚖️ Rava's Analysis: Removal vs. Sanctification from a Ground-Level Vessel

✅ Removal — Clearly Valid

  • Rava: This is obvious — no doubt required
  • Proof: The frankincense bowls are removed from the Table of Shewbread
  • The Table stands directly on the floor of the Sanctuary
  • If removal from a ground-level surface were invalid, the entire shewbread service would be disqualified
  • Ruling: Removal from a ground-level vessel is fully valid

❓ Sanctification — Unresolved Dilemma

  • Rava raises a genuine dilemma about sanctification
  • Option A: Derive from meal offering laws → raising required for sanctification
  • Option B: Derive from blood collection laws → ground level is sufficient
  • Rava initially leans toward deriving from blood — but this is then challenged
  • The dilemma remains unresolved (תֵּיקוּ)

👘 Blood Sprays onto a Garment — Does It Require Laundering?

A priest is sprinkling blood of a חַטָּאת (sin offering). Some blood sprays onto a garment.
↓
When exactly did the blood spray onto the garment?
BEFORE the sprinkling left his hand
Garment requires laundering
The blood is still part of the valid sprinkling act
AFTER the sprinkling left his hand
No laundering required
This blood is mere remainder — not part of the valid sprinkling
↓
Red Heifer (Para 3:9) — Abaye's ruling: Between sprinklings, the priest wipes his finger on the edge of the bowl (keforei = wiping bowls, Ezra 1:10). After all sprinklings are complete, he wipes his hand on the heifer's body.
