QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yehuda, when a priest intends to leave the blood of an offering until the next day, what do all authorities agree?
A. The offering is fit, since intention does not affect halakhic status
B. The offering is unfit, since intention to perform an invalidating act is equivalent to the act
C. Only Rabbi Eliezer deems it unfit; the Rabbis deem it fit
D. Only the Rabbis deem it unfit; Rabbi Eliezer deems it fit
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda holds that intent to leave blood overnight is equivalent to the act itself — just as actually leaving blood overnight invalidates the offering, intending to do so also invalidates it, and on this point all agree.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why did Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua weep at the conclusion of his exchange with Yosef the Babylonian?
A. He was embarrassed at being publicly corrected over three days
B. He was moved by how precious Torah study was to Yosef, who persisted until he obtained the true ruling
C. He was mourning that Yosef would soon return to Babylonia
D. He realized his entire career of rulings had been mistaken
Correct: B — Rabbi Elazar wept tears of admiration and joy, declaring: 'Happy are you, Torah scholars, for whom matters of Torah are exceedingly dear!' He praised Yosef's extraordinary dedication in pursuing the correct halakha.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
The Gemara explains that the mishna's case of 'did not pour oil' cannot mean no oil was poured at all. What does it actually mean?
A. The oil was poured after the handful was removed rather than before
B. Less than the required amount of oil was poured
C. A non-priest performed the pouring instead of a priest
D. The oil was poured into the wrong vessel
Correct: C — Since the verse explicitly requires oil on a meal offering, 'did not pour' cannot mean no pouring occurred. The Gemara explains the mishna refers to a case where a non-priest performed the pouring — and the offering is still fit.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is Rabbi Zeira's principle regarding the mixing of oil and flour in a meal offering?
A. Any meal offering that can be physically mixed must be mixed, or it is invalid
B. For any measure of flour suitable for mixing, failure to actually mix does not invalidate the offering
C. Only the sixty-tenths meal offering requires mixing to be valid
D. Mixing may only be omitted for meal offerings that exceed sixty tenths of an ephah
Correct: B — Rabbi Zeira teaches that for any measure of flour that is physically suitable for mixing with oil, the omission of actual mixing does not invalidate the meal offering — mixing is a mitzva ideally but not essential to validity.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Rav Naḥman attempt to resolve the contradiction between the mishna (non-priest pouring = fit) and Rabbi Shimon's baraita (rites belong to priests)?
A. He argues Rabbi Shimon's baraita is a lone opinion and not halakha
B. He distinguishes between meal offerings of priests and meal offerings of Israelites
C. He distinguishes between biblical law requirements and rabbinic stringencies
D. He argues Rabbi Shimon only addresses cases where the non-priest acted intentionally
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman resolves the apparent contradiction by distinguishing categories: Rabbi Shimon's baraita addresses meal offerings brought by priests (stricter rules), while the mishna addresses meal offerings of Israelites (more lenient rules regarding non-priest performance).
