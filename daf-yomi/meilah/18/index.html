⚖️ Non-Priest Rites: Valid or Invalid?

Rabbis
The mishna rules a meal offering fit even if a priest did not pour the oil — meaning a non-priest who performed the pouring did not invalidate it
Rabbi Shimon
'Among the sons of Aaron' — any rite entrusted to priests that a priest denies is valid causes him to lose his priestly portion; these rites belong to priests
Rav Naḥman
No contradiction: Rabbi Shimon speaks of meal offerings of priests; the mishna addresses meal offerings of Israelites — different legal categories
Rava
But the oil-pouring law for priest meal offerings is derived from Israelite meal offerings — so if non-priests may perform it for Israelites, the same applies to priests!
Verse-Based Resolution 'He shall bring it to Aaron's sons' — the bringing precedes the priestly rites, implying pre-bringing acts are valid even when performed by a non-priest

🔍 Rabbi Yehuda: Intent to Leave Blood Overnight

Rabbi Yehuda
Rabbis and Rabbi Eliezer only dispute intent to drink blood or burn meat. But if one intends to leave blood until the next day, all agree the offering is unfit — intention equals the forbidden act itself
Gemara Challenge
A baraita contradicts this! Yosef the Babylonian asked Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua about this very case three times — and each time Rabbi Elazar ruled the offering fit, implying no universal agreement
Resolution
Rabbi Yehuda DID teach all agree it is unfit. When Yosef said 'you returned what I had lost,' he meant Rabbi Elazar finally corrected his ruling back to Rabbi Yehuda's original teaching
Key Phrase Explained 'You have returned to me that which I had lost' — Yosef's teacher (Rabbi Yehuda) taught the offering is unfit; Rabbi Elazar's prior rulings had contradicted this, and now he was restored to the correct ruling

😢 The Moving Story of Yosef the Babylonian

אַשְׁרֵיכֶם תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה חֲבִיבִין עֲלֵיכֶם בְּיוֹתֵר
Aggadah Yosef the Babylonian, a student of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, asked Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua the same halakhic question three times — evening, morning, and evening again. Each time Rabbi Elazar ruled the offering was fit.

Finally, Rabbi Elazar admitted: 'It seems to me that my halakhot were not accurate until now.'

Upon hearing this correction, Rabbi Elazar's eyes streamed with tears of admiration: 'Happy are you, Torah scholars, for whom matters of Torah are exceedingly dear!' He recited a verse in Yosef's honor.

🌾 Mishna: Omitted Rites That Do Not Invalidate

Did not pour oil (יְצִיקָה) onto the meal offering → offering is fit
↓
Did not mix oil (בְּלִילָה) into the meal offering → offering is fit
↓
Did not break loaves (פְּתִיתָה) into pieces, or broke into larger pieces than required → offering is fit
↓
Did not add salt (מְלִיחָה) → offering is fit
↓
Did not wave (תְּנוּפָה) the omer or sota meal offering → offering is fit
↓
Did not bring the offering near (הַגָּשָׁה) the altar → offering is fit
↓
⚠️ These are all mitzvot l'chatchila — obligatory ideally — but their omission or imperfection does not invalidate the offering

⚖️ Rabbi Shimon vs. Rabbis — Non-Priests and Meal Offering Rites

Rabbis — Offering is Fit

  • Verse: 'He shall bring it to Aaron's sons' — the bringing precedes the priestly rites
  • Acts performed before priestly service are not priestly rites per se
  • Pouring, mixing, breaking by a non-priest = offering valid
  • Rav Naḥman (v.2): even meal offerings from which a handful is not removed follow this rule

Rabbi Shimon — Priests' Portion at Stake

  • Verse: 'Among the sons of Aaron' — entrusted rites must be validated by priests
  • A priest who denies the validity of these rites forfeits his priestly portion
  • Rav Naḥman: R. Shimon speaks of meal offerings of priests specifically
  • Rava's challenge: the law for priest-offerings derives from Israelite-offerings — distinction collapses
