QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, what does Rabbi Yosei hold about piggul intent and the two Shavuot loaves?
A. The two loaves are two separate bodies, so intent on one never affects the other
B. The two loaves are one body; intent spanning both renders both piggul, but intent for only one renders neither piggul
C. Any intent during slaughter of one lamb renders both loaves piggul automatically
D. The loaves can never become piggul since they are sanctified food items
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan explains that Rabbi Yosei treats the two loaves as one body. When the priest 'mixes' his intent (olive-bulk spanning both), both become piggul; when he 'separates' by intending only one loaf, the intent is insufficient for either.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Rav Ashi's baraita refute Rav Huna's interpretation of Rabbi Yosei?
A. The baraita shows Rabbi Yosei requires intent specifically during the sprinkling rite
B. The baraita (in Rabbi Yosei's name) shows that even indirect intent — not aimed at the meat itself — renders the meat piggul, proving shared status between items
C. The baraita proves that only Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, not Rabbi Yosei, rules on piggul
D. The baraita demonstrates that two thighs are always treated as two bodies
Correct: B — Rav Ashi cites a baraita where Rabbi Yosei says that intent to pour remaining blood renders the meat piggul — even though the intent wasn't about the meat. This shows indirect/shared intent suffices, undermining Rav Huna's 'two bodies' reading.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
In the dispute about combining half-intents across different sacrificial rites, what does Levi's baraita teach?
A. Intents during slaughter and sprinkling always combine since both are permitting factors
B. Only intents during the same rite can combine to form an olive-bulk
C. Intents during the four rites — slaughter, receiving, carrying, sprinkling — do not combine to render an offering piggul
D. Combining intents is only relevant for meal offerings, not animal sacrifices
Correct: C — Levi's baraita explicitly states that intentions during the four sacrificial rites do not combine. Each rite is treated independently for purposes of piggul.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Abaye explain the various cases where the Rabbis (and Rabbi Yosei) disqualify offerings even without full piggul liability?
A. Each case reflects a general principle that all half-intents combine across permitting factors
B. Each ruling is a specific rabbinic decree based on analogy to a similar case — not a general combining principle
C. The disqualifications are all Biblical-level prohibitions derived from the verse about piggul
D. Abaye holds all such cases should actually carry full karet liability
Correct: B — Abaye argues that each rabbinic disqualification is a targeted decree modeled on an analogous case (e.g., the handful decree modeled on frankincense; the frankincense decree modeled on the sinner's meal offering). There is no general rule that half-intents combine.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In the mishna closing Daf 14, Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis dispute what happens when one Shavuot loaf becomes impure before blood-sprinkling. What is Rabbi Yehuda's position and his reasoning?
A. The pure loaf is eaten and the impure loaf is burned; communal offerings may be divided
B. Both loaves are burned, because communal offerings are never divided — you cannot treat one portion differently from the other
C. The impure loaf is redeemed for money and new loaves are baked
D. The priest sprinkles the blood anyway and the tzitz effects atonement for the impurity
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda holds that since communal offerings are never divided (אֵין חוֹלְקִין בְּקָרְבַּן צִבּוּר), both loaves must go to burning together — you cannot eat the pure one while burning the impure one.
