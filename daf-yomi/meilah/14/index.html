⚖️ The Great Piggul Dispute: One Body or Two?

Rav Huna
Rabbi Yosei holds the two thighs (or two loaves) are two distinct bodies. Piggul intent on one does not render the other piggul.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Rabbi Yosei holds they are one body. Intent on one thigh does render the other piggul. For the two loaves: if the priest combines his intent (olive-bulk spanning both), both are piggul; if he separates, neither is.
Rav Ashi
Refutation of Rav Huna: A baraita (in Rabbi Yosei's name) shows that even where the priest had no intent regarding the meat itself, the meat is rendered piggul — proving that indirect intent suffices, and the two items share one status.
Ravina
Further refutation: The mishna (13a) shows that intent to burn the handful renders the remainder piggul. If indirect intent works there, all the more so when the intent touches the offering directly — supporting Rabbi Yoḥanan.
Conclusion Rabbi Yoḥanan's view prevails: the two loaves are one body. Intent mixing both = piggul for both. Intent for only one = neither is piggul (the intent is 'separated').

🔄 Do Half-Intents Across Rites Combine?

Some Say (Amoraim)
Intents during slaughter AND sprinkling combine: both are permitting factors, so half-intent at slaughter + half-intent at sprinkling = full olive-bulk = piggul.
Levi (Baraita)
Intents during the four rites do NOT combine — slaughter, receiving, carrying, sprinkling are each independent. A split intent never adds up to piggul liability.
Abaye
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds that intents do not combine even when each involves half a permitting factor + half a measure — e.g., one lamb slaughtered with intent for half an olive-bulk from each of two different loaves.
Rava bar Rav Ḥanan
Challenge: If Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi agrees that intent during a full permitting factor + half measure does combine, why does he rule no piggul in the two-loaves case? It must be a special rabbinic decree.
Abaye's Resolution Each rabbinic disqualification ruling is a decree based on analogy to a similar case — not a general principle that all half-intents combine.

📜 Rabbinic Decrees: Disqualification by Analogy

Rabbi Yosei's Decree

  • Mishna (13a): Intent to burn the handful the next day disqualifies the remainder
  • Why? Decree: concern that intent involving the handful of frankincense might be confused with intent involving the regular handful
  • The two cases are analogous — so disqualification extends by decree

The Rabbis' Decree

  • Mishna (16a): Intent during burning of one frankincense bowl (but not the other) disqualifies by decree
  • Decree: modeled on the case of a sinner's meal offering which has no frankincense — the two are treated in parallel
  • Similarly: one lamb slaughtered with intent for both loaves = disqualified (not full piggul liability)

Shewbread Analogy

  • Burning one frankincense bowl with intent to eat both arrangements of shewbread the next day
  • The shewbread is disqualified by rabbinic decree — due to the other bowl of frankincense
  • Pattern: the Rabbis consistently extend disqualification to paired items as a protective fence

💧 Mishna: One of Two Loaves Becomes Impure

צִיץ מְרַצֶּה עַל טֻמְאַת צִבּוּר
מַחֲלֹקֶת — Dispute Case: One of the two Shavuot loaves (or one arrangement of shewbread) becomes ritually impure before the blood is sprinkled (the act that permits them for consumption).

Rabbi Yehuda: Both loaves go to burning — communal offerings are never divided; you cannot burn one and eat the other.

The Rabbis: The pure loaf is eaten as normal; only the impure loaf is burned. Communal offerings can be split when circumstances require.

Gemara (Rabbi Elazar): This dispute applies only when impurity occurs before sprinkling. After sprinkling, both agree the pure loaf is eaten.

Rav Pappa: Their disagreement centers on the tzitz (High Priest's frontplate) — does it effect atonement for impure communal offerings, thereby allowing the pure portion to remain permitted?

📋 Me'ilah 14 — Summary Map

Core Question: Are the two loaves / two thighs one body or two bodies?
↓
Rav Huna: Two bodies → intent on one never affects the other
Rabbi Yoḥanan: One body → intent spanning both = piggul for both; isolated intent = neither
↓
Rabbi Yoḥanan's view upheld by baraita (Rav Ashi) and mishna (Ravina): indirect intent suffices; shared body = shared status
↓
Half-intents across rites: Do NOT combine per Levi's baraita. Rabbinic disqualifications are case-by-case decrees, never general principle.
↓
Mishna Coda: If one loaf becomes impure — Rabbi Yehuda burns both (no dividing communal offerings); Rabbis permit the pure loaf; dispute hinges on whether the tzitz effects atonement.
