↓

↓

Priest removes the handful (kemitza)
↓
✅ Lishmah — for its own sake
Offering is valid and satisfies the owner's obligation
❌ Shelo Lishmah — for another offering's sake
Offering is valid but does NOT satisfy the owner's obligation

⚖️ Shelo Lishmah: Meal Offering vs. Animal Offering

🌾 Meal Offering

  • Each type has a unique mode of preparation
  • Sinner's offering: no oil or frankincense
  • Jealousy offering: coarse barley flour
  • Preparation method itself reveals intent
  • Therefore: improper intent is recognizable → can disqualify (R. Shimon)

🐂 Animal (Slaughtered) Offering

  • One uniform manner of slaughter for all offerings
  • One manner of blood collection for all
  • One manner of blood sprinkling for all
  • Mode of action does not reveal intent
  • Therefore: shelo lishmah is valid and satisfies obligation

📜 Verse Proof — Deviating from Protocol

מוֹצָא שְׂפָתֶיךָ תִּשְׁמֹר וְעָשִׂיתָ (דברים כג:כד)
Deuteronomy 23:24 "That which has gone out of your lips you shall observe and do; according to what you have vowed as a gift offering to the Lord your God, that which you have promised with your mouth." The Gemara derives: Just as a vowed gift offering may not be deviated from ab initio, so too a meal offering already sacrificed shelo lishmah retains full sanctity — one may never change its protocol even after the improper act. The offering remains permanently bound to its original designation.

🔄 Resolving Rabbi Shimon's Contradiction

Rav Ashi
R. Shimon's two statements are not contradictory. Where he says the offering SATISFIES the obligation: that is a 'change of sanctity' (e.g., voluntary offering removed for sake of a sinner's offering — different category). Where he says it does NOT satisfy: that is a 'change of name' (one voluntary offering removed for sake of another voluntary).
Rabba
Distinction: Change of sanctity = still satisfies the obligation. Change of name = does not satisfy. The mishna discusses change of name, which is why the owner's obligation remains unfulfilled.
Abaye
Challenge to Rabba: If the Torah's disqualification of meal offerings through improper intent is derived from comparison to sin and guilt offerings — what difference does it make to me whether it is a change of name or change of sanctity? The same logic should disqualify both equally!
Rabba
The comparison to sin/guilt offerings is not a mere scriptural decree but logical reasoning. R. Shimon holds that only intent which is NOT 'recognizably false' — i.e., not in clear contradiction to the rites being performed — can disqualify. Recognizably false intent is legally disregarded.
R. Shimon's Core Principle Improper intent disqualifies only when it is consistent with the rites being performed (not recognizably false). Intent that obviously contradicts the action is ignored by halakha.
