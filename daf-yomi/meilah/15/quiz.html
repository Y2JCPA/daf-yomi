QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Gemara's conclusion (backed by Rav Huna, Rav Ashi, and Ravina), what is true about the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis?
A. They dispute whether the tzitz works for impure items consumed by priests
B. They dispute whether the tzitz works for impure items that ascend the altar
C. Their dispute is NOT about the tzitz (frontplate) at all
D. They dispute whether the tzitz works for the Paschal offering specifically
Correct: C — Three independent proofs — from Rav Huna bar Rav Natan, Rav Ashi, and Ravina — all demonstrate that the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis cannot concern the tzitz. Their actual dispute lies elsewhere.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the todah render its loaves piggul, but the loaves cannot render the todah piggul?
A. The loaves are more numerous and thus have greater sanctity
B. The blood of the todah is the matir (permit) for the loaves — the loaves have no permitting role over the offering
C. Piggul rules are stricter for animal offerings than grain offerings
D. The todah is slaughtered first, giving it temporal priority
Correct: B — The Gemara explains: it is the blood of the todah that renders its loaves permitted (to be consumed). Since the loaves do not permit the offering, they cannot render it piggul. Piggul flows from permitter to permitted.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
In Rabbi Elazar's dilemma, the Gemara challenges Rav's ruling with an a fortiori argument. How is that argument rejected?
A. The a fortiori is accepted and Rav's ruling is overturned
B. A proof from kilayim (diverse kinds) shows we do not always apply a fortiori across different legal categories
C. The Gemara finds a verse that explicitly permits the loaves to become piggul
D. Rava intervenes with a different principle about fixed offerings
Correct: B — The Gemara brings a baraita about hemp and arum planted in a vineyard: Torah-forbidden seeds are prohibited but the vines are not sanctified. This shows that a fortiori reasoning does not always bridge Torah-level and rabbinic-level rules — rejecting the challenge.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rava's explanation, what is Rabbi Meir's reasoning for holding that libations can become piggul through intent at the time of slaughter?
A. Rabbi Meir holds that libations are always considered consecrated before the animal offering
B. Rabbi Meir holds the libations are physically present at slaughter and therefore affected
C. Rabbi Meir holds the libations become fixed to this specific offering at the moment of slaughter and cannot be reassigned
D. Rabbi Meir relies on a gezeirah shavah connecting libations to the blood service
Correct: C — Rava explains that Rabbi Meir holds the libations become קבועים (fixed/assigned) to this particular offering at the moment of slaughter. After that point, they may not be used with a different offering — therefore piggul-intent at slaughter can affect them.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
The Gemara notes that the mishna needed to teach BOTH the todah case AND the Shavuot lambs case. Why?
A. They involve different numbers of loaves so the rules differ
B. Each case teaches something the other cannot: they differ in whether the accompanying item is consumed or burned on the altar
C. Had only the todah been taught, we might think the Shavuot lambs rule is stricter; had only the lambs been taught, we might think the todah rule is more lenient
D. The two cases are taught for different audiences — one for kohanim, one for Israelites
Correct: C — The Gemara explains that both cases are necessary: from the todah alone one might have reasoned differently about the lambs (since the lambs render the loaves permitted in a unique way), and vice versa. Each case teaches a nuance the other cannot supply.
