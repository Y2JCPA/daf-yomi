🏅 Does the Tzitz Dispute Concern the Frontplate At All?

Rav Huna b. Rav Natan
Can the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis really be about the frontplate? Even Rabbi Yehuda concedes the tzitz works for impure items that ascend the altar! Their dispute must concern something else entirely.
Rav Ashi
Additional proof from the Paschal offering mishna (Pesachim 80a): the tzitz cannot be what Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis argue about — the context there confirms this.
Ravina
Further proof from the mishna about the two loaves of Shavuot: if their dispute were about the tzitz, Rabbi Yehuda would have said 'both are burned' — but he did not say that!
Conclusion
Three independent proofs — Rav Huna, Rav Ashi, Ravina — all converge: the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis is NOT about the frontplate (tzitz).
📌 Key Principle The tzitz effects acceptance for impure items that ascend the altar — this is undisputed. The question between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis lies elsewhere.

🍞 How the Thanks Offering Renders Its Loaves Piggul

Kohen slaughters the Todah
with intent to eat an olive-bulk outside its permitted time
↓
The Todah itself
Piggul ✗
Rendered piggul — liable for eating it
Accompanying Loaves
Also Piggul ✗
The blood of the todah permits the loaves — so they share the piggul fate
↓
Reverse is NOT true
Intent concerning the loaves alone does NOT render the todah piggul.
The loaves have no matir role over the offering.

⚖️ One-Way Piggul: Todah vs. Shavuot Lambs

🐄 Thanks Offering (Todah)

  • Todah → renders its loaves piggul ✓
  • Loaves → do NOT render todah piggul ✗
  • Reason: blood of todah is the matir (permit) for the loaves
  • Loaves have no permitting role over the offering
  • The mishna: 'the thanks offering renders the loaves piggul'

🐑 Shavuot Lambs (Two Loaves)

  • Lambs → render the two loaves piggul ✓
  • Two loaves → do NOT render the lambs piggul ✗
  • Reason: blood of the lambs permits the loaves to be eaten
  • Same asymmetric matir logic as the todah case
  • Both cases needed: each teaches what the other cannot

🤔 Rabbi Elazar's Dilemma: Does Combined Intent Render Loaves Piggul?

Rabbi Elazar
If one slaughters the todah intending to eat an olive-bulk from it AND from its loaves the next day — does the combined intent render the loaves piggul? I'm not asking about the todah; I'm asking whether intent spanning both can piggul the loaves.
Rav
Even in this case: the loaves ARE rendered piggul. The thanks offering is NOT rendered piggul.
The Gemara
Challenge: By a fortiori — if the todah, which CAN render the loaves piggul, is itself NOT piggul — can the loaves become piggul? The lesser should certainly not!
Rejection
We do not apply this a fortiori. Proof from kilayim baraita: hemp and arum in a vineyard are Torah-forbidden; the vines are not sanctified. Torah-level and rabbinic-level rules do not combine in an a fortiori.
Three Versions This dilemma is taught in three versions: (1) todah + loaves, (2) Shavuot lambs + loaves, (3) Rabbi Abba's version — one lamb with intent about the other lamb. All resolve similarly.

🫙 Libations & Piggul: Rabbi Meir vs. the Rabbis

Mishna (end of daf): The animal offering renders its libations piggul from when they are consecrated in the vessel — not before. (Statement of Rabbi Meir)
↓
Rabbis challenge: A person may bring his offering today and the libations up to ten days later! If the blood does not permit them immediately, how can piggul-intent at slaughter affect them?
↓
Rabbi Meir replies: I too spoke only of libations that come with this offering. Once brought together, they are fixed to it — one may not switch them to another offering after slaughter.
↓
Rava explains: Rabbi Meir holds that the libations become fixed (קבועים) to this offering at the moment of slaughter — therefore piggul-intent at slaughter can affect them.
↓
Parallel — Leper's Oil (לוג שמן): If the guilt offering of the metzora becomes piggul, the log of oil is also liable for piggul. Rabbis object: the oil can be switched to another leper's offering — so it should not be considered part of this offering.
