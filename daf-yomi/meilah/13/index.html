⚖️ Rabbi Yosei vs. Rabbis: What Renders a Meal Offering Piggul?

Rabbis
If one removes the handful intending to burn the frankincense the next day, the meal offering is piggul — the frankincense is bound up with the offering.
Rabbi Yosei
The frankincense is not part of the meal offering's own preclusion — it is a separate permitting factor. A permitting factor cannot render another permitting factor piggul.
Reish Lakish
The same rule applies to the two bowls of frankincense of the shewbread — burning one bowl with piggul intent does not render the shewbread piggul.
Rav Huna
Rabbi Yosei would hold that piggul intent regarding one of the two Shavuot loaves affects only that loaf — just as ritual impurity to one limb does not spread to all limbs.
Core Principle Does intent regarding one permitting factor (matir) render a paired or linked offering piggul? Rabbi Yosei: no — each matir stands independently.

🔍 Abaye: Why Does the Mishna Need to Teach Both Consume & Burn Cases?

Abaye
Why does the mishna need to teach that intent to consume half an olive-bulk + burn half an olive-bulk do not combine? Can't we derive this from the earlier rule about consume-and-consume not joining?
Gemara
If intent to consume a typical-consume item and consume a typical-not-consumed item don't join, shouldn't consume and burn obviously not join either — since they are even more different?
Abaye
The mishna is still necessary! In this case, one intends to consume half in the typical manner for that half, and to burn the other half in the typical manner for that half — each intent aligns with its own part.
Gemara
One might have thought that since each half-intent is appropriate to its respective portion, they should combine into a whole piggul. The mishna teaches us they do not.
Key Distinction When consume-intent matches the consume-half and burn-intent matches the burn-half, the two halves might seem to form a coherent piggul together — the mishna negates this.

⚔️ Animal Offering vs. Meal Offering: Piggul by Frankincense Intent

Animal Offering (Rabbis' Model)

  • Slaughtered with intent to sacrifice the altar-portions the next day → piggul
  • The altar-portions are the permitting factor (matir) of the sacrifice
  • Intent during slaughter involving the matir renders the whole offering piggul
  • Rabbis: meal offering should work identically — intent re: frankincense = piggul

Meal Offering (Rabbi Yosei)

  • Handful removed with intent to burn frankincense next day → NOT piggul, only pasul
  • Frankincense is a separate permitting factor — it permits the priests to eat the remainder
  • The handful is the matir of the meal offering; frankincense is matir of the shewbread
  • One permitting factor cannot render another permitting factor piggul (matir b'matir)

🧪 Rav Mari's Proof: Non-Priest Collecting Frankincense Disqualifies

Mishna (12a) rule: Any rite during which improper intent can render an offering piggul — if performed by a non-priest, it invalidates the offering
↓
Rabbi Yannai's ruling: A non-priest who collects (mekabeitz) the frankincense from a meal offering — this collection is invalid and disqualifies the offering
↓
Rav Mari's proposed proof from mishna: Placing the handful into a vessel is listed among piggul-rendering rites — so it is comparable to collecting blood into a service vessel, which is a valid analogy
↓
Gemara challenges: Are these really analogous? When blood is collected into a vessel, the vessel confers sanctity on the blood as a matir. But the handful is already sanctified before placement — the vessel adds nothing!
↓
True reason for handful placement: Since it is impossible to sacrifice the handful on the altar without first placing it in a vessel, this step is an indispensable prerequisite — therefore it is a significant sacrificial rite
↓
Unifying principle: Both blood collection and handful placement share vessel sanctity — a service vessel sanctifies a permitting factor (blood or handful) for the altar. This is why both rites are subject to the same rules.
