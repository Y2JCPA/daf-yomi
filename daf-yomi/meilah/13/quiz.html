QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Abaye, why does the mishna still need to teach that intent to consume half + burn half do not combine into piggul?
A. Because consuming and burning are always treated as identical intentions
B. Because when each intent matches its respective half's typical manner, one might think they combine to form a whole piggul
C. Because the mishna is addressing a case where only frankincense is involved
D. Because burning intent is always stronger than consuming intent and overrides it
Correct: B — The novelty is that when one intends to consume the consume-half in its typical manner and to burn the burn-half in its typical manner, each intent perfectly matches its part — one might reason they should join. The mishna teaches they still do not.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In what case does Rabbi Yosei concede that removing the handful renders the meal offering piggul?
A. When one intends to burn the frankincense the next day
B. When one intends to eat the remainder or burn the handful the next day
C. When one intends to eat both the remainder and the handful
D. Only when the handful is removed by a non-priest with improper intent
Correct: B — Rabbi Yosei concedes piggul when the intent involves the handful itself — the direct matir of the meal offering. He disputes only the case of frankincense, which is a separate permitting factor.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Reish Lakish's ruling about the two bowls of frankincense accompanying the shewbread?
A. Burning either bowl with piggul intent renders the shewbread piggul
B. Both bowls must be burned simultaneously for piggul to apply
C. Burning one bowl with piggul intent does not render the shewbread piggul
D. The shewbread is never subject to piggul rules since it is not a standard meal offering
Correct: C — Reish Lakish extends Rabbi Yosei's matir b'matir principle: the frankincense is a permitting factor for the shewbread, but one permitting factor cannot render another piggul — so burning one bowl with piggul intent is insufficient.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does the Gemara reject the analogy between placing the handful in a vessel and collecting blood into a service vessel as the reason for the handful-placement rite's significance?
A. Because only blood collection is subject to piggul rules, not handful placement
B. Because the vessel sanctifies blood as a permitting factor, but the handful is already sanctified before placement — the vessel adds nothing new
C. Because the handful and blood collection occur at different stages of the offering process
D. Because non-priests are permitted to collect blood but not to place the handful in a vessel
Correct: B — For blood, the collection into the vessel is the act that confers its status as a matir. For the handful, the sanctity exists before placement — so the vessel-sanctity analogy fails. The true reason is that placement is an indispensable prerequisite to sacrificing the handful.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What does Rav Naḥman's baraita specify about liability to karet for the two Shavuot loaves?
A. Liability arises whenever either loaf alone is rendered piggul
B. There is never liability to karet for the two loaves under any circumstances
C. Liability to karet requires that one's piggul intent covered an olive-bulk of both loaves
D. Liability depends solely on whether the accompanying lambs were slaughtered with piggul intent
Correct: C — Rav Naḥman's baraita teaches that one is liable to karet only when the piggul intent covered an olive-bulk from each of the two loaves — supporting the view that the loaves are independent units requiring separate, adequate intent for full piggul liability.
