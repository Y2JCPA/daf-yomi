⚖️ The Intermingled Handful: Fit or Unfit?

Rabbis
If a kometz (handful) is intermingled with any other kometz — even from another standard meal offering — the mixture is unfit. Once combined, the correct portion cannot be separated and offered properly.
Rabbi Yehuda
A kometz mixed with another standard kometz is still fit. Only when intermingled with the meal offering of the anointed priest or meal offering of libations is it unfit — those are entirely burned with no fixed kometz portion.
Rabbi Yochanan
Remarkably, both the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda derive their rulings from the same verse — the Yom Kippur blood sprinkling — yet they disagree on which principle is operative: min b'mino (same type) or oleh l'mizbeach (ascends to the altar).
Core Tension When sanctified portions are combined inseparably, does the offering become invalid? The answer depends on whether the disqualifying factor is the type-identity of the mixture or the altar-bound status of what was mixed.

🧂 Salt & Wood: Must Come From Communal Sources

Salt for offerings: The Torah granted Israelites the right to use salt from the lishka (chamber) — funded by the communal half-shekel contributions. Salt belongs to all Israel, not private donors.
↓
Wood for the altar: A baraita raises the question — one might have thought individuals could donate private wood. A verse is required to teach otherwise.
↓
Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua's analogy: Just as the altar itself was never used by an ordinary person — it was built exclusively for divine service — so too the wood and fire must not have been previously used by an ordinary person.
↓
Therefore: all altar wood must be dedicated, unused material from communal sources — private or previously-used wood is disqualified.
↓
Challenge from Araunah: King David used Araunah's threshing sledges as altar firewood — wasn't that previously-used wood? The Gemara resolves this as an exceptional case under divine instruction.

🪵 What Are Morigim? Araunah's Threshing Sledges

הִנֵּה הַבָּקָר לָעֹלָה וְהַמֹּרִגִּים וּכְלֵי הַבָּקָר לָעֵצִים — שמואל ב כד:כב
Tangential Inquiry The verse in Samuel records: "And Araunah said to David: Let the king take and offer what seems good; behold the oxen for the burnt offering and the morigim and the yokes of the oxen for wood." What is a morigim? • Ulla: It is a turbal bed — but this expression was unfamiliar in Babylonia. • Rav Yehuda: It is a threshing sledge — a wooden board with iron teeth on the underside, dragged over harvested grain to separate the kernels from the stalks. King David repurposed Araunah's agricultural threshing boards as firewood for the emergency altar — demonstrating that even used wooden implements can serve the altar in extraordinary circumstances.

🥣 Mishna: Which Intermingled Handfuls Are Unfit?

Rabbis: All Cases Unfit

  • Kometz + kometz of another standard offering ✗
  • Kometz + meal offering of priests ✗
  • Kometz + meal offering of the anointed priest (High Priest) ✗
  • Kometz + meal offering of libations ✗
  • Reason: once combined, the correct separate portions cannot be distinguished and offered

Rabbi Yehuda: Only Some Cases Unfit

  • Kometz + kometz of another standard offering ✓ FIT
  • Kometz + meal offering of priests ✗ Unfit
  • Kometz + meal offering of anointed priest ✗ Unfit
  • Kometz + meal offering of libations ✗ Unfit
  • Reason: priest/anointed priest/libation offerings are entirely burned — no fixed kometz — so there is no separable portion and the mixture is impossible to offer

🔬 When Is a Sacrificial Mixture Not Nullified?

A sacrificial substance is mixed with another substance.

Is it min b'mino — same type mixed with same type?
↓
YES — same type
Rabbi Yehuda: Not nullified under any circumstances. Same-type mixtures always preserve their identity.
(e.g., blood + blood, kometz + kometz)
NO — different types
Does the substance ascend to the altar (oleh l'mizbeach)?
↓
YES — ascends to altar
Rabbis: Not nullified. Altar-bound substances retain their identity in any mixture, regardless of type-sameness.
NO — does not ascend
Standard majority rule applies — the minority substance is nullified by the majority.

🔗 Rabbi Yochanan: One Verse, Two Opposing Derivations

Rabbi Yochanan
Both the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda derive their rulings from the same Yom Kippur verse — where the High Priest intermingles the blood of the bull and the goat before sprinkling. Yet they extract opposite principles.
Rabbis (from verse)
The verse teaches: substances that ascend to the altar are not nullified in a mixture. The example happens to be same-type (blood + blood), but the operative rule is oleh l'mizbeach — had the items been different types they still would not be nullified.
Rabbi Yehuda (from verse)
The verse teaches: any min b'mino mixture is never nullified. The altar-ascending context is incidental — the rule derives from the same-type quality, and applies universally even outside the Temple.
Gemara (objection)
Perhaps both conditions must hold simultaneously — same type AND ascending to altar — before non-nullification applies? The Gemara rebuts: each criterion independently prevents nullification, as established by the verse's dual qualities.
The Yom Kippur Verse The High Priest's mixing of bull-blood and goat-blood satisfies both criteria at once: it is min b'mino (same type: blood + blood) and oleh l'mizbeach (both ascend to the altar). From this single case, two universal principles are derived — in opposite directions.
