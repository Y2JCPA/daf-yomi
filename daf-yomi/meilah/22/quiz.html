Me'ilah 22 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua, why must wood burned on the altar not have been previously used by an ordinary person?
A. The Torah explicitly prohibits secondhand wood in the Temple service
B. Previously-used wood may harbor tumah (impurity) that disqualifies it
C. Just as the altar itself was never used by an ordinary person, the wood placed upon it must also be undedicated
D. Private wood donations could create uncertainty about ownership and tithing obligations
Correct: C — Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua draws an analogy: the altar was built exclusively for divine service and was never put to ordinary use. By the same logic, the wood and fire placed upon it must also have been previously undedicated — not used by any ordinary person before being brought for the altar.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does Rav Yehuda identify as the meaning of 'morigim' (מוֹרִגִּים) in the verse about Araunah?
A. Ornamental wooden beams used in palace construction
B. Threshing sledges — wooden boards with iron teeth dragged over grain to separate the kernels
C. A type of agricultural cart used to transport grain to the threshing floor
D. Iron plowshares used to break up hardened soil before planting
Correct: B — Rav Yehuda explains that morigim are threshing sledges — agricultural implements with iron teeth on the underside, dragged over harvested grain to separate the kernels from the stalks. King David repurposed Araunah's threshing boards as firewood for his emergency altar offering.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
In the mishna, according to Rabbi Yehuda, a kometz intermingled with a kometz from another standard meal offering is:
A. Unfit, because any intermingling of handfuls creates an inseparable mix
B. Fit, because same-type mixtures of kometz do not render each other invalid
C. Unfit only if the two offerings belonged to different people
D. Fit only if both offerings were brought voluntarily (not obligatory)
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda holds that a kometz mixed with another standard kometz remains fit. He only rules as unfit those handfuls intermingled with offerings that are entirely burned — like the meal offering of the anointed priest or libations — because those have no separate kometz portion to distinguish, making proper offering impossible.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is Rabbi Yochanan's key insight about the dispute between the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda?
A. The Rabbis follow a Sinaitic oral tradition while Rabbi Yehuda relies on logical inference
B. Both the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda derive their positions from the same verse about the Yom Kippur blood sprinkling
C. Rabbi Yehuda's ruling is stricter and was adopted as the normative halakha by later authorities
D. The dispute only applies to meal offerings but not to blood sacrifices
Correct: B — Rabbi Yochanan teaches that both the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda base their positions on the same verse describing the Yom Kippur service, where the High Priest mixes the blood of the bull and the goat before sprinkling. From this single case — which involves both a same-type mixture and altar-bound substances — each authority extracts a different operative principle.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to the Rabbis' reading of the Yom Kippur verse, what is the primary rule about non-nullification?
A. Same-type (min b'mino) mixtures are never nullified, regardless of their altar status
B. Substances that ascend to the altar (oleh l'mizbeach) are not nullified in any mixture
C. Nullification requires both same-type AND altar-ascending qualities to be absent
D. The minority portion is only nullified when it is less than one-sixtieth of the total mixture
Correct: B — The Rabbis derive from the Yom Kippur verse that substances which ascend to the altar are not nullified in a mixture — this is the operative principle. The verse uses same-type blood as its example, but the Rabbis hold that the rule applies to altar-bound items even of different types. Had the items been different types that ascend to the altar, they still would not be nullified.
