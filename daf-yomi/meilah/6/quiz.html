QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the Gemara conclude that a Biblical verse is necessary to exclude tereifa from sacrifice?
A. Because tereifa is explicitly named as forbidden in Leviticus 1
B. Because every a fortiori inference from valid sources can be refuted by unique features of those sources
C. Because Rav Sheisha derived it from the laws of diverse kinds
D. Because the Sages enacted a rabbinic fence around the altar
Correct: B — Rav Ashi shows that every attempt to derive tereifa's exclusion by kal va-chomer fails — either due to unique features of the proposed analogies or because the inference collapses in circular refutation. Three separate Biblical verses are ultimately required.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the significance of Rav Ashi's 'visible defect' argument against the a fortiori inference?
A. A blemished animal and a caesarean-born animal both have visible or known defects, unlike a tereifa whose wounds may be internal and hidden
B. A blemished animal is permitted for consumption while a tereifa is not
C. A caesarean-born animal is never sanctified as a firstborn, unlike a tereifa
D. The Torah explicitly mentions visible defects as disqualifying for the altar
Correct: A — Rav Ashi argues that the common element of blemished animals and caesarean-born animals is that their disqualifying condition is visible or publicly known. A tereifa may have hidden internal perforations that go undetected — making it a distinct category that cannot be derived by analogy.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does the mishna explicitly state 'both the meal offering of a sinner AND all other meal offerings' rather than simply 'all meal offerings'?
A. Because the meal offering of a sinner uses barley instead of wheat
B. According to Rabbi Shimon, since the sinner's offering is deliberately inferior (no oil, no frankincense), one might think standard disqualification rules do not apply to it
C. Because the sinner's offering requires a different priest to remove the handful
D. Because Ben Beteira holds the sinner's offering follows entirely different laws
Correct: B — Rabbi Shimon rules that the sinner's meal offering lacks oil and frankincense so the sinner does not profit from a fine offering. One might then think it also escapes standard disqualification rules. The mishna's phrasing explicitly includes it to prevent this error.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to the Gemara's conclusion, what is the key factor that determines whether Ben Beteira's leniency (returning the handful) applies?
A. Whether the priest who removed it was an acute mourner or merely impure
B. Whether the handful has already been sanctified by placement in a service vessel
C. Whether the meal offering belongs to a sinner or an ordinary person
D. Whether the removal occurred inside or outside the Temple courtyard
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman concludes that placement of the handful in a service vessel completes the sacrificial rite. Before this sanctification, Ben Beteira permits returning the handful for proper re-removal. After the handful is placed in the vessel, the rite is complete and it cannot be undone.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How many separate Biblical verses does the Gemara require to exclude a tereifa from sacrifice, and what distinguishes each scenario?
A. One verse suffices for all tereifa scenarios
B. Two verses — one for before sanctification, one for after
C. Three verses — covering: (1) tereifa before sanctification, (2) owner sanctified it after it became tereifa, (3) it became tereifa after sanctification
D. Four verses — including a dedicated verse for animal tithe offerings
Correct: C — The Gemara requires three verses because each addresses a unique scenario: 'well-watered pastures' excludes tereifa never sanctified; 'of the herd' (Lev. 1:3) excludes tereifa sanctified after becoming tereifa; 'whatever passes under the rod' (Lev. 27:32) excludes an animal that became tereifa after sanctification.
