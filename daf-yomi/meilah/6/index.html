⚖️ Can Tereifa's Exclusion Be Derived by Logic Alone?

Rav Sheisha b. Rav Idi
Try deriving tereifa's disqualification by a fortiori from fat & blood — both are permitted items whose sacrifice is forbidden. But fat & blood are notable in that they come from a generally permitted animal. Inference fails!
Rav Ashi
The refutation exists from the outset. Whether you derive from a blemished animal or a caesarean-born animal, each has a unique feature the other lacks. They collapse into circular refutation — a verse is definitively required.
Rav Aḥa Sava
Caesarean-born proves you can't reject the inference on those grounds — it isn't sanctified as a firstborn, showing each case has unique features. The circular refutation holds and kills any inference!
Rav Ashi
Their common element: their general prohibition was NEVER permitted for sacrifice. But tereifa's prohibition WAS permitted — e.g., pinching of a bird sin offering. Also, both blemished and caesarean have visible/known defects; tereifa may be hidden.
Rav Aḥa bar Rava
The 'permitting' for tereifa — what does it mean? For a bird burnt offering, the bird becomes tereifa through the pinching itself. For a sin offering, priests eat it — but that is eating, not sacrificing a tereifa animal!
Conclusion Every attempt to derive tereifa's exclusion via kal va-chomer fails. Multiple Biblical verses are required — each addressing a distinct scenario of when and how an animal becomes tereifa relative to its sanctification.

📜 Why Three Separate Verses Are Needed for Tereifa

Question: Can one verse exclude tereifa from all sacrificial scenarios?
↓
Verse 1: "Well-watered pastures of Israel"
Excludes tereifa that was never sanctified — owner did not designate it for sacrifice before it became tereifa
Verse 2: "Of the herd" (Lev. 1:3)
Excludes tereifa where owner sanctified it after it already became tereifa
Verse 3: "Whatever passes under the rod" (Lev. 27:32)
Excludes tereifa that was sanctified first and became tereifa afterward
↓
All three verses are indispensable — without each, one would permit tereifa in at least one scenario

⚔️ Rav Ashi vs. Rav Aḥa Sava: The Circular Refutation Debated

Rav Ashi
Common element of blemished + caesarean: their prohibition was NEVER permitted for sacrifice. Tereifa differs — the prohibition was once permitted (bird sin offering pinching renders the bird permitted to priests). Therefore no kal va-chomer can succeed.
Rav Aḥa bar Rava
That 'permitting' is no proof! In a bird burnt offering, the bird only becomes tereifa via the pinching — it was whole before. In a sin offering, the priests eat it, but we're talking about sacrificing a tereifa, not eating one. Your 'permitted tereifa' does not exist!
Rav Ashi
Then refute differently: common element of both — their blemish or defect is VISIBLE and KNOWN (a blemished animal shows its wound; caesarean birth is witnessed). A tereifa may have hidden internal perforations. Their visible defect is a notable distinction that blocks the inference.
Final Ruling Whether via the 'never-permitted' argument or the 'visible defect' argument, the a fortiori inference cannot stand. A Biblical verse is required לְהוֹצִיא — to exclude tereifa from the altar.

🫙 Mishna: Which Removals of the Handful Disqualify the Meal Offering?

כָּל הַמְּנָחוֹת שֶׁקָּמַץ מֵהֶן זָר אוֹ אוֹנֵן... פְּסוּלוֹת
משנה — מנחות פרק ב Both the meal offering of a sinner (מנחת חוטא) and all other meal offerings are disqualified when the handful is removed by:

• זָר — A non-priest
• אוֹנֵן — An acute mourner (relative died, not yet buried)
• טָמֵא — A ritually impure priest
• מְחוּסַר בְּגָדִים — A priest lacking priestly vestments
• מְחוּסַר כַּפָּרָה — A priest pending atonement offering
• A priest who immersed but awaits nightfall (טְבוּל יוֹם)

If removed with the left hand: disqualified.
Ben Beteira: return the handful and re-remove with the right hand.

If a stone, grain of salt, or pinch of frankincense entered the handful: disqualified, as an oversized or lacking handful is unfit.

📖 Why Does the Mishna Single Out the Meal Offering of a Sinner?

Simple Formulation

  • Mishna could say: 'All meal offerings whose handful was removed by a non-priest are disqualified'
  • The sinner's meal offering would be automatically included
  • No reason to mention it separately

Rabbi Shimon's Concern

  • Meal offering of a sinner has NO oil and NO frankincense
  • Reason: so the sinner will not profit (לא יהא חוטא נשכר)
  • Also lacks libations — so his offering is not of superior quality
  • One might think: since it is degraded, the disqualification rules don't apply to it!

Mishna's Ruling

  • Despite inferior ingredients, the sinner's meal offering follows ALL standard disqualification rules
  • The unusual phrasing 'both... and all others' explicitly prevents this mistaken inference
  • Rabbi Shimon's logic about inferiority does not affect the validity of the handful-removal laws

✋ Rav's Ruling: Returning the Handful — Ben Beteira's Extended Principle

Scenario: A non-priest removes the handful of a meal offering — standard rule disqualifies it
↓
Rav rules: The handful should be returned to the meal offering vessel (not yet permanently disqualified)
↓
Challenge: Is this not simply Ben Beteira's rule for left-hand removal? Why does Rav need to state it?
↓
Resolution: Ben Beteira's rule extends to ALL unfit persons (not only left-hand), and Rav clarifies this novel extension
↓
Critical limit: If the handful was already placed in a service vessel (sanctified in כְּלִי שָׁרֵת), the rite is complete — it cannot be returned
↓
Rav Naḥman's insight: Removal of the handful begins the rite; placement in the vessel completes it. Ben Beteira's leniency applies only before this completion
