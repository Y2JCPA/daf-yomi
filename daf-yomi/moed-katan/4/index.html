⚖️ Why Is Pre-Shemita Plowing Forbidden?

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi (implied)
The verse (Exodus 34:21) refers to the Sabbatical Year itself — prohibiting plowing that enters into Shemita and reaping that carries over from Shemita into the eighth year.
Rabbi Yishmael
The verse refers to Shabbat, not Shemita. The prohibition against plowing before Rosh HaShana of the Sabbatical Year is a halakha l'Moshe miSinai — an oral law transmitted directly from Sinai.
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
The halakha l'Moshe miSinai permits plowing around young saplings until Rosh HaShana — thereby teaching that for mature trees, plowing before Rosh HaShana is forbidden.
Rav Ashi
Rabban Gamliel's court followed Rabbi Yishmael. They nullified only the rabbinically extended prohibitions (Beit Shammai's 'from Passover,' Beit Hillel's 'from Shavuot'), but left the Sinaitic core — forbidden from Rosh HaShana — fully intact.
Bottom Line All agree: plowing is forbidden before Rosh HaShana of the Sabbatical Year. The dispute is whether the source is a Biblical verse (applied to Shemita) or a halakha l'Moshe miSinai (while the verse addresses Shabbat).

🌧️ Why Is Rainwater Forbidden? Rabbi Ile'a vs. Rav Ashi

Rabbi Ile'a (citing Rabbi Yoḥanan)
A rabbinic decree was enacted against rainwater cisterns because they resemble water drawn with a shadoof — a gezeira (precautionary fence). In reality, rainwater is different from drawn water.
Rav Ashi
No gezeira is needed — rainwater actually becomes like drawn water! Once the supply drops, one ends up drawing water with effort. The concern is about real labor, not mere appearances.
Practical Difference
Case: A stream fed by a pool that flows continuously.
Rav Ashi: Permitted — it never runs out, so no real labor concern.
Rabbi Ile'a might still prohibit if it resembles forbidden water visually — but likely concedes here.
Rabbi Zeira's Ruling (Shmuel) Streams that draw from pools of collected rainwater are permitted on Chol HaMoed, since they flow without ceasing — not comparable to drawing water by hand.

🚰 Two Reasons to Forbid Digging a New Water Channel (Chol HaMoed / Shemita)

Reason 1: Mar'it Ayin (Appearance)

  • Digging a channel looks like hoeing the field to onlookers
  • They do not know it is for water — they assume forbidden agricultural labor
  • Even if water flows in immediately after digging, it is still prohibited
  • Prohibition is due to how it appears to others
  • Ameimar taught the mishna explicitly in these terms

Reason 2: Preparing Banks for Planting

  • Digging a channel actually prepares the soil along its banks
  • The loosened banks become fit for planting — real agricultural benefit
  • If water flows in immediately, banks are not enhanced — may be permitted
  • Prohibition stems from actual agricultural improvement
  • Difficulty: a Shevi'it mishna permits piling manure without this concern

🌿 Hierarchy of Permitted Agricultural Acts on Chol HaMoed

Spring-fed irrigation — Fully permitted. Water flows freely and continuously. No excess labor, no concern it will run out.
↓
Streams fed by collected pools — Permitted (Rabbi Zeira / Shmuel), provided the stream flows without stopping and the majority of the field can be watered while it lasts (Rav Pappa).
↓
Watering vegetables to eat on the Festival — Permitted. The purpose is immediate ochel nefesh (Festival food need). Doing so purely to improve appearance or enhance future growth: forbidden.
↓
Thinning dense vegetable beds — Permitted if the thinned vegetables are eaten on the Festival itself. Not permitted if the sole purpose is to improve the remaining growth.
↓
Rainwater cisterns and shadoof — Forbidden on Chol HaMoed. Shadoof = excessive labor; rainwater = rabbinic decree or actual labor risk. The Gemara records Ravina threatening excommunication for violations witnessed on the road.
