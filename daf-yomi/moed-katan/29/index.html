⚖️ Words of Departure & Eternal Ascent

לֵךְ לְשָׁלוֹם / לֵךְ בְּשָׁלוֹם — Psalms 84:8
Core Theme Daf 29 opens with vivid metaphors for the difficulty of death, then pivots to a precise halakhic distinction: the farewell phrase for the deceased differs from that for the living. Biblical prooftexts — Absalom's tragic end vs. Moses' success — clinch the ruling. The daf closes with the ideal of ceaseless spiritual ascent: moving between synagogue and study hall, and even in the World-to-Come, the scholar never stops climbing.

🗣️ The Two Farewell Phrases

To the Deceased

  • Say: לֵךְ בְּשָׁלוֹם
  • "Go IN peace" (be-shalom)
  • ❌ Do NOT say: "Go to peace"
  • Source: Genesis 15:15 — God tells Abraham he will go to his fathers in peace
  • The deceased has arrived — peace is already present

To the Living

  • Say: לֵךְ לְשָׁלוֹם
  • "Go TO peace" (le-shalom)
  • ❌ Do NOT say: "Go in peace"
  • Source: Jethro to Moses (Exodus 4:18) → Moses succeeded
  • The living are still journeying toward peace

📜 Biblical Proof: The Consequences of Each Phrase

Which farewell is correct for the living?
Rabbi Levi bar Ḥayyata brings scriptural proof
↓

"Go IN peace"
לֵךְ בְּשָׁלוֹם
II Samuel 15:9
Absalom rebelled and was hanged
✅ Jethro → Moses
"Go TO peace"
לֵךְ לְשָׁלוֹם
Exodus 4:18
Moses went and succeeded in his mission
↓
Ruling: "Go IN peace" implies final arrival — appropriate only for the deceased. The living are sent toward peace, not declared to be within it.

⚓ How Difficult Is Death? Two Opinions

First Opinion
Death is as difficult as a knotted rope (tzippori) passing through an eye (veshet) in a ship's rigging — a tangled, bulky mass forced through the narrowest of openings.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Death is as difficult as a halyard (pitirei) passing through an eye (veshet) — a thick, stiff rope forced through a tiny opening in the rigging.
Shared Message Both sages reach the same conclusion through different images: the soul's departure from the body is a wrenching, almost impossible struggle — something large and unwieldy forced through the smallest of passages. Death is never easy.

🕍 Moving Between Sacred Spaces — Meriting the Shekhinah

Rabbi Levi's Teaching: Continuous movement between mitzvot draws the Divine Presence
↓
📍 Synagogue → Study Hall
Leaving the mitzva of tefillah (prayer) to pursue the mitzva of Torah study
↓
📍 Study Hall → Synagogue
Leaving the mitzva of Torah study to return to the mitzva of tefillah
↓
✨ Result: Merits to receive the Shekhinah (Divine Presence)
Each transition is not a departure from holiness but a deepening of it
↓
Insight: The movement itself between sacred obligations is a spiritual act — one never truly leaves holiness, only ascends within it
