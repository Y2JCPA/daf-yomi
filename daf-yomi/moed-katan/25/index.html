🪡 Who Must Rend Garments (Keriah)?

Someone dies — who is obligated to rend?
↓
Close Relative
Always obligated — Mishna rule
Torah Scholar
Everyone rends as if a close relative
Upright Person (tzaddik)
Obligated only if present at moment of death
Neither category
No obligation to rend
↓
For a Torah Scholar: the keriah obligation lasts as long as the eulogy continues — even if one was not present at death

🔥 Was Rav Safra Worthy of Keriah?

Other Sages
We did not rend our garments over Rav Safra — he never disseminated Torah publicly. He was not our teacher.
Abaye
Is it only one who teaches publicly who qualifies as a Torah Scholar?! Even one who learns privately and deeply is a great Sage deserving of keriah!
Other Sages
Fine — but what was done is done. It is too late to rend now.
Abaye
We have a rule: For a Torah Scholar, the obligation holds as long as the eulogy is ongoing. The eulogy is not yet finished — rend now!
Ruling Private Torah study — not only public teaching — is sufficient to obligate keriah upon one's death.

📦 Rav Huna's Burial — Decisions at Every Step

Proposal: Place a Torah scroll on Rav Huna's bier, as is done for great scholars.
↓
Rav Ḥisda objects: "Did he hold of this practice in his lifetime? We must not mock him by doing at death what he would not have approved alive." — Scroll not placed.
↓
Problem: The bier was too large to fit through the narrow doorway of the house.
↓
Proposal: Lower the bier from the roof. Rav Ḥisda objects: "A scholar's honor demands he exit through the door." — The doorpost was removed instead.
↓
Proposal: Transfer his body to a smaller bier to fit through the door.
↓
Rav Ḥisda objects: "A scholar must be carried out on the first bier on which he was placed." — He remained on his original bier through the widened doorway.

🎙️ Famous Eulogies for the Sages

Ravina

  • Final opening: "Date-palms, hang your heads in sadness over the righteous man likened to a date-palm."
  • Bar Kippok proposed: 'If cedars went up in flame...' — rejected by bar Avin as undignified (fire/hook imagery).
  • Bar Avin proposed: 'Cry for him who went away weeping...' — Rav Ashi was offended, as it implied others were lesser.

Rabbi Yochanan

  • Rabbi Yitzḥak ben Elazar opened: "Today is like the day the sun set at noon" (Amos 8:9).
  • Rabbi Ami mourned him as a close relative — both 7 days and 30 days.
  • Rabbi Abba challenged this practice; Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak supported Rabbi Ami.

Rabbi Zeira

  • "The land of Shinar conceived and gave birth to him" — born in Babylonia.
  • "The land of beauty raised her delightful one" — grew great in Eretz Yisrael.
  • "Woe to her, Eretz Yisrael, that she has lost her precious vessel."

תַּלְמִיד חָכָם — כָּל זְמַן שֶׁעוֹסְקִין בְּהַסְפֵּדוֹ, חַיָּבִין לִקְרֹעַ עָלָיו
⚖️ Halachic Principle: Tearing and Repairing One who tears their garment (keriah) for a Torah Scholar may tack (loosely sew) the tear once they turn their face away from the bier — but not before. This applies even during Chol HaMoed. The tear for a close relative, by contrast, must remain open for the full mourning period. Rav Ḥisda's rule: burial honors must reflect what the deceased himself would have sanctioned in life.
