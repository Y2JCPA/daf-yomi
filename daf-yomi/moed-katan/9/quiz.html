Moed Katan 9 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the opinion that one may not marry on Chol HaMoed due to 'mixing joys,' why is marrying on the eve of the Festival permitted?
A. The wedding must fully conclude before the Festival begins
B. The wedding joy comes first and transitions naturally into Festival joy rather than competing with it
C. Only Rabbinically ordained marriages require separation of joys
D. The Festival joy is considered to retroactively sanctify the wedding
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that according to the 'mixing joys' opinion, marrying on the eve is permitted because the joys flow sequentially — the wedding joy precedes and then transitions into the Festival, rather than two independent joys competing simultaneously on Chol HaMoed itself.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
From which event and verse does the Talmud derive the principle that one may not mix one joy with another?
A. Moses receiving the Torah — Shavuot coincided with the Golden Calf
B. The Tabernacle dedication — Moses held two separate ceremonies
C. Solomon's Temple dedication — he held 7 + 7 = 14 days, keeping Temple dedication and Sukkot separate
D. The return from Babylonian exile — Ezra separated the rededication from Rosh Hashana
Correct: C — The source is Solomon's Temple dedication (I Kings 8:65): 'Seven days and seven days — fourteen days.' Solomon held two distinct 7-day celebrations rather than combining the Temple dedication with Sukkot, demonstrating that one must not mix two joys.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What ultimately justified the Jewish people eating on Yom Kippur during the year of Solomon's Temple dedication?
A. The Sanhedrin issued an emergency ruling suspending the fast
B. A kal v'chomer from the Tabernacle dedication overriding Shabbat, confirmed by a Divine Voice declaring all destined for World-to-Come
C. Solomon himself received a prophecy permitting it
D. The verse 'fourteen days' was interpreted to explicitly include Yom Kippur
Correct: B — The people reasoned: if the lesser Tabernacle dedication could override Shabbat (proven from the tribal princes' offerings on consecutive days including Shabbat), surely the greater Temple dedication could override Yom Kippur. Though this kal v'chomer could be refuted, a Divine Voice confirmed God's pardon by declaring all Israel destined for the World-to-Come.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
When Rabbi Yonatan ben Asmai said 'May your house be destroyed and your lodging place be inhabited,' what was the true meaning according to Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai?
A. That his home would be renovated while he temporarily relocated
B. That he should become a wandering scholar spreading Torah
C. That his eternal home (World-to-Come) should be 'built' while he lives long in this temporary world
D. That he should merit to rebuild the Temple during his lifetime
Correct: C — Rabbi Shimon decoded the blessing: 'house' = the World-to-Come (one's permanent dwelling) — may it be established for you. 'Lodging place' = this world (a temporary inn) — may you inhabit it long. The apparent curse was actually a wish for both long life in this world and a share in the World-to-Come.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Yehuda forbid a woman from applying lime (sid) on Chol HaMoed, and what is the Rabbinic counter-position?
A. Rabbi Yehuda forbids it as excessive labor; Rabbis permit minimal amounts
B. Rabbi Yehuda forbids it as immodest display; Rabbis permit it in private
C. Rabbi Yehuda forbids it because it temporarily disfigures her causing Festival distress; Rabbis permit it because eventual joy outweighs current distress
D. Rabbi Yehuda forbids it only for unmarried women; Rabbis permit it for married women
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehuda holds that lime renders a woman temporarily ugly and disfigured, causing her distress during the Festival itself — which is prohibited. The Rabbis apply the principle that distress now leading to joy later is permitted. The Gemara works to show Rabbi Yehuda is consistent by distinguishing the gentile debt-repayment case (where distress is permanent) from cosmetic lime (where joy follows).
