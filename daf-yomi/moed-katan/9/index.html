🤔 Why No Marriage on Chol HaMoed? Two Opinions Reconciled

Opinion A
No marriage on Chol HaMoed because of joy — one must not mix one joy (wedding) with another joy (Festival). The celebrations are each diminished.
Reconciliation A
Marrying on the eve is permitted: the wedding joy precedes the Festival. The joys flow sequentially rather than competing on the Festival itself.
Opinion B
No marriage on Chol HaMoed because of excessive exertion — wedding preparations are exhausting labor that violates the Festival spirit.
Reconciliation B
Marrying on the eve is permitted: the primary exertion is only one day before the Festival. Afterward, excessive celebrations beyond the norm are not obligatory.
Bottom Line Both opinions agree: marrying on the eve of a Festival is permitted even though the 7-day celebration continues into Chol HaMoed

📖 Deriving 'Don't Mix Two Joys' — Unpacking the Proof

Question: What is the Torah source for not mixing one joy with another?
↓
Written re: Temple dedication (I Kings 8:65):
'Solomon held the feast… seven days and seven days — fourteen days'
He did NOT combine the Temple dedication with Sukkot
↓
❌ Rejected
Maybe Solomon just didn't delay a planned date — but if a wedding accidentally falls on a Festival, mixing would be OK?
✅ Counter
A mitzva must be completed without delay — he couldn't leave any Temple construction undone. The 14-day separation was intentional and normative.
↓
✅ Proven: Solomon deliberately separated the two celebrations, establishing the principle that sacred joys must each be honored fully and distinctly

🙏 The Cryptic Blessings of Rabbi Yonatan ben Asmai

מועד קטן ט׳ עמוד ב׳ — ברכות בלשון נסתר
Story: Veiled Benedictions Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai sent his son to receive blessings from his disciples, Rabbi Yonatan ben Asmai and Rabbi Yehuda son of converts. They said: "May your house be destroyed, your lodging place inhabited, your table confused, and may you not see a new year." The son returned distressed — these sounded like curses. His father decoded each blessing:
• 'House destroyed' = may your true house (World-to-Come) be built; this world is only a lodging • 'Lodging inhabited' = may you live long in this temporary world • 'Table confused' = may you have many children filling your table with noise and life • 'Not see a new year' = may your wife never die so you never need to remarry
What appeared to be curses were among the deepest blessings a sage could offer.

💄 Women's Cosmetics on Chol HaMoed: Permissions and Debates

✅ Permitted (All Opinions)

  • Painting eyelids with kohl
  • Applying face paint and rouge
  • General adornment and beautification
  • All ages permitted — even elderly women
  • Popular adage: 'A woman of sixty, like a girl of six, runs after the sound of the drum'
  • Rav Ḥisda: Even a woman standing at the edge of her grave may adorn herself

⚖️ Disputed: Lime (Sid) Application

  • Rabbi Yehuda FORBIDS: Lime temporarily disfigures — she suffers distress during the Festival itself
  • Rabbis PERMIT: Current distress leading to future joy is acceptable (she will be beautiful afterward)
  • Tension: Rabbi Yehuda seems inconsistent — he forbids distress-now/joy-later here but elsewhere too
  • Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak: Festival labors are unique category — all permitted on basis of eventual joy
  • Ravina: Rabbi Yehuda only forbids when gentile is always distressed (debt repayment), not here

🌿 Shemen HaMor — Special Case

  • Used by girls who reached physical maturity but not legal majority (under 12)
  • Purpose: remove unwanted hair; girls embarrassed by premature growth
  • Identified as: setaket perfume (Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya) OR unripe olive oil — acidic, acts as depilatory (Rav Yirmeya bar Ami)
  • Rav Beivai spread lime on his daughter limb by limb — she became so beautiful he received 400 dinars as her dowry!
