⚔️ Does the Festival Count Toward the 30-Day Period?

Abaye
The baraita — 'A Festival counts toward the 30 days' — refers only to burial before the Festival. If burial was during the Festival, it cannot count.
Rava
No — that baraita refers precisely to burial during the Festival. The Festival itself counts toward sheloshim even when shiva never formally began.
Ravin (citing Rabbi Yoḥanan)
Even if one buried his dead during the Festival, the Festival counts toward the thirty-day period of mourning — shiva is entirely suspended and subsumed.
Rabbi Elazar
Rabbi Elazar ruled similarly for Rabbi Pedat: burial during the Festival still allows the Festival days to count toward sheloshim.
Ruling The Festival counts toward the 30-day period even when burial occurred during the Festival itself — shiva is entirely cancelled and the Festival days are credited.

🛏️ Overturning the Bed Before a Festival — The Dispute

Rabbi Eliezer / Beit Shammai

  • If one overturned the bed for 3 days before the Festival
  • He is exempt — no need to resume after the Festival
  • Rav Huna citing Rabbi Yoḥanan: even one day or one hour before the Festival is sufficient
  • Ravina confirmed this ruling at Sura-on-Euphrates: "Even one day, even one hour"

Rabbis / Beit Hillel

  • Even 3 days of overturning before the Festival is insufficient
  • Must resume overturning the bed after the Festival ends
  • The Festival's interruption nullifies the prior observance
  • Full resumption of mourning rites is required when the Festival concludes

👴 Rabbi Ḥiyya Receives Distant Tidings of His Siblings

Rav (nephew of Rabbi Ḥiyya) comes to visit him in Babylonia
↓
Rabbi Ḥiyya asks: "Is your father Aivu still alive?" — Rav stays silent (Aivu, his paternal half-brother, had died)
↓
Rabbi Ḥiyya asks: "Is your mother [my sister] still alive?" — Rav again stays silent (she too had died)
↓
Rabbi Ḥiyya removes his shoes — performing the mourning rite of shoe-removal for distant tidings of two siblings
↓
Rabbi Ḥiyya then enters the bath-house — showing he observed only one day, not seven days of mourning
↓
Three halakhot derived:
(1) A mourner is forbidden to wear shoes
(2) Distant tidings require only one day of mourning
(3) A partial day counts as a full day

👨‍👩‍👧 Must a Husband Mourn for His Wife's Relatives?

Rabbi Akiva
One mourns for his own close relatives only — not for his relatives' relatives. There is no Torah obligation to mourn second-degree kin.
Rabbis
One mourns for his relatives AND for their close relatives — second-degree kin are included, but only privately in his home, not publicly.
Rav Huna (to Mar Ukva)
When your brother-in-law died, you erred. You should have mourned with your wife, overturning the bed and observing the rites of mourning in your home.
Baraita
When his father-in-law or mother-in-law dies, a husband may not force his wife to apply eye-paint or rouge. He must overturn the bed and mourn with her.
Practical Ruling A husband must overturn the bed and observe mourning rites alongside his wife when her parents die — this is not an independent full mourning obligation but a shared expression of solidarity.
