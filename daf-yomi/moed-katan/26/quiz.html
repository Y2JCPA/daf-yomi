QUESTION 1 OF 5
From which biblical episode does the Gemara derive the obligation to rend for one's Torah teacher, and that such a rent may never be properly mended?
A. David tearing his clothes upon hearing of Saul's death (II Samuel 1)
B. Elisha tearing his clothes when Elijah ascended in the whirlwind (II Kings 2:12)
C. Hezekiah tearing his clothes upon hearing Rab-shakeh's blasphemy (II Kings 19)
D. Jeremiah weeping over the burning of his scroll by Jehoiakim (Jeremiah 36)
Correct: B — Elisha cried 'My father, my father!' — which Rav Yosef translates as 'my teacher, my teacher.' The phrase 'he rent them in two pieces' teaches the rent may never be mended. Reish Lakish's objection that Elijah was still alive is answered: since Elisha would 'see him no more,' Elijah was considered dead to him.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why did Shmuel NOT rend his garments when informed that King Shapur killed 12,000 Jews in Mezigat Caesarea?
A. He doubted the reliability of the report
B. He held keriah for evil tidings applies only in the Land of Israel
C. Evil tidings require keriah only when the majority of Israel is affected
D. He had already torn his garments for a related tragedy earlier that year
Correct: C — The Gemara rules that keriah for evil tidings (שמועות רעות) is required only when the majority of Israel is affected — as derived from David's tearing for 'the people of the Lord.' 12,000, though tragic, was not the majority of Israel.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira — whose ruling Shmuel declares to be the halakha — what is the rule when one simultaneously receives news of a parent's death and another relative's death?
A. One rent covers all deaths; the parent's loss is included
B. Separate rents must be made for every individual relative
C. One rent covers all other relatives, but a separate dedicated rent is made for the parent
D. The parent's death alone requires keriah; others are waived when simultaneous
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira rules that while a single rent may cover all other relatives simultaneously, a parent requires its own separate rent. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak explains: one has only one father and one mother — there is no 'other loss' to add theirs onto; they stand alone.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What does Rabbi Ḥelbo cite in the name of Rav Huna regarding seeing a Torah scroll that has been torn (not burned)?
A. One rent is made for the scroll as a whole unit
B. Two rents are made — one for the parchment, one for the writing
C. Keriah is only required if the majority of the scroll is destroyed
D. One rends and the rent may subsequently be mended, unlike a burned scroll
Correct: B — Rabbi Ḥelbo in the name of Rav Huna: seeing a torn Torah scroll obligates two rents — one for the damaged parchment and one for the sacred writing. The source is Jeremiah 36:27–28, which distinguishes between the physical scroll and the divine words inscribed upon it.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
When a person arrives at the ruins and encounters the Temple Mount and Jerusalem's destruction together, what is the correct procedure according to the baraita?
A. Rend for Jerusalem first, then extend the same rent for the Temple
B. Make two entirely separate, non-extended rents simultaneously
C. Rend for the Temple first, then extend that same rent downward for Jerusalem
D. A single rent suffices for both since both losses occurred in the same catastrophe
Correct: C — The baraita rules: rend first upon seeing the Temple's ruins, then extend the same rent for Jerusalem. This applies when one encounters the Temple area first. If one sees Jerusalem's ruins before reaching the Temple Mount, separate rents are required for each. The extension method avoids requiring an entirely new garment for each sacred site.
