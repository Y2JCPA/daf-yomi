✂️ Occasions Requiring Keriah (קְרִיעָה)

Never Properly Mended

  • Death of father or mother
  • Death of one's primary Torah teacher (רַב מֻבְהָק)
  • Death of the Nasi (prince/communal president)
  • Death of the Av Beit Din (court president)
  • Hearing God's name blasphemed (Chillul Hashem)
  • Witnessing a Torah scroll being burned

May Be Mended After Mourning

  • Death of other close relatives (son, brother, sister)
  • Extensions of existing rents for additional relatives
  • Borrowed garment torn in mourning (Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel)
  • Seeing ruins of cities of Judea or Jerusalem
  • Rents made on behalf of a minor child to arouse grief

⚔️ Elijah Is Still Alive — Was Elisha's Keriah Valid?

Reish Lakish
But isn't Elijah still alive?! Keriah is for the dead — why did Elisha rend his garments when Elijah merely ascended to heaven in the whirlwind?
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Since it is written 'and he saw him no more' (II Kings 2:12), Elijah was considered as dead from Elisha's perspective — he was permanently inaccessible to him.
Rav Yosef
The verse 'My father, my father!' (II Kings 2:12) means: my teacher, my teacher — who was better for Israel's protection with his prayers than chariots and horsemen. This is our source for keriah upon a teacher's death.
Halakhic Takeaway From Elisha rending for Elijah (II Kings 2:12) we derive: one must rend for father, mother, and one's primary Torah teacher — and the phrase 'he rent them in two pieces' teaches the rent may never be properly mended.

📣 Evil Tidings (שְׁמוּעוֹת רָעוֹת) — When Must One Rend?

Terrible news is received
e.g., defeat in battle, mass killing of Jews
↓
Majority of Israel affected
Obligated to rend
Source: David rent his clothes for Saul, Jonathan, and 'the people of the Lord' (II Sam. 1:11–12)
Minority of Israel affected
No obligation
Shmuel did NOT rend when 12,000 Jews were killed at Mezigat Caesarea — not the majority of Israel
↓
Note: King Shapur claimed he never killed Jews — that episode involved his soldiers acting without orders

🏛️ Rending Upon Seeing Sacred Places in Ruin

Sees cities of Judea in ruin — recites: "Your sacred cities are become a wilderness" (Isaiah 64:9) → rends garment
↓
Sees Jerusalem in its desolation — recites: "Zion is become a wilderness, Jerusalem a desolation" (Isaiah 64:9) → rends garment
↓
Sees the Temple Mount in ruin — recites: "Our holy Temple... is burned with fire, and all our pleasant things are laid waste" (Isaiah 64:10) → rends
↓
Correct order when arriving together: rend first for the Temple, then extend the same rent for Jerusalem. If Jerusalem is seen first — make a separate rent for each.
↓
Each viewing obligates keriah — even if one has seen the ruins before, each new encounter requires rending

⚖️ Multiple Bereavements — Extending the Same Rent

Son Dies First → Then Father

  • Initial rent (for son): in lower portion of garment
  • When father dies: extend the rent upward
  • Upper extension = father's rent (never mendable)
  • Lower original = son's rent (mendable after 30 days)
  • The unmendable portion travels with the parent's loss

Father Dies First → Then Son

  • Initial rent (for father): in upper portion of garment
  • When son dies: extend the rent downward
  • Upper original = father's rent (never mendable)
  • Lower extension = son's rent (mendable after mourning)
  • Father's portion remains permanently torn

Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira's Rule (Halakha — Shmuel)

  • All other relatives together: one rent suffices
  • Father or mother: requires its own dedicated rent
  • Reason (Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak): one has only one father and one mother — there is nothing to 'add' to
  • Even in a lenient-vs-strict dispute, Shmuel rules strictly here
