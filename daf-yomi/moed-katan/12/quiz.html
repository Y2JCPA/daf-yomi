QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Pappa, when is it prohibited for gentile contractors to work outside the Shabbat limit on Chol HaMoed?
A. Always — outside the limit is never a lenient zone
B. When another city is in close proximity to the work site
C. When the Jew personally supervises the work
D. When the work involves constructing a permanent building
Correct: B — Rav Pappa teaches that even outside the Shabbat limit, proximity to another city makes it prohibited — passersby from that city will see the work and assume the Jew commissioned it on Shabbat or the Festival.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
On what basis did Rabbi Yehuda permit breeding a she-donkey on Chol HaMoed, against the general prohibition?
A. A she-donkey is not considered a domestic animal for these laws
B. The owner needs the animal for Festival travel and must ensure its health
C. Lest her estrus end and she lose her chance to conceive at that cycle
D. Because donkey-breeding is never considered a significant labor
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehuda permits it specifically because if the opportunity is missed and she 'cools off,' she will not be receptive again until her next cycle — a real financial loss that overrides the general prohibition.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Abaye's traditional principle about the structure of Chol HaMoed halakhot?
A. They are identical to Shabbat in every respect
B. They are like Samaritan law — entirely unpredictable, case by case
C. They are like Shabbat: some Torah-exempt acts are Rabbinically prohibited, some are permitted outright
D. They are more lenient than a regular weekday in all matters of loss prevention
Correct: C — Abaye cites a tradition that Chol HaMoed mirrors Shabbat's two-tier structure: some acts that are exempt by Torah law are nevertheless Rabbinically forbidden on Chol HaMoed, while others are permitted from the outset.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why did the Gemara justify Rav Huna's harvesting of his crop (attached to the ground) on Chol HaMoed?
A. He used a shinui (altered method) so it was categorically different labor
B. He relied on the lenient individual baraita that permits attached crops
C. He falls in the category of 'nothing to eat' — dire need permits the labor
D. His gentile workers acted independently without his instruction
Correct: C — The Gemara explains that Rav Huna is in the category of ein lo ma yochal — one who has nothing to eat — which constitutes extreme need and justifies even the otherwise prohibited harvesting of crops still attached to the ground.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rav Yosef moved wooden beams during the day on Chol HaMoed. How did he justify this as acting 'discreetly'?
A. He moved them through a back alley rather than the main street
B. He hired gentile workers so it did not appear as his own labor
C. For heavy beams, daytime is actually discreet — fewer people help at night, making nighttime more conspicuous
D. He broke the move into small stages spread over several days
Correct: C — Rav Yosef argued that context determines what counts as discreet. For large, heavy beams that require many helpers, moving them at night would attract more attention and helpers. Moving them in daylight is therefore the genuinely discreet option.
