QUESTION 1 OF 5
What does the term בֵּית הַשְּׁלָחִין (beit hashelahin) denote, and how does the Gemara derive its meaning?
A. A field watered by rain; derived from a verse about rainfall in Deuteronomy
B. A field requiring supplemental irrigation; the Gemara derives 'thirst' from a verse in Isaiah about parched land
C. A field planted with multiple species; derived from the laws of diverse kinds
D. A field near a public cistern; derived from a verse about communal water storage
Correct: B — The Gemara derives that 'beit hashelahin' connotes thirst from the verse in Isaiah (35:1): 'The wilderness and the parched land [tziyah] shall be glad.' A thirsty field requires irrigation — without it, the crop will be lost.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to the baraita cited by Rav Pappa, what does Rabbi Yehuda permit from a newly emerged spring on Chol HaMoed?
A. Only irrigating beit hashelahin, same as an old spring
B. Constructing entirely new water channels for the public
C. Irrigating even a beit haba'al (a field that ordinarily suffices with rainwater)
D. Watering crops on Yom Tov itself in addition to Chol HaMoed
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehuda is more lenient than the anonymous mishna: since a new spring's walls have not yet stabilized and it may collapse and be lost, one may use all its water — even to irrigate a beit haba'al that does not normally need supplemental watering.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Rabba and Rav Yosef disagree about which Shabbat prohibition one who weeds or waters seedlings violates. What is Rav Yosef's reasoning?
A. It resembles plowing because both loosen the earth
B. It resembles reaping because both remove unwanted material from the ground
C. It resembles sowing because both promote plant growth
D. It resembles threshing because both separate the useful from the unwanted
Correct: C — Rav Yosef reasons that just as sowing promotes the growth of planted crops, so too weeding (removing competition) and watering (nourishing the plant) both achieve the objective of enhancing plant growth. Therefore the relevant prohibition is sowing.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Abaye explain why the mishna permits irrigating beit hashelahin during the Sabbatical Year?
A. Irrigation is not considered agricultural labor in any rabbinic ruling
B. The field owner will suffer a financial loss, which always overrides all Shemitah restrictions
C. The mishna refers to Shemitah in the present era, when it is only a Rabbinic decree — per Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
D. The spring emerged on the Festival itself, triggering a separate leniency that overrides Shemitah
Correct: C — Abaye explains that the mishna follows Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, who teaches that in the current era (with Yovel inactive), Shemitah observance is mandated only by Rabbinic decree. Since the restriction is Rabbinic rather than Torah-level, the Sages permitted irrigation to prevent significant crop loss.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does the Gemara reject Rav Huna's identification of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov as the anonymous tanna of the mishna?
A. Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov lived a generation after the mishna was compiled
B. Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov explicitly permits all irrigation on Chol HaMoed in a separate ruling
C. His ruling addresses only extra profit-seeking labor — we cannot infer he would also restrict labor performed to prevent a considerable loss
D. The Gemara found he contradicts himself regarding beit haba'al in a different tractate
Correct: C — Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov's ruling (don't irrigate the whole field via channels) only restricts labor done to gain extra profit beyond what is needed. The Gemara points out that we cannot assume he would equally restrict labor in cases of considerable financial loss — a fundamentally different and more pressing situation.
