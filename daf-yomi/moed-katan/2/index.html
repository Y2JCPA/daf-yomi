🌾 Two Field Types: The Core Distinction

בֵּית הַשְּׁלָחִין Beit HaShelahin

  • Literally: field of thirst
  • Cannot survive on rainwater alone
  • Supplemental irrigation is essential
  • Without water → imminent crop loss
  • ✓ May irrigate on Chol HaMoed
  • ✓ May irrigate during Shemitah
  • Source: "The wilderness and the parched land" (Isa. 35:1)

בֵּית הַבַּעַל Beit HaBa'al

  • Literally: field of settlement
  • Sustained by rainwater alone
  • Irrigation is supplemental — not needed
  • Extra watering = profit, not loss prevention
  • ✗ May NOT irrigate on Chol HaMoed
  • Exception: new spring (Rabbi Yehuda)
  • Source: "As a young man takes a maiden" (Isa. 62:5)

🚜 Weeding/Watering Seedlings on Shabbat — Which Av Melachah?

Rabba
One who weeds or waters seedlings on Shabbat is forewarned for plowing (חֲרִישָׁה). Just as plowing loosens the earth to prepare it for growth, so too weeding and watering loosen the soil.
Rav Yosef
One is forewarned for sowing (זְרִיעָה). Just as sowing promotes plant growth, weeding removes competitors and watering nourishes — both promote growth of the existing plant.
Abaye
Both positions are difficult! Rabba: can the same act trigger liability for both plowing AND sowing? Rav Yosef: Rav Kahana taught that pruning on Shabbat incurs liability for both planting and reaping simultaneously — so your distinction fails too.
Rav Yosef
A baraita supports me: one who weeds among diverse kinds (כִּלְאַיִם) is flogged. This only makes sense if weeding = sowing — for sowing diverse kinds is Torah-forbidden. Plowing diverse kinds carries no such prohibition.
Rabba
That baraita needs no appeal to plowing at all — one who weeds diverse kinds is flogged for the independent prohibition of maintaining a mixture (מְקַיֵּם כִּלְאַיִם), not for plowing.
תֵּיקוּ — Unresolved The Gemara concludes: the question is difficult according to both opinions. The Shabbat category for weeding/watering is left undecided — a rare teku in an early sugya.

👨‍⚖️ Quest for the Anonymous Tanna of the Mishna

The Question: The mishna permits irrigating beit hashelahin but not beit haba'al. Which named tanna holds exactly this position?
↓
Rav Huna: It is Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov — who rules one may channel water tree-to-tree but not irrigate an entire field (limits labor to preventing loss, not gaining profit).
↓
Gemara rejects Rav Huna: Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov only addressed extra profit. Did he also rule about cases of significant financial loss? We cannot assume so — the two cases are fundamentally different!
↓
Rav Pappa: The mishna follows Rabbi Yehuda, whose baraita teaches: new spring → even beit haba'al permitted; old spring → only beit hashelahin.
↓
Gemara challenges Rav Pappa: Maybe Rabbi Yehuda's leniency applies only to a new spring (fear of collapse) — not to old springs. In that case, the mishna's ruling for old springs still has no match!
↓
Resolution (Amud B): If the mishna doesn't match Rabbi Yehuda, it matches no tanna at all. Therefore it must follow Rabbi Yehuda — the interpretation that aligns best with the overall ruling.

📜 Shemitah Today — Why Does the Mishna Permit Irrigation? The problem: If Shemitah is a Torah prohibition, how can the mishna simply permit irrigating a field? Abaye's answer: The mishna discusses Shemitah in the present era — when it is only a Rabbinic decree. This follows the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's reasoning: The Torah juxtaposes release of the land (Shemitah) with release of loans (Shemitat Kesafim). Just as monetary release applies only when Yovel is active, so too land release is Torah-mandated only when Yovel is in force. Today without Yovel → Shemitah = Rabbinic → leniencies apply. Rava's alternative: Even if you say Shemitah is Torah-level today (like the Rabbis who disagree with R. Yehuda HaNasi), the mishna may still be correct — because irrigation of beit hashelahin is not one of the primary forbidden labors (אָבוֹת מְלָאכוֹת) of Shemitah explicitly prohibited by the Torah.
