QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, what defines 'temporary stitches' (makhliv) for a craftsman sewing on Chol HaMoed?
A. He sews with a different, thinner thread than usual
B. He sews every other stitch, not completing the full seam
C. He uses a larger needle so the stitching is easily removable
D. He sews only along one side of the garment, not both
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan defines makhliv as skipping stitches — the craftsman does not complete all the stitches required, leaving the seam deliberately unfinished and therefore temporary.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the final accepted interpretation of the bed-cord dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosei, per Ravin's version?
A. Rabbi Meir permits tying lengthwise only; Rabbi Yosei forbids all tying
B. Both agree on full weaving being permitted; they dispute whether loose cords may be tightened
C. Both agree that interweaving means full weaving; they dispute whether brand-new cords may be braided
D. Rabbi Meir permits tightening; Rabbi Yosei permits full interweaving
Correct: C — Ravin's revised tradition: everyone agrees interweaving means tying lengthwise and widthwise. The sole dispute is about braiding entirely new cords — Rabbi Meir permits it, Rabbi Yosei forbids it.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rava's ruling identify whether a person clearing his field intends to thresh or to plant?
A. By asking him to state his intention under oath before he begins
B. By whether he performs the work before or after midday
C. By where the earth goes: mound onto mound suggests threshing; furrow into furrow suggests planting
D. By whether a rabbi is present to attest his purpose
Correct: C — Rava provides an objective test: if earth is cast from one mound onto another, the intent is threshing (permitted); if moved between furrows, the intent is agricultural planting (prohibited). Physical evidence reveals purpose.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does Rav Ḥama prohibit trimming the hooves of a donkey that turns millstones on Chol HaMoed?
A. Hoof-trimming is a veterinary craft restricted to professionals
B. One may only grind what is needed for the Festival, so the mill-donkey beyond that quantity is unnecessary
C. The donkey will be in pain and unable to work, violating animal welfare laws
D. It constitutes completing a new utensil, which is rabbinic construction labor
Correct: B — Since one may only grind grain actually needed for the Festival on Chol HaMoed, excessive milling — and thus the mill-donkey's enhanced performance — is unnecessary. Trimming hooves for that purpose therefore lacks Festival justification.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What halakhic principle allows Ravina to collect debts in Shanvata during Chol HaMoed?
A. Debt collection involves no physical labor and was never prohibited
B. Rav Ashi issued a blanket regional dispensation for financial transactions
C. The debtors consented in advance to being approached during the Festival
D. Since the debtors gather only for the Festival market and may be unreachable afterward, failing to collect now would cause significant loss
Correct: D — Rabbi Yosei bar Avin's principle — that a matter which will result in significant loss if left unattended is permitted — applies here. The debtors' unique availability during the Festival market means that missing this opportunity constitutes a genuine irreversible loss.
