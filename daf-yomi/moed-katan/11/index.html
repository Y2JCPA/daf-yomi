⚒️ Permitted vs. Prohibited Work on Chol HaMoed

✅ Permitted (Layman's Work)

  • Simple traps (oharei) — Rav permitted Ḥiyya bar Ashi
  • Constructing ovens — Rav Yehuda permitted Ami the oven maker
  • Crafting sieves — Rav Yehuda permitted Rabba bar Ashabi
  • Railing built from palm and bay branches (not permanent)
  • Plastering oven cracks with hand or foot (not a presser)

❌ Prohibited (Craftsman's Work)

  • Complex nets (izlei) — professional weaving
  • Plastering with a presser — too skilled and permanent
  • Smithing repairs — loud, professional metalwork
  • Permanent, skillfully built railings
  • Any expert labor without a Festival need or loss

🐟 The Bedita River Fish Debate

Narrator
The Bedita River overflowed during Chol HaMoed, depositing vast quantities of fish. Rava permitted the townspeople to trap, bring home, and salt them.
Abaye
The mishna says: preserved food may only be prepared on Chol HaMoed if one can eat it on the Festival itself. You cannot possibly eat that massive quantity of fish during the Festival!
Rava
These freshly salted fish can be eaten right now — by pressing out the salt and brine. Shmuel himself was once served recently salted fish prepared by pressing. They qualify as Festival food.
Abaye
But the sheer volume makes it impossible to eat it all during the Festival. The obvious purpose is preservation for after the Festival — which the mishna prohibits!
Rava's Position Since pressing makes salted fish immediately edible, salting qualifies as preparing food for the Festival — even if much will inevitably remain afterward.

😢 What Others May Do for a Mourner

Triggering scenario: A mourner had already turned over his olives (הפך זיתיו) in preparation for pressing when bereavement struck
↓
Default rule: A mourner is prohibited from performing labor during the days of mourning
↓
Permitted: Others may press the olives and plug the barrels on his behalf — financial loss overrides the restriction
↓
Rabbi Yehuda adds: Others may sow a plowed field or flax field for him if delayed sowing would cause a loss
↓
Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: If the mourner is the only skilled worker who can do it properly, he himself may press the olives and plug the barrels
↓
Public craftsmen (barbers, bath attendants): if Festival eve arrives while in mourning, they may resume serving the public
↓
Location rule: Work commissioned by the mourner may be done in another's house — but not within the mourner's own home

🐟 Wisdom from Adda the Fisherman

אמר רב אמר לי עדא צייד: כוורא — כי כוורא שריוהו במיא, וכי אחוה מלחוה
Rav reports: Adda the Fisherman (עדא צייד) told me… On quality: A fish that has sat for some time and is close to spoiling is at its best.

On broiling: Broil a fish with its 'brother' — salt (which, like fish, comes from water).

On boiling: Boil a fish in its 'father' — water itself.

On digestion: After eating fish, cress, and milk — let the body carry them, not the bed. Walk; do not immediately lie down.

Context: Rav once visited Rav Shapir's home and was served a fish that was ⅓ cooked, ⅓ salted and pressed, and ⅓ broiled — so exquisite he said he had not tasted its like since his teacher's table.
