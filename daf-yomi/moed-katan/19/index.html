⚖️ Does Part of the Day Count as a Full Day? (מקצת היום ככולו)

Abba Shaul
מקצת היום ככולו — Part of a day is like a full day. Therefore seven days of mourning before a Festival suffice to cancel the shloshim. The seventh day (even partially observed) counts as complete.
Rabbis
A full eighth day is required before the Festival for shloshim to be nullified. We do not apply 'part of a day' to this calculation.
Abaye
The halakha follows Abba Shaul for the seventh day of shiva, and the Rabbis themselves concede to Abba Shaul regarding the thirtieth day of shloshim.
Rava
The halakha follows Abba Shaul only for the thirtieth day — not for the seventh day of shiva.
Sages of Nehardea
The halakha follows Abba Shaul for both the seventh day and the thirtieth day.
Practical Result Once comforters rise to leave on the seventh day of mourning, the mourner may immediately bathe — even before the full day ends (Rav Amram citing Rav, per Abba Shaul).

🕍 How Shabbat vs. Festival Interact with Mourning

Shabbat

  • Counts as one of the seven days of shiva
  • Does NOT interrupt or nullify mourning
  • Public mourning prohibited on Shabbat
  • Private mourning (אבלות שבלב) continues
  • Does not cancel shloshim under any condition

Pilgrimage Festival (יום טוב)

  • Interrupts AND nullifies the mourning decree
  • Does NOT count toward mourning days
  • Cancels shiva if 3+ days observed prior
  • Cancels shloshim if 7–8 days observed prior
  • Counts toward shloshim for late-buried (debated)

Shavuot / Rosh HaShana / Yom Kippur

  • R. Eliezer: Shavuot today = Shabbat (no extra days follow)
  • Rabban Gamliel: Rosh HaShana & Yom Kippur = Festival
  • Rabbis: Neither like Shabbat nor full Festival
  • Post-Temple, Shavuot lacks the extra offering days
  • Dispute applies to whether these interrupt shloshim

💈 Deriving the 30-Day Mourning Period (שלושים)

The Question: From where do we derive that mourning lasts thirty days?
↓
Gezerah Shava (גזירה שווה): Verbal analogy between pera (פרע — disheveled hair) in mourning (Leviticus 10:6) and pera in nazirite law (Numbers 6:5)
↓
Nazirite = 30 Days: An unspecified naziriteship lasts thirty days (Rav Mattana)
↓
Source for Nazirite's 30 Days: Derived from the word יִהְיֶה (yihyeh — 'he shall be'), whose numeric value alludes to thirty
↓
Conclusion: Just as the nazirite lets his hair grow for thirty days, so too the mourner is restricted from hair-cutting for thirty days — establishing the shloshim period
↓
Rav Huna b. Rav Yehoshua adds: Even Abba Shaul agrees that on the third day of mourning — the eve of a Festival — bathing is still prohibited until nightfall, since 'part of a day' does not relax all restrictions simultaneously

🧵 Writing Tefillin, Mezuzot & Spinning Tzitzit on Chol HaMoed

Baraita
One may write tefillin and mezuzot for himself on Chol HaMoed. For others — only as a favor (בחינם), never for payment.
Rav / Rabba bar bar Ḥana
The practical halakha: one may write and sell tefillin and mezuzot in the usual manner, up to an amount needed for his livelihood (פרנסתו).
Mishna
One may spin sky-blue wool (תכלת) for tzitzit on one's thigh — but not in the ordinary way with a spindle. An altered manner is required on Chol HaMoed.
Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel; R. Ḥiyya bar Abba citing R. Yoḥanan
The halakha is that one may spin sky-blue wool even with a stone or a spindle on Chol HaMoed, because of the great need for the mitzva (משום דצורך מצוה).
Key Principle Melakha on Chol HaMoed is permitted for a mitzva need or for livelihood — ideally in an altered manner, but the mitzva purpose may override even that requirement.
