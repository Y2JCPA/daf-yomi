🌞 How Do We Derive Daytime-Only Leprosy Examination?

Abaye
The word 'but' (אַךְ) in 'but on the day it appears' is a limiting term — sometimes you examine, sometimes you do not. This teaches delay is permitted on Festivals. Then the word 'day' (יוֹם) separately teaches: examination is by day only, not night.
Rava
The entire phrase 'but on the day' is superfluous — the Torah could have written simply 'when it appears.' The extra words teach that delay is sometimes permitted. For daytime-only, I derive from 'as far as the priest's eyes can see' (Lev. 13:12).
Abaye
If I used Rava's source for daytime-only, I might think it applies only to house leprosy (where the owner says 'it seems to me'). My derivation from 'on the day' applies universally to all leprosy.
Rava
The verse 'as far as the priest's eyes can see' also disqualifies a priest blind in one eye. I use the homeowner's verse — 'there seems to me as it were a plague' — as an additional proof for daytime-only inspection.
Shared Halakhic Conclusion Both Abaye and Rava agree: leprosy examination is (1) postponed during Festivals and (2) valid only during daylight hours. They differ only in which words of scripture teach each rule.

🏚️ House Leprosy — A Torah Novelty (Chiddush) Wood and stones are normally not susceptible to ritual impurity — yet the Torah creates a special exception: tzaraat habayit (house leprosy) renders even inanimate building materials impure. This is classified as a chiddush requiring its own scriptural source.

Two separate verses are needed:
Verse 1 — 'But on the day it appears in him' (Lev. 13:14): teaches that a priest may delay examination to save the homeowner's utensils from impurity.
Verse 2 — 'Then the priest shall command that they empty the house' (Lev. 14:36): teaches that examination is postponed during a Festival, so the homeowner's utensils are not declared impure and ruined.

Neither verse alone would teach both leniencies — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds both are indispensable.

🦴 Gathering the Bones of One's Parents on Chol HaMoed

Rabbi Meir — Permits

  • May gather and reinter parents' bones during Chol HaMoed
  • The joy of the pilgrimage Festival is upon him and overrides grief
  • In ancient times, temporary burial preceded final ancestral reinterment
  • Abaye resolves the baraita contradiction: not because the act is joyous — but because Festival joy prevails over the sadness involved
  • The act is a mitzva of honoring parents and proper burial

Rabbi Yosei — Forbids

  • May NOT gather bones on Chol HaMoed
  • Reason: it is a source of mourning — grief of loss is actively renewed
  • Even if he is glad to reinter them in ancestral graves, the pain dominates
  • The activity is fundamentally incompatible with Festival joy
  • No distinction is drawn between parents and other relatives

😢 The 30-Day Pre-Festival Lamentation Prohibition

Mishna Rule: One may not arouse lamentation (ye'orer) for a deceased relative, and may not eulogize him, during the 30 days before a pilgrimage Festival (Pesach, Shavuot, Sukkot).
↓
What is 'arousing lamentation'? According to Rav (transmitted from the West / Eretz Yisrael): hiring professional eulogizers to re-mourn a death that occurred long before — deliberately rekindling grief.
↓
Why specifically 30 days? Shmuel explains: A deceased person is not yet forgotten from the heart within 30 days. Renewed grief so close to the Festival will carry over and be felt on the Festival, ruining the required joy of Yom Tov.
↓
Cautionary tale (Rav): A man saved his earnings specifically to ascend to Jerusalem for the Festival. A professional eulogizer stood at his door; the man's wife wept and he wept with her — spending all his Festival money on the eulogizer. He missed the Festival entirely.
↓
Practical scope: The prohibition targets re-aroused grief for old losses — not fresh mourning, which follows its own halakhic track. The concern is that artificially renewed pain will contaminate Festival joy.

💒 Why Is Marriage Forbidden on Chol HaMoed?

Rav (via Rav Daniel)
Derived from: 'You shall rejoice in your Festival' (Deut. 16:14) — 'your Festival' joy specifically, implying one joy at a time. One may not mix two separate joys (אין מערבין שמחה בשמחה) — the joy of marriage with the joy of the Festival.
Ulla
The reason is not about mixing joys but about excessive labor: wedding preparations demand enormous effort, which constitutes forbidden labor (melachah) during the Festival period.
R' Yitzḥak Nappaḥa
The reason is neither of the above — it is due to the neglect of the mitzva of rejoicing on the Festival. A man preoccupied with his new bride will neglect his obligation to rejoice in the Festival's joy.
Gemara — Practical Implication
These reasons diverge in practice: Does the prohibition extend to all those the Sages prohibit from marrying on Chol HaMoed? The Gemara raises this from a baraita — to be continued on Daf 8b–9.
What IS permitted Reuniting with one's wife after separation, and a woman performing all her usual cosmetic treatments (adornments) on Chol HaMoed — both are permitted. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees on lime depilation as it temporarily disfigures.
