🛏️ When Do Mourners Overturn Their Beds?

Rabbi Eliezer
From when the corpse is taken out of the opening of his house — at that moment the mourners must overturn their beds.
Rabbi Yehoshua
From when the tomb is sealed with the grave cover — only then do the mourners overturn their beds.
Incident: Rabban Gamliel
Both opinions were enacted in practice: R. Eliezer instructed overturning when the body left the house; R. Yehoshua instructed it again when the tomb was sealed.
Practical Outcome Both authorities are honored — overturning begins when the body leaves the house (R. Eliezer) and is reinforced at the sealing of the tomb (R. Yehoshua).

🛋️ What Is a Dargash? — Four Opinions

Ulla

  • A good-luck bed (גַּדָּא)
  • Ornamental; not used for sleeping
  • Difficulty: why does a king recline on the ground at the mourner's meal if this bed isn't slept on?

Rav Taḥalifa (via Ravin)

  • A leather bed
  • Reported from the West (Eretz Yisrael)
  • Identified in the market of leather workers

Rabbi Yirmeya

  • Interlacing is from the inside
  • Loops pass through holes in the frame itself
  • Regular bed: interlacing from the outside — key structural difference

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel (Halakha)

  • For a dargash: loosen the loops holding the pillows
  • The bed collapses on its own
  • R. Ya'akov bar Aḥa: halakha follows RSbG

⚖️ Rabbinic Enactments: Equalizing Burial Customs

Meal baskets: Wealthy used silver/gold baskets; poor used wicker → Poor embarrassed → Enacted: all use plain wicker baskets
↓
Wine cups: Wealthy used white glass; poor used colored glass → Embarrassed → Enacted: all use colored glass cups
↓
Uncovering faces: Wealthy uncovered; poor covered (faces blackened by famine) → Enacted: all faces are covered equally
↓
Biers: Wealthy on dargash; poor on plain bier → Embarrassed → Enacted: all carried on plain bier — due to honor of the poor
↓
Shrouds: Expensive shrouds → Relatives sometimes abandoned bodies out of shame → Rabban Gamliel enacted: plain linen shrouds for all

😢 Limits of Grief — Measured Mourning

Rav (via Rav Yehuda)
Anyone who grieves excessively over his dead — refusing consolation — will in the end weep for another person. Grief must have limits.
Rabbi Levi
Days 1–3: see yourself as if a sword lies between your thighs. Days 4–7: sword is in the corner. After 7: sword passes before you. Dread diminishes in stages.
Jeremiah 22:10 (Baraita)
'Weep not for the dead' = not excessively. 'Neither bemoan him' = not beyond appropriate measure. 'Weep sore for him that goes away' = one who dies without children.
Rav Yehuda (Rav)
When a person dies in a city, all residents are prohibited from performing work until he is buried — communal solidarity with the dead.
Key Principle Mourning has prescribed stages and measured limits. Both insufficient and excessive grief are problematic. The Torah sets a humane, structured framework for bereavement.
