⚔️ Contradicting Baraitot: May New Cisterns Be Dug?

Baraita 1
One may clean existing cisterns for the public on Chol HaMoed — but one may not dig new cisterns, even for the public.
Rabbi Yaakov (citing Rabbi Yoḥanan)
Baraita 1 applies only when the public does not need them. When the public does need them, even digging new cisterns is permitted!
Gemara Challenge
But Baraita 2 explicitly permits digging for the public — doesn't this contradict your reading of Baraita 1?
Gemara Resolution
No contradiction: Baraita 1 = no need (nothing permitted). Baraita 2 = public need (digging permitted). Individual need = cleaning only.
Rav Ashi
The Mishna itself is precise: "One may tend to all other public needs" — the word all includes new digging when the public requires it.
Core Principle Need determines permission: public need overrides standard Chol HaMoed labor restrictions.

🌳 What Gets Marked? Certain vs. Uncertain Ritual Impurity

✅ Must Be Marked (Uncertain Impurity)

  • Overhanging boughs — tree branch over possible grave creates tent impurity
  • Protruding stones — stones jutting from wall may overhang a buried corpse
  • Beit haperas — field where a grave was plowed, scattering bones
  • Weepers' field — where corpse was transferred; bones may have been dropped
  • Reason: Public is unaware and may inadvertently become impure

❌ Courts Do NOT Mark (Certain or Minimal)

  • Olive-bulk of flesh — will decay below minimum size; burn teruma instead (Rav Pappa)
  • Barleygrain-sized bone — too small; imparts only contact/carrying impurity
  • Certain impurity — everyone already knows; no marking needed
  • Trampled beit haperas — foot traffic has cleared bones; considered pure (Rav Yehuda bar Ami citing Ulla)
  • Reason: Either already known or impurity risk is negligible/resolved

🌾 The Three Types of Beit HaPeras

Type 1: Field Where a Grave Was Plowed
A known grave was accidentally plowed, scattering bones throughout the field. Rav Pappa: This type requires marking — scattered bones may create tent impurity.
↓
Type 2: Field Where a Corpse Was Lost in Transit
A body was being carried through the field and became lost. The location of remains is unknown, raising concern throughout the field.
↓
Type 3: Weepers' Field (שְׂדֵה בוֹכִין)
Where the public escort handed the corpse to the burial party. Avimi: The owners despaired of recovering any dropped bones — this despair itself creates the concern.
↓
Key Leniency — Trampled Beit HaPeras:
A beit haperas that has been well-trodden by foot traffic is ritually pure — passersby have certainly cleared away any scattered bones. (Rav Yehuda bar Ami citing Ulla)
↓
Key Leniency — Trees on the Boundary:
If trees stand along the field's border next to a public domain, the grave cannot be outside the trees (no one buries in public domain). The area of concern is limited to within the tree boundary. (Ulla)

💡 Ordering One's Ways — Psalms 50:23 as Halakha and Ethics

וְשָׂם דֶּרֶךְ אַרְאֶנּוּ בְּיֵשַׁע אֱלֹהִים — תהלים נ:כג
וְשָׂם דֶּרֶךְ אַרְאֶנּוּ בְּיֵשַׁע אֱלֹהִים Ravina derives the obligation to mark graves from Psalms 50:23: "And to him who orders his way, I will show the salvation of God." One who marks ritually pure paths — enabling others to walk safely — merits divine salvation. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi adds an ethical dimension: Whoever contemplates how to act in the most appropriate way in this world merits seeing the salvation of the Holy One, Blessed be He. Rabbi Yannai applied this to his own student: the student wisely refrained from challenging his master publicly on Shabbat of a Festival (when crowds gathered), lest Rabbi Yannai be embarrassed. He ordered his way — knowing when to speak and when to hold back. Rabbi Yannai read this very verse in his praise.
