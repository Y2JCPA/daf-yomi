✂️ Who May Cut Hair on Chol HaMoed?

Permitted to Cut Hair / Launder

  • One returning from overseas
  • One released from prison
  • One released from ostracism (נידוי)
  • One released from a vow not to launder
  • A baby (born before or during Festival)
  • One with only a single shirt

Reason for General Prohibition

  • Must enter Festival freshly groomed
  • Priestly watch analogy (Ta'anit 15b)
  • Priests forbidden to cut hair lest they enter their watch unkempt
  • Similarly: prepare before Festival begins
  • Negligence before Festival = no relief during it

Disputed & Nuanced Cases

  • One who lost item erev Yom Tov (Rabbi Zeira's query)
  • Craftsman who lost item (Rav Ashi's version)
  • Mourner on Festival — private vs. public practices
  • Ostracized person: Rava holds ostracism continues
  • Leper (metzora): practices remain in force

⚖️ The Priestly Watch — Source of the Prohibition

Gemara's Question: Why prohibit haircuts on Chol HaMoed? Shouldn't honoring the Festival permit it?
↓
Mishna (Ta'anit 15b): Members of the priestly watch and the non-priestly watch (אנשי מעמד) are forbidden to cut hair or launder during their Temple service week
↓
Rabba bar bar Ḥana in name of Rabbi Elazar: Reason — so they do not enter their watch period looking unkempt (מנוולין)
↓
Applied to Festival
One must groom before the Festival. Failure = no exemption during Chol HaMoed.
Exceptions Granted
Those who truly could not prepare (overseas, prison, vow, ostracism) receive relief on Chol HaMoed.

🤔 Lost Item Before the Festival — A Valid Excuse?

Rabbi Zeira
If someone lost an item erev Yom Tov and was busy searching — no time to cut hair or launder. Is this like one who 'couldn't prepare'? Should he be permitted on Chol HaMoed?
Abaye
No! The Sages forbade decorated Syrian cakes for Passover lest people tarry and miss the chametz deadline. Similarly, we do not exempt those who were negligent — even for a valid reason — lest the exception swallow the rule.
Rabbi Zeira
But Rabbi Asi said in Rabbi Yoḥanan's name: one with only a single shirt may launder it on Chol HaMoed! By your Syrian cake logic, he should have obtained a second shirt beforehand!
Mar b. Rav Ashi
His belt is proof (חגורו מוכיח עליו)! When he launders his only shirt he must wear his outer garment tied with a belt — publicly demonstrating he truly has no other garment. No deception is possible.
Ruling One who lost an item is NOT exempt — negligence cannot be rewarded. The single-shirt leniency stands because the belt makes the need publicly verifiable.

😢 Mourning, Ostracism & Festival Joy — Who Yields?

Gemara (14b)
A mourner does NOT practice mourning on a Festival — 'וְשָׂמַחְתָּ בְּחַגֶּךָ' (Devarim 16:14). The communal positive mitzvah of rejoicing overrides the individual's positive mitzvah of mourning.
Gemara
However — private mourning practices (those not visible to others) ARE still observed even on the Festival. Only public expressions are suspended.
Rav Yosef
What about one who is ostracized (מנודה) on the Festival? A baraita states the court continues to adjudicate ostracism cases — implying ostracism is practiced!
Abaye
No proof — 'the court judges' means they deliberate, but do not issue verdicts on the Festival itself. No new ostracism is actually imposed.
Rava
The baraita says 'they permitted HIM' (singular) — only one specific individual received permission for a haircut, not all who are ostracized. This proves ostracism generally continues on the Festival.
Key Distinction Public mourning → suspended by Festival joy. Private mourning → continues. Ostracism (per Rava) → continues during Festival. Leper's practices → also continue (derived from Vayikra 13:45).

🏥 Leper (Metzora) Status During the Festival

Question: Must a metzora observe his practices — torn clothes (בגדים קרועים), disheveled hair, calling 'טָמֵא טָמֵא!' — during the Festival?
↓
Abaye's proof attempt: A baraita states that 'during days of his impurity on the Festival, he does not observe practices of ostracism' — implying Festival joy overrides the leper's restrictions.
↓
Gemara rejects: The baraita uses a 'needless to say' (אין צריך לומר) style — it only addresses ostracism-type restrictions during impurity days, not the full metzora regime.
↓
Rava's proof (Vayikra 13:45): 'וְהַצָּרוּעַ אֲשֶׁר בּוֹ הַנֶּגַע' — the extra 'and the leper' includes even the High Priest, showing these practices are universally and continuously binding.
↓
Conclusion: The metzora's distinctive practices (torn clothes, disheveled hair, calling out) remain in force even on the Festival — they are not overridden by Festival joy.
