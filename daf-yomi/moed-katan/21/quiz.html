QUESTION 1 OF 5
From which verse does the Gemara ultimately derive that kriah must be performed while standing?
A. Job 1:20 — 'Then Job arose, and rent his coat'
B. II Samuel 13:31 — 'Then the king arose, and rent his garments'
C. Deuteronomy 34:8 — 'The days of weeping and mourning for Moses ended'
D. Psalms 19:9 — 'The statutes of the Lord are right, rejoicing the heart'
Correct: B — The Gemara rejects Job as a source because Job may have done something extra beyond the requirement. David is preferred: a king does not stand without cause, implying his standing during kriah was obligatory, not optional.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rabbi Eliezer, on which day(s) must a mourner fully remove his tefillin (with no exceptions)?
A. Only the first day
B. The first two days
C. The first three days
D. Whenever new visitors arrive, on any day of the shiva
Correct: A — Rabbi Eliezer holds the first day of mourning is uniquely severe. On the second day and beyond, the mourner removes tefillin only when new faces (who had not yet seen him in tefillin) arrive, and re-dons them when those visitors leave.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What special exception permitted Rabbi Akiva to greet the crowd during the first three days of mourning for his sons?
A. A rabbi's Torah obligations supersede mourning restrictions
B. Greetings on behalf of the deceased's honor are always permitted
C. Showing public respect (kavod ha-tzibbur) is treated differently
D. The death of children is a lesser form of mourning
Correct: C — The Gemara explains that Rabbi Akiva's address to the assembled crowd was permissible because honoring and showing respect to the public (kavod ha-tzibbur) is treated as a distinct category that overrides the prohibition on extending greetings during days 1–3.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rav Idi bar Avin, why may a mourner extend shalom to others after seven days but others should not yet extend shalom to him?
A. The mourner is ritually impure until thirty days have passed
B. The mourner is himself at peace (shalom) but others have not yet fully consoled him
C. Extending shalom first is a sign of complete healing, which only the mourner can declare
D. It is a rabbinic penalty to remind the mourner of communal obligations
Correct: B — Rav Idi bar Avin: after seven days the mourner may say 'shalom' because he himself is at peace (shalom) with his loss. But others should not yet extend shalom toward him, as from their perspective he has not been fully consoled — his grief is still their concern.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
If a mourner arrives at another family's house of mourning during the first three days of his own bereavement, how does he count his mourning?
A. He restarts his count from the moment of arrival
B. He maintains his own independent count regardless of the household
C. He counts together with the household from the time of their burial and completes mourning with them
D. He counts only from when he personally views the deceased
Correct: C — The baraita teaches: if a mourner arrives from a nearby place during days 1–3, he joins his mourning count with the household's — counting from the burial — and completes shiva together with them. Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba clarifies this applies specifically to arrival from a nearby location within the first three days.
