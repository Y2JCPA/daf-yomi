🕯️ Mourning Periods and Their Graduated Rules

Days 1–3 (Most Severe): No tefillin, no work at all, no greetings extended or received, no bathing, anointing, shoes, or marital relations; may not enter another mourner's house
↓
Days 3–7: May don tefillin; may work privately at home; may respond when greeted but still may not initiate greetings; may visit another mourner's house but sits among the mourners, not the consolers
↓
Days 8–30: May extend greetings normally; others may still speak words of consolation to him; consolation for non-parental relatives ends here
↓
After 30 days (parents: after 12 months): Full return to normalcy; speaking consolation is like probing a healed wound — Rabbi Meir likens it to a physician re-opening a set bone

✂️ The Source for Standing During Kriah

Rami bar Ḥama
Kriah must be performed standing — as it is written: 'Then Job arose, and rent his coat' (Job 1:20)
Gemara Challenge
Perhaps Job did something extra beyond what is required — this cannot serve as an obligatory source!
Gemara (Revised Derivation)
Derive instead from David: 'Then the king arose, and rent his garments' (II Sam. 13:31) — a king does not stand unless obligated
Ameimar to Rav Ashi
The verse says David lay on the ground — but actually it was as if on the ground: he had overturned his bed. Thus Ameimar himself mourned on a flipped bed
✅ Halakhic Conclusion Kriah must be performed while standing. The operative proof-text is David (II Samuel 13:31), not Job, because a king standing signals obligation.

📿 Tefillin During Mourning: Three Positions

Rabbi Eliezer

  • Day 1: remove tefillin entirely
  • Day 2 onward: remove only when new visitors arrive, re-don when they leave
  • Source: 'days of weeping' (Deut. 34:8) — plural = 2; first day uniquely severe
  • Moses's burial day was sui generis; day 1 for others mirrors it

Rabbi Yehoshua

  • Days 1–2: remove tefillin
  • Day 3 onward: may don tefillin
  • Source: same 'days' verse — minimum 2 days of mourning without tefillin
  • Moses was different (no human buried him); regular mourning = 2 full days

Ulla's Ruling (Halakha)

  • Follow Rabbi Eliezer: remove tefillin only day 1
  • Follow Rabbi Yehoshua: may don from day 2
  • Rava: once donned on day 2, need not remove even if new visitors arrive
  • Mitzva principle: a positive mitzva (tefillin) applies even mid-mourning

🤐 Greeting Rules Across the Mourning Periods

Someone encounters a mourner — when may greetings occur?
↓
Days 1–3
Mourner may not extend or respond to greetings
Exception: honoring the public (cf. Rabbi Akiva at his sons' eulogy)
Days 3–7
Mourner may respond when greeted, but may not initiate
After Day 7
Mourner may extend and respond normally
Others should not greet him until he is fully consoled (Rav Idi bar Avin)
↓
Special rule: If the greeter did not know the person was in mourning — mourner may respond even during days 1–3

🏠 Mourner Visiting Another House of Mourning

Arrives from a nearby place during days 1–3: He counts mourning days together with the household from the time of burial and completes his mourning with them
↓
Where does the visitor sit? Not among the consolers — but in the place designated for the mourners themselves
↓
Rabba bar bar Ḥana's dilemma: A death occurred in his family and he considered not going out to deliver his regular Torah exposition
↓
Rabbi Ḥanina's ruling: If the public needs him, a Torah scholar may go out and deliver his lesson even during shiva — 'the public's need overrides'
↓
The method: Like Rabbi Yehuda bar Ilai's practice — another rabbi would speak and the mourner would confirm with a slight nod, not actively delivering the teaching himself
