🔄 Core Debate: Does Erroneous Uprooting Disqualify an Offering?

Rabba
Erroneous uprooting does NOT constitute real uprooting. If a priest mistakenly thought an offering was a different type and intended accordingly, the offering is still valid — the error negates the effect of the misidentification.
Rabbi Zeira
Objection from Rabbi Shimon! A meal offering from which the handful was removed for the sake of a different meal-offering type is disqualified. This proves erroneous uprooting DOES invalidate an offering.
Abaye
No — Rabbi Shimon's case is different. The mode of preparation reveals the offering's identity (shallow pan vs deep pan), so the priest's mislabeling is necessarily conscious, not erroneous. Animal offerings have no such visible distinction, so Rabba's rule still stands.
Key Distinction Conscious mislabeling (offering reveals its own type) = valid uprooting → disqualifies. Erroneous mislabeling (no visible distinction) = not real uprooting → does not disqualify.

🐑 Shavuot Lambs Slaughtered as Rams — What Is the Ruling?

Rabba bar bar Ḥana
If the two Shavuot lambs were slaughtered for the sake of rams rather than lambs, they are still valid and the community fulfills its obligation.
Rav
Agreed — valid even when slaughtered for rams.
Rav Ḥisda
Rav's ruling applies only when he thought they were rams but intended for lambs. But if he thought rams AND intended for rams — the obligation is not fulfilled. The intention corresponds to a non-existent offering.
Rava
Objection: the mishna in Gittin (54a) shows that even conscious wrong intention about offering type does not necessarily prevent fulfillment — yet another case contradicts this framework!
Rav Ḥisda's Distinction Thought ram / Intended lamb → Valid (erroneous, not real uprooting). Thought ram / Intended ram → Invalid (intention has no real object).

🍞 Why Meal Offerings Differ from Animal Offerings

Meal Offerings

  • Each type has a distinct mode of preparation
  • Shallow pan (מַחֲבַת) vs deep pan (מַרְחֶשֶׁת) are visually distinguishable
  • The offering itself reveals its identity
  • Therefore: removing handful for wrong type = conscious uprooting
  • → Per Rabbi Shimon: disqualifies the offering

Animal Offerings

  • One mode of slaughter for all offering types
  • One mode of blood collection for all
  • One mode of sprinkling for all
  • The animal reveals nothing about its designation
  • Therefore: intending for wrong type = erroneous uprooting
  • → Per Rabba: does NOT disqualify

📅 Community with Limited Animals — Daily vs Additional Offerings

Dilemma (Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin):
Treasury has enough for either daily offerings of tomorrow OR additional offerings of today — not both. Which takes priority?
↓
Rav Ḥisda's Resolution
Derive from the mishna itself: failure to sacrifice daily offerings does not prevent additional offerings. This must refer to a scarcity case — proving neither cancels the other.
Abaye's Rebuttal
The mishna may only address order of sacrifice when both are available, not scarcity. The dilemma remains open from the mishna alone.
↓
Further proof (Arakhin 13a): Six inspected lambs are kept in the Chamber of Lambs — enough for three days of daily offerings. This prioritizes daily offerings over additional offerings.
↓
Ben Bag Bag: The daily offering requires four days of prior inspection (gezerah shavah from Paschal offering). This explains why six lambs = three days of coverage.

⚖️ Rabbi Shimon's Qualification: When Does Non-Prevention Apply?

The Mishna's Rule: Failure to sacrifice daily offerings does not prevent additional offerings, and vice versa.
↓
Rabbi Shimon's Condition: This applies only when the omission was due to circumstances beyond control (אֹנֶס) or due to inadvertent neglect (שׁוֹגֵג).
↓
Exception: If the daily offering was intentionally omitted (מֵזִיד), the additional offerings of that day do NOT satisfy the community's obligation.
↓
Reasoning: Deliberate abandonment of the daily offering is a graver violation; allowing additional offerings to stand in such a case would implicitly condone the intentional sin.
↓
Ravina and Rav Ashi: Debate whether the Chamber of Lambs should hold 6, 7, or 8 inspected lambs, accounting for Shabbat eve and Shabbat afternoon daily offerings.
