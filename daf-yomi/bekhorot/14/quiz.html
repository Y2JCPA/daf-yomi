QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yoḥanan's resolution, when does piggul intent for one Shavuot loaf render the other loaf piggul as well?
A. Always — the two loaves are always one body regardless of the priest's intent
B. Only when the priest had intent during sprinkling, not during slaughter
C. When the priest combined his intent — intending to consume an olive-bulk from both loaves together
D. Never — Rabbi Yosei holds the two loaves are always two separate bodies
Correct: C — Rabbi Yoḥanan explains that if the priest combined his intent (directing his mind to an olive-bulk from both loaves), the verse renders them one body and both become piggul. But if he separated his intent (intending only for one loaf), only that loaf becomes piggul.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Rav Huna argue we cannot learn Rabbi Yosei's opinion from the first baraita?
A. The baraita was authored anonymously with no attributed sage
B. The baraita follows Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's opinion, not Rabbi Yosei's
C. Rabbi Yosei explicitly contradicted that baraita in a later teaching
D. The baraita discusses animals, not loaves, so no inference is possible
Correct: B — Rav Huna argues that the baraita was authored according to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's opinion. Since it doesn't reflect Rabbi Yosei's view, no inference can be drawn about what Rabbi Yosei holds regarding the two loaves being one or two bodies.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What does Levi's baraita teach about intentions occurring during the four sacrificial rites?
A. They always combine to create piggul since all four are essential rites
B. Only slaughter and sprinkling combine, as they are the primary permitting factors
C. Intentions during the four rites do not combine to render an offering piggul
D. They combine only if each individual intent involves at least a quarter olive-bulk
Correct: C — Levi's baraita explicitly states that intentions occurring during the four sacrificial rites — slaughter, blood reception, carrying the blood, and sprinkling — do NOT combine to create piggul liability. Each rite is treated independently.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the basis for Abaye's argument that the Rabbis would NOT decree disqualification in the baraita's case of two lambs?
A. The Rabbis require a formal Sanhedrin vote before issuing any new decree
B. There is no parallel case where that pattern of intent (half-measure at each of two different lambs) produces piggul
C. The Rabbis hold that piggul cannot apply to permitting factors in Shavuot offerings
D. Two lambs are considered entirely separate offerings with no shared piggul status
Correct: B — Abaye's framework is that Rabbinic decrees of disqualification are always issued by analogy to an existing piggul case. In the baraita's scenario (each lamb slaughtered with intent for half a measure from a different loaf), there is no existing parallel case — therefore there is no basis for a rabbinic decree.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the principle behind Rabbi Yehuda's ruling in the Mishna that BOTH Shavuot loaves must be burned when one becomes impure?
A. Impurity spreads automatically from one loaf to the other through proximity
B. A communal offering cannot be divided — אין קרבן ציבור חלוק
C. The High Priest's frontplate cannot effect atonement for intentional impurity
D. The Torah mandates that all Shavuot offerings be treated as a single unit in all circumstances
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda applies the principle that a communal offering (קרבן ציבור) cannot be divided. Since the two loaves form a single communal offering brought together, if one is disqualified, both must go to the place of burning — the offering cannot be split so that one part is offered and the other burned.
