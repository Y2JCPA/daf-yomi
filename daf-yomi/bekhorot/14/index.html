🍞 Central Question: Are the Two Shavuot Loaves One Body or Two?

Two Shavuot loaves brought together with two lambs — one offering or two independent units?
↓
Rav Huna (initial reading)
Two distinct bodies — piggul intent on one loaf does NOT render the other piggul
Rabbi Yoḥanan (resolution)
One body — combined intent renders both piggul; separated intent affects only the intended loaf
↓
Further dispute: Do half-measure intentions during different sacrificial rites combine to create piggul?

⚔️ Rav Huna Defended and Refuted

Rav Huna
The first baraita follows Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, not Rabbi Yosei — we cannot infer Rabbi Yosei's opinion from it. Rabbi Yosei holds two thighs are one body.
Rav Ashi
Refutation! Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi quotes Rabbi Yosei: one who slaughtered bulls/goats with intent to pour the blood tomorrow has rendered the offering piggul — even without direct intent regarding the meat!
Ravina
Another refutation from Mishna 13a: removing a handful with intent to burn it tomorrow — Rabbi Yosei says the remainder becomes piggul despite no direct intent about it. So intent on one part clearly affects another!
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Resolution: Rabbi Yosei holds the two thighs are ONE body. For two loaves: if priest combined intent (olive-bulk from both), both become piggul. If separated (intent for only one), only that loaf is piggul.
Key Outcome Rabbi Yoḥanan resolves all contradictions: Rabbi Yosei sees two-part offerings as a unified body — piggul intent on part can flow to the whole, depending on how the priest directed his mind.

🐑 Piggul Intent: Two Shavuot Loaves & Two Lambs

Priest slaughters one or both Shavuot lambs with intent to consume from the loaves tomorrow
↓
Intent COMBINED: olive-bulk from BOTH loaves
Both loaves become piggul — liable for eating either one
Intent SEPARATED: olive-bulk from ONE loaf only
Only that loaf becomes piggul; the other remains permitted
↓
Edge case: Each lamb slaughtered with intent for half an olive-bulk from a different loaf — never a full measure for any single loaf
↓
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: Not piggul — intent was always 'half and half,' never a complete measure for either loaf

⚖️ Do Intentions During Different Rites Combine?

Some Say: YES, Combine

  • Half olive-bulk intent at slaughter + half at sprinkling = piggul
  • Both slaughter and sprinkling are permitting factors (מתירים)
  • Together they constitute a complete prohibited consumption intent

Levi's Baraita: NO Combining

  • The four rites: slaughter, blood reception, carrying blood, sprinkling
  • Intentions across these four rites do NOT combine
  • Each rite stands independently for piggul liability

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi

  • Slaughtering one lamb with half-measure intent for each of two different loaves = NOT piggul
  • The intent was split between two loaves — never a full measure for one
  • Abaye: this ruling applies specifically to the half-and-half scenario

🏛️ Abaye's Framework: Rabbinic Decrees Work by Analogy

Rabbi Yosei's Decree: Intent to burn the handful of frankincense tomorrow disqualifies the offering — decreed because it parallels intent about the handful of flour, which is a genuine permitting factor
↓
Rabbis' Decree #1 (Handfuls): Intent involving one handful disqualifies due to the case of a sinner's meal offering — which has no frankincense, so the handful alone permits, making them analogous
↓
Rabbis' Decree #2 (Two Lambs): Slaughtering one lamb with intent to consume both loaves tomorrow — Rabbis disqualify by decree even though strictly only one loaf should follow
↓
Rabbis' Decree #3 (Shewbread): Burning one bowl of frankincense with intent for both shewbread arrangements → second arrangement disqualified by rabbinic decree due to the other bowl
↓
Abaye's Conclusion: In the baraita (each lamb slaughtered for half a measure from a different loaf) — NO parallel piggul case exists where this pattern produces piggul. Therefore NO rabbinic decree applies and Rabbis would not disqualify it.
