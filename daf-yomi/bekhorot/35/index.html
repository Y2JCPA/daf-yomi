⚖️ Halakhot l'Moshe miSinai vs. Rabbinic Requirements

Halakha l'Moshe miSinai

  • Titora (base) must be present
  • Straps must be black on outside
  • Tefillin must be perfectly square
  • Knot: Dalet (head) and Yod (arm)

Rabbinic Elaborations

  • Ma'ebarta (strap channel) required — Abaye
  • Furrows must reach down to titora — Abaye
  • Parchment examined before writing — Abaye
  • Straps must hang to index finger length

Fitness (Kashrut) Rulings

  • Wrong parshiyot order → unfit
  • Two adjacent torn walls → unfit (Rava)
  • Two non-adjacent torn walls → fit
  • Outer hide intact → fit despite inner tears

⬛ Black Straps: Sinai Law or Flexible Ruling?

Rabbi Yitzḥak
The requirement that tefillin straps be black is a halakha l'Moshe miSinai — directly transmitted from Sinai. There is no dispute.
Baraita Objection
A baraita teaches: whether straps are green, black, or white — they are valid. This implies color is irrelevant and contradicts Rabbi Yitzḥak!
Gemara Resolution
The baraita discusses the inner side of the straps; Rabbi Yitzḥak speaks of the outer, visible side. Black is mandated only on the exterior.
Follow-up Difficulty
But why should the inner side matter at all? It is not visible — what disqualifying concern could there be with red straps on the inside?
Gemara Answer
Sometimes the strap flips and the inner side becomes visible. A red interior could present a 'deprecatory matter' when accidentally exposed.
Ruling Outside of straps: black required (Sinai). Inside: flexible, but red may be problematic if exposed.

Walls between the 4 compartments of tefillin shel rosh have torn
↓
Outer hide still intact
Fit — Rav Huna: as long as the outer hide surface is not torn, tefillin remain valid
Two walls have torn
Must determine which walls and whether they are adjacent
↓
Walls are non-adjacent (not aligned with each other)
Fit — Rava permits; the compartments retain their integrity
Walls are adjacent (side by side)
Unfit — Rava: two adjacent torn walls create a breach that disqualifies the tefillin

🪢 The Knot of Tefillin — Shape, Placement, and Cosmic Meaning

Tefillin shel rosh knot: Must form the shape of the letter דָּלֶת (Dalet — ד) — a halakha l'Moshe miSinai (Rav Yehuda bar Rav Shmuel bar Sheilat in Rav's name)
↓
Tefillin shel yad knot: Must form the shape of the letter יוֹד (Yod — י)
↓
Combined: שִׁין (Shin) on the batim + דָּלֶת (Dalet) knot on head + יוֹד (Yod) knot on arm = שַׁדַּי — a Name of God written across the body
↓
The knot must rest above — on the head, not the neck (Rav Yehuda): so that Israel should be above and not below
↓
The knot must face forward (panim) — not toward the back — corresponding to God's face turning toward Israel
↓
"All nations shall see the Name of God called upon you" (Deut. 28:10) — Rabbi Eliezer the Great: this is the tefillin shel rosh, whose visible knot makes nations tremble

📜 The Order of the Parshiyot — Why Position Is Everything

הֶחֱלִיף אֶת הַחִיצוֹנָה בִּפְנִימִית — פָּסוּל
Core Ruling: Wrong Order = Unfit The four Torah passages placed in tefillin must occupy their fixed positions. Placing an inner passage (e.g., Exodus 13:11–16) where an outer passage belongs (Exodus 13:1–10's position), or vice versa, renders the tefillin pasul (unfit).

Rava's explanation: the passage that must face outward needs to 'see the air' — it must be positioned at its designated outer location. This is not merely a formal arrangement; the spatial orientation of each parasha is integral to the tefillin's validity.

The same principle applies to tefillin shel rosh: Deuteronomy 11:13–21 placed in the wrong position disqualifies the tefillin regardless of whether all four passages are physically present.
