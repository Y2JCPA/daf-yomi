QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava's interpretation of Rabbi Shimon, what is the consequence if priests intentionally fail to offer the morning Tamid?
A. The entire day's Temple service is permanently canceled
B. Those same sinning priests may not offer the afternoon Tamid, but other innocent priests may serve
C. A communal fast must be declared before service can resume
D. The afternoon Tamid is offered as normal regardless of intent
Correct: B — Rava clarifies that punishment is personal — the priests who sinned are barred from the afternoon service, but others who did not sin may serve. The altar itself is not made permanently idle by the sin of individuals.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does Abaye prove that the golden altar was inaugurated with the afternoon incense, not the morning?
A. The verse explicitly says 'in the evening you shall light it' regarding the first incense
B. Ashes are removed from the Candelabrum in the morning, proving lamps were lit the previous evening — so the first act was an afternoon lighting
C. Aaron's inaugural speech in Leviticus mentions sunset as the time of first service
D. The word for morning (boker) in Exodus 30:7 is interpreted as referring to a previous afternoon
Correct: B — Abaye's proof: since ashes are cleaned from the lamps each morning, the lamps must have been burning since the evening before. This means the very first service performed — lighting the Candelabrum — happened in the afternoon, inaugurating the altar then.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why may neither an individual nor the community bring a voluntary gift offering of incense on the inner altar?
A. There is no halakhic precedent: individuals have no incense obligation (so no model for voluntary), and the community's fixed obligation does not extend to extras
B. The inner altar was reserved exclusively for the High Priest's personal use
C. Voluntary incense was only permitted on the outer altar during Yom Tov
D. The Talmud records that the Rabbis enacted a fence-decree against all extra incense
Correct: A — The Gemara derives that voluntary offerings require an analogous obligatory offering as a model. Individuals have no incense obligation at all; the community's obligation is fixed and does not generate a template for extras. Rav Pappa explains the baraita uses the lo tzricha (it need not even be said) literary style.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
When a High Priest dies after offering his morning half of the chavitin, what must the replacement High Priest do?
A. He offers the remaining half from the first High Priest's isaron, since it was already set aside
B. He brings his own complete isaron, divides it, sacrifices half in the afternoon, and discards the other half
C. He waits until the next morning to begin his own offering cycle
D. He offers a complete isaron undivided as a special inauguration offering
Correct: B — The replacement must bring his own complete isaron from his own home, divide it in half, offer one half in the afternoon, and discard the other. The result: two halves are sacrificed (one from each High Priest) and two halves are wasted — neither may be eaten since the entire chavitin is burned.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What does the word tufinei (Leviticus 6:14) teach about the High Priest's griddle-cake offering?
A. That the loaves must be formed in a distinctive twisted shape unique to the High Priest
B. That the loaves must be soaked in oil before baking, not after
C. That the loaves must be beautiful and fresh when baked — therefore preparation overrides Shabbat since they cannot be made the day before
D. That the loaves require a double baking process, first boiled then baked
Correct: C — Rav Huna reads tufinei as te'afena na'a — they must be beautiful when baked. Since preparing them on Friday would leave them stale by Shabbat morning, they must be freshly kneaded and baked on Shabbat itself, overriding the labor prohibition.
