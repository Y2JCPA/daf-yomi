🫱 The Four Kemitza Rites — and What Happens Shelo Lishmah

1. Kemitza (קמיצה) — Priest removes a handful from the meal offering with three middle fingers
↓
2. Hag'asha (הגשה) — Places the handful into a service vessel (כלי שרת)
↓
3. Holakha (הולכה) — Conveys the handful to the altar
↓
4. Haktara (הקטרה) — Burns the handful on the altar fire
↓
Result (lishmah): The remainder (שיריים) is now permitted to the priests for consumption
↓
Result (shelo lishmah): Offering is valid — but owner's obligation is not discharged. Any single rite performed shelo lishmah triggers this rule.

🔥 Abaye Challenges Rabba: What Intent Actually Disqualifies?

Rabba
Only intent that is not recognizably false (לא ניכר שקרן) can disqualify an offering. A voluntary mincha can be prepared just like a sinner's mincha — so the intent to sacrifice one for the other is not visibly contradicted by the act.
Abaye
But the whole rule that intent disqualifies a mincha is derived from the Torah's comparison to sin and guilt offerings! Why should it matter whether the intent is recognizable? The Torah's comparison applies equally to all cases.
Rabba
Rabbi Shimon treats the Torah's comparison as a logical reason (טעמא), not merely a legal peg. Therefore the principle extends only where the underlying logic applies — i.e., where the rite does not visibly contradict the intent.
Gemara
Test case: a bird burnt offering pinched above the red line, with intent for a bird sin offering — is this recognizably false? No! A bird sin offering may also be pinched above the line (after the fact). Therefore the intent is plausible and does disqualify.
Rabba's Principle Improper intent disqualifies only when the ritual act does not visibly contradict the stated intent. If the act itself could belong to either offering type, the wrong intent has full halakhic effect.

🔄 Three Resolutions of the Contradiction in Rabbi Shimon

Rav Ashi ✓

  • Satisfies obligation: Change of sanctity (שינוי קודש) — rite done for a different type of offering
  • Does not satisfy: Change of owner (שינוי בעלים) — rite done for a different person's offering
  • Mishna discusses change of sanctity → fits Rav Ashi ✅
  • This is the resolution the Gemara ultimately accepts

Rabba ✗

  • Satisfies: Change of sanctity (שינוי קודש)
  • Does not satisfy: Change of owner (שינוי בעלים)
  • Problem: Mishna discusses change of sanctity yet rules it does not satisfy — directly contradicts Rabba's resolution ✗
  • Abaye presses this objection against Rabba

Rava ✗

  • Satisfies: All rites performed with the same wrong intent throughout
  • Does not satisfy: Mixed intent — some rites lishmah, some not
  • Problem: Mishna discusses consistent single wrong-intent throughout — yet rules it does not satisfy ✗
  • Rava's resolution also cannot explain the Mishna

The Word 'But' (אלא) — A Key Talmudic Derivation The Mishna rules that rites done shelo lishmah are fit — but (אלא) they did not satisfy the owner's obligation. The Gemara asks: why add "but"? The word teaches that the only deficiency is non-fulfillment of obligation. The offering itself is fully valid and binding — it is still prohibited (אסור לשנות) to repurpose or deviate from it afterward. The remainder (שיריים) must still be eaten as a meal offering by the priests. Proof-verse (Deut. 23:24): "That which has gone out of your lips you shall observe and do... that which you have promised with your mouth." The Gemara: Even a gift offering already sacrificed (with wrong intent) may not be altered — because the vow still stands. So too, a shelo lishmah mincha remains a valid mincha.
