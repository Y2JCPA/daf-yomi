✋ When Must the Right Hand Be Used in Temple Service?

Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish (via Rabba bar bar Ḥana):
Wherever finger or priesthood is mentioned in a verse, only the right hand may be used
↓
Rava's ruling
Either finger alone OR priesthood alone suffices — but only for rites indispensable to atonement
Rabbi Shimon's position
Both finger AND priesthood must appear together in the verse to mandate right hand
↓
Gemara tests with blood collection and sprinkling → each position resolved through careful analysis of superfluous verses

🫙 Two Verses, One Law: Oil Placement on the Leper

Verse 1 (Lev. 14:17 — wealthy leper):
"Upon the blood of the guilt offering"
Verse 2 (Lev. 14:28 — poor leper):
"Upon the place of the blood of the guilt offering"
↓
If only Verse 1 existed
Oil only when blood is still present on thumb/toe — but if blood is gone, no oil at all?
If only Verse 2 existed
Oil only when blood has been wiped away — but if blood still present, no oil?
↓
Both verses are needed: the oil is placed whether or not the blood remains on the right thumb and big toe

✋ Does 'Priesthood' Alone Require the Right Hand?

Rava
Wherever finger OR priesthood appears in a verse, the left hand is disqualified — either one alone is sufficient to require the right hand.
Abaye
But conveyance of the daily burnt-offering limbs to the altar ramp — priesthood is written there, yet it can be performed with the left hand!
Rava
That rule applies only to rites indispensable to atonement. Conveying limbs does not preclude atonement if omitted.
Abaye
Then consider blood collection — it IS indispensable to atonement and priesthood is written: 'Aaron's sons, the priests, shall present the blood' (Lev. 1:5)!
Gemara
That difficulty only arises for Rava. Rabbi Shimon requires both finger AND priesthood together — he explains the superfluous priestly mention in the blood-collection verse differently.
Conclusion The right-hand requirement (per Rava) applies to atonement-indispensable rites where 'finger' or 'priesthood' appears in the verse.

👐 Wealthy Leper vs Poor Leper: The Oil Rites

Wealthy Leper (Lev. 14:14–17)

  • Brings a guilt-offering lamb
  • Blood placed on right ear, right thumb, right big toe
  • Oil placed upon the blood (v. 17)
  • Uses a full log of oil
  • 'Right foot' → verbal analogy for yavam (ḥalitzah ceremony)
  • 'Right ear' → verbal analogy for Canaanite slave (ear-piercing at doorpost)

Poor Leper (Lev. 14:25–28)

  • Brings a guilt-offering bird or lamb
  • Blood placed on right ear, right thumb, right big toe
  • Oil placed upon the place of the blood (v. 28)
  • Left hand explicitly mentioned (vv. 26–27)
  • Left-hand reference comes to disqualify the priest's right hand — if the priest uses his right hand for the poor leper's oil rite, it is invalid

🔗 Verbal Analogies Derived from the Leper Passage

"Right foot" (Lev. 14:17) — verbal analogy (gezerah shavah) to the yavam: just as the leper's right foot is specified, so too the yavam's right foot is used during ḥalitzah (the levirate dissolution ceremony)
↓
"Right ear" (Lev. 14:17) — verbal analogy to the Canaanite slave who wishes to remain: just as the leper's right ear, so too the slave's right ear is pierced at the doorpost (Exod. 21:6)
↓
First "hand" in leper passage — verbal analogy to removal of the kometz (handful) from a meal offering: the handful must be scooped with the right hand
↓
Second "hand" in leper passage — verbal analogy to sanctification of the handful (placing it into a service vessel): this too must be performed with the right hand
↓
Rabbi Yehuda son of Rabbi Ḥiyya: The meal offering is compared by verse to sin and guilt offerings — therefore burning the handful must be performed with the right hand, just as those offerings require right-hand service
