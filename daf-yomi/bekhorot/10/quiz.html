QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the Torah need two separate verses about oil placement for the leper — 'upon the blood' (Lev. 14:17) and 'upon the place of the blood' (Lev. 14:28)?
A. One applies to the right thumb and the other to the right big toe
B. Each verse covers a different scenario: oil when blood is still present, and oil when blood has been removed
C. The second verse is a general repetition for emphasis with no new law
D. One verse applies to a wealthy leper and the other to a poor leper in terms of quantity only
Correct: B — The Gemara explains: if only 'upon the blood' existed, one might think oil is placed only while blood is present; if only 'upon the place' existed, one might think oil is placed only after blood is removed. Both verses together teach that oil is placed regardless of whether the blood remains.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rava, what additional condition must be met for 'priesthood' mentioned in a verse to disqualify use of the left hand?
A. The verse must also explicitly mention the right hand
B. The verse must also mention a finger alongside priesthood
C. The rite must be indispensable to atonement
D. The rite must involve direct contact with blood
Correct: C — Rava limits the right-hand requirement to rites that preclude atonement if omitted (indispensable rites). This resolves Abaye's challenge from conveyance of limbs to the altar ramp — that rite is not indispensable to atonement, so priesthood there does not require the right hand.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What law does the Torah's explicit mention of the poor leper's left hand (Lev. 14:26–27) teach?
A. That a poor leper may perform the oil rite using his own left hand
B. That the priest must use both hands simultaneously for the oil rite
C. That the priest's right hand is disqualified — if he uses his right hand for the poor leper's oil rite, it is invalid
D. That a left-handed priest must use his stronger (left) hand
Correct: C — Rav Sheisha son of Rav Idi explains: the Torah specifies the left hand for the poor leper's oil rite precisely to disqualify the priest's right hand. If a priest performs this ritual with his right hand rather than his left, the rite is invalid.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
From where does Rabbi Yehuda son of Rabbi Ḥiyya derive that burning the kometz on the altar must be done with the right hand?
A. From the explicit phrase 'right hand' in the meal offering passage
B. From the verbal analogy comparing 'hand' in the leper passage to 'hand' of the meal offering
C. From a comparison of the meal offering to sin and guilt offerings, whose service requires the right hand
D. From the general principle that all Temple service requires the right hand
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehuda son of Rabbi Ḥiyya derives it from the verse that compares the meal offering to a sin offering and a guilt offering. Since those require right-hand service, so too the burning of the kometz must be performed with the right hand.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What does the verbal analogy from the word 'foot' in the wealthy leper passage (Lev. 14:17) teach?
A. That the kometz must be presented using the right foot
B. That the yavam's right foot is used in the ḥalitzah (levirate dissolution) ceremony
C. That the poor leper's oil is applied to his left foot
D. That priests must stand on their right foot when performing any service
Correct: B — The Gemara derives a gezerah shavah (verbal analogy) from 'foot' written in the wealthy leper passage to 'foot' written in the ḥalitzah ceremony, teaching that the yavam's right foot — not the left — is used when a widow removes the shoe during ḥalitzah.
