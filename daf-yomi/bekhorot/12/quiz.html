QUESTION 1 OF 5
Which of the following correctly lists all four service acts that can render a meal offering piggul if performed with improper intent?
A. Slaughter, sprinkling blood, burning the handful, eating the remainder
B. Removal of the handful, placing in a service vessel, conveying to the altar, burning the handful
C. Grinding, sifting, removal of handful, placing oil
D. Placing oil, mixing, removal of handful, conveying to altar
Correct: B — The Mishna specifies four acts analogous to the four blood-service acts of animal offerings: קְמִיצָה (removing the handful), placing it in the service vessel, conveying it to the altar, and הֶקְטֵרָה (burning it on the altar).
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the core disagreement between Rav Huna and Rava regarding a remainder that became lacking in measure?
A. Rav Huna says it can be piggul; Rava says it cannot
B. Both agree it cannot be rendered piggul under any opinion
C. Rav Huna says it cannot be piggul; Rava says it can
D. Both agree it can be rendered piggul under all opinions
Correct: C — Rav Huna holds the lacking remainder CANNOT be rendered piggul — its deficiency is intrinsic to the meat itself. Rava counters that it CAN be rendered piggul, comparing the lack to leaving the courtyard, which he treats as an external disqualification.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does Abaye say Rabbi Ḥiyya omitted 'or an olive-bulk of its handful' — rather than because the remainder was already lacking?
A. Because frankincense has no minimum measure requirement
B. Because Rabbi Ḥiyya follows Rabbi Elazar, who rules one is not liable for burning less than the full handful
C. Because the handful is always smaller than an olive-bulk by definition
D. Because Rabbi Ḥiyya holds piggul does not apply to meal offerings at all
Correct: B — Abaye demonstrates that Rabbi Ḥiyya follows Rabbi Elazar's opinion requiring the full handful for liability — not a partial olive-bulk. Therefore the omission reflects Rabbi Elazar's ruling, not evidence that a lacking remainder can be rendered piggul.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to the Mishna on 12b, if a priest intended to eat half an olive-bulk of the remainder and to burn half an olive-bulk outside its designated time, the offering is:
A. Piggul — one who eats is liable for karet
B. Pasul (invalid) but no karet liability
C. Fit (כָּשֵׁר) — because eating and burning intent do not combine
D. Pasul — because even partial improper intent during burning disqualifies
Correct: C — The Mishna rules the offering is כָּשֵׁר (fit) because אֲכִילָה (eating) and הֶקְטֵרָה (burning) are distinct modes of consumption. They cannot be combined to form the requisite olive-bulk of improper intent — each must independently reach an olive-bulk.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rabbi Yehuda's principle regarding combined time-and-area intent states that piggul with karet liability results when:
A. Intent regarding the area precedes intent regarding the time
B. Both intents occur simultaneously in a single service act
C. Intent regarding the time precedes intent regarding the area
D. The priest performs all four acts with identical improper intent
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehuda rules: if מַחֲשֶׁבֶת זְמַן (time-intent — eating the next day) precedes מַחֲשֶׁבֶת מָקוֹם (area-intent — eating outside the designated place), the offering is piggul with karet. If area-intent precedes time-intent, the offering is merely pasul.
