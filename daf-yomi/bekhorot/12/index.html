🔥 Can a Lacking Remainder Be Rendered Piggul?

Rav Huna
Even Rabbi Akiva agrees that burning a lacking remainder is NOT effective for piggul. The remainder itself is intrinsically diminished — this is not a mere external disqualification.
Rava
On the contrary — even Rabbi Eliezer would agree a lacking remainder CAN be rendered piggul. A lack in measure is like leaving the courtyard — an external factor, not an intrinsic flaw in the meat itself.
Rav Huna
The meat that left the courtyard remains as it was — the disqualification is from outside. But a lacking remainder is changed intrinsically. These cases cannot be compared!
Rava
I prove from Rabbi Ḥiyya's mishna: he omits 'or an olive-bulk' for the remainder because it already stands at less than an olive-bulk — proving such remainders can still be rendered piggul.
Core Dilemma (12a) If the remainder of a meal offering becomes lacking in measure between removal of the handful and its burning on the altar — can the burning still render it piggul and exempt it from misuse of consecrated property (מְעִילָה)?

⚡ Permitting Factor: Sacrificed כְּמִצְוָתוֹ vs. שֶׁלֹּא כְּמִצְוָתוֹ

✅ Sacrificed Properly — כְּמִצְוָתוֹ

  • Handful removed in silence — no improper intent
  • Placed in vessel: no improper intent
  • Conveyed to altar: no improper intent
  • Burned on altar with improper intent (e.g., eat outside its area)
  • → Piggul: one who eats the remainder is liable for karet
  • The permitting factor was valid at outset; improper intent at burning disqualifies

❌ Not Sacrificed Properly — שֶׁלֹּא כְּמִצְוָתוֹ

  • Handful removed with improper intent (eat outside its area)
  • Then placed, conveyed, and burned with intent to eat next day
  • Permitting factor already disqualified at first act
  • → Pasul: offering is invalid
  • → No piggul: no liability for karet
  • The second improper intent cannot create piggul atop an already-disqualified service

🏛️ Abaye Challenges Rava's Proof from Rabbi Ḥiyya's Mishna

Rava
Proof from Rabbi Ḥiyya's mishna! He omits 'or an olive-bulk' regarding the remainder's consumption — because the remainder already stands at less than an olive-bulk. This shows lacking remainders CAN be rendered piggul.
Abaye
No proof! Rabbi Ḥiyya follows Rabbi Elazar, who rules one is not liable for burning less than a full handful. So he could not write 'olive-bulk of its handful' — not because the remainder was lacking!
Rava
If Rabbi Ḥiyya follows Rabbi Elazar, then the text should read 'to burn its handful AND its frankincense outside' — why does he omit the frankincense entirely?
Abaye
Rabbi Ḥiyya specifically addresses the meal offering of a sinner (מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא), which has no frankincense at all! That is why the unusual phrasing is fully justified — no proof for Rava.
Resolution (12b) Rava concedes the proof is defeated and reformulates his position based on a baraita about shewbread — but Abaye challenges that proof as well, invoking the distinction between the frontplate's power of acceptance for impurity vs. for bread that left the Temple.

⚖️ Eating and Burning Do Not Join Together

אֲכִילָה וּהֶקְטֵרָה אֵינָן מִצְטָרְפוֹת — Eating and burning do not combine
MISHNA — Menachot 12b If a priest intended to eat half an olive-bulk of the remainder and to burn half an olive-bulk of it outside its designated time or area — the offering is כָּשֵׁר (fit). Why? Eating (אֲכִילָה) and burning on the altar (הֶקְטֵרָה) are fundamentally distinct modes of consumption. An olive-bulk of improper intent must consist of the same type of act. Half-intent-to-eat and half-intent-to-burn cannot merge into one complete disqualifying unit. Abaye: This ruling holds even according to the Rabbis — not only Rabbi Eliezer. The mishna teaches it as an independent, universally accepted principle. Rabbi Yirmeya had suggested this mishna follows only Rabbi Eliezer's lenient view, but Abaye broadens its application to all opinions.
