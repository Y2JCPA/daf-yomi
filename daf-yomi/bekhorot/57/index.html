🔥 Turning Meat on Shabbat: The Core Dispute

Rabbi Yochanan
If one placed meat on coals and turned it over, he is liable for cooking. If he did not turn it over, he is exempt — roasting one side alone is insufficient to constitute prohibited cooking.
Rava
Even without turning, if a fig-bulk is fully roasted on one side in a single concentrated spot, he is liable — just as drilling a single hole on Shabbat creates full liability regardless of size.
Rava (second version)
Even if the fig-bulk of fully roasted meat is distributed across two or three separate spots, he is still liable — completeness of result, not location, determines the cooking prohibition.
Ben Derosai Standard Food cooked to ⅓ of the usual cooking process is 'the food of ben Derosai' — minimally edible, and a recognized threshold for Shabbat liability.

📏 Were the Dry Measuring Vessels Consecrated?

Rabbi Yoshiya

  • Dry measuring vessels were anointed and consecrated
  • Shewbread is consecrated when placed into the dry mold
  • Vessels anointed both inside and outside
  • Supported by Rabbi Yosei HaGelili (per Rabbi Yochanan's version)

Rabbi Yonatan

  • Dry measuring vessels were NOT consecrated by anointing
  • Shewbread only consecrated when placed on the Table itself
  • Proof: these vessels do not consecrate items inside them
  • Liquid vessels anointed inside but not outside

Rav Pappa's Challenge to Rabbi Yonatan

  • If dry vessels unconsecrated, when is shewbread first consecrated?
  • Shewbread kneaded on a leather spread, not in a vessel
  • Torah requires no specific receptacle for kneading
  • Therefore: consecration must occur on the Table itself

⚗️ Rav Mari's Dilemma: Leavening Atop the Altar

"אֲשֶׁר תַּקְרִיבוּ לַה'" — That you shall bring to the Lord (Vayikra 2:11)
Unresolved Dilemma A priest stands at the top of the altar and leavens a meal offering. Is he liable?

Argument for liable: Any offering whose burning has not yet been performed is considered as if the sacrifice is incomplete — still within the scope of the prohibition.

Argument for exempt: "That you shall bring to the Lord" — once the offering reaches the top of the altar, the act of bringing is complete. The prohibition no longer applies.

The Gemara does not resolve this dilemma.
