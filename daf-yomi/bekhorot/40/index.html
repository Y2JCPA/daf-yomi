🏛️ The Great Dispute: Linen Cloak & Tzitzit

Beit Shammai
A linen cloak is EXEMPT from tzitzit. Tekhelet (sky-blue) strings must be wool — attaching wool to linen violates kilayim (mixed fibers), and the positive mitzva does not override it.
Beit Hillel
A linen cloak is OBLIGATED in tzitzit. The positive mitzva of tzitzit overrides the kilayim prohibition — halakha follows this view.
R. Eliezer b. R. Tzadok
Anyone who affixes sky-blue strings to a linen cloak in Jerusalem is considered one who causes astonishment — it appears unseemly to observers.
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
Even granting Beit Hillel's ruling, the Sages prohibited affixing ritual fringes to linen garments — the Gemara investigates the real reason for this decree.
The Central Question of Daf 40 Halakha follows Beit Hillel (linen requires tzitzit), yet a rabbinic decree prohibits it. Why? The entire daf unfolds Rava's answer: the fear of ta'aseh v'lo min he'asuy — strings attached after a rip are invalid, and reusing them violates kilayim.

🏛️ Beit Shammai vs. Beit Hillel: Linen Cloak Tzitzit

Beit Shammai — Exempt

  • Linen cloak is exempt from tzitzit
  • Tekhelet = wool; wool on linen = kilayim violation
  • Positive mitzva does not override this prohibition
  • White linen strings alone cannot fulfill the obligation

Beit Hillel — Obligated

  • Linen cloak is obligated in tzitzit
  • Positive mitzva of tzitzit overrides the kilayim prohibition
  • Reish Lakish: where possible, fulfill both; mitzva takes precedence
  • Halakha follows Beit Hillel — but a rabbinic decree still intervenes

✂️ Ta'aseh v'Lo Min He'asuy

תַּעֲשֶׂה וְלֹא מִן הֶעָשׂוּי — דברים כב:יב
Core Principle — Deuteronomy 22:12 "You shall prepare yourself twisted cords" — ta'aseh, you shall make it; v'lo min he'asuy, and not from what has already been prepared.

When a linen cloak rips within 3 fingerbreadths of the corner, the corner is severed. Existing tzitzit strings become invalid. If one reattaches those very same strings to the repaired corner, they were "prepared" before that corner existed — violating this rule and constituting kilayim without any mitzva purpose.

Because of this decree, Rabbi Zeira personally untied and removed his tzitzit from his linen cloak.

🔄 Tzitzit on a Three-Cornered Garment: Valid After Completion?

Rava (Rav Seḥora/Rav Huna)
If tzitzit were affixed to a 3-cornered garment (exempt), and one then completed the 4th corner — the tzitzit are valid. Completing the corner retroactively obligates the garment and validates the attachment.
Baraita (Pious Early Generations)
The early pious men affixed white and sky-blue strings after weaving only 3 fingerbreadths of the garment — well before it was complete! This suggests early attachment is valid, seemingly contradicting ta'aseh v'lo min he'asuy.
Rava (Resolution)
When one adds a second set of tzitzit to an already-fulfilled garment, one violates 'do not add to the mitzvot' — this very act validates the original strings retroactively, counting as an action of preparation.
Rav Pappa (Challenge)
From where do we know Rabbi Zeira meant a case of adding strings? Perhaps he meant a case where one intended to nullify the original strings — the question of intent remains unresolved.
Rabbi Zeira's Related Ruling White and sky-blue strings affixed to an exempt garment are not subject to the prohibition of diverse kinds (kilayim) — since no mitzva obligation exists there, the mitzva exception does not apply, yet the strings themselves are not forbidden.
