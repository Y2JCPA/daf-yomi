🐐 'Of the Goat' — Laying of Hands on Communal Sin Offerings

Tanna Kamma
The phrase 'of the goat' (מן העזים) includes goats brought as communal sin offerings for idol worship in the requirement of laying hands on the offering's head.
Ravina
This works well according to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that Nahshon's offering was included in the laying-of-hands requirement. But according to Rabbi Shimon — what is the derivation?
Mar Zutra b. Rav Mari
Even for Rabbi Yehuda: for what Nahshon's offering was included (laying of hands), it is included; for what it was not included (northward slaughter), it is not. So 'of the goat' cannot derive northward slaughter from Nahshon's offering.
Key Term מן העזים — 'of the goat' extends certain Temple laws to communal sin offerings, but the scope of inclusion must be carefully bounded

🗡️ Must the Slaughterer Also Stand in the North?

Initial reading of 'it': The animal must be in the north, but the slaughterer need not stand there
↓
Challenge from Rabbi Aḥiyya's baraita: The burnt offering verse already teaches the slaughterer need not stand in the north — so 'it' seems redundant!
↓
Clarification: Without the verse one might think: since the priest must stand in the north to collect blood, perhaps the slaughterer must also stand there — 'it' excludes this requirement
↓
Conclusion: 'It' = necessary to distinguish slaughter position from blood-collection position — both are independent obligations

🕊️ Which Offerings Require Northward Slaughter?

Requires North (צפון)

  • Sin offering (חטאת) — goat of the king
  • Burnt offering (עולה) — entirely consumed on altar
  • Guilt offering (אשם) — atones; highly stringent
  • Communal sin offerings for idol worship

Does NOT Require North

  • Bird offerings — killed by melikah, not slaughter
  • Paschal offering (קרבן פסח) — excluded by 'it'
  • The slaughterer himself need not stand in north
  • Offerings of princes — excluded by separate verse

Rejected Derivations for Paschal Offering

  • From burnt offering — refuted: burnt offering entirely consumed (כליל)
  • From sin offering — refuted: sin offering atones for karet-level sins
  • From guilt offering — refuted: guilt offering has unique stringencies
  • All three-way combinations (kelal u'ferat) also fail

🐄 Firstborn with Constricted Blood — May One Cause a Blemish?

Rabbis
One may let the firstborn's blood even by cutting at a site that causes a permanent blemish — provided one does not then slaughter the animal on the basis of that same blemish.
Rabbis (56b, extended)
Once the blemish is established through blood-letting, the animal may thereafter be slaughtered on the basis of that blemish.
Rabbi Yehuda
Even if the firstborn would die without the procedure, one may not let its blood at all. The prohibition against causing a blemish on a consecrated animal is absolute.
Rabbi Meir
Derived from 'there shall be no blemish in it' (לא יהיה בו מום): even inflicting a blemish on an already-blemished consecrated animal is forbidden — the prohibition applies continuously.
Unanimous Rule (Rabbi Yoḥanan) All Sages agree: castrating a consecrated animal after another has already castrated it still incurs full liability — 'you shall not do this in your land' (Lev. 22:24) prohibits the act regardless of prior state
