🔥 Does Burning the Handful with Frankincense-Intent Create Piggul?

Rava
Burning DOES render burning piggul — burning the handful with intent to burn the frankincense the next day renders the meal offering piggul, even according to the Rabbis. I prove this from the mishna which lists burning alongside the other service acts.
Rav Ḥisda citing Rav
Burning does NOT render burning piggul. Rabbi Meir's rule applies only where the handful — the permitting factor of the remainder — is the object of improper intent. The frankincense is not the permitting factor for the remainder, so intent about it cannot create piggul.
Final Clarification (Rav Adda bar Ahava) Rabbi Ḥanina holds: burning alone does not render burning piggul. But if one burns BOTH the handful and the frankincense with intent to eat the remainder the next day, the offering IS piggul — the frankincense-burning completes the permitting act and locks in the improper intent.

🗣️ Rava's Proof Challenged — Burning vs. Other Service Acts

Rava
The mishna (12a) lists four acts that render piggul: removing the handful, placing it in a vessel, conveying it to the altar, and burning. For all four, whether one's intent was to eat the remainder OR to burn the frankincense the next day — it is piggul.
Gemara (Rejection)
No proof! For the first three acts — removal, placement, conveyance — both types of intent equally render piggul. But burning is different: only intent to eat the remainder creates piggul. Intent to burn the frankincense next day does not.
Rav Menashya bar Gadda (Rav Ḥisda/Rav)
Rabbi Meir's piggul ruling applies where the handful is the permitting factor and one intends to eat the remainder. Here, burning the handful with intent for the frankincense — the frankincense is not what permits the remainder — so no piggul is created.
Key Principle Piggul intent must concern the relationship between the permitting act and the thing it permits. The handful permits the remainder — not the frankincense.

⚖️ Mishna Dispute: Intent for Unusual Consumption or Burning

Rabbi Eliezer

  • Intent to consume beyond its time an item not usually consumed → offering is unfit
  • Intent to burn beyond its time an item not usually burned → offering is unfit
  • Verse 'he'akhol ye'akhel' (mixed verb forms) equates unusual burning with unusual consuming
  • Two different forms teach two distinct laws: each type of unusual intent independently invalidates
  • Tannaitic dispute: some say R. Eliezer means unfit by Torah with karet; others say unfit but no karet

The Rabbis

  • Intent for unusual consumption does not render the offering piggul
  • Verse teaches: 'consuming' language and 'burning' language are interchangeable for piggul purposes
  • Verse also teaches: minimum measure for piggul-intent is an olive-bulk (as with eating)
  • Unusual intent (burning what is eaten, eating what is burned) = offering is merely unfit, not piggul
  • The two verb forms (infinitive + future) need not teach separate laws about unusual intent

🕯️ Rav Hamnuna on Rabbi Ḥanina — A Teaching Worth All His Learning The case: One burned the handful with intent to burn the frankincense the next day — and then actually burned the frankincense.

Rabbi Ḥanina rules: the offering IS piggul.

What is the novelty? Rav Adda bar Ahava explains that Rabbi Ḥanina holds two things simultaneously:
① Burning the handful alone with frankincense-intent does not create piggul (burning does not render burning piggul)
② But when the frankincense is subsequently burned, that act completes the permitting service — and the improper intent expressed during the handful's burning now attaches to this completed act, rendering the offering piggul.

The novelty: the intent was formed during the handful but takes effect through the frankincense-burning.
