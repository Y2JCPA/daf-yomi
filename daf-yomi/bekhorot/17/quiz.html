Bekhorot 17 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Ḥisda in the name of Rav, what is the ruling if a kohen burns the handful with intent to burn the frankincense the next day?
A. The offering is piggul and the one who eats it is liable to karet
B. The offering is not piggul
C. The offering is unfit but not piggul
D. The offering is completely valid
Correct: B — Rav Ḥisda explicitly states in Rav's name: burning does not render burning piggul. The frankincense is not the permitting factor for the remainder, so intent about it cannot create piggul.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Rav Menashya bar Gadda say that burning the handful with frankincense-intent does NOT create piggul?
A. Because frankincense is not a valid sacrifice at all
B. Because Rabbi Meir's piggul rule applies only where the handful — the permitting factor — is burned with intent regarding the remainder it permits
C. Because the kohen must verbalize his intent for it to be legally significant
D. Because the frankincense must be burned before the handful for piggul to apply
Correct: B — Rav Menashya explains that Rabbi Meir's piggul ruling covers intent about the handful (permitting factor) regarding the remainder (what it permits). Frankincense is not the permitting factor for the remainder, so this case falls outside the rule.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the novelty in Rabbi Ḥanina's ruling as explained by Rav Adda bar Ahava?
A. That burning always renders burning piggul, contradicting Rav
B. That the offering is piggul only if both the handful and frankincense are burned with intent to eat the remainder the next day
C. That intent during frankincense-burning is irrelevant; only handful-burning intent matters
D. That the two-step process of burning handful then frankincense is itself forbidden
Correct: B — Rabbi Ḥanina holds two things: (1) burning alone does not render burning piggul, so the handful's burning with frankincense-intent alone is insufficient. (2) But when the frankincense is subsequently burned — completing the permitting service — improper intent expressed during the handful attaches, and the offering becomes piggul.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In the Mishna (17a), what does Rabbi Eliezer rule about one who removes the handful with intent to consume — beyond its time — something whose manner is NOT to be consumed?
A. The offering is valid and permitted to be eaten
B. The offering is piggul and the intent is fully equivalent to normal piggul
C. The offering is unfit (pasul)
D. The offering must be re-offered the same day
Correct: C — Rabbi Eliezer rules the offering is unfit (pasul). A tannaitic dispute exists over whether this constitutes piggul with karet liability or merely unfitness — but the basic ruling of invalidity belongs to Rabbi Eliezer.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What do the Rabbis derive from the doubled verb forms 'he'akhol ye'akhel' in the verse about piggul?
A. That burning and eating are completely identical for all halakhic purposes
B. That unusual intent (consuming what is burned, burning what is consumed) always creates piggul
C. That the language of consumption and burning are interchangeable for piggul, and that the minimum piggul-measure is an olive-bulk
D. That only consuming language — not burning language — can create piggul intent
Correct: C — The Rabbis derive two lessons: (1) whether the priest frames his intent as 'consumption on the altar' or 'burning on the altar,' either language creates piggul; and (2) just as eating requires an olive-bulk, so too piggul-intent requires intent to consume/burn an olive-bulk.
