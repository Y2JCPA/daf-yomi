🔥 The Piggul Debate: Handful vs. Frankincense

Rabbi Meir
If a priest has piggul intent during the burning of either the handful or the frankincense, the meal offering becomes piggul — even if only one of the two permitting factors was performed with such intent.
Rabbis
The meal offering is only piggul if one has piggul intent during the burning of both the handful and the frankincense. Intent during one permitting factor alone is insufficient.
Mishna Exception In the meal offering of a sinner (מנחת חוטא) and the sota jealousy offering, the Rabbis concede to Rabbi Meir: the handful is the sole permitting factor (no frankincense), so intent during the handful alone renders it piggul.

🏺 Conveying the Handful — Two Opinions

Reish Lakish
Conveying (הולכה) the handful is not a permitting factor. Therefore, piggul intent during conveying the handful alone does not render the offering piggul — the frankincense must also be conveyed with intent.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
The law of conveying the handful follows the law of removing (קמיצה). Just as intent during removal of the handful alone renders it piggul (sole permitting factor at that stage), so too intent during conveying the handful alone suffices.
Rava Explains Rabbi Yoḥanan Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that any rite which does not itself permit the offering — like conveying — if performed with intent on even one of the two permitting factors (handful, not frankincense), still renders the offering piggul, since the sacrificial chain is engaged.

🐑 Piggul Intent and the Shavuot Lambs

Lamb A slaughtered with intent to eat Loaves next day

  • One of the two lambs is slaughtered with piggul intent for the two loaves
  • Ruling: That lamb is piggul; the other lamb remains fit
  • The two loaves are not sanctified yet — they remain permissible
  • Similarly: one bowl of frankincense with intent for shewbread → that bowl is piggul, the other is fit

Lamb A slaughtered with intent to eat Lamb B next day

  • Slaughtering Lamb A with intent to consume Lamb B the next day
  • Ruling: Both lambs are fit (כשרות) — no piggul at all
  • Reason: Piggul intent must concern the permitting factor of the offering being sacrificed
  • Lamb B's flesh does not permit Lamb A — so the intent is legally irrelevant

🌱 Sesame-Seed Piggul: Partial Burning and Incremental Intent

Scenario: A priest repeatedly burns a sesame-seed-sized (כשומשום) portion of the handful and frankincense, each time intending to eat a sesame-seed-sized amount of the remainder the next day
↓
Question: Can such incremental piggul intent — never intending a full olive-bulk (כזית) at once — render the offering piggul?
↓
View 1 — Piggul: The manner of burning is in small portions; the manner of eating is therefore also in small portions — this is a valid piggul intent
↓
View 2 — Not Piggul: Since the intent was never for an olive-bulk at one time, it does not constitute actionable piggul intent and the offering remains valid
↓
Gemara's conclusion: The dispute cannot be resolved by mapping it onto Rabbi Meir, the Rabbis, or Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi — each opinion may apply only in its own specific context. The sesame-seed case remains an unresolved question (תיקו).
