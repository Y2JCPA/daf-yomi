⚔️ Does Slaughter Alone Consecrate the Loaves?

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
Slaughter alone partially consecrates the loaves. The verse uses zevaḥ (slaughter) — indicating consecration begins at the moment of slaughter, before the blood is sprinkled.
Rabbi Elazar b. Rabbi Shimon
Only slaughter + sprinkling together consecrate the loaves. The verse says ve-hikriv (he shall offer) — the complete sacrificial act is required.
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
If only ve-hikriv were written, I would agree. But the separate term zevaḥ teaches that slaughter alone already begins the consecration process.
Rabbi Elazar b. Rabbi Shimon
I need zevaḥ for Rabbi Yoḥanan's teaching — that the Shavuot sheep form a bond with the loaves, analogous to the nazirite's ram with its basket of bread.
Source Verse Derived from the nazirite: "He shall offer the ram as a peace offering [zevaḥ shlamim] to the Lord, with the basket of unleavened bread" (Numbers 6:17)

📊 Practical Differences: Partial vs. No Pre-Sprinkling Consecration

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi

  • Slaughter alone = partial consecration
  • Loaves rendered unfit if they leave the courtyard after slaughter
  • Loaves can transfer sanctity to their substitutes after slaughter
  • Slaughter for own sake + sprinkling not for own sake → partial (or full per Rava)
  • Sprinkling for own sake completes full consecration

Rabbi Elazar b. Rabbi Shimon

  • Slaughter alone = no consecration whatsoever
  • Loaves leaving courtyard before sprinkling: no effect (not yet holy)
  • Loaves cannot transfer sanctity before sprinkling
  • Only slaughter + sprinkling together produce consecration
  • Practical difference from R. Yehuda HaNasi: the yatza question

🔥 Rabbi Eliezer vs. Rabbi Akiva: Loaves Outside the Courtyard

Rav Sheshet
Both Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva follow Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi — slaughter alone consecrates the loaves. Their dispute is about sprinkling when loaves have left the courtyard.
Rabbi Eliezer
Once sacrificial portions left the courtyard, sprinkling is completely void. The loaves cannot be eaten and are not subject to the laws of piggul or notar.
Rabbi Akiva
Sprinkling is still effective even after the loaves left the courtyard. One is liable for misuse, piggul, notar, and eating while ritually impure.
Rav Pappa
Objection! Perhaps they dispute only when the loaves returned to the courtyard. Maybe Rabbi Akiva follows Rabbi Elazar b. Rabbi Shimon — since loaves weren't yet consecrated when they left, they weren't disqualified!
Mishna Source Mishna Me'ila 6b: sacrificial portions of lesser-sanctity offerings that left the Temple courtyard before sprinkling

🍞 The Dilemma of Lost Loaves After Slaughter

The Case (Rabbi Yirmeya to Rabbi Zeira): Two Shavuot sheep are slaughtered for their own sake, establishing a bond with the loaves — then the loaves are lost. Can the sheep now be slaughtered again for their own sake?
↓
First clarification: Rabbi Zeira asks — do you have any case where something is fit when done for its own sake, but then becomes unfit for its own sake? That would be unprecedented.
↓
Precedent raised: The Paschal offering — fit for its own sake at Pesach time, but must be brought as a peace offering (not for its own sake) after its designated time passes.
↓
Refined dilemma: Something already slaughtered for its own sake: can it become rejected from being slaughtered again for its own sake when circumstances change (loaves lost)?
↓
Second precedent: The thanks offering — if its loaves break and are disqualified, the offering can still be sacrificed (see Daf 46a).
↓
Rabbi Zeira's distinction: The thanks offering is called shlamim (peace offering) by the Torah — and peace offerings are valid without loaves. The Shavuot sheep are fundamentally loaf-dependent; without loaves, the act is incomplete.
