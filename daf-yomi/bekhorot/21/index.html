↓
Requires Salt
Meal offerings (minḥa)
Limbs of burnt offering on altar
Excluded by Verse
Wine libations — sprinkled, not burned
Blood — sprinkled on altar corner
Wood — not susceptible to impurity
Incense — offered on inner altar

🧂 Salt Required vs. Salt Excluded

Requires Salt

  • All meal offerings (minḥa)
  • Limbs of the burnt offering before the altar
  • Anything brought upon the altar fire
  • Salt covenant applies to all standard offerings

Excluded by Biblical Derivation

  • Wood — not susceptible to ritual impurity
  • Wine libations — sprinkled on altar base, not burned
  • Blood — sprinkled on corners of the altar
  • Incense — brought on the inner golden altar separately

🩸 Cooked Blood: Prohibition or Not?

Ze'eiri
Blood that was cooked (curdled by fire) — one does not transgress a prohibition by drinking it
Abaye
Objection! A baraita states that blood curdled in the sun is still prohibited — how is fire-curdled blood any different?
Rava
No contradiction: fire-curdled blood is disqualified from the altar entirely, so the blood prohibition no longer applies. Sun-curdled blood retains altar fitness.
Abaye
But sun-curdled blood is also disqualified! Rabbi Mani raised this: does disqualification from the altar remove the prohibition against consuming blood?
Rava
The baraita discusses blood of external sin offerings; Ze'eiri discusses inner sin offering blood — the rules differ between them
Key Distinction Inner sin offering blood cooked by fire: no prohibition. External sin offering blood congealed by sun: prohibition remains.

🫙 Congealed Blood: When Is One Liable?

Blood became congealed — was it eaten?
↓
External Sin Offering
Rav Ḥisda: Liable — verse says 'priest shall take the blood'; congealed blood is still suitable for sprinkling on the external altar
Inner Sin Offering
Rava: Also liable — its blood corresponds to blood that is suitable for use in external offerings, so the prohibition applies
↓
Rav Pappa's corollary: Even blood that dripped from the slaughtering knife is subject to the blood prohibition — it was once altar-suitable

⚖️ Proper Salting of Offerings: How Much Is Enough?

Verse: "You shall season" (temlach) — establishes that salt must be applied to every meal offering
↓
First limit (Rabba bar Ulla): Do not mix in massive quantities of salt, like straw (teven) kneaded into clay — this interpretation is rejected
↓
Second limit (Rav Ashi): Do not infuse the entire offering with salt taste throughout, as bina (understanding) penetrates and transforms a person
↓
Practical ruling by Abaye: Salt both sides of meat, let the blood drain, rinse, then cook — this constitutes proper salting
↓
Best salt (Sodomite salt): The verse says 'do not omit salt' — use salt that is always present, i.e., Sodomite salt kept continuously in the Temple
