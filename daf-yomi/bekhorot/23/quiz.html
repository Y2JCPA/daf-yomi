QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Reish Lakish, what is the halakha if oil was added to the handful removed from a sinner's meal offering?
A. The offering is completely invalid — the verse forbids all oil contact
B. The handful is fit — the verse governs initial preparation, not what happens after removal
C. The offering must be repeated with a new handful taken without oil
D. The priest is liable for misuse of sacred items (me'ilah)
Correct: B — Reish Lakish holds the handful remains fit. The prohibition 'he shall put no oil upon it' (Lev. 5:11) addresses how the offering is initially prepared, not subsequent mixing of oil into the already-removed handful.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Rava's dilemma about a handful whose oil was squeezed onto the wood first connects to a broader question about:
A. Whether two handfuls from the same offering can be combined
B. Whether substances contiguous to altar-items are legally considered part of those items
C. Whether a priest who is tamei (impure) can perform the handful removal
D. Whether the sinner's offering requires verbal confession before removal
Correct: B — Rava's dilemma hinges on whether contiguous substances (oil on the wood next to the handful) are legally considered part of the ascending item. This connects directly to R' Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish's dispute about whether a bone contiguous to meat joins it for the olive-bulk measure.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Ḥisda, carcass meat IS nullified in a larger quantity of slaughtered meat. Why?
A. Because carcass meat is of lesser sanctity and always defers to slaughtered meat
B. Because slaughtered meat, being the majority, cannot become carcass meat — so the two are different types and the minority is nullified
C. Because carcass meat imparts impurity and must be treated stringently in all cases
D. Because the majority always nullifies the minority regardless of type identity
Correct: B — Rav Ḥisda follows the larger/nullifying quantity. Since slaughtered meat CANNOT become carcass meat, the two are treated as different types — and the minority (carcass) is nullified in the majority (slaughtered). The reverse does not apply because carcass can theoretically become slaughtered.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Rabbi Zeira's gezerah shavah (verbal analogy) on the word 'burning' (הקטרה) teaches:
A. That handfuls must be burned before remainders are distributed to the priests
B. That remainders and handfuls never nullify each other — a Torah-level edict independent of quantity
C. That only the kohen gadol may burn the handful on Yom Kippur
D. That burning outside the Temple with an olive-bulk of meat incurs lashes
Correct: B — Rabbi Zeira derives from the shared word הקטרה (burning) applied to both handfuls and remainders: just as two mixed handfuls do not nullify each other and are both burned, so too handfuls and remainders cannot nullify each other. This is a Torah edict independent of the Rav Ḥisda/Rabbi Ḥanina nullification debate.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Per the Mishna, if a handful was intermingled with a meal offering from which a handful had not yet been removed, and the priest burned the mixture b'diavad (after the fact), what is the ruling?
A. Both offerings are invalid and the owners must bring entirely new offerings
B. The offering from which the handful was taken satisfies that owner's obligation
C. Only the second offering (the non-removed one) is satisfied — the first is lost
D. No obligation is satisfied since the burning was done improperly
Correct: B — The Mishna rules that while one should not burn such a mixture l'chatchila, if burned b'diavad, the meal offering whose handful was already properly removed does satisfy its owner's obligation. The non-removed offering, however, does not have its obligation fulfilled.
