🍑 Teruma: Fresh Figs vs. Dried Figs

Fresh → Dried (Permitted)

  • May separate 10 fresh figs as teruma for 90 dried
  • Only in a place where the custom is to dry figs
  • Rationale: fresh figs will shrink to dried eventually
  • Dried figs are exceptional — they do not expand when wet (Rav Dimi)
  • Cannot extrapolate this leniency to other produce

Dried → Fresh (Forbidden)

  • May NOT separate dried figs as teruma for fresh
  • Even where a priest is present
  • Fresh figs will shrink as they dry — teruma share becomes too small
  • Violates the principle of generous teruma giving
  • Applies even according to Rabbi Elazar son of Rabbi Yosei

⚖️ Is a Priest Present? — The Baraita's Two Clauses

Gemara
The first clause permits fresh-for-dried only where the custom exists. But if a priest IS present, why does custom matter? Give the teruma immediately!
Gemara
Obviously the first clause addresses a case where NO priest is present — the fresh figs might spoil before one is found. Hence custom is required.
Gemara
Then the latter clause — forbidding dried-for-fresh — must address a case where a priest IS present. The two clauses address different situations!
Gemara
Correct. Yes — the baraita's two clauses speak of two distinct scenarios under a single tanna's ruling.
Rav Pappa
We may interpret a single tanna as addressing two different situations — but we do NOT split clauses between two different tannaim. That is the weaker reading.
Rav Pappa's Hermeneutical Principle Prefer reading one tanna's ruling as covering two scenarios over attributing different clauses to two tannaim.

📜 Deriving the Prohibition Against Leavening the Meal Offering Remainder

Mishna: One who allows the remainder of a meal offering to leaven violates a prohibition.
Question: What is the biblical source?
↓
Reish Lakish: Derived from Lev. 6:10 — "It shall not be baked with leaven. I have given it as their portion"
"Their portion" = the priests' share; it must not become chametz
↓
Challenge: That same verse is needed to teach that baking is singled out for its own separate prohibition!
↓
Resolution: The verse contains two distinct phrases:
• "It shall not be baked with leaven" → separate lav for baking
• "Their portion" → lav for leavening the remainder
↓
Further Challenge (Rabbi Aptoriki): The generalization (Lev. 2:11) and the detail (Lev. 6:10) are distanced from each other — standard klal u'frat does not apply!
↓
Rav Adda bar Ahava: Even distanced generalizations and details can teach — proven from the sin offering laws (Lev. 4 and 6)

The Princes of Israel and the Northern Slaughter

וְשָׁחַט אֹתוֹ בִּמְקוֹם אֲשֶׁר יִשְׁחַט אֶת הָעֹלָה — ויקרא ד:כד
👑 Final Resolution — The 'It' Exclusion The term אֹתוֹ ('it') in Leviticus 4:24 is exclusionary. One might think: since the goats of Nahshon and the princes require semikha (laying on of hands), they should also require slaughter in the north. The term 'it' teaches otherwise — the princes' sin offerings brought in the Wilderness were not slaughtered in the north of the Tabernacle. They are included in the general law via Lev. 6:18, but the exclusion reminds us they stand apart from the king's specific derivation. Rav Ashi further clarifies that the verses form a detail followed by a generalization, not the reverse — reshaping the entire hermeneutical analysis.
