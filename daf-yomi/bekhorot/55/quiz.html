QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why may one NOT separate dried figs as teruma for fresh figs?
A. Dried figs are of lesser monetary value than fresh figs
B. The fresh figs given will shrink as they dry, making the teruma portion too small
C. There is no local custom to convert fresh figs into dried ones
D. A priest must be present before any cross-type teruma separation is valid
Correct: B — The Gemara explains the asymmetry: fresh figs shrink when dried, so the teruma separated now will represent less produce than intended — violating the mandate for generous teruma.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is Rav Pappa's hermeneutical principle established in this daf?
A. A baraita's conflicting clauses must be attributed to two different tannaim
B. We prefer reading both clauses as one tanna addressing two distinct situations over splitting them between two tannaim
C. When a mishna has two clauses we always follow the more lenient one
D. Fresh and dried produce may never be mixed for teruma purposes under any circumstance
Correct: B — Rav Pappa rules that interpreting one tanna's ruling as covering two different scenarios is stronger than attributing different clauses to two tannaim.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
From which biblical phrase does the Gemara ultimately derive the prohibition against leavening the REMAINDER of a meal offering?
A. "No meal offering…shall be made with leaven" (Lev. 2:11) — the general prohibition
B. "It shall not be baked with leaven" — the baking stage specifically
C. "Their portion" — the priests' share referenced in Lev. 6:10
D. "You shall burn no leaven nor any honey" (Lev. 2:11) — the prohibition on burning
Correct: C — After resolving that "it shall not be baked" is needed for the baking lav, the Gemara concludes that "their portion" — the priestly share — is the source for the prohibition on leavening the remainder.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Aptoriki argue that the standard klal u'frat (generalization-and-detail) hermeneutic cannot be applied in the meal offering prohibition discussion?
A. Both verses are generalizations — there is no detail to work with
B. The generalization (Lev. 2:11) and the detail (Lev. 6:10) are distanced from each other in the Torah
C. The rule only applies to purity laws, not sacrificial laws
D. Rabbi Aptoriki agrees it applies — his objection is about something else
Correct: B — Rabbi Aptoriki holds that standard klal u'frat derivations require proximity; when the generalization and detail appear in different sections of the Torah, the hermeneutic does not operate the same way.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What does the Gemara ultimately conclude that the exclusionary term אֹתוֹ ('it') in Leviticus 4:24 teaches?
A. That ALL sin offerings must be slaughtered in the north without exception
B. That the king's sin offering is more stringent than a commoner's in every respect
C. That the sin offerings of Nahshon and the tribal princes were not slaughtered in the north of the Tabernacle
D. That the sin offerings of princes do not require semikha (laying on of hands)
Correct: C — The Gemara resolves that 'it' excludes the goats of Nahshon and the other princes — despite their offerings requiring semikha, they were not slaughtered in the north, since they were brought in special wilderness circumstances before the permanent law was crystallized.
