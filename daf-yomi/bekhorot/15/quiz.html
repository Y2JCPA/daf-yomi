QUESTION 1 OF 5
What do multiple proofs from Rav Huna b. Rav Natan, Rav Ashi, and Ravina establish about the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis?
A. They dispute whether the tzitz effects acceptance for impure items that normally ascend the altar
B. Their dispute is NOT about the tzitz — all agree it effects acceptance for impure items that ascend the altar
C. Rabbi Yehuda holds the tzitz never effects acceptance for any impure offering
D. The dispute is about whether impure libations may be offered even with the tzitz's help
Correct: B — All three Amoraim bring independent proofs — from baraita (impure frankincense), Pesachim (Paschal offering), and the two loaves mishna — showing that even Rabbi Yehuda agrees the tzitz effects acceptance for impure altar items. Their dispute must concern something else.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to the Mishna (15a), why does a thanks offering render its loaves piggul, but the loaves do NOT render the thanks offering piggul?
A. The loaves are holier than the offering and therefore can only be affected, not affect others
B. The blood rites of the offering are what permit the loaves — piggul intent during those rites affects everything they permit; the loaves have no corresponding permitting act for the offering
C. Both the offering and loaves can render each other piggul symmetrically under all circumstances
D. The loaves render the offering piggul only if the priest explicitly declared piggul intent during the waving
Correct: B — The offering's blood rites are the permitting act for the loaves. Piggul intent during those rites contaminates everything they permit. The loaves have no sacrificial rite that 'permits' the offering, so the relationship is inherently one-directional.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the core of Rabbi Elazar's dilemma presented before Rav?
A. Whether one is liable for eating piggul if he consumed only the loaves but not the offering
B. Whether piggul intent split across both the offering and its loaves — combining to form a single olive-bulk — can render the loaves piggul
C. Whether the thanks offering itself becomes piggul if the priest intended piggul regarding the loaves only
D. Whether libations can combine with loaves to create a single olive-bulk of piggul liability
Correct: B — Rabbi Elazar specifically sets aside the simple case (intent about the offering alone rendering loaves piggul) and focuses on whether combined intent — part from the offering, part from the loaves — for a single olive-bulk can still render the loaves piggul. Rav rules: yes, loaves become piggul; offering does not.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does the Gemara resolve the a fortiori challenge against Rav's ruling in Rabbi Elazar's dilemma?
A. The a fortiori is accepted and Rav's ruling is overturned — the loaves are not piggul
B. The a fortiori is rejected by analogy to the kilayim (diverse kinds) baraita: Torah-level and rabbinic-level prohibitions operate independently, so leniency in one direction does not require leniency in the other
C. The a fortiori is accepted but modified to apply only when the intent concerned the loaves alone
D. The a fortiori is rejected because Rabbi Elazar later agreed with Rav's reasoning on other grounds
Correct: B — The Gemara cites a baraita about kilayim in a vineyard where hemp and arum (Torah-prohibited) were deemed forbidden while other seeds (rabbinically prohibited) were permitted — demonstrating that we do not always apply a fortiori reasoning across different levels of prohibition.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is Rava's explanation of Rabbi Meir's position on libations becoming piggul?
A. Libations can never become piggul because they are liquids, not solid sacrificial food
B. Rabbi Meir holds that libations are fixed to the specific offering at the time of its slaughter and cannot be transferred to another offering — therefore piggul intent during slaughter affects them once they are consecrated in the vessel
C. Rabbi Meir agrees with the Rabbis that libations become piggul only when poured on the altar, not before
D. Libations become piggul automatically whenever the offering is piggul, regardless of when they were consecrated
Correct: B — Rava explains that Rabbi Meir holds the libations become 'fixed' to the specific offering at the moment of slaughter — they cannot be used with a different offering. Therefore, the blood rites at slaughter render the libations susceptible to piggul from the moment they are consecrated in the vessel.
