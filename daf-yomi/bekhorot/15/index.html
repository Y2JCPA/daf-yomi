🍞 Menachot 15 — Piggul Across Paired Offerings

פִּגּוּל — disqualifying intent to consume a sacrifice outside its permitted time or place
Core Theme The daf opens by resolving that Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis do NOT dispute about the tzitz (frontplate) for impure altar items — multiple proofs confirm this. The daf then pivots to the asymmetric piggul relationship: an offering can render its accompanying loaves piggul through its blood rites, but loaves cannot render the offering piggul. Rabbi Elazar probes whether combined intent across offering and loaves can render the loaves piggul. The daf closes with Rabbi Meir's dispute with the Rabbis over when libations become subject to piggul.

🔮 Is the Tzitz Dispute Really About Impurity?

Rav Huna b. Rav Natan
Can this really be the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis? Even Rabbi Yehuda concedes the tzitz effects acceptance for impure items that normally ascend the altar — the baraita about impure frankincense bowls proves it!
Rav Ashi
Proof from Pesachim (80a): Rabbi Yehuda agrees the tzitz works for the entire community's Paschal offering when impure. The dispute between him and the Rabbis must concern something entirely different.
Ravina
Further proof from the two loaves of Shavuot: if the dispute were about tzitz, Rabbi Yehuda should say both loaves are burned because the tzitz effects acceptance. He says they're burned but gives no such reasoning — so the tzitz is not the issue!
Conclusion
All three proofs converge: the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis is not about whether the tzitz effects acceptance for impure items. That point is agreed upon by all.
Resolution Rav Huna b. Rav Natan, Rav Ashi, and Ravina each independently demonstrate that the tzitz is not the crux of the Rabbi Yehuda/Rabbis disagreement.

🥩 Asymmetric Piggul: Offering vs. Loaves

Thanks Offering → Loaves ✅

  • Slaughtering todah with piggul intent renders the loaves piggul
  • Blood rites of the todah are what permit the loaves to be eaten
  • Piggul intent during those blood rites affects everything they permit
  • Source: derived via gezeira shava from Leviticus

Loaves → Thanks Offering ❌

  • Intent regarding the loaves does NOT render the todah piggul
  • The loaves have no sacrificial act that 'permits' the offering
  • Permitting relationship flows one way: offering permits loaves only
  • One-directional dependence is intrinsic to how piggul works

Shavuot Lambs → Two Loaves ✅

  • Slaughtering lambs with piggul intent renders the two loaves piggul
  • Lambs' blood rites permit the two loaves to be eaten and waved
  • Same logic as todah: slaughter permits loaves, intent affects loaves
  • Both cases (todah and Shavuot) are taught — each has unique features

Two Loaves → Shavuot Lambs ❌

  • Intent regarding the two loaves does NOT render the lambs piggul
  • Loaves have no rite that 'permits' the lambs
  • Asymmetric by design — mirrors the todah case exactly
  • Both cases necessary: todah case alone would not have taught Shavuot

🤔 Rabbi Elazar's Dilemma — Combined Piggul Intent

Rabbi Elazar's Question to Rav:
One slaughters the thanks offering intending to eat a combined olive-bulk — part from the offering, part from its loaves — outside permitted time. Are the loaves rendered piggul?
↓
Not the simple case
If intent was entirely about the offering alone → loaves are clearly piggul. That's the mishna's base rule.
The real dilemma
Does piggul intent split across offering and loaves combine into one olive-bulk to render the loaves piggul?
↓
Rav's Ruling: Yes — the loaves are rendered piggul. But the thanks offering itself remains valid.
↓
Gemara Challenges: A fortiori — if the offering, which CAN render loaves piggul, is NOT itself rendered piggul by loaves-intent, then surely combined intent should not render the loaves piggul either?
↓
Rejection: We do not apply this a fortiori. Proof from baraita about kilayim (diverse kinds): Torah-level and rabbinic-level prohibitions differ in severity — leniency in one direction does not preclude strictness in the other.

🍷 Rabbi Meir vs. Rabbis — Libations and the Leper's Oil

The Mishna (end of 15a): An animal offering renders its libations piggul from the moment the libations are consecrated in the vessel — this is Rabbi Meir's view. Libations do not render the offering piggul.
↓
Baraita — Rabbi Meir's Reasoning: The blood of the offering permits the libations to be offered on the altar. Any item whose permitting act was performed with piggul intent is itself subject to piggul.
↓
Rabbis' Objection: A person may bring his offering today and the accompanying libations up to ten days later! If the libations can be delayed and separated from the offering, the blood of the offering does not truly 'fix' or 'permit' them at slaughter.
↓
Rabbi Meir's Response: I spoke only about libations already consecrated in a vessel. Once in the vessel, they are fixed to this specific offering and cannot be transferred to another. At that point the earlier slaughter's blood rites do govern them.
↓
Rava's Explanation: Rabbi Meir holds that libations become 'fixed' to the offering at the time of slaughter — they cannot be switched to another offering. Therefore the blood rites at slaughter render them susceptible to piggul.
↓
Parallel — Leper's Log of Oil: Same dispute applies to the log of oil accompanying the leper's guilt offering (Leviticus 14). Rabbi Meir: oil is fixed to the guilt offering → piggul applies. Rabbis: oil can be switched to another guilt offering → piggul does not apply.
