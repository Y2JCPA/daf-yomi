QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabba, when does a meal offering removed not for its own sake still fulfill the owner's obligation?
A. Always — meal offerings never require lishmah
B. When the false intent is recognizable to observers (היכרא)
C. When the priest verbally declares his intent before removal
D. Only when the owner's original vow was ambiguous
Correct: B — Rabba's key principle is 'recognizability' (היכרא): if bystanders can clearly see that the priest acted with false intent, the offering is not disqualified. Hidden or ambiguous false intent, however, does invalidate the offering.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does Rava derive from Leviticus 6:7 that all meal offerings share one law?
A. The verse repeats the word 'offering' twice, creating a verbal analogy
B. The definite article in 'the meal offering' indicates one unified law governing all varieties
C. A rabbinic tradition transmitted by Rabbi Shimon's students confirms this reading
D. The verse appears adjacent to the laws of burnt offerings, creating a contextual link
Correct: B — Rava reads 'And this is the law of the meal offering' as establishing a single unified legal framework. Because all meal offerings fall under one law, intent for any meal offering is legally equivalent to proper intent for any other.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What challenge does Rav Aḥa son of Rava raise against Rav Ashi's vessel-intent resolution?
A. The verse 'And slaughter it' (Lev. 4:33) directly contradicts the vessel-intent principle
B. Rabbi Shimon also validates removing a handful from a dry offering for an oil-mixed offering, where the difference is in preparation — not just the vessel
C. Rav Ashi's principle was already rejected by an earlier Tannaic ruling
D. A guilt offering's ram wool versus a goat's black hair disproves the vessel-only theory
Correct: B — Rav Aḥa points out that Rabbi Shimon validates even the case of dry vs. oil-mixed meal offerings, where the visible difference is in the preparation itself — not merely the vessel. This cannot be explained by vessel-intent alone, undermining Rav Ashi's resolution.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does Rabba say a Paschal offering slaughtered for the sake of a guilt offering does NOT effect acceptance?
A. Paschal offerings are in a completely different sacrificial category
B. The age difference between first-year and second-year animals is not readily apparent to observers
C. Guilt offerings require the animal to be examined by a priest first
D. The Paschal offering's time-restriction makes any wrong intent automatically recognizable
Correct: B — Rabba explains that an animal in its first year can appear larger than its age, while a second-year animal can appear younger. The age distinction is therefore not 'on people's minds' as a clearly recognizable marker — so the false intent is not identifiable, and the offering does not effect acceptance.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What do Rabba, Rava, and Rav Ashi all agree upon, despite their different resolutions?
A. A meal offering removed not for its own sake never fulfills the owner's obligation
B. Meal offerings are subject to a more lenient lishmah standard than slaughtered offerings
C. Rabbi Shimon's opinion is rejected by the final halakha in all cases
D. The verse 'And this is the law of the meal offering' is the sole basis for leniency
Correct: B — All three Amoraim agree that Rabbi Shimon distinguishes between meal offerings (more lenient lishmah requirement) and slaughtered offerings (stricter lishmah requirement). They differ only in explaining the precise legal mechanism behind this distinction.
