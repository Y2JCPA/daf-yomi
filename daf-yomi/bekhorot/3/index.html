🍞 Three Amoraim Resolve One Contradiction

The Problem: Two baraitot cite Rabbi Shimon with contradictory rulings — does a meal offering whose handful was removed not for its own sake (shelo lishmah) fulfill the owner's obligation?
↓
Rabba
Valid only when false intent is recognizable to observers (היכרא). If hidden, offering is disqualified.
Rava
Always valid: verse teaches one unified law for all meal offerings (Lev. 6:7).
Rav Ashi
Valid because intent about the vessel type alone is legally inconsequential.
↓
All three agree: Meal offerings operate under a more lenient lishmah standard than slaughtered offerings — they differ only on the reason why.
3 / 9

⚔️ Rabba's Resolution: Recognizability as the Key

Gemara
Why does Rabbi Shimon rule that a meal offering removed not for its own sake still fulfills the owner's obligation?
Rabba
Because the false intent is recognizably false (היכרא). When observers see a pan offering treated as a deep-pan offering, the error is plain — and since recognizably false intent is disregarded, the owner fulfills his obligation.
Gemara
Challenge: A bird sin offering whose blood was sprinkled below the line for the sake of a burnt offering — the action proves wrong intent! Shouldn't it also effect acceptance?
Rabba
No — observers might think the blood had already been sprinkled below properly, and the above-line squeezing was incidental. The false intent is not clearly recognizable, so it does not effect acceptance.
Gemara
But Rabbi Shimon never discussed bird offerings! He said: meal offerings are not similar to slaughtered offerings — meaning only certain slaughtered offerings resemble meal offerings in this rule.
Core Principle — Rabba היכרא (Recognizability): Only when an observer can plainly see that the priest acted with false intent does Rabbi Shimon's leniency apply. Ambiguous or hidden wrongdoing is treated as genuine disqualification.

Test 1: Burnt offering slaughtered for the sake of a sin offering — sin offerings must be female; a male animal cannot be one. Recognizable?
↓
Female lamb
Tail obscures sex → not clearly recognizable → does not effect acceptance under Rabba
Female goat
Sex easily visible → clearly recognizable → should effect acceptance. But this challenges Rabba!

Test 2 — Paschal vs. Guilt Offering: First-year vs. second-year animal. Rabba's answer: the age difference is not readily apparent — an animal in its first year can appear older. Not recognizable.

📜 Rava vs. Rav Ashi: Two Alternative Readings of Rabbi Shimon

Rava's Resolution

  • Verse: "And this is the law of the meal offering" (Lev. 6:7)
  • The single definite article teaches: one unified law governs all meal offerings
  • Intent for any meal offering = valid intent for any other meal offering
  • Contrast: "And slaughter it for a sin offering" (Lev. 4:33) — 'it' demands the specific offering's own identity
  • Implication: Rabbi Shimon holds a sin offering brought for a different sin offering is valid; the Rabbis disagree

Rav Ashi's Resolution

  • Principle: Intent about the vessel type (pan vs. deep-pan) is legally inconsequential
  • The priest only had intent about the container, not the offering's legal category
  • Rabbi Shimon's logic: Mode of preparation proves the offering's identity — the offering speaks for itself
  • Slaughtered offerings: One manner of slaughter for all → wrong intent does affect legal identity
  • Challenge from Rav Aḥa: Dry vs. oil-mixed offering — preparation itself differs, yet R. Shimon still validates it!

🏺 Rav Ashi's Full Argument and Its Challenges

The Ruling: One removes a handful from a pan meal offering while intending a deep-pan offering → offering is still valid (Rabbi Shimon)
↓
Rav Ashi's Principle: The priest's intent was only about the vessel type. Vessel-type intent is inconsequential — the offering's own preparation proves what it legally is.
↓
Why Slaughtered Offerings Differ: All slaughtered offerings share one manner of slaughter AND one manner of blood-sprinkling. The offering cannot prove its own identity → wrong intent matters.
↓
Challenge from Rav Aḥa son of Rava: Rabbi Shimon also validates removing a handful from a dry meal offering for the sake of an oil-mixed offering. Here the preparation visibly differs — vessel logic cannot explain this!
↓
Second Challenge: Slaughtering a burnt offering for the sake of a peace offering (shelamim) — can the priest claim his intent was merely for 'peace between himself and God'? Rejected: the offering itself is called shelamim (Lev. 7:33) — the name is objectively attached to the animal, not to a vague intent.
