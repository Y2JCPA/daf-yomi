QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Gemara on 13a, why is intent to consume half an olive-bulk + intent to burn half an olive-bulk insufficient to create piggul?
A. Because burning is only relevant for the altar portions, not the eaten portions
B. Because intent to consume and intent to burn are distinct modes of piggul and do not combine
C. Because each half-portion is independently below the minimum olive-bulk measure
D. Because the mishna only addresses combining two consuming intents, not mixed intents
Correct: B — The Gemara teaches that intent to consume and intent to burn are fundamentally distinct categories of piggul intent. Even when each half is treated in accordance with its typical manner, the two modes do not join together to form the forbidden olive-bulk.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Yosei rule that intent to burn the frankincense the next day renders the meal offering unfit but NOT piggul?
A. Because frankincense is not consumed and therefore cannot be the subject of piggul intent
B. Because frankincense is a permitting factor (matir) and a matir cannot render another matir piggul
C. Because the frankincense is placed separately from the meal offering on the altar
D. Because only intent regarding the handful can render a meal offering piggul
Correct: B — Rabbi Yosei holds that a permitting factor (matir) cannot render another permitting factor piggul. The frankincense serves as the matir that permits the meal offering to be eaten — it is not 'part of the preclusion' of the offering itself. Hence intent regarding it disqualifies but does not create piggul.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the final Gemara reasoning that proves collection of frankincense by a non-priest is invalid?
A. Frankincense is more sacred than the handful and thus requires stricter priestly standards
B. Since placement of the handful in a vessel and blood collection both involve a service vessel sanctifying a permitting factor, they are equivalent — and blood collection requires a priest
C. A baraita explicitly states that non-priests are excluded from all frankincense-related rites
D. Because the frankincense cannot be ignited without priestly intention, only a priest may handle it
Correct: B — The Gemara concludes that both blood collection and placement of the handful involve a service vessel sanctifying a permitting factor for the altar. This structural equivalence means that just as blood collection requires a priest, so does collection of the frankincense from a meal offering.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yosei, why does the tanna need to state that 'Rabbi Yosei concedes' piggul applies when one intends to burn the handful the next day?
A. To emphasize that Rabbi Yosei changed his opinion from an earlier ruling
B. So that the tanna can contrast it with the frankincense case and prevent the mistaken inference that Rabbi Yosei never concedes piggul for meal offerings
C. Because Rabbi Yosei's students disagreed with him on this point
D. To teach that the handful creates piggul only when the priest has explicit intent
Correct: B — The tanna states 'Rabbi Yosei concedes' to allow the teaching of the contrasting frankincense case. Without the concession language, one might assume Rabbi Yosei never rules piggul for meal offerings — the contrast clarifies that he only exempts from piggul when a matir is involved.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the core issue in the dispute between Rav Huna and Rav Naḥman regarding the two Shavuot loaves?
A. Whether both loaves must be offered simultaneously or may be offered in sequence
B. Whether piggul intent concerning one loaf leaves the other loaf unaffected (Rav Huna / R. Yosei) or whether combined intent spanning both loaves is required even for Rabbi Yosei
C. Whether a non-priest may eat the two loaves if the priests are ritually impure
D. Whether the lambs accompanying the loaves can independently create piggul for the loaves
Correct: B — Rav Huna holds that according to Rabbi Yosei each loaf is independent — piggul intent for one does not affect the other, analogous to ritual impurity of one limb not spreading to the whole. Rav Naḥman challenges this from a baraita that seems to require intent spanning both loaves even according to Rabbi Yosei.
