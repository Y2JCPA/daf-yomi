🌾 Piggul in Meal Offerings — Intent, Permitting Factors & Arrangements

מנחות י"ג
Core Theme Daf 13 explores the complex rules of piggul (invalid sacrificial intent) applied to meal offerings. The Gemara asks: do intent to consume and intent to burn combine across half-portions? Can the frankincense — itself a permitting factor (matir) — generate piggul for the meal offering? Is collection of frankincense by a non-priest valid? And do the two Shavuot loaves form one unit, so that piggul intent for one affects the other?
3 / 9

🔥 Does Intent to Consume + Intent to Burn Combine for Piggul?

Abaye
The mishna must be teaching a novel case: one intended to consume half an olive-bulk and to burn half an olive-bulk tomorrow. Do these two halves combine to form a forbidden olive-bulk of piggul intent?
Gemara
We could already infer that consuming and burning do not combine from the mishna's case of 'consuming an item typically consumed' + 'consuming an item typically not consumed' — those don't combine either. So why is the new case necessary?
Abaye
The new case IS necessary! In the prior inference, neither half is treated in accordance with its typical manner. But here — one half is consumed as typical, one half is burned as typical — one might think these combine. The mishna teaches that they still do not.
Gemara
Correct. Intent to consume and intent to burn are fundamentally distinct modes of piggul. Even when each half-portion is individually treated in its typical manner, the two modes of intent do not join together.
Ruling Intent to consume and intent to burn are distinct categories of piggul — they do not combine even when each half is treated in accordance with its typical manner.

📜 Rabbi Yosei: When Does a Meal Offering Become Piggul?

Priest removes handful from meal offering with improper next-day intent
↓
Intent to eat the remainder or burn the handful next day
Rabbi Yosei concedes: This IS piggul — liable to karet
Intent to burn the frankincense next day
Rabbi Yosei: Offering is unfit (pasul) — but NOT piggul, no karet
↓
Key distinction: The handful is part of the meal offering itself; the frankincense is a separate permitting factor (matir) — and a matir cannot render another matir piggul

🫒 Can Frankincense Render the Meal Offering Piggul? — Rabbi Yosei vs. Rabbis

Rabbi Yosei

  • A matir (permitting factor) cannot render another matir piggul
  • Frankincense is the matir for the meal offering — it is not 'part of the preclusion' of the offering itself
  • Intent regarding frankincense = offering is unfit, not piggul — no karet
  • Same rule applies to the two bowls of frankincense of the shewbread: intent for one bowl does NOT render the shewbread piggul
  • Reason: each bowl is a matir for the other arrangement; matir ≠ piggul-creator

The Rabbis

  • Intent regarding the frankincense does render the meal offering piggul
  • The 'matir cannot render another matir piggul' rule applies only when each is a separate independent matir
  • Here, frankincense is the sole permitting factor for the meal offering — so it creates full piggul
  • Burning one bowl of shewbread frankincense with piggul intent does render the shewbread piggul
  • The frankincense is so intertwined with the offering that it triggers normal piggul rules

🏺 Can a Non-Priest Collect the Frankincense? — Rav Mari's Analysis

Rabbi Yannai's ruling: Collection of frankincense from a meal offering by a non-priest is not valid and disqualifies the meal offering.
↓
Rav Mari's proof from the mishna (12a): The mishna lists 'placing the handful in a vessel' as a piggul-creating rite, equating it with significant sacrificial acts like slaughter and blood application.
↓
Proposed analogy: 'Placing the handful in a vessel' parallels collection of blood into a service vessel — just as blood collection requires a priest, so does frankincense collection.
↓
Challenge: Are these truly parallel? Blood fills the service vessel and is thereby sanctified; placing the handful in a vessel is merely preparatory — the vessel does not sanctify the handful in the same way.
↓
Resolution attempt: Since it is impossible to sacrifice the handful without first placing it in a vessel, this placement is deemed a significant independent rite — therefore it requires priestly performance.
↓
Final Gemara answer: Both acts — blood collection and handful placement — involve vessel-sanctification of a permitting factor. The service vessel sanctifies the handful (or blood) for altar use. This shared logic makes them equivalent, confirming that frankincense collection requires a priest.

🍞 Two Shavuot Loaves — Does Piggul Intent for One Affect the Other?

Rav Huna
According to Rabbi Yosei: if one had piggul intent concerning only the right side of one loaf, only that portion is rendered piggul — not the second loaf. Disqualifying intent is no stronger than ritual impurity: if one limb became impure, the whole offering did not become impure. So too here, one loaf's piggul does not spread.
Rav Naḥman
I object from a baraita: 'The Rabbis say there is no karet liability for the two loaves unless one had piggul intent with regard to an olive-bulk of both loaves together.' In accordance with whose opinion is this? If it follows the Rabbis of the mishna — they hold even one loaf's piggul makes both piggul! So this baraita must follow Rabbi Yosei.
Rav Huna
No — this baraita must follow the Rabbis of the mishna after all. The Rabbis agree that for karet liability specifically, one must have an olive-bulk of intent combining both loaves. The Rabbis hold the loaves are one unit for piggul status, but the karet threshold still requires the full olive-bulk from both.
Rav Naḥman
Then whose opinion is the baraita? If it is the Rabbis, one loaf already makes both piggul — why require intent spanning both? The baraita's position is difficult and requires further analysis.
Unresolved Dispute Do the two Shavuot loaves form one sacrificial unit (piggul for one = piggul for both) or are they independent? The identity of the baraita's author remains under debate.
