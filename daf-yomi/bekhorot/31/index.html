↓

Cannot sew — scroll remains unfit

Parchment is suitable; sewing permitted
No — ungalled ('old')

🌾 Untithed Produce Mixed With Tithed — Seeking a Remedy

Rabbi Shimon Shezuri's untithed produce (tevel) became mixed with a larger quantity of tithed (permitted) produce
↓
He asked Rabbi Tarfon: What remedy is available?
↓
By Torah law
Minority tevel is nullified in the tithed majority — exempt from tithes
By Rabbinic law
Not nullified — one must still separate tithes from a proxy
↓
Remedy: Separate tithes from gentile produce on behalf of the mixed tevel (per some versions of Rabbi Tarfon's ruling)

📄 Parchment Types for Torah Scroll Repair

Old Parchment

  • Not processed with gall (oak-gall tannin)
  • Called 'old' regardless of actual age
  • Cannot be sewn to restore fitness
  • Even a 2-line tear cannot be repaired with this material

New Parchment

  • Processed with gall (oak-gall tannin)
  • Called 'new' regardless of actual age
  • May be sewn when tear extends into 2 lines
  • Required material for a Torah scroll to be fit for use

🔄 Rolling Direction of the Mezuza

שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ ה׳ אֶחָד
Rav Huna's Practice Rabbi Ḥelbo testified: I saw Rav Huna wrap (roll) a written mezuza from the word eḥad toward the word shema — rolling left to right, so that when unrolled, Shema appears first. This mirrors the natural reading order of the opening verse:

Shema → Yisrael → Adonai → Eloheinu → Adonai → Eḥad

By rolling from the end (eḥad) toward the beginning (shema), the text of Shema faces outward when stored.
