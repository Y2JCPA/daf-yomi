⚖️ Rabbi Yoḥanan vs. Reish Lakish — Three Disputes About Meal Offerings

Rabbi Yoḥanan
Oil mixed outside the Temple courtyard → Disqualified. Even if the priesthood requirement is relaxed, the service must still occur inside the Temple precincts using a service vessel.
Reish Lakish
Oil mixed outside → Valid. From the removal of the handful onward, pouring and mixing are merely a mitzva of the priesthood — not indispensable rites. Location cannot disqualify them.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Meal offering lacking before handful removal → owner may refill from home. The handful has not yet established the offering, so additions are still permitted.
Reish Lakish
Lacking before handful removal → Disqualified. The verse absolutely forbids a lacking offering; one may not restore it.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Remainder lacking after handful removed → Burn the handful; give remainder to priests — as long as some remainder still survives, the offering is valid.
Reish Lakish
Remainder lacking after removal → Disqualified. The remainder must have been whole at the very moment the handful was removed.
The Core Question Does the removal of the handful 'lock in' the offering's status — permitting later flexibility — or must every subsequent rite be performed with full Temple stringency?

⚗️ Mixing Oil Outside the Temple Courtyard

Reish Lakish
Valid. The verse 'he shall bring it' teaches: from the removal of the handful onward, the Sages derived that all remaining rites are a mitzva of the priesthood only — they do not invalidate the offering if performed outside.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Disqualified. A meal offering's performance must occur in a service vessel. Granted the priesthood is not strictly required, yet performance inside the Temple courtyard remains mandatory regardless.
Reish Lakish
Proof: "And he shall pour oil upon it and place frankincense thereon" (Lev. 2:1) — pouring and mixing are supplementary acts; the Torah did not restrict them to the Temple interior.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Counter: The verse establishing that priestly service is not required does not eliminate the location requirement. The two stringencies operate independently.
Underlying Principle Consuming an offering ≠ performing its service. It is not disrespectful for a priest to serve in the master's domain; but eating may occur elsewhere — hence the separate verse in Numbers 18:9 permitting priests to eat offerings outside.

  • 📏 Lacking before handful removal → Disqualified; cannot refill
  • ⚖️ Remainder lacking after removal → Disqualified

  • 🔍 Shewbread baraita: follows only Rabbi Eliezer, not Rabbi Yehoshua

✋ Halakha: Left-Hand Removal — Derivation The Gemara asks: from where do we derive that removing the handful with the left hand disqualifies a meal offering?

Rabbi Zeira derives it from the purification ritual of the leper (Lev. 14:15–16): the verse explicitly states "the priest shall take…with his left hand" — specifically requiring the left hand for pouring the oil on the leper's palm.

The Torah uses the word 'left' four times in that passage (twice for the poured oil, twice for the log of oil). This repetition signals that left-hand use is uniquely mandatory for the leper — thereby implying that in all other Temple service, the right hand is required.

The Gemara refines: one cannot simply infer from the leper passage that left is permitted generally. The four-fold repetition establishes that left is exclusive to the leper rite, so removing the handful with the left hand is invalid.
