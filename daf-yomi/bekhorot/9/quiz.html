QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Reish Lakish, what is the halakhic status of a meal offering whose oil was mixed outside the Temple courtyard?
A. Disqualified — the service must occur inside the courtyard
B. Valid — after the handful is removed, pouring and mixing are merely a priestly mitzva
C. Valid only if the priest intended to complete the rite inside
D. Disqualified — a service vessel was not properly used
Correct: B — Reish Lakish holds that from the removal of the handful onward, the Sages derived that the remaining rites are a mitzva of the priesthood — not critical rites that invalidate the offering. Therefore, mixing oil outside the courtyard is valid.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Yoḥanan permit the owner to refill a meal offering that became lacking before the handful was removed?
A. Because the removal of the handful has already established the offering's final status
B. Because the removal of the handful has not yet established the offering, so additions are still permitted
C. Because a meal offering can be refilled at any stage by rabbinic enactment
D. Because the priest has discretionary authority to permit refilling before the service begins
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan reasons that the removal of the handful is the act that 'establishes' the offering. Before that point, the offering has not yet been fully constituted as a sanctified korban, so the owner is free to restore the full measure with flour from home.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What does Rav Pappa observe about Reish Lakish's silence in the shewbread dispute?
A. Reish Lakish agreed that the shewbread baraita refuted his position entirely
B. Reish Lakish was absent and not present for that portion of the discussion
C. Reish Lakish did not raise the potential distinction between communal and individual offerings
D. Reish Lakish conceded that Rabbi Yoḥanan's three proofs were all valid
Correct: C — Rav Pappa notes that Reish Lakish remained silent rather than argue that communal offerings (like shewbread) are different from individual ones — since ritual impurity can be overridden for communal offerings (tzibur). This distinction could have defended his position.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
From where does Rabbi Zeira derive that removing the handful of a meal offering with the left hand disqualifies it?
A. A direct verse in Leviticus 2 specifying that the priest must use his right hand
B. A halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai (tradition from Sinai) preserved in the Mishnah
C. The passage about the leper's purification oil, where the left hand is explicitly and uniquely required
D. A gezera shava connecting the meal offering to the sin offering of the High Priest
Correct: C — Rabbi Zeira derives it from the leper's purification passage (Lev. 14:15–16), which mentions 'left hand' four times. This repetition signals that left-hand use is exclusively required for the leper rite — implying that in all other Temple service, the right hand is mandatory.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rav Malkiyu cites two verses to disqualify lacking meal offerings. How does the Gemara ultimately explain why both verses are needed?
A. One verse addresses lacking before the handful; the other addresses lacking after the handful
B. One verse disqualifies the offering for the owner; the other disqualifies the remainder for the priests
C. Both verses address the same case — the second adds emphasis for a repeated violation
D. One verse applies to voluntary meal offerings; the other to obligatory ones
Correct: B — The Gemara concludes that one verse (Lev. 2:2 — 'of its fine flour') teaches that the offering cannot be validly used if lacking, disqualifying it for the owner's purposes. The second verse (Lev. 2:3 — 'that which is left shall be Aaron's') teaches that the priests receive the remainder only if the offering was whole — addressing the priests' right to consume it.
