5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Ashi, how is the requirement to place tefillin on the left arm derived?
A. From the juxtaposition of arm-binding with the mezuza obligation
B. From the word yadkha spelled with a heh, indicating the weaker hand
C. From the verse describing Joseph's right hand as yad yemino
D. From an oral tradition attributed to Moses at Sinai
Correct: B — Rav Ashi derives it from yadkha (Exodus 13:16) spelled with a heh — read as yadkheh, meaning the weaker hand, which for a right-handed person is the left arm.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What was Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's initial reaction to Peleimu's question about a two-headed person donning tefillin?
A. He praised the question as a profound halakhic dilemma
B. He immediately ruled that the person must don two pairs of tefillin
C. He told Peleimu to either exile himself or accept excommunication
D. He deferred the question to the Sanhedrin for deliberation
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi thought the question was absurd or disrespectful and told Peleimu to exile himself or accept excommunication — until a real two-headed newborn was born and the question proved practical.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the key practical difference between the Rabbis and Rabbi Yishmael regarding the four tzitzit fringes?
A. The Rabbis require wool fringes; Rabbi Yishmael permits linen
B. The Rabbis say all four are one mitzva — missing one blocks the rest; Rabbi Yishmael says each fringe is independent
C. Rabbi Yishmael requires five fringes on a five-cornered garment; the Rabbis require only four
D. The Rabbis exempt left-handed persons from tzitzit; Rabbi Yishmael does not
Correct: B — The Rabbis hold the four fringes are one unified mitzva — absence of any one prevents fulfillment with the others. Rabbi Yishmael holds each is a discrete mitzva. Shmuel rules the halakha follows Rabbi Yishmael.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What does Rav Sheisha son of Rav Idi rule about cutting a corner of a garment to avoid the tzitzit obligation?
A. It works — a three-cornered garment is exempt from tzitzit
B. It is forbidden as wanton destruction of a garment
C. It does not help — the garment now has five corners and still requires tzitzit
D. It is effective only if the garment was already exempt before the cut
Correct: C — Cutting a corner turns a four-cornered garment into a five-cornered garment — which still requires tzitzit on all corners. The person has not achieved any exemption.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning, why must head tefillin be placed on the crown rather than literally 'between the eyes'?
A. Because the forehead is not considered part of the head for tefillin purposes
B. Because the crown, like the bicep, is a place susceptible to ritual impurity, paralleling the arm placement
C. Because placing tefillin literally between the eyes would obstruct vision
D. Because the forehead is mentioned separately in a different verse
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda derives by analogy: arm tefillin is placed where the limb is susceptible to ritual impurity from leprosy. Head tefillin must similarly be on such a place — the hair-bearing crown qualifies, while the literal 'between the eyes' area presents a different leprosy complication and is excluded.
