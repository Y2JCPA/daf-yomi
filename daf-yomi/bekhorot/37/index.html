✋ Deriving the Left-Arm Requirement for Tefillin

Rabbi Yosei HaḤorem
Proof from yad meaning left hand fails — in Genesis 48:17, the right hand is also called yad yemino, so yad alone does not specify left
Rabbi Natan
'Bind them on your arm' is juxtaposed with 'write them on doorposts' — just as writing is done with the dominant hand, binding is on the non-dominant (left) arm
Rav Ashi
Yadkha is spelled with a heh — read as yadkheh, the weaker hand; for a right-handed person, that is the left arm
Tannaitic Dispute on Rav Ashi's Reading Rav Ashi's yadkha-heh derivation is itself subject to a tannaitic dispute: one tanna reads it as 'the left hand specifically'; another reads it as 'the weaker hand' — directly relevant to left-handed persons

Person dons tefillin — which arm is required?
↓
Right-Handed Person
Dons on left arm — the weaker hand
Unanimous ruling
Left-Handed Person
Baraita 1: dons on right arm (weaker hand)
Baraita 2: dons on left arm
Resolution: ambidextrous → left arm; true left-hander → right arm

👤 The Two-Headed Newborn

מי שיש לו שתי ראשים — על אי זה מהן מניח תפילין
Peleimu's Dilemma Before Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi Peleimu asked Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: One who has two heads — on which head does he don tefillin?

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, thinking it absurd: 'Either exile yourself from here or accept excommunication!'

But then a man arrived: 'My wife gave birth to a two-headed child — how much must I pay for firstborn redemption?'

A ruling was issued: he pays two sela'im — one per head. Peleimu's question was entirely practical. A child with two heads is treated like one ravaged, and the firstborn redemption still applies per the word 'yet' in Numbers 18:15.

🧵 Tzitzit: One Mitzva or Four Discrete Mitzvot?

Rabbis (Mishna)

  • All four fringes constitute one unified mitzva
  • Absence of any one fringe prevents fulfillment with the remaining three
  • A five-cornered garment: all five fringes are required (one mitzva demands completeness)
  • Carrying a torn-fringed garment on Shabbat: removing it is necessary (incomplete mitzva = no dignity override)

Rabbi Yishmael

  • Each of the four fringes is a separate, independent mitzva
  • Absence of one does not prevent fulfillment with the others
  • A five-cornered garment: unclear whether all five or only four corners require fringes
  • Halakha follows Rabbi Yishmael — ruled by Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel

🧠 Precise Placement: Arm and Head Tefillin

Arm Tefillin — on the bicep: School of Menashe derives from 'on your arm' that it means the upper arm / bicep, not the hand
↓
'It shall be a sign for you' (Exodus 13:9) — hidden beneath the sleeve, a sign for yourself but not visible to others (Rabbi Eliezer)
↓
Opposite the heart: Rabbi Ḥiyya, Rav Aḥa b. Rav Avya, and Ameimar all directed the tefillin of the arm to rest opposite the heart
↓
Head Tefillin — on the crown: 'Between your eyes' means where hair grows on top of the head, not the literal forehead
↓
Rabbi Yehuda's principle: Just as the arm placement must be on flesh susceptible to ritual impurity (leprosy), so too the head placement must be such a place — the hairline crown qualifies; the literal 'between the eyes' does not (hair and flesh there = leprosy susceptibility in a different way)
