QUESTION 1 OF 5
When Rav stated that 'the height of the Candelabrum is nine handbreadths,' what did he actually mean?
A. The total height of the Menorah from base to top is nine handbreadths
B. The lampstand body alone is nine handbreadths; the total height including the base is eighteen handbreadths
C. The Menorah was placed on a nine-handbreadth platform to raise it
D. The six branches together span nine handbreadths horizontally
Correct: B — Rav clarified to Rav Shimi bar Ḥiyya: 'When I said nine, I was not referring to the total height, which is eighteen handbreadths — I meant the lampstand body is nine.' The three-step stone in Tamid was used for the cleaning procedure, consistent with a total height of eighteen handbreadths.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to the famous aggada (Rav Yehuda citing Rav), what did Moses find God doing when he ascended on High?
A. Fashioning the two Tablets of the Law with fire
B. Showing the angel Gabriel how to construct the Menorah
C. Sitting and tying crowns (taggin) on the letters of the Torah
D. Revealing the Ark, Table, and Candelabrum of fire to Moses
Correct: C — Rav Yehuda says Rav says: When Moses ascended on High, he found the Holy One, Blessed be He, sitting and tying crowns on the letters of the Torah. God explained these were for a future sage — Akiva ben Yosef — who would derive heaps of laws from each stroke.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What comforted Moses after he sat bewildered at the end of the eighth row in Rabbi Akiva's study hall?
A. God told Moses that Rabbi Akiva had personally studied Moses' original teachings
B. A student asked Rabbi Akiva the source of a law, and Rabbi Akiva replied it was a law given to Moses at Sinai
C. Moses suddenly understood the entire discussion
D. Rabbi Akiva turned and acknowledged Moses sitting at the back
Correct: B — When a student asked Rabbi Akiva the source of a ruling, Rabbi Akiva answered: 'It is a law given to Moses at Sinai (halacha l'Moshe miSinai).' Moses' strength was restored — he saw that his own transmission of Torah was being honored even in a discussion he could not follow.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does the shape of the letter heh represent this world, and what does its design teach about repentance?
A. Its large size represents the vast number of people in this world
B. Its closed form represents divine boundaries protecting the world from chaos
C. Its open bottom resembles a portico — one may freely exit (sin), but cannot re-enter the same way; repentance requires Heavenly assistance
D. Its two strokes represent the two Tablets, the foundation of this world
Correct: C — The Gemara explains: the letter heh, open at the bottom like a portico, represents this world — anyone wishing to leave (i.e., sin) may exit freely. But one cannot re-enter from below by one's own power; per Reish Lakish, the Evil Inclination overpowers a person daily, and only God's help enables repentance.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to the baraita that refutes Rav's ruling on Torah scroll errors, when must a scroll be interred?
A. If it contains any two consecutive scribal errors
B. If any single column has three or more errors
C. If every column has four or more errors
D. If it is missing an entire verse or passage
Correct: C — The baraita states: a scroll with three errors on every column may still be corrected, but if there are four errors on every column it shall be interred. This refutes Rav, who implied that even two errors per column necessitates correction (suggesting a lower threshold). Abaye adds: if even one column has only three errors, that column's correctability enables the entire scroll to be corrected.
