🕎 How Tall Was the Menorah?

Rav
The height of the Candelabrum is nine handbreadths.
Rav Shimi bar Ḥiyya
Objection! The Tamid mishna says there was a stone with three steps before the Menorah so the priest could reach it to clean the lamps — this implies it was far taller than nine handbreadths!
Rav
Shimi, is it really you asking me this? When I said nine, I meant the lampstand body alone. The total height is eighteen handbreadths. The base accounts for the rest.
Gemara
The three-step stone was used for the cleaning procedure — not evidence that the Menorah stood only nine handbreadths from the ground.
📐 Menorah Decorations (derived from Exodus 25:31–34) 22 goblets: 4 on main shaft + 18 on six branches • 11 knobs • 9 flowers — each count derived from the repetition of plural forms in the Biblical verses

🔥 The Heavenly Prototypes

אָרוֹן שֶׁל אֵשׁ וְשֻׁלְחָן שֶׁל אֵשׁ וּמְנוֹרָה שֶׁל אֵשׁ יָרְדוּ מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם
Rabbi Yosei b. Rabbi Yehuda (Baraita) + Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba citing Rabbi Yoḥanan Three vessels descended from the Heavens as fire: an Ark of fire, a Table of fire, and a Candelabrum of fire. Moses saw their celestial form and fashioned the earthly Tabernacle vessels in their likeness.

Separately, the angel Gabriel appeared girded with a wide belt (pesikiyya), like an artisan who tucks his garments to work freely, and demonstrated to Moses how to fashion the Menorah.

The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: three matters were difficult for Moses to grasp precisely until God showed them to him with His finger — the form of the Candelabrum, the exact size of forbidden creatures, and the new moon.

👁️ Moses Ascends and Meets Rabbi Akiva

Moses ascends on High and finds God tying crowns (taggin) on the letters of the Torah
↓
Moses asks: "Master of the Universe, who is preventing You from giving the Torah without these ornaments?"
↓
God replies: "In the future, a man named Akiva ben Yosef will derive heaps and heaps of laws from each and every stroke."
↓
Moses asks to see him. God: "Return behind you." Moses goes and sits at the end of the eighth row in Rabbi Akiva's academy — he cannot understand the discussion and his strength wanes
↓
A student asks Rabbi Akiva the source of a ruling; Rabbi Akiva answers: "It is a law given to Moses at Sinai." Moses is comforted.
↓
Moses sees Rabbi Akiva's flesh being weighed in a butcher's market (martyrdom). He cries: "Is this the Torah and this its reward?!" God: "Be silent — this intention arose before Me."

✡️ Letters and the Creation of Worlds

Letter ה (Heh) — This World

  • Shaped like a portico — open at the bottom on one side
  • Anyone who wishes to leave (sin) may do so — the exit is open
  • But one cannot re-enter the same way: repentance requires Heavenly assistance (Reish Lakish)
  • The suspended left leg hints at this: the gap is small and inaccessible from below
  • "For in the Lord [beYah] is God, an everlasting Rock [Tzur olamim]" — these two letters created both worlds (Isaiah 26:4)
  • behibare'am (Genesis 2:4) read as beheh bera'am: God created this world with heh

Letter י (Yod) — World-to-Come

  • The smallest letter in the Hebrew alphabet
  • Represents the World-to-Come
  • Small size alludes to the few righteous who merit it
  • Yod and Heh together form Yah — a Divine Name used for both worlds
  • The left side of the heh faces downward toward the World-to-Come, hinting at the return of the wicked who repent
  • Seven letters require three crowns (ziyyunin): shin, ayin, tet, nun, zayin, gimmel, tzadi

📜 Torah Scroll Errors — Correct or Inter?

Examine the Torah scroll: count errors per column
↓
2 errors per column
May be corrected
Rav's ruling (but the baraita implies even 3 is acceptable — refutation of Rav)
3 errors per column
May be corrected
Baraita ruling — the standard threshold
4 errors per column (entire scroll)
Shall be interred
Too many errors; scroll is unfit and must be buried
↓
Special case (Abaye to Rav Yosef): If one column has only 3 errors (correctable threshold), that column's correctability enables correction of the entire scroll, even if other columns have 4 errors
↓
Similarly: a perforated leg of the letter heh in a mezuza or tefillin — if the minimum measure remains, it is fit; if not, it is unfit (Agra's case before Rabbi Abba)
