5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the mishna, if a kometz (handful) of one standard meal offering is mixed with the kometz of another standard meal offering, what is the ruling?
A. It is fit, since both are the same type and neither is superior
B. It is unfit, because neither handful can be separately presented on the altar
C. It depends on which handful was separated first
D. It is fit if the majority volume belongs to one offering
Correct: B — The mishna rules it unfit. Once the two handfuls intermingle, they cannot be separated, and each kometz must be offered individually. The mixture is inseparable and thus cannot be properly presented.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Rabbi Yohanan says both the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda derive their opinions about min b'mino from the same source. What is that source?
A. The verse about the covenant of salt in Leviticus
B. The verse about Araunah's threshing boards offered to King David
C. The verse about the High Priest combining bull and goat blood on Yom Kippur
D. The mishna in Zevahim about blood mixed with water
Correct: C — Rabbi Yohanan identifies the Yom Kippur passage where the High Priest mixes the blood of the bull and goat as the single shared source. The Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda interpret its lesson differently, but both derive from this one verse.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the principle of 'min b'mino' as debated on this daf?
A. A majority substance nullifies a minority of the same type
B. A same-type substance in contact with its own kind is not nullified by it
C. Sacred items transferred to mundane use become permanently disqualified
D. Wood for the altar must originate from the same source as the altar stones
Correct: B — Min b'mino means 'same type with same type.' The principle holds that when a substance mixes with an identical type of substance, neither nullifies the other — in contrast to ordinary bitul, where a minority is nullified by a different-type majority.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua uses a kal va'chomer (a fortiori argument) to derive that altar wood must be unused. What is the comparison he makes?
A. Just as priests must be unblemished, their tools must also be pristine
B. Just as the altar itself was never put to mundane use, neither should its wood be
C. Just as the Temple vessels were new at consecration, all materials must be new
D. Just as libation wine must be from a sealed flask, so wood must be uncut
Correct: B — Rabbi Elazar reasons: the altar was built solely to serve God and never used for ordinary purposes. By a kal va'chomer, the wood placed upon it — a less inherently sacred item — certainly should not have been previously employed for mundane uses.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
The Gemara questions the rule against used wood by citing the case of Araunah's 'morigim.' What challenge does this case raise, and how is it resolved?
A. Araunah's boards were stone, not wood — so no challenge exists to the wood rule
B. Araunah's threshing boards were previously used implements; the Gemara resolves that royal implements in good condition are not considered 'used up'
C. The Gemara concludes that used wood is actually permitted when donated by a king
D. Araunah's boards were never actually used — they were newly made for the king
Correct: B — The Gemara challenges: if Araunah offered his threshing boards (morigim) as wood for David's altar, weren't they previously used? The resolution: the king's implements, being royal property kept in excellent condition, do not fall into the category of worn-out or previously exploited wood.
