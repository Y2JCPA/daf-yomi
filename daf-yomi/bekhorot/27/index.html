⚖️ The Principle of מְעַכֵּב (Indispensability)

Each component of a sacrificial rite may be indispensable (מְעַכֵּב) to its paired or grouped components
↓
If one component is missing or invalid, the entire offering or rite may be disqualified
↓
Indispensability is derived from: verbal repetition of key words, juxtaposition of components in a single verse, or the term חֻקָּה (statute)
↓
Scope: meal offering components (handful, flour, oil, frankincense), festival offerings (Yom Kippur goats, Shavuot sheep, shewbread), and purification rites (lulav, red heifer, nazirite, leper)

⚖️ Three Categories of Mutually Indispensable Items (Mishna 27a–b)

Meal Offering Components

  • Handful (קֹמֶץ) — minority prevents majority
  • Tenth of an ephah of flour — minority prevents majority
  • Log of oil — minority prevents majority
  • Fine flour & oil — each prevents the other (juxtaposition in verse)
  • Handful & frankincense — each prevents the other (repeated pairing)

Festival & Special Offerings

  • Two goats of Yom Kippur — each prevents the other
  • Two sheep of Shavuot — each prevents the other
  • Two arrangements of shewbread — חֻקָּה (statute) term
  • Two bowls of frankincense (shewbread) — each prevents the other
  • Four loaves of thanks offering — juxtaposed to nazirite

Purification & Lulav Rites

  • Four lulav species — tannaitic dispute on binding
  • Three red heifer species (cedar, hyssop, scarlet wool)
  • Two types of nazirite loaves — 'law of his naziriteship'
  • Four species of leper purification — 'this shall be the law'
  • Seven sprinklings of red heifer — each prevents the others

🌿 Must the Four Species Be Physically Bound Together?

Rav Ḥanan bar Rava
The mishna's indispensability rule applies only when one lacks some species. If one has all four, failure to take each is not necessarily a disqualification — they need not be bound together as a single unit
Baraita (Objection)
The species are like a bundle: those that produce fruit have a bond with those that do not. They must all be bound together — just as Israel's prayers are accepted only when the entire nation is united as one
Rabbi Yehuda
Binding is required by law: via גְּזֵרָה שָׁוָה — the term לְקִיחָה (taking) for lulav is the same term used for the hyssop bundle at Pesach. Just as hyssop must be an אֲגֻדָּה (bundle), so too the four species must be bound
Rabbis
Binding is not required: לְקִיחָה by hand (יָד) is sufficient for lulav, unlike hyssop where a bundle is explicit. Binding is a mitzva only due to הִדּוּר מִצְוָה — beautification of the commandment (Exodus 15:2: 'This is my God and I will beautify Him')
Halakhic Resolution A final baraita rules: there is a mitzva to bind the myrtle and willow with the lulav, but if one did not bind them, it is still fit — in accordance with the Rabbis

💧 Validity of the Seven Red Heifer Sprinklings (27b)

Priest performs 7 sprinklings of red heifer blood
opposite the entrance to the Sanctuary
↓
Performed לִשְׁמָהּ & toward entrance
✅ Fully valid — purification rite is complete
Not לִשְׁמָהּ (not for its own sake)
❌ Invalid — intent is indispensable for red heifer
Direction slightly off or completely wrong
⚠️ Disputed baraitot — resolved: slightly off = valid; completely wrong direction = invalid
↓
If any one of the 7 sprinklings is omitted entirely → entire rite void
Source: חֻקָּה (statute) — any placement of blood omitted means nothing was accomplished

🏛️ Unauthorized Entry into the Holy of Holies — Leviticus 16:2

אַל יָבֹא בְכָל עֵת אֶל הַקֹּדֶשׁ מִבֵּית לַפָּרֹכֶת (ויקרא ט״ז:ב׳)
Rabbis vs. Rabbi Yehuda The verse: 'He shall not come at all times into the holy place [הַקֹּדֶשׁ], within the Curtain [לִפְנֵי הַכַּפֹּרֶת]'

Rabbis: 'Holy place' = the Sanctuary (Heikhal); 'within the Curtain' = the Holy of Holies. Both are prohibited zones. Entering the Sanctuary unlawfully = lashes; entering the Holy of Holies = death at Heaven's hand.

Rabbi Yehuda: 'Holy place' = Holy of Holies; 'within the Curtain' clarifies the same space. Only the Holy of Holies is the forbidden zone — one who enters it without authorization receives lashes. The phrase el penei haKaporet (before the Ark Cover) establishes that one is liable only if entering directly before the Ark, not via a roundabout path.

School of R. Eliezer b. Ya'akov: 'Before [el penei]' throughout Torah indicates directly facing — a paradigm derived from the Yom Kippur sprinkling verse (Leviticus 16:14)
