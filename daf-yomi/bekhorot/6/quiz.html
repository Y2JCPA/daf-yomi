QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does Rav Ashi argue that a verse is necessary to disqualify the tereifa from sacrifice, even setting aside the a fortiori debate?
A. Because tereifa animals are too rare for a logical inference to apply
B. Because the tereifa's general prohibition was permitted — bird sin offering pinching renders it fit — so any a fortiori collapses from the outset
C. Because the verse 'of the herd' was already written and therefore must exclude tereifa
D. Because blemished animals also require a verse and tereifa is fully analogous to them
Correct: B — Rav Ashi's core point: since pinching a bird sin offering validates the offering despite tereifa-like slaughter, the tereifa's prohibition has already been 'permitted' in some context. This unique feature makes every a fortiori inference from blemished or caesarean animals refutable before it begins.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does Rav Ashi respond to Rav Aḥa Sava's argument that a caesarean-born animal refutes the a fortiori inference?
A. A caesarean birth is not a real disqualification, so the case is irrelevant
B. A caesarean-born animal is not sanctified with firstborn status, but a firstborn tereifa IS — so the caesarean case is actually weaker, not stronger
C. The caesarean case involves a different verse entirely and cannot be compared
D. Only blemished animals, not caesarean-born ones, can serve as the basis for an a fortiori
Correct: B — Rav Ashi argues that a caesarean-born animal's position is weaker than a tereifa's: it is not sanctified as a firstborn, whereas a firstborn tereifa is sanctified. This asymmetry means the caesarean cannot refute the a fortiori — it merely illustrates a less-sanctified case.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to the Gemara's resolution, why are all three verses excluding tereifa from sacrifice necessary?
A. Each verse applies to a different species of animal (cattle, sheep, birds)
B. Each verse covers a distinct scenario: injury before sanctification, injury after sanctification, and injury during the tithing process
C. Three witnesses are required for any capital disqualification in Temple law
D. The Sages disagreed and each verse reflects one opinion
Correct: B — Without each verse, one could infer a leniency for that specific scenario. Verse 1 covers post-sanctification injury, Verse 2 covers pre-sanctification dedication, and Verse 3 (animal tithe) covers the case of injury during tithing — each is indispensable.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What does Ben Beteira say should happen if a priest removed the handful with his left hand?
A. The meal offering is permanently disqualified — left-hand removal is an invalid rite
B. The handful is burned on the altar as is, since intention matters more than method
C. He returns the handful to the vessel and removes it again with his right hand — the offering is not disqualified
D. A second, qualified priest must remove a fresh handful from the remaining flour
Correct: C — Ben Beteira holds that left-hand removal does not permanently disqualify — the handful is returned to the meal offering vessel and properly re-removed with the right hand. Rav extends this logic to other unfit removals, such as those performed by a non-priest.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rav Naḥman's final position, at what point does the removal of a handful become irreversible?
A. The moment the unfit person's fingers close around the flour
B. When a second priest witnesses the act of removal
C. When the handful is placed inside a service vessel, completing and sanctifying the rite
D. When the priest who removed it leaves the Temple courtyard
Correct: C — Rav Naḥman concludes that removal alone begins a sacrificial rite but does not complete it. The rite becomes final — and the disqualification sealed — only when the handful is placed in a service vessel (קידוש בכלי). Before that moment, Ben Beteira permits returning and correcting the handful.
