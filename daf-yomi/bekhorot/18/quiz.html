Bekhorot 18 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yehuda's initial position, what is the ruling if a priest intended to leave the blood until the next day?
A. The offering is fit, since no improper act was performed
B. Everyone agrees the offering is unfit
C. The Rabbis deem it fit; Rabbi Eliezer deems it unfit
D. The offering is unfit only if the priest actually left the blood
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda argues that since actually leaving blood overnight invalidates the offering, intending to do so must also invalidate it — making the offering unfit according to all opinions. The Gemara later challenges this from the Yosef the Babylonian story.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why did Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua weep after his exchange with Yosef the Babylonian?
A. He was embarrassed that Yosef had corrected him publicly
B. He realized Yosef had been wrong all along
C. Out of joy at how deeply Torah scholars love Torah
D. Because he could not remember the correct ruling
Correct: C — Rabbi Elazar wept with joy, moved by Yosef's tireless persistence in seeking halakhic truth. He praised him: 'Happy are you, Torah scholars, for whom matters of Torah are exceedingly dear' — citing Psalm 119:97.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to the mishna on 18a, if a priest did NOT pour the oil onto the meal offering, what is the ruling?
A. Unfit — pouring oil is an indispensable rite
B. Fit only if the oil was poured before the handful was removed
C. Fit — this omission does not invalidate the meal offering
D. Unfit if the omission was intentional, fit if accidental
Correct: C — The mishna explicitly lists pouring oil among rites whose omission does not invalidate. The Gemara (18b) clarifies this refers to a case where a non-priest performed the pouring — the meal offering remains fit.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rav Naḥman resolve the contradiction between the mishna (non-priest pouring oil = fit) and Rabbi Shimon's baraita (pouring is a priestly rite)?
A. The mishna refers to an Israelite's meal offering; the baraita refers to a priest's meal offering
B. The mishna is a later ruling that supersedes the baraita
C. Rabbi Shimon's baraita applies only in the Second Temple period
D. The baraita discusses the inner altar only, the mishna the outer
Correct: A — Rav Naḥman distinguishes the two texts by subject: the mishna discusses Israelite meal offerings (where non-priest pouring is acceptable), while Rabbi Shimon's baraita discusses priestly meal offerings (where all rites are exclusively priestly). Rava challenges this distinction.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rabbi Zeira's explanation on 18b, what determines whether omitting the mixing of oil invalidates a meal offering?
A. Whether the priest acted intentionally or accidentally
B. Whether the measure of flour was suitable for mixing — if so, omission does not invalidate
C. Whether the offering was wheat or barley flour
D. Whether the oil was poured before or after the broken pieces were arranged
Correct: B — Rabbi Zeira explains: for any measure of flour that is suitable (i.e., small enough) for proper mixing, failure to actually mix does not retroactively invalidate the offering. There is a mitzva to mix, but it is not indispensable to validity.
