⚖️ Improper Intent: Does Planning to Leave Blood Overnight Invalidate?

Rabbi Yehuda
If the priest actually left the blood overnight, the offering is unfit. Therefore, if he intended to leave it overnight, everyone agrees the offering is also unfit — intention follows the act.
Rabbi Elazar (Amora)
Even in a case where one merely intended to leave the blood (not drink it), the dispute still applies: Rabbis deem it fit, Rabbi Eliezer deems it unfit — there is no distinction between the two sub-cases.
Rabbis
We deem the offering fit when the improper intent does not involve performing a sacrificial rite outside its proper time or place. Intent to drink blood or burn meat falls outside this category.
Rabbi Eliezer
The offering is unfit — even intent to leave blood until the next day disqualifies, since the blood presentation is itself a core sacrificial rite whose time-limit is violated by such intent.
Core Tension Rabbi Yehuda claims leaving blood overnight is universally disqualifying. The Gemara challenges this from a baraita about Yosef the Babylonian — ultimately clarifying that the Tannaitic dispute applies even to intent-to-delay alone.

📖 The Story of Yosef the Babylonian

אַשְׁרֵיכֶם תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה חֲבִיבִין עֲלֵיכֶם בְּיוֹתֵר
אַשְׁרֵיכֶם — Happy Are You, Torah Scholars Yosef the Babylonian studied under Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and then brought a pointed halakhic question to Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua: if a priest intended to leave the blood until the next day, is the offering fit or unfit?

Rabbi Elazar answered: Fit. Yosef returned that evening and asked again — again: Fit. He returned the following morning — again: Fit.

At this point Rabbi Elazar paused and said: 'It seems to me that my halakhot were not accurate until now.' Yosef responded warmly: 'My teacher, yes — the offering is fit, as you have now confirmed what I learned from Rabbi Yehuda!'

Rabbi Elazar's eyes streamed with tears and he declared:
'Happy are you, Torah scholars, for whom matters of Torah are exceedingly dear!'
He cited the verse: מָה אָהַבְתִּי תוֹרָתֶךָ כָּל הַיּוֹם הִיא שִׂיחָתִי — 'How I love Your Torah; it is my meditation all day' (Ps. 119:97)

🫙 Mishna: Rites Whose Omission Does NOT Invalidate the Meal Offering

Did not pour the oil (yetzikah — יְצִיקָה) onto the meal offering
↓
Did not mix the oil into the flour (belilah — בְּלִילָה)
↓
Did not break the loaves into pieces (petitah — פְּתִיתָה) — even if broken into larger pieces than required
↓
Did not add salt (meliḥah — מְלִיחָה) to the meal offering
↓
Did not wave (tenufah — תְּנוּפָה) the omer or the meal offering of a sota
↓
Did not bring the offering to the southwest corner of the altar
↓
✔ In all these cases: the meal offering is fit — these preparatory rites are obligatory, but their omission does not retroactively disqualify

⚖️ Rav Naḥman vs Rava: Can Priests' and Israelites' Meal Offerings Differ?

Rav Naḥman's Resolution

  • Mishna (non-priest pours oil → fit) refers to an Israelite's meal offering
  • Baraita (Rabbi Shimon: pouring is exclusively priestly) refers to a priest's meal offering
  • No contradiction — these are two distinct categories with different rules
  • In a priest's meal offering, all rites are entrusted solely to priests

Rava's Objection

  • The obligation to pour oil in a priest's meal offering is derived from the law of an Israelite's — how can the derived case be stricter than its source?
  • If non-priest pouring is valid for Israelite offerings, it cannot be invalid for priestly offerings
  • Rav Naḥman's distinction collapses: a gezerah shavah cannot create a stringency beyond its model
