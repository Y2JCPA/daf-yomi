QUESTION 1 OF 5
What are the two possible rationales behind Rabbi Shimon's ruling that a meal offering whose handful was removed for another meal offering's sake is valid?
A. Purposeful intent toward any valid offering suffices; OR the verse 'one law for all meal offerings' (Lev 6:7)
B. The offering was already consecrated and cannot be retroactively disqualified; OR the priest acted in good faith
C. The owner gave verbal consent at the time of removal; OR the handful was later re-dedicated
D. A ruling of the Sanhedrin permits it; OR an oral tradition from Sinai overrides the standard rule
Correct: A — Rav Hoshaya's unresolved dilemma: either Rabbi Shimon holds that directed (if misdirected) intent is sufficient, or he derives from Leviticus 6:7 that one uniform law governs all meal offerings. Each rationale yields different outcomes in borderline cases.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does the Gemara establish that the meal offering of jealousy (Sotah) is disqualified when its handful is removed not for its own sake?
A. An explicit verse in Numbers directly disqualifies it
B. A gezera shava on the word עוון connecting it to the sin offering
C. A halakha transmitted as a tradition from Moses at Sinai
D. The word הוא appears in the jealousy offering passage
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman confirms: Numbers 5:15 uses עוון for the jealousy offering and Leviticus 10:17 uses עוון for the sin offering — the shared term creates a verbal analogy (gezera shava), applying the sin offering's stringency. However, this is a partial gezera shava.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What does Rav Huna say in the name of Rav that the word 'it' (הוא) teaches regarding a guilt offering whose owner has already atoned or died?
A. It must be sacrificed immediately as a burnt offering
B. It is redeemed and the money goes to communal offerings
C. It is left to graze until it develops a blemish and is then redeemed — not sacrificed as a burnt offering
D. It is given to the priests to use as they see fit
Correct: C — "It is a guilt offering" (Lev 7:5) — the word הוא teaches that the animal retains its guilt-offering identity. When its purpose lapses (owner died or atoned), it is consigned to grazing until blemished; only then is it redeemed. It may not be brought as a burnt offering while unblemished.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why is the omer meal offering excluded from the mishna's general rule that meal offerings whose handfuls were removed not for their sake are valid?
A. Because the omer atones for a specific sin and is therefore treated like a sin offering
B. Because it requires a Kohen Gadol to perform the removal
C. Because it is both a communal offering and time-set (16 Nisan), placing it outside the mishna's scope
D. Because the omer is brought only once annually and cannot be corrected if misidentified
Correct: C — The Gemara specifies: the mishna's rule covers only individual meal offerings whose time is not fixed. The omer is communal AND time-set — both conditions take it outside the mishna's ruling, so misidentified removal disqualifies it.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is Rabbi Yirmeya's explanation for why the guilt offerings of the nazirite and leper are disqualified when slaughtered not for their sake, unlike other guilt offerings?
A. They are classified as a sub-category of sin offering and inherit its stringency directly
B. They 'render fit' (מכשירין) rather than merely atone (מכפרין), and the Torah is more stringent with offerings in the renders-fit category
C. The Torah explicitly prohibits substituting or misidentifying these guilt offerings in Numbers and Leviticus
D. They require the owner's physical presence and articulated intent at the exact moment of slaughter
Correct: B — Rabbi Yirmeya distinguishes: standard guilt offerings atone for past transgressions. The nazirite's and leper's guilt offerings are prerequisites for rejoining the community or consuming sanctified food — they 'render fit.' The Torah treats this renders-fit function more stringently, disqualifying them when slaughtered not for their sake.
