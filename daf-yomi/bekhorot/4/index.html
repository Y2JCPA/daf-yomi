⚖️ Two Rationales, One Unresolved Dilemma

Possibility A
Rabbi Shimon validates the offering because purposeful intent suffices: removal for the sake of another valid meal offering is still directed, intentional service — misdirection does not nullify the act.
Possibility B
Rabbi Shimon derives from "This is the law of the meal offering" (Lev 6:7) that one law governs all meal offerings — misidentification can therefore never disqualify any of them.
❓ Rav Hoshaya's Dilemma The two rationales yield different rulings in edge cases. Rav Asi refused to resolve the dilemma because the responses of Rabba, Rava, and Rav Ashi each have their own internal difficulties — the question is left open (תיקו).
3 / 9

🔍 Three Attempted Resolutions — All Rejected by Rav Asi

Rabba
Distinguish: removal for the sake of another meal offering is valid (Rabbi Shimon); removal for a completely different type of offering is not covered by his leniency.
Rava
The verse "This is the law of the meal offering" establishes a single law for all meal offerings — wrong-sake removal is universally valid, regardless of which specific offering was intended.
Rav Ashi
Distinguish by vessel: removing a handful for a meal offering in the same vessel — valid. Removing for an offering prepared in a different vessel — disqualified.
⚠️ Rav Asi's Position Each resolution has a flaw Rav Asi could not accept, so the dilemma of Rav Hoshaya remains unresolved throughout the sugya.

🔤 The Word 'It' (הוא) — Same Word, Different Function

Sin Offering

  • "It is a sin offering" (Lev 4:24)
  • הוא = exclusionary marker
  • Slaughtered not for its sake → DISQUALIFIED
  • Torah explicitly set this offering apart as uniquely strict
  • Source: disqualification cannot be derived from it to other offerings

Guilt Offering (Standard)

  • "It is a guilt offering" (Lev 7:5)
  • Same word הוא — but repurposed
  • Slaughtered not for its sake → VALID (generally)
  • Rav Huna (Rav): 'it' teaches that if owner died or atoned otherwise, the animal grazes until blemished, then redeemed — not sacrificed as a burnt offering

Meal Offering of Sinner / Jealousy

  • No direct 'it' verse applies
  • Disqualification derived via gezera shava on עוון
  • Partial analogy: only the surplus-money halakha is fully imported
  • Disqualification for wrong-sake is independently derived from context
  • Slaughtered not for its sake → DISQUALIFIED

🩺 Why Nazirite and Leper Guilt Offerings Are Stricter

Starting Point (Zevachim 2a): All slaughtered offerings sacrificed not for their sake are valid — except the Passover offering and sin offering. Standard guilt offerings are therefore valid when misidentified.
↓
The Problem: The Gemara asks — why are the guilt offerings of the nazirite who became impure and the leper disqualified when slaughtered not for their sake? The general rule should protect them!
↓
Rabbi Yirmeya's Answer: The Torah differentiates between guilt offerings that atone (מכפרין) and those that render fit (מכשירין). Offerings that render fit — enabling the person to re-enter the camp or partake of sanctified food — are held to a more stringent standard.
↓
Rabbi Yehuda's Objection: But there are offerings that render fit whose funds can still be redirected after the owner's death (e.g., to communal offerings) — doesn't that prove leniency exists for them too?
↓
Rav Pappa's Refinement: There is no instance of a fixed manner of rendering fit that continues after the owner's death. The nazirite's and leper's guilt offerings require the specific owner's participation — they cannot transfer, confirming their unique stringency.
