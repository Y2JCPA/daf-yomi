⚖️ Rav Ḥisda's Principle: When Does a Jew Bless?

Rav Ḥisda
For any mitzva that is valid when performed by a gentile, if a Jew performs it he does not recite a blessing — since the act is not exclusively his obligation.
Gemara (challenge)
Circumcision is valid when done by a gentile, yet a Jew who circumcises does recite a blessing! This directly contradicts Rav Ḥisda's rule.
Resolution
Rav himself holds that circumcision by a gentile is invalid — derived from 'himmol yimmol': only one who is circumcised may circumcise others. No contradiction.
Final Distinction
For mitzvot where performance completes the act (circumcision, tefillin), a Jew blesses even if a gentile could validly do it. For preparatory acts (building a sukka), no blessing at that stage.
Core Question Is the Jew's act the completion of the mitzva (גמר מצווה), or merely its preparation? That determines whether a blessing is recited.

📐 Where to Insert the Tzitzit String

Where must the tzitzit hole be located on the garment corner?
↓
Rav Pappa (41b) — Maximum
Hole must be within 3 fingerbreadths of the edge — so the strings remain 'on the corner' as required
Rabbi Ya'akov citing Rabbi Yoḥanan — Minimum
Hole must be at least one full thumb-joint from the edge — otherwise the fabric may tear and strings fall off
↓
Both rules apply together: hole must be between 1 thumb-joint and 3 fingerbreadths from the edge
↓
Story: Ravina's cloak was torn — his hole fell too close to the edge. Rav Samma asked about the violation; Rav Ashi consoled him: 'Sages of Eretz Yisrael are like two of us.' (חד מינייהו כתרי מינן)

🔵 How to Dye Tekhelet (Sky-Blue Wool) — Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yehuda to Abaye

Step 1: Obtain blood of the ḥilazon (חִילָּזוֹן) — a sea creature — and gather various herbs
↓
Step 2: Place the ḥilazon blood and herbs into a pot and bring to a boil together
↓
Step 3: Take a small wad of wool, wrap it in brine, and insert it into the boiling dye mixture as a test
↓
Step 4: Evaluate the test — if the color is correct, dye the actual tzitzit strings לִשְׁמָהּ (for the sake of the mitzva)
↓
⚠️ Critical rule: Burn the test wad of wool so it cannot be mistaken for valid tekhelet — and the dye used in testing is unfit for actual strings (Tanna Kamma; disputed by R' Yoḥanan ben Dahavai)
↓
Purchase rule: Tekhelet has no reliable visual test — it may therefore only be purchased from a recognized מֻמְחֶה (expert)

🙏 Must One Recite a Blessing When Attaching Tzitzit?

Rav Adda bar Ahava
Tzitzit is a chovat habegged — an obligation of the garment. The blessing is recited when wearing the garment: לְהִתְעַטֵּף בְּצִיצִית
Other Opinion
Tzitzit is a chovat hagavra — a personal obligation. The blessing is recited when attaching the strings at the time of making: לַעֲשׂוֹת צִיצִית
Rav Naḥman
I heard Rav Adda reciting 'לַעֲשׂוֹת צִיצִית' while attaching the strings — proof the blessing is at the time of making, not wearing! What is this tzitzi sound I hear?
Rav Adda bar Ahava
I was merely chanting it as a niggun (melody) — not as a formal blessing recitation. My position remains: the blessing belongs to the act of wearing.
Practical Stakes If tzitzit is a garment-obligation, attaching strings to someone else's garment creates no obligation to bless. If it is a personal obligation, the attacher must bless at attachment.

🪡 Rav vs. Shmuel: Which Threads Are Valid for Tzitzit?

Rav — Integral Threads Valid

  • ✅ Threads protruding from fabric like thorns (kotzim)
  • ✅ Leftover sewing threads still attached to the garment
  • ✅ Fringe threads from the garment's woven border (gadil)
  • ❌ Threads that detached and were re-tied — invalid
  • Principle: threads must already be part of the garment structure

Shmuel — Spinning Lishma Required

  • ❌ All of Rav's cases above — invalid for a different reason
  • ❌ Wool swatches not spun specifically for this mitzva — also invalid
  • ✅ Only strings spun לִשְׁמָהּ (for the sake of tzitzit) are valid
  • Analogy to tefillin: just as writing must be לשמה, spinning must be לשמה
  • This dispute parallels a tannaitic dispute about patching tefillin with kosher vs. non-kosher skin
