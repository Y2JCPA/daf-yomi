⚖️ Which Offering Is Included in the Oil Requirement?

Baraita
The meal offering of priests should be included in the oil requirement — it resembles the omer in three ways: both are brought daily, both are prepared by fire, and both are sacrificed on the outer altar (not inside).
Counter
On the contrary! The shewbread should be included — it resembles the omer: both are eaten by priests, and both are subject to piggul (improper sacrificial intent).
Baraita
The verse states 'Anyone' (Lev. 2:1) — referring to all individual meal offerings. The meal of priests is an individual offering; the shewbread is communal. Therefore 'Anyone' includes the former.
Conclusion
Meal offering of priests is included in the oil requirement. The shewbread is excluded. The same 'Anyone' logic applies identically to the frankincense requirement.
Key Verse — Lev. 2:15 "And you shall put oil upon it and lay frankincense upon it" — 'upon it' teaches placement on the omer specifically, excluding the shewbread (no oil) and the libation meal offering (no frankincense).

🚫 Rules for the Sinner's Meal Offering (מנחת חוטא)

Baseline rule: The sinner's meal offering must have neither oil nor frankincense — it atones for sin and therefore bears no adornment (Lev. 5:11)
↓
If oil is added: The offering is immediately disqualified — the Torah states 'He shall not put oil upon it,' a prohibition that renders it invalid with no remedy
↓
If frankincense is added (intact): The offering is NOT disqualified — the priest gathers and removes the frankincense; the offering is then valid
↓
Why the difference? For oil: the verse implies disqualification. For frankincense: 'For it is a sin offering' (Lev. 5:12) indicates the Torah preserves its validity as long as frankincense can be removed
↓
Timing matters: The prohibition applies only if the additive is placed before the handful is removed. After the handful — no prohibition is violated
↓
Special case: Placing a vessel with oil on top of a vessel containing the meal offering does not constitute a violation — indirect contact is insufficient

🔧 Rabba bar Rav Huna's Dilemma — Ground Frankincense on a Sinner's Offering

Rabba bar Rav Huna
If frankincense was ground into fine powder before being placed on the sinner's meal offering — what is the law? The powder cannot be gathered and removed. Is the offering now disqualified?
First Proof (Gemara)
From the mishna: 'He should gather and remove it, and it is then valid' — this implies validity depends on the ability to remove the frankincense. Ground powder cannot be removed → offering is disqualified.
Rejection
Perhaps the mishna gives two independent reasons for validity: (1) the Torah did not disqualify it, AND (2) it can be removed. The dilemma remains unresolved from the mishna alone.
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
A baraita resolves it: If the priest had improper intent (piggul) after the frankincense was gathered, the offering is disqualified — implying the offering was valid at that point. Validity presupposes removability was possible.
Rav Ashi
Any matter within one's power to remedy is not deemed rejected. Intact frankincense CAN be gathered → not rejected → improper intent takes effect. Ground frankincense CANNOT be gathered → offering IS rejected → disqualified.
Rav Adda's Ruling Rav Adda rules in accordance with Rav Ashi, citing Rabbi Yehuda (Yoma 62a): even Rabbi Yehuda, who accepts the concept of 'rejected offerings,' agrees that when remedy is possible, the offering is not rejected. Rav Ashi's principle is authoritative.
