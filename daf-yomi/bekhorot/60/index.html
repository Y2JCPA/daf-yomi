📋 Four Categories of Meal Offerings (Mishna)

Bringing Near Only

  • Fine-flour meal offering
  • Pan meal offering
  • Deep-pan meal offering
  • Oven-baked meal offering
  • Meal offering of priests (R. Yehuda)
  • Meal offering of anointed priest (R. Yehuda)

Waving Only

  • Meal offering of a sota
  • Waved without bringing near to the altar

Both: Bringing Near & Waving

  • Meal offering of a sinner (per verse)
  • Omer meal offering
  • Two loaves (Shavuot)
  • Peace-offering loaves (todah)

Neither

  • Shewbread (lechem hapanim)
  • Meal offering brought with libations
  • Communal obligatory meal offerings

🔢 Rav Pappa's Principle — Menachot 60a Anywhere the mishna states that one 'brings a meal offering,' one must bring ten items of the same type — either ten loaves or ten wafers. This excludes Rabbi Shimon's view that a person who vows a meal offering baked in an oven may bring five loaves and five wafers as a mixture. The first tanna holds one must choose one type and bring ten of that type only.

🧑‍⚖️ Do Priestly Meal Offerings Require Bringing Near?

First Tanna
The meal offering of priests and the meal offering of the anointed priest DO require bringing near (הגשה). They are standard voluntary meal offerings subject to the same rites as any fine-flour offering.
Rabbi Shimon
These offerings do NOT require bringing near. There is no removal of a handful (קמיצה) in their sacrifice — the entire offering is burned. The rule is: wherever waving applies, bringing near also applies. Since there is neither waving nor handful removal here, bringing near is also absent.
Gemara
From where is bringing near derived for all meal offerings? The verse 'And you shall bring it to the priest' (Lev. 2:8) — 'The meal offering' (with the definite article) indicates the entire meal offering must be brought near before the handful is removed.
Baraita
'And you shall bring' also teaches that an individual Israelite may donate and bring the omer offering — otherwise one might have concluded only the community brings it, analogous to the two loaves (a communal wheat offering).
Key Principle Wherever waving (תְּנוּפָה) applies, bringing near (הַגָּשָׁה) also applies — but bringing near can exist without waving.

⚖️ Can Bringing Near for Sota's Offering Be Derived Logically?

Must the sota's meal offering require bringing near?
Attempt to derive by kal vachomer (a fortiori)
↓
Argument: Sinner's meal offering (lenient — no waving) requires bringing near → a fortiori the sota's meal offering should too
↓
Rebuttal: Sinner's offering is from wheat; sota's offering is from barley — different grain, possibly different rules
↓
Counter-proof: Omer (also barley) requires bringing near — so barley grain is no bar to the requirement
↓
Rebuttal: Omer is notable — it comes at a fixed time and requires waving; sota's offering does not wave
↓
Result
Inference reverts to starting point — each case has a unique feature the others lack; common-element inference also fails
Conclusion
A verse is required: 'and it shall be drawn near' teaches that sota's meal offering requires bringing near

📜 Rabbi Shimon vs. Rabbi Yehuda — Competing Derivations

Shared premise: The requirement of bringing near must be derived from a verse for both the omer and the sota's meal offering
↓
Rabbi Shimon: 'And you shall bring' (Lev. 2:8) in the deep-pan context → includes the omer in bringing near. 'And it shall be drawn near' → includes the sota's offering.
↓
Rabbi Yehuda: 'And you shall bring' is needed to teach that an individual Israelite may bring the omer (not only the community). Therefore it is unavailable to derive the omer's bringing-near requirement.
↓
Rabbi Yehuda continues: No verse is needed for the omer because bringing near can be derived a fortiori from the meal offering of a sinner — the omer is more stringent (requires waving), so it certainly requires bringing near.
↓
Challenge to Rabbi Yehuda: But the omer comes from barley while the sinner's offering comes from wheat — how can the inference hold? The barley distinction is neutralized by the sota's offering (also barley, also requires bringing near per Rabbi Yehuda's own verse).
↓
Conclusion: Both rabbis agree a verse-based derivation is required somewhere; they dispute only which verse covers which offering — 'and you shall bring' vs. 'and it shall be drawn near.'
