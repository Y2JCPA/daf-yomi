QUESTION 1 OF 5
When one amplificatory expression follows directly after another (ribuy achar ribuy), what is the legal effect according to the Gemara on Menachot 60a?
A. It expands the law to include additional cases
B. It restricts the application of the law
C. It creates a new independent obligation
D. It establishes a rabbinic fence around the Torah law
Correct: B — The principle established is that ribuy achar ribuy — one amplificatory expression following another — serves only to restrict (mi'ut), limiting the law's scope rather than broadening it. This is applied to the oil and frankincense restrictions.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rav Pappa's principle, if someone vows to bring a meal offering baked in an oven, which combination is valid?
A. Five loaves and five wafers
B. Any combination totaling ten items
C. Ten loaves or ten wafers — one type only
D. One loaf as a representative of the whole
Correct: C — Rav Pappa rules that a mishna's mention of 'a meal offering' means ten items of a single type. This explicitly excludes Rabbi Shimon's leniency of mixing five loaves and five wafers. The first tanna requires homogeneity: all loaves or all wafers.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Shimon rule that the meal offering of the anointed priest does not require bringing near (הגשה)?
A. Because it is a communal offering brought by the public
B. Because it is made from barley, not wheat
C. Because there is no removal of a handful in its sacrifice
D. Because the verse 'and you shall bring' does not apply to priests
Correct: C — Rabbi Shimon's reasoning: wherever waving (tenufah) applies, bringing near (hagashah) also applies. Priestly meal offerings are entirely burned — there is no handful removal and no waving. Since neither condition applies, bringing near is also not required.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In the baraita's extended logical argument (60b), why does the inference that the sota's meal offering requires bringing near ultimately 'revert to its starting point'?
A. Because sota's offering is a rabbinic institution, not biblical
B. Because each case used in the inference has a unique feature the other lacks, preventing a valid common-element derivation
C. Because the sota's offering does not require a priest to perform the service
D. Because the sota's offering comes from wheat, not barley
Correct: B — The baraita runs through multiple inference attempts — from the voluntary offering, the sinner's offering, the omer, and common elements — but each case has a distinguishing characteristic that invalidates using it as a proof. When no inference survives, a verse is required.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yehuda both use verses to derive requirements for bringing near. What does Rabbi Yehuda use 'and you shall bring' (Lev. 2:8) to teach, if not the omer's bringing near?
A. That the meal offering of a sinner requires bringing near
B. That priests are exempt from bringing near
C. That an individual Israelite (not only the community) may donate and bring the omer offering
D. That ten items are required for every meal offering vow
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehuda holds that 'and you shall bring' is needed to teach that an individual Jew may bring the omer — without this verse one might have concluded that only the community brings it (since the community brings the two loaves, a communal wheat meal offering). Therefore this verse is unavailable for deriving the omer's bringing-near requirement.
