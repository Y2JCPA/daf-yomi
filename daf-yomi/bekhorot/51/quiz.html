QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava, why does the chavitin (High Priest's griddle-cake offering) override Shabbat?
A. Because it accompanies the daily tamid offering which itself overrides Shabbat
B. Because the verse says 'on a griddle,' requiring a vessel — so it cannot be prepared before Shabbat and must be baked fresh
C. Because the High Priest is personally commanded to offer it and personal obligations override Shabbat
D. Because it is a communal offering comparable to the daily tamid
Correct: B — Rava derives the Shabbat override from 'on a griddle' (Leviticus 6:14), indicating a vessel is required. Since the griddle-vessel is needed for preparation, the chavitin cannot simply be baked the day before — it must be fresh, requiring Shabbat to be overridden.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
From which offering does the baraita ultimately derive that the chavitin requires 3 log of oil?
A. The voluntary meal offering (minḥat nedavah)
B. The meal offering of a sinner (minḥat ḥotet)
C. The libation meal offering accompanying the sacrifice of sheep
D. The libation meal offering accompanying the sacrifice of bulls
Correct: C — The chavitin and the libation meal offering of sheep both involve one-tenth of an ephah of flour. This shared characteristic (along with shared traits like vessel requirement and Shabbat override) makes the sheep-libation the better analogy, yielding 3 log of oil for the chavitin.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rabbi Shimon derive from the verse that the community must fund the chavitin when the High Priest dies?
A. From the phrase 'the anointed priest from among his sons shall offer it' — sons = the nation
B. From 'it shall be wholly made to smoke' — wholly implies communal ownership
C. From 'it is a statute forever [olam] to the Lord' — olam (world/community) means the public pays
D. From 'with the oil' — oil symbolizes communal anointing and therefore public responsibility
Correct: C — Rabbi Shimon reads olam (eternity) in 'a statute forever [olam] to the Lord' as a double meaning — olam also means world/community. The offering is therefore a statute of the world, meaning it comes from public funds.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rabbi Abbahu resolve the apparent contradiction between Rabbi Shimon's Torah-law position and the Shekalim mishna listing this as a Rabbinic court ordinance?
A. He says Rabbi Shimon retracted his Torah-law position in favor of the court ordinance
B. He says the mishna in Shekalim is a minority opinion and Rabbi Shimon disagrees with it
C. He says there were two stages: Torah law required community funding, and the court later enacted a formal ordinance reinforcing it after heirs sometimes refused to pay
D. He says the Torah law applies only during Temple times while the ordinance applies post-Temple
Correct: C — Rabbi Abbahu explains both norms coexist without contradiction: the Torah baseline mandates community funding, but after cases where heirs paid voluntarily (causing confusion) or refused entirely (threatening the offering), the court formally ordained community funding — reinforcing, not replacing, the Torah law.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does the baraita reject deriving the chavitin's oil from the voluntary meal offering (suggesting only 1 log)?
A. Because the voluntary meal offering does not require oil at all
B. Because the voluntary meal offering requires two-tenths of an ephah, not one-tenth like the chavitin
C. Because the voluntary meal offering lacks the characteristics of the chavitin — no vessel required, no Shabbat override, not an obligatory fixed offering
D. Because the voluntary meal offering is brought by non-priests while the chavitin is a priestly offering
Correct: C — The baraita uses the mnemonic letters tav-beit-shin-tet to represent characteristics the chavitin shares with the sheep-libation offering (vessel required, fixed obligation, Shabbat override, communal context). The voluntary meal offering shares none of these, making it a poor analogy source despite both using one-tenth of an ephah.
