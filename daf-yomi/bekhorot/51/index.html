⚔️ Two Great Disputes of Menachot 51

Rabbi Yishmael b. R. Yoḥanan ben Beroka
3 log of oil required for the chavitin — derived by verbal analogy from the libation meal offering of sheep, which also uses one-tenth of an ephah of flour.
Rabbi Shimon
3 log of oil required — the verse adds oil 'here' (chavitin) and 'there' (sheep libation). Just as the sheep libation uses 3 log, so does the chavitin.
Rabbi Yehuda
Heirs fund the offering. Derived from: 'the anointed priest from among his sons shall offer it' — the sons (heirs) both bring and fund it from the estate.
Rabbi Shimon
Community funds the offering. Derived from: 'it is a statute forever [olam] to the Lord' — olam (world/community) indicates the public bears this cost.
Rabbi Abbahu's Resolution Community funding is Torah law. The court ordinance merely reinforced it after some heirs refused to pay — two overlapping obligations, not a contradiction.

🫙 How Much Oil for the Chavitin? — The Gezerah Shavah Analysis

Problem: Leviticus 6:14 says 'it shall be made with the oil' — extra oil is mandated, but how much?
↓
Path A: Derive from Voluntary Meal Offering
Shared traits: one-tenth ephah, individual → 1 log oil
BUT: voluntary offering lacks vessel, Shabbat override, fixed obligation
Path B: Derive from Sheep Libation Offering
Shared traits: one-tenth ephah, vessel required, Shabbat override, obligatory → 3 log oil
Characteristics: tav-beit-shin-tet match
↓
Both Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Shimon conclude: ✅ 3 log of oil are required for the chavitin — stronger analogy wins

⚰️ High Priest Dies Mid-Year — Who Funds the Daily Chavitin?

Rabbi Yehuda
The heirs fund it from the estate. Source: 'the anointed priest from among his sons shall offer it' — his sons bring it and therefore pay for it. They offer whole portions (not halves), because 'it' implies the full tenth-ephah.
Rabbi Shimon
The community funds it from public property. Source: 'it is a statute forever [olam] to the Lord' — the world [olam] = the public pays. The sons verse is needed for other teachings.
Gemara
Challenge to Rabbi Shimon: A mishna in Shekalim (7:6) lists community-funding of the chavitin as one of seven court ordinances — implying it is Rabbinic, not Torah law!
Rabbi Abbahu
Resolution: Rabbi Shimon holds this is indeed Torah law. There were two stages: (1) Torah law mandating community funding, then (2) a court ordinance reinforcing it after heirs sometimes refused to pay.
📋 Mishna (51b) If the High Priest died and they did not appoint another in his stead — R. Shimon: brought from public funds; R. Yehuda: brought from the property of the deceased priest's heirs.

📊 Competing Sources for Chavitin Oil Quantity

1 Log of Oil

  • Source: voluntary meal offering (minḥat nedavah)
  • Shared trait: one-tenth ephah of flour
  • Weakness: voluntary offering has no vessel, no Shabbat override, not obligatory
  • Weakness: different category entirely — individual vs. priestly duty
  • ❌ Rejected as the analogy source

3 Log of Oil

  • Source: libation meal offering of sheep (minḥat nesachim)
  • Shared traits: one-tenth ephah, vessel required, Shabbat override, fixed obligation
  • Supported by R. Yishmael b. R. Yoḥanan ben Beroka (verbal analogy)
  • Supported by R. Shimon (verse addition: 'with the oil')
  • ✅ Accepted — final halakha

More Than 3 Log

  • Source: if derived from bulls/rams libation offering
  • Bulls libation uses more oil per offering
  • Weakness: chavitin differs from bulls offering on multiple grounds
  • Sheep offering is closer match (same flour quantity)
  • ❌ Rejected as overly expansive

🔄 Evolution of Chavitin Funding — Rabbi Abbahu's Historical Account

Stage 1 — Torah Baseline: When a High Priest dies without a replacement, Torah law requires the griddle-cake offering to continue from communal/public funds (R. Shimon's position).
↓
Stage 2 — Voluntary Heir Payment: In practice, some heirs willingly paid for the chavitin from the deceased High Priest's estate — acting beyond the strict Torah requirement.
↓
Stage 3 — Problem of Refusal: Other heirs refused to pay, causing uncertainty about whether the daily offering would be brought at all — threatening continuity of Temple service.
↓
Stage 4 — Court Ordinance: The rabbinic court formally enacted one of its seven ordinances: the chavitin must come from public funds in such cases, removing all ambiguity.
↓
Result — Two Overlapping Norms: Torah law mandates community funding; the court ordinance reinforces it. Rabbi Shimon holds both, R. Abbahu explains this is not a contradiction.
