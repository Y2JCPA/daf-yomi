🫙 Do Service Vessels Sanctify Without Intent?

Challenge
If a priest returns the handful to the meal offering vessel, it should become sanctified — and therefore disqualified! The vessel sanctifies whatever is placed inside it!
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Service vessels sanctify items ONLY when placed there with specific intent to be sanctified. Since the priest intends to return the handful, not sanctify it, no sanctification occurs.
Inference
This implies that WITH intent, service vessels DO sanctify. But Reish Lakish raised precisely this as a dilemma before Rabbi Yoḥanan — was it ever resolved?
Rav Amram
Even granting that vessels sanctify without intent — one can return the handful in a heaped bowl (levisa), where it rests on top and never enters the vessel interior. But this raises further questions…
Central Question of the Daf Service vessels sanctify their contents — but does this require the priest's conscious intent, or does it happen automatically on contact?

🔄 The Avimi & Rav Ḥisda Paradox

Gemara
The text says Rabbi Avimi learned in the study hall of Rav Ḥisda — but wasn't Avimi the elder and greater sage? How could he sit in his student's academy?
Rav Ḥisda
I received many blows (kulfei) from Avimi on account of that halakha — he would mock me when I questioned his rulings!
Resolution
Avimi was indeed the original teacher, but tractate Menaḥot was 'uprooted' from him — he forgot it entirely. So he came to Rav Ḥisda, his own student, to recover his lost learning.
Rav Naḥman
I encountered Avimi returning from Rav Ḥisda's study hall and asked him how one removes a handful. He could only answer by pointing at vessels — unable even to articulate the words!
Life Lesson Even a great Amora can forget his learning. Avimi returned to his own student's beit midrash in humility — to recover what was lost.

📐 Removing the Handful From a Vessel on the Ground

Avimi's Position

  • The vessel MUST be raised off the ground before use
  • Demonstrated by pointing to a vessel on the floor: 'The priest places it in this vessel'
  • Rav Naḥman objected: But it is resting on the ground!
  • Avimi: And let it require even THREE priests — one to raise the vessel, one to sanctify, one to remove the handful
  • Rav Naḥman: But the mishna (12a) lists all rites and never mentions raising the vessel!

Rava's Ruling

  • Removal from a vessel on the GROUND is obviously valid
  • Proof: Bowls of frankincense removed from the Table of Shewbread — the Table rests on the ground!
  • The mishna (12a) lists all rites with no mention of raising → raising is NOT required
  • Dilemma remains: Is SANCTIFICATION valid when vessel is on the ground?
  • Answer: Derived from blood collection rules — same logic applies

🩸 Blood Collection: The Full-Measure Requirement

Source Verse (Lev. 4): Rav Taḥlifa bar Avimi derives — blood is not validly sanctified when collected across two separate vessels
↓
Dilemma raised: What about collecting less than a full measure in a single vessel? Parallel to water of purification (invalid) — or a distinct rule?
↓
Rabbi Zerika citing Rabbi Elazar: Even for blood — collecting less than a full kezayit-measure in one vessel does NOT constitute valid sanctification
↓
Three derivations from one verse:
(1) 'the priest shall dip' — must dip fully
(2) 'in the blood' — full measure must be present in the vessel initially
(3) 'of the blood' — must sprinkle from the blood of this particular matter
↓
Why both phrases are needed: 'Shall dip' alone → might permit wiping if measure was once present. 'In the blood' alone → might permit if full measure was there at start even if now depleted. Both together → full measure + actual dipping required
↓
Practical consequence: Residual blood on the priest's finger from a previous sprinkling does NOT count toward the next — he must draw fresh blood from the vessel each time
