📜 Mishna: The Frontplate and the Impure Handful

וְהַצִּיץ מְרַצֶּה עַל הַטֻּמְאָה — The frontplate effects acceptance for impurity (Exodus 28:38)
צִיץ — The Frontplate If the handful (kometz) of a meal offering became ritually impure and the priest nonetheless burned it on the altar, the frontplate (tzitz) worn by the High Priest effects acceptance — and the remainder is eaten by the priests. But if the handful left its designated area before being sacrificed, or was sacrificed with wrong intent (piggul), the tzitz does not effect acceptance and the offering is disqualified.

⚔️ Probing the Limits of Tzitz Atonement

Rabbi Zeira
Why doesn't tzitz atone for an offering that left the courtyard? Its prohibition was also permitted in a certain case — Pesach sacrifices could be eaten in the streets of Jerusalem!
Abaye
The verse says 'that they may be accepted before the Lord' (Exod 28:38). Tzitz only covers a sin whose general prohibition was permitted before the Lord — inside the Temple. An offering outside the courtyard was never permitted before the Lord.
Rabbi Ile'a
Then why not say tzitz atones for left-hand service? On Yom Kippur, the High Priest performs the incense service with his left hand inside the Holy of Holies — a permitted case!
Abaye
The verse says Aaron 'shall bear the sin committed with the sacred items' — this means tzitz atones for a sin committed with the offering itself, and I deferred it. This excludes the incense spoon, which is a vessel, not the offering.
Rav Ashi
An additional reason: the verse says hakodashim (plural) — the tzitz atones for sins involving multiple offerings. Left-hand service is a singular act and falls outside this category entirely.
Rav Sima's Challenge Why not say tzitz atones for a blemished animal that was sacrificed? Blemished animals were permitted in certain cases (e.g., as a voluntary gift). — Rav Ashi answers: The verse explicitly states 'it shall not be accepted' (Lev 22:23) — a direct biblical rejection that overrides any tzitz atonement.

📊 Tzitz Coverage: Accepted vs. Rejected Cases

✅ Tzitz EFFECTS Acceptance

  • Handful of meal offering became ritually impure
  • Blood of an offering became impure (sprinkled unwittingly)
  • Flesh and fat of offering became impure inside the Temple
  • Communal offerings brought in a state of impurity
  • Individual's impure offering — with conditions (see baraita dispute)

❌ Tzitz Does NOT Atone

  • Offering that left the Temple courtyard (yatza)
  • Left-hand service — sin not committed 'with the offering itself'
  • Blemished animal — Torah says explicitly 'not accepted'
  • Piggul — wrong intention at the time of slaughter
  • Offerings of gentiles — 'for them' excludes non-Jews

🔍 Resolving the Baraita Contradiction (Amud B)

Baraita 1: If impure blood was sprinkled unwittingly → accepted. If sprinkled intentionally → not accepted.
↓
Baraita 2: Tzitz effects acceptance for blood, flesh, and fat that became impure — seemingly regardless of intent.
↓
Rav Yosef (rejected): Baraita 1 = Rabbi Eliezer; Baraita 2 = Rabbi Yosei. Rejected — Rabbi Yosei's leniency toward teruma does not automatically extend to consecrated items.
↓
Rav Hisda (rejected): Reverse the attributions — Baraita 1 = Rabbi Yosei; Baraita 2 = Rabbi Eliezer. Rejected — Rabbi Eliezer's position on teruma cannot be assumed to carry over to kodashim.
↓
Ravina (final): Distinguish the dimension of impurity contraction (unwitting/intentional) from the dimension of sprinkling (unwitting/intentional). Each dimension is ruled independently. Unwitting contraction → always accepted; intentional contraction → not accepted.
↓
Rav Sheila (alternative): The reverse split — sprinkling determines acceptance; how impurity was contracted is irrelevant to the tzitz's operation.
