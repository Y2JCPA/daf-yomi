QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the baraita, why may an individual not bring two loaves as a voluntary gift offering?
A. Individuals may never bring grain offerings of any kind
B. The verse 'You may bring' is in the plural, indicating the commandment is addressed to the community
C. The two loaves contain leaven, which individuals are never permitted to bring
D. The verse 'You may bring' is in the singular, which excludes communities but allows individuals
Correct: B — The plural form 'you may bring' addresses the community, teaching that only a community may bring two loaves — and only as an obligatory offering, not as a voluntary gift.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the key halakhic difference between leaven and honey regarding Temple practice?
A. Honey is permitted for certain altar offerings; leaven is never permitted anywhere in the Temple
B. Leaven's prohibition is permitted for the two loaves and thanks offering bread; honey is only permitted for priests eating meal offerings
C. Both leaven and honey are permitted for the two loaves on Shavuot
D. Honey is permitted for the two loaves; leaven is only permitted for eating purposes
Correct: B — Leaven's general prohibition is permitted in the Temple context for specific leavened offerings (two loaves, thanks offering bread), while honey has no such offering exception — it is only permitted for priests eating the remainder of meal offerings.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Ḥisda, what is the practical halakhic difference between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva?
A. Whether a community may bring two loaves as a gift offering
B. One who brings the meat of a bird sin offering up to the altar — Eliezer says no violation, Akiva says violation
C. Whether burning less than an olive-bulk of leaven is prohibited
D. Whether the leper's log of oil is subject to the notar prohibition
Correct: B — Rav Ḥisda says the practical difference is the bird sin offering meat case: Rabbi Eliezer requires prior fire application to some part (absent here), while Rabbi Akiva prohibits bringing any consecrated item not designated for fire.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the root of the disagreement between Abaye and Rava about partial burning of leaven?
A. Whether the verse 'you shall not burn' applies to the altar or to all fires
B. Whether a handful (kometz) can exist in a quantity less than two olive-bulks
C. Whether burning honey on the altar also constitutes a violation of the leaven prohibition
D. Whether the two loaves are subject to the general leaven prohibition
Correct: B — Abaye holds a handful can be smaller than two olive-bulks (so partial burning is significant), while Rava holds a handful is never less than two olives — making sub-olive burning halakhically irrelevant.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Abaye, why is one not flogged for burning a leaven-and-honey mixture on the altar?
A. The prohibition only applies to burning leaven alone, not mixtures
B. Flogging requires a positive commandment to be violated alongside a negative one
C. The prohibition 'you shall not burn' is a lav shebiklelut — a general prohibition covering multiple acts — which does not trigger malkot
D. The mixture renders both the leaven and honey non-identifiable, negating the prohibition
Correct: C — Abaye holds that a lav shebiklelut — a prohibition general enough to encompass many distinct acts — does not trigger flogging. Since 'you shall not burn any of it' covers leaven, honey, and more, it is too broad to carry malkot liability.
