🍋 Leaven vs Honey: What Is Permitted in the Temple?

Leaven (חָמֵץ)

  • ❌ May not be burned on the outer altar (Lev 2:11)
  • ✅ Permitted: Two loaves of Shavuot are leaven
  • ✅ Permitted: 2 of 4 loaves of the thanks offering are leaven
  • ❌ Priests may NOT eat meal offerings with leaven
  • 📌 General prohibition permitted for specific Temple offerings

Honey (דְּבַשׁ)

  • ❌ May not be burned on the outer altar (Lev 2:11)
  • ❌ No Temple offering context where honey is brought to the fire
  • ✅ Priests MAY eat their portion of meal offerings with honey
  • 📌 Prohibition permitted only for eating the remainder, not for offering
  • 📌 No communal offering exception analogous to the two loaves

🏛️ May an Individual Bring Two Loaves as a Gift Offering?

Initial Reasoning
An individual may donate two loaves as a voluntary gift offering — just as the community brings them on Shavuot as an obligatory offering!
Baraita
The verse says "You may bring" in the plural — addressed to the community. This tells us: only a community may bring two loaves, not an individual acting on his own.
Counter-thought
Perhaps only an individual is excluded — but a community acting voluntarily (not as an obligation) could still bring two loaves as a gift?
Baraita — Final Ruling
Neither an individual nor a community may bring two loaves as a voluntary gift. They are exclusively a communal obligatory offering of Shavuot. The word אֹתָם ("them") comes to teach this exclusivity.
📜 Source Verse "As an offering of first produce you may bring" — the plural 'you' restricts this to the community's obligatory Shavuot offering alone

🐦 Rami bar Ḥama's Dilemma: Bird Sin Offering Meat on the Altar

The Case: One takes the meat of a bird sin offering and places it on the altar.
In a normal bird sin offering, the blood is sprinkled and the body is burned — the meat is never separately offered.
↓
Question to Rav Ḥisda: Does this violate the prohibition of bringing up notar (leftovers)?
↓
🔵 Interpretation A
The prohibition requires that fire has already been applied to some portion of the offering.

In a bird sin offering, no part is designated for the altar fire separately — the whole bird is burned.
→ No violation of notar
🔴 Interpretation B
The prohibition applies to any consecrated item brought to the altar that was not designated to be burned there.

The meat was never meant to be offered this way.
→ Violation occurs
↓
Gemara: This dilemma maps onto the tannaitic dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva

🌾 Abaye vs Rava: What Counts as a Punishable Burning of Leaven?

Abaye
There is such a thing as a handful smaller than two olive-bulks. Therefore, burning even less than an olive-bulk of leaven can be a halakhically significant act. The verse 'any leaven' teaches that one is liable even for burning less than an olive-bulk.
Rava
There is no such entity as a handful less than two olive-bulks. A handful is always at least two olives. Therefore, the prohibition 'do not burn it' refers to the full two-olive handful. Burning less than an olive-bulk is halakhically insignificant.
Abaye
Since a partial handful is possible and significant, 'any leaven' adds a new prohibition: one is liable even for burning part of the handful.
Rava
Since a handful is never less than two olives, 'any leaven' only teaches that one is liable for burning any complete handful — not a sub-olive amount, which is never counted.
Root Dispute Whether a kometz (handful) can exist in a quantity less than two olive-bulks — a dispute with cascading consequences for the laws of leaven

👊 Abaye vs Rava: How Many Lashes for Burning Leaven + Honey?

Rava
One who burns a mixture of leaven AND honey on the altar receives four sets of lashes. The verse 'any leaven, and any honey, you shall not burn any of it' contains multiple distinct prohibitions — each triggers its own flogging.
Abaye
One is not flogged for a lav shebiklelut — a general prohibition covering many different prohibited acts. 'You shall not burn' encompasses leaven, honey, and more in a single mitzva. Such prohibitions do not trigger malkot.
Version 1 of Abaye
Abaye concedes two lashes: one specifically for leaven and one specifically for honey — since those sub-clauses are specific enough. He only disputes the additional lashes from the general 'any of it.'
Version 2 of Abaye
Abaye holds not even one set of lashes. The entire prohibition 'you shall not burn' is general in nature — like the prohibition against muzzling — and no specific act under it triggers flogging.
🔑 Key Principle לָאו שֶׁבִּכְלָלוֹת — A prohibition that encompasses many distinct acts: according to Abaye, one is not flogged for violating it, since the Torah did not single out one specific act
