⚖️ Is Salt Indispensable? Rav Meets the Mishna

Rabbi Yehuda
There is a covenant written about salt, ensuring that offerings must always be salted — this establishes it as an eternal, indispensable requirement.
Rabbi Shimon
It is stated: "An everlasting covenant of salt" (Num. 18:19), and it is stated regarding the meal offering in Lev. 2:13 — a gezera shava links them.
Rav Yosef
Rav holds that only rites repeated in the verses are indispensable. Salt is not truly repeated — so a meal offering without salt is still fit, matching our mishna.
Gemara
But isn't salt repeated? "Every meal offering you shall season with salt" — and also "You shall sacrifice salt with all your offerings" (Lev. 2:13)! That looks like two verses!
Resolution
The second verse is required for the klal-prat-klal derivation — it cannot simultaneously serve to establish repetition. Alternatively: since "covenant" is written about salt, it is treated as if it were repeated.
Key Principle The word בְּרִית (covenant) applied to salt elevates it to the status of a repeated commandment — making it indispensable even by Rav's own logic.

🔥 Limbs of Burnt Offering vs. Blood: The Salt Debate

🟢 Limbs of Burnt Offering — Salted

  • Burned directly on altar fire
  • Requires wood (like meal offering)
  • Subject to me'ila (misuse of consecrated property)
  • Forbidden to consume while ritually impure
  • Must be placed in a sacred vessel
  • Requires accompanying wine libation
  • → 6 parallels with meal offering: salt required

🔴 Blood — Not Salted

  • Sprinkled or poured — not burned on fire
  • Does not require wood for itself
  • Different rules for ritual impurity
  • Different me'ila rules apply
  • Not brought in one vessel with libations
  • Does permit the offering (like handful) — but insufficient alone
  • → Not comparable enough to handful: no salt

Voluntary Wood as a Korban

וְכִי תַקְרִיב קָרְבַּן מִנְחָה — "קָרְבָּן" לְרַבּוֹת עֵצִים לַמִּזְבֵּחַ (ויקרא ב:א)
🪵 Novel Halakha The superfluous word korban in Leviticus 2:1 — beyond what is needed for the meal offering — teaches that one may voluntarily donate wood as an independent offering to be burned on the altar.

Rava: Such donated wood requires the removal of a handful, paralleling the meal offering.
Rav Pappa: Disagrees — wood does not require a handful.

Despite being burned on the altar, wood is excluded from the salt requirement by the phrase "meal offering of yours" — wood is not "yours" in the same personal sense as a meal offering you have prepared.
