🕎 The Menorah's Indivisible Wholeness

כֻּלָּן מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה — All are indispensable to one another
Core Principle Every component of the Menorah — its seven branches, seven lamps, goblets, knobs, and flowers — is indispensable: the absence of any one invalidates the whole. The Gemara explores the biblical sources for these requirements and a rich dispute over what materials may be used to fashion the Menorah. Similarly, all four Torah passages in tefillin and mezuza are mutually indispensable. The daf also addresses the validity of sprinklings performed not for their own sake, resolved by Rava's key distinction between technical effect and fulfillment of obligation.

🩸 Sprinkling Not For Its Own Sake — Two Baraitot in Conflict

Rav Yosef
The baraitot do not contradict each other. The first follows Rabbi Eliezer, who juxtaposes the leper's guilt offering with the Nazirite's guilt offering — making sprinkling 'not for its own sake' invalid. The second follows the Rabbis, who reject that juxtaposition and deem it valid.
Rava
Both baraitot follow the Rabbis. When one baraita says 'valid,' it means the blood achieved its technical effect — rendering the offering permitted for consumption. But the owner has not fulfilled his personal obligation. Effect and fulfillment are distinct!
Halakhic Takeaway Rava's resolution prevails: a sprinkling performed not for its own sake can simultaneously be 'valid' (technically effective) yet leave the mitzva obligation undischarged — a crucial distinction in Temple law.

Unfit — must come from a complete block (הָאֶשֶׁת); assembling scraps is invalid

↓

Preferred — hammered from a single block per the verse
Cast/Molded ✓
Valid post-facto — "will be made" permits other methods

📜 Dispute: Can Temple Vessels Be Made from Non-Metal Materials?

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi

  • Applies generalizations and details (כְּלָל וּפְרָט)
  • General: 'make a Candelabrum of...' → Detail: 'pure gold'
  • Only items similar to the detail — i.e., metals — are valid
  • Wood is invalid for fashioning Temple service vessels
  • Supported by a baraita: wooden vessels are unfit

Rabbi Yosei son of Rabbi Yehuda

  • Applies amplifications and restrictions (רִבּוּי וּמִעוּט)
  • Amplification → Restriction → Re-amplification
  • Anything similar in kind to the restriction is included
  • Wood is also valid for Temple service vessels
  • Cited proof: Hasmonean kings used wooden candelabra in the Temple

Handbreadths 1–3 (base): The base and its decorative flower — the foundation of the Menorah
↓
Handbreadths 4–5 (bare shaft): Plain shaft with no decoration or branches
↓
Handbreadth 6 (first knob): A knob from which two branches emerge — one left, one right — rising to the full height of the Menorah
↓
Handbreadth 7 (bare): Plain shaft above the first knob
↓
Handbreadth 8 (second knob): Another knob with two more branches emerging toward each side
↓
Handbreadth 9 (bare): Plain shaft above second knob
↓
Handbreadth 10 (third knob): Final knob with the last pair of branches — all six side-branches now accounted for
↓
Handbreadths 11–18 (central shaft continues): Goblets shaped like Alexandrian cups (long and narrow); knobs like Cherethite apples; flowers like columns of the Temple porch
