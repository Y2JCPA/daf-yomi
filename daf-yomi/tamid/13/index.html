🌿 Menachot 13 — Piggul Intent: What Combines, What Stays Separate?

Can intent to eat half an olive-bulk + intent to burn half an olive-bulk combine for piggul liability?
↓
Abaye's ruling
No — intent to eat and intent to burn are categorically distinct; they never join
Mishna still needed
Teaches the novel case: intending per typical manner for one half, but not for the other
↓
Rabbi Yosei: removing handful with next-day intent about remainder or handful → piggul
↓
But intent about the frankincense next day → Rabbi Yosei: only unfit, not piggul — frankincense is a separate permitting factor
3 / 9

🔗 Abaye: Why Is the Mishna Necessary?

Abaye
Why do I need this mishna? We can already infer from it that intent to eat and intent to burn do not combine — each type of intent must reach a full olive-bulk on its own!
Gemara
The mishna is still needed! It teaches the novel case where one intended to eat something typically eaten and also to eat something typically burned — one intent in accord with typical manner, one not.
Abaye
But the case of eating half + burning half is even more obvious — for each half he intends in accordance with its own typical manner! One would think those halves should combine!
Gemara
Precisely — you might have thought they do combine since both intents align with typical use. The mishna teaches they do not. Eating-intent and burning-intent remain forever separate.
Underlying Rule Intent to eat and intent to burn are not merely quantitatively different — they are categorically distinct modes of piggul intent that can never be combined.

✋ Rabbi Yosei vs. The Rabbis: Piggul in Meal Offerings

Rabbi Yosei

  • Handful removed with intent to eat remainder next day → piggul ✓
  • Handful removed with intent to burn handful next day → piggul ✓
  • Handful removed with intent to burn frankincense next day → only unfit, no karet
  • Reason: Frankincense is a separate permitting factor — not part of the meal offering's permitting unit; one permitting factor does not render another piggul

The Rabbis

  • Intent about any component of the offering next day → piggul
  • Frankincense intent also renders the meal offering piggul
  • Argument: Just as slaughtering an animal with intent about altar-portions = piggul, so any next-day intent here qualifies
  • The permitting-factor-cannot-render-another-piggul rule applies only when the factors are of different types — not here

🌿 Frankincense: Does One Permitting Factor Render Another Piggul?

Meal offering has two permitting factors:
1. Handful (kometz) — burned on altar
2. Frankincense (levonah) — burned separately
↓
Priest burns frankincense with intent to burn the other bowl or the handful next day
↓
Rabbi Yosei
Not piggul — one permitting factor cannot render a different permitting factor piggul; offering is merely unfit
The Rabbis
Piggul — this rule applies only across different-type offerings; within the same offering, any permitting factor counts
↓
Reish Lakish: Same applies to the two bowls of frankincense of the shewbread — burning one bowl with next-day intent for the other does not render the shewbread piggul

👨‍🍳 Non-Priest Collecting Frankincense: Rav Mari's Proof from the Mishna

Rabbi Yannai rules: A non-priest who collects (kometz) frankincense from a meal offering performs an invalid act and disqualifies the offering.
↓
Question asked: What is the halakhic source? Why should frankincense collection require a priest like handful removal?
↓
Rabbi Yirmeya: Derived from scripture — the verse applying priestly service requirements extends to frankincense collection just as to kometz.
↓
Rav Mari's alternate proof from Mishna 12a: That mishna lists the placing of the handful in a service vessel as one of four piggul-generating rites of the meal offering.
↓
Rav Mari argues: Since placing the handful in a vessel is listed among significant rites, it must be because it is indispensable — without this act, the handful cannot be sacrificed. This makes it comparable to blood collection in animal offerings.
↓
Gemara rejects the proof: The placing-in-vessel rite is significant not because of indispensability but because it involves vessel-sanctification of a permitting factor — the same principle applies to frankincense collection.

🍞 Two Shavuot Loaves: Rav Huna vs. Rav Naḥman

Rav Huna
Rabbi Yosei would say: if one had piggul intent for one of the two Shavuot loaves, only that loaf becomes piggul. The other loaf remains valid! Each loaf is judged independently — just as impurity of one limb does not contaminate the whole offering.
Rav Naḥman
I object from a baraita! The Rabbis say: there is no karet liability for the two loaves unless one had piggul intent for an olive-bulk from both loaves. This implies that when intent covers both, both become piggul together!
Rav Huna
That baraita reflects only the Rabbis — who hold that intent for one loaf renders both piggul. Rabbi Yosei would disagree, holding each loaf is an independent unit.
Rav Naḥman
But the baraita may actually record Rabbi Yosei's view! Even he would say: to incur karet for both loaves you need piggul intent covering an olive-bulk of each — meaning both can become piggul together under his view too.
Core Tension Rabbi Yosei's principle of independent loaves — does it mean piggul intent for one never affects the other, or only that karet liability requires intent explicitly covering both?
