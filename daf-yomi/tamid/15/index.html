⚖️ Three Proofs: The Tzitz Dispute Is Not About Impurity

Rav Huna b. Rav Natan
Can the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis really concern the tzitz? Even Rabbi Yehuda agrees it effects acceptance for impure items that normally ascend the altar!
Rav Ashi
Proof from the Paschal offering mishna (Pesachim 80a): Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis debate there too — but that dispute clearly has nothing to do with the frontplate.
Ravina
Proof from the two loaves: If the dispute were about the tzitz, Rabbi Yehuda should say 'both loaves are burned' — but he does not say that!
Gemara
All three proofs converge: the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis concerns something other than the frontplate's effect on impure offerings.
✅ Conclusion The tzitz (frontplate) effecting acceptance for altar-bound impure items is agreed upon by all. The real dispute lies elsewhere.

🤔 Rabbi Elazar's Dilemma: Does Combined Intent Render Loaves Piggul?

Rabbi Elazar
If one slaughters a thanks offering intending to eat an olive-bulk combining from the offering AND from its loaves the next day — are the loaves rendered piggul? The intent spans both, but the loaves can only be rendered piggul via intent specific to them.
Rav
Even so — the loaves ARE piggul, but the thanks offering itself is NOT piggul.
Gemara (challenge)
Kal va-chomer! If the thanks offering — which CAN render loaves piggul — is itself NOT rendered piggul by intent about loaves, surely combined intent toward the offering should not render loaves piggul either!
Rav (rejects)
We do not apply a kal va-chomer to be lenient with piggul. The baraita about diverse kinds in a vineyard — where Torah and rabbinic prohibitions are not equated — proves that not all logical inferences apply across categories.
⚡ Key Rule A kal va-chomer (a fortiori inference) is not applied to create leniency in piggul cases.

🍷 Libations of an Animal Offering — When Does Piggul Take Effect?

Rabbi Meir

  • Libations are fixed to this offering at the moment of slaughter
  • They cannot subsequently be switched to another offering
  • Therefore, piggul intent at slaughter reaches the libations
  • Liability begins when the libations are consecrated in the vessel
  • The guilt offering's blood similarly fixes the leper's log of oil

The Rabbis

  • One may bring the offering today and libations up to ten days later
  • The blood of the offering does not render libations permitted at slaughter
  • Therefore libations are independent of the offering
  • Piggul intent at slaughter should not affect them
  • The leper's log of oil can also be switched to another guilt offering

🫙 Rava's Resolution: Why Rabbi Meir's Logic Is Internally Consistent

לוג שמן של מצורע — ויקרא י״ד:י׳–כ׳
Rava's Teaching Rabbi Meir's position rests on a single premise:

At the moment of an animal offering's slaughter, the libations (and the leper's log of oil) become irrevocably fixed to that specific offering.

Because they are fixed, the blood's eventual permitting effect is already determined — and piggul intent at slaughter therefore reaches forward to render them piggul.

The Rabbis disagree because practically, one can use those libations with a different offering — demonstrating they are not truly fixed.

Rava: Rabbi Meir holds fixation is a legal status established at slaughter, regardless of practical substitutability.
