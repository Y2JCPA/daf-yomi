QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Gemara, what is the scriptural source that failure to sacrifice the minority of the handful disqualifies the entire meal offering?
A. The word 'handful' (komtzo) appears twice in the Torah (Lev. 2:2 and 6:8), teaching the full handful is required
B. The Rabbis enacted a rabbinic decree to protect the Temple service
C. The verse states 'all of it' specifically regarding the handful
D. The juxtaposition of the handful with the frankincense in the same verse
Correct: A — The Gemara derives this from the double mention of 'his handful' in Leviticus 2:2 and 6:8. The repetition teaches that the entire handful must be present — minority prevents majority.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is Rabbi Yehuda's basis for requiring the lulav to be bound with the myrtle and willow?
A. The verse 'This is my God and I will beautify Him' (Ex. 15:2)
B. A gezera shava: 'taking' of the four species equals 'taking' of the hyssop bundle used at the Pesach offering
C. The statute (ḥok) language written regarding Sukkot
D. The juxtaposition of lulav and etrog in the same verse of the Torah
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda uses a verbal analogy (gezera shava): the term 'taking' in the four species verse (Lev. 23:40) parallels 'taking' of the hyssop bundle at Pesach (Ex. 12:22). Just as hyssop must be bundled, so too the four species must be bound together.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to the Gemara's conclusion, what is the mitzva fulfilled by binding the lulav species together?
A. The biblical obligation to take 'the four species' as a single unit
B. Fulfilling hiddur mitzva — beautifying the commandment — based on 'This is my God and I will beautify Him'
C. A rabbinic enactment requiring bundling to prevent the willow from falling
D. A symbolic reminder of the unity required on Yom Kippur
Correct: B — The Gemara concludes that even according to the Rabbis (who do not require binding), there is a mitzva to bind — it fulfills hiddur mitzva (beautification of the commandment), derived from 'This is my God and I will beautify Him' (Exodus 15:2).
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How do the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda disagree about unauthorized entry into the sacred areas of the Temple?
A. Rabbis: only Yom Kippur entry is forbidden; Rabbi Yehuda: entry is always forbidden
B. Rabbis: 'holy place' and 'within the Curtain' describe one prohibition with death at the hand of Heaven; Rabbi Yehuda: Sanctuary = lashes, Holy of Holies = death
C. Rabbis: no punishment applies unless entry is intentional; Rabbi Yehuda: punishment applies even unintentionally
D. Both agree on the prohibition but disagree only about which High Priest performs the Yom Kippur service
Correct: B — The Rabbis read both phrases of Lev. 16:2 as describing one area with one penalty (death at Heaven's hand). Rabbi Yehuda distinguishes two areas: entering the Sanctuary (holy place) incurs lashes; entering within the Curtain (Holy of Holies) incurs death at the hand of Heaven.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What does the Mishna teach about the seven sprinklings of the red heifer's blood, and why?
A. Only the first and last sprinklings are indispensable; the middle ones are optional
B. All seven sprinklings are mutually indispensable — failure to perform any one of them means the entire rite is void, since statute language applies
C. The seven sprinklings are a rabbinic addition; biblically only three are required
D. Each sprinkling atones for a separate sin and they are performed independently
Correct: B — The Mishna teaches that the seven sprinklings of the red heifer blood are all mutually indispensable — failure to perform even one means the priest has accomplished nothing. This is derived from the statute (ḥok) language used regarding the red heifer purification rite.
