🩸 Why Two Verses for Leper Oil Placement?

Two Torah verses on placing oil on the leper's right thumb and big toe:
Leviticus 14:17 — 'upon the blood of the guilt offering'
Leviticus 14:28 — 'upon the place of the blood of the guilt offering'
↓

I'd think: oil only while blood still present — once wiped away, no oil placement

I'd think: oil only after blood is wiped away — while blood remains, no oil
↓
Both verses together: oil is placed on the right thumb and big toe regardless of whether blood is still present or already wiped away

🩸 Wealthy Leper vs. Poor Leper — The Two Verses

Wealthy Leper (Lev. 14:17)

  • Oil placed 'upon the blood of the guilt offering'
  • Implies: only while blood is still on the thumb and toe
  • Would exclude placement after blood is wiped away
  • Also carries verbal analogies: 'foot' (→ yibum) and 'ear' (→ Hebrew slave)

Poor Leper (Lev. 14:28)

  • Oil placed 'upon the place of the blood of the guilt offering'
  • Implies: only after blood has been wiped away
  • Would exclude placement while blood still present
  • Left hand mention: disqualifies priest's right hand for leper rite if right thumb is missing

⚖️ Rava: What Do the Leper Verses Really Teach?

Questioner
The two leper verses together establish that oil is placed on the right thumb and big toe — but isn't that obvious from 'upon the place of the blood'? Why do we need the first verse?
Rava
The verses are not primarily teaching about the leper's own rite — that location is already clear. Their true purpose is to generate verbal analogies (gezerot shavot) that teach other laws.
Rava
'His right foot' (Lev. 14:17) creates a verbal analogy with 'foot' in the yibum passage — teaching that yibum and chalitzah rites require the right foot.
Rava
'Tip of the right ear' creates a verbal analogy with 'ear' in the Hebrew slave passage — connecting leper purification to the ear-boring rite at the doorpost.
Hermeneutical Principle When a verse appears redundant for its own subject, the Gemara assumes it is teaching a gezerah shavah — a verbal analogy linking shared terms to different legal contexts.

✋ The Right Hand Rule

כָּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר אֶצְבַּע וּכְהֻנָּה — אֵין כָּשֵׁר אֶלָּא יָמִין
Rabba bar bar Ḥana citing Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish Any place in the Torah where an action is performed with a finger or by a priest — the left hand is disqualified. Only the right hand is valid.

Rava's clarification: Either condition alone suffices — if a verse mentions finger OR priesthood, the right hand is required for atonement-critical rites.

Rabbi Shimon's stricter view: Both finger AND priesthood must be present in the verse to mandate the right hand — priesthood alone is insufficient.
