QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav's interpretation of Rabbi Yehoshua, when is the meal offering rendered invalid due to impurity of the remainder?
A. When any part of the remainder becomes impure
B. Only when ALL of the remainder becomes impure
C. When more than half the remainder becomes impure
D. When the impurity occurred before the handful was removed
Correct: B — Rav clarifies that Rabbi Yehoshua's ruling applies only when the entire remainder became impure. If only part of the remainder became impure, what remains is still significant and the offering is fit — the handful may be sacrificed.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
From where does Rabbi Yoḥanan (in the name of Rabbi Yishmael) derive that blood is sprinkled even if only fat remains from an offering?
A. 'It is most holy, as the sin offering' (Lev. 6:10)
B. 'For a pleasing aroma to the Lord' (Lev. 17:6)
C. 'And he shall sprinkle the blood upon the altar' (Lev. 1:5)
D. 'The fat belongs to the Lord' (Lev. 3:16)
Correct: B — The verse 'for a pleasing aroma to the Lord' (Lev. 17:6) teaches that blood is sprinkled whenever anything that produces a pleasing aroma — which includes fat — remains from the offering. This serves as the source for the fat rule.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the source for Rabbi Shimon's ruling that the handful does not require sanctification in a service vessel?
A. The handful is already inherently sanctified upon removal from the meal offering
B. The verse 'It is most holy, as the sin offering' (Lev. 6:10) — comparing it to offerings that need no special vessel
C. Service vessels are only required for blood offerings, not grain offerings
D. A baraita explicitly exempts the handful from vessel requirements
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda son of Rabbi Ḥiyya explains that Rabbi Shimon derives this from 'It is most holy, as the sin offering, and as the guilt offering' (Lev. 6:10). Just as the sin and guilt offerings have their burning regulated by this comparison, so too the handful — with no extra requirement for a service vessel at the altar stage.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, from when precisely does the sacrifice of the handful permit the remainder for priestly consumption?
A. From when the priest places the handful upon the altar
B. From when the fire first ignites on the handful
C. From when the majority of the handful is consumed by fire
D. From when the smoke of the handful first ascends
Correct: C — Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that the remainder becomes permitted only once the majority of the handful is consumed by fire — not merely when it ignites. This is derived from the verse about furnace smoke (Gen. 19:28): a furnace does not release smoke until the fire has consumed the majority of the fuel.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
If a priest burned the handful of a meal offering in two separate increments, what is the halakha according to Rabbi Zeira's analysis?
A. Always invalid — the handful must be burned all at once
B. Valid if each increment is at least an olive-bulk in size
C. Valid only according to Rabbi Shimon; the Rabbis invalidate split burning
D. Valid only if the split was unintentional
Correct: B — The Mishna rules that burning in two increments is valid. Rabbi Zeira explains the underlying logic: the dispute hinges on whether a sub-olive-bulk burning has significance. If each increment is at least an olive-bulk, both opinions agree the offering is valid.
