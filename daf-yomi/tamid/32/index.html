📜 Open or Closed? The Mezuza Passage Dispute

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar
Wrote mezuza in the open manner: second passage begins on the following line, leaves space above and below text
↓
Rav (via Rav Ḥananel)
Halakha follows Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar — but only regarding spacing above/below text, not the open/closed arrangement of passages
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
Closed manner = mitzva ab initio
Open manner = permitted after the fact
Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's open manner is merely a leniency

💬 What Exactly Did Rav Rule? Abaye vs. Rav Yosef

Abaye
You agree that Rav's ruling in favor of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar concerns only the spacing above and below the text — not the open/closed manner. This means Rav holds one must follow established customs, as we see from his ḥalitza ruling!
Rav Yosef
Correct. Rav said: if Elijah comes and rules one may not perform ḥalitza with a sandal — we do not listen, because we have an established tradition that ḥalitza is performed with a sandal.
Rav Kahana (citing Rav)
Another version: if Elijah says no ḥalitza with a shoe, we listen; but if he says no ḥalitza with a sandal either — we do not listen. The difference between versions: may one use a plain shoe ab initio?
Abaye (conclusion)
Either way, both versions agree: the established custom of using a sandal cannot be overridden, even by a prophetic voice. The practice is binding.
Underlying Principle Rav holds that established communal practice (minhag) is halakhically binding and cannot be nullified even by new rulings — a foundational principle derived here.

📿 Phylacteries vs. Mezuza — How They Differ

Phylacteries (Tefillin)

  • Written on parchment (klaf), hair side facing outward
  • Does NOT require scoring of lines on parchment
  • May be written without copying from a written text
  • Higher sanctity — cannot be reduced to mezuza
  • One may NOT convert tefillin passages into a mezuza ab initio

Mezuza

  • Written on parchment (klaf), hair side facing outward
  • REQUIRES scoring of lines before writing
  • May be written without copying from a written text
  • Lesser sanctity — worn tefillin may become mezuza
  • Closed passage arrangement is mitzva ab initio; open is valid

⬇️ Can You Reduce Sanctity? Tefillin → Mezuza

Worn phylacteries — may the parchment be repurposed as a mezuza?
↓
Rule: sanctity may be reduced (tefillin → mezuza) but never increased (mezuza → tefillin)
Just as a Torah scroll may become a mezuza in theory, tefillin may become a mezuza
↓
Problem
Tefillin parchment does not require scoring; mezuza parchment does. Worn tefillin parchment may therefore be unfit for a mezuza!
Resolution (Tanna'im Dispute)
Rabbi Yirmeya citing Rav: both tefillin and mezuza may be written without copying from a text — showing their laws overlap. The dispute about scoring remains a tanna'itic argument.

🚪 Five Requirements for a Valid Mezuza

1. Parchment: Written on klaf (parchment), hair-side. Dokhsostos (flesh-side or coarser hide) renders it unfit.
↓
2. Scored lines: Unlike tefillin, mezuza parchment must have lines scored into it before writing.
↓
3. Proper intention: Must be written with scribal intention for each letter — writing it casually like an ordinary letter or missive renders it unfit.
↓
4. Affixed to doorpost: Must be attached directly to the doorpost itself — hanging it on a stick or rod in the entrance is unfit.
↓
5. Within the airspace: Must be placed inside the airspace of the entrance, not on the outside — fulfilling the verse "upon your doorposts".
