Tamid 18 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yehuda (as initially understood), if a priest intends to leave blood until the next day, what is the ruling?
A. Rabbis say fit, Rabbi Eliezer says unfit — they dispute this case
B. Everyone agrees the offering is unfit
C. Everyone agrees the offering is fit
D. The offering is fit but requires a replacement blood presentation
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda held that while Rabbis and Rabbi Eliezer dispute unusual acts (drinking blood, burning meat), all agree that intentionally leaving blood overnight renders the offering unfit — as if actually left overnight.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why did Yosef the Babylonian ask Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua the same question three times before revealing Rabbi Yehuda's ruling?
A. He suspected Rabbi Elazar was forgetful
B. He wanted to ensure consistency and that Rabbi Elazar was not simply guessing
C. The Gemara does not explain his reasoning
D. He was testing whether Rabbi Elazar would change his ruling under pressure
Correct: B — Yosef asked repeatedly to confirm the ruling was consistent and firmly held — only then did he reveal the opposing tradition from Rabbi Yehuda, showing his deep love of Torah and commitment to arriving at the correct halakha.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does the mishna need to explicitly state that an offering is valid even if the priest broke the loaves into large pieces (pittim merubbot)?
A. Because large pieces look disrespectful and might be thought to disqualify
B. Because breaking at all might be thought to disqualify when there is no obligation to break
C. Because only the kohen gadol may break loaves into small pieces
D. Because the omer offering has a stricter breaking requirement
Correct: B — The mishna already teaches that not breaking is valid. One might incorrectly reason: once broken, it must be broken correctly. The explicit ruling of pittim merubbot teaches that even imperfect breaking does not invalidate.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the basis for the Rabbis' position that a non-priest pouring oil does not invalidate the meal offering?
A. Pouring oil is never mentioned in the Torah as a priestly act
B. The verse 'he shall bring it to Aaron's sons' applies only from the kemitza onward
C. Rabbi Yehuda explicitly permitted non-priests to pour oil
D. A baraita states pouring oil is optional in all cases
Correct: B — The Rabbis read the verse as directing the priestly requirement (kemitza and beyond) to Aaron's sons — earlier preparatory rites like pouring oil are not covered by this requirement and may be done by non-priests.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Rav Naḥman resolve the contradiction between Rabbi Shimon's baraita (non-priest pouring oil = invalid) and the mishna (non-priest pouring oil = valid)?
A. The baraita is a rejected minority view; the mishna is authoritative
B. The baraita refers to a priest's meal offering; the mishna to an Israelite's meal offering
C. The baraita addresses intentional violation; the mishna addresses accidental omission
D. The two sources discuss different Temple periods
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman distinguishes by subject: Rabbi Shimon's baraita addresses a priest's own meal offering (where the pouring obligation is derived from an Israelite's offering and is therefore stricter), while the mishna addresses an Israelite's meal offering.
