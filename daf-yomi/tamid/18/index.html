⚔️ The Piggul Debate: Rabbi Eliezer vs. the Rabbis

Rabbis
If one intends to drink the blood or burn the meat outside its time — the offering is fit. This is an unusual act not part of normal service, so the intention cannot render it piggul.
Rabbi Eliezer
Even such unusual intentions disqualify the offering. Any intent to perform a sacrificial act outside its proper time renders the offering unfit — no distinction between usual and unusual acts.
Rabbi Yehuda
The dispute applies only to unusual acts (drinking blood, burning meat). But if one intends to leave the blood until the next day — everyone agrees the offering is unfit!
Rabbi Elazar
Not so — even intending to merely leave blood overnight is disputed. Rabbi Eliezer deems it unfit; the Rabbis deem it fit. There is no basis to distinguish this from the other cases.
Story-Based Proof Yosef the Babylonian's persistent questioning of Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua — asking three times — ultimately reveals that Rabbi Yehuda himself may have taught that all agree it is unfit, yet Rabbi Elazar had been ruling it fit all along.

📜 Yosef the Babylonian and Rabbi Elazar's Tears

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi taught Yosef the Babylonian that if one intends to leave blood until the next day, all agree the offering is unfit.
↓
Yosef posed this case to Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua: "What if one intended to leave the blood overnight?" — Rabbi Elazar replied: "The offering is fit."
↓
Yosef repeated the question that evening — Rabbi Elazar again ruled: "The offering is fit." Yosef still did not reveal his teacher's view.
↓
Yosef asked a third time the following morning. Only then did he reveal: "My teacher Rabbi Yehuda ruled that all agree it is unfit!"
↓
Rabbi Elazar's eyes streamed with tears: "Happy are you, Torah scholars, for whom matters of Torah are exceedingly dear!" — He reconsidered and said: "You have returned to me what I had lost."

Pouring oil, mixing, breaking, salting, waving, bringing to altar — omission does not invalidate the offering b'diavad
❌ Rite is essential to the offering
Kemitza (handful removal) and burning the handful on the altar — omission renders the offering unfit

🧑‍🍳 Who May Perform Meal Offering Rites?

Rabbis — Non-Priest May Pour Oil

  • The verse "he shall bring it to Aaron's sons" applies only from the kemitza onward
  • Earlier rites (pouring, mixing, breaking) may be performed by a non-priest without invalidating
  • A non-priest who pours oil: offering remains valid

Rabbi Shimon — All Rites Require a Priest

  • Any sacrificial rite entrusted to Aaron's sons must be performed by a priest
  • A priest who does not admit validity of these rites has no share in priestly gifts
  • Pouring oil is among the listed priestly rites — non-priest performance invalidates

✂️ Pittim Merubbot — Breaking into Large Pieces

פִּתִּים מְרֻבּוֹת — אִם פְּתָתָן פְּתִיתָה גַּסָּה, כְּשֵׁרָה
Mishna Ruling Explained The mishna teaches: if a priest broke the meal offering loaves into larger pieces (pittim merubbot) than required, the offering is still fit. The Gemara asks: why state this if we already know omitting the breaking entirely is valid? Answer: lest you say the offering is fit only when loaves are wholly unbroken (since there is no obligation), but once broken — even imperfectly — it might be invalid. The mishna teaches that large-piece breaking also does not disqualify.
