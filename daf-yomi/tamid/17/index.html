🔥 Does Burning Render Burning Piggul?

Sharp Ones of Pumbedita / Rava
Burning does render burning piggul — burning the handful with intent to burn the frankincense the next day renders the entire meal offering piggul, even according to the Rabbis.
Rav Ḥisda citing Rav
Burning does not render burning piggul — Rabbi Meir's rule applies only when improper intent involves the handful, the actual permitting factor for the remainder. Frankincense is not the handful's permitting factor.
Core Question The handful (kometz) permits the meal offering's remainder. Frankincense (levona) permits its own portion. Can intent during one permitting act contaminate the other? Abaye confirmed: Rav Ḥisda stated this ruling explicitly in the name of Rav himself.

⚖️ The Central Dispute: Can Burning Create Piggul?

Rava (citing Mishna 12a)
Burning DOES render burning piggul. The mishna lists four permitting acts: removing the handful, placing it in a vessel, conveying it to the altar, and burning it. Since wrong intent during any of these creates piggul, burning with intent to burn the frankincense the next day should likewise create piggul — the acts are analogous.
Rav Ḥisda / Rav
Burning does NOT render burning piggul. The Gemara rejects Rava's analogy: for the other three acts, wrong intent to eat the remainder OR burn the frankincense equally creates piggul. But for burning specifically, only intent to eat the remainder (the normal permitting act) counts — not intent regarding the frankincense.
Abaye's Verification Rav Menashya bar Gadda sat before Abaye and reported this ruling. Abaye pressed him: did Rav Ḥisda say this in Rav's name? Confirmed: yes. It was also stated explicitly that Rav Ḥisda says that Rav says: burning does not render burning piggul.

🌿 Rabbi Ḥanina's Teaching — A Significant Novelty

קִטֵּר קֹמֶץ עַל מְנָת לְקַטֵּר לְבוֹנָה לְמָחָר — פִּגּוּל וְחַיָּיב כָּרֵת
Rav Hamnuna: Equivalent to All My Learning The Case: One burned the handful with intent to burn the frankincense the next day — and then also burned the frankincense with intent to burn it the next day.

Result (per Rav Adda bar Ahava): The offering is piggul and one is liable for karet.

Why the novelty? Rabbi Ḥanina holds that burning the handful alone ≠ piggul (like Rav). But when both the handful and the frankincense are burned with improper intent, they together complete the permitting act — and the combined wrong intent triggers full piggul with karet.

This resolves the ambiguity: Rabbi Ḥanina agrees with Rav on burning alone, yet finds a scenario where karet applies.

📜 Mishna Debate: Piggul for Unusual Items

Rabbi Eliezer

  • Intent to consume an item not normally consumed (e.g., the handful) beyond its time → offering is unfit
  • Intent to burn an item not normally burned beyond its time → offering is unfit
  • Derives from doubled language he'akhol ye'akhel: burning and eating are equated, so unusual consumption/burning creates unfitness
  • One opinion: liable by Torah law and subject to karet

The Rabbis

  • Improper intent involving items NOT typically consumed or burned does not render piggul
  • Only intent regarding the typical act — eating what is eaten, burning what is burned — creates piggul
  • The doubled Torah language teaches: an olive-bulk minimum applies equally to both eating and burning intent
  • Unusual intent → offering is merely unfit (pasul), not piggul; no karet
