QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Ḥisda citing Rav, what is the ruling if one burns the handful with intent to burn the frankincense the next day?
A. The offering is piggul and one is liable for karet
B. The offering is not piggul, because frankincense is not the permitting factor for the remainder
C. The offering is unfit (pasul) but not piggul
D. The ruling depends on whether the priest verbally stated his intent
Correct: B — Rav Ḥisda in the name of Rav rules that burning does NOT render burning piggul. The handful is the permitting factor (matir) for the meal offering's remainder, not the frankincense — so improper intent during burning with reference to the frankincense does not trigger piggul.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the novel ruling in Rabbi Ḥanina's teaching as resolved by Rav Adda bar Ahava?
A. Burning the handful alone with wrong intent is always piggul
B. Only the frankincense burning matters for piggul status
C. When both the handful and the frankincense are burned with intent to burn them the next day, the offering is piggul with karet liability
D. Rabbi Ḥanina agrees with Rava that any burning creates piggul
Correct: C — Rav Adda explains that Rabbi Ḥanina actually holds like Rav — burning the handful alone does not render piggul. The novelty is that when BOTH the handful AND the frankincense are burned with improper intent, they together complete the permitting act and render the offering piggul with karet.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rava attempt to prove that burning renders piggul from the mishna in Menachot 12a?
A. He cites Rabbi Eliezer's ruling about unusual items
B. He argues that since burning is listed alongside removing, placing, and conveying as a permitting act, wrong intent during burning should equally create piggul
C. He derives it from the doubled Torah language he'akhol ye'akhel
D. He brings a baraita where Rav explicitly permits it
Correct: B — Rava reasons from the mishna's list of four permitting acts (removing, placing in vessel, conveying, burning): just as wrong intent during any of the first three creates piggul regardless of whether one intends to eat the remainder or burn the frankincense, burning should likewise render piggul.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
From which Torah expression does Rabbi Eliezer derive that improper intent involving unusual items (burning what is normally eaten, etc.) can render an offering unfit?
A. The prohibition against eating blood — lo tochal
B. The doubled language he'akhol ye'akhel, which equates the language of burning with the language of eating
C. The verse prohibiting piggul — ki piggel — repeated twice in Leviticus
D. The commandment for timely consumption of sacrifices on the same day
Correct: B — Rabbi Eliezer derives from the doubled expression he'akhol ye'akhel that the Torah equates burning with eating. Since the verse uses both verb forms, it teaches that improper intent regarding unusual acts — burning what is normally eaten, eating what is normally burned — also creates unfitness.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
The Gemara (Amud B) raises: according to Rabbi Eliezer, if improper intent involving unusual items renders the offering unfit, is one liable for karet?
A. Always liable for karet, according to all opinions
B. Never liable, since the offering is only pasul not piggul
C. It is a tannaitic dispute — one opinion holds liable by Torah law and karet applies; another holds it is Rabbinic and no karet
D. Liable only if the intent was stated aloud during the service
Correct: C — Rav Asi tells Rabbi Zeira that the question of karet liability for unusual-item intent according to Rabbi Eliezer is itself a tannaitic dispute. One tanna holds Rabbi Eliezer deems it a Torah violation with karet; another holds it is only Rabbinic (and Rabbi Yehuda vs. Rabbi Elazar in the baraita reflects this exact disagreement).
