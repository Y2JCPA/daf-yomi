Tamid 21 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava's resolution of the contradiction between Ze'eiri and the baraita, what is the key factor determining whether cooked blood is prohibited?
A. Whether the blood came from a kosher or non-kosher animal
B. Whether the blood was curdled by fire or by the sun
C. Whether the blood had been salted before cooking
D. Whether the blood came from an inner or outer sin offering
Correct: B — Rava distinguishes: blood curdled by fire loses its identity as blood entirely (Ze'eiri — not prohibited), while blood curdled by the sun retains its blood nature (the baraita — prohibited). This resolves the apparent contradiction.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rabbi Ḥisda, in which case is one liable for eating congealed blood?
A. Congealed blood of inner sin offerings only
B. Congealed blood of external sin offerings
C. Any congealed blood regardless of source
D. No one is ever liable for eating congealed blood
Correct: B — Rabbi Ḥisda rules that one who eats congealed blood of the external (outer altar) sin offerings is liable, because the Torah's instruction 'the priest shall offer it' implies the blood retains its status even when congealed.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why is incense (קְטֹרֶת) excluded from the requirement to add salt to offerings?
A. Because incense is not susceptible to ritual impurity
B. Because incense is poured on the corner of the altar like blood
C. Because incense is sacrificed on the inner altar, not the outer altar
D. Because incense is not a food offering
Correct: C — The verse's salting requirement applies to offerings on the outer altar. Incense is burned on the inner golden altar inside the Sanctuary, so it is excluded from the general salt requirement.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What does the phrase 'you shall not omit (תַּשְׁבִּית) salt from your meal offering' teach about the salt to be used?
A. The individual offerer must bring salt from home
B. Large quantities of salt must be mixed throughout the offering
C. One should use salt that is continuously available (e.g., salt of Sodom)
D. The salt must be finely ground before application
Correct: C — The word תַּשְׁבִּית (omit/cease) teaches that one should use salt that never 'rests' or runs out — salt that is continuously present. The Gemara identifies this as salt of Sodom, which was always available in the Temple.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does the Gemara prove from a scriptural analogy that the congregation — not the individual offerer — must provide salt for offerings?
A. From the analogy to wood, which is always provided by the congregation for the altar fire
B. From 'an everlasting covenant of salt,' paralleling the shewbread's 'everlasting covenant' provided by the congregation
C. From the fact that salt is stored in the Temple's Chamber of Salt
D. From ben Bukhri's testimony about priestly contributions at Yavne
Correct: B — Numbers 18:19 calls salt 'an everlasting covenant of salt,' which parallels Leviticus 24:8's 'everlasting covenant' of the shewbread — which comes from the congregation. The verbal analogy (gezera shava) proves salt too is provided by the congregation.
