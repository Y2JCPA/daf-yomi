🩸 The Blood Debates: When Is Cooked Blood Prohibited?

Ze'eiri
Blood that was cooked — one does not transgress a prohibition by drinking it.
Abaye
Objection! A baraita states that one who drinks cooked blood is liable — direct contradiction!
Rava
No difficulty: Ze'eiri speaks of blood curdled by fire — it loses its blood identity; the baraita speaks of blood curdled by the sun.
Abaye
But even sun-curdled blood was disqualified from the altar — so shouldn't it also be excluded from the prohibition against consuming blood?
Abaye
Resolution: The baraita refers to external sin offering blood (still altar-fit); Ze'eiri refers to inner sin offering blood (never placed on outer altar).
Key Distinction Fire-curdled blood loses its identity as blood entirely. Sun-curdled blood retains it. Inner sin offering blood was never destined for the outer altar and may thus be excluded from the prohibition.

⚖️ Congealed Blood: Who Is Liable?

Blood becomes congealed — is one liable for eating it?
↓
External Sin Offering
Rabbi Ḥisda: Liable — Torah says 'the priest shall offer it,' implying congealed blood retains its blood status
Inner Sin Offering
Rava: Also liable — since corresponding blood exists for outer offerings, it counts as altar-suitable blood
↓
Rav Pappa's conclusion: One who eats congealed blood of inner sin offerings is liable on two counts — once for blood, once for inner-offering blood

🚫 Items Excluded from the Salt Requirement

Wood

  • Not susceptible to ritual impurity
  • Therefore excluded from salting requirement
  • Per Rabbi Yishmael son of R. Yoḥanan ben Beroka

Wine Libations & Blood

  • Not placed on the fire of the altar
  • Sprinkled on the corner of the altar
  • Verse explicitly excludes blood from salting

Incense (קְטֹרֶת)

  • Offered on the inner golden altar
  • Not the outer altar
  • Excluded from the general salting rule

Meal Offerings (מְנָחָה)

  • INCLUDED — must be salted
  • Burned on the outer altar
  • 'Every meal offering you shall season with salt' (Lev. 2:13)

🧂 How to Salt an Offering — Correct Amount and Method

"You shall season" (תִּמְלַח) — establishes the positive obligation to add salt to every meal offering placed on the altar
↓
"You shall not omit salt" (תַּשְׁבִּית) — salt must never run out; use salt that is continuously (שׁוֹבֶתֶת) available — i.e., salt of Sodom
↓
Not too much: One might think to mix in large amounts 'like straw (תֶּבֶן) into clay.' The verse says 'from your meal offering,' meaning applied to it, not dissolved throughout
↓
Not total infusion: One might think to saturate the offering with salt flavor 'as understanding (בִּינָה) infuses wisdom.' The verse limits this — apply externally only
↓
Abaye's practical ruling: Apply salt to both sides of the meat, let blood drain, rinse it off, then cook — this satisfies the obligation

📜 Who Must Provide Salt for Offerings?

Opening Question
One who vows a meal offering — must he bring his own salt from home, or does the Temple treasury provide it?
Argument: Like Wood
Compare to wood: just as the congregation provides wood for the altar fire, so the congregation provides the salt.
Argument: Like Frankincense
Compare to frankincense: just as frankincense accompanies the meal offering in one vessel (from the owner), so should salt — the individual brings it.
Resolution from Verse
"It is an everlasting covenant of salt" (Num. 18:19) — just as the shewbread 'everlasting covenant' comes from the congregation (Lev. 24:8), so too salt comes from the congregation.
Final Ruling The Temple treasury (congregation) provides salt for all offerings — like wood, not frankincense. The individual offerer need not bring salt from home.
