⚖️ Rav Naḥman and the Tanna: The Gezera Shava on 'Iniquity'

Tanna (Baraita)
Surplus money designated for a meal offering of jealousy is used for communal gift offerings — just as surplus from a sin offering is.
Rav Naḥman
You speak well! Numbers 5:15 says 'bringing iniquity (עָוֺן) to remembrance' about the jealousy offering, and Leviticus 10:17 says 'to bear the iniquity (עָוֺן) of the congregation' about the sin offering — a valid gezera shava.
Gemara (Challenge)
If we derive halakhot from this verbal analogy, shouldn't a guilt offering also be disqualified when sacrificed not for its sake?
Gemara (Resolution)
No — the analogy is between 'avon' and 'avon,' but the guilt offering verse uses 'avono' (his iniquity) — a different form that cannot be linked by the same analogy.
The Gezera Shava עָוֺן (avon) = עָוֺן (avon): Numbers 5:15 ↔ Leviticus 10:17. But עֲוֺנוֹ (avono) in the guilt offering verse is distinct and cannot anchor the same analogy.

📜 How the Word 'It' (הוּא) Operates Differently in Sin vs. Guilt Offerings

The word 'It' (הוּא / hu) appears in verses about both sin offerings and guilt offerings — but functions differently in each
↓
Sin Offering — Leviticus 4:24
'It is a sin offering'
→ Must be slaughtered for its own sake
→ Not for its sake = DISQUALIFIED
Guilt Offering — Leviticus 7:5
'It is a guilt offering'
→ 'It' teaches: even if owner dies or sin was atoned, offering retains guilt-offering status and goes to pasture
→ Generally NOT disqualified when not for its sake
↓
Meal Offering of Sinner inherits the sin offering's stringency — disqualified when not done for its sake, by analogy to the sin offering's 'it'

🌾 Rav's Teaching: The Omer and Special Guilt Offerings

Standard Meal Offerings

  • Brought by individuals
  • No fixed calendar date
  • Not for their sake → valid but don't fulfill obligation
  • Mishna's general rule applies

Omer Meal Offering

  • Communal offering — brought on behalf of all Israel
  • Fixed time: 16 Nisan — cannot be delayed
  • Not for its sake → DISQUALIFIED (Rav)
  • Two factors exclude it from Mishna's leniency: communal + time-bound

Guilt Offerings of Nazirite & Leper

  • Come to 'render fit' (מַכְשִׁיר), not merely atone
  • Enable entry into the camp / participation in offerings
  • Not for their sake → DISQUALIFIED (Rav)
  • Purpose entirely fails — person remains unfit

🔄 Rabbi Yirmeya: Why Render-Fit Offerings Are Stricter

General Principle: Guilt offerings that come to atone (l'chaper) are valid when slaughtered not for their sake — they achieve something even improperly performed.
↓
The Exception — Render-Fit Offerings: The guilt offerings of the nazirite who became impure and of the leper come to render fit (l'hachshir) — enabling the person to re-enter the Israelite camp or participate in sanctified offerings.
↓
Why Stricter? The Torah differentiates: for render-fit offerings, the entire purpose is contingent on proper intent. If not done for their sake, the person is simply not rendered fit — the offering achieves nothing.
↓
Rav Pappa's Clarification: Rabbi Yirmeya's principle is limited — we do not find any render-fit offering that operates after the owner's death, unlike some atonement offerings which have post-death analogues.
↓
Conclusion: The disqualification of nazirite and leper guilt offerings is not merely a formal rule but reflects a functional reality — an improperly performed render-fit offering leaves its owner in an unchanged state.
