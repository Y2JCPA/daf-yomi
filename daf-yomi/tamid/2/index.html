כל המנחות שנקמצו שלא לשמן — כשרות, אלא שלא עלו לבעלים לשם חובה
📜 The Mishna's Core Ruling All meal offerings from which a handful was removed not for their sake (שלא לשמן) are fit for sacrifice (כשרות) — but they did not satisfy the owner's obligation. The Gemara focuses on the word 'but' (אלא): this signals that the only deficiency is the unfulfilled obligation. The offering itself remains valid, and the priest may not deviate from it a second time — proven from the verse in Deuteronomy 23:24 about vows.

⚖️ Meal Offerings vs. Slaughtered Offerings — Why Different Rules?

Meal Offerings (מנחות)

  • Different types have different modes of preparation
  • Sinner's offering: no oil or frankincense
  • Voluntary offering: has oil and frankincense
  • Mode of preparation proves the intent
  • Therefore improper intent can disqualify

Slaughtered Offerings (זבחים)

  • One manner of slaughter for all offerings
  • One manner of sprinkling blood for all
  • One manner of collecting blood for all
  • Mode of rite cannot reveal intent
  • Therefore improper intent is less significant

🔄 Resolving Rabbi Shimon's Contradiction — Three Approaches

Rabbi Shimon (Statement 1)
A voluntary meal offering removed not for its sake — valid AND satisfies the owner
Rabbi Shimon (Statement 2)
A voluntary meal offering is like a guilt offering — valid but does NOT effect acceptance
Rabba
שינוי קודש (Change of Sanctity): removed for sake of a different type of offering → does not satisfy.
שינוי שם (Change of Name): removed for sake of another offering of the same type → satisfies.
Rav Ashi
Here (satisfies): removed for sake of another meal offering — same category. There (does not satisfy): removed for sake of a different named offering. The mishna follows Rabbi Shimon on this reading.
Key Outcome Only Rav Ashi's resolution allows the mishna to fit Rabbi Shimon. Under Rabba's or Rava's resolutions, the mishna cannot be explained according to Rabbi Shimon.

🤔 Can the Mishna Follow Rabbi Shimon?

Mishna: kometz removed for sake of another meal offering → valid, obligation not fulfilled

Per Rav Ashi
✅ Fits Rabbi Shimon — 'different offering' (שינוי שם) means obligation not satisfied
Per Rabba
❌ Does not fit — change of name should satisfy per Rabba, yet mishna says it doesn't
Per Rava
❌ Does not fit — mishna discusses change of name case, which Rava says satisfies the owner
↓

🐦 Abaye Challenges Rabba — Recognizable vs. Non-Recognizable False Intent

Rabba
Rabbi Shimon holds: only intent that is not recognizably false disqualifies. Intent for a different type contradicts the rite visibly — so it is recognizably false and disqualifies the offering.
Abaye
But improper intent for meal offerings is derived from the Torah's comparison to sin offerings and guilt offerings (gezeira shava). If the Torah derives this, why should recognizability matter at all?
Rabba
Rabbi Shimon interprets the rationale behind the Torah's ruling (דרש טעמא דקרא). He holds the verse teaches a principle, not just a rule. Therefore: intent that openly contradicts the rite differs fundamentally from a mere deviation of intent.
Gemara (Bird Offering)
Applied to practice: a bird burnt offering pinched above the red line for the sake of a bird sin offering — should this effect acceptance? The rite of pinching above vs. below differs per offering, so it IS recognizably false intent...
Methodological Principle Rabbi Shimon's approach — דרש טעמא דקרא (deriving from rationale, not just text) — produces rulings that are logically consistent with the reason behind the law, not merely its letter.
