Tamid 2 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Mishna, what is the status of a meal offering whose handful was removed not for its own sake?
A. Completely disqualified — the priest must start over
B. Valid and fully satisfies the owner's obligation
C. Valid but does not satisfy the owner's obligation
D. Valid only if the owner consents retroactively
Correct: C — The Mishna rules such offerings are kosher (valid for sacrifice) but do not fulfill the owner's obligation. The Gemara notes the word 'but' (אלא) signals this is the only deficiency.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does the Gemara prove from the verse in Deuteronomy 23:24 ('that which has gone out of your lips…')?
A. That vows cannot be annulled once made
B. That a meal offering already sacrificed with improper intent may not be deviated from further
C. That improper intent disqualifies any sacrifice
D. That the priest must verbally declare his intent before removing the handful
Correct: B — The verse teaches that once an offering goes out in fulfillment of a vow, one may not deviate from it — so even an offering whose kometz was removed improperly cannot be treated differently thereafter.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does the Gemara distinguish meal offerings from slaughtered offerings regarding the effect of improper intent?
A. Meal offerings are less holy than animal offerings
B. Different meal offering types have different modes of preparation that reveal intent; animal offerings have one uniform mode for all
C. The Torah explicitly excludes animal offerings from intent-based disqualification
D. Meal offerings are brought by the poor, so they receive stricter rules
Correct: B — The Gemara explains: different meal offerings have distinct preparations (e.g., the sinner's offering has no oil), so the mode of preparation can reveal improper intent. Slaughtered offerings share one mode of slaughter and blood-rite for all types.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rabba resolve the contradiction between Rabbi Shimon's two statements?
A. One statement applies to Biblically mandated offerings, the other to rabbinic ones
B. Rabbi Shimon retracted one statement in a later teaching
C. One refers to change of sanctity (different type), the other to change of name (different offering of same type)
D. One applies when the owner is present, the other when he is absent
Correct: C — Rabba distinguishes: שינוי קודש (change of sanctity) — removed for a different type of offering — does not satisfy the owner. שינוי שם (change of name) — removed for another offering of the same type — does satisfy.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What methodological principle does Rabba attribute to Rabbi Shimon in his response to Abaye?
A. Rabbi Shimon rules stringently in all cases of doubt
B. Rabbi Shimon interprets the rationale behind Torah rulings (דרש טעמא דקרא), so only non-recognizably false intent disqualifies
C. Rabbi Shimon holds that intent is irrelevant once the physical rite is completed
D. Rabbi Shimon follows the principle that the minority opinion is always more stringent
Correct: B — Rabba explains that Rabbi Shimon derives laws from the reason behind Torah verses, not just their text. Therefore, recognizably false intent (which visibly contradicts the rite) is treated differently — it disqualifies — while subtle improper intent operates by different rules.
