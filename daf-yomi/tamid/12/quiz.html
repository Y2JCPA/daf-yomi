QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Huna, why is burning the handful NOT effective in rendering a lacking remainder as piggul?
A. Because the remainder was never consecrated to begin with
B. Because the deficiency in measure is intrinsic to the remainder itself
C. Because only eating intent can create piggul, not burning intent
D. Because Rabbi Akiva explicitly rules the remainder is exempt
Correct: B — Rav Huna distinguishes between external disqualifications (e.g., leaving the Temple courtyard) and intrinsic ones (lacking measure). A lacking remainder is internally deficient, so the handful's burning cannot render it piggul.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is Rava's proof that a lacking remainder CAN still become piggul?
A. A direct statement from Rav Huna agreeing with him
B. The fact that sprinkling blood always removes meat from misuse liability
C. Rabbi Ḥiyya's mishna omits 'or an olive-bulk' for the remainder, implying the remainder was already lacking yet piggul still applies
D. The special rule of the meal offering of a sinner
Correct: C — Rava infers from Rabbi Ḥiyya's mishna: since 'or an olive-bulk' is omitted for the remainder (but taught for outside-place intent), the mishna must deal with a remainder already lacking in measure — yet piggul still applies.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Abaye refute Rava's proof from Rabbi Ḥiyya's mishna?
A. Rabbi Ḥiyya follows Rabbi Elazar, who requires the full handful for liability — so 'an olive-bulk' is omitted for the handful too, for an unrelated reason
B. Rabbi Ḥiyya was discussing only the meal offering of a sota, not ordinary meal offerings
C. Abaye argues Rabbi Ḥiyya made a scribal error in his text
D. Rabbi Ḥiyya holds that piggul can never apply to meal offerings at all
Correct: A — Abaye explains that Rabbi Ḥiyya follows Rabbi Elazar, who holds one is not liable for burning less than the full handful — therefore the omission of 'an olive-bulk' for the handful reflects this view, not anything about the remainder's measure.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to the Mishna on 12b, what is the ruling when one intends to eat half an olive-bulk of the remainder AND burn half an olive-bulk outside its designated time?
A. The offering is piggul and one is liable to karet
B. The offering is disqualified but there is no karet liability
C. The offering remains fit, because eating-intent and burning-intent do not combine
D. The ruling depends on which intent was formed first
Correct: C — The Mishna rules that eating-intent and burning-intent are separate categories that do not join together (אין מצטרפין). Since neither half-measure alone reaches the olive-bulk minimum, the offering remains fit.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to whose opinion does Rabbi Yirmeya explain the Mishna (12b), where eating and burning intent CAN combine?
A. The Rabbis (the majority view)
B. Rabbi Yehuda
C. Rabbi Eliezer
D. Rabbi Akiva
Correct: C — Rabbi Yirmeya states that the Mishna follows Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that improper intent can cross from 'consumption by a person' (eating) to 'consumption by the altar' (burning) — allowing two half-measures of different types to combine for piggul.
