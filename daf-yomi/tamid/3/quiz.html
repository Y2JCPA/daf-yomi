QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabba, what is the critical factor that determines whether improper intent disqualifies a Temple offering?
A. Whether the priest acted deliberately or accidentally
B. Whether the improper intent is recognizable to outside observers
C. Whether the offering belongs to the most-sacred or lesser-sanctity category
D. Whether the owner was physically present during the service
Correct: B — Rabba's principle is that recognizably false intent disqualifies an offering. If onlookers can tell from the priest's actions that something is wrong, the offering is invalid. If the actions appear completely normal, the offering remains valid despite the internal improper intent.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Which verse does Rava cite to prove that a meal offering whose handful was removed for the sake of a different meal offering is still valid?
A. 'And he shall slaughter it for a sin offering' (Leviticus 4:33)
B. 'And this is the law of the meal offering' (Leviticus 6:7)
C. 'Who sacrifices the blood of the peace offering' (Leviticus 7:33)
D. 'You shall bring your offering from the cattle' (Leviticus 1:2)
Correct: B — Rava derives from Leviticus 6:7 — 'And this is the law (singular) of the meal offering' — that one unified law governs all meal offerings. Therefore removing a handful for the sake of any other meal offering still fulfills the owner's obligation. He contrasts this with Lev. 4:33, where 'it' demands slaughtered offerings be designated specifically for themselves.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does Rav Ashi rule that a pan meal offering whose handful was removed with intent for a deep-pan meal offering is valid?
A. Because meal offerings are less sacred than animal offerings and held to a lower standard
B. Because the priest's intent is always presumed correct unless proven otherwise
C. Because intent concerning the type of vessel is halakhically inconsequential
D. Because the owner can always bring a second offering to correct the first
Correct: C — Rav Ashi rules that intent about vessel type (pan vs. deep pan) carries no halakhic weight. He supports this from a mishna (102b) showing that one who vowed a deep-pan offering and brought it in a pan has still fulfilled his vow — meaning the two vessel-types are legally interchangeable.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does the Gemara challenge Rabba using the case of a goat sin offering slaughtered as a guilt offering?
A. Because goat sin offerings and guilt offerings have identical blood-sprinkling rites
B. Because guilt offerings must be female, making a female goat indistinguishable
C. Because a guilt offering is a ram with white wool while a goat has black hair — the difference is obvious
D. Because guilt offerings are also brought for the same sins as goat sin offerings
Correct: C — A guilt offering must be a ram, which has white wool. A goat has distinctively black hair. Anyone observing the rite would immediately see that this black-haired goat cannot possibly be a white-wooled guilt offering. Under Rabba's own principle, this maximally recognizable difference should disqualify the offering — challenging his framework.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
On what do Rabba, Rava, and Rav Ashi all agree despite their different approaches to meal offerings?
A. Slaughtered offerings performed not-for-their-own-sake are always disqualified
B. Slaughtered offerings are valid if the difference between offerings is not visually obvious
C. Only burnt offerings are disqualified by improper intent; peace offerings remain valid
D. Slaughtered offerings are valid whenever the owner was unaware of the priest's wrong intent
Correct: A — All three amora'im agree that slaughtered offerings performed not-for-their-own-sake (שלא לשמה) are disqualified. Rava derives this from Leviticus 4:33: 'And he shall slaughter it for a sin offering' — the word 'it' mandates that every slaughtered offering must be designated specifically for itself, with no deviation permitted.
