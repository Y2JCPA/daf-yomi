QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav, in which specific scenario do Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis actually disagree about piggul in a meal offering?
A. When both the handful and frankincense are burned with piggul intent
B. When the handful is burned first with piggul intent and then the frankincense in silence
C. When the handful is burned in silence and then the frankincense is burned with piggul intent
D. When neither the handful nor the frankincense had any piggul intent
Correct: C — Rav limits the Rabbi Meir/Rabbis dispute to: handful burned in silence first, then frankincense with piggul intent. But if the handful had piggul intent first and frankincense was silent, Rav says all agree it is piggul — the first intentional act fixes the offering's status for all subsequent rites.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why do all authorities — including the Rabbis — agree that piggul intent during the handful alone renders a sinner's meal offering piggul?
A. Because a sinner's offering has no remainder permitted for eating
B. Because a sinner's meal offering contains no frankincense — the handful is the sole permitting factor
C. Because Rabbi Meir's opinion is accepted as law for all sin offerings
D. Because a sinner's offering is burned entirely on the altar without a remainder
Correct: B — A sinner's meal offering (and a sota's jealousy offering) contain no frankincense. Since the handful is therefore the only permitting factor — not merely part of it — even the Rabbis agree that piggul intent during it alone is fully sufficient to render the offering piggul.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the dispute between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish regarding conveying (carrying) the handful to the altar?
A. Whether conveying is considered a valid sacrificial act at all
B. Whether piggul intent during the conveying of the handful renders the offering piggul
C. Whether the handful must be conveyed in a special vessel
D. Whether conveying occurs before or after the removal of the handful
Correct: B — Reish Lakish holds that conveying is only part of the permitting factors (like burning only the handful without frankincense), so intent during conveying alone does not create piggul. Rabbi Yoḥanan equates conveying with removing the handful — since intent during removing alone creates piggul, so too does intent during conveying.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does the Gemara reconcile the three baraitot that give 43, 47, and 48 blood presentations on Yom Kippur?
A. The three counts refer to three different High Priests performing the service simultaneously
B. 43 and 47 reflect two different methods of counting; 48 follows Rabbi Eliezer who adds one more presentation to the 47 count
C. 43 is the minimum, 47 includes optional presentations, and 48 counts repeated applications
D. Each number represents a different Temple era with different halachic requirements
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that 43 and 47 reflect different counting methods for the standard presentations, while 48 is the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer, who counts one additional presentation beyond the 47 — explicitly cited as reconciling the third baraita.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
When burning only a sesame-seed amount of the handful with intent to eat a sesame-seed amount of the remainder the next day, what is the core halachic question?
A. Whether the priest must verbalize piggul intent or if mental intent suffices
B. Whether a sesame-seed is a halachically significant measure for both burning and consumption in sacrificial law
C. Whether burning part of the handful invalidates the remaining portion of the handful
D. Whether piggul intent must be formed before or during the burning
Correct: B — The debate centers on measure: one opinion holds that since the manner of burning (sesame-seed portions) matches the manner of intended consumption (sesame-seed portions), it constitutes valid piggul. The other holds that a sesame-seed is too small to be a halachically meaningful measure for either burning or consumption, so no piggul is created.
