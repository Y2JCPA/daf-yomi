⚖️ The Great Mixture Dispute: One Verse, Two Principles

Rabbis
From the Yom Kippur bull-and-goat blood verse: items that ascend the altar never nullify one another — because both bloods go up on the altar, neither is batel. The handful mixed with another handful is unfit; the category of ma'aleh b'mizbe'aḥ protects each component.
Rabbi Yehuda
From the same verse: min b'mino (same-type substances) are never nullified — bull blood and goat blood are both sacrificial blood, the same category. A handful mixed with the meal offering of the anointed priest or libations is especially unfit because those are consumed entirely on the altar.
Rabbi Yoḥanan's Insight Both the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda derive their conflicting principles from the identical verse about Yom Kippur blood sprinkling — each extracts a different operative rule from the same proof-text.

🧂 Why Salt and Wood Come from Communal Funds

Salt on offerings — mandatory for all sacrifices. The Gemara asks: who supplies it?
↓
The Merciful One granted salt rights to Israelites who donate half-shekels — this fund supplies the altar's salt. Individuals have no standing to bring it privately.
↓
Wood for the altar fire — must likewise come from communal supplies, not private stock.
↓
Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua's reasoning: Just as the altar was never used for personal purposes (built solely for God's service), the wood and fire on it must also never have served ordinary personal use.
↓
Challenge: What about Araunah's threshing boards — previously-used wood offered on David's altar? The Gemara resolves this as a special emergency circumstance.

🐂 Araunah's Threshing Boards: Used Wood on the Altar?

מוֹרִגִּים — threshing sled; term unfamiliar in Babylonia, requiring Rav Yehuda's explanation
Gemara Challenge & Resolution The verse (II Samuel 24:22) states Araunah offered David "the threshing boards [morigim] for wood" — implying previously-used wood was placed on the altar!

Ulla explains: morigim is a turbal — a threshing sled. Rav Yehuda clarifies: an iron-toothed implement. The Gemara resolves the contradiction: this was an extraordinary emergency (the plague); normal fitness rules for wood are not overridden by this episode.

🤲 Mishna: Handbook of Handful Mixtures

All Agree: Unfit (First Tanna)

  • Handful + handful of another standard meal offering
  • Handful + meal offering of priests (eaten entirely by priests)
  • Handful + meal offering of the anointed High Priest
  • Handful + meal offering of libations
  • Reason: once mixed, the handfuls cannot be properly separated and offered

Rabbi Yehuda's Distinction: Why Especially Unfit

  • Handful + anointed priest's offering → unfit: that offering is entirely burned
  • Handful + libation meal offering → unfit: also entirely burned, no remainder
  • These are kalil (wholly consumed) — impossible to separate remainder from handful
  • Handful + regular priests' offering: different reasoning applies
  • The wholly-burned offerings share the 'ascends entirely' characteristic

The Blood Parallel (Zevaḥim 77b)

  • Blood of valid offering mixed with water: fit if it looks like blood
  • Appearance standard applies even if water is the majority
  • This parallel case introduces the broader question of nullification
  • Sets the stage for the Yom Kippur bull-goat blood debate

📖 Rabbi Yoḥanan: Stress-Testing Both Readings

Rabbis' Reading Tested
Objection: Perhaps the verse only teaches both criteria together — altar-bound AND same-type — are needed to prevent nullification?

Response: Had the Torah used a same-type example alone, we'd think same-type is the rule. The fact it uses altar-bound items proves altar-bound is the operative principle, even for different types.
Rabbi Yehuda's Reading Tested
Objection: Perhaps the goat's blood isn't really nullified — it only appears so because it mingles. Maybe both criteria are required?

Response: Had the Torah used an altar-bound/different-type example, we'd say altar-bound is the rule. The same-type example proves min b'mino alone suffices.
Logical Method The Gemara repeatedly applies the same dialectical move: had the verse chosen a different example, we would have derived a narrower rule — the specific example chosen teaches the broadest possible principle.

⚖️ Does a Sacrificial Mixture Cause Nullification?

Two sacrificial substances are mixed together
↓
Same type (min b'mino)
Rabbi Yehuda: Never nullified — each component retains its identity regardless of proportion
Both ascend the altar
Rabbis: Never nullified — altar-bound items always maintain their sacrificial status
Neither criterion met
Standard bitul (nullification) rules apply — majority nullifies minority
↓
Gemara's conclusion: Each sage holds their own criterion alone is sufficient — the other factor in the Yom Kippur verse is coincidental, not causative. Each reading is internally consistent.
