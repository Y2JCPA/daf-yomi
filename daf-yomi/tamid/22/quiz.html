Tamid 22 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
From where does Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua derive that wood for the altar must come from communal, previously-unused supplies?
A. From the half-shekel chamber laws governing salt
B. By analogy: just as the altar itself was never used for personal purposes, neither may its wood
C. From the verse about Araunah's threshing boards given to David
D. From Rav Yehuda's ruling that turbal beds are unfit kindling
Correct: B — Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua reasons analogically: the altar was constructed solely for divine service and never served personal use — therefore the wood placed on it must likewise be free of any prior personal use.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Yehuda rule that a handful mixed with the meal offering of the anointed High Priest is especially unfit?
A. Because the High Priest's offering is more sacred and cannot be mingled with lesser offerings
B. Because the handful would be nullified by the greater volume of the High Priest's offering
C. Because the High Priest's offering is entirely burned (kalil) — there is no remainder, making separation impossible
D. Because the High Priest must personally bring his offering without any admixture
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehuda focuses on the structural feature: the anointed priest's meal offering is wholly consumed on the altar (kalil) — unlike a standard offering with a remainder eaten by priests. Once mixed, no portion can be set aside, rendering the combined mixture unfit.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, what is remarkable about the dispute between the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda?
A. They disagree about which tractate's laws apply to meal offering mixtures
B. Both derive their opposing principles from the exact same verse about Yom Kippur
C. Rabbi Yehuda accepts the Rabbis' ruling in practice even while disputing the reason
D. Their dispute is actually about a scribal error in the Mishna's wording
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan observes that both sages draw on the Yom Kippur blood-sprinkling verse (where bull blood and goat blood are mixed) — the Rabbis extract the 'altar-bound' principle, while Rabbi Yehuda extracts the 'same-type' principle from the identical proof-text.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does the Gemara resolve the challenge from Araunah's threshing boards — previously-used wood that was placed on David's altar?
A. The Gemara rules Araunah's boards were never actually used as a threshing implement
B. The Gemara explains the rule against previously-used wood was only enacted later, after David's time
C. The Gemara treats it as an emergency exception (the plague) that does not override ordinary fitness standards
D. Ulla explains that morigim actually refers to freshly cut wood, not a threshing sled
Correct: C — The Gemara resolves the contradiction by treating David's use of Araunah's boards as an extraordinary emergency circumstance (to stop the plague), not a normative ruling — it does not establish a precedent for permitting previously-used wood on the altar.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
The Gemara challenges Rabbi Yehuda's reading: perhaps the Yom Kippur verse only prevents nullification when BOTH criteria are met — same-type AND altar-bound. How is this objection answered?
A. The Gemara shows the verse explicitly lists the two criteria as alternatives, not conditions
B. Since the verse uses a same-type example, had altar-bound been the rule the Torah would have used a different-type example — the same-type choice proves same-type alone is the principle
C. Rabbi Yehuda concedes this point and revises his ruling to require both criteria
D. The Gemara cites a second verse where only the same-type criterion appears without altar-bound
Correct: B — The Gemara applies its standard dialectic: had the verse wanted to teach that altar-bound prevents nullification, it would have used a different-type example. By choosing same-type blood (bull and goat are both sacrificial blood), the verse signals that same-type status alone is the operative principle — no additional 'altar-bound' criterion is needed.
