⚖️ Three Running Disputes: Rabbi Yoḥanan vs. Reish Lakish

Rabbi Yoḥanan
Oil mixed outside the courtyard — disqualified. Even after the handful is removed, service must still occur inside the courtyard with a service vessel.
Reish Lakish
Oil mixed outside — valid. From the removal of the handful onward, mixing is merely a mitzva of the priesthood, not a formal Temple service requiring the courtyard.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Offering lacking before removal — owner brings flour from home and fills it. Lacking after removal — still burn the handful; remainder goes to the priests.
Reish Lakish
Offering lacking before removal — disqualified. Remainder lacking after removal but before burning — disqualified; the handful may not be burned.
Core Tension Does the meal offering's validity depend on the courtyard and service vessel for all rites, or only for those performed before the removal of the handful?

🫙 Slide 1: Oil Mixed Outside the Courtyard

Reish Lakish
Valid. The verse teaches that from the removal of the handful onward, pouring and mixing are solely a mitzva of the priesthood — they are not indispensable service. Therefore location outside the courtyard does not disqualify.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Disqualified. Granted, the priestly requirement may fall away after the handful removal — but the requirement to perform rites inside the courtyard using a service vessel remains fully in force.
Reish Lakish
Eating sacrificial meat inside the courtyard is also required, yet we do not say eating constitutes formal service. So too, mixing after removal is not formal service — its location is irrelevant.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Eating is not service at all. But mixing is a mitzva performed with a service vessel — the vessel itself demands courtyard performance. The analogy to eating fails.
Source Verse "And he shall pour oil upon it and place frankincense" (Leviticus 2:1) — Reish Lakish reads this as permitting outside performance; Rabbi Yoḥanan reads it as requiring courtyard and vessel.

📉 Slide 2: Meal Offering Lacking Before Handful Removal

Meal offering arrives at the Temple lacking its full required measure
↓
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Owner brings flour from home and fills the missing amount. Since the handful has not yet established the offering, adding flour is still permitted.
Reish Lakish
The offering is disqualified. Derived from the verse: 'And he shall remove of its fine flour' — the flour must be whole and complete for removal to be valid.
↓
Proof cited by Rabbi Yoḥanan: The Mishna (Nega'im 14:10) — if the leper's log of oil became lacking before the priest poured from it, the priest brings another log. This supports the ability to supplement a lacking offering.
↓
Reish Lakish's counter: The leper's log is different — it is not yet established as an offering at that stage. A meal offering once dedicated operates under stricter rules.

🤲 Slide 4: Deriving the Left-Hand Disqualification — Rabbi Zeira

Mishna states: If the priest removed the handful with his left hand, the meal offering is unfit. But where does the Torah require the right hand for handful removal?
↓
Rabbi Zeira's source: The purification rite of the metzora (leper) — Leviticus 14 repeatedly specifies the priest pours oil onto his own left hand.
↓
The four-fold specification of the left hand in that passage is exceptional — it signals that ordinarily the right hand is required for Temple service, and the leper's rite is a deliberate deviation.
↓
Challenge: Perhaps the opposite — since the leper's rite requires the left hand, deduce that left hand is generally valid for service?
↓
Response: The fourfold repetition of 'left hand' in the leper passage teaches that this case is unique. All other Temple rites revert to the default: right hand required.
↓
Further challenge (Rabbi Yirmeya): Another verse states 'upon the thumb of his right hand' — should we now require the right thumb specifically for all service? The Gemara continues to unpack the hermeneutics of this passage.

📜 Slide 5: Rav Malkiyu — Two Verses, Two Cases

וְהַנּוֹתֶרֶת מִן־הַמִּנְחָה לְאַהֲרֹן וּלְבָנָיו (ויקרא ב:ג)
Rav Malkiyu's Dual-Verse Argument Two separate verses in Leviticus 2 address a lacking meal offering:

Verse 1 — Leviticus 2:2: "And he shall remove… of its fine flour" — the word 'of' implies the flour must be whole; if the offering lacks before removal, the removal is invalid.

Verse 2 — Leviticus 2:3: "But that which is left of the meal offering shall be Aaron's and his sons'" — the priests receive the remainder only when a full measure was present at the time of removal. If the remainder became lacking after removal, it is not given to the priests — disqualified.

Why two verses? Each addresses a different stage of disqualification. Together they constitute a decisive refutation of Rabbi Yoḥanan's two lenient rulings — the Gemara ultimately rejects Rav Malkiyu's reading and preserves Rabbi Yoḥanan's positions by reinterpreting the verses.
