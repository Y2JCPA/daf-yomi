QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Reish Lakish, if a priest mixed the oil of a meal offering outside the Temple courtyard after the handful was removed, what is the status of the offering?
A. Disqualified — all rites must be performed inside the courtyard
B. Valid — after removal of the handful, mixing is only a priestly mitzva, not formal service
C. Valid only if the kohen gadol later ratifies the act inside the courtyard
D. Disqualified only if the mixture was poured back into a non-service vessel
Correct: B — Reish Lakish holds that from the removal of the handful onward, pouring and mixing are solely a mitzva of the priesthood — they are not indispensable Temple service, so their location outside the courtyard does not disqualify the offering.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, if a meal offering is found to be lacking its full measure before the handful is removed, what is the correct course of action?
A. The offering is disqualified and must be buried in the Temple courtyard
B. The priest may add flour from the Temple's communal storehouse
C. The owner brings flour from his own home and fills the missing amount
D. The owner must dedicate an entirely new meal offering
Correct: C — Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that since the handful has not yet been removed, the offering has not been fully established. The owner may still supplement it with his own flour from home, since no point of no return has been reached.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
From where does Rabbi Zeira derive that removing the handful with the left hand disqualifies the meal offering?
A. From the verse 'the priest shall take a handful' — 'taking' implies the dominant right hand
B. From the purification rite of the leper, which specifies the left hand as a deliberate exception, revealing that the right hand is normally required
C. From a verse about Aharon's right-hand consecration at the Tabernacle dedication
D. This is a halakha transmitted to Moshe at Sinai with no scriptural basis
Correct: B — Rabbi Zeira derives the right-hand requirement from the leper's rite in Leviticus 14, where the Torah specifies 'left hand' four times as an explicit exception. This shows that 'left hand' is the abnormal case — all other Temple rites default to the right hand.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What does Rav Malkiyu argue based on the two verses in Leviticus 2 concerning a lacking meal offering?
A. One verse disqualifies a lacking offering before handful removal; the other disqualifies a lacking remainder after removal — refuting both of Rabbi Yoḥanan's lenient rulings
B. One verse applies to individual offerings and one to communal offerings — they address different categories entirely
C. One verse is the primary law and the other is an asmakhta (mere support), carrying no independent legal weight
D. Both verses refer to the same scenario; the repetition simply intensifies the prohibition
Correct: A — Rav Malkiyu argues that Leviticus 2:2 addresses a meal offering lacking before the removal of the handful, and Leviticus 2:3 addresses one that becomes lacking afterward. Both cases are disqualified — which would refute Rabbi Yoḥanan's lenient positions in both disputes. The Gemara ultimately rejects this reading.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, if the remainder of a meal offering becomes lacking between the removal of the handful and the burning of the handful on the altar, what is the ruling?
A. The handful is burned on the altar and whatever remainder exists — however small — is given to the priests
B. The handful may not be burned since there is no longer a valid full remainder
C. The priest must wait and see if the remainder is replenished before deciding
D. The handful is buried rather than burned to avoid a blessing made in vain
Correct: A — Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that once the handful is validly removed, the burning may proceed even if the remainder later became lacking. The existing (even diminished) remainder retains its status and is given to the priests. Reish Lakish disagrees and disqualifies both the burning and the remainder.
