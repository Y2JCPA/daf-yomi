QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yoḥanan's clarification (14b), what does Rabbi Yosei hold about the two thighs of a peace offering regarding piggul?
A. They are two separate bodies — intent for one does not affect the other
B. They are one body — piggul intent for one renders the other piggul as well
C. Only the right thigh is subject to piggul law
D. The thighs can only become piggul through direct intent, never indirectly
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan (14b) clarifies that Rabbi Yosei considers the two thighs as one body (גּוּפָא חֲדָא). Therefore, piggul intent regarding one thigh renders the other piggul as well — just as mixed intent regarding both Shavuot loaves renders both piggul.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does Rav Ashi challenge Rav Huna's position about piggul intent?
A. He cites Abaye's ruling that combining half-measures is prohibited
B. He shows from a baraita that intent to pour remaining blood (not eat the meat) can render the meat piggul
C. He proves that the handful itself becomes piggul when intent is directed at it
D. He argues that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi explicitly supports Rav Huna's view
Correct: B — Rav Ashi (14a) cites a baraita where intent to pour the remaining blood outside its boundary the next day renders the offering piggul — yet blood has no minimum measure for piggul. The meat becomes piggul via indirect intent, demonstrating that piggul intent need not target the forbidden item directly.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does Ravina's challenge from the mishna (13a) refute Rav Huna?
A. Because the mishna shows that the handful can itself become piggul
B. Because intent to burn the handful renders the remainder piggul, yet the handful cannot itself become piggul — proving indirect intent creates piggul
C. Because the mishna demonstrates that combining intents across two rites creates piggul
D. Because Ravina shows that Rabbi Yosei authored the ruling in the mishna
Correct: B — The mishna says intent to burn the handful the next day renders the remainder piggul. But the handful itself cannot become piggul (Zevahim 42b: permitting factors don't become piggul). So the remainder becomes piggul through indirect intent — contradicting Rav Huna's view.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does Abaye rule that half-olive-bulk intentions across two separate lamb slaughterings do NOT combine to create piggul?
A. Because Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi explicitly ruled they cannot combine
B. Because the two lambs are two separate bodies with no Torah unity
C. Because there is no analogous case where such combining creates full piggul, so no rabbinic decree applies
D. Because piggul can only be created during blood sprinkling, not slaughter
Correct: C — Abaye argues that rabbinic decrees of disqualification were only enacted where a fully analogous piggul case exists in other circumstances. Since no existing case features half-measures across two separate lamb slaughterings combining into piggul, there is no basis for a decree here.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In the mishna at the end of 14b, when one Shavuot loaf becomes impure before blood sprinkling, what is the dispute?
A. Rabbi Yehuda: both loaves are eaten; Sages: both are burned
B. Rabbi Yehuda: both loaves are burned; Sages: the pure loaf is eaten and the impure one is burned
C. Rabbi Yehuda: only the impure loaf is burned; Sages: the priest immerses and re-performs the rite
D. Both Rabbi Yehuda and the Sages agree the frontplate atones and both loaves are eaten
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda holds that communal offerings cannot be divided — both loaves go to the place of burning together. The Sages disagree and permit the pure loaf to be eaten while only the impure one is burned. The Gemara (Rabbi Elazar, Rav Pappa) notes this dispute turns on whether the High Priest's frontplate effects atonement here.
