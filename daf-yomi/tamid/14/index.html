🔥 The Great Piggul Dispute

Rav Huna
The baraita about the two loaves follows Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, not Rabbi Yosei. We cannot infer from it that Rabbi Yosei considers the two thighs as one body.
Rav Ashi
Refutation! A baraita in Rabbi Yosei's name: if one intends to pour the remaining blood outside its boundary the next day, the meat becomes piggul — even though the intent was never about the meat itself!
Ravina
Refutation from our own mishna! Intent to burn the handful renders the remainder piggul — yet the handful cannot itself become piggul. Indirect intent clearly creates piggul in other parts.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Rabbi Yosei holds the two thighs are one body — intent for one renders the other piggul. For two loaves: mixed intent (olive-bulk from both) = both piggul; separated intent (one loaf only) = only that loaf piggul.
Resolution Rabbi Yoḥanan clarifies Rabbi Yosei's position: the two thighs form one body, as do the two Shavuot loaves. 'Mixing' intent (spanning both) contaminates both; 'separating' intent leaves the other pure.

⚔️ Rav Huna vs. Two Challengers

Rav Huna
The baraita about the two loaves is Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's ruling, not Rabbi Yosei's. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi requires intent combining both loaves for piggul; we learn nothing about Rabbi Yosei's view on thighs.
Rav Ashi
From a baraita: Rabbi Yosei rules that if one slaughters a burnt offering with intent to pour the remaining blood outside tomorrow, the offering is piggul. But blood has no minimum measure! The meat becomes piggul via indirect intent — refuting Rav Huna.
Ravina
From our mishna (13a): intent to burn the handful tomorrow renders the remainder piggul. But can the handful itself become piggul? No! (Zevahim 42b: 'The permitting factor does not become piggul.') So indirect intent does render other parts piggul.
Both Challenges Stand Rav Huna's view that piggul intent must directly reference the forbidden item is refuted from multiple independent sources. Rav Ashi and Ravina both demonstrate that indirect piggul intent operates across parts of an offering.

🔄 When Do Piggul Intentions Combine Across Rites?

Half olive-bulk intent during slaughter + half olive-bulk intent during sprinkling — do they combine?
↓
"Some Say" (lenient view)
Both slaughter and sprinkling are permitting factors — yes, the two halves combine to create piggul liability
Levi's Baraita (strict view)
Intentions during the four sacrificial rites do not combine — each rite stands alone for piggul
↓
Abaye's Resolution: Rabbis only decreed disqualification where an analogous full piggul case exists. Where no such analogue exists (e.g., half-measures across two separate lamb slaughterings), there is no decree and no combining.

🍞 Two Loaves: Intent That Mixes vs. Intent That Separates

Intent 'Mixes' the Loaves

  • Priest intends olive-bulk from both loaves combined
  • The verse treats the two loaves as one body (חָדָא גּוּפָא)
  • Result: both loaves become piggul
  • Rabbi Yosei agrees: mixed intent = one body = both piggul
  • Analogous to: both thighs rendered piggul together

Intent 'Separates' the Loaves

  • Priest intends olive-bulk from only one specific loaf
  • He mentally distinguishes between the two loaves
  • Result: only the one loaf he intended becomes piggul
  • Rabbi Yosei: separated intent = each loaf stands alone
  • Open question: what if intent involves only one type among four loaf types of a todah offering?

🚿 Mishna: When One Shavuot Loaf Becomes Impure

טָמֵא אַחַד מִשְּׁנֵי הַלֶּחֶם — נֶחְלְקוּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וַחֲכָמִים
מִשְׁנָה — Menachot 14b If one of the two Shavuot loaves (or one arrangement of shewbread) became ritually impure: Rabbi Yehuda: Both loaves must go to the place of burning — communal offerings (קָרְבַּן צִבּוּר) cannot be divided; the community brings both or neither. The Sages: The pure loaf is eaten in its sanctity; only the impure loaf is burned. Rabbi Elazar (Gemara): This dispute applies specifically when impurity occurred before blood sprinkling — the act that renders the loaves permitted. Rav Pappa: Their underlying disagreement is whether the High Priest's frontplate (צִיץ) effects atonement for ritual impurity in this case.
