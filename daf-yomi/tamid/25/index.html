👑 What Does the Tzitz Atone For?

Tzitz (High Priest's Frontplate)
Exodus 28:38 — 'Aaron shall bear the sin of the sacred items'
↓
✅ Atones
Ritual impurity of an individual's offering sacrificed in the Temple
❌ Does NOT Atone
Offerings that left the courtyard, left-hand service, blemished animals, gentile offerings
↓
Abaye's Key Principle: 'That they may be accepted before the Lord' — tzitz covers only sins whose general prohibition is sometimes suspended within the Temple itself

🔄 Narrowing the Tzitz's Scope — Objections and Responses

Rabbi Zeira
Why not say the tzitz atones for offerings that LEFT THE COURTYARD? That prohibition was suspended for the communal Pesach offering!
Abaye
'That they may be accepted BEFORE THE LORD' — only sins whose general prohibition is permitted before the Lord, i.e., in the Temple itself, are covered. Leaving the courtyard takes it away from the Lord's presence.
Rabbi Ile'a
Why not say the tzitz atones for LEFT-HAND SERVICE? That prohibition is suspended on Yom Kippur for the Kohen Gadol!
Abaye
'Aaron shall bear the sin of the SACRED ITEMS [hakodashim]' — the tzitz atones only for a sin that was committed IN the sacred item itself. The hand used for service is not a sacred item.
Rav Ashi
Additionally, 'hakodashim' (plural) teaches that the OBJECT of the sin must itself be holy — the hand performing left-hand service does not qualify as a sacred object.
📜 Key Verse Exodus 28:38 — 'And it shall be always upon his forehead, that they may be accepted before the Lord'

💉 Impure Blood Sprinkled on the Altar — Amud B Dispute

Blood of an offering contracted ritual impurity
A priest then sprinkled it on the altar
↓
Unwittingly (b'shogeg)
Universally accepted: Offering accepted and atones for the owner
Intentionally (b'meizid)
Disputed between baraitot:
Baraita 1 — accepted
Baraita 2 — NOT accepted
↓
Ravina's Final Resolution: The distinction is not intentional vs. unwitting sprinkling, but rather whose impurity: the owner's vs. the priest's. Both baraitot can be read consistently on their own terms.

🔀 Three Attempts to Resolve the Baraitot Contradiction

Rav Yosef

  • Baraita 1 (accepted) = Rabbi Yosei — Sages do NOT penalize intentional impurity
  • Baraita 2 (not accepted) = Rabbi Eliezer — Sages DO penalize
  • Objection: Did Rabbi Yosei really extend this to impure edible portions (not just blood)?

Rav Ḥisda

  • Reverses attribution: Baraita 1 = Rabbi Eliezer, Baraita 2 = Rabbi Yosei
  • Rabbi Eliezer: Tzitz accepts impure edible portions of offerings
  • Objection: Rabbi Eliezer holds sprinkling ineffective outside courtyard — yet Sages still penalize intentional impurity?

Ravina (Accepted Resolution)

  • Impurity contracted unwittingly OR intentionally → offering accepted
  • Sprinkling performed unwittingly OR intentionally → accepted
  • Baraitot differ on WHOSE impurity (owner vs. priest), not on intentional vs. unwitting

📖 Core Principle — The Tzitz and Permanent Exclusions

כָּל מוּם לֹא יִהְיֶה בּוֹ — כִּי לֹא לְרָצוֹן יִהְיֶה לָכֶם (ויקרא כב:כ)
👑 Blemished Animals: An Absolute Exclusion Rav Sima challenged Rav Ashi: why not say the tzitz atones for blemished animals sacrificed on the altar, since their prohibition is sometimes suspended (e.g., a firstborn animal with a blemish may be slaughtered for eating)? Rav Ashi responded: the verse states 'it shall NOT be accepted' (Leviticus 22:23) and 'it shall NOT be acceptable for you' (Leviticus 22:20) — these absolute formulations permanently exclude blemished offerings from tzitz atonement, unlike impurity which has no such absolute exclusionary verse.
