📜 Rav's Foundational Principle

כל מקום שנאמר תורה וחוק — לא נאמרו אלא לעכב
Core Teaching Rav establishes: wherever the Torah uses law (תורה) and statute (חוק) for a sacrificial rite, their combination signals that failure to perform those rites invalidates the offering. The Gemara challenges this with cases where only one term appears — nazirite, thanks offering, leper, Yom Kippur — and progressively refines the principle. A parallel dispute between Rav and Shmuel concerns whether repeated verses in the Torah independently mark a rite as indispensable.

🔀 Does a Verse Apply to Both Preceding AND Succeeding Matter?

Rabbis
The phrase 'with his finger' in the blood-sprinkling verse applies in both directions — to the preceding act (collection of blood) and the succeeding act (placement of blood). A verse may simultaneously govern what comes before and after it.
Rabbi Shimon
A verse cannot be interpreted as referring to both preceding and succeeding matter. The term 'hand' is stated only for placement, not collection — therefore even disqualified priests may collect blood, as no restriction is written there.
Abaye
This is precisely the point of dispute: the Rabbis hold that 'with his finger' governs both directions. Rabbi Shimon denies this principle entirely.
Gemara
Rabbi Shimon's actual reasoning: the letter vav (ו) — 'and he shall bring it to Aaron's sons, the priests' — adds the priest's role to the preceding act of pouring oil, requiring priestly involvement there specifically.
Rabbi Shimon's Tool The conjunctive vav (ו) links the priest to the immediately preceding act. This is not a 'both directions' reading — it is a forward linkage from the pouring of oil to the priest.

📜 Rav's Principle: Law & Statute → Indispensability

Rav's Rule: Where a verse states both law (תורה) and statute (חוק) for a sacrificial rite, failure to perform that rite invalidates the offering
↓
Only 'Law' stated
Nazirite, Thanks Offering (Toda), Leper — yet certain rites ARE indispensable. Challenge!
Only 'Statute' stated
Yom Kippur (two goats), Meal Offering eating — yet rites are indispensable. Challenge!
↓
Resolution: Rav means: even where only one term appears, either law or statute alone suffices to teach indispensability — both terms express the same principle
↓
Meal Offering Exception: The 'statute' written there refers to eating — but the Gemara concludes that statute-by-eating applies to the entire offering. Only rites explicitly repeated in verse (groats and oil, Lev. 2:16) are indispensable

🔁 Rav vs Shmuel — Do Repeated Verses Render Rites Indispensable?

Rav

  • Every rite of the meal offering that the Torah repeats is repeated specifically to teach that its absence invalidates the offering
  • 'His handful' (Lev. 2:2) is repeated in Lev. 6:8 — using the hand rather than a utensil is therefore indispensable
  • Aaron's eighth-day inauguration service ('he took it in his hand and sacrificed it') confirms 'with the hand' as a binding rule for all generations
  • Repetition = indispensability, regardless of whether the context was permanent or temporary

Shmuel

  • Shmuel agrees in principle that repeated verses can signal indispensability
  • However, Shmuel refuses to derive permanent halakha from a temporary, one-time situation — the eighth-day inauguration was never meant to set ongoing law
  • Exception: the twelve princes' offerings, each repeated once — Shmuel does derive permanent halakha from those, because twelve-fold repetition is sufficient
  • A single temporary instance ≠ established halakha

🏛️ Where Must the Meal Offering Be Brought — the Altar Corner Debate

Challenge (Rav Kahana & Rav Asi): 'Bringing to the corner of the altar' is repeated in verses — yet it is apparently not indispensable? This seems to contradict Rav's principle!
↓
Answer: That verse does not repeat a mitzva — it merely defines the location where the meal offering is to be brought. Location verses are definitional, not obligatory repetitions.
↓
Baraita — Two Competing Phrases: 'In front of the altar' = southern side of the altar. 'Before the Lord' = western side of the altar. They seem to conflict.
↓
Rabbi Eliezer's Principle: When fulfilling one phrase would completely nullify the other, choose the reading that preserves both partially — bring the meal offering to the southwest corner, satisfying both directions at once.
↓
Rav Ashi: Rabbi Eliezer holds the entire altar stood in the northern part of the Temple courtyard — thus 'before the Lord' (west) and 'in front of the altar' (south) converge at the altar's southwest corner without contradiction.

↓
Rabbis: Yes — verse applies in both directions simultaneously
