QUESTION 1 OF 5
Rav Ashi argues that a verse is necessary to disqualify tereifa because the a fortiori from a blemished animal is refutable. What is the unique feature of a blemished animal that blocks the inference?
A. A blemished animal is visible to the naked eye, while a tereifa may have internal injuries
B. The Torah rendered those who sacrifice like that which is sacrificed — a blemished priest also may not perform the service
C. A blemished animal is never sanctified, so no verse is needed
D. A blemished animal's prohibition was once permitted, unlike a tereifa
Correct: B — Rav Ashi: What is notable about a blemished animal? The Torah rendered those who sacrifice like that which is sacrificed — just as a blemished animal is unfit, so too a blemished priest is unfit for service. Tereifa has no such parallel with the officiant, so the a fortiori fails.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does ben Beteira rule should be done if a priest removed the meal offering handful with his left hand?
A. The meal offering is permanently disqualified and must be destroyed
B. A second priest must perform a fresh removal immediately
C. The handful is returned to the vessel and removed again with the right hand
D. The handful is placed on the altar as-is, since the priest's intent was valid
Correct: C — Ben Beteira: the handful removed with the left hand is returned to the meal offering vessel, and the priest then removes it again properly with his right hand. This is the paradigm case from which Rav derives that the same applies to a handful removed by a non-priest.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Naḥman's final ruling, at exactly what point does the removal of a handful by an unfit person become an irrevocably complete sacrificial rite?
A. The moment the unfit person's hand touches the meal offering flour
B. When the handful is physically separated from the meal offering in the vessel
C. When the handful is placed inside a service vessel for sanctification
D. When the handful is brought to the southwestern corner of the altar
Correct: C — Rav Naḥman ruled: while removal of the handful constitutes a sacrificial rite, it is incomplete until the handful is placed inside a service vessel (קידוש כלי). Before that sanctification, ben Beteira permits returning the handful; after sanctification, the rite is complete and the offering is disqualified.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does the Gemara conclude that all three verses — "of the herd," "well-watered pastures," and "under the rod" — are each necessary to exclude tereifa?
A. Three witnesses are required by Torah law for any disqualification
B. Each verse applies to a different type of offering: burnt, sin, and peace
C. Each verse covers a different scenario: sanctified while already tereifa, born tereifa, or rendered tereifa after sanctification
D. The three books of the Torah (Leviticus, Numbers, Deuteronomy) must each provide support
Correct: C — Without all three verses, one might limit the exclusion to only the scenario not covered by the others. "Of the herd" covers sanctification of an already-tereifa animal; "well-watered pastures" covers one born tereifa; "under the rod" covers an animal rendered tereifa after being sanctified by its owner.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rav Aḥa son of Rava challenges Rav Ashi's argument that tereifa's prohibition was "once permitted." What example does Rav Ashi point to as the relevant permitted case?
A. A tereifa animal offered on a private altar before the Tabernacle was built
B. The pinching of a bird sin offering, which permits the tereifa bird for priestly consumption despite the lack of proper slaughter
C. A tereifa that is eaten by a non-Jew, for whom its prohibition does not apply
D. A tereifa dedicated to Temple maintenance rather than as an altar offering
Correct: B — The Gemara settles on this: the pinching (melikah) of a bird sin offering renders the bird — even a tereifa — permitted to the priests for consumption, despite the fact that it was not slaughtered with a knife. This constitutes a case where the tereifa's general prohibition was "permitted," distinguishing it from blemished animals and caesarean-born animals whose prohibition was never permitted.
