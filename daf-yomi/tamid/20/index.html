🧂 The Salt Requirement: What Must Be Salted?

R. Yehuda
There is a covenant stated regarding salt — offerings must always be salted. The covenant derives from Numbers 18:19.
R. Shimon
A textual parallel: 'It is an everlasting covenant of salt' (Num. 18:19) — the gezerah shavah links the two passages and confirms the eternal salt obligation.
Rav
Only sacrificial rites repeated in the Torah are indispensable. Since salt is not repeated, if one omitted it the meal offering remains fit — consistent with our Mishna.
Rav Yosef
The term 'brit' (covenant) written about salt is equivalent to a repetition — covenant language alone establishes indispensability!
Core Question of the Daf Which items burned on the altar require salting, and is the salt requirement indispensable (me'akev)? The Gemara derives the answer through klal-prat-klal.

📜 Klal-Prat-Klal: Deriving What Requires Salting

Klal (Generalization): 'You shall sacrifice salt with all your offerings' (Lev. 2:13)
↓
Prat (Detail): 'Every meal offering of yours you shall season with salt' — the handful of the meal offering (kometz)
↓
Klal (Generalization again): 'You shall sacrifice salt with all your offerings' (Lev. 2:13)
↓
✅ Included in salting
Items for which other things come as a requirement — just as wood is required to burn the handful
❌ Excluded from salting
Items that do not have other things coming as a requirement for them (e.g., blood, remainder eaten by priests)

⚖️ Is Salt Indispensable? Rav vs. The Baraita

Rav
Salt is not repeated in a second verse, so it is not indispensable — the meal offering is valid even without salt. This matches our Mishna.
Gemara (Challenge)
But isn't salt mentioned explicitly: 'Every meal offering of yours you shall season with salt'? Isn't that a second verse — making it indispensable?
Rav Yosef
Alternatively: the 'brit' (covenant) written about salt functions like a repetition — covenant language implies eternal, indispensable obligation.
Gemara (Resolution)
The verse 'every meal offering of yours' is not an independent repetition but the 'prat' (detail) in a klal-prat-klal analysis — it defines scope, not indispensability alone.
Also: Non-Priest Cannot Salt Rav Yosef notes: a non-priest cannot approach the altar to salt the handful — so even discussing whether a non-priest 'salted it' is theoretical, reinforcing why the salt rite's details are carefully analyzed.

🔥 Limbs of Burnt Offering vs. Blood: Who Gets Salted?

🔥 Limbs of Burnt Offering — Salted ✅

  • Requires wood to be burned on the altar ✅ (like a meal offering)
  • Subject to me'ilah (misuse of consecrated property) ✅
  • Forbidden to eat in a state of ritual impurity ✅
  • Entirely consumed on the altar — not eaten ✅
  • Shares many characteristics with the meal offering → included

🩸 Blood — Not Salted ❌

  • Does NOT require wood ❌
  • NOT subject to me'ilah ❌
  • Impurity restrictions differ ❌
  • Sprinkled on altar — not burned on it ❌
  • Does render offering permitted — but differences outweigh → excluded

🪵 Voluntary Wood Offering — A New Korban

קָרְבָּן מִנְחָה — the extra 'korban' derives the voluntary wood offering (Lev. 2:1). וְכָל מִנְחָתְךָ בַּמֶּלַח תִּמְלָח — 'every meal offering of yours you shall season with salt' (Lev. 2:13)
Baraita — Derived from Lev. 2:1 The superfluous word 'korban' in 'when one brings a korban mincha' (Lev. 2:1) teaches that one may voluntarily donate wood as a sacrificial offering for the altar — the minimum amount is two logs.

Rava: This voluntary wood offering requires removal of a handful (like a meal offering) — a handful of wood is removed separately.
Rav Pappa: Disagrees with Rava's requirement.

Meanwhile, the phrase 'every meal offering of yours' is used to exclude wood from the salting requirement — wood is not a meal offering per se, even if it functions like one.
