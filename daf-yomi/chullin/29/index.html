📏 Exactly Half a Siman — Majority or Not?

Rv
Rav: Exactly half is NOT like the majority. You need at least one hair's breadth MORE than half to constitute "majority" for valid shechitah.
RK
Rav Kahana: Exactly half IS like the majority — half a siman counts as "majority of one siman" for a bird.
Rava's distinction: For treifah (determining if an animal is fatally injured), we need a "clearly visible majority" — exactly half doesn't look like a majority. But for shechitah, the standard might be different. The Gemara ultimately establishes: half is NOT the majority. You need more than half.

⏱️ The Big Question: WHEN Is Shechitah Accomplished?

RL
Reish Lakish (citing Levi the Elder): Shechitah is accomplished only at its CONCLUSION — the final moment when the majority is severed. Everything before is just cutting; the halachic act occurs at the instant the majority is complete.
RY
R' Yochanan: Shechitah is accomplished FROM BEGINNING TO END — the entire cutting process, from first contact to completion, is the halachic act. Every moment of the cut is "shechitah."
Why does this matter? It affects cases where something changes mid-cut: if a disqualifying thought occurs, or if one person starts and another finishes. If shechitah is only at the end, the person/intent at the END is what counts. If it's from beginning to end, the entire process must be kosher throughout.

👥 Gentile + Jew — Each Cuts One Siman

A gentile cuts one siman of an animal. A Jew cuts the other siman.
↓
Is this valid shechitah?
↓
Rava: Everyone agrees — INVALID. Even R' Yochanan (shechitah from beginning to end) agrees here: the gentile's cut is part of the shechitah, and a gentile's shechitah is invalid. Even though the Jew completed it, the first siman was already disqualified.

🔪 Yom Kippur — One Kohen Starts, Another Finishes

The Kohen Gadol begins slaughtering the Yom Kippur bull
↓
He becomes unable to continue. Can another Kohen finish the slaughter?
↓
Reish Lakish: Yes — valid. Since shechitah is only at the conclusion, and the second Kohen finishes it, the service was completed by a valid Kohen.
↓
The Torah says "the entire Yom Kippur service shall be by one Kohen" — but Reish Lakish says this means: the second Kohen completes it if necessary (not that a different person invalidates it)

🐦 Bird Olah — Simanim Pinched in Different Places

A bird Olah requires both simanim to be pinched
↓
What if one siman was pinched below the red line (like a Chatat) and the other above (like an Olah)?
↓
This relates to the "beginning vs. end" debate: if shechitah is at the end, the second siman's location determines validity. If throughout — both must be in the right place.

🎓 Key Principle: "When does an act happen?" sounds philosophical, but it's deeply practical. If shechitah is only at the conclusion, then everything before is preparatory — and problems during the preparation may not matter. If the act spans the entire process, every moment must be kosher. This debate echoes through many areas of halacha.
