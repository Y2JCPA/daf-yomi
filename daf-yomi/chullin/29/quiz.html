Chullin 29 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
If exactly half of a siman is cut (not more, not less), does this count as "majority" for valid shechitah?
The Gemara establishes that exactly half a siman does NOT count as the majority. You need at least slightly more than half. Rava explains that for treifah, we need a "clearly visible majority" — exactly half doesn't look like a majority. The same standard applies to shechitah. Even one additional hair's breadth over half is sufficient.
Question 2 of 5
According to Reish Lakish, when is the halachic act of shechitah accomplished?
Reish Lakish (citing Levi the Elder) holds that shechitah is accomplished only at its conclusion — the final instant when the majority of the siman is severed. Everything before that moment is merely preparation. R' Yochanan disagrees, holding the entire process from beginning to end constitutes the act of shechitah.
Question 3 of 5
A gentile cuts one siman and a Jew cuts the other siman of an animal. Is this valid?
Rava establishes that everyone agrees this is invalid. Even Reish Lakish (shechitah at the end) concedes here: the gentile's cut created one of the two required simanim, meaning part of the shechitah was performed by a gentile. Both simanim contribute to the halachic act, and the gentile's contribution disqualifies the entire process.
Question 4 of 5
On Yom Kippur, if the Kohen Gadol starts slaughtering but becomes unable to continue, can another Kohen finish?
Reish Lakish holds that another Kohen can finish. Since shechitah is accomplished only at its conclusion, the second Kohen is the one performing the halachic act. The verse about "one Kohen" performing the service is interpreted to mean: the conclusion (which is the halachic moment) must be by an authorized Kohen — not that the entire physical process must be done by one person.
Question 5 of 5
Why does the "beginning vs. end" debate matter practically?
This debate has wide practical impact. If shechitah is from beginning to end (R' Yochanan), then any disqualifying factor during any part of the cutting process — wrong intent, a different person, a different location — invalidates the entire shechitah. If it's only at the conclusion (Reish Lakish), problems during the early cutting may not matter as long as the conclusion is kosher.
