Chullin 39 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
If someone slaughters an animal intending to sprinkle its blood for idol worship, what is Rabbi Yochanan's ruling?
Rabbi Yochanan rules the slaughter invalid and benefit from the animal forbidden. His reasoning: intent "transfers" from one sacrificial rite to another — intent for idolatrous blood-sprinkling infects and disqualifies the slaughter act itself, even though the slaughter was performed correctly.
Question 2 of 5
What is the fundamental principle underlying Reish Lakish's lenient ruling?
Reish Lakish holds that intent cannot "jump" between distinct sacrificial rites. Slaughter is one act; blood-sprinkling is another. Intent during slaughter is judged only by the slaughter act itself — future intent for how blood or fat will be used is irrelevant to whether the slaughter was valid. Therefore: valid slaughter, permitted benefit.
Question 3 of 5
How does the Gemara prove that Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish are internally consistent — not just ruling situationally?
The Gemara shows that in a Temple case — where a Kohen slaughters a Chatat correctly (l'shmah) but intends to sprinkle its blood for a different offering's sake — both sages apply the same principle. Rabbi Yochanan still says invalid; Reish Lakish still says valid. This proves they apply their principle universally, not just when it's convenient.
Question 4 of 5
Rabbi Yosei rules that "intent does not invalidate non-sacred slaughter." How does Rav Sheshet explain this WITHOUT contradicting Rabbi Yochanan?
Rav Sheshet resolves the apparent contradiction: Rabbi Yosei is saying that a GENTILE's idolatrous intent doesn't affect the Jewish slaughterer's valid act. This is about whose intent counts — not about whether intent transfers between rites. Rabbi Yosei's ruling is therefore unrelated to the Rabbi Yochanan / Reish Lakish dispute.
Question 5 of 5
Why did the Gemara need BOTH the idol worship case AND the Temple Chatat case to be stated?
Classic tzrikhuta reasoning: from the idol case alone, you might think Rabbi Yochanan only transfers intent because idolatry is uniquely severe. From the Temple case alone, you might think Reish Lakish only permits because the slaughter was explicitly correct. Together, both cases prove each sage applies their principle universally — transfer of intent is always relevant (Yochanan) or never relevant (Reish Lakish) between rites.
