🪆 The Case: Idol-Intent Slaughter

Setup: Someone slaughters an animal properly — correct method, correct simanim. But their intent is to use the blood or fat in idol worship afterward. The slaughter act itself was technically fine. Does the idolatrous future-intent invalidate it?
✂️ Proper slaughter performed (windpipe + gullet cut correctly)
↓
🩸 Intent: "I will sprinkle this blood / burn this fat for an idol"
↓
R' Yochanan: ❌ Invalid. Benefit forbidden. Intent transfers from one rite to another.
Reish Lakish: ✅ Valid. Benefit permitted. Intent does NOT transfer between rites.
3 / 8

⚖️ The Core Dispute: Transfer of Intent

Background: In the Temple, intent during one sacrificial rite can disqualify an entire offering — this is the concept of machshava (disqualifying thought). The dispute: does this principle apply outside the Temple?

Rabbi Yochanan — YES, transfers

  • 📖 We derive non-sacred slaughter from Temple slaughter
  • 🔗 Intent for a future rite infects the slaughter act
  • 🩸 Intending to sprinkle blood for idols = the whole animal is forbidden
  • ⚠️ The acts are legally linked

Reish Lakish — NO, stays separate

  • ✂️ Slaughter is judged by slaughter alone
  • 🚫 Future intent for blood/fat rites = irrelevant
  • 🔓 Each act in the sequence stands independently
  • ✅ Clean slaughter = kosher animal

🔄 They're Consistent: The Temple Chatat Parallel

The Gemara proves both sages apply their principle universally, not just in the idol case. They dispute the same point in a Temple context:
Kohen slaughters a Chatat (sin offering) correctly — l'shmah (for its own sake)
↓
But intends to sprinkle its blood for a different offering's sake (not l'shmah)
↓
R' Yochanan: ❌ Unfit. Transfer of intent applies even in Temple — the blood-intent disqualifies the whole offering.
Reish Lakish: ✅ Valid. The slaughter was done correctly l'shmah — a later rite's intent can't retroactively disqualify it.

📐 Tzrikhuta: Why State Both Cases?

Q
They already stated their dispute in the idol case. Why repeat it for the Temple Chatat case? One case should be enough to know their positions!
Ans
Without the idol case: you'd think R' Yochanan only says intent transfers because idolatry is uniquely severe. Maybe normal inter-rite intent doesn't transfer. Without the Temple case: you'd think Reish Lakish only permits because the slaughter was explicitly l'shmah. Maybe if the slaughter itself was flawed, he'd agree it's invalid.
Both cases together show each sage applies their principle universally: intent always transfers (Yochanan) or never transfers (Reish Lakish) between rites — regardless of whether we're talking about idolatry or Temple acts, and regardless of whether the slaughter was correct.

🧐 Rav Sheshet's Challenge: Rabbi Yosei

RavSh
Objection! Rabbi Yosei rules: "intent does not invalidate non-sacred slaughter." That supports Reish Lakish. But Rabbi Yosei also says inside the Temple, intent DOES invalidate. How can he hold both positions?
RavSh
Actually — I'll answer my own question. Rabbi Yosei's "intent doesn't invalidate" refers specifically to a gentile's intent. Only the Jewish slaughterer's intent matters halachically. The gentile's idolatrous thoughts are irrelevant regardless.
The distinction: Rabbi Yosei is answering a different question — whose intent counts? (Only the Jew's.) Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish are asking — when intent matters, does it transfer between rites? These are two separate questions entirely.
