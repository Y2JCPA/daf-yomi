Chullin 39

חולין לט
Intent for idol worship during slaughter: does it invalidate the meat? Rabbi Yochanan vs. Reish Lakish — and whether intent can "jump" between sacrificial rites

🎯 The Core Question

Setup

What if someone slaughters an animal with intent to:

  • Sprinkle its blood as an idol worship rite
  • Burn its fat as an idol worship rite

The slaughter itself was done correctly. But the intent was for idolatry. Does this invalidate the meat? Is benefit from it forbidden?

⚖️ Rabbi Yochanan vs. Reish Lakish

Core Machloket

Rabbi Yochanan

❌ Slaughter is invalid; benefit is forbidden.

Reasoning: intent "transfers" from one sacrificial rite to another. Intent to sprinkle/burn for idols infects the slaughter itself.

Reish Lakish

✅ Slaughter is valid; benefit is permitted.

Reasoning: intent does NOT transfer between rites. Slaughter is defined by the slaughter act alone — intent for a future rite doesn't retroactively invalidate it.

🔗 The "Transfer of Intent" Principle

Key Concept

In Temple sacrifices, intent during one sacrificial rite can disqualify the offering. For example, if a Kohen slaughters a Chatat with intent to perform the blood-sprinkling rite for the wrong purpose — the whole offering is disqualified (pigul).

The question is: does this "transfer of intent" principle apply outside the Temple, in regular slaughter?

Rabbi Yochanan says YES

We derive non-sacred slaughter from Temple slaughter — so transfer of intent applies. Intent for a future idolatrous blood rite infects the slaughter.

Reish Lakish says NO

Outside the Temple, each rite stands alone. Slaughter is judged only by the slaughter act — future intent for blood or fat rites is irrelevant to its validity.

🔄 They Argue This Way Consistently

Consistent Position

The Gemara notes this dispute is not ad hoc — Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish apply the same logic to a Temple case:

If a Kohen slaughters a Chatat properly (for its own sake) but intends to sprinkle its blood for a different offering's sake:

  • Rabbi Yochanan: Invalid — transfer of intent disqualifies even when the slaughter itself was l'shmah
  • Reish Lakish: Valid — slaughter done correctly for its own sake cannot be retroactively disqualified by intent during a later rite

Both sages are internally consistent across Temple and non-Temple contexts.

🤔 Why Both Cases Had to Be Stated

Tzrikhuta

The Gemara explains why the Amoraim needed to state their dispute in both the idol-worship case AND the Temple case:

  • From the idol case alone: perhaps Rabbi Yochanan only holds intent transfers because idolatry is especially severe
  • From the Temple case alone: perhaps Reish Lakish only permits because the slaughter was explicitly done correctly (l'shmah)
  • Together: we see both sages apply their principle universally, not situationally

🧐 Rav Sheshet's Challenge

Objection

Rav Sheshet challenges both positions from the Mishna (from a previous chapter): Rabbi Yosei rules that a gentile's intent for idol worship is irrelevant to non-sacred slaughter — "intent does not invalidate non-sacred slaughter."

This seems to support Reish Lakish — but wait. Rabbi Yosei also says inside the Temple, intent DOES transfer. How can Rabbi Yosei say intent transfers inside but not outside?

Rav Sheshet answers: Rabbi Yosei is making a different point — he's saying the gentile's intent doesn't count (only the Jewish slaughterer's intent matters). It's not about transfer between rites at all.

📌 Summary

Key Takeaways
  • Slaughtering with intent to use blood/fat for idol worship: Rabbi Yochanan = invalid; Reish Lakish = valid
  • The root dispute: does intent "transfer" between sacrificial rites (yes per R' Yochanan; no per Reish Lakish)?
  • Both sages are internally consistent across idol worship and Temple cases
  • Rabbi Yosei's ruling ("intent doesn't invalidate non-sacred slaughter") refers to a gentile's intent — not the Jewish slaughterer's transfer-of-intent question
