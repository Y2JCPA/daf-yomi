🍗🥛 Fowl and Cheese: May They Be Eaten Together?

Beit Shammai
Fowl (עוף) and cheese may be placed on the table together but may not be eaten together — even rabbinically we maintain strict separation at the meal.
Beit Hillel
Fowl and cheese may be both placed on the table AND eaten at the same meal — since the Torah's prohibition only covers an animal nursed by its mother, fowl-milk is only a rabbinic decree and thus more lenient.
Root of the Dispute Whether fowl + milk is a Torah-level prohibition or merely rabbinic. Beit Shammai treats it with near-Torah stringency; Beit Hillel, knowing it is rabbinic, permits even eating them together.

⏳ Mar Ukva and the Waiting Period Between Meat and Milk

Mar Ukva's humility: "I am like chometz ben yayin — vinegar, son of wine — compared to my father in this matter."
↓
Mar Ukva's own practice: After eating meat, he would not eat milk at the same meal — but at the next meal he was permitted to eat dairy.
↓
His father's stricter practice: After eating meat, his father would not eat milk until the following day — a near full-day separation ensuring complete digestion.
↓
Later codification: The widespread custom to wait six hours between meat and milk is rooted in approximating the stricter standard of full digestion before eating dairy.

🍽️ Beit Shammai vs. Beit Hillel: Fowl and Cheese at the Table

Beit Shammai 🔴

  • Fowl and cheese may be placed on the same table
  • May NOT be eaten together at the same meal
  • Treats the rabbinic decree with near-Torah stringency
  • Maintains physical separation as an extra safeguard

Beit Hillel 🔵

  • Fowl and cheese may be placed on the same table
  • MAY be eaten together at the same meal
  • Fowl-milk is rabbinic only — leniency is appropriate
  • No appearance concern since the rabbinic basis is well-known

🧠 Why Does the Torah Repeat the Prohibition Three Times?

לֹא תְבַשֵּׁל גְּדִי בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ — שמות כג:יט | שמות לד:כו | דברים יד:כא
Hermeneutic Principle — אין מקרא יוצא מידי פשוטו The Gemara asks: if the Torah wrote "Do not cook a kid in its mother's milk" identically three times (Shemot 23:19, 34:26; Devarim 14:21), each repetition must teach something new. No word of Torah is superfluous. The Sages derive three distinct, independent prohibitions — cooking, eating, and benefit — each carrying its own malkot (lashes) if violated intentionally.
