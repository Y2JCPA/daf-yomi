🥩 Flow of the Matanot Kehuna

A non-sacred animal (chulin) is slaughtered by an Israelite or obligated party
↓
Separate the zeroa — right foreleg, from joint to shoulder blade
↓
Separate the lechayim — both cheeks with the tongue included
↓
Separate the keiva — the maw (fourth stomach) with its surrounding fat
↓
All three given raw to a kohen of the owner's choosing — eaten anywhere, in any state of purity

📜 Torah Source — Devarim 18:3 The entire obligation of the three gifts flows from a single verse: 'This shall be the priests' due from the people, from those who offer a slaughter-offering — whether ox or sheep — they shall give to the priest the foreleg, the cheeks, and the maw.' The Gemara analyzes each term to define scope, quantity, and which animals are included.

⚖️ Can the Kohen Waive or Sell His Gift?

Rav
A kohen may waive his right to the matanot before they reach his possession — just as any beneficiary may disclaim a gift before acquisition (zechiya) takes effect.
Shmuel
The verse calls them mishpat — a legal obligation, not a voluntary gift. The owner is bound to give; the kohen cannot nullify the owner's duty by waiving his share.
Rava
Once the animal is slaughtered, the gifts are fixed in the kohen's domain. But the owner retains the right to choose which kohen receives them — this is the one discretionary element.
Practical Ruling The owner may give the three gifts to any kohen he wishes — he is not bound to the local kohen or the one present at the slaughter. This choice is the owner's prerogative.

🔄 Matanot Kehuna vs. Terumah — Key Distinctions

Matanot Kehuna

  • Source: animal slaughter (Devarim 18:3)
  • Given raw — kohen prepares as desired
  • Eaten anywhere, no purity required
  • Kohen and entire household may eat
  • Applies outside Israel (Amoraim dispute extent)
  • Owner chooses which kohen receives them

Terumah Gedolah

  • Source: produce — grain, wine, oil (Bamidbar 18)
  • May be given processed or raw
  • Must be eaten in ritual purity (tahara)
  • Kohen and household — not guests
  • Biblical obligation only in Israel
  • No Torah-specified minimum quantity
