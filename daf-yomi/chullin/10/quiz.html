Question 1 of 5
Which three liquids are forbidden if left uncovered (due to snake danger)?
The three liquids specifically forbidden when left uncovered are water, wine, and milk. The concern is that a snake may have drunk from them and deposited venom. The time threshold is how long it takes a snake to emerge from a nearby hiding place, drink, and return.
Question 2 of 5
According to the final halacha, when is slaughter with a later-found notched knife considered valid?
The halacha follows: when bones were cut with the knife after slaughter, we attribute the notch to the bone-cutting (Rav Chisda — valid). When no bones were cut, there is no explanation for when the notch appeared, so the slaughter is invalid (Rav Huna's stringent view applies).
Question 3 of 5
What practical lesson does Rav Yosef's incident with 13 animals teach?
When Rav Yosef discovered a notched knife after slaughtering 13 animals, he declared all 13 treifot. The lesson: examine the knife between every single slaughter, so that at worst one animal is invalidated, not many. Rav Kahana made this his regular practice.
Question 4 of 5
What does the leprous house narrative prove about chazakah?
The priest exits the leprous house and locks it — at that moment, he can no longer see the leprous mark. Yet the Torah declares everything inside tamei. This proves we rely on chazakah: the mark's status (as it was when last observed) is presumed to continue, even without ongoing verification.
Question 5 of 5
Why does the Gemara reject the suggestion that the priest exits the leprous house backward?
The Gemara's first refutation: "exiting backward is not called exiting" — the verse requires a genuine exit. The second: the verse says he exits "to the door of the house" and then locks it — implying normal forward movement, then turning to lock. The High Priest's backward exit from the Holy of Holies (cited as counter-evidence) is rejected because that is a special exception.
