5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
What are the "seven liquids" relevant to susceptibility to ritual impurity?
The seven liquids are: water, dew, oil, wine, milk, blood, and honey — derived from Leviticus 11:34,38. Only food that first contacts one of these becomes susceptible to ritual impurity. Other liquids don't trigger susceptibility.
Question 2 of 5
The school of Rabbi Yishmael excludes which type of blood from rendering food susceptible to impurity?
The school of Rabbi Yishmael derives from "drinks the blood of carcasses" that only blood flowing naturally qualifies. Blood expelled in an arterial surge (kishush) is excluded — it is categorically different from regular flowing blood and does not render food susceptible to impurity.
Question 3 of 5
In the case of blood splashing onto a teruma gourd during slaughter, what is Rabbi Chiyya's position?
Rabbi Chiyya's ruling is "abeyance" — he considers it a genuine halakhic uncertainty. Rav Ashi later interprets this as a permanent, unresolvable teku: Rabbi Chiyya believes there is no clear answer and the uncertainty remains forever.
Question 4 of 5
What is the root of the dispute between Rabbi (Yehuda HaNasi) and Reish Lakish regarding slaughter blood?
The dispute is fundamentally about when slaughter "happens." Rabbi holds it takes effect throughout the act, so the blood qualifies. Reish Lakish holds slaughter is only defined at its conclusion — meaning blood during the act is just "wound blood," not slaughter blood, and doesn't render food susceptible.
Question 5 of 5
Rav Ashi interprets Rabbi Chiyya's ruling of "abeyance" (teiku) as what?
Rav Ashi says Rabbi Chiyya's "abeyance" is permanent — forever. This is the classic concept of teku: some halakhic questions are genuinely unresolvable and remain suspended without resolution. Rabbi Chiyya identified the status of slaughter blood as one such case.
