Chullin 36

חולין לו
Blood of slaughter: does it render food susceptible to impurity? A three-way dispute and the concept of an unresolvable halakhic uncertainty

🌊 Background: The "Seven Liquids" Rule

Foundation

Food only becomes susceptible to ritual impurity if it first comes into contact with one of seven liquids: water, dew, oil, wine, milk, blood, or honey. This is derived from Leviticus 11:34,38.

Key question for this daf: Does blood from slaughter count as one of these liquids — specifically, does it "render food susceptible" to impurity?

📜 The School of Rabbi Yishmael's Limiting Principle

Baraita

The verse says "drinks the blood of carcasses" — the school of Rabbi Yishmael derives from here that only blood that flows freely from a carcass renders food susceptible. Blood expelled in a sudden arterial surge (kishush) does not render food susceptible.

Why not? Arterial spurting is different in character — it's more like a wound ejection than a normal liquid.

⚖️ The Three-Way Dispute: Blood Splashed on Teruma

Core Machloket

Case: Someone slaughters an animal and blood splashes onto a gourd of teruma. Does the blood render the gourd susceptible to impurity?

Rabbi (Yehuda HaNasi)

✅ The gourd IS rendered susceptible — slaughter blood counts as one of the seven liquids

Rabbi Chiyya

❓ Leaves it in abeyance — uncertain whether this blood qualifies

Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish

❌ Blood of slaughter does NOT render food susceptible — slaughter is only defined at its conclusion

🔑 Rav Pappa's Resolution

Key Insight

Rav Pappa reconciles the opinions by distinguishing two scenarios:

  • Blood present continuously from start to end of slaughter: Even Rabbi Chiyya agrees this is a dispute (Rabbi vs. Reish Lakish)
  • Blood wiped off before slaughter concludes: Rabbi Chiyya holds it in abeyance because it might be "wound blood" — not qualifying as slaughter blood at all

The key legal question is: when does slaughter "happen" — throughout the act, or only at its conclusion?

🏁 When Does Slaughter "Occur"?

Machloket Principles

Rabbi (implied)

Slaughter takes effect throughout the act — blood throughout the process counts as slaughter blood

Rabbi Chiyya

Slaughter is only defined at its conclusion — earlier blood is "wound blood" with uncertain status

Reish Lakish

Slaughter is defined only at conclusion — so no blood before that moment counts as slaughter blood, and thus doesn't render food susceptible

♾️ Rav Ashi: An Eternal Uncertainty

Notable Ruling

Rav Ashi interprets Rabbi Chiyya's ruling of "abeyance" as a permanent, unresolvable uncertainty — not a placeholder for future resolution.

"One places the matter in abeyance — forever."

This is the concept of teku — some questions in halacha are genuinely unresolvable and remain suspended indefinitely. Rabbi Chiyya identified this as one of them.

🌾 Bonus: Can Dry Meal Offering Portions Become Impure?

Reish Lakish's Dilemma

The daf ends with a related dilemma raised by Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish: can a dry, un-oiled portion of a mincha (meal offering) become susceptible to impurity?

Rabbi Elazar responds with a verse: food must first contact water to become susceptible. The question is whether the sanctity of the sacrifice substitutes for the liquid requirement.

The Gemara leaves this as an open discussion — the principle that "sanctity doesn't substitute for liquid contact" is raised, with textual proofs examined.

📌 Summary

Key Takeaways
  • Blood of slaughter: Rabbi says it renders food susceptible; Reish Lakish says it doesn't; Rabbi Chiyya holds it in eternal abeyance
  • The dispute hinges on when slaughter takes legal effect (throughout vs. only at conclusion)
  • Arterial surge blood (kishush) is excluded from the seven liquids even by those who count slaughter blood
  • Rav Ashi: Rabbi Chiyya's "abeyance" is permanent — a true teku
  • Dry meal offering susceptibility: another open question about whether sanctity substitutes for liquid contact
