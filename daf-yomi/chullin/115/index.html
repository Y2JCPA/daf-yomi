📜 What Do the Three Repetitions Teach?

R' Yishmael
All three appearances of lo tevashel gedi ba'chalav imo prohibit cooking — one verse for each of the three festival pilgrimage occasions mentioned in context.
R' Akiva
Each verse teaches a separate prohibition: (1) cooking, (2) eating, (3) deriving benefit — three complete and independent bans.
Gemara
Halakha follows R' Akiva. One who both cooks and eats basar b'chalav is liable to two separate sets of lashes.
Biblical Source (×3) לֹא תְבַשֵּׁל גְּדִי בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ — Shemot 23:19, Shemot 34:26, Devarim 14:21

🐄 Scope of the Basar B'Chalav Prohibition

Torah Prohibition

  • Domestic animals only (בהמה)
  • Cattle, sheep, goats — meat cooked in milk
  • Cook, eat, and benefit all forbidden
  • Lashes (מלקות) for each violation

Rabbinic Prohibition

  • Poultry (עוף) with milk — gezeira
  • Wild animals (חיה) per some opinions
  • Protective fence around Torah law
  • Rabbinic lashes (מכת מרדות) only

Not Prohibited

  • Fish (דגים) with milk — no issur
  • Locusts with milk
  • Non-kosher animals (already separately forbidden)
  • Pareve foods with dairy

🦅 Why Are Birds Excluded from Biblical Prohibition?

Rava
The verse says gedi ba'chalav imo — 'in its mother's milk.' A bird does not nurse its young with milk, so the biblical prohibition cannot apply.
Gemara
Nevertheless, the Rabbis decreed poultry with milk forbidden as a gezeira — lest one confuse it with biblically forbidden meat-and-milk combinations.
Question
Why no decree for fish? Fish looks so unlike meat that there is no risk of confusion with the biblical prohibition — so no rabbinic fence was erected.
Key Distinction Poultry: forbidden rabbinically (marat ayin — appearance). Fish: entirely permitted. Degree of visual similarity to the biblical case determines whether a rabbinic decree applies.

🥩 The Nevelah Juxtaposition in Devarim 14:21

לֹא תֹאכְלוּ כָל נְבֵלָה... לֹא תְבַשֵּׁל גְּדִי בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ (דברים יד:כא)
Contextual Derasha The third verse of basar b'chalav appears immediately after lo tochlu kol nevelah (do not eat any carcass). The proximity is deliberate: just as nevelah is forbidden to eat but permitted for benefit (you may sell it to a non-Jew), basar b'chalav is stricter — even deriving benefit is prohibited. The Torah places them side by side to highlight this contrast.
