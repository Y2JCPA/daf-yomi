💬 How Much Water Is Required?

Rav
A single revi'it (quarter-log) of water is sufficient for washing both hands together in one pouring.
Shmuel
One must use a revi'it per hand — each hand individually requires its own quarter-log to constitute a valid washing.
Rav Nachman
The halacha follows Rav: a single revi'it poured over both hands fulfills the obligation, though adding more water is praiseworthy.
Practical Ruling One revi'it (~86ml) suffices for both hands. Rav Pappa reports being stringent to use more water even in the field as a matter of personal piety.

🏺 Valid vs. Invalid Vessels for Netilat Yadayim

✅ Valid Vessels

  • Whole, intact vessels of any material (wood, metal, stone, earthenware)
  • Vessels with a proper receptacle (beit kibul) capable of holding water
  • Minimum capacity of at least a revi'it
  • Portable containers — not attached to the ground

❌ Invalid Vessels

  • Broken vessels no longer capable of holding water
  • The ground itself — even a natural hollow or pit
  • Vessels permanently attached or affixed to the ground
  • Vessels lacking a proper receptacle (e.g., a flat board)

🤲 Proper Procedure for Netilat Yadayim

Step 1: Fill a valid, intact vessel with at least a revi'it of water
↓
Step 2: Hold the vessel in the left hand; begin washing the right hand first (or dominant hand)
↓
Step 3: Pour water over the right hand, ensuring it covers the entire hand up to the wrist
↓
Step 4: Transfer the vessel to the washed hand; pour over the left hand in the same manner
↓
Step 5: Rub hands together and recite the blessing: al netilat yadayim
↓
Step 6: Dry hands thoroughly before reciting HaMotzi — drying is part of the mitzvah

🧂 Mayim Acharonim — The Forgotten Obligation

מַיִם אַחֲרוֹנִים חוֹבָה — מִפְּנֵי מֶלַח סְדוֹמִית שֶׁמְּסַמֵּא אֶת הָעֵינַיִם
Gemara Ruling The Gemara establishes that mayim acharonim is obligatory (chova), not merely optional. The reason: Melach Sodomit — a fine, caustic salt absorbed into food that can blind the eyes if touched afterward. Rav Pappa declares: "I am careful about mayim acharonim even when traveling in the field." Though Sodomite salt is rare today, authorities maintain the obligation as a binding rabbinic enactment.
