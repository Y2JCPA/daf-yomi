QUESTION 1 OF 5
Which of these is NOT one of the four signs of a Shoteh listed in the Mishna?
A. Goes out alone at night
B. Sleeps in a cemetery
C. Speaks to animals as if they understand
D. Tears their clothing
Correct: C. Speaking to animals is NOT one of the four signs. The four signs of a Shoteh are: (1) going out alone at night, (2) sleeping in a cemetery, (3) tearing their clothing, and (4) losing what is given to them. All four indicate a fundamental lack of awareness or rational behavior.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
A knife falls from a shelf and passes through an animal's throat, making a perfect cut through both simanim. Is this valid shechitah?
A. Yes — the physical cut was perfect
B. No — shechitah requires human intent (kavvanah); an accidental cut is invalid
C. Only if a Kohen witnessed it
D. Yes, but only b'dieved
Correct: B. Shechitah requires "koach gavra" — human force and intent. Even if the physical cut was technically perfect, the absence of human intent renders the animal a neveilah. A falling knife, a machine, or any accidental cut cannot produce kosher meat.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the halachic principle of "Rov" (majority) as applied to shechitah?
A. Most of the knife must pass through the throat for valid shechitah
B. We follow the majority — since most slaughterers are competent, we assume unknown Jewish slaughterers acted properly
C. The majority of an animal's blood must be drained for it to be kosher
D. Most animals in the marketplace are already checked for treifot
Correct: B. "Rov" (majority) is a foundational halachic principle. Applied here: since most people who slaughter know how to do it properly, we can presume that meat slaughtered by an unknown Jewish person was done correctly. This is a presumption, not a certainty — if we know someone is incompetent, Rov doesn't help.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
The Mishna's "Cheresh" (who should not slaughter) refers specifically to whom?
A. Anyone with hearing loss
B. A deaf-MUTE — someone who can neither hear nor speak
C. Someone who is blind and deaf
D. Anyone who doesn't speak Hebrew
Correct: B. "Cheresh" in the Mishna specifically means a deaf-mute. A person who is deaf but CAN speak, or mute but CAN hear, has full legal competence and may slaughter. The deaf-mute's exclusion is based on their reduced ability to learn, communicate, and maintain awareness during the act.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
If a minor (Katan) slaughtered an animal with an adult watching the entire process, is the meat kosher?
A. No — a minor can never produce kosher meat
B. Yes — valid b'dieved (after the fact), since the adult supervised
C. Only if the minor is over age 10
D. Only if the minor is the child of a Kohen
Correct: B. A minor should NOT slaughter l'chatchila (ideally). However, if they DID slaughter with an adult supervising the entire process, the shechitah is valid b'dieved. The adult's presence ensures the technical requirements were met. This is one of the key l'chatchila/b'dieved distinctions in Chapter 1.
