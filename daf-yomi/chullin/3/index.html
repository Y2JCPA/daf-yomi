🤯 Defining the "Shoteh" — Who Is Incompetent?

The Mishna establishes that a Shoteh may not slaughter. But who qualifies as a Shoteh?
Sign of a ShotehWhat It Indicates
🌙 Goes out alone at nightDisregard for personal safety
🪦 Sleeps in a cemeteryAbnormal behavior / unawareness
👕 Tears their clothingDestructive behavior without reason
🗑️ Loses what is given to themCannot maintain property / responsibility
Q
Must ALL four signs be present? Or just one?
RY
R' Yochanan: Even ONE of these behaviors qualifies a person as a Shoteh — if it is clearly done without rational reason.
RH
R' Huna: Only if ALL four are present together. A single odd behavior doesn't make someone legally incompetent.

🔪 The Lost Knife — A Fascinating Case

Q
Someone threw a knife at a wall, it passed through an animal's throat on the way, and the animal died from the cut. Is this valid shechitah?
A
❌ NO! Shechitah requires INTENT (kavvanah). The person didn't intend to slaughter — the knife accidentally passed through the animal's throat. Without intent to perform the act, there is no shechitah.
Q
What if someone dropped a knife and it fell on an animal's throat?
A
Also ❌ INVALID. Same reason: the person didn't intend to slaughter. Even if the cut was technically perfect, the lack of human intent renders it a neveilah.
Key principle: Shechitah requires koach gavra (human force + intent). A machine, a falling knife, or an accidental cut cannot produce kosher meat — even if the physical action was identical to proper shechitah.

📜 "Rov" — The Majority Principle

A foundational halachic concept that arises here for the first time in this masechet:

What Is "Rov"?

  • 📊 "Rov" = "majority" or "most"
  • ⚖️ Halachic principle: follow the majority
  • 🔪 Applied here: MOST people who slaughter know how to do it properly
  • ✅ Therefore: if we find meat slaughtered by an unknown Jew, we assume it was done correctly
  • 📖 Torah source: Shemot 23:2 (follow the majority)

Applied to Shechitah

  • 🔪 Most slaughterers are competent → we trust unknown slaughterers
  • 🐄 Most animals are healthy → we don't suspect every animal is treif
  • 👤 Most Jews believe in Torah → we trust a Jew's shechitah by default
  • ⚠️ But: if we KNOW someone is incompetent → Rov doesn't help
  • 💡 Rov is a presumption, not a certainty

⏰ L'Chatchila vs. B'Dieved — A Key Framework

L'Chatchila (Ideally / From the Start)
What you SHOULD do before performing shechitah
↓
✅ Use a sharp, properly checked knife
✅ Have an adult, competent Jewish person slaughter
✅ Slaughter in daylight or with good visibility
✅ Check the simanim (trachea/esophagus) afterward
↓
B'Dieved (After the Fact)
What we rule if the ideal wasn't followed
↓
✅ Minor slaughtered WITH supervision → valid
✅ Slaughtered at night → valid
✅ Blind person slaughtered → valid
❌ Non-Jew slaughtered → NEVER valid, even b'dieved

🔇 The Deaf Person (Cheresh) — Why Excluded?

Important nuance: The Mishna's "Cheresh" means a deaf-MUTE — someone who can neither hear nor speak. A person who is deaf but CAN speak, or who speaks but cannot hear, has a different status.
CategoryCan They Slaughter?Reason
Deaf-mute (Cheresh)❌ No (l'chatchila)Cannot learn/communicate instructions; reduced awareness
Deaf but speaks✅ YesCan learn and communicate; full mental competence
Mute but hears✅ YesCan hear instructions; full mental awareness
Temporarily deaf✅ YesHas prior knowledge; competent
