⚔️ Does Ben Peku'a Require Its Own Shechita?

R' Meir
A ben peku'a — a live fetus extracted from a properly slaughtered mother — requires NO further shechita to be eaten. The mother's slaughter covers it entirely.
R' Shimon
Agreed that no separate shechita is needed. Moreover, even if the ben peku'a later dies on its own, it cannot become neveilah — it is already in a state of permitted-ness from birth.
Gemara
The operative principle: שחיטת אמו מטהרתו — the mother's slaughter purifies it. Yet secondary laws (kisuy, eiver min hachai) remain disputed for this unique category.
Key Term בן פקועה (Ben Peku'a) — lit. 'the extracted one'; a live, full-term fetus removed from a ritually slaughtered animal. It occupies a unique halachic category unlike any normal-born animal.

📜 What Is Ben Peku'a?

בהמה שנשחטה ונמצא בה עובר — שחיטת אמו מטהרתו (חולין עה)
Foundation When a pregnant animal undergoes valid shechita and a live fetus is found inside, that fetus is a בן פקועה. The Torah's slaughter requirement is considered fulfilled through the mother. But this creates cascading questions: Can it become neveilah? Must its blood be covered? What of its limbs while alive?

⚖️ Ben Peku'a vs. Normally Born Animal

Ben Peku'a

  • No shechita required to eat
  • Cannot become neveilah upon death
  • Kisuy hadam: disputed (some exempt it)
  • Eiver min hachai from it: disputed
  • Unique pariah category — sui generis

Normally Born Animal

  • Requires full shechita to be permitted
  • Dies without shechita → neveilah
  • Kisuy hadam required if it is a chaya
  • Eiver min hachai prohibition fully applies
  • Standard halachic category

🦴 Eiver Min HaChai — Limb from Living Ben Peku'a

Question: A limb is severed from a living ben peku'a. Does eiver min hachai (limb from a living creature) apply?
↓
R' Yochanan: The prohibition of eiver min hachai still applies. The ben peku'a's body was never individually 'slaughtered' — the mother's shechita covered the whole but not severed parts.
↓
Resh Lakish: The mother's shechita creates a blanket dispensation for the entire fetus and all it contains — the limb is therefore permitted.
↓
Implication: If eiver min hachai applies, the limb is forbidden even though eating the whole ben peku'a is permitted — a novel asymmetry.
↓
Practical ruling: A limb severed from a living ben peku'a requires its own shechita or is otherwise prohibited. The whole-body heter does not extend to actively severed parts.
