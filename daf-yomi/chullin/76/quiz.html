Chullin 76 Quiz

QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Mishnah on Chullin 76, what happens if a fetus's foreleg protrudes during a difficult birth and is then retracted before the mother is slaughtered?
A. The limb is fully permitted after the mother's shechita
B. The limb is forbidden because of ever min hachai
C. The limb is only forbidden if it remained outside for more than one minute
D. The limb is forbidden only if the fetus later died
Correct: B — The Mishnah explicitly rules: even though the limb was retracted, it is forbidden as ever min hachai. The prohibition took hold at the moment of protrusion and is not undone by retraction or the mother's slaughter.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What additional prohibition applies specifically to a protruding hindleg that does NOT apply to a protruding foreleg?
A. Prohibition of chelev (forbidden fat)
B. Prohibition of gid hanasheh (sciatic nerve)
C. Prohibition of blood (dam)
D. Prohibition of nevelah (unkosher slaughter)
Correct: B — The gid hanasheh (sciatic nerve) runs through the hindleg. When the hindleg protrudes, both ever min hachai AND the gid hanasheh prohibition apply — giving the hindleg a double prohibition that the foreleg does not have.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to the discussion in Chullin 76, one who eats the gid hanasheh of a protruding hindleg may be liable for how many sets of malkot (lashes)?
A. None — the prohibitions cancel each other out
B. One set — the issurim merge into one
C. Two sets — ever min hachai and gid hanasheh are independent prohibitions
D. Three sets — an additional rabbinic prohibition adds a third count
Correct: C — Because ever min hachai and gid hanasheh are two independent Torah prohibitions, one who eats the gid hanasheh of a protruding hindleg potentially incurs two separate sets of malkot — one for each violation.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Does the valid shechita (slaughter) of the mother animal render the previously-protruded limb permitted for consumption?
A. Yes — shechita always purifies all parts of the animal
B. Yes — but only if the limb was retracted before slaughter
C. No — the ever min hachai prohibition remains despite the mother's shechita
D. Only if the limb is inspected and declared kosher by a posek
Correct: C — Shechita does not remove the ever min hachai status from a limb that protruded. The prohibition is permanent once it takes effect — the limb would require its own separate consideration and cannot be rectified by the mother's slaughter.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does the Mishnah on Chullin 76 specifically discuss the foreleg (yad) rather than the hindleg (regel) as its primary example?
A. Forelegs protrude more commonly during birth than hindlegs
B. The foreleg presents the simpler one-prohibition case; the Gemara then derives the more complex hindleg ruling with its additional gid hanasheh dimension
C. The Torah explicitly mentions forelegs in the context of ever min hachai
D. Hindlegs are anatomically incapable of protruding during birth
Correct: B — The Mishnah teaches the base case using the foreleg (one prohibition: ever min hachai). The Gemara then extends and complicates the ruling by addressing the hindleg, which introduces the additional issue of gid hanasheh and the question of double liability.
