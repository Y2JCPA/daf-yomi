QUESTION 1 OF 5
How many times does 'Do not cook a kid in its mother's milk' appear in the Torah, and what does each occurrence teach?
A. Once — it teaches only the prohibition of cooking meat and milk
B. Twice — one for cooking, one for eating
C. Three times — each teaches one of: cooking, eating, and deriving benefit
D. Four times — one each for cooking, eating, benefit, and nullification
Correct: C — The verse appears three times (Shemot 23:19, 34:26; Devarim 14:21). Were it written only once we would know only one prohibition. Each repetition independently teaches one of the three prohibitions: cooking, eating, and deriving benefit from meat-milk mixtures.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rabbi Akiva, why is cooking poultry with milk only a rabbinic prohibition and not biblical?
A. Because poultry is not a kosher species according to Torah law
B. Because birds do not nurse their young — they have no mother that produces milk
C. Because the Rabbis simply chose not to include it in their ruling
D. Because poultry meat has a different texture and appearance than mammal meat
Correct: B — Rabbi Akiva reasons from the word 'imo' (its mother) — the verse implies a mother that produces milk to nurse her young. Since birds hatch from eggs and their mothers produce no milk, the biblical verse cannot apply to poultry. Poultry-milk is therefore a rabbinic prohibition only.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Which animals are subject to the BIBLICAL prohibition of basar b'chalav?
A. All animals that are kosher, including poultry and wild animals
B. Only goats, since 'gedi' literally means a young goat
C. The three domesticated species: cattle (shor), sheep (keves), and goat (ez)
D. Wild animals (chayyot) only, since they are hunted and therefore more stringently governed
Correct: C — The biblical prohibition applies specifically to the three domesticated species: ox/cattle (shor), sheep/lamb (keves), and goat (ez). Wild animals (chayyot) and poultry are excluded from the biblical prohibition and are forbidden only rabbinically. Fish and locusts are entirely permitted.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the key practical legal difference between violating the biblical basar b'chalav prohibition versus the rabbinic one?
A. There is no practical difference — both carry identical penalties under Talmudic law
B. Biblical violation incurs lashes (malkot); rabbinic violation does not carry lashes
C. Only biblical violations require the offender to bring a korban (sacrifice)
D. Rabbinic violations carry a stricter penalty because they show disrespect for the Sages
Correct: B — One who violates a Torah prohibition — cooking or eating beef, lamb, or goat with milk — is liable for lashes (malkot). One who violates the rabbinic decree (e.g., poultry with milk) does not receive lashes, though the act is still fully forbidden and may carry other consequences.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the status of fish or locusts served or cooked together with milk?
A. Forbidden biblically, as they fall under the general meat-milk prohibition
B. Forbidden rabbinically as a protective fence enacted by the Sages
C. Fully permitted — they are not considered 'basar' subject to the meat-milk prohibition
D. Permitted to cook together but forbidden to eat once combined
Correct: C — Fish (dagim) and locusts (chagavim) are entirely permitted and are not subject to the basar b'chalav prohibition, either biblically or rabbinically. The Mishnah in Chullin explicitly states they may be cooked or served with milk. A later custom adds stringency for fish with meat (not milk), but fish with milk remains permitted.
