QUESTION 1 OF 5
What is the minimum amount of food (shiur) required for it to become susceptible to tumah?
A. A revi'it (quarter-log)
B. A kezayit (olive's worth)
C. A ke-beitzah (egg's volume)
D. Any amount, however small
Correct: B — The Gemara establishes that a kezayit is the minimum shiur for food tumah. Less than a kezayit — even if edible — cannot become tamei.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In the dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish about tziruf (combining foods): who holds that half-kezitin of two different foods DO combine to form a full shiur?
A. Reish Lakish
B. Rav Pappa
C. Rabbi Yochanan
D. Rav Ashi
Correct: C — Rabbi Yochanan holds they combine, deriving from the word כל ('all') in the verse מכל האוכל. Reish Lakish disagrees, holding each food must independently reach its shiur.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is 'hechsher ochlin' (הכשר אוכלין)?
A. A rabbinic decree making all food tamei by default
B. The rendering of food susceptible to tumah through contact with one of seven liquids
C. A priestly blessing that sanctifies food for Temple use
D. The process of salting meat to remove blood
Correct: B — Hechsher means the food was contacted by one of the seven halachic liquids (water, dew, wine, oil, blood, milk, honey) with the owner's desired consent — permanently making it susceptible to tumah.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Does marrow (מוח) inside an intact bone count as 'ochel' (food) for tumah purposes?
A. No — it is protected by the bone and thus not considered exposed food
B. Yes — marrow is edible and counts as food even when enclosed in bone
C. Only if the bone has already been cracked open
D. Only if the marrow amounts to a full kezayit on its own
Correct: B — The Gemara holds that marrow is edible (people eat it by cracking the bone) and therefore qualifies as ochel. The enclosing bone itself is not food, but the marrow inside it is.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to the Gemara, can liquids and solid foods combine (do tziruf) to together meet the shiur for tumah?
A. Yes — all authorities agree liquids and solids combine
B. No — all authorities agree they cannot combine
C. This is disputed; Rabbi Yochanan permits it but the practical halacha follows Reish Lakish that they do not combine
D. Only if the liquid is water or wine, not other halachic liquids
Correct: C — The question of whether liquids and solids combine is debated. While Rabbi Yochanan applies his broad tziruf principle, the majority of Rishonim rule that liquids and solids are fundamentally different categories and do not combine — each must independently meet its own shiur.
