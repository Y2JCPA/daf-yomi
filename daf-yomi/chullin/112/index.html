⚖️ Does Soaking Equal Cooking? The Kavush Dispute

Rav
Kavush k'mevushal — soaking food in a forbidden liquid for 24 hours is halachically equivalent to cooking it. Meat soaked in milk becomes forbidden with full biblical force, just as if it had been cooked in milk.
Shmuel
Soaking is not cooking. The Torah forbids meat cooked in milk — this requires actual heat. Soaking may warrant a rabbinic restriction in some cases, but it cannot produce a biblical basar b'chalav prohibition.
Rav Huna
The salt used to salt chelev is itself forbidden — it draws out the taste of the chelev and becomes saturated with that prohibition. Melach memashech: salt has unique drawing power.
Gemara
The tzir (brine) that flows from salted chelev is likewise forbidden — it carries the absorbed taste of the chelev. Whatever the salt draws out, the resulting liquid carries forward.
Core Principle Salt is a halachically active agent: what it draws out, it absorbs; what it absorbs, it can transmit. This single principle drives the rulings on chelev-salt, tzir, kavush, and salting meat with cheese.

💧 Tzir — The Brine That Carries Prohibition

צִיר הַחֵלֶב אָסוּר — The brine of chelev is forbidden (Chullin 112b)
Halacha Tzir is the liquid that seeps out when meat or forbidden fat is salted. The Gemara rules on 112b that tzir takes on the halachic character of its source:

• Tzir of chelev (forbidden fat) → forbidden like chelev itself
• Tzir of neveilah (non-kosher meat) → forbidden like neveilah
• Tzir of kosher meat → permitted, though the blood-removal function of salting is precisely what generates the brine

The debate on this daf focuses on how much tzir is needed to create a prohibition when it contacts a neutral food, and whether a k'zayit threshold applies.

🧂 Is Salt Used on Chelev Forbidden?

Rav Huna
Salt used to salt chelev is forbidden. The function of salt is to draw out moisture and taste — it draws the chelev's essence into itself. Eating this salt would be equivalent to consuming chelev.
Question
But there is no cooking here, no heat, no liquid medium — can taste truly transfer through dry contact between salt and fat?
Rav Huna
Salt is unique — מֶלַח מְמַשֵּׁךְ. Its drawing power is intrinsic; this is precisely why we salt meat to remove blood. This same force draws the chelev's taste into the salt crystals.
Conclusion
Ruled forbidden. Moreover, if this salt is subsequently used on kosher meat, it can transmit the taste of chelev to that meat — creating a secondary cascade of prohibition.
מֶלַח מְמַשֵּׁךְ The 'drawing power of salt' is the halachic engine of this entire sugya — it explains why salting creates legal equivalence to liquid-based taste transfer, even without heat.

🥩🧀 Meat & Cheese Salted Together — Three Scenarios

Stringent: Direct Contact

  • Meat placed directly on top of cheese (or reversed)
  • Salt draws out meat juices → flow onto the cheese
  • Creates basar b'chalav without any cooking
  • Salt-transfer is sufficient to create the prohibition
  • Ruling: Both the meat and the cheese are forbidden

Permitted: Separate Vessels

  • Meat and cheese salted in completely separate vessels
  • No direct contact; no brine flow between them
  • Each item's salt draws only from its own source
  • No taste transfer between meat and dairy occurs
  • Ruling: Both remain fully permitted

Disputed: Side by Side

  • Meat and cheese placed adjacent without stacking
  • Brine may flow toward the other item depending on slope
  • Depends on proximity, tilt of surface, and liquid volume
  • Rishonim debate whether this requires stringency
  • Practical ruling: treated stringently due to doubt
