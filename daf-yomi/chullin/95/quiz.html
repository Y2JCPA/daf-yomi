QUESTION 1 OF 5
What is the practical halakhic difference between the view that simanim are d'Oraita versus d'Rabbanan?
A. D'Oraita simanim require two witnesses; d'Rabbanan simanim require only one
B. D'Oraita simanim can permit things forbidden by biblical law; d'Rabbanan simanim only address rabbinic-level doubts
C. D'Oraita simanim must be written; d'Rabbanan simanim can be verbal
D. The distinction affects only lost objects, not kashrut questions
Correct: B — The core difference: if simanim are biblically valid (d'Oraita), we can rely on them even where the underlying prohibition is biblical. If only rabbinic (d'Rabbanan), they cannot resolve a biblical-level doubt.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does 'basar she'nistallem min ha'ayin' mean, and why does it create a kashrut problem?
A. Meat that was frozen — its texture changes and kashrus may be affected
B. Meat left out of Jewish supervision — there is concern a non-Jew may have switched it with non-kosher meat
C. Meat separated from its siman — we can no longer identify it as the original piece
D. Meat hidden in a sealed container — we cannot inspect it for infestation
Correct: B — 'Nistallem min ha'ayin' means the meat was out of sight (unattended). When left with a non-Jew, there is concern it may have been exchanged with non-kosher meat — hence it is forbidden.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
The principle 'uman lo mara umnutei' justifies relying on a kosher butcher's product. What is the underlying logic?
A. A professional butcher is required by law to have rabbinic certification, so we trust the legal system
B. A craftsman would not ruin his professional reputation by selling forbidden meat, so his very livelihood constitutes a chazakah
C. The Torah exempts certified craftsmen from standard kashrut suspicion
D. We rely on the majority of butchers being kosher, and this principle reinforces that majority
Correct: B — The reasoning is reputational: a butcher's entire livelihood depends on his kashrut certification. He would not jeopardize it by selling non-kosher meat — this professional stake is treated as a form of chazakah.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
When meat is found in a marketplace where most shops are certified kosher, which principle permits it — and what is its source?
A. Chazakah — the meat retains its prior permitted status
B. Eid echad — a single witness attested to its kashrut
C. Rov — we follow the majority, which is a biblical principle derived from 'אחרי רבים להטות'
D. Siman — there is an identifying mark linking it to a kosher shop
Correct: C — We apply 'rov' (majority): since most shops in the marketplace are certified kosher, we assign the found meat to the majority. Rov is a Torah principle — 'incline after the majority' (Shemot 23:2).
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rav's practice modeled in Chullin 95, what standard should a Torah scholar apply when served meat at a meal?
A. Always refuse meat unless he personally witnessed the shechita
B. Rely solely on the technical rov of the city's kosher shops without further inquiry
C. Assess the household's kashrut chazakah and inquire about uncertain origins — going beyond technical permissibility
D. Accept all meat served by any Jewish host, since every Jew is presumed to observe kashrut
Correct: C — Rav modeled careful inquiry: he relied on a household's established kashrut chazakah but was stringent when origins were uncertain. This sets a higher standard for Torah scholars than the technical minimum.
