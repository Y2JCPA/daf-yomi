⚖️ Does Oto V'et Beno Apply to the Koi?

Tanna Kamma
The koi (כּוֹי) — an animal of uncertain status — is subject to oto v'et beno out of doubt. We cannot slaughter it with its mother or offspring on the same day, lest it truly be a domesticated species.
R' Eliezer
The koi is a unique created entity — it has no definitive parent species in the halachic sense. Since its origins are uncertain, the prohibition of oto v'et beno does not apply at all.
R' Akiva
We must be stringent in all directions: treat the koi as domesticated for some purposes and as wild for others. For oto v'et beno, apply the prohibition — the safek (doubt) demands it.
Rava
The key question is whether safek oto v'et beno carries the penalty of lashes. Even if we rule stringently, a doubtful case cannot incur corporal punishment — only a certain violation can.
Core Tension When a species is genuinely uncertain (neither clearly wild nor domestic), do we apply biblical prohibitions stringently — and if so, with what penalty?

🦌 What is the Koi?

כּוֹי — בְּרִיָּה בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ הִיא, וְלֹא הִכְרִיעוּ בָהּ חֲכָמִים אִם בְּהֵמָה הִיא אִם חַיָּה
Talmudic Definition The koi (כּוֹי) appears throughout the Talmud as an animal that defies classification. It may be a hybrid of a goat and deer, or a rare species that was once known but is now lost. Because it is uncertain whether it belongs to behema (domesticated animal) or chaya (wild animal), the Sages declare: "This is an animal whose status our Rabbis could not determine." Its meat may be eaten, but covering the blood (kisuy ha-dam) is required without a blessing — lest it truly be a chaya.

🐂 Kilayim — Does Crossbreeding Prohibition Apply to Hybrids?

First View
The Torah's prohibition of kilayim b'vehema (crossbreeding animals) applies only when crossing two distinct known species. If a mule (offspring of horse and donkey) mates with another mule, there is no new act of kilayim — the mixed status already exists.
Challenging View
Any act of causing two different-species animals to mate violates kilayim — even if one of them is already a hybrid. The prohibition targets the act of crossbreeding itself, not merely the biological distance between the species.
Abaye
What about a mule bred with a horse? The offspring would be three-quarters horse — the practical question is whether such gradations matter halachically, or if any non-identical species pairing triggers the prohibition.
Rava
The Torah uses the phrase "לֹא תַרְבִּיעַ בְּהֶמְתְּךָ כִּלְאַיִם" — you shall not crossbreed your animal with a different kind. "Kind" (min) is the operative word. If two animals share overlapping origins, the violation is at least a doubt.
Verse לֹא תַרְבִּיעַ בְּהֶמְתְּךָ כִּלְאַיִם — "You shall not crossbreed your animal as mixed kinds" (Vayikra 19:19)

📊 Four Animals & Their Kilayim / Oto V'et Beno Status

🐄 Shor (Ox/Cow)

  • Definite behema (domesticated)
  • Oto v'et beno: fully applies
  • Kilayim: prohibited to breed with other species
  • Lashes for violation: Yes
  • Kisuy ha-dam: Not required

🦌 Tzvi (Deer)

  • Definite chaya (wild animal)
  • Oto v'et beno: does NOT apply
  • Kilayim: prohibited to breed with behema
  • Lashes for kilayim: Yes
  • Kisuy ha-dam: Required with blessing

🦬 Koi

  • Status: uncertain (safek)
  • Oto v'et beno: prohibited rabbinicaly
  • No lashes — safek cannot yield malkot
  • Kilayim: treated stringently
  • Kisuy ha-dam: required without blessing

🐴 Pered (Mule)

  • Offspring of horse × donkey (kilayim itself)
  • Oto v'et beno: debated — parent species were behema
  • Kilayim: mule × mule — uncertain if new violation
  • Cannot reproduce naturally in most cases
  • Represents limits of halachic categories
