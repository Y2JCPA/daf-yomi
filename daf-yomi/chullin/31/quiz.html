Chullin 31 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
How did R' Yona bar Tachlifa know that his arrow cut the bird from the front of the neck?
The physical evidence tells the story: the feathers at the front of the neck were unraveled (displaced) by the arrow's passage. This is a clear, objective indication that the cut came from the front — exactly where shechitah must occur. The Gemara accepts this physical proof as sufficient evidence of proper shechitah direction.
Question 2 of 5
How did R' Yona solve the blood-covering requirement for a bird slaughtered mid-flight?
Before shooting, R' Yona would designate the earth of the entire valley (patka) as the lower covering for the blood. Wherever the blood fell, it would land on earth that had been pre-designated for the mitzvah of covering blood. This creative solution satisfies the Torah requirement while accommodating the unusual circumstances.
Question 3 of 5
If someone slaughters an animal and accidentally decapitates it in one motion, when is the shechitah valid?
The key is the knife length. If the knife extends beyond the neck by enough distance, it proves that the drawing motion (proper shechitah) had already cut through the simanim before the head came off. A short knife raises the concern that the head was torn off by force (derasah) rather than properly cut through smooth drawing.
Question 4 of 5
Can one immerse for purification in the arc of a wave that has not yet landed?
The Gemara distinguishes between a detached wave that has landed (valid — it's gathered water on the ground, like a natural mikvah) and the arcs of waves still in the air (invalid — airborne water is not "gathered"). A mikvah requires water that is settled/collected, not water in transit through the air.
Question 5 of 5
Why does the halacha care about knife length in the decapitation case?
The concern is evidence: if the knife is short and the head came off, we cannot be sure the simanim were cut through proper shechitah (smooth drawing motion) rather than through the brute force of decapitation (which would be derasah — pressing). A long knife proves: the blade traveled through the simanim before reaching the point where force could tear the head off. Length = proof of proper process.
