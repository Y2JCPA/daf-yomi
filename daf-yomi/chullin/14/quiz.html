Chullin 14 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
One who slaughters on Shabbat intentionally — what is the halachic status of the meat?
The Mishna states the slaughter is valid — the technical act was performed correctly, so the animal is not neveilah. However, Rav rules (in the opinion of R' Yehuda) that the meat may not be eaten that day — either because of muktzeh or because the meat is "nolad" (newly created on Shabbat). After Shabbat, the meat may be eaten.
Question 2 of 5
What is the concept of "nolad" and how does it apply to Shabbat slaughter?
Nolad means "newly created" — something that didn't exist in this form before Shabbat. Slaughtered meat is nolad because before Shabbat it was a live animal (not food); it became meat only through the act of slaughter on Shabbat. R' Yehuda (and Rav Ashi's understanding of his position) forbids using such newly created items on Shabbat itself.
Question 3 of 5
Why does R' Yehuda reject the principle of breirah (retroactive designation)?
R' Yehuda holds that an item's status must be determined at the relevant moment — not retroactively redefined by what happens later. So an animal cannot be "retroactively designated as food" just because you later slaughter it. The Rabbis disagree, holding breirah works — the slaughter reveals that the animal was always destined to be food.
Question 4 of 5
According to Rav Dimi of Neharde'a, what is the law when one slaughters on Shabbat for a critically ill person?
Rav Dimi rules that when slaughter is permitted (for pikuach nefesh — to save a life), the resulting meat is permitted to healthy people too. The reason is that this was a permitted act of slaughter — there is no penalty, and the meat is not muktzeh, since the slaughter was halachically sanctioned. This differs from forbidden Shabbat slaughter where a penalty applies.
Question 5 of 5
What broader principle does the Shabbat slaughter discussion illustrate about halachic validity vs. permission?
The daf illustrates a key principle: validity and permission are separate. Shabbat slaughter is valid (the animal is kosher, not neveilah) but the meat may be forbidden to eat. A transgressive act can still "work" halachically. Similarly, eating forbidden food is forbidden — but the eating "happened." These are separate categories that must be analyzed independently.
