🕍 The Paradox of Shabbat Slaughter

The Mishna rules: One who slaughters on Shabbat or Yom Kippur — even though he is liable for the death penalty — the slaughter is technically valid. The animal is not neveilah.
But Rav adds: Although the slaughter is valid, consumption of the meat is forbidden that day — as the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda. The sinner gains no benefit from his transgression.
Why is it valid halachically if it's done by sin?
↓
The act of shechitah fulfills the technical requirements — sin and kashrut are independent categories
↓
But you cannot eat it that day — the Sages penalized the sinner (or the meat is muktzeh)

⏰ Breirah — Retroactive Designation

The concept: Can something be retroactively defined as having been "designated" from an earlier point? R' Yehuda says NO — designation must be made explicitly before Shabbat. R' Shimon says YES — breirah works.

R' Yehuda — No Breirah

  • 📋 Must designate the animal before Shabbat begins
  • 🐄 An undesignated animal is muktzeh — set aside from use
  • 🍖 Even if slaughtered (illegally on Shabbat) — cannot eat it that day
  • ⚠️ The meat is "newly created" on Shabbat — not prepared before

Rabbis — Breirah Works

  • 📋 The slaughter itself designates the meat retroactively
  • 🐄 The animal was always "potentially food"
  • 🍖 Forbidden only because of the Shabbat violation, not muktzeh
  • ✅ A different prohibition — not a muktzeh issue

🤒 Slaughtering for the Ill on Shabbat

A critically ill person (choleh she'yesh bo sakana) needs meat on Shabbat
↓
Permitted to slaughter for pikuach nefesh ✅
↓
May a healthy person eat that meat on Shabbat?
↓
Rav Dimi of Neharde'a:
Yes! Healthy person may eat. Slaughter was permitted — no penalty applies.
Rav Pappa:
Sometimes yes, sometimes no — depends whether enough remains for the ill person's needs.

📜 Muktzeh — What Does "Set Aside" Mean?

RH
The reason Shabbat-slaughtered meat is forbidden is muktzeh — the animal was "set aside from use" when Shabbat began because it wasn't designated as food.
Ab
That applies to raw fruit you set aside to become vinegar — but a living animal is always potentially food. How can it be muktzeh?
RA
Rav Ashi's resolution: R' Yehuda's reason is not muktzeh per se — it's that meat is "new" (nolad) on Shabbat, created fresh through the act of slaughter. And newly created items cannot be used on Shabbat.
Nolad (newly created): Like broken pottery or squeezed fruit juice — the item didn't exist in this form before Shabbat. Shabbat-slaughtered meat is "new" in the sense that it wasn't food (was a live animal) before the slaughter.

🔄 Why Shabbat Slaughter Is Fundamentally Different

📋

Before Shabbat

A living animal is not food — it's an animal. It cannot be muktzeh as food it hasn't become food yet.

🕍

Shabbat Begins

The animal's status is locked. If not pre-designated, it cannot become food during Shabbat.

🍖

Slaughter on Shabbat

Creates "new" meat — nolad. R' Yehuda forbids it; Rabbis permit eating (though the slaughter itself is still a sin).

The key distinction: Cooked food (already food before Shabbat) was "prepared." Meat from a living animal (slaughtered on Shabbat) is "newly created" — nolad — even according to those who reject muktzeh.
