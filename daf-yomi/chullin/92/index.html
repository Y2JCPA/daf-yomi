📜 Non-Jews and the Thirty Mitzvot

קִבְּלוּ עֲלֵיהֶן שְׁלֹשִׁים מִצְווֹת וְאֵין מְקַיְּימִין אֶלָּא שָׁלֹשׁ
Core Teaching — Ulla Ulla teaches that the nations of the world accepted thirty mitzvot upon themselves, yet in practice they honor only three: they do not write marriage contracts for male unions (כְּתוּבָּה לְזָכָרִים), they do not openly weigh human flesh in the marketplace, and they show respect for the Torah of Israel. The daf then pivots to detailed halachic analysis of gid hanasheh — the sciatic nerve prohibition rooted in Jacob's wrestling with the angel.

📜 Ulla's Teaching on the Thirty Mitzvot

אָמַר עוּלָּא קִבְּלוּ עֲלֵיהֶן שְׁלֹשִׁים מִצְווֹת וְאֵין מְקַיְּימִין אֶלָּא שָׁלֹשׁ
Aggadic Statement — Chullin 92a Ulla declares: The non-Jewish nations accepted thirty mitzvot upon themselves, yet actively observe only three:

1. They do not write a ketubbah (marriage document) for male unions
2. They do not sell human flesh by weight in the public marketplace
3. They show honor and reverence for the Torah of Israel

The gemara emphasizes: even incomplete observance is noted. Non-Jews who keep these minimal standards are acknowledged and praised by the tradition.

🤝 The Three Laws Non-Jews Actually Keep

No Male Marriage Contracts

  • Non-Jews do not write a formal ketubbah for male unions
  • Even among nations permitting such relations, the formal legal-religious document is withheld
  • This reflects a retained sense of natural moral order
  • The ketubbah confers social legitimacy — withholding it is significant

No Selling Human Flesh

  • Human flesh is not openly weighed and sold in public markets
  • Despite other moral failures, basic human dignity is maintained
  • This boundary distinguishes civilized society from complete moral collapse
  • The marketplace is the test — public commerce sets communal norms

Honor for Torah

  • Non-Jews acknowledge and respect the Torah of Israel
  • They rise before a Torah scroll even without observing its commands
  • Recognition of divine sanctity is itself praiseworthy
  • This acknowledgment maintains a spiritual connection to revelation

🦵 The Fat of the Gid — What Is Forbidden?

Rav Nachman
Question: The fat (חֵלֶב) that surrounds the gid hanasheh — what is its halachic status? If it falls into a pot of food, does it render the contents forbidden?
Rava
The fat that is located above the hip joint (כְּנֶגֶד הַפֶּרֶק) — it has substance and imparts taste, so it IS forbidden like the gid itself. If it fell in the pot, it may render the contents forbidden.
Rav Nachman
And the fat below the joint — what of that?
Rava
Below the joint, the fat is hardened like wood — it has no real substance or flavor. It does NOT render the pot forbidden if it falls in, because tasteless matter does not transmit prohibition.
Rav Huna
We must also consider: the gid hanasheh itself — does its prohibition extend to both legs of the animal, or only the right hind leg where Jacob was struck?
Practical Ruling The key distinction: fat above the hip joint is substantive → forbidden. Fat below the joint is hardened matter → permitted. This location-based ruling is central to the practical removal of the gid.

🤼 Origin and Scope of Gid HaNasheh

Jacob wrestles the angel (Bereishit 32)
The angel strikes the kaf yerech — the hip socket sinew
↓
Torah command recorded: 'Therefore Israel does not eat the gid hanasheh to this day' (Gen. 32:33)
One of the few mitzvot written in the Torah narrative before Sinai
↓
Which animals?
Domesticated animals (בְּהֵמָה) and wild animals (חַיָּה) — but NOT fowl, which lack a true hip socket
Which leg?
Both hind legs — since the Torah says 'thigh' generally; some restrict to right leg only
Fowl?
Fowl have no hip socket in the same sense — rabbinic ruling exempts them from the biblical prohibition
