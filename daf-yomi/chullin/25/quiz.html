Chullin 25 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
How does an earthenware vessel become ritually impure?
Earthenware vessels have a unique impurity pathway: the interior airspace. The Torah says "into which any of them falls" — the word "into" (tokho) teaches that impurity enters through the hollow interior. Even mustard seeds filling the vessel become impure if an impure item enters the airspace above them. The outside of the vessel does NOT transmit impurity.
Question 2 of 5
Why can't we derive via kal va'chomer that earthenware should ALSO become impure from outside contact?
The verse "every open vessel that has no sealed cover upon it is impure" (Bamidbar 19:15) ties earthenware impurity specifically to its opening/mouth. This blocks the kal va'chomer: even though it seems logical that earthenware (which is MORE susceptible via airspace) should also be susceptible via contact, the Torah's specific limitation to the vessel's opening prevents that inference.
Question 3 of 5
Are bone vessels (e.g., crafted from animal horns) susceptible to ritual impurity?
R' Yishmael b. R' Yochanan ben Beroka derives that bone vessels are susceptible because the Torah lists animal skins as vessel material alongside wood. Since both skin and bone come from animals, vessels crafted from bones (like expensive horn vessels) are also susceptible to impurity.
Question 4 of 5
At what point does a vessel become susceptible to impurity?
A vessel becomes susceptible to impurity once it is complete enough to serve its intended function. If it still needs structural work (base, rim, handle) it's not yet susceptible. But if only decorative finishing remains (smoothing, gems), it IS susceptible because it can already function as a vessel.
Question 5 of 5
Why do bitter and sweet almonds have opposite tithing obligations at different stages of ripeness?
Tithing obligation is tied to edibility. Bitter almonds are edible when small/unripe (people eat them as a snack) but too bitter when fully ripe. Sweet almonds are the opposite — inedible when small but delicious when ripe. Since tithing tracks the point of consumption, their obligations are mirror images of each other.
