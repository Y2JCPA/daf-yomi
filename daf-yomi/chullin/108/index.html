🔄 The Nat Bar Nat Dispute

Rav
Nat bar nat d'hetera — a secondary flavor originating from a permitted source — is entirely permitted, even for a nazir who must avoid all grape products.
Questioner
What about nat bar nat d'issura? If a pot absorbed forbidden food, then cooked permitted food — does that secondary flavor still carry the original prohibition?
Rav Yehuda
This is the crux. The flavor has traveled through two stages — pot absorbed from issur, then food absorbed from pot. Is the forbidden character diminished by this chain?
Rava
D'hetera is clearly permitted. But nat bar nat d'issura retains its forbidden character — the nature of the source, not merely the distance of transmission, determines the law.
Core Principle נותן טעם בר נותן טעם — 'A flavor that is itself a secondary flavor': when does the chain of transmission reduce its legal significance?

🐟 Fish Cooked in a Meat Pot — May It Be Eaten with Milk?

The Case
Fish was cooked in a meat pot ben yomo (used for meat the same day). The fish absorbed meat flavor. May one now eat this fish together with milk?
Lenient View
Fish is not meat — it was never prohibited with milk. The absorbed flavor is already nat bar nat (once removed). The fish itself remains permitted; the nat bar nat of hetera-type absorption is insignificant.
Stringent View
Eating meat-flavored fish with milk effectively creates a meat-milk combination. Even though it is nat bar nat, the combination produces a maris ayin (appearance of prohibition) and Rabbinically it is forbidden.
Rava
The ruling: a ben yomo meat pot gives the fish strong enough flavor that eating it with milk is Rabbinically forbidden. An eino ben yomo pot transmits weakened, deteriorated flavor — greater leniency may apply.
Practical Ruling Fish cooked in a meat pot (ben yomo) should not be eaten with milk — Rabbinically forbidden due to absorbed meat flavor, even though fish itself is not classified as meat

📅 Ben Yomo vs. Eino Ben Yomo

בן יומו — Same Day

  • Pot used for forbidden/meat food within the past 24 hours
  • Absorbed flavor is still 'fresh' and potent (לא נפגם)
  • Transmits full, effective forbidden taste to new food
  • Treated stringently — nearly as if food itself is present
  • Result: nat bar nat may still render food forbidden

אינו בן יומו — Not Same Day

  • Pot last used for forbidden food more than 24 hours ago
  • Absorbed flavor has 'deteriorated' and turned bitter (נפגם)
  • Transmits only a weakened, unpleasant edge of flavor
  • Such nifgam flavor typically does not forbid the food
  • Result: nat bar nat in this case is generally permitted

📜 The Nazir as Proof — Rav's Source Case

נותן טעם בר נותן טעם דהיתירא — שרי, ואפילו לנזיר (חולין קח)
נזיר — Foundational Proof Text Rav Yehuda cited in the name of Rav: nat bar nat d'hetera is permitted even for a nazir.

A nazir is forbidden from all grape products. If wine was absorbed into a vessel and then water was stored in it, the nazir may drink that water — the secondary grape flavor is legally insignificant.

This case establishes the foundational principle: not every chain of flavor transmission carries full legal weight. The critical variables are (1) whether the original source was hetera or issura, and (2) the freshness of the vessel's absorption.
