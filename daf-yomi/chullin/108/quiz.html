QUESTION 1 OF 5
What does 'nat bar nat' (נותן טעם בר נותן טעם) literally mean, and why is it legally significant?
A. A flavor that is itself only a secondary absorbed flavor — legally distinct from direct flavor transfer
B. The prohibition against combining meat and milk in the same pot
C. A vessel that has never been used for any forbidden food
D. A food nullified within a mixture of 60 parts permitted food
Correct: A — Nat bar nat means 'flavor-giver of a flavor-giver' — the flavor emitted by something that itself only absorbed flavor, not the original substance. This secondary status raises the question of whether ta'am k'ikar still applies fully.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rav, what is the law regarding nat bar nat d'hetera (secondary flavor from a permitted source)?
A. Forbidden, because any absorbed flavor fully retains its legal nature regardless of source
B. Permitted — even for a nazir who must avoid all grape products
C. Permitted for Israelites but still forbidden for kohanim and nezirin
D. Forbidden Rabbinically but permitted by Torah law
Correct: B — Rav rules that nat bar nat d'hetera is entirely permitted, demonstrated by the nazir case: wine absorbed in a vessel, then water stored in that vessel — the nazir may drink the water. Secondary permitted flavor has no legal force.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
If fish is cooked in a ben yomo meat pot and one wishes to eat the fish with milk, what is the ruling?
A. Fully permitted — fish is categorically never considered meat under any circumstances
B. Permitted by Torah law but forbidden Rabbinically due to absorbed meat flavor
C. Forbidden by Torah law since the pot directly gives full meat substance
D. Permitted only if the fish weighed more than 60 times the pot's absorbed flavor
Correct: B — Fish is not meat and the absorbed flavor is nat bar nat, so Torah law does not prohibit it. However, Rabbis forbade eating meat-pot fish with milk due to the absorbed meat flavor — a Rabbinic extension to prevent appearances of prohibition (maris ayin).
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What makes a vessel 'ben yomo' and why does this status matter for halacha?
A. The vessel was immersed in a mikveh that same day, making its absorbed flavors more significant
B. The vessel was used for the relevant food within the past 24 hours, giving it potent, undeteriorated absorbed flavor
C. The vessel is brand new and has never previously absorbed any flavor at all
D. The vessel was inspected by a rabbi on that day and certified as having absorbed taste
Correct: B — Ben yomo means the vessel was used within the last 24 hours. The absorbed flavor is still fresh and potent (lo nifgam). After 24 hours (eino ben yomo) the flavor deteriorates and turns bitter — such nifgam flavor does not render other foods forbidden.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
The principle 'ta'am k'ikar' (טעם כעיקר) underlies the entire nat bar nat discussion. What does it mean in practice?
A. Only the physical substance of forbidden food is prohibited — flavor alone is never legally significant
B. Absorbed flavor renders food forbidden just as if the forbidden substance itself were physically present
C. Flavor can only be transmitted once before it loses all legal significance entirely
D. The majority of a food always overrides any minority absorbed flavor
Correct: B — Ta'am k'ikar means the absorbed flavor of forbidden food is legally treated as the substance itself. This is why a pot absorbing issur matters. The nat bar nat debate asks: does this principle apply even to flavor absorbed at a second remove?
