⚖️ Is Ta'am k'Ikar Biblical or Rabbinic?

Rav
Ta'am k'ikar d'oraita — taste is equivalent to substance by Torah law. If forbidden flavor is absorbed into food, that food is biblically forbidden, not merely rabbinically.
Levi
Ta'am k'ikar is only d'rabbanan — the Torah forbids the substance itself, not its absorbed flavor. Flavor transfer is a rabbinic safeguard.
Gemara
The practical difference: if food absorbs forbidden flavor but no actual substance remains, Rav deems it biblically forbidden while Levi deems it only rabbinically so.
Accepted Ruling Halacha follows that ta'am k'ikar is d'oraita — absorbed forbidden flavor creates a biblical prohibition, making the 1/60 nullification threshold a Torah-level requirement.

🔄 Nat Bar Nat — The Chain of Flavor

Primary absorption: Forbidden food (chelev, treif) cooks in a pot → pot walls absorb primary forbidden flavor (nat rishon)
↓
Pot retains flavor: Even after the forbidden food is removed, the walls hold the absorbed taste
↓
Secondary absorption: Permitted food is cooked in same pot → absorbs secondary flavor from walls (nat bar nat)
↓
Critical distinction: If the pot is ben yomo, this secondary flavor may still forbid; if eino ben yomo, the degraded flavor is noten ta'am lifgam
↓
Nat bar nat d'heteira: When the original absorbed flavor was itself from a permitted source, the secondary transfer is always permitted — no nullification required

🕐 Ben Yomo vs. Eino Ben Yomo

Ben Yomo

  • Pot used within the last 24 hours
  • Absorbed flavor is still 'sharp' (חריף)
  • Flavor transfer is halachically significant
  • Food cooked in it may become forbidden
  • Requires 60x ratio to nullify the pot's contribution
  • Treated as if still in direct contact with forbidden food

Eino Ben Yomo

  • Pot NOT used for more than 24 hours
  • Absorbed flavor has degraded and spoiled
  • Classified as noten ta'am lifgam (spoiled flavor)
  • Spoiled flavor does not render food forbidden
  • No 60x ratio required — permitted automatically
  • A leniency even for initially forbidden absorbed flavor

🐟 The Fish & Meat — Classic Nat Bar Nat Case

נטל בר נטל דהיתירא — שרי | Secondary transfer of permitted flavor is permitted
Rav's Ruling (Chullin 97b) Fish was salted together with kosher meat and absorbed its flavor. That fish was then cooked in a clean, pareve pot. Question: Does the pot now require kashering?

Analysis: The fish absorbed meat flavor (nat). The pot absorbed secondary flavor from the fish (nat bar nat). Since the original meat was permitted, this is nat bar nat d'heteira — a secondary transfer of a permitted flavor.

Ruling: The pot remains kosher. This is the paradigmatic case that nat bar nat of permitted substances carries no prohibition.
