🦴 Matnot Kehunah: The Three Priestly Gifts

זְרוֹעַ / Zeroa

  • The right foreleg
  • From shoulder to knee joint
  • Source: Devarim 18:3
  • Given after slaughter

לְחָיַיִם / Lechayayim

  • The two cheeks/jaws
  • Includes the tongue
  • Must be given whole
  • Non-transferable to non-kohen

קֵבָה / Keivah

  • The maw (abomasum)
  • Fourth stomach of ruminant
  • Given with its contents
  • Disputed: must it be intact?

🤔 Kohen Who Slaughters His Own Animal

Mishnah
A kohen who slaughters an animal for himself — he is entitled to keep the zeroa, lechayayim, and keivah for himself. He need not give them to another kohen.
Gemara Q
But what is the source? The Torah says 'you shall give (תִּתֵּן) to the kohen' — this implies another kohen, not oneself!
Rav Huna
The gifts belong to the kohen by right of priesthood. When the owner IS the kohen, the obligation is fulfilled in his own person — no transfer is required.
Rava
This holds only if he slaughtered for his own consumption. If he slaughtered on behalf of a Yisrael (Israelite), he must separate the gifts for the owner to then give back to him — לֹא יְשַׁנֶּה.
Ruling A kohen slaughtering his own animal retains the gifts; if slaughtering for an Israelite, the ritual of giving must still be performed.

👩 Can a Bat Kohen Receive the Priestly Gifts?

Rabbi Yochanan
A bat kohen (daughter of a priest) who is married to a Yisrael — she may not eat terumah. By the same logic, she cannot receive the matnot kehunah.
Reish Lakish
I disagree! Matnot kehunah are different from terumah. The Torah says 'לַכֹּהֵן' — to a kohen. A bat kohen retains her priestly lineage regardless of whom she marries.
Abaye
The Mishnah implies the gifts are given to the kohen's household (בֵּית אָבִיו). This could include daughters. But once she leaves that household through marriage to a non-kohen, she exits the circle.
Rava
The practical ruling: she may not receive matnot kehunah once married to a non-kohen, just as she loses her terumah rights. Her husband's status governs her access to priestly perquisites.
Halacha A bat kohen married to a non-kohen does NOT receive the priestly gifts; she follows her husband's status in this matter.

📋 Procedure for Giving Matnot Kehunah After Slaughter

Step 1 — Valid Shechita: Confirm the animal was slaughtered according to halacha (שְׁחִיטָה כְּשֵׁרָה). Invalid slaughter creates no gift obligation.
↓
Step 2 — Separate the three gifts: Remove the zeroa (right foreleg), lechayayim (cheeks with tongue), and keivah (maw). These must be physically separated.
↓
Step 3 — Determine owner's status: Is the animal owned by a Jew? If it belonged to a non-Jew at time of slaughter, no gift obligation applies — even if a Jew performed the slaughter.
↓
Step 4 — Identify recipient kohen: Give to any eligible kohen. If the slaughterer is himself a kohen, he retains the gifts. The kohen need not be present at slaughter.
↓
Step 5 — Kohen takes possession: Once the kohen acquires the gifts (זָכָה), they are fully his property. He may eat them, give them to his household, or — according to some opinions — even sell them.
