🥚 Kosher vs. Non-Kosher Egg — The Two Signs

Kosher Egg ✅

  • One end pointed, one end round
  • Yolk (חלמון) located inside
  • White (חלבון) surrounds the yolk
  • Both signs must be present to permit

Non-Kosher Egg ❌

  • Both ends are equal (round-round or pointed-pointed)
  • Yolk (חלמון) located outside
  • White (חלבון) is inside — inverted structure
  • Either sign alone renders it suspect

⚔️ Treifah Bird's Eggs — Do They Become Forbidden?

Rav
Eggs produced by a treifah bird are forbidden. The prohibition of treifah extends to its products — what emerges from the forbidden is itself forbidden.
Shmuel
Eggs of a treifah bird are permitted. The egg is a distinct entity; the treifah status of the mother does not transfer to a separate product she produces.
Rava
The halacha follows Shmuel: eggs of a treifah are permitted. The egg was formed independently and is not subsumed within the treifah prohibition itself.
Key Distinction The debate hinges on whether eggs are considered an extension of the bird's body or an independent entity. Shmuel's view — they are independent — becomes normative practice.

📋 Eggs Found Inside a Slaughtered Bird — Step by Step

Step 1: A kosher bird undergoes valid shechita (ritual slaughter)
↓
Step 2: Eggs are discovered in the oviduct (בֵּית הָרֶחֶם) of the slaughtered bird
↓
Step 3 — Key Question: Are the eggs gulmerin — fully formed with a complete shell?
↓
If shell is complete: Eggs are permitted — treated as separate entities, not part of the mother
↓
If shell is incomplete: Eggs are still permitted — they are included within the shechita of the mother bird
↓
Critical Exception: If the mother was a treifah, the eggs are subject to the Rav–Shmuel dispute — but the ruling follows Shmuel: permitted

📖 Why the Torah Doesn't List Egg Signs Explicitly

סִימָנֵי בֵּיצִים: חֶלְמוֹן מִבִּפְנִים וְחֶלְבּוֹן מִבַּחוּץ — כְּשֵׁרָה; חֶלְמוֹן מִבַּחוּץ וְחֶלְבּוֹן מִבִּפְנִים — טְרֵפָה
Rabbinic Derivation The Torah specifies signs for kosher animals (split hoof + chews cud) and fish (fins + scales), but not for eggs. The Rabbis of the Gemara derived egg signs by logical inference: since a kosher bird produces the egg, the egg's physical characteristics must reflect its kosher origin. Rav taught that these signs — shape and yolk placement — are reliable enough to permit eggs found in the marketplace, even without witnessing the parent bird. This parallels the principle that observable physical markers suffice as halachic identifiers.
