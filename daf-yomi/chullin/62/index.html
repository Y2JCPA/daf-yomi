🦅 Bird Kashrut: Signs vs. Tradition

Tanna Kamma
Any bird that dores — seizes prey with its talons and eats it while gripped — is non-kosher. This is the decisive disqualifying test.
R' Eliezer b. R' Tzadok
Any bird whose toes divide equally — two against two — is non-kosher, for that pattern marks a bird of prey.
Abaye
Where signs are ambiguous, we rely on mekubal — established communal tradition — to certify that a species is kosher.
Gemara
All four positive signs together provide certainty: mursaa (crop), peelable gizzard lining, extra toe, and the bird does not dores.
🔑 Core Rule A bird with all four kosher signs and no dores behavior is permitted. But communal tradition (mekubal) may also establish kashrut when direct observation of signs is impractical.

🐦 Kosher vs. Non-Kosher Birds: The Sign System

✅ Kosher Signs

  • מֻרְסָא — Crop (food pouch in throat)
  • Peelable gizzard inner lining
  • אֶצְבַּע יְתֵרָה — Extra toe
  • Does NOT dores (seize with talons)

❌ Non-Kosher Signs

  • Dores — holds food in talons while eating
  • Toes split equally two-and-two
  • No crop present
  • Cannot peel gizzard lining

📜 Mekubal Override

  • Communal tradition certifies species
  • Used when signs are hard to observe
  • Cannot override witnessed dores behavior
  • Ashkenazim require both signs AND tradition

🦕 The Bar Yuchni — Wonders of Creation

בַּר יוּכְנִי — נָפְלָה בֵּיצָתוֹ וְשִׁקְּעָה שִׁשִּׁים כְּרַכִּים, וְקְלִפָּתָהּ שָׁבְרָה שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת אֲרָזִים
📖 Aggadic Narrative (62b) The Talmud recounts the legend of the Bar Yuchni (בַּר יוּכְנִי), a bird of incomprehensible size. When one of its eggs fell, it flooded sixty cities and its shell splintered three hundred cedar trees. Some sages identify the Bar Yuchni with the Ziz of Tehillim (50:11): "I know every bird of the mountains." The story is brought as aggadic illustration of God's mastery over creation — creatures exist in forms beyond human imagination.

📜 The Role of Mekubal (Tradition) in Bird Identification

The Problem: The Torah lists non-kosher birds by name (Vayikra 11, Devarim 14) rather than giving complete definitive signs — implying that knowledge of species identity is essential.
↓
The Solution — Mekubal: Where a community has an unbroken tradition that a bird species is kosher, that tradition is halachically valid and may substitute for direct sign-by-sign verification.
↓
Why tradition works: The Torah itself relies on oral tradition for species identification. Many ancient communities had reliable knowledge passed down through generations of practice.
↓
Hard Limit — Dores: If a bird is actually observed doresing, no tradition can override. The behavioral disqualifier is absolute and trumps any received custom.
↓
Ashkenazic stringency: Due to uncertainty about species identity over time, Ashkenazim require both tradition AND the presence of kosher signs before permitting a bird not explicitly listed as kosher.
