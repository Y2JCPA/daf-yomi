📏 Does Torah Speak in Exaggeration?

תּוֹרָה דִּבְּרָה לְשׁוֹן גּוּזְמָא
Core Question Chullin 90 explores a profound tension: the Torah, which is entirely true, sometimes employs hyperbolic language. The Sages teach "Torah dibra lashon gozmah" — the Torah spoke in exaggerated terms — explaining verses like "cities great and fortified up to the heavens" (Deut. 1:28). The daf also praises silence and humility as the secret source of Israel's spiritual greatness.

🤫 The Merit of Silence

גְּדוּלָּתָן מִקַּטְנוּתָן — Their greatness comes from their smallness
Aggadic Teaching The Sages teach: Israel's greatness comes from their humility. When God declared 'I am the Lord your God' (Ex. 20:2), Israel held silent before Him. Said the Holy One: 'By your life — because you held your mouths closed before Me, I will repay you greatly.' The same mouth that proclaimed 'I am the Lord' also commanded 'Honor your father and mother' — equating reverence for God with reverence for parents.

The formula: גְּדוּלָּתָן מִקַּטְנוּתָן — their greatness flows from their smallness.

📖 Lashon Gozmah: Exaggerated Text vs. The Principle

Exaggerated Statements

  • "Cities great and fortified up to the heavens" (Deut. 1:28) — the spies' intimidated report
  • "Blood reached the horses' bridles" — prophetic end-of-days imagery (cf. Joel, Zech.)
  • "We were like grasshoppers in our own eyes" (Num. 13:33) — spies' self-perception
  • Og's iron bed: nine cubits long — conveying his terrifying giant stature (Deut. 3:11)

The Governing Principle

  • Torah speaks in human language — including hyperbole
  • Gozmah does not compromise Torah's truthfulness
  • It communicates emotional reality, not literal measurement
  • Prophets and Sages also used gozmah when the context prevents confusion
  • A scholar must be careful: only where it is obviously non-literal

⚖️ May Scholars Use Exaggerated Language?

Restrictive View
A Torah scholar must never depart from strict truth. Scripture's gozmah is a unique dispensation for sacred text — it does not license ordinary Rabbinic speech or teaching to employ hyperbole.
Permissive View
The Sages themselves used gozmah to make teachings vivid and memorable. When the statement is so extreme that every listener understands it cannot be literal — a 'davar she-eino' — it is entirely permitted.
Talmudic Resolution
Three places are identified where the Rabbis employed lashon gozmah: the size of Og's bed, the magnitude of a great army, and dimensions of the Temple Court — all recognized as rhetorical intensification, not deception.
Halakhic Bottom Line Gozmah is a legitimate rhetorical device when context makes it unmistakably figurative. A Torah scholar must guard his speech carefully — exaggeration that could mislead is forbidden.
