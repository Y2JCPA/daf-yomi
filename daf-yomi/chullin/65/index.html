↓
Four physical signs required: four legs, four wings, kra'ayim (jumping legs), and wings covering the majority of the body

📜 The Four Torah-Permitted Locust Species

אַרְבֶּה Arbeh

  • The common locust
  • Most widely recognized species
  • Explicitly listed in Vayikra 11:22
  • Universally accepted as kosher

סָלְעָם Sal'am

  • Second listed species
  • Exact species identity uncertain
  • Some identify as a bald locust
  • Permitted by Torah text

חַרְגּוֹל Chargol

  • Third listed species
  • Some identify with a cricket type
  • Debated in medieval commentaries
  • Permitted by Torah text

חָגָב Chagav

  • Fourth species / possibly generic term
  • Yochanan ben Nuri: all 'chagav' types are permitted
  • Central to the Gemara's debate
  • Requires sign + name verification

🔍 The Four Signs of a Kosher Locust

Sign 1 — Four Legs: arba raglayim — must have exactly four walking legs
↓
Sign 2 — Four Wings: arba kenafayim — must possess four distinct wings
↓
Sign 3 — Kra'ayim: כְּרָעַיִם — elongated jumping legs above the regular legs, used for leaping
↓
Sign 4 — Wings Cover the Body: Wings must cover the rov (majority) of the creature's body length
↓
All four signs must be present. A creature lacking even one sign is forbidden regardless of its name

⚖️ Are Physical Signs Sufficient — or Is Masorah Required?

Yochanan ben Nuri
All species bearing the name חָגָב are permitted. The Torah's enumeration of four species is representative — any locust with the four signs and a locust-type name is kosher. Signs alone can suffice.
Rabbis
Physical signs alone are not sufficient. We require reliable species identification through masorah — a continuous oral tradition confirming that this specific species is indeed a permitted type.
Yochanan ben Nuri
Even a species whose precise name is unknown — if it clearly bears all four physical signs, it should be permitted. The Torah gave us signs precisely so we do not need to rely solely on tradition.
Gemara / Conclusion
Both criteria operate together: the four signs establish the category, but masorah is necessary in practice to confirm permissibility — especially when species names are ambiguous or overlapping.
Practical Outcome Ashkenazic Jews avoid all locusts (loss of masorah). Yemenite and some Sephardic communities retain traditions permitting specific identified species.

🤝 The Principle of Masorah in Locust Identification

כָּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ כְּרָעַיִם — כָּשֵׁר — Any locust possessing jumping legs is kosher (Chullin 65)
Core Concept — מַסּוֹרֶת The Gemara teaches that even where the Torah provides physical signs (simanim), the identification of kosher locusts ultimately depends on masorah — a reliable chain of communal tradition passing down which specific species are permitted. The signs are necessary but not always sufficient: they define the category while masorah confirms membership. This principle reflects a broader Talmudic theme: written law and oral tradition work in tandem. When the chain of transmission breaks — as it did in many communities following the disruptions of exile — even a creature meeting every physical criterion becomes halachically suspect.
