Chullin 41 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
Why does the Gemara cite the bird sin offering to support Rav Huna's position?
The bird sin offering can simultaneously violate slaughter on Shabbat, slaughter outside the Temple, and slaughter for idol worship. The Gemara argues: the Tanna only needs to specify this triple-violation case if the act of slaughter actually renders the animal forbidden — otherwise there's nothing to be liable for. This implies one CAN render another's property forbidden, supporting Rav Huna.
Question 2 of 5
What does the wine libation case in Gittin 52b prove about rendering another's property forbidden?
The wine libation case (Gittin 52b) proves the point clearly: when someone pours another's wine as an idol libation, the Mishna says he is liable to pay damages (if deliberate). This liability only makes sense if his act actually rendered the wine forbidden — otherwise he hasn't caused any real loss. One person's act can therefore render another's property halachically forbidden.
Question 3 of 5
According to the Mishna, why is it forbidden to let slaughter blood flow into the sea?
The prohibition is based on mar'it ayin — the appearance to onlookers. Ancient idol worship included offerings to seas, rivers, and natural bodies of water. Blood flowing into the sea looks identical to such an offering, even if the slaughterer has no idolatrous intent. The Rabbis prohibited it entirely to avoid this appearance.
Question 4 of 5
Why is it permitted to let slaughter blood flow into a pit (even without water)?
Both reasons apply: there is no idol associated with earth/pits, AND blood flowing into the ground looks like (and fulfills) the mitzvah of covering the blood (kisui hadam). Any observer seeing blood flow into earth would think "he's covering the blood" — a Torah commandment. There is no appearance of idol worship whatsoever.
Question 5 of 5
What is the principle of mar'it ayin and why does it matter in halacha?
Mar'it ayin (the appearance to the eye) is the Rabbinic principle that even technically permitted acts can be forbidden if they create a false impression of prohibited behavior. It's not limited to idol worship — it applies wherever an act could mislead observers into thinking something forbidden is occurring. The goal is to protect the integrity of Torah observance from misperception.
