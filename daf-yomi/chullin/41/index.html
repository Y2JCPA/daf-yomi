🐦 The Bird Sin Offering Puzzle

The Gemara asks: Rav Huna says slaughtering another's animal for idol worship renders it forbidden. But the Tanna of a baraita specifically uses a "bird sin offering" as his example. Why specify a bird offering — what does that add?
If a person cannot render another's property forbidden, why does the baraita need a special case of a bird offering?
↓
A bird sin offering is unique: it can violate THREE prohibitions simultaneously — slaughter on Shabbat, outside the Temple, for idol worship
↓
But this triple-violation is only possible if the act CAN render the animal forbidden!
↓
This implies the baraita assumes one CAN render another's property forbidden — supporting Rav Huna's position

📜 Parallel from Gittin 52b

The Gemara brings a parallel Mishna from Tractate Gittin: Three cases of damaging another's property — do they render the item forbidden, requiring payment?
CaseActIf UnwittingIf Deliberate
Rendering impureMakes another's food tameiExempt from paymentLiable
Mixing terumaMixes teruma into another's produceExemptLiable
Wine libationPours wine as idol libationExemptLiable
Significance: The wine libation case proves that one person CAN render another's wine forbidden through their act — otherwise there would be nothing to pay damages for! This supports Rav Huna: actions, not just ownership, determine what becomes forbidden.

🧑‍🤝‍🧑 Jewish vs. Gentile Slaughterer

Q
The Gittin case involves a Jew pouring the wine libation. Does the same apply when a GENTILE pours another's wine for idol worship — is the wine rendered forbidden?
Ans
Yes — even a gentile's act of libation renders another's wine forbidden. The prohibition of yayin nesech (idol libation wine) applies regardless of whether the person who poured it owns the wine.
Rav Acha, son of Rava: Adds a nuance — if a Jew was forewarned that slaughtering for idols is punishable by death, and he acknowledged the warning and did it anyway, he renders the animal forbidden even according to Rav Nachman. The deliberate, forewarned act is in a different category entirely.

🗡️ New Mishna: Where Can Slaughter Blood Flow?

The Mishna introduces a new topic: Where may slaughter blood flow? The concern is mar'it ayin — the appearance of idol worship.
❌ Forbidden: Into seas, rivers, or vessels
Looks like you're offering the blood to the sea/river, or collecting it for idol use
↓
✅ Permitted: Into a round excavation (pit) filled with water
The water mixes with and dilutes the blood — clearly not a meaningful offering
↓
✅ Permitted: Into a pit with no water
Blood flows into earth — an act of concealment (covering the blood), not idol worship

👁️ Mar'it Ayin — The Appearance Principle

Mar'it ayin (מַרְאִית עַיִן) — "the appearance to the eye." Even when an act is technically permitted, if it looks like a forbidden act to an observer, the Rabbis may prohibit it.

Why Seas/Rivers Forbidden

  • 🌊 Ancient idol worship included offerings to the sea and rivers
  • 👁️ Blood flowing into the sea looks like a dedication to sea gods
  • ❌ Even if you have no such intent, observers will think you do
  • ⚠️ Prohibited to avoid the appearance of idol worship

Why Pits Are Permitted

  • 🕳️ Blood into a pit = covering the blood (a mitzvah!)
  • 👁️ No idol associated with holes in the ground
  • ✅ Even observers understand this as the mitzvah of kisui hadam
  • 💧 Water in pit: dilution makes it obviously non-intentional
