Chullin 40 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
If someone slaughters an animal "for the sake of the mountain itself" (not for an idol on it), what is the ruling?
Invalid slaughter (the intent was idolatrous in character) but benefit is permitted — the mountain itself is not a dedicated idol object. Nothing has been consecrated to an idol, so no object becomes halachically forbidden for benefit. Abaye uses this distinction to resolve an apparent contradiction between sources.
Question 2 of 5
Why does slaughtering "for an idol dedicated on a mountain" result in the animal being forbidden for benefit — unlike slaughtering "for the mountain"?
A dedicated idol is a halachically forbidden object. When you slaughter an animal as an offering to it, the animal becomes dedicated to and associated with the idol — which renders it forbidden for benefit (like anything used in idol worship). A mountain, however, is just nature — no object has been consecrated, so no benefit prohibition attaches.
Question 3 of 5
Two people hold one knife together and slaughter — one with idol intent, one with kosher intent. What is the ruling?
The joint slaughter is invalid. When two people perform one inseparable act together, the invalid intent of one partner contaminates the entire act. There is no way to divide the slaughter into "valid half" and "invalid half" — it's one act with mixed intent, and the disqualifying intent prevails.
Question 4 of 5
According to Rav Huna, if someone else's animal was laid before an idol, what happens when you cut even one siman?
Rav Huna rules that cutting even one siman of an animal prostrated before an idol renders the animal forbidden. The act of slaughter is not merely a personal declaration (like bowing) — it physically transforms the animal by completing the idol-worship dedication. This is why it can affect property that doesn't belong to the slaughterer.
Question 5 of 5
What is the core dispute between Rav Huna and Rav Nachman regarding idol-slaughter of another's animal?
The root dispute is whether one person can render another person's property forbidden. Rav Huna says yes — just as certain acts upon an object can sanctify or forbid it regardless of ownership, so too idol-slaughter. Rav Nachman says no — "a person cannot render forbidden something that isn't his." The Gemara shows this maps onto an existing Tannaic dispute.
