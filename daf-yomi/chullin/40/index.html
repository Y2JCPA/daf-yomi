🗿 Slaughtering "For the Mountain" — What's the Rule?

The Mishna teaches: If someone slaughters an animal for the sake of mountains, hills, seas, rivers, or the wilderness — the slaughter is unfit. But benefit from the animal is permitted.
Why "unfit" but benefit permitted? These aren't actual idols — mountains and rivers are just nature.
↓
Abaye's answer: Two different cases in the sources —
↓
"For the mountain itself"
Slaughter is unfit (idolatrous intent) but benefit permitted — the mountain is not an actual idol object
"For the idol on the mountain"
Slaughter is unfit AND benefit forbidden — this IS actual idol worship with a dedicated idol object

☠️ "Offerings to the Dead" — A Special Category

The Gemara infers: The Mishna lists mountains etc. as "unfit but benefit permitted." This implies "offerings to the dead" (i.e., to idols) are not in this same lenient category — they are worse.
Type of SlaughterSlaughter Valid?Benefit Permitted?
For the mountain/river itself❌ Unfit✅ Permitted
For an idol dedicated on the mountain❌ Unfit❌ Forbidden
"For the dead" = for actual idols❌ Unfit❌ Forbidden
For a legitimate purpose✅ Valid✅ Permitted
Key distinction: Nature (mountains, rivers) is not idolized — slaughtering "for" it is bizarre intent but doesn't create a forbidden object. An actual idol is a forbidden object — anything dedicated to it becomes forbidden for benefit.

🤝 Two People, One Knife

The Mishna: Two people hold a knife together and slaughter. One has idol-intent; one has legitimate intent. What happens?
✂️ Joint slaughter: Person A (idol intent) + Person B (kosher intent)
↓
❌ The slaughter is invalid
↓
Why? Person B's kosher intent should "save" it!
↓
Because each person's action is inseparable — they are one act. The invalid intent of one partner contaminates the entire joint act.
↓
✅ Exception: if Person A's portion of cutting was already complete before Person B began — then Person B's valid slaughter stands alone

🐄 Rav Huna: Animal Prostrated Before an Idol

R'Huna
If someone else's animal was laid before an idol (as if in worship), once you cut even ONE siman (windpipe or gullet) of that animal — you have rendered it forbidden. The act of slaughter completes the idol-worship dedication.
Source
This follows Ulla quoting Rabbi Yochanan: "Although bowing to another's animal doesn't render it forbidden [since you can't sanctify what isn't yours], the act of slaughter is different — slaughter actively completes the dedication to the idol."
The logic: Bowing to someone else's animal = your personal act, doesn't transfer to the object. But slaughter is an act upon the animal that physically transforms it — it completes the idol-worship ritual and makes the animal forbidden even though it doesn't belong to you.

⚔️ Can You Forbid Someone Else's Property?

R'Huna
Yes — slaughtering another's animal for idol worship renders it forbidden, even though it belongs to someone else.
R'Nachman
No! A person cannot render forbidden something that doesn't belong to him. Just as you can't consecrate another's property, you can't forbid it either.
The Gemara shows this is a Tannaic dispute: Some Tannaim hold one person CAN render another's property forbidden (through certain acts); others disagree. Rav Huna follows the stricter Tannaim; Rav Nachman, Rav Amram, and Rav Yitzchak follow the lenient view — and they argue their position works even according to the strict Tannaim, because slaughter-for-idol is still categorically different from bowing.
