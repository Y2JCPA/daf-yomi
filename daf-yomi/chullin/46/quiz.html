Chullin 46 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
According to Shmuel, there are three successive branching points behind the pelvis. If the spinal cord is cut after the third branch, what is the ruling?
Shmuel teaches that the spinal cord has three branching points behind the pelvis. Before the first = definitely treifah. After the third = definitely kosher. The uncertain zone is between the first and third branches. R' Yehuda tried to show this on actual animals but couldn't find one where the anatomy was clearly visible.
Question 2 of 5
According to Rav Pappa, where must an olive-bulk of liver remain for the animal to be kosher?
Rav Pappa rules stringently: to satisfy both R' Zeira (gallbladder location) and Rav Adda bar Ahava (right kidney location), an olive-bulk must remain at each. This is the practical ruling. What if the remaining liver is scattered in small pieces, or thin like a strip, or flat? Those questions remain unresolved (teku).
Question 3 of 5
Why can't you use hot or cold water to test a lung for perforations?
The Gemara explains the precise reason: hot water causes the lung to contract, which could close an existing perforation and give a false kosher reading. Cold water hardens the lung tissue and may cause it to crack, creating a new perforation. Only tepid water preserves the lung in its natural state for an accurate diagnostic test.
Question 4 of 5
If the outer membrane of the lung is entirely removed so it looks like a "red date," what is the ruling?
Rabba teaches that a lung whose outer membrane was completely removed — so it appears red like a date (since the inner membrane is red) — is kosher. The inner membrane alone is sufficient to protect the lung. This is actually a stronger case than a simple perforation: if removing the entire outer membrane is kosher, then a mere perforation in the outer membrane is certainly kosher.
Question 5 of 5
What broader principle does the tepid water test illustrate about the Talmud's approach to treifot?
The tepid water test is a prime example of applied science within halacha. The rabbis didn't just establish categories — they developed a reliable, replicable diagnostic procedure: submerge in tepid water, inflate, check for bubbles. They even explained why the water temperature matters (hot contracts, cold cracks). This test was used by shochtim for centuries, showing how halachic reasoning and empirical observation work together.
