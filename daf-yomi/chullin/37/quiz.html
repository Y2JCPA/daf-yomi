Chullin 37 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
According to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, when is slaughter of a moribund (near-death) animal valid?
Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel requires the most demanding standard: the animal must convulse with BOTH its foreleg AND its hind leg after slaughter. This double convulsion is his proof that the animal was sufficiently alive at the moment of slaughter.
Question 2 of 5
From which verse does the Gemara ultimately derive that slaughtering a moribund animal is permitted?
The Gemara goes through several failed proofs before landing on Leviticus 7:24: "The fat of a carcass and the fat of a treifah may be used for any purpose." The Sages derive from this verse that neveilah and treifah are distinct categories — implying a dying animal is separate from both and thus can be rendered permitted through slaughter.
Question 3 of 5
A small animal extends its foreleg during slaughter but does NOT restore it to the bent position afterward. What is the halachic ruling?
The Mishna explicitly rules this invalid. Extending a leg and not restoring it is a reflexive death spasm — the body releasing its final tension. A genuine sign of life requires purposeful two-directional movement: extending AND restoring (or bending AND extending).
Question 4 of 5
Why might someone initially think slaughtering a near-death animal would NOT render its meat permitted?
The concern is that a near-death animal may be halachically classified as a neveilah even while still technically alive. If the Torah's prohibition on neveilah includes animals in an irreversible death process, then slaughter wouldn't help — you'd just be slaughtering something already "forbidden." The Gemara works to prove this is not the case.
Question 5 of 5
In Rabbi Shimon's case of night slaughter with blood-splattered walls found the next morning — why is the slaughter valid?
Blood spattering onto walls requires significant force — the kind of arterial pressure that only exists in a living animal. Finding this evidence the morning after slaughter retroactively proves the animal was alive (with real blood pressure) at the moment of slaughter. Rabbi Shimon considers this as valid a sign of life as a visible convulsion.
