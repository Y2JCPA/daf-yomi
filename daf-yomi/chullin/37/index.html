Chullin 37

חולין לז
Slaughtering a moribund animal: is it valid? Signs of life, post-slaughter convulsions, and the permissibility of "danger" slaughter

📜 The Mishna: Slaughtering a Dying Animal

Mishna

The Mishna introduces a new topic: slaughtering an animal that is in danger of imminent death (goses or safek treifah). Is such slaughter valid, and does it permit eating the meat?

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel

Valid only if the animal convulses with both its foreleg AND hind leg after slaughter — demonstrating it was alive

Rabbi Eliezer

Valid if blood spurts from the neck wound — spurting blood is sufficient proof of life at moment of slaughter

The Rabbis

Valid as long as the animal showed any sign of life at slaughter — convulsions of foreleg or hind leg suffice

❓ Why Should a Dying Animal's Meat Be Forbidden?

Gemara Question

The Gemara asks: why would we even think slaughtering a moribund animal is invalid? It's still alive!

The concern: perhaps the animal is effectively a neveilah (carcass) in all but name — near death and not truly "alive" in the halachic sense. If so, slaughter wouldn't help.

The Gemara pushes back: "You shall not eat an unslaughtered carcass" — by inference, a properly slaughtered animal is permitted, even if it was nearly dead.

🔄 The Derivation Dance: Three Failed Proofs

Gemara Logic

The Gemara tries several ways to prove a dying animal is permissible by slaughter:

  • From "neveilah" — rejected: maybe a dying animal IS a neveilah
  • From "treifah" — rejected: maybe a dying animal IS a treifah
  • Combining both — rejected: you can't use two uncertain categories to prove a third

The Gemara ultimately derives permissibility from a different verse: "The fat of a carcass and the fat of a treifah may be used for any purpose, but you shall not eat it" (Leviticus 7:24). The verse distinguishes neveilah and treifah from each other — implying a dying animal is separate from both.

🦵 What Counts as a Valid Convulsion?

Practical Halacha

The Mishna specifies different convulsions for small vs. large animals:

Small Animal (sheep, goat)

Must extend a bent foreleg OR bend an extended foreleg. Extending and NOT restoring = invalid (it's a death spasm, not a life sign)

Large Animal (cow, ox)

Same principle but the movements required are more deliberate — must restore the limb to opposite position

The key distinction: a life sign requires purposeful movement — extension AND restoration, not just a reflexive jerk.

🌙 Rabbi Shimon's Night-Slaughter Case

Specific Case

Rabbi Shimon: if someone slaughters at night and finds the walls splattered with blood the next morning — the slaughter is valid. Why?

Spurting blood that reaches the walls proves the animal was very much alive when slaughtered. The force required for such spurting demonstrates arterial pressure and true life.

"One who slaughters at night and the next day found walls full of blood — the slaughter is valid."

This also supports the view that spurting blood can be a sufficient life indicator.

📌 Summary

Key Takeaways
  • Slaughtering a moribund (near-death) animal IS valid — its meat is permitted
  • This is derived from Leviticus 7:24, distinguishing neveilah and treifah fat
  • Signs of life required: convulsion (Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel requires foreleg AND hind leg), or blood spurting (Rabbi Eliezer)
  • A death spasm (extending without restoring) does NOT count as a sign of life
  • Night slaughter: blood on the walls proves life at moment of slaughter
