Question 1 of 5
How does one test whether a shechitah knife has a disqualifying nick?
The fingernail test is the standard: run your fingernail lightly along both sides and the edge of the blade. If the fingernail catches at any point, the knife has a disqualifying nick and may not be used for shechitah. This physical test is more reliable than visual inspection alone.
Question 2 of 5
What happens to a slaughterer who refuses to present his knife to a Torah scholar?
Rav Huna's ruling is severe: the slaughterer is placed in cherem (ostracism), removed from his role, and his meat is proclaimed treif. This isn't just about one knife — it's about maintaining the integrity of the system. If slaughterers can bypass inspection, the entire communal trust in kashrut breaks down.
Question 3 of 5
Why is a tooth still attached in the mouth invalid for shechitah?
The principle is that a person standing on the ground is considered connected to it. Therefore, a tooth still in the mouth or a fingernail still on the finger is "attached to the ground" (mechubar l'karka). Since Daf 16 established that attached instruments are invalid, the tooth is invalid. Once removed (detached), the same tooth becomes valid.
Question 4 of 5
Where on the windpipe is valid for shechitah?
The valid zone for shechitah is within the cartilage rings, starting from the large upper ring (thyroid cartilage) and extending downward. Above the large ring is invalid. The rings don't need to fully encircle the windpipe — as long as they cover the majority of the circumference, cutting within them is valid.
Question 5 of 5
What does Rav Ashi rule when a slaughterer's knife is examined after the fact and found to be intact?
Rav Ashi rules practically: if the knife is found intact, the meat is kosher. His concern is with results, not procedure. However, Mar Zutra pushes back — if we don't enforce the procedure, slaughterers will become lax. The tension between practical results and systemic enforcement is a real-world governance question.
