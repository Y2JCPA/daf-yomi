🔍 How Much of a Defect Disqualifies?

How large must a nick in the knife be to disqualify it?
↓
Standard: Sufficient for a fingernail to be impeded on it — the "fingernail test"
↓
For the altar:
R' Shimon b. Yochai: a deficiency the size of a garlic peel disqualifies
For shechitah:
Even smaller — any defect that catches the fingernail
Two standards, one test: The altar has its own deficiency standard. For shechitah knives, the test is the fingernail — run it lightly across the blade edge. If it catches at any point, the knife is disqualified.

⚡ The Slaughterer Who Didn't Show the Knife

RH
Rav Huna: A slaughterer who did not present his knife before a Torah scholar — we ostracize him and remove him from his position.
Rv
Rava bar Chinnana: I ostracized a slaughterer who refused, removed him, and proclaimed his meat unfit.
RA
Rav Ashi: But what if his knife was later found to be intact? Then his meat should be permitted! The concern was defective knife — if the knife is fine, the meat is fine.
MZ
Mar Zutra to Rav Ashi: Shouldn't we still be concerned? If we don't enforce knife presentation, slaughterers will become lax and eventually use defective knives!
The tension: Is the penalty about the specific knife or about maintaining the system? Rav Huna's approach: enforce the system. Rav Ashi: judge each case on its merits.

🦷 Detached Tooth and Fingernail

Detached (from the body)

  • 🦷 Tooth pulled out → valid for shechitah
  • 💅 Fingernail removed → valid for shechitah
  • ✅ Rabba bar bar Chana: permitted ab initio
  • 📋 Same rule as any sharp detached object

Still Attached (in the mouth/hand)

  • 🦷 Tooth still in mouth → INVALID
  • 💅 Fingernail still on finger → INVALID
  • ❌ Treated as "attached to the ground" (body = ground)
  • 📋 The Mishna forbids attached tooth/nail
Why "attached to the ground"? A person standing on the ground is considered connected to the ground. Therefore, anything still attached to the person's body is halachically "attached to the ground" — and we learned in Daf 16 that attached instruments are invalid for shechitah.

🫁 The Rings of the Windpipe (Trachea)

Anatomy lesson: The windpipe (kaneh) is made of C-shaped cartilage rings stacked on top of each other. The Gemara needs to determine: where exactly on the windpipe is valid for shechitah?
The windpipe has a large upper ring (the thyroid cartilage) and smaller rings below
↓
Where do you cut?
↓
Within any of the rings: Valid — even if the ring doesn't encircle the entire windpipe, as long as it encircles the majority
↓
Above the large ring (thyroid): Invalid — this is not the "place of shechitah"

📏 Diverting the Knife — R' Yosei b. R' Yehuda

RY
R' Yosei b. R' Yehuda: If one slaughters within the large upper ring and left a thread-breadth of ring connected over the full circumference — the shechitah is valid.
RS
Rav and Shmuel agree with one part of his teaching: the large upper ring is valid for shechitah. But they disagree about specific details.
RY
Rav Yosef: R' Yosei b. R' Yehuda makes TWO statements — and Rav and Shmuel accept only one of them.
Diversion: If the knife starts in the right place but "diverts" (slips above or below the valid area mid-cut), the validity depends on where and how much was cut in the proper vs. improper location.

🌍 R' Zeira Moves to Eretz Yisrael

R' Zeira's journey: When R' Zeira moved from Babylonia to Eretz Yisrael, he adopted the local lenient practice regarding knife diversion — following the view of R' Yosei b. R' Yehuda.
Rav Yosef was angry: "I learn from Rav Yehuda, who is meticulous in transmission. R' Zeira eats meat that I would not eat!" Rav Yosef felt that Babylonian stringency should not be abandoned just because one moved.
The resolution: R' Zeira argued that a permanent immigrant adopts local customs entirely. A visitor keeps both sets of stringencies. Since R' Zeira had no plans to return to Babylonia, he followed Eretz Yisrael's rulings.
