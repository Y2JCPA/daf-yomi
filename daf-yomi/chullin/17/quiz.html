Chullin 17 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
According to R' Akiva, how did Jews eat non-sacrificial meat in the wilderness?
R' Akiva holds that nechira (stabbing) was the permitted method in the wilderness. Shechitah became mandatory only upon entering Eretz Yisrael. This differs from R' Yishmael, who holds that non-sacrificial meat was entirely forbidden in the wilderness.
Question 2 of 5
According to R' Yishmael, what was the rule for eating meat in the wilderness?
R' Yishmael's view is that "meat of desire" (basar ta'avah) — meaning regular meat not from a sacrifice — was forbidden in the wilderness. Jews could only eat meat from animals brought as offerings to the Mishkan. When they entered Eretz Yisrael and were distant from the Temple, meat of desire became permitted through shechitah.
Question 3 of 5
Why can't nechira be re-permitted now that we are in exile, far from the Temple?
The Gemara addresses this directly: once a standard is elevated (from nechira to shechitah), it doesn't revert even when circumstances change. The Torah's halachic development is progressive — standards go up, not back down. Even though we are far from the Temple, the requirement for shechitah remains permanent.
Question 4 of 5
What are Rava's three categories of knife notches?
Rava classifies notches by their effect when a fingernail is drawn across them. "Catches" (ogeret) — the fingernail gets stuck: completely invalid. "Entangles" (masrekes) — fibers tangle but don't catch: may not slaughter, validity debated. "Smooth-ish" (sasa) — slightly rough like an awn of grain but no actual notch: valid.
Question 5 of 5
From where does Rav Chisda derive that examining the knife is a Torah obligation?
Rav Chisda derives the obligation from a verse connected to David HaMelech and Shmuel, where the emphasis on "this" (specific instrument) implies that one must first verify the knife before using it. This makes knife examination a Torah-level obligation, not merely a rabbinic safeguard.
