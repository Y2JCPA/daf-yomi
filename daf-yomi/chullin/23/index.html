🐑 Disqualifying Animals — Two Key Exclusions

Bestiality Object

  • 🚫 An animal used for bestiality
  • ❌ Permanently disqualified from the altar
  • 📜 Source: "their corruption is in them, there is a blemish in them" (Vayikra 22:25)
  • ⚠️ Even if the sin occurred to the animal involuntarily

Idol Worship Object

  • 🚫 An animal worshipped as an idol
  • ❌ Disqualified from the altar
  • 📜 Same verse: "corruption" = also idol worship
  • ⚠️ But: an animal that merely passed before an idol? Debate.
Gemara clarifies: The verse "their corruption is in them" uses the word moshchatam (corruption) — a word also used for idol worship in Devarim 4:16. This links the two: both forms of "corruption" disqualify an animal from the altar.

📏 The Palges — An In-Between Animal

What is a Palges? An animal that has passed its first year (too old to be a lamb) but has not yet completed its second year (not yet a full ram). It is in the "twilight zone" between the two categories.
Year 1: Keves (lamb) — valid for certain offerings at this age
↓
Beyond Year 1 but before Year 2: Palges — the ambiguous middle
↓
Year 2+: Ayil (ram) — valid for certain offerings, with larger accompanying offerings

⚖️ Is a Palges Its Own Category?

RY
R' Yochanan: A palges IS its own entity — a distinct halachic category, neither lamb nor ram. It has its own rules about accompanying offerings.
RL
Reish Lakish: A palges is NOT its own category. It is treated as a doubtful case — sometimes lamb, sometimes ram. One must stipulate when offering it.
The practical difference: If a palges is its own category, it may require unique accompanying offerings (different from both lamb and ram). If it's a doubtful case, you must cover both possibilities with stipulations (tenai), preventing a transgression of the altar service.

🧩 How to Stipulate for a Palges

If palges is doubtful, the offerer says: "If this is a ram, this grain offering is its required accompaniment. If this is a lamb, this grain offering is its required accompaniment."
↓
But can you stipulate for TWO DIFFERENT THINGS simultaneously?
↓
Reish Lakish: Yes — a person may stipulate two alternatives, and whichever applies will retroactively be correct
↓
This is a rare application of breirah (retroactive determination) in Temple service

🎓 Key Principle: Natural categories don't always fit neatly into halachic boxes. When an animal (or person, or object) falls between two defined categories, the Sages must determine: is this a third, distinct category, or is this a case of doubt requiring stipulation? The answer affects practical law significantly.
