Chullin 23 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
How does the Gemara derive that idol worship disqualifies an animal from the altar?
The Gemara uses a word connection (gezeirah shavah): the word "moshchatam" (corruption/blemish) in Vayikra 22:25 about altar disqualification is the same root as "tashchitun" in Devarim 4:16, which describes making idols. This verbal link extends the altar disqualification to animals connected to idol worship.
Question 2 of 5
What exactly is a "palges"?
A palges is defined by age: it has completed its first year (it's no longer a keves — a lamb) but has not yet completed its second year (it's not yet a full ayil — a ram). This twilight zone created a genuine halachic problem because different offerings require different accompanying grain and wine, and the rules for lamb and ram differ significantly.
Question 3 of 5
What is R' Yochanan's view on the halachic status of a palges?
R' Yochanan holds that a palges is its own halachic entity — "an entity in and of itself" (davar bifnei atzmah). This means it has its own fixed requirements for grain offerings and wine libations, rather than being a doubtful case between lamb and ram. Reish Lakish disagrees, treating it as a case of doubt that requires stipulation.
Question 4 of 5
According to Reish Lakish, how should an offerer handle a palges in the Temple?
Reish Lakish holds the palges is a doubtful case. The solution is stipulation: the offerer makes a conditional declaration covering both possibilities — if it's a ram, the larger grain offering applies; if it's a lamb, the smaller one applies. This is a rare Temple application of conditional stipulations to resolve category uncertainty.
Question 5 of 5
Why does the grain offering amount matter so much in the palges discussion?
Lamb requires 1 tenth of an ephah of flour; a ram requires 2 tenths. Bringing the wrong amount for the wrong animal constitutes a deficient or excess service. Since the Temple service must be exact, the uncertainty about whether a palges is a lamb or ram creates a genuine practical problem — you could inadvertently violate the altar service on either side.
