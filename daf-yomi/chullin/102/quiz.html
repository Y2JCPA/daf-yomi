QUESTION 1 OF 5
What is the Biblical source for the prohibition of Ever Min HaChai?
A. Leviticus 11 — laws of kosher animals
B. Exodus 22 — laws of damages and property
C. Genesis 9:4 — 'flesh with its life-blood you shall not eat'
D. Deuteronomy 12 — laws of slaughter in the Land
Correct: C — Ever Min HaChai is one of the Seven Noahide Laws, derived from Genesis 9:4: 'Only flesh with its life (nefesh) — its blood — you shall not eat.' Given to Noah, it applies to all humanity and was reinforced for Israel at Sinai.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does the Gemara mean when it says the flesh must retain its 'nefesh' to be prohibited as ever min hachai?
A. The flesh must have a pleasant smell indicating freshness
B. The animal must have been alive at the moment the limb was severed
C. The flesh must contain visible blood within it
D. The limb must have been removed by a person, not naturally fallen off
Correct: B — 'Basar b'nafsho' — flesh with its life — requires that the animal was still living when the limb or flesh was severed. If the animal was already dead (e.g., a neveilah), ever min hachai does not apply, though other prohibitions may.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the dispute between Rami bar Chama and Rava regarding 'basar min hachai'?
A. Whether birds are subject to the prohibition or only mammals
B. Whether flesh alone (without a complete limb) is independently forbidden from a living animal
C. Whether the shiur is a kazayit or a k'beitzah for this prohibition
D. Whether Noahides are more or less stringent than Jews in this area
Correct: B — Rami bar Chama holds that flesh alone from a living animal (basar min hachai) is independently forbidden based on the word 'basar' in the verse. Rava qualifies this, explaining the verse contextualizes the nefesh requirement. The Gemara accepts basar min hachai as a distinct prohibition.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
If a limb is severed from a living animal and the animal is subsequently slaughtered (shechted), what is the halacha for the severed limb?
A. It becomes permitted — shechita retroactively permits all parts of the animal
B. It remains forbidden as ever min hachai — shechita does not retroactively permit an already-severed limb
C. It depends whether the limb still had sensation when the animal was slaughtered
D. It becomes permitted only if the limb was reattached before slaughter
Correct: B — Once a limb is severed from a living animal, it is forbidden as ever min hachai — subsequent slaughter of that animal does NOT retroactively permit the already-severed limb. However, limbs still attached to the animal at the time of shechita become permitted through the slaughter.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the minimum amount (shiur) required to incur lashes for eating ever min hachai?
A. Any amount at all — there is no minimum for this severe prohibition
B. A k'beitzah (egg's volume) — larger than most food prohibitions
C. A kazayit (olive's worth) of flesh
D. Half a kazayit — since it applies to both Jews and Noahides it is stricter
Correct: C — The standard shiur for food-related prohibitions is a kazayit (olive's worth). The Gemara applies this measure to ever min hachai — one is liable for lashes only upon eating a kazayit of prohibited flesh. This is consistent with other Biblical food prohibitions.
