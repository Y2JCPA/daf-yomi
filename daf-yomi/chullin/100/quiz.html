QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Hachamim, why does the gid hanasheh not forbid the broth when cooked in a pot?
A. Because it becomes nullified in 60 parts of permitted meat
B. Because the gid has no flavor (ein bo ta'am) and cannot spread its prohibition
C. Because cooking dissolves the prohibition of the gid itself
D. Because the prohibition of gid only applies when eaten directly, not in mixtures
Correct: B — Hachamim hold the gid hanasheh is flavorless. Since prohibition spreads through mixtures only via flavor (ta'am), the tasteless gid cannot forbid the broth — even though the gid itself remains permanently forbidden.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is 'davar sheyesh lo shem levad' and what is its practical consequence for gid hanasheh?
A. An item with a pleasant taste, which becomes permitted when mixed with 60 parts permitted food
B. A distinct, named entity that retains its identity in any mixture and can therefore never be nullified
C. A rabbinic category of food requiring double wrapping during transport
D. An item whose prohibition depends solely on intention rather than physical properties
Correct: B — Davar sheyesh lo shem (a thing with its own name) is never batel — no ratio suffices. The gid is always identifiable as 'the gid,' so it must always be physically removed from any mixture regardless of how much permitted food surrounds it.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the core dispute between R. Yehuda and Hachamim on this daf?
A. R. Yehuda forbids only the right leg's gid; Hachamim forbid both legs
B. R. Yehuda says gid is only a rabbinic prohibition; Hachamim say it is biblical
C. R. Yehuda holds the gid does impart flavor so the whole pot is forbidden; Hachamim hold it has no flavor so only the gid is forbidden
D. R. Yehuda requires 120 parts to nullify the gid; Hachamim require only 60
Correct: C — The dispute is about ta'am (flavor): R. Yehuda — yesh bo ta'am, the gid has flavor, forbidden mixture. Hachamim — ein bo ta'am, the gid is flavorless, only the gid is removed and the mixture is permitted. Halacha follows Hachamim.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
If a gid hanasheh falls into a pot and is still physically intact, what is the practical halacha?
A. If there are 60 parts of permitted meat, everything including the gid is permitted
B. Discard the entire pot — the gid's presence automatically forbids everything
C. Remove and discard the gid; the remaining broth and meat are permitted
D. Wait until the gid fully dissolves, then the mixture becomes automatically permitted
Correct: C — The gid must always be removed (it is a named entity, never nullified). Per Hachamim (halacha), since the gid has no flavor, removing it resolves the situation — the rest of the pot is permitted.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why is the standard bitul b'shishim (1-in-60) rule irrelevant for gid hanasheh according to Hachamim?
A. Because gid requires a stricter ratio of 1 in 120, not 1 in 60
B. Because bitul b'shishim only applies to liquids, not solid pieces of meat
C. Because the 60x rule neutralizes flavor, but the gid has no flavor to begin with — making the ratio meaningless
D. Because biblical prohibitions can never be nullified by any ratio under rabbinic law
Correct: C — Bitul b'shishim works by diluting flavor below the threshold of detection. Since Hachamim hold the gid has no flavor at all, there is nothing to dilute — the concept simply does not apply. The gid's issue is its identity (davar sheyesh lo shem), not its flavor.
