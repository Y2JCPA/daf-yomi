🍖 Does Gid HaNasheh Forbid the Broth?

R. Yehuda
The gid hanasheh DOES impart flavor when cooked. The rule of ta'am k'ikar (flavor equals substance) therefore applies — if the gid gives taste to the broth, the entire pot becomes forbidden just as if the gid itself were present.
Hachamim
The gid hanasheh is tasteless (ein bo ta'am). Since it contributes no flavor, the prohibition cannot travel into the broth or surrounding meat. The gid alone is forbidden — remove it, and the rest of the pot is permitted.
Halacha The law follows Hachamim: remove the gid, and the mixture is permitted. The gid's lack of flavor is its defining halachic characteristic.

🏷️ Davar Sheyesh Lo Shem — A Thing With Its Own Name

דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שֵׁם לְבַד — אֵינוֹ בָּטֵל לְעוֹלָם
Core Principle The Gemara establishes that a davar sheyesh lo shem levad (an item possessing its own distinct name and identity) is never nullified in a mixture — regardless of ratio. The gid hanasheh always remains identifiable as 'the gid,' so it can never be declared batel even if surrounded by 1,000 times its weight in permitted meat. It must always be physically removed.

⚖️ Three Nullification Categories Compared

Bitul B'Shishim

  • Standard rule: 1 part forbidden in 60 permitted
  • Applies to flavorful forbidden substances
  • Flavor presumed undetectable at 1:60
  • Does NOT apply to gid (no flavor to nullify)
  • Example: forbidden fat in a stew

Ein Bo Ta'am (No Flavor)

  • Item has no perceptible taste
  • Cannot spread prohibition through mixture
  • Gid hanasheh per Hachamim
  • 60x ratio irrelevant — flavor doesn't exist
  • Result: mixture permitted once gid removed

Davar Sheyesh Lo Shem

  • Distinct, named, identifiable entity
  • Never nullified — any ratio
  • Identity persists in any mixture
  • Gid is always 'the gid'
  • Must be physically removed always

🔍 Practical Ruling: Gid Found in a Cooked Pot

Step 1 — Identify: Is the gid still physically intact and recognizable in the pot?
↓
Step 2 — Remove: Remove the gid immediately. It is forbidden regardless of any ratio or mixture size — it is a davar sheyesh lo shem.
↓
Step 3 — Assess the broth: Did the gid impart any flavor? Apply the ta'am test (or consult a rav).
↓
Step 4 — Per Hachamim (halacha): Since the gid is flavorless, the broth and remaining meat are fully permitted.
↓
Step 5 — Edge cases: If the gid dissolved beyond recognition or is from a non-kosher animal, additional stringencies may apply — consult a posek.
