Chullin 12 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
According to Rav Nachman, why can't you eat meat when you arrived mid-slaughter (without knowing the slaughterer's expertise)?
The case Rav Nachman is discussing is where you know the slaughterer is NOT an expert. You cannot use rov to override this positive knowledge of a disqualification. Rov only applies when expertise is unknown — then we say "the majority of those associated with slaughter are experts" and permit it.
Question 2 of 5
If you find an anonymous slaughtered chicken in the marketplace, what is the halacha?
Anonymous slaughtered meat found in the marketplace is permitted by rov — the majority of those who slaughter animals are knowledgeable in the halachot of shechitah. Since we have no specific reason to suspect this particular animal, rov applies and the meat is permitted.
Question 3 of 5
You send an agent to slaughter a chicken and return to find it already slaughtered. What permits you to eat it?
Two factors combine: (1) Chezkat shliach — the presumption that an agent carries out the mission he was sent on. (2) Rov — even if someone else slaughtered it, the majority of slaughterers are experts. Rav Nachman famously joked "eat a kor of salt over it" — meaning think hard and you'll see both principles permit it.
Question 4 of 5
According to R' Natan's view, can a thrown knife produce valid shechitah?
R' Natan holds that even inadvertent slaughter can be valid if the technical requirements are met. If a thrown knife passed through the animal's neck with a natural back-and-forth motion (like a saw cutting), cutting the necessary simanim — that counts as shechitah. Rava attributes this ruling to R' Natan specifically.
Question 5 of 5
What does "the majority of those associated with slaughter are experts" ultimately establish?
This principle creates a halachic presumption (chazakah) that enables normal commerce and life. Without it, every piece of meat would require certified verification of the specific slaughterer's expertise — an impossible standard in a functioning community. The Gemara establishes that this is an agreed-upon principle, not a tannaim dispute.
