🐦 Melikah — Pinching Bird Offerings

Background: In the Temple, certain bird offerings (like the Chatat bird) were killed by the Kohen's thumbnail — not by shechitah. This is called melikah (pinching). The Gemara explores how melikah and shechitah differ.

Shechitah (Slaughter)

  • 🗡️ Back-and-forth cutting motion
  • ✅ Valid for regular animals and birds
  • 🐦 For birds: front of neck (throat side)
  • 🔪 Requires a detached instrument

Melikah (Pinching)

  • 💅 Kohen's thumbnail cuts from back of neck
  • ✅ Only for bird offerings in the Temple
  • 🐦 From the nape — opposite of shechitah
  • ❌ A knife used for melikah invalidates it

📜 Is Bird Shechitah Biblical or Rabbinic?

V1
View 1: Bird slaughter is Biblical (d'oraita). Source: "And he shall slaughter it at the side of the altar" — includes birds. Therefore invalid ripping of simanim is also Biblical.
V2
View 2: Bird slaughter is only Rabbinic. The Torah verse refers to animals only. For birds, the Torah only specified melikah — shechitah was added by the Rabbis.
Rav Ashi's counter-argument: If bird shechitah is Biblical, then ripping the simanim should also be Biblically forbidden. If it's Rabbinic, then ripping simanim would only be Rabbinically forbidden — the stakes of the dispute are significant for how severely violations are treated.

🔪 Pinching with a Knife — What Happens?

A Kohen performs melikah using a knife instead of his thumbnail
↓
The melikah is invalid — it must be done with the thumbnail, not an instrument
↓
Why is a knife invalid for melikah when it's valid for shechitah?
↓
The Torah specifies the method: the Kohen's own thumbnail is the instrument of melikah. A knife is a different act entirely — it becomes slaughter, not pinching.
↓
If the knife cuts the simanim from the back: it's shechitah from the wrong direction — also invalid for an offering

🗺️ Location of Melikah vs. Shechitah on a Bird

🐦
MELIKAH
From the nape (back of neck)
Kohen's thumbnail
Temple offerings only
SHECHITAH
From the throat (front of neck)
Any sharp instrument
Regular consumption
R' Yirmeya: "What is valid for slaughter is not valid for pinching, and what is valid for pinching is not valid for slaughter." They operate on opposite sides of the bird's neck — a beautiful symmetry in the Torah's laws.

🦴 Broken Neck Bone with Most Flesh Cut

Ze'eiri: If the neck bone of an animal is broken AND most of the surrounding flesh is cut — the animal is a treifah (fatally wounded)
↓
Where do we learn this from?
↓
Rav Chisda: We learn it from a Mishna in Zevachim — if a Kohen pinched a bird offering with a knife (instead of his thumbnail), it is as if he cut with a knife
↓
An animal missing most of its neck flesh cannot survive — regardless of whether the bone is completely severed
Application: This applies to the 18 categories of treifot (fatal conditions). An animal with its neck bone broken and most flesh severed is automatically treif — it cannot live, so shechitah (even perfect shechitah) cannot render it kosher.

🎓 Key Principle: The Torah created two separate systems for bird and animal killing: shechitah (cutting the throat) and melikah (pinching the nape). These are not interchangeable — each has its own place, instrument, and context. The symmetry is deliberate: front vs. back, instrument vs. body part, profane vs. sacred.
