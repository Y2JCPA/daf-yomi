📜 The Biblical Source

וְזֶה יִהְיֶה מִשְׁפַּט הַכֹּהֲנִים מֵאֵת הָעָם מֵאֵת זֹבְחֵי הַזֶּבַח — דברים יח:ג
מצות עשה דאורייתא The Torah (Devarim 18:3) commands three mandatory gifts from every properly slaughtered chulin animal: זְרוֹעַ (zero'a — foreleg/shoulder), לְחָיַיִם (lechayim — the cheeks), and קֵבָה (keiva — the maw/stomach). They are given to any Kohen who presents himself to claim them — no specific Kohen is designated.

🤝 Kohen–Israelite Partnership Dispute

Question
Two partners — one Kohen, one Israelite — slaughter an animal together. Is the Israelite's share obligated in matanot?
Rav Huna
The Kohen's co-ownership exempts the entire animal. We derive this by analogy to teruma: when a Kohen holds a share, the whole is treated as priestly property.
Rav Yehuda
The Kohen's share exempts only his own portion. The Israelite's proportional share remains fully obligated — ownership is tracked individually.
Rava
The halacha follows Rav Yehuda. The obligation attaches to the Israelite's portion at the moment of slaughter, regardless of who co-owns the rest.
Practical Ruling An Israelite partner must calculate his ownership fraction and give the corresponding proportion of each of the three gifts to a Kohen.

👑 Exempt vs. Obligated Animals

Exempt from Matanot

  • Animals slaughtered for the royal table (melech's feast)
  • Consecrated animals — kodshim governed by Temple law
  • Animals with no private owner at time of slaughter
  • Wild animals (chayot) — Rabbinic disagreement; some hold exempt

Obligated in Matanot

  • Any privately owned chulin animal slaughtered in Israel
  • Animal slaughtered for a private wedding or feast
  • Israelite's portion in a Kohen–Israelite partnership
  • Animal bought specifically for personal consumption

📏 Step-by-Step: Fulfilling Matanot Kehuna

Slaughter: Perform valid shechita on a privately owned chulin animal intended for consumption
↓
Identify the three parts: Locate zero'a (right foreleg), lechayim (two cheeks with jawbone), and keiva (fourth stomach/maw)
↓
Separate physically: No verbal declaration is required — the act of separation fulfills the obligation
↓
Present to any Kohen: Even a tamei (ritually impure) Kohen or a Kohen who is a minor may receive — no purity requirement
↓
Kohen's discretion: No kedusha restrictions apply — the Kohen may eat, sell, feed to dogs, or otherwise use the gifts freely
