Chullin 21 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
Why must the Kohen NOT cut through the majority of the surrounding flesh when performing melikah on a bird Chatat?
For a bird Chatat, the sprinkling of blood is done while the head remains connected to the body. If the Kohen cut through most of the surrounding flesh, the head would separate — preventing the blood-sprinkling service from being performed properly. The connection of head to body is a specific requirement of the Chatat bird service.
Question 2 of 5
What does R' Elazar b. R' Shimon rule about how many simanim must be cut for a bird offering?
R' Elazar b. R' Shimon takes a stricter-than-standard position: for a bird sin offering, he requires one complete siman to be fully cut. The Rabbis require only the majority of one siman. This debate about "one" vs. "majority of one" reflects precise analysis of the Torah's language regarding the offering's service.
Question 3 of 5
Shmuel rules that the law of broken neck bone with most flesh cut applies to a person. What is the halachic consequence?
Shmuel extends Ze'eiri's animal law to humans: a person whose neck bone is broken and most surrounding flesh is severed is legally dead for tumah purposes — they impart tumah like a corpse even while still breathing. The halacha's definition of death is physiological viability, not the presence of breath alone.
Question 4 of 5
According to the Rabbis, how many simanim must be cut for valid shechitah of a bird (not a Temple offering)?
The Rabbis rule that for a bird, one siman (either the windpipe or the gullet) must be cut — and specifically the majority of that one siman. This is a leniency compared to animals (which require majority of both simanim). The Mishna (coming in Daf 27) will codify this rule explicitly.
Question 5 of 5
What is the difference in the treatment of the head between a bird Chatat and a bird Olah?
The key difference reflects their respective Temple services. The Chatat's blood must be sprinkled while the head is still connected — so the flesh is preserved to keep head and body joined. The Olah is entirely burned on the altar — both head and body are offered — so complete severing is permitted and the head is processed separately.
