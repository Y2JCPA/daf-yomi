🐦 How to Pinch a Bird Chatat (Sin Offering)

The Baraita teaches: For a bird Chatat, the Kohen cuts the spinal column and neck bone WITHOUT cutting a majority of the surrounding flesh. Why? To keep the head attached while sprinkling blood — the head and body must remain connected.

Bird Chatat (Sin Offering)

  • 💅 Kohen pinches from the nape
  • 🔗 Head stays attached to body
  • 🩸 Blood sprinkled while connected
  • 1️⃣ Only one siman cut (per R' Elazar)

Bird Olah (Burnt Offering)

  • 💅 Kohen pinches from the nape
  • ✂️ Head may be fully severed
  • 🔥 Head and body both burned
  • 2️⃣ Both simanim cut (per Rabbis)

⚖️ How Many Simanim for a Bird?

Rb
The Rabbis: For a bird, one must cut two simanim (windpipe and gullet). "Two" means both must be cut — same as an animal.
RE
R' Elazar b. R' Shimon: For a bird sin offering, only one siman needs to be cut — derived from the word "majority of two" in one source meaning one full siman.
The Gemara's resolution: When the baraita says "two" it follows the Rabbis (both simanim). When it says "majority of two" it follows R' Elazar (one complete siman). These are not contradictory — they reflect different opinions within the same text.

🦴 Broken Neck Bone — Applies to Humans!

Ze'eiri: Animal with broken neck bone + majority of flesh cut = treifah
↓
Rav Yehuda says in the name of Shmuel: The same rule applies to a PERSON
↓
A person whose neck bone is broken and most surrounding flesh is severed = legally dead for tumah purposes
↓
That person imparts tumah as a corpse — even if still breathing!
The parallel: The Torah's definition of viability is consistent across animals and humans. An organism that cannot survive — even if still showing signs of life — is treated as if already dead for halachic purposes. This applies to both tumah law and treifah law.

📜 R' Yishmael's Derivation

RI
"According to the ordinance" — this phrase links the bird sin offering to the bird burnt offering. Just as the burnt offering requires both simanim cut, so too the sin offering.
RE
R' Elazar b. R' Shimon: The link works differently — the sin offering teaches the burnt offering, not the reverse. The sin offering (one siman) shows the minimum; the burnt offering requires that minimum too.
The phrase "according to the ordinance" appears in the Torah connecting different bird offering types. The Sages debate which is the model and which is the application — determining whether the standard flows up (from lenient to strict) or down (strict to lenient).
