⚖️ How Much Gid to Be Liable?

Tanna Kamma
One who eats the gid ha-nashe is liable only if he eats a k'zayit (olive's bulk). This is the standard shiur for food prohibitions.
Rabbi Yehuda
The gid is chashuv — significant in its own right. Even eating mashehu (any amount at all) from the gid incurs lashes, because its prohibition comes from its identity, not its bulk.
Gemara
The dispute hinges on whether the gid is treated like other forbidden foods (requiring k'zayit) or like distinct prohibited objects (e.g., shkotzim) where any amount triggers liability.
Core Question Is gid ha-nashe prohibited because of what it is (intrinsic, identity-based) or because of what it is eaten (quantity-based)? The answer determines the shiur for lashes.

🦴 The Gid HaNashe — Background

עַל כֵּן לֹא יֹאכְלוּ בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת גִּיד הַנָּשֶׁה (בראשית לב:לג)
Foundational Prohibition The gid ha-nashe (sciatic nerve) is forbidden by Torah law (Bereishit 32:33), commemorating Jacob's injury. It applies to both the hindquarters of pure (kosher) and impure (non-kosher) animals — a point debated extensively on this daf.

🐄 Gid from a Non-Kosher Animal — Two Prohibitions or One?

Rabbi Yochanan
If one eats the gid ha-nashe of a neveilah (improperly slaughtered animal), he is liable for two sets of lashes: one for gid ha-nashe and one for neveilah. These are distinct prohibitions and do not merge.
Reish Lakish
He is liable for only one set of lashes. The neveilah prohibition subsumes the gid prohibition — the gid is already prohibited as part of the neveilah, so it adds nothing new.
Abaye
Rabbi Yochanan's view follows the principle of chavilot chavilot — each prohibition stands independently, and a person can receive separate lashes for each distinct biblical transgression committed in a single act.
Halakhic Principle Can a single act of eating violate two separate negative commandments simultaneously, triggering double lashes? This depends on whether the prohibitions are truly independent.

🫙 Does Gid Impart Flavor to the Pot?

Gid ha-nashe falls into a pot of permitted meat while cooking
↓
Yes — it imparts flavor
The entire pot becomes forbidden. Gid, like other prohibited substances, transmits its forbidden status through taste (ta'am k'ikar).
No — it does not impart flavor
The pot remains permitted. Gid is sinew, not meat; it has no real taste to transmit.
↓
Practical ruling: The gid is removed and the pot's status depends on whether the ratio allows bitul (nullification) and whether flavor was actually imparted

⚖️ Shiur Comparison: Gid vs. Other Prohibitions

Gid HaNashe

  • Tanna Kamma: k'zayit required
  • Rabbi Yehuda: mashehu (any amount)
  • Dispute: is it quantity- or identity-based?
  • Applies to kosher and non-kosher animals

Neveilah / Treifah

  • Standard shiur: k'zayit
  • Applies only to kosher species
  • Lashes for eating minimum amount
  • Gid of neveilah — double prohibition debate

Sheretz / Non-Kosher Creatures

  • Mashehu — any amount incurs lashes
  • Prohibition is identity-based, not quantity
  • Classic case of non-bulk prohibition
  • Contrast with food-based prohibitions

🔢 How the Gemara Derives the Shiur for Gid

Starting point: All food prohibitions generally require a k'zayit to incur Torah lashes — based on the verse linking eating to prohibition.
↓
Exception raised: Sheretz (creeping creatures) incurs lashes for any amount — because the prohibition attaches to the object itself, not to the act of eating a quantity.
↓
Question applied to gid: Is gid more like neveilah (quantity-based prohibition, needs k'zayit) or more like sheretz (identity-based, any amount)?
↓
Rabbi Yehuda's position: Gid is chashuv — it is a distinct forbidden entity. Like sheretz, mashehu suffices. The Torah forbade the gid as an object, not merely as food.
↓
Practical outcome: The halakha is disputed; one should be stringent and avoid even a mashehu of gid, while the standard shiur for lashes follows the majority Tannaic view of k'zayit.
