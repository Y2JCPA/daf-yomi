🗣️ Rav vs. Shmuel — Does Soft Hide Join with Flesh?

Rav
The soft hide of a neveilah joins together with the flesh to complete a kezayit for tumah. Since the hide is still considered food, it counts toward the minimum shiur.
Shmuel
The hide does not join with the flesh. Each type of ochel stands on its own — they cannot be combined unless they are the same category of food substance.
Practical Ruling The halacha follows Rav: soft hide may combine with flesh of the same neveilah to form the kezayit required to convey tumah.

⚖️ Shiurim for Tumah haOchlin — Key Measurements

Kezayit כַּזַּיִת

  • Minimum amount of neveilah flesh to convey tumah
  • Required for most food-based tumah transmission
  • Soft hide + flesh may combine to reach this amount
  • ~Half an egg's volume (approx. 28–30cc)

Beitzah בֵּיצָה

  • Full egg volume — stricter shiur for some tumah types
  • Required when the food source is tevel or certain other categories
  • Two kezeitim together = approximately one beitzah
  • Used also for Shabbat and other halachic contexts

Mashehu מַשֶּׁהוּ

  • Any amount — applies to terumah tumah in specific cases
  • Rabbinic stringency beyond Torah minimum
  • Not the standard rule for regular ochel tumah
  • Reflects special sanctity of priestly portions

🤝 The Yad (Handle) Principle

יָד מִצְטָרֶפֶת לְטוּמְאָה וְאֵינָהּ מִצְטָרֶפֶת לְשִׁיעוּר
Mishnaic Rule A yad (handle) is a part of a food that is inedible itself but attached to an edible portion — such as a bone protruding from meat or a stem attached to fruit. The yad is treated as part of the food for purposes of tumah: one who touches the yad is considered to have touched the ochel. However, the yad does not count toward the kezayit measurement itself — only the edible portion does. A shomar (guard), which protects the food (like a nutshell), similarly conveys tumah when touched but does not add to the shiur.

💧 Hechsher Tumah — The 7-Liquid Requirement

Step 1 — The food must be detached: Produce or meat still attached to the ground or carcass cannot receive tumah. It must be fully separated.
↓
Step 2 — Contact with one of 7 liquids: The food must be wetted by water (mayim), dew (tal), wine (yayin), oil (shemen), blood (dam), milk (chalav), or bee honey (devash) — י״ד שֶׁמֶן וְדָם
↓
Step 3 — The wetting must be desired: The owner must have wanted the liquid contact (ki yitten — כִּי יִתֵּן). Unwanted wetting does not create susceptibility.
↓
Step 4 — Exposure to a source of tumah: After hechsher, the food comes into contact with a tamei person or object of sufficient tumah level.
↓
Step 5 — The food is now tamei: It can now convey tumah to other foods, to a person's hands, or to vessels — depending on the level of tumah it received.
