טַעַם כְּעִיקָּר — הַטַּעַם אָסוּר כִּגּוּף הָאִיסּוּר עַצְמוֹ
📖 Ta'am K'Ikar — Taste Is Equivalent to Substance One of the most foundational principles in hilchot ta'arovot (mixture laws) is established here: the flavor (ta'am) imparted by a forbidden food is treated as equivalent to the forbidden food itself (k'ikar = the substance).

If gid hanasheh is cooked in a pot of permitted meat, even if the gid itself is removed, the broth and meat that absorbed its taste are forbidden. The Torah does not distinguish between eating the forbidden item and tasting its flavor — both constitute a violation.

Source: Derived from the Nazir (Bamidbar 6:4) — the Torah forbids a Nazir even grape seeds and grape skin, which themselves are not wine but merely give taste to it.

📜 Is Ta'am K'Ikar Biblical or Rabbinic?

R' Yochanan
The prohibition of ta'am k'ikar is d'oraita — biblical. The proof is from the Nazir: the Torah explicitly prohibits grape seeds and skin (Bamidbar 6:4), which are mere flavor-givers. We derive that anywhere forbidden flavor is imparted, the Torah treats it as eating the substance itself.
Resh Lakish
I agree it applies broadly, but the Nazir case may be a chiddush (novel stringency) specific to the Nazir's vow — not necessarily a general biblical rule. One could argue ta'am k'ikar in other contexts is d'rabbanan (rabbinic), and the Nazir verse cannot be extended universally.
Halacha L'Ma'aseh The accepted ruling follows R' Yochanan: ta'am k'ikar is d'oraita. One who eats a mixture where forbidden flavor was imparted transgresses a biblical prohibition.

🔀 Min B'Mino vs. Min B'She'eino Mino

Min B'Mino — Same Species

  • Forbidden item is the same species as the permitted food
  • Example: non-kosher meat mixed into kosher meat
  • Both taste identical — a taster cannot detect the forbidden item
  • Since ta'am cannot distinguish them, you cannot rely on rov (majority)
  • Requires full bitul b'shishim (60:1 ratio) to be permitted
  • Some hold: min b'mino is never batel by taste — only by strict count

Min B'She'eino Mino — Different Species

  • Forbidden item is a different species from permitted food
  • Example: non-kosher fish cooked into chicken broth
  • A chakham (expert taster) can potentially detect the foreign flavor
  • Standard rule: forbidden item nullified if 60× or more present
  • If detectable flavor remains even with 60× → mixture is still forbidden
  • If no detectable flavor and 60× present → permitted

🧪 How to Rule on a Ta'arovet: Step-by-Step

Step 1 — Identify the issur: What is the forbidden item? Is it d'oraita (biblical) or d'rabbanan (rabbinic)? Biblical issurim are treated more stringently.
↓
Step 2 — Check for cooking/heat: Was the forbidden item cooked together with the permitted food in a hot pot (kli rishon)? Heat transfers flavor.
↓
Step 3 — Ta'am k'ikar test: If cooked together and flavor was imparted → the mixture is forbidden regardless of ratio. Remove what you can; consult a posek.
↓
Step 4 — Count the ratio: If no flavor was imparted, measure the ratio of permitted to forbidden. Is there 60 times the volume of the forbidden item?
↓
Step 5 — Min b'mino check: Is the forbidden item the same species as the permitted food? If so, rov alone is insufficient — you must have 60:1. If yes to 60:1 → permitted. If not → forbidden.
