📖 Overview

חולין קט ב
🔑 The Great Principle of Chullin 109b The Talmud declares: כָּל דַּאֲסַר לַן רַחֲמָנָא שְׁרָא לַן כְּוָותֵיהּ — "Everything the Merciful One forbade us, He permitted us something equivalent." Four striking examples pair each major prohibition with a permitted analog, revealing that the Torah never leaves one truly deprived — Divine wisdom balances every restriction with a corresponding leniency.

🦴 Gid HaNashe: Which Thigh Is Forbidden?

Rav
The gid hanashe prohibition applies to the right thigh only. The angel struck Yaakov on one side — the primary, dominant side. Scripture directs our attention there.
Shmuel
Both thighs are forbidden. The verse 'therefore the Children of Israel do not eat the gid hanashe' does not specify a side — and where the Torah is silent, we must apply stringency.
Rav
The narrative of Bereishit is our guide — the angel touched only one hollow. This is the textual and logical basis for limiting the prohibition.
Shmuel
Narrative is descriptive, not prescriptive. The halachah must cast a wider net — both thighs carry the prohibition, and that is the established practice.
הלכה למעשה The accepted ruling follows Shmuel: both the right and left gid hanashe are forbidden in all kosher animals.

🔄 The Four Forbidden–Permitted Pairs

❌ Forbidden by Torah

  • 🩸 Dam (blood) — forbidden to consume in any form
  • 🫙 Chelev — forbidden internal organ fat
  • 🐷 Chazir (pork) — non-kosher animal, entirely forbidden
  • 🚫 Niddah — marital relations during impurity period

✅ Permitted Parallel

  • 🫀 Liver — resembles blood, releases blood, yet fully permitted when broiled
  • 🐑 Shumet — tail fat of sheep, similar texture, entirely permitted
  • 🐟 Shibuta brain — kosher fish whose brain tastes like pork
  • 💍 Besulah blood — hymen blood of virgin bride, halachically permitted

🐟 The Shibuta Fish: Pork's Kosher Twin

The Problem: The Torah forbids eating chazir (pig). Pork's distinctive taste was considered uniquely desirable in the ancient world — a significant prohibition.
↓
The Divine Principle: The Gemara teaches that God never creates a prohibition without providing a permitted equivalent — the Torah is generous even in its restrictions.
↓
The Shibuta Identified: The shibuta (likely a mullet, sheatfish, or similar river fish) possesses a brain whose flavor closely resembles that of pork.
↓
Kosher Status Confirmed: The shibuta has fins and scales — it is unambiguously a kosher fish. Its brain, therefore, is entirely permitted to eat.
↓
The Takeaway: A person who keeps kosher need not feel deprived of the pork experience — the Merciful One has provided a permitted analog. This exemplifies the Torah's balance of restriction and generosity.
