Chullin 33 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
What does Reish Lakish mean when he says the lung is "like it was placed in a basket" after the windpipe is cut?
Reish Lakish's metaphor: a basket protects items placed inside it. Once the windpipe is cut (first stage of shechitah), the lung is "protected" — whatever happens to it afterward (like a perforation) cannot make the animal a neveilah (unslaughtered carcass), because the slaughter process has already commenced. The animal is in the process of becoming kosher.
Question 2 of 5
For EATING the animal, how many simanim must be cut?
For eating purposes, everyone agrees: the majority of both simanim must be properly cut. Cutting only the first siman doesn't permit eating — the shechitah is not yet complete. The Gemara's unresolved question is specifically about neveilah-tumah (impurity status), not about whether one may eat the meat.
Question 3 of 5
Why does the Gemara treat the "eating" standard differently from the "tumah" standard?
Eating and tumah are two different legal axes. "May I eat this?" requires complete, valid shechitah. "Does this object convey neveilah-impurity?" asks when the animal stops being a potential neveilah. Partial shechitah might answer the tumah question differently than the eating question. The Sages treated each independently.
Question 4 of 5
What is the unresolved question (teiku) in Daf 33?
The unresolved dilemma: when the first siman is cut during shechitah, does this alone begin to remove the neveilah-tumah status from the foreleg? Or must both simanim be fully cut before ANY part of the animal loses its potential neveilah-tumah status? The Gemara leaves this as a teiku — an unanswered question.
Question 5 of 5
If non-sacred food is prepared at the purity level of sacrificial food, what happens when an impure person eats it?
Non-sacred food prepared at sacrificial purity level, when eaten by an impure person, transmits a stricter degree of impurity (first-degree) than food prepared at teruma level (second-degree). The preparation level "upgrades" the food's sensitivity to impurity. The Gemara debates the exact degrees, with Ulla and R' Yochanan offering different analyses.
