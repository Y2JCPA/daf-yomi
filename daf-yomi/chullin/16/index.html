⚙️ Slaughter with a Mechanism

A wheel with a knife attached — the wheel spins and the knife cuts the simanim
↓
Is this valid shechitah? Who is the "slaughterer"?
↓
↓
Human-powered wheel:
Valid — the person's force drives the knife
↓
Water-powered wheel:
Depends on whether the person initiated it or the water acts independently
Key issue: Shechitah requires human agency — "koach gavra" (a person's force). If the mechanism operates by human power, the slaughter is valid. If it operates by natural force (water, wind), it becomes the question of the next slide.

🌊 The Waterwheel Problem

Rv
Rava's dilemma: if a knife is attached to a waterwheel and the wheel turns by water power, cutting the animal's neck — is this valid?
RP
Rav Pappa compares it to murder: if someone diverts a stream of water onto a person and they drown, the diverter is liable — because he initiated the force!
Gm
But there's a difference: for murder, liability requires only causation. For shechitah, the requirement is active cutting — the person must be the one performing the act.
Rav Pappa's principle: Indirect causation through natural forces can create liability for damage — but may not satisfy the more specific requirement of shechitah, which needs direct human action on the knife.

🪨 Attached vs. Detached — The Core Rule

InstrumentStatusValid for Shechitah?
Regular knife (detached)Detached✅ Always valid
Flint emerging from a wallAttached to ground❌ Invalid (Mishna)
Reed growing from groundAttached to ground❌ Invalid
Knife embedded in wallDetached → reattached⚠️ Disputed (Rava's dilemma)
Detached flint/reedWas attached, now detached✅ Valid
The Mishna's rule: One may slaughter with anything — stone, glass, reed — as long as it is detached from the ground. The Torah says "and you shall slaughter" — implying a hand-held, movable instrument.

🗡️ Knife Embedded in a Wall

Rava's dilemma: a knife was originally detached but then embedded in a wall. What's its status?
↓
Option 1: "Detached and then attached" → follows its current status (attached) → invalid
Option 2: "Detached and then attached" → follows its original status (detached) → valid
↓
Baraita proof: "One embedded a knife in a wall and slaughtered with it — his slaughter is valid"
↓
Shmuel: Valid — but only if the animal's neck was moved across the knife (not the knife across the neck)

🌾 Parallel: Attached Water & Tumah

The Gemara draws a parallel between attached/detached instruments and attached/detached water for tumah susceptibility:

Water Attached to Ground

  • 🌊 Water in a stream or spring
  • 📋 Cannot render seeds susceptible to tumah
  • 🔗 Connected to its source — not "detached"

Water Detached from Ground

  • 💧 Water collected in a vessel
  • 📋 CAN render seeds susceptible to tumah
  • ✅ "Detached" = independent entity
The principle is the same: Both for shechitah instruments and for tumah susceptibility, "attached to the ground" has a specific halachic meaning that limits the item's capacity to produce halachic effects.

📖 Rav's Teaching from R' Chiyya

Rb
Rav sat behind R' Chiyya, and R' Chiyya sat before Rebbi (R' Yehuda HaNasi). Rebbi asked: "From where do we know that shechitah must be with a detached instrument?"
Rb
The verse says: "And you shall slaughter... and you shall eat" — implying a distinct act of cutting with a separate instrument, not tearing with something attached.
The chain of transmission: Rav → R' Chiyya → Rebbi. This daf gives us a glimpse of how Torah was transmitted in the beit midrash — students sitting behind their teachers, absorbing the discussions.
