5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
If one slaughters with a knife attached to a human-powered wheel, is the slaughter valid?
A human-powered mechanism is valid because the person's force (koach gavra) is what drives the knife. The wheel is merely a tool that transmits the person's strength. Shechitah requires human agency, and here the human is the active cause of the cutting.
Question 2 of 5
Why is slaughter with a flint naturally emerging from a wall invalid?
The Mishna rules that slaughter requires a detached instrument. A flint naturally part of a wall is "attached to the ground" (mechubar l'karka) and therefore invalid. The Torah says "and you shall slaughter" — implying a distinct, hand-held instrument. Once the flint is detached from the wall, it becomes valid.
Question 3 of 5
What does Shmuel rule about a knife embedded in a wall?
Shmuel validates slaughter with an embedded knife because the knife was originally detached. However, he requires that the animal be moved across the knife rather than the knife being moved across the animal. This way, the human acts directly on the animal while the knife serves as a stationary cutting edge.
Question 4 of 5
How does the concept of attached vs. detached apply to water and tumah?
The parallel is precise: just as a shechitah instrument must be detached from the ground, water must be "detached" (collected, no longer connected to its source) to render seeds susceptible to tumah. Water still flowing in a stream or spring is "attached to the ground" and cannot create tumah susceptibility for produce.
Question 5 of 5
What broader principle connects the instrument rules for shechitah?
The Gemara establishes that valid shechitah has two requirements: a qualified person performing the act, and a proper instrument that is detached from the ground. Together, these ensure that slaughter is an active, intentional human deed using a distinct tool — not a passive or natural process.
