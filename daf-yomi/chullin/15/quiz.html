Chullin 15 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
According to R' Yochanan HaSandlar, if one unwittingly cooked on Shabbat, who may eat the food and when?
R' Yochanan HaSandlar is the strictest opinion. For unwitting Shabbat cooking: others may eat it after Shabbat, but the person who cooked it may never eat it. For intentional cooking: no one may ever eat it. He holds that one should never benefit from a Shabbat violation.
Question 2 of 5
Why can't a metal lamp that was lit on Shabbat be moved even after it goes out?
The lighting was a deliberate action that set the lamp aside from all other uses for Shabbat. This is "muktzeh machmat issur" — set aside through a prohibited action. Even after the flame goes out, the lamp retains its muktzeh status for the remainder of Shabbat because it was actively designated as unmovable.
Question 3 of 5
What does "fit to be chewed" (ra'ui l'hitalech) mean in the context of nolad?
If food was already edible before Shabbat (even if raw), cooking it on Shabbat does not create nolad — the food already existed in an edible form. The prohibition of nolad applies only when something transforms from non-food into food (like a living animal being slaughtered into meat). "Fit to be chewed" means it was already food.
Question 4 of 5
According to the halacha, when one slaughters for a critically ill person on Shabbat, may healthy people eat?
The halacha follows Rav Dimi of Neharde'a: once the slaughter is permitted (for saving a life), the resulting meat is fully permitted. Since the act itself was halachically sanctioned, there is no penalty and no muktzeh issue. Healthy people may eat the meat on Shabbat itself.
Question 5 of 5
When moving permanently from one community to another with different customs, what is R' Zeira's view?
R' Zeira argues that the rule of keeping both communities' stringencies applies only to a visitor who plans to return. Someone who permanently relocates (as R' Zeira did from Babylonia to Eretz Yisrael) adopts the local custom of the new community entirely. This is why he followed the lenient Eretz Yisrael practice.
