R' Yochanan HaSandlar is strictest: Even unwitting cooking on Shabbat — the person who cooked may never eat it. Others may eat it, but only after Shabbat. His view: you should not benefit from a Shabbat violation, even an accidental one.

🔥 The Burning Lamp — A Muktzeh Lesson

Q
Why can't we move a metal lamp that was lit on Shabbat — even after it goes out? Metal lamps don't become repugnant like clay ones.
Gm
Because he "set it aside by direct action" — by lighting it, he actively designated it as unmovable for Shabbat. This is muktzeh machmat issur.
Q
But isn't this the same as slaughtering on Shabbat? The person "set aside" the animal by not designating it before Shabbat!
Gm
No — the lamp case involves an active deed (lighting). The animal was passively undesignated. Different categories of muktzeh.

📜 Rav Ashi's Resolution — Nolad

Why does R' Yehuda forbid Shabbat-slaughtered meat if the animal isn't actively "set aside"?
↓
Rav Ashi: R' Yehuda's reason is nolad — the meat is "newly created." Before Shabbat it was a living animal, not food.
↓
Nolad items are forbidden on Shabbat — they didn't exist in this form when Shabbat began
↓
Compare: broken pottery, squeezed juice — all "new" forms that didn't exist pre-Shabbat

🍖 "Fit to Be Chewed" — Cooked vs. Raw

Food That Was "Ready"

  • 🥩 Raw meat that can be chewed
  • 🥕 Vegetables edible raw
  • ✅ Not nolad — it was food before Shabbat
  • 📋 If cooked on Shabbat: forbidden only as a penalty, not as nolad

Food That Was "Not Ready"

  • 🐄 Living animal (not yet food)
  • 🌾 Raw grain still on the stalk
  • ❌ Becomes nolad when processed on Shabbat
  • 📋 Forbidden as a newly created entity
The distinction matters: If food was already edible (even raw), cooking it on Shabbat violates Shabbat but doesn't create nolad. But slaughtering an animal transforms a non-food into food — that's nolad.

🤒 Slaughter for the Ill — Expanded Rules

Critically ill person needs meat on Shabbat
↓
Slaughter permitted — pikuach nefesh
↓
May healthy people eat from that meat?
↓
Rav Pappa: If the ill person needs only a k'zayit, and the animal provides far more — healthy people may eat the rest
Rav Dimi: Once slaughter is permitted, the entire animal is permitted — healthy may eat freely
↓
Halacha: Permitted for healthy person on Shabbat — since the slaughter itself was sanctioned

🔄 Moving Between Communities

R' Zeira's case: When R' Zeira moved from Babylonia to Eretz Yisrael, he adopted the lenient local practice on certain slaughter questions — even though Babylonia was stricter.
The principle: When traveling from place to place, the Sages impose the stringencies of both the place you left and the place you arrived. But R' Zeira argues this applies only to someone who plans to return — not one who permanently relocated.
Application: Since R' Zeira moved permanently to Eretz Yisrael, he followed the local custom entirely. A visitor would have had to keep both sets of stringencies.
