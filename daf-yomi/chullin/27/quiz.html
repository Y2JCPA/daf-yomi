Chullin 27 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
What is the definitive rule stated in the Mishna for valid shechitah?
The Mishna states the foundational rule: a bird requires cutting the majority of one siman (either windpipe or gullet). An animal requires cutting the majority of both simanim (windpipe AND gullet). This distinction reflects the bird's smaller anatomy and the different derivations for each species' shechitah requirements.
Question 2 of 5
How does Rav Kahana derive that shechitah must be performed at the neck?
Rav Kahana connects the word "v'shachat" to the root "shach" meaning to bend or slope down. The neck is the place on the animal that bends downward. The Gemara challenges this (the tail also bends!) and responds that "shach" implies downward bending, which fits the neck — it slopes down from the head.
Question 3 of 5
What does the verse "This is the law of the animal and of the bird" teach about shechitah?
The juxtaposition works in both directions. From animal to bird: just as an animal avoids being a neveilah through shechitah, so must a bird have shechitah. From bird to animal: just as a bird offering's service involves the front of the neck, so is shechitah for an animal from the front of the neck (not the back/nape). Each species teaches the other.
Question 4 of 5
Why does the Torah list "the head" separately from "the pieces" in describing the burnt offering (Vayikra 1:8)?
The verse says "the pieces, the head, and the fat" — listing the head separately from the rest of the body parts. This implies the head was already separated from the body before the cutting into pieces began. Since the head is separated at slaughter, this proves that shechitah occurs at the neck (the junction of head and body).
Question 5 of 5
How does the blood-covering mitzvah prove that bird shechitah is a Torah-level obligation?
The Torah commands covering the blood after slaughter of birds and wild animals. If bird shechitah were merely Rabbinic (meaning you could also kill a bird by stabbing), why would the Torah specifically mandate covering blood after shechitah? The Torah's commandment to cover blood after slaughter implies that slaughter (shechitah) itself is a Torah-level requirement for birds.
