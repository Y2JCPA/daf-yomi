Chullin 44 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
Why did the Divine Voice (Bat Kol) declare that the halacha follows Beit Hillel?
The Gemara explains that the reason the halacha follows Beit Hillel is their humility (anavim). They would teach Beit Shammai's positions before their own, demonstrating intellectual honesty and respect for opposing views. The Talmud draws a direct line between character and authority: the more humble school earned the right to determine halacha.
Question 2 of 5
A person decides to follow the stringencies of BOTH Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel for maximum piety. What does the baraita say about this approach?
The baraita is clear: cherry-picking the stringencies of both schools is not piety but foolishness. The verse "the fool walks in darkness" is applied because such a person follows no coherent system of reasoning. True halachic observance means adopting a consistent legal framework — either Beit Shammai or Beit Hillel (and after the Bat Kol, Beit Hillel) — not constructing an artificial hybrid of maximally strict positions.
Question 3 of 5
The Gemara gives two resolutions for the contradiction between "halacha always follows Beit Hillel" and "one who wishes may follow Beit Shammai." What is the second, more radical resolution?
The second resolution is remarkable: even after the Bat Kol declared for Beit Hillel, one may still follow Beit Shammai — because Rabbi Yehoshua established the principle (in the famous Oven of Akhnai incident) that a Bat Kol cannot override the majority opinion of the Sages. Human halachic reasoning takes precedence over even a voice from Heaven. This is one of the Talmud's boldest statements about rabbinic authority.
Question 4 of 5
According to Rav Nachman, where on the gullet is the upper boundary for valid slaughter?
Rav Nachman teaches that slaughter is valid on the gullet up to the point near the jaw where only enough space for a hand-grip remains. Above that point, the area is too close to the jaw for proper slaughter. The Gemara also clarifies that the bottom handbreadth of the rumen (adjacent to the gullet) is considered "inner rumen" — meaning it's part of the stomach, not the gullet, for slaughter purposes.
Question 5 of 5
Shmuel rules that if the entrance of the gullet was completely detached from the jaw, the animal is kosher. What principle does this illustrate?
Shmuel's ruling illustrates a core treifah principle: the simanim (windpipe and gullet) being detached from their anchor point (the jaw) is not the same as the simanim being ripped apart themselves. Treifah from "ripping" requires the simanim themselves to be torn completely. Partial detachment, or detachment from the jaw while the simanim remain intact, does not render the animal treifah. The organ's own integrity matters more than its attachment.
