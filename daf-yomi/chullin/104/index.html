🍗 Poultry & Cheese at the Same Table

Beit Shammai
Poultry and cheese may not be brought to the same table together — it looks too much like mixing meat and milk. One must keep them entirely apart.
Beit Hillel
Poultry and cheese may be placed on the same table — the rabbinic prohibition covers eating them together, but sharing a table poses no real confusion with biblical basar b'chalav.
Gemara
Why is poultry with milk forbidden at all? The Torah says 'a kid in its mother's milk' — poultry has no mother's milk! The Rabbis made a gezeira (decree) lest one confuse poultry with actual flesh.
Halacha We follow Beit Hillel: poultry and cheese may share a table. Eating them together in the same bite/serving remains rabbinically forbidden.

🐟 Fish & Meat — Danger, Not Kashrut

כל הבשר אסור לבשל בחלב חוץ מבשר דגים וחגבים — אבל דג עם בשר אסור משום סכנה
⚠️ Sakanah The Gemara states that eating fish and meat together poses a physical danger (sakanah). This is not a basar b'chalav prohibition at all — fish and locusts are explicitly exempt from the meat-milk rules. Nevertheless, one must clean between eating fish and meat due to this health concern. Fish with milk, however, is fully permitted.

⏳ Mar Ukva and His Father — Two Levels of Piety

Mar Ukva's Father

  • Ate meat at one meal
  • Did not eat cheese until the following day
  • Waited a full day-cycle (24 hours) between meat and milk
  • Extreme piety — the most stringent practice recorded

Mar Ukva Himself

  • Ate meat at one meal
  • Would not eat cheese at the same meal
  • At a different meal — even same day — permitted
  • Said: "I am like vinegar son of wine" compared to his father

🔄 How the Rabbinic Layers Were Built

Torah foundation: "Do not boil a kid in its mother's milk" (Exodus 23:19) — applies to cattle and small livestock only
↓
Rabbinic Tier 1: Poultry extended into the prohibition as a gezeira — people might confuse it with biblical meat
↓
Rabbinic Tier 2: Dispute about the table (Beit Shammai vs. Hillel) — concern for marit ayin, appearances
↓
Waiting requirement: Not explicit in Torah — rabbinic derivation based on meat residue; Mar Ukva models meal-to-meal minimum
↓
Separate concern: Fish + meat forbidden not as dairy/meat but as sakanah — illustrates kashrut and health laws can overlap but remain distinct
