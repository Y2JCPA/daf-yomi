QUESTION 1 OF 5
What ratio does Rav Yehuda require to nullify a gid hanasheh that fell into a pot of broth?
A. Simple majority — more than 50%
B. 60 times the volume of the gid
C. 100 times the volume
D. No ratio can nullify a Torah prohibition
Correct: B — Rav Yehuda rules in the name of Rav that 60× the volume of the gid is required (bitul b'shishim). This is a rabbinic safeguard, even though the gid itself has no discernible flavor.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does the principle 'ein bo ta'am' mean in relation to the gid hanasheh?
A. The gid has a very pleasant taste
B. The gid imparts no discernible flavor to the mixture
C. The gid's prohibition is only rabbinic in origin
D. The gid cannot be seen in the mixture
Correct: B — Ein bo ta'am means the gid has no real flavor — it does not impart taste to the mixture. Since Torah prohibition of mixtures is based on flavor transfer (ta'am k'ikar), this creates a legal question about whether it should prohibit at all.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is 'chatichah na'aseit neveilah' and why is it halachically significant?
A. A piece of meat that becomes kosher when cooked
B. A forbidden piece that itself acquires neveilah status, raising the question of whether it counts toward its own 60× nullification
C. The process of removing the gid before cooking
D. A ruling permitting neveilah to be sold to non-Jews
Correct: B — Chatichah na'aseit neveilah means the forbidden piece acquires neveilah status once it falls in. The key debate is whether this piece may be counted in the 60× calculation — the stringent view says it cannot count toward its own nullification.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does the Gemara require 60× even for a flavorless substance like the gid hanasheh?
A. The gid actually has a strong flavor according to all Rishonim
B. The number 60 corresponds to the 60 tractates of the Mishnah
C. The Rabbis imposed 60× as a precautionary safeguard (chumra) to protect Torah-level prohibitions
D. Torah law itself mandates 60× for all forbidden foods
Correct: C — The Rabbis decreed 60× as a conservative safeguard even for flavorless forbidden items. Without this decree, leniency might spread to other Torah prohibitions where flavor is harder to assess.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the key difference between 'min b'mino' and 'min b'shelo mino' in halachic nullification?
A. Min b'mino requires majority; min b'shelo mino requires 60×
B. Min b'shelo mino (different kinds) can be assessed by taste and nullified by majority; min b'mino (same kind) cannot be taste-tested, so the Rabbis require 60×
C. Both categories always require 60× with no exceptions
D. Min b'mino is a Torah concept; min b'shelo mino is only rabbinic
Correct: B — When forbidden and permitted items are different kinds, a taster can detect the flavor — so majority suffices. When they are the same kind, taste cannot distinguish them, so the Rabbis required 60× as an objective threshold.
