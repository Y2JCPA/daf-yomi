⚔️ Does Gid HaNasheh Prohibit the Broth?

Rav Yehuda
If a gid hanasheh fell into a pot: if there is 60 times the volume of the gid in the broth, the broth is permitted. This is bitul b'shishim.
Questioner
But why require 60×? The gid has no flavor at all — it should be nullified even if a single gid fell into an entire ocean of broth!
Rav Yehuda
The 60× rule is a rabbinic safeguard. Even if the gid imparts no discernible taste, the Rabbis decreed 60× as a precaution against leniency with Torah prohibitions.
Rava
If we truly say ein bo ta'am — it has no flavor — then we should rule leniently! Why impose a ratio at all? The broth was never actually prohibited.
הלכה Rav Yehuda's ruling stands: 60× required even for flavorless gid — a rabbinic chumra to protect the Torah prohibition.

🥩 Chatichah Na'aseit Neveilah — Does the Piece Count Against Itself?

The Case

  • A piece of forbidden meat (neveilah) falls among kosher pieces
  • The forbidden piece is itself called 'chatichah na'aseit neveilah'
  • Question: does the forbidden piece count toward the 60× ratio needed to nullify it?

Stringent View

  • The forbidden piece CANNOT count toward its own nullification
  • You need 60× the forbidden piece, NOT including that piece itself
  • Reasoning: the issur cannot be complicit in its own bitul
  • Result: stricter calculations required

Lenient View

  • Once mixed, the piece joins the general mixture
  • Count ALL pieces including the formerly forbidden one
  • Once batel, it loses its individual forbidden status
  • Result: easier to reach the required 60×

👅 Ein Bo Ta'am — The Flavor Principle

טַעַם כְּעִיקָּר — כָּל שֶׁיֶּשׁ בּוֹ לְתֵת טַעַם, אָסוּר; וְגִיד הַנָּשֶׁה — אֵין בּוֹ טַעַם
יסוד הלכתי The Torah's mechanism for forbidden mixtures is ta'am k'ikar — flavor is legally equivalent to the substance itself. If a forbidden food imparts its flavor to a mixture, the mixture becomes forbidden. Conversely, if a substance has ein bo ta'am (no flavor), it arguably should not prohibit a mixture at all. The Gemara applies this to the gid hanasheh — the sciatic nerve imparts virtually no flavor — yet the Rabbis still required 60× as a hedge against leniency. This tension between logical Torah analysis and rabbinic conservatism drives the entire sugya.
