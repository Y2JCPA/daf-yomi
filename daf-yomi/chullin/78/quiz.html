QUESTION 1 OF 5
To which animals does the biblical prohibition of oto v'et beno apply according to the Mishnah and Gemara on Chullin 78?
A. All animals, both domesticated and wild, without distinction
B. Only wild animals such as deer and gazelle (chayot)
C. Domesticated kosher animals — ox, sheep, and goat (behemot)
D. Only animals consecrated for Temple offerings
Correct: C — Leviticus 22:28 specifies 'ox, sheep, or goat,' limiting the biblical prohibition to these domesticated animals. Wild animals (chayot) are debated but are not clearly included at the biblical level.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the Mishnah rule that oto v'et beno applies to females but not males?
A. Males are ritually exempt from all slaughter-related prohibitions
B. The mother-offspring relationship is identifiable through the female line; paternity cannot be tracked
C. The verse uses feminine grammatical forms throughout
D. Males are typically used for Temple offerings and receive separate rules
Correct: B — We can identify the mother of an offspring, but we cannot reliably identify which male fathered it. Since the prohibited relationship (dam and young) must be verifiable, the prohibition tracks the maternal line only.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
If a seller slaughters a mother animal and then sells the offspring to a buyer, may the buyer slaughter the offspring that same day?
A. No — once any slaughter in the pair occurs, all persons are forbidden that day
B. Yes — the buyer did not perform the first slaughter and bears no restriction
C. Only if the transaction was completed before noon
D. Only if the buyer was unaware of the first slaughter
Correct: B — The prohibition attaches to the person who performed the first slaughter. The buyer played no role in that slaughter, so he is not bound by the restriction. The seller, however, may not slaughter the other animal that day.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the legal consequence of intentionally violating oto v'et beno?
A. The second slaughter is invalid and the meat is forbidden (neveilah)
B. The offender pays a monetary fine to the injured party
C. Lashes (malkot) are administered, but the slaughter remains valid
D. Karet (excision) because it involves disrespect for a Torah commandment
Correct: C — Violating oto v'et beno incurs lashes (malkot) if done intentionally. Crucially, the slaughter itself is still valid — the meat is permitted. The transgression is in the act, not the product.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why might a ben pekuah (fetus found in a slaughtered animal) stand outside the normal oto v'et beno framework?
A. Ben pekuah is considered a non-kosher animal and no prohibitions apply
B. The ben pekuah never underwent standard shechita itself, so it may not trigger or be subject to the oto v'et beno chain
C. Ben pekuah is treated identically to any other animal for all halachic purposes
D. The Gemara rules that ben pekuah and its offspring are explicitly forbidden on the same day
Correct: B — Since the ben pekuah's permissibility derives from its mother's slaughter rather than its own, it exists in a unique halachic category. The Gemara debates whether it falls within the oto v'et beno framework at all, suggesting it and its offspring may be slaughtered on the same day.
