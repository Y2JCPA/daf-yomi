📜 Biblical Source & Mishnah Ruling Leviticus 22:28 prohibits slaughtering a mother and its offspring on the same day. The Mishnah (beginning of Chapter 5) rules: the prohibition applies to females only — because only the maternal relationship is reliably identifiable. One cannot know which male fathered which offspring, so male animals are not subject to the restriction.

🦌 Are Wild Animals (Chayot) Included?

Tanna Kamma
The verse specifies 'ox, sheep, or goat' — domesticated animals (בהמות טהורות). Wild animals such as deer and gazelle are excluded from the biblical prohibition of oto v'et beno.
R. Shimon b. Yochai
The compassionate purpose behind oto v'et beno — sparing the mother from witnessing her offspring's slaughter — applies equally to wild animals. The prohibition should extend to chayot.
Gemara
The sugya examines whether 'shor o seh' is exhaustive or exemplary. If the prohibition's rationale is compassion (צער בעלי חיים), logic suggests extending it; if purely textual, chayot are excluded.
Conclusion The halacha follows that chayot are not subject to oto v'et beno at the biblical level; the prohibition applies only to the three domesticated species explicitly mentioned in the verse.

⚖️ Buyer vs. Seller — Who Is Forbidden?

Seller Slaughters First

  • Seller slaughters the mother (or offspring)
  • Sells the other animal to a buyer
  • Buyer wishes to slaughter it that same day
  • ✅ Buyer is PERMITTED — he did not perform the first slaughter
  • Seller bears the restriction for that calendar day

Buyer Slaughters First

  • Buyer acquires both mother and offspring
  • Slaughters one of them during the day
  • Now wishes to slaughter the other same day
  • ❌ Buyer is FORBIDDEN — he performed the first slaughter
  • Proceeding = lashes (מלקות)

Neither Knew

  • Two people each unaware of the other's slaughter
  • Each slaughters one of a dam-offspring pair same day
  • Second slaughterer may be exempt from lashes
  • Requires knowledge (ידיעה) for full biblical liability
  • Halachic debate on unintentional violation

🍼 Ben Pekuah and Oto V'et Beno

Ben Pekuah Defined: A live fetus found inside an animal after slaughter — it never underwent its own shechita
↓
Basic Ruling: The mother's shechita covers the fetus; the ben pekuah may be eaten without separate ritual slaughter
↓
Question for Oto V'et Beno: If a ben pekuah grows up and has offspring, may they both be slaughtered on the same day?
↓
The Issue: Since the ben pekuah itself was never 'slaughtered' in the standard sense, is it considered part of the oto v'et beno chain?
↓
Analysis: The Gemara examines whether 'slaughter' (שחיטה) as a triggering act for oto v'et beno applies where the animal's permissibility derived from another's shechita
↓
Implication: Ben pekuah may stand outside the normal oto v'et beno framework, making it and its offspring permissible to slaughter on the same day
