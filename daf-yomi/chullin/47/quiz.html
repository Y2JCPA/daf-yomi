Chullin 47 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
According to Rava, what is the normal lobe configuration of a kosher animal's lung?
Rava teaches: the lung has five lobes (unnei). When the animal hangs by its legs facing the inspector, three lobes are on the right and two on the left. If a lobe is missing, extra (in most positions), or switched (2 right, 3 left), the animal is treifah. Mareimar later ruled that an extra lobe in line with the others is kosher, overriding Rava on that point.
Question 2 of 5
What is the "rose lobe" (vardah), and why is it significant?
When Rav Ashi was about to declare an animal treifah due to an extra lobe between the two sides, Rav Huna Mar bar Avya corrected him: butchers knew this extra lobe as the "little rose lobe" — it's a normal anatomical variant found in many grazing animals. The key distinction: it must be on the inner face (facing the heart) to be kosher. On the back of the lung, even a tiny extra lobe renders the animal treifah.
Question 3 of 5
A lung that is green like a leek is ruled kosher. What evidence does the Gemara bring for this?
R' Natan traveled to Cappadocia and met a woman whose two sons had died from circumcision. Her third baby was green (pale) — indicating insufficient blood. R' Natan told her to wait until "his blood enters him." The baby recovered after circumcision. The Gemara uses this real-world pediatric case as evidence that green is a recoverable condition — and therefore a green lung is kosher.
Question 4 of 5
If a lung has completely liquefied and can be poured like water, under what condition is the animal still kosher?
R' Yochanan rules that internal deficiency is not considered a deficiency for treifah purposes — what matters is the structural framework. Rava adds a critical condition: the bronchi must still exist. To verify: pour the liquefied lung into a clear glazed vessel. White streaks in the liquid = dissolved bronchi = treifah. No white streaks = bronchi intact = kosher.
Question 5 of 5
What does the distinction between internal and external deficiencies teach us about the treifah system?
The key principle emerging from daf 47 is that treifah is about structural function, not appearance. A lung can dissolve internally, change color, or lose tissue — and still be kosher if its membranes and bronchi are intact. Conversely, a tiny perforation in the membrane can render an otherwise healthy-looking lung treifah. The system cares about the organ's ability to sustain the animal's life, not how it looks.
