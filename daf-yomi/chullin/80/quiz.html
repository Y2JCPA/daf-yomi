QUESTION 1 OF 5
What is the direct biblical source for the prohibition of oto v'et bno?
A. Deuteronomy 22:6 — the law of sending away the mother bird
B. Leviticus 22:28 — do not slaughter it and its offspring on the same day
C. Exodus 23:19 — do not boil a kid in its mother's milk
D. Numbers 19:2 — the red heifer purification ritual
Correct: B — Leviticus 22:28 states explicitly: 'An ox or a sheep — it and its offspring — you shall not slaughter on the same day.' This verse is the direct source for oto v'et bno, distinct from the milk-and-meat prohibition (Exodus 23:19) and the bird's nest law (Deuteronomy 22:6).
QUESTION 2 OF 5
A seller sold both a cow and its calf on the same day but cannot remember which sale came first. What is the halacha for the seller regarding the remaining animal?
A. The seller may slaughter freely — the prohibition transferred to the buyers upon sale
B. The seller may not slaughter the remaining animal that day due to the doubt
C. The seller must wait 24 hours from the first sale, then may slaughter
D. There is no restriction since the animals are now in others' possession
Correct: B — Since the seller knows a parent-offspring relationship exists between the two animals, the doubt about which was sold first creates a safek that binds the seller. The seller cannot be certain that slaughtering the remaining animal would not complete an oto v'et bno violation, so it is forbidden that day.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Regarding a tumtum (animal with undetermined sex) and oto v'et bno, what is the Gemara's ruling on malkot?
A. Full malkot — tumtum is treated as definitively male for all halachic purposes
B. No malkot — gender uncertainty creates a safek regarding the Torah-level prohibition
C. Double malkot — we are stringent on both possible genders
D. Malkot only if the tumtum is subsequently found to be male upon examination
Correct: B — The Torah uses specific gendered language in the oto v'et bno prohibition. Since a tumtum's sex is entirely unresolved, there is genuine doubt about whether the full Torah-level prohibition applies. This safek bars malkot. However, a rabbinic stringency still prohibits the act l'chatchila.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
If two buyers each purchase one animal from a known mother-calf pair, and both slaughter their animals on the same day, who receives malkot?
A. Both buyers receive malkot since both participated in the same-day violation
B. Only the buyer who purchased the mother is liable
C. The second buyer to slaughter receives malkot; if simultaneous, neither does
D. The seller is liable since he enabled the situation by selling both on the same day
Correct: C — The oto v'et bno violation is completed only by the second slaughter. Therefore the second buyer is the one who violated the prohibition and receives malkot (assuming prior warning). If both slaughtered simultaneously, the sequence cannot be determined and certainty is lacking — so neither receives malkot, though the prohibition was still violated.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Which of the following scenarios does NOT trigger the prohibition of oto v'et bno at all?
A. A person slaughters the mother animal and then the offspring on the same day
B. A person slaughters the offspring and then the mother on the same day
C. One animal is slaughtered and the other dies of natural causes on the same day
D. A person is warned and then slaughters both animals simultaneously
Correct: C — The verse uses the language 'lo tishchatu' — 'you shall not slaughter.' Only the act of shechita (ritual slaughter) on both animals triggers the prohibition. If one animal is slaughtered and the other dies of natural causes, illness, or accident, there is no second act of shechita and therefore no violation of oto v'et bno.
