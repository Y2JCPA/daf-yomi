Chullin 28 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
According to the majority view (Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi), is bird shechitah a Torah obligation or a Rabbinic enactment?
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds that bird shechitah is a Torah obligation, derived from "as I have commanded you" — implying God taught Moshe detailed shechitah laws at Sinai, including for birds. R' Elazar HaKappar is the lone dissenting voice, holding it is Rabbinic. The blood-covering mitzvah supports the majority view.
Question 2 of 5
For a bird, which siman(im) must be cut for valid shechitah?
Rav Nachman rules that for a bird, cutting either the windpipe OR the gullet is sufficient. The Mishna says "one siman" without specifying which, implying either one is valid. This is a leniency specific to birds; animals require both simanim to be cut.
Question 3 of 5
A duck is brought whose windpipe might have been severed before shechitah. Why can't we simply slaughter and then examine?
The concern is that the slaughterer might cut exactly where the windpipe was already severed. After the cut, you'd see a severed windpipe and be unable to tell whether it was already cut before shechitah (making it neveilah) or if the shechitah itself severed it (making it kosher). The evidence is destroyed by the very act you're testing.
Question 4 of 5
What is Rav Yosef b. Rava's solution to the duck problem?
Rav Yosef b. Rava's elegant solution: examine the windpipe from the outside before making any cut. A prior severance of the windpipe is visible externally (through the skin/tissue). Once you confirm the windpipe is intact, you can proceed with shechitah confidently. This preserves the evidence before the cut destroys it.
Question 5 of 5
R' Yehuda requires cutting the jugular veins for valid bird shechitah. According to Rav Chisda, what does "cutting" the veins actually mean?
The Gemara clarifies that R' Yehuda's "cutting" means puncturing the veins at the moment of slaughter — not severing them separately. The veins are nicked as part of the shechitah action itself, causing blood to flow immediately during the process. This ensures proper blood drainage, which is particularly important for birds according to R' Yehuda.
