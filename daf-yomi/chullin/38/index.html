Chullin 38

חולין לח
Defining "convulsion": when does a post-slaughter movement prove the animal was alive? Rav, Shmuel, Rav Chisda, and Rava debate timing and type

🎯 The Core Question: What Counts as a "Convulsion"?

Central Debate

From Daf 37, we know that post-slaughter convulsion can validate the slaughter of a near-death animal. But exactly what kind of movement counts, and when during the slaughter does it need to occur?

This daf features a four-way dispute among Amoraim, each with different standards for timing and type of movement.

🐄 The Four Amora Positions on Timing

Core Machloket

Rav

Convulsion at any point during slaughter is valid. Even mid-slaughter movement proves life.

Rav Chisda

Convulsion must occur at the conclusion of slaughter — movement only at the end counts

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak

Convulsion even at the beginning of slaughter counts — the most lenient timing position

Rava

Convulsion must be at the conclusion of slaughter — same as Rav Chisda but with different reasoning

🔊 Does Lowing (Mooing) Count?

Specific Case

Shmuel rules: if an animal lows (makes sound), excretes excrement, or wiggles its ear during slaughter — these count as valid convulsions.

However, Rabbi Yosei quotes Rabbi Meir: lowing is NOT a convulsion if the throat muscles are already severed, since the sound then comes from air forced out mechanically, not from living muscular effort.

"If it lows, or excreted excrement, or wiggled its ear during the slaughter, that is a convulsion."

The Gemara reconciles: lowing counts only when the vocal cords are still intact — then it indicates real muscular life. Once the neck is cut through, lowing is merely mechanical.

🦵 What Movements Are "Death-Engenders"?

Rav Anan from Shmuel

Shmuel taught: certain movements are actually caused by death itself — they look like life signs but are actually death spasms:

  • Foreleg was bent, and the animal straightened it = death-engendered (NOT a life sign)
  • Foreleg was extended, and the animal bent it = a sign of LIFE ✅

The logic: a dying animal's muscles relax. A bent limb naturally straightens as muscles lose tension. But bending an extended limb requires active muscle contraction — only possible while alive.

⏱️ Rav Chisda: Only Conclusion Movements Count

Rav Chisda's Proof

Rav Chisda holds: only a convulsion at the conclusion of slaughter is a valid life sign. His proof from the Mishna:

The Mishna says a small animal that "extends its foreleg and does not restore it — the slaughter is INVALID." This implies the extension happened at the conclusion (when the slaughter was complete) — and it still wasn't enough. So only at-conclusion movements matter, and they must be restorative.

Rava agrees with this conclusion but via different reasoning from a verse about a bull.

🌅 Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak: Beginning Counts Too

Lenient View

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak disagrees — a convulsion even at the beginning of slaughter is a valid life indicator.

His proof: Rabbi Shimon's case (from Daf 37) of blood spurting to the walls overnight. Blood spurting with force at the beginning of slaughter would also demonstrate life.

The Gemara pushes back: perhaps spurting is qualitatively different from limb convulsion and can't prove what regular convulsions prove about timing. The debate remains unresolved.

📌 Practical Summary

Bottom Line
  • Post-slaughter movements can validate slaughter of a near-death animal
  • Timing dispute: Rav (any time) vs. Rav Chisda/Rava (conclusion only) vs. Rav Nachman (beginning also fine)
  • Type: bending an extended limb = life sign; straightening a bent limb = death spasm
  • Lowing counts only if neck is still intact (real muscular movement); not if mechanically produced by air
  • The standard halakhic ruling follows that conclusion-of-slaughter convulsions are required (Rav Chisda/Rava)
