QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Beit Hillel, why is bird meat cooked in milk only a rabbinic prohibition?
A. Birds are tamei (impure) and excluded from all dietary laws
B. Birds produce no mother's milk, so the biblical verse 'do not cook in its mother's milk' cannot apply
C. The Sages explicitly exempted birds in a special synod decree
D. Birds are considered ownerless (hefker) and not subject to ownership-based laws
Correct: B — Beit Hillel reasons that since birds do not nurse their young with milk, the biblical verse referencing 'mother's milk' is inapplicable by definition. The prohibition is therefore a rabbinic fence to prevent confusion with the d'oraita prohibition.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What makes a pot 'ben yomo,' and why does it matter for basar b'chalav?
A. The pot was purchased today; new pots are always stricter
B. The pot was used for meat within the last 24 hours and still imparts actual (non-degraded) flavor
C. The pot is owned by a minor (ben = son, yom = day)
D. The pot was used only during daylight hours on the same day
Correct: B — Ben yomo literally means 'child of the day' — within 24 hours of last use. Such a pot still carries absorbed meat flavor that can be actively imparted (נותן טעם לשבח). After 24 hours, absorbed flavor is considered פגם (degraded) and the mixture may be permitted.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the standard ratio for bitul b'shishim in an accidental meat-milk mixture?
A. 10 parts permitted to 1 part forbidden
B. 100 parts permitted to 1 part forbidden
C. 60 parts permitted to 1 part forbidden
D. Any ratio suffices if the mixing was unintentional
Correct: C — Bitul b'shishim (nullification in sixty) is the standard: if there are 60 times as much of the permitted substance as the forbidden, the forbidden's flavor is considered imperceptible and the mixture is permitted — but only for accidental, not intentional, mixtures.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Which of the following is FULLY permitted (not even rabbinically forbidden) when combined with milk?
A. Chicken schnitzel
B. Venison (deer meat)
C. Grilled salmon
D. Non-kosher beef
Correct: C — Fish has no mother's milk relationship. The Talmud explicitly permits fish with dairy — there is no biblical or rabbinic prohibition. (A later Sephardic custom of avoiding fish with milk exists for health reasons unrelated to basar b'chalav.)
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How long does the Rambam require waiting after eating meat before consuming dairy?
A. One hour, to allow the meal to settle
B. Until the next meal of the same day
C. Three hours, as a middle-ground ruling
D. Six full hours, due to fat and sinew lingering between the teeth
Correct: D — The Rambam rules strictly: six hours must elapse after eating meat before dairy may be consumed. His reasoning is that fatty meat residue and sinew can remain lodged between teeth for up to six hours, and eating dairy during that period constitutes an effective mixing of meat and milk.
