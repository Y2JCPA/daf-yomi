🥩 The Three Priestly Gifts (Matanot Kehuna)

זְרוֹעַ — Zeroa

  • The foreleg / shoulder
  • Right foreleg of the animal
  • From breast joint down to hoof
  • Given raw to the kohen

לְחָיַיִם — Lechayayim

  • The cheeks / jaw-bones
  • Both cheeks included
  • From eye socket to chin
  • Given raw to the kohen

קֵיבָה — Keiva

  • The maw / abomasum (4th stomach)
  • Rennet-containing stomach
  • Domestic animals only
  • Given raw to the kohen

📜 Mishnah: Laws of the Three Gifts

Torah Source (Devarim 18:3): Give the kohen the zeroa, lechayayim, and keiva from every slaughtered animal
↓
Applies to domestic animals (behema) — cattle, sheep, and goats
↓
Does NOT apply to wild animals (chaya) such as deer or to birds
↓
Applies both in the Land of Israel and outside the Land
↓
Applies only to non-sacred (chulin) animals — consecrated animals are exempt
↓
The slaughterer bears the obligation and must give the gifts to any available kohen

📐 How Much Is the Zeroa?

Tanna Kamma
The zeroa extends from the breast joint (פֶּרֶק הָעֶלְיוֹן) all the way down to the hoof — the entire foreleg is given to the kohen.
R. Yehuda
The zeroa refers only to the upper foreleg — the portion above the knee joint. The lower leg is not included in the gift.
Rav Huna
The foreleg given to the kohen begins at the breast/shoulder joint (בֵּית הַנַּחַת) and extends downward — this is the authoritative measure.
Gemara
We derive the scope from the zeroa of the shelamim peace-offering — the biblical analogy sets the standard for how much flesh is included.
Practical Ruling The zeroa is the right foreleg from the shoulder joint downward — a generous, prized cut of meat. The kohen receives it raw and uncooked.

⚔️ The Kohanim Who Took by Force

חַטַּאת הַנְּעָרִים גְּדוֹלָה מְאֹד אֶת פְּנֵי ה׳ — 'The sin of the young men was very great before God' (Shmuel I 2:17)
Aggadic Warning The Gemara invokes the infamous conduct of Chofni and Pinchas, sons of Eli the High Priest (I Shmuel 2). They sent servants to seize meat before the fat was even burned on the altar — by brute force if necessary. Scripture condemns this as a grave sin. The Talmud uses their story to underscore that while a kohen is entitled to matanot, demanding or seizing them aggressively is a corruption of the sacred role.
