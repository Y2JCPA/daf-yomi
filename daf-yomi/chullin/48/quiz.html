Chullin 48 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
When a lung is found attached to the chest wall, how does Rava (via Ravin bar Sheva) say you determine whether the lung was perforated or the chest wall was injured?
Rava's procedure: use a sharp thin knife to carefully separate the lung from the chest wall. Then examine the chest wall. If a defect (wound or diseased area) is found in the wall itself, the attachment was caused by the chest wall injury — the lung is kosher. If the chest wall is clean and healthy, the attachment must have been caused by a perforation in the lung — treifah. This is a real surgical diagnostic procedure.
Question 2 of 5
Rav Nachman rules that a chest wall can seal a lung perforation — but only in a specific location. Where?
Rav Nachman distinguishes between two situations: if the lung is sealed by the chest wall in the area where it naturally rests (the front lobe-rib junction), the seal is reliable and permanent — the animal is kosher. Outside that natural growth area, the seal is not considered reliable. Additionally, the lung must be "tangled" in the flesh between the ribs. A wound-membrane (scab) is never a valid seal under any circumstances.
Question 3 of 5
In the debate about a needle found in the lung, what is the core disagreement between those who deem it kosher vs. treifah?
Everyone agrees that internal deficiency per se doesn't render an animal treifah. The dispute is about the needle's path: R' Yochanan's camp says the needle entered through the respiratory route (inhaled or swallowed into the airways) — no perforation occurred, so kosher. Reish Lakish's camp says it must have punctured its way out of the digestive system — that perforation renders it treifah. This is a question of forensic anatomy, not legal categories.
Question 4 of 5
A thick needle is found embedded in the liver with its eye facing inward (toward the liver). What does Rav Ashi rule?
Rav Ashi's logic: a thick needle has a non-sharp eye. If the eye faces inward (buried in the liver), the needle was drawn sharp-end-first through a blood vessel — the sharp tip grabbed a vessel and was pulled through the circulation to the liver. No gullet perforation occurred, so the animal is kosher. If the eye faces outward (toward the stomach), the needle punched sharp-end-first out of the gullet, perforating it — treifah. Thin needles are different: both ends are sharp, so orientation tells you nothing, and the ruling is treifah regardless.
Question 5 of 5
Rav Nachman walked through the butcher market and saw lungs covered in large cysts "like jugs." What did he do?
Rav Nachman walked through the market, saw these heavily cyst-covered lungs, and remained silent. In the Talmud, when a sage sees something being done and says nothing, it's taken as implicit approval. His silence is the ruling: large cysts on the lung — even extensive ones — don't render the animal treifah. Similarly, R' Ami and R' Asi said nothing when they saw lungs with hard growths "like rocks" in the Tiberias market. The scholars' presence and silence in the marketplace was itself a form of practical halachic ruling.
