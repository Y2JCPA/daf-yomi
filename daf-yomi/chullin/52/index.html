🐺 Drusa: Which Predators Render an Animal Treifa?

Mishnah
If a wolf pounced on small cattle, a lion on large cattle, a bear, leopard, or panther on any domesticated animal — these constitute drusa (דְּרוּסָה) and the animal is treifa, even if no external wound is visible to the eye.
Shmuel
A snake that bites an animal does not create drusa. The concern with predators is their claws (ציפורניים) penetrating to the innards. A snake has no claws — therefore a snake-bitten animal is not rendered treifa by drusa.
Rav Huna
Regarding the spine (שִׁדְרָה): if the majority of the spinal cord (חוּט הַשִּׁדְרָה) is severed at any single point — even if the vertebrae appear outwardly intact — the animal is treifa. The cord itself is the critical organ.
Gemara
Why does a wolf suffice for small cattle but not large? The Sages calibrated each threshold by the predator's capacity to fatally injure that animal's innards. A wolf's claws can penetrate a sheep's body cavity but cannot reach the vital organs of an ox.
Core Principle Drusa (דְּרוּסָה) — a predator pounce — creates a presumption of invisible internal fatal damage, rendering the animal treifa even without visible wounds. The threshold predator differs by animal size.

⚖️ Which Predator Creates Drusa for Which Animal?

Small Cattle (בְּהֵמָה דַּקָּה)

  • Sheep and goats
  • 🐺 Wolf (זְאֵב) → Treifa
  • 🦁 Lion → Treifa (a fortiori)
  • 🐻 Bear, Leopard → Treifa
  • Large predators always qualify

Large Cattle (בְּהֵמָה גַּסָּה)

  • Oxen and cows
  • 🦁 Lion (אַרְיֵה) → Treifa
  • 🐻 Bear (דֹּב) → Treifa
  • 🐆 Leopard (נָמֵר) → Treifa
  • 🐺 Wolf alone → NOT sufficient

Birds (עוֹף)

  • Domesticated and wild birds
  • 🦅 Hawk/Falcon (בַּז) → Treifa
  • 🐈 Cat → Treifa for small birds
  • 🐍 Snake → NOT drusa (Shmuel)
  • Examine wound site independently

🐍 Shmuel: A Snake Bite Is Not Drusa

אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: נָחָשׁ אֵינוֹ דּוֹרֵס — A snake does not constitute drusa
Halakhic Ruling — שְׁמוּאֵל Shmuel rules that a snake (נָחָשׁ) that bites an animal does not create the status of drusa. The treifa of drusa is grounded specifically in claw penetration (ציפורניים) reaching the body cavity and causing invisible fatal internal injury. A snake has no claws — its mechanism of harm is entirely different. Therefore, even a venomous snakebite does not trigger the drusa presumption, though the specific wound must still be halakhically examined.

🔍 Examining an Animal After a Predator Attack

Step 1 — Identify the predator: Determine which animal attacked and match it to the size category of the victim (small cattle, large cattle, or bird).
→
Step 2 — Apply the drusa threshold: If the predator qualifies for that animal's size (e.g., wolf for sheep, lion for cattle), presume drusa and potential treifa status immediately.
→
Step 3 — Inspect skin and hide externally: Look for claw punctures. Even without visible penetration, internal damage may be present — external appearance does not clear the animal.
→
Step 4 — Examine internal organs post-shechita: Open the animal and inspect lungs, intestines, and organs along the attack site. Check the spinal cord (חוּט הַשִּׁדְרָה) if the attack was near the back.
→
Step 5 — Render the verdict: If claws penetrated to the innards → treifa. If the spinal cord majority is severed → treifa. If internal organs are intact and no drusa threshold was met → may be kosher.
