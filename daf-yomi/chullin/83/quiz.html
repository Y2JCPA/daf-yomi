QUESTION 1 OF 5
If Reuven slaughters the mother and Shimon (a completely different person and owner) slaughters the offspring on the same day — who is liable for violating oto v'et beno?
A. Reuven, because he slaughtered first and 'started' the problem
B. Shimon, because he completed the forbidden pair with the second slaughter
C. Both are equally liable for the same violation
D. Neither — the prohibition only applies when one person owns both animals
Correct: B — The Gemara rules that the second slaughterer bears the prohibition — the Torah's 'do not slaughter it and its offspring' falls on whoever completes the pair on that day. The first slaughterer acted permissibly. Ownership is irrelevant to liability.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the Mishnah permit butchers in four or five adjacent stalls to sell freely on erev Yom Tov without checking for oto v'et beno?
A. Erev Yom Tov is entirely exempt from oto v'et beno as a Rabbinic enactment
B. The Rabbis waived the prohibition purely for economic reasons with no legal basis
C. The large number of animals makes it statistically improbable (rov) that any two animals sold are mother and offspring
D. The prohibition only applies to one's own animals, not to purchased ones
Correct: C — The dispensation is based on rov (statistical majority) — with so many animals slaughtered on erev Yom Tov, the chance that any two animals sold across stalls are actually a mother-offspring pair is negligible, and the Rabbis therefore permitted selling freely.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
If Reuven slaughters the mother and then declares the offspring 'hefker' (ownerless) before Shimon slaughters it on the same day — does this likely circumvent the oto v'et beno prohibition?
A. Yes — Shimon is a new, unrelated owner with no connection to Reuven's slaughter
B. No — the prohibition attaches to the animal's status on that day, not to who owns it
C. Only if Shimon genuinely did not know the mother had been slaughtered
D. Yes — hefker always legally severs the chain between prior and subsequent acts
Correct: B — The Gemara holds that oto v'et beno attaches to the animal itself. Once the mother was slaughtered that day, the offspring became the 'second half' of a forbidden pair for that calendar day. Declaring it hefker cannot reset that animal's status — the day remains the same.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the biblical source for the prohibition of oto v'et beno?
A. Deuteronomy 22:6 — the commandment to send away the mother bird from the nest
B. Leviticus 22:28 — 'Do not slaughter it and its offspring on the same day'
C. Exodus 23:19 — 'Do not boil a kid in its mother's milk'
D. Numbers 19:2 — the laws of the red heifer
Correct: B — Leviticus 22:28 states: וְשׁוֹר אוֹ שֶׂה אֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ לֹא תִשְׁחֲטוּ בְּיוֹם אֶחָד — 'An ox or sheep, you shall not slaughter it and its offspring on the same day.' This verse is the direct biblical source for oto v'et beno.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Does the prohibition of oto v'et beno apply only when the mother is slaughtered before the offspring, or also when the offspring is slaughtered before the mother?
A. Only when the mother is slaughtered first — she is the 'primary' animal protected
B. Only when the offspring is slaughtered first — protecting the continuation of the species
C. Both orders are forbidden — slaughtering either one first and then the other on the same day violates the prohibition
D. The order is irrelevant for the prohibition itself but determines who receives malkot
Correct: C — The prohibition applies in both orders — whether the mother is slaughtered first or the offspring is slaughtered first. The Torah forbids slaughtering 'it and its offspring' (in any combination) on the same day. The second act, regardless of which animal it involves, triggers the violation.
