🗓️ The Erev Yom Tov Dispensation

Problem: On festival eves, many animals are slaughtered. How can butchers sell freely without risking an oto v'et beno violation?
↓
Mishnah's rule: Four or five adjacent butcher stalls may sell on erev Yom Tov without checking whether any two animals sold are mother and offspring.
↓
Reason (Rov): The sheer number of animals being slaughtered makes it statistically improbable that any two sold animals are actually a mother-offspring pair.
↓
Practical limit: If a buyer purchases from one stall and then immediately from the next adjacent stall, some authorities still require concern about the pairing.
↓
Principle: The Rabbis permitted a practical leniency (היתר) so that festival preparations would not be disrupted by an impossible-to-verify prohibition.

⚖️ Who Receives Malkot — First or Second Slaughterer?

Questioner
If Reuven slaughtered the mother in the morning, and Shimon slaughtered the offspring in the afternoon — who is liable for malkot?
Rava
The second slaughterer receives malkot. The Torah says 'do not slaughter it and its offspring' — the prohibition is violated by whoever completes the forbidden pair.
Questioner
But what if Shimon did not know that Reuven had already slaughtered the mother earlier that day?
Rava
Without התראה (forewarning), the violation still occurred — but Shimon cannot receive malkot, since lashes require that the person was explicitly warned beforehand.
Gemara
And Reuven — the first slaughterer? He acted entirely permissibly at the time of his slaughter. He bears no prohibition whatsoever.
Key Ruling Only the second slaughterer can violate oto v'et beno — the prohibition is triggered by the second act of slaughter. Ownership is irrelevant; even two different people can together create the violation.

🔄 Does Hefker (Ownerless Declaration) Break the Prohibition?

View: Hefker Might Help

  • Reuven slaughters the mother, then declares the offspring hefker (ownerless)...
  • ...Shimon acquires the now-ownerless offspring and slaughters it on the same day.
  • Perhaps Shimon is a 'new' owner with no personal connection to the first slaughter?
  • If the prohibition is owner-based (personal obligation), the ownership break might reset it.

View: Hefker Does NOT Help

  • The Torah's prohibition is on the act of slaughter — it attaches to the animal's status that day.
  • אותו ואת בנו — 'it and its offspring' refers to the animals themselves, not their owner.
  • Once the mother was slaughtered today, that calf became the 'other half' of a forbidden pair for this calendar day.
  • No ownership transfer — hefker, sale, or gift — can 'reset' the animal's status on that day.

📜 Core Principle: The Nature of Oto V'et Beno

וְשׁוֹר אוֹ שֶׂה אֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ לֹא תִשְׁחֲטוּ בְּיוֹם אֶחָד — ויקרא כב:כח
Leviticus 22:28 The prohibition of oto v'et beno (אותו ואת בנו) — slaughtering a mother animal and its offspring on the same calendar day — is categorized as a chok (divine decree) whose complete rationale is not given in the Torah. The Gemara on this daf clarifies a crucial structural point: the prohibition attaches to the animal's status on that calendar day, not to the identity or ownership of the slaughterer. Ownership transfers, declarations of hefker, and purchases across butcher stalls do not automatically permit what would otherwise be a violation. The second slaughterer bears the prohibition regardless of ownership — the bond between mother and offspring is protected by the act itself.
