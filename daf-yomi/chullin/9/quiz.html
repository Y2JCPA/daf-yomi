Chullin 9 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
According to Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav, what three skills must a Torah scholar learn?
Rav Yehuda says that Rav requires a Torah scholar to learn three practical skills: writing (for Torah texts and documents), shechitah (ritual slaughter), and milah (circumcision). These are essential communal services a scholar should be able to provide.
Question 2 of 5
What is the "chazakah of issur" regarding a living animal?
Rav Huna's foundational principle: A living animal stands in a presumptive status of prohibition (chezkat issur) — it's an "ever min hachai" (limb of a living creature) and forbidden to eat. Only verified proper slaughter can break this presumption. Any doubt about the slaughter means the prohibition stands.
Question 3 of 5
Why does Rav Huna say danger (sakana) is treated more stringently than prohibition (issur)?
When life is at stake, the Sages are more stringent than in cases of regular halachic prohibition. For exposed water (possible snake venom), we don't rely on probability that it's safe. But for a meat prohibition, we might follow the majority or apply chazakah. The principle: "chamira sakanta me'issura" — danger is more severe than prohibition.
Question 4 of 5
In the case of a knife found to be notched after slaughter, what does Rav Huna rule?
Rav Huna, consistent with his principle of chazakat issur, rules the slaughter is invalid. Since we can't prove the notch occurred after slaughter, we can't break the animal's presumptive forbidden status. Rav Chisda disagrees, saying if the slaughterer cut bones afterward, we can attribute the notch to that. Rav Yosef famously invalidated 13 animals based on Rav Huna's strict view.
Question 5 of 5
What concept does the "wolf and the innards" case illustrate about halachic reasoning?
The wolf scenario explores a layered doubt: the animal might have had a pre-existing perforation (making it treifah), and the wolf may have punctured at the same spot. This compound uncertainty (sfek sfeka) is used to analyze when we can rely on the status quo versus when we must assume the worst. It connects to the broader principle that chazakah of issur requires affirmative proof of permissibility.
