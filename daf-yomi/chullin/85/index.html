🤝 Reverse Order — Is There a Biblical Prohibition?

Tanna Kamma
If one slaughtered the offspring first, the parent becomes forbidden to slaughter that day — but no biblical lashes (malkot) are incurred if one proceeds to slaughter the parent. The verse speaks of parent-then-offspring as the primary case.
R' Yehuda
The verse oto v'et beno — 'it and its offspring' — covers both directions equally. Whether parent or offspring is slaughtered first, the reverse slaughter the same day incurs full biblical lashes.
Halacha The Halacha follows Tanna Kamma: slaughtering offspring before parent creates a prohibition on the parent that day, but without biblical malkot — only a rabbinic-level transgression if violated.

🧬 Kilayim Animals — Three Halachic Positions

Lenient View

  • Kilayim animals lack clear species identity
  • Oto v'et beno requires a defined parent-offspring within a halachic species
  • No certain biblical violation where lineage is blurred — fully permitted

Stringent View

  • Physical mother-offspring relationship exists in nature regardless of species purity
  • Torah concerns the natural bond, not halachic species classification
  • Treat as behema: forbidden to slaughter parent and offspring same day

Middle Ground (Ruling)

  • Accept stringency in practice: do not slaughter suspected pairs same day
  • No biblical lashes since status is a safek (doubtful)
  • Rabbinic fence around possible violation is sufficient

🩸 Kisuy HaDam — When and How to Cover Blood

Shechita performed on a chaya (deer, gazelle) or bird (dove, chicken)
↓
Blood flows onto the ground — mitzvah of kisuy hadam is triggered immediately
↓
Shochet present
Shochet covers blood with earth (afar) — recites beracha al kisuy hadam
Shochet fails / absent
Any Jew present fulfills the mitzvah — but recites no beracha, as obligation was primarily the shochet's
↓
Behema (ox, sheep, goat) — no kisuy hadam required, even if blood pools

⚖️ Handling Safek Parentage in Oto V'et Beno

Step 1 — Identify: Determine whether two animals could be parent and offspring based on age, appearance, and herd history
↓
Step 2 — Check same-day slaughter: Was either animal already slaughtered today? If yes, a safek arises before slaughtering the other.
↓
Step 3 — Consider rov (majority): If most animals in the herd are unrelated, some permit relying on the statistical majority — but stringent authorities reject this for a Torah prohibition.
↓
Step 4 — When in doubt, delay: The safest course is to wait until the following day to slaughter the second animal, fully eliminating any possible violation.
↓
Step 5 — No malkot for safek: Even if a violation occurred unknowingly due to genuine uncertainty about parentage, no biblical lashes are administered.
