QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Tanna Kamma, if one slaughters a calf in the morning and then wants to slaughter its mother that afternoon, what is the halacha?
A. Both slaughters are fully permitted; oto v'et beno only applies when the parent is slaughtered first
B. The mother is forbidden that day, but no biblical lashes are given if she is nonetheless slaughtered
C. The mother is forbidden and full biblical lashes are given if she is slaughtered
D. The mother is forbidden only if the original slaughter was performed by the owner
Correct: B — The Tanna Kamma rules that the reverse order (offspring slaughtered before parent) creates a prohibition on slaughtering the parent that same day. However, unlike the standard order (parent first), this does not incur biblical malkot if violated — it is a rabbinic-level prohibition in this case.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does slaughtering the parent before the offspring incur biblical lashes, while the reverse order (offspring first) does not, according to the Tanna Kamma?
A. The Torah only prohibits the more common commercial practice, which is always parent-first
B. The verse 'oto v'et beno' refers specifically to slaughtering the parent and then proceeding to its offspring — this is the primary biblical case
C. Lashes only apply when the more valuable animal (parent) is slaughtered first
D. The reverse order was added by rabbinic decree and cannot carry biblical-level consequences
Correct: B — The Tanna Kamma understands the verse 'oto v'et beno' as addressing the scenario where one slaughters the parent first and then proceeds to the offspring. This is the primary biblical prohibition carrying malkot. The reverse order is derived by extension and therefore only reaches rabbinic-level prohibition.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Which categories of animals require kisuy hadam (covering of blood) after shechita?
A. All kosher animals whose blood is collected on the ground
B. Only domesticated animals: cows, sheep, and goats
C. Wild animals (chayot) and birds exclusively
D. Any animal slaughtered outside a Temple context
Correct: C — Kisuy hadam applies exclusively to chayot (wild kosher animals such as deer and gazelle) and birds. Domesticated animals — behemot such as cows, sheep, and goats — are explicitly exempt from this mitzvah.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
If a shochet fails to perform kisuy hadam, can another person cover the blood?
A. No — the obligation is personal to the shochet and the mitzvah opportunity is permanently lost
B. Yes — any Jew present may cover the blood, though they do not recite a beracha
C. Yes — any Jew may cover it and still recites the full beracha as if they were the shochet
D. Only the owner of the animal may step in to perform kisuy hadam
Correct: B — While the primary obligation falls on the shochet who performed the slaughter, if they fail to act, any Jew present may and should cover the blood to fulfill the mitzvah. However, because the obligation was not primarily theirs, they do not recite a beracha over the act.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the practical ruling regarding a kilayim (hybrid) animal and the prohibition of oto v'et beno?
A. Kilayim animals are fully exempt — the prohibition requires clear species membership
B. Kilayim animals are treated identically to pure behemot and carry full biblical lashes
C. The status is doubtful: stringent practice forbids slaughtering suspected parent-offspring pairs the same day, but no biblical lashes apply
D. Kilayim animals may not be slaughtered at all under Jewish law
Correct: C — Because kilayim hybrids have uncertain halachic species status, the biblical prohibition of oto v'et beno cannot definitively apply. Nevertheless, stringent authorities rule that we treat them as behemot for the purpose of this prohibition — out of doubt (safek). No biblical malkot are administered even if violated, since the status remains uncertain.
