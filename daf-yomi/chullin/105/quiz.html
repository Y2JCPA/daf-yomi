QUESTION 1 OF 5
How does Mar Ukva describe himself in comparison to his father's meat-dairy stringency?
A. As a student surpassing his teacher
B. As vinegar compared to wine
C. As wine compared to vinegar
D. As equally stringent in practice
Correct: B — Mar Ukva humbly declares 'I am like vinegar compared to wine beside my father' — his father waited a full day, while Mar Ukva only waits until the next meal.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the halachic status of eating poultry with milk according to Rav Chisda on this daf?
A. Permitted by both Torah and rabbinic law
B. Forbidden by Torah law
C. Forbidden by rabbinic decree
D. Forbidden only as a minhag chumra
Correct: C — Rav Chisda explains that poultry with milk is a rabbinic prohibition — the Torah verse about cooking a kid in its mother's milk applies only where a mother–offspring relationship exists, but the Rabbis extended the fence to poultry.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why did Chazal declare Mayim Acharonim (post-meal handwashing) to be obligatory?
A. To honor the Shabbat table before Kiddush
B. Because of the danger of Sodomite salt (Melach Sedomit) which can blind the eyes
C. As preparation for reciting Birkat Hamazon
D. To demonstrate gratitude after eating
Correct: B — The Gemara explicitly states Mayim Acharonim is 'chova' (obligatory) because of Melach Sedomit — a salt virtually identical in appearance to regular salt that can cause blindness if touched to the eyes.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What was the specific practice of Mar Ukva's father regarding meat and dairy?
A. He waited exactly six hours
B. He washed his hands between courses at the same meal
C. He did not eat dairy until the following day
D. He avoided only hard cheese after meat
Correct: C — Mar Ukva tells us his father would not eat cheese until the next day — a full 24-hour personal stringency, far beyond the accepted halachic baseline which Mar Ukva himself followed.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is Melach Sedomit and why is it halachically significant on this daf?
A. A bitter herb consumed at the Passover Seder
B. A Sodomite grain used in ancient bread-making
C. A salt from the Sodom region, indistinguishable from regular salt, that can blind if it reaches the eyes
D. A type of incense used in Temple service
Correct: C — Melach Sedomit is a finely ground salt from the Sodom region. It is physically dangerous — salt residue on hands from a meal could be rubbed into the eyes, causing blindness. This concrete danger is the reason the Rabbis declared Mayim Acharonim obligatory.
