Chullin 96 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Chullin 96, what is the halachic status of the gid hanashe of a non-kosher (tamei) animal?
A. Biblically forbidden, identical to a kosher animal's gid
B. Forbidden rabbinically, since the biblical prohibition applies only to kosher animals
C. Completely permitted, since the entire animal is already forbidden anyway
D. Permitted only if the gid was removed before the animal was slaughtered
Correct: B — The Gemara concludes that the biblical prohibition of gid hanashe applies only to tahor (kosher) animals, since the verse addresses those who may eat animals. The gid of a tamei animal carries no additional biblical prohibition — only a rabbinic one.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does the principle 'eino noten ta'am' mean as applied to gid hanashe?
A. The gid imparts a pleasant flavor to meat when cooked, making it desirable
B. The gid does not impart significant flavor, so the broth it is cooked in remains permitted
C. The gid can never make any mixture forbidden under any circumstances
D. Only the inner cord of the gid gives flavor; the outer sheath does not
Correct: B — 'Eino noten ta'am' means the gid hanashe does not impart forbidden flavor into the liquid or food it is cooked with. Therefore, the broth in which a gid was cooked is generally permitted — though the gid itself must still be removed.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
If gid hanashe (from a kosher bovine) falls into a pot of kosher beef stew, what is the ruling even if the ratio is 1,000:1?
A. The stew is permitted since gid does not impart flavor
B. The stew is permitted because the ratio exceeds the required 60:1
C. The stew is forbidden regardless of ratio due to min b'mino
D. The stew is permitted only if the gid is immediately removed while cold
Correct: C — Gid hanashe (beef) mixed with beef meat is min b'mino — same species mixed with same species. The halacha is that min b'mino is never nullified (eino batel), even at a ratio of 1,000:1, because the identical flavor cannot be 'overwhelmed.'
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the fundamental reason why min b'mino (same species in same species) cannot be nullified?
A. The Torah explicitly prohibits bitul in all same-species cases
B. The flavors of the forbidden and permitted substances are identical, so the forbidden taste cannot be 'lost'
C. The Rabbis instituted a strict fence against same-species mixtures to prevent deception
D. The forbidden substance is visually identical and must therefore always be physically removed
Correct: B — The logic of bitul (nullification) relies on the taste of the forbidden minority being overwhelmed by the majority. When the forbidden and permitted foods are the same species, their tastes are identical — so the forbidden flavor cannot be lost. It simply 'joins' the majority, remaining present.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
From which biblical event does the prohibition of gid hanashe originate?
A. The Exodus from Egypt and the laws of the Passover sacrifice
B. Jacob wrestling with the angel, who struck his hip socket (gid hanashe)
C. The Sinaitic laws of korbanot (sacrificial offerings)
D. Noah's covenant after the flood, when meat consumption was first permitted
Correct: B — Genesis 32:33 states explicitly: 'Therefore Bnei Yisrael do not eat the gid hanashe to this day, because [the angel] struck Jacob's hip socket.' The prohibition is a permanent commemoration of Jacob's injury during his wrestling match with the divine being.
