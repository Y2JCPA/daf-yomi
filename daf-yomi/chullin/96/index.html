🦴 Gid HaNashe — Scope, Flavor, and Nullification

Background: The Torah (Gen. 32:33) forbids eating the gid hanashe (sciatic nerve) in memory of Jacob's wrestling match with the angel, who struck his hip socket.
↓
Daf 96 Question 1: Does this prohibition apply to non-kosher (tamei) animals, or only to kosher (tahor) animals that Jews are otherwise permitted to eat?
↓
Daf 96 Question 2: If gid hanashe is cooked with other meat, does it render the entire mixture forbidden through imparted flavor?
↓
Key Ruling — Eino Noten Ta'am: The gid hanashe does not impart significant flavor; therefore the broth/liquid in which it cooked is generally permitted.
↓
Key Ruling — Min B'Mino: If gid (beef nerve) mixes with beef meat, they are the same species — the gid is never nullified regardless of ratio.

🕍 The Prohibition of Gid HaNashe

עַל כֵּן לֹא יֹאכְלוּ בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת גִּיד הַנָּשֶׁה אֲשֶׁר עַל כַּף הַיָּרֵךְ
Torah Source The Torah states: "Therefore the children of Israel shall not eat the sciatic nerve [gid hanashe] which is on the hip socket, to this day" (Gen. 32:33). This prohibition commemorates the angel striking Jacob's thigh. It applies to all kosher animals — both domesticated (beheimah) and wild (chayah), in both the right and left hind legs. It is one of the few prohibitions explicitly anchored to a narrative event in the Torah.

🐑 Does Gid HaNashe Apply to Non-Kosher Animals?

First View
The gid hanashe of a non-kosher animal is also forbidden. The prohibition targets the gid itself universally — it is not contingent on the animal being otherwise permitted. Wherever there is a sciatic nerve, the prohibition applies.
Second View
The gid hanashe of a non-kosher animal is NOT biblically forbidden. The verse says 'Bnei Yisrael shall not eat' — addressed to those who may eat animals at all. A non-kosher animal's gid carries no additional biblical prohibition; the whole animal is already forbidden.
Halachic Outcome The accepted ruling follows the second view: the gid of a tamei animal is forbidden only rabbinically. Its primary biblical prohibition is the non-kosher animal itself, not the gid specifically.

🍲 Gid Cooked in a Pot — Does It Forbid the Mixture?

Gid HaNashe falls into a pot of kosher meat and is cooked together
↓
If gid imparts flavor (noten ta'am)
Entire pot is forbidden — the taste of the gid spreads through the meat and liquid
If gid does NOT impart flavor (eino noten ta'am)
Pot may remain permitted — since gid produces no real taste, it cannot forbid by flavor
↓
Halacha: Gid HaNashe is eino noten ta'am — the broth in which it is cooked alone is permitted. But the gid itself must still be removed and discarded.

🔄 Min B'Mino vs. Min B'Eino Mino — Nullification Rules

Min B'Mino (Same Species)

  • Forbidden substance mixes with identical permitted substance
  • Example: gid hanashe (beef nerve) cooked with kosher beef meat
  • Result: NEVER nullified — even 1 in 1,000
  • The forbidden item is indistinguishable in taste from the majority
  • Reason: identical flavor means the forbidden portion simply 'blends in' — it is never overwhelmed
  • Rabbinic ruling: must remove the gid and discard it; the meat's status requires a posek

Min B'Eino Mino (Different Species)

  • Forbidden substance mixes with a different permitted substance
  • Example: gid (beef) falls into a pot of vegetable soup
  • Result: Nullified at 60:1 (batel b'shishim)
  • The distinct flavor of the forbidden item is lost in the majority
  • Reason: different flavors allow the minority taste to be genuinely overwhelmed
  • Provided gid does not impart its own flavor, the mixture may be permitted
