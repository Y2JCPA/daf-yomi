⚖️ Does a Non-Jew's Shechita Trigger Oto V'et Bno?

Rav Pappa
If a non-Jew slaughtered the mother (rendering it neveilah), a Jew who then slaughters the offspring is exempt from lashes — the non-Jew's act is not considered a valid 'shechita' that triggers the prohibition.
Abaye
The prohibition of oto v'et bno is about the relationship between mother and offspring — the act of killing one still creates the prohibition regardless of who performed it. The Jew who slaughters the second should be liable.
Rava
We must distinguish: the Torah says לֹא תִשְׁחֲטוּ — 'you shall not slaughter.' If the first act was not a valid shechita, there is nothing from which to prohibit the second slaughter. Rav Pappa's reasoning stands.
Gemara Conclusion
The non-Jew's slaughter of the first animal does not create an oto v'et bno prohibition for the Jew slaughtering the second — since the first was neveilah, it is as though no 'shechita' occurred in the eyes of halacha.
Source Verse אֹתוֹ וְאֶת-בְּנוֹ לֹא תִשְׁחֲטוּ בְּיוֹם אֶחָד (ויקרא כב:כח) — 'It and its offspring you shall not slaughter on the same day' (Leviticus 22:28)

🔀 Who Is Liable — First or Second Slaughterer?

Person A slaughters the mother (permitted — no prior knowledge)
↓
Person A has now triggered the oto v'et bno prohibition
↓
Person A slaughters offspring same day
Person A receives malkot (lashes) — he knowingly violated the prohibition
Person B slaughters offspring same day
Person B receives malkot — the prohibition applies to any person who slaughters the second animal
No one slaughters offspring that day
No violation — the prohibition resets at nightfall. The offspring may be slaughtered the next day.

🔄 Mother First vs. Offspring First — Does Order Change the Law?

Mother Slaughtered First

  • Classic case of oto v'et bno
  • Slaughtering the offspring that same day is forbidden
  • If violated: second slaughterer receives lashes
  • Both animals remain kosher b'dieved
  • Prohibition is explicit in the verse

Offspring Slaughtered First

  • Equally prohibited — Gemara derives from verse
  • Slaughtering the mother that same day is forbidden
  • If violated: second slaughterer receives lashes
  • Both animals remain kosher b'dieved
  • Rabbinic derivation extends the prohibition symmetrically

🏪 The Market Presumption (Chazakat HaShuk)

חזקת השוק — רוב בהמות שבשוק לא נשחטה אמה או בתה אותו היום
Key Ruling The Gemara establishes that in a city market, a buyer need not fear that the animal he purchases may trigger an oto v'et bno concern. The presumption is that most animals in a large market have not had a related animal slaughtered that same day. A butcher who sells animals in a market is not assumed to have already slaughtered the parent or offspring. However, if the buyer receives explicit information that the related animal was already slaughtered that day, he may not proceed.

📅 Oto V'et Bno on Erev Yom Tov — The Special Problem

Erev Yom Tov scenario: A person slaughters an animal before the holiday — a permitted act
↓
The tension: He wishes to slaughter the related animal (mother or offspring) on Yom Tov itself for the holiday meal — this is normally permitted as shechita on Yom Tov is allowed for food
↓
Problem identified: If the two animals are mother and offspring, slaughtering the second on Yom Tov violates oto v'et bno — even though Yom Tov shechita is generally allowed
↓
Rabbinic concern: The Gemara raises whether one should warn people to be careful about this scenario, as the Yom Tov permit for shechita might lead people to forget the oto v'et bno restriction
↓
Practical ruling: The oto v'et bno prohibition overrides the general Yom Tov permission — one may not slaughter the related animal on Yom Tov if the first was slaughtered Erev Yom Tov
