📜 Mishnah — Chapter 4 Opens (68a) If a laboring animal's fetus extended its foreleg BEFORE shechita — that limb is forbidden as ever min hachai, yet the rest of the fetus is permitted by the mother's slaughter. If it emerged AFTER shechita — the entire fetus is permitted, since shechita covers all contents of the animal at the moment of slaughter.

⚖️ Does Returning the Limb Before Shechita Help?

Rabbi Yochanan
If the fetus's limb emerged before shechita but was pushed back inside before slaughter — it is permitted. The limb was never truly severed from the living animal; its return restores its original status.
Reish Lakish
Even if returned inside, the limb remains forbidden. Once it emerged in the status of ever min hachai, that prohibition was acquired and cannot be undone simply by replacing it.
Core Question Can a prohibition acquired upon emergence be reversed by returning the limb before slaughter?

🔍 Before vs. After Shechita — Side by Side

Limb Emerged BEFORE Shechita

  • Limb = ever min hachai — biblically forbidden
  • Prohibition attaches the moment of emergence
  • Rest of fetus is permitted by mother's shechita
  • Dispute: does returning the limb help? (Yochanan vs. Reish Lakish)

Limb Emerged AFTER Shechita

  • Entire fetus is fully permitted
  • Shechita covers all contents present at moment of slaughter
  • No ever min hachai concern arises
  • No separate shechita needed for fetus

🐑 Ben Pekuah — The Fetus of a Slaughtered Mother

Mother animal receives valid shechita (ritual slaughter)
↓
A live fetus is found inside the slaughtered mother
↓
The fetus (בן פקועה) is considered as if itself already slaughtered by virtue of its mother
↓
Ben pekuah may be eaten without any additional shechita of its own
↓
Ben pekuah's later offspring: Tannaitic dispute whether they also require no shechita — the principle of ירך אמו extends further
