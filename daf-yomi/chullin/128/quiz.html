QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to R' Yehuda, which parts of a sheretz count toward the minimum measure for tum'ah?
A. All parts — flesh, bones, and skin — combine equally
B. Only the parts that are normally eaten by people
C. Only the blood and liquids, not solid portions
D. Only the outer skin, since that is what one handles
Correct: B — R' Yehuda holds that tum'at ochlim requires the item to qualify as 'ochel' (food). Therefore only parts that people normally eat count toward the shiur; inedible parts like certain bones or hide cannot join because they are not 'food' in any halachic sense.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the effect of 'machshava' (intent) in the laws of tum'at ochlim according to R' Shimon?
A. It has no effect — only objective, normal edibility determines ochel status
B. It can make an inedible item susceptible to impurity by designating it as food
C. It purifies an already-impure food item when the owner intends to discard it
D. It is only relevant for liquids, not solid foods
Correct: B — R' Shimon teaches that a person's intention (machshava) to eat something — even something not normally consumed — elevates that item to the status of 'ochel,' making it susceptible to tum'at ochlim. This reflects the principle that the Torah's food-impurity categories are sensitive to human intent.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the minimum volume required for a food item to be susceptible to tum'at ochlim?
A. K'zayit — the volume of an olive
B. K'beitza — the volume of an egg
C. K'adashah — the size of a lentil
D. Revi'it — a quarter-log of liquid
Correct: B — The shiur (minimum measure) for tum'at ochlim is a k'beitza — the volume of an egg. Items smaller than this cannot convey or receive food impurity. The k'adashah (lentil-size) is relevant to the sheretz's own impurity, while k'beitza governs ordinary food items.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
R' Meir's ruling that all parts of a sheretz combine is grounded in which principle?
A. The Torah classifies sheretz impurity separately from food impurity — it is a gezeirat hakatuv applying to the whole creature
B. He agrees with R' Yehuda but considers more animal parts to be normally edible
C. Only the blood of the sheretz conveys impurity; the rest is irrelevant
D. He derives from context that the sheretz's skin was historically eaten
Correct: A — R' Meir views sheretz impurity as a divine decree (gezeirat hakatuv) applied to the sheretz as a unified whole. Because the impurity does not derive from the food-impurity framework but is its own category, all parts of the creature — edible or not — contribute to the minimum measure.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the fundamental disagreement between R' Yehuda and R' Shimon regarding inedible animal parts?
A. Whether inedible parts can ever become tamei through contact with a corpse (tum'at meit)
B. Whether a person's intent to eat an inedible part is sufficient to give it ochel status for tum'ah purposes
C. Whether the sheretz's minimum measure is k'adashah or k'beitza
D. Whether a Kohen who eats inedible parts violates a Torah prohibition
Correct: B — The dispute is precisely about whether machshava (intent) is operative: R' Yehuda holds that ochel status is determined objectively by what people normally eat, so intent cannot transform an inedible item into halachic 'food.' R' Shimon holds that personal intent can designate even unusual items as food, bringing them within the tum'at ochlim framework.
