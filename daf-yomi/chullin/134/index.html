⚔️ The Aggressive Kohen — Rav Huna's Ruling

Rav Huna
A kohen who seizes the matanot by force from a reluctant owner — what he has taken, he has taken. He is not required to return them.
Questioner
But he is acting like a robber! How can we validate such aggressive behavior from a member of the priestly class?
Rav Huna
The Torah grants him an absolute right to these gifts. The owner has no legal basis to withhold them. But taking them by force causes him to be called a rasha — he is shamed among the kohanim.
Rava
We see that legal entitlement and righteous conduct are distinct. A person may act within their rights yet still be condemned for the manner in which they exercise those rights.
Moral Principle Rights can exist without virtuous exercise. The kohen's legal claim does not sanctify aggressive conduct — he keeps the gifts but loses esteem.

🥩 The Three Priestly Gifts in Detail

זְרוֹעַ — Zero'a

  • Right foreleg of the animal
  • From the knee joint to the shoulder
  • Given raw, not cooked
  • Source: Devarim 18:3
  • Corresponds to Pinchas raising his arm (Midrash)

לְחָיַיִם — Lechayayim

  • Both upper and lower jawbones
  • Debate whether tongue is included
  • Given to kohen after slaughter
  • Corresponds to Pinchas's prayer
  • Symbolizes kohen's role as Torah teacher

קֵבָה — Keivah

  • Fourth stomach (maw/abomasum) of ruminant
  • Must be given with fat deposits intact
  • Owner may not drain fat beforehand
  • Most valuable of the three gifts
  • Corresponds to Pinchas's zeal (Midrash)

📏 Rules of the Keivah Gift

The keivah (maw) is removed during slaughter along with the other organs
↓
Fat deposits naturally present inside the keivah belong to the kohen as part of the gift — they are not the owner's to remove
↓
Question: may the owner drain or scrape out the fat before handing over the keivah?
↓
Ruling: No — the kohen receives the keivah as it naturally is, fat included. The fat is considered integral to the gift.
↓
If the owner drained the fat before handing it over, he has not fulfilled his obligation and must compensate the kohen for the fat's value
↓
Practical rule: The kohen may sell or use the fat — it is fully his property once received

📜 Covenantal Foundation of the Gifts

וְזֶה יִהְיֶה מִשְׁפַּט הַכֹּהֲנִים מֵאֵת הָעָם... הַזְּרֹעַ וְהַלְּחָיַיִם וְהַקֵּבָה — דְּבָרִים י״ח:ג׳
כִּי בוֹ בָּחַר ה׳ The priestly gifts are not charity — they are a covenantal obligation. The Torah states: "You shall give him" (Devarim 18:3), using language of command. The kohen receives no tribal land inheritance; the matanot, along with terumah and tithes, form the divinely mandated system sustaining those dedicated to Temple service. Withholding them is equated with stealing from the kohen. The Talmud notes that one who consistently withholds these gifts will eventually be unable to benefit from a kohen's blessings.
