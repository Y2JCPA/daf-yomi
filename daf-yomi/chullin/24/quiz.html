Chullin 24 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
The Torah calls the Red Heifer a "chukat" (statute). Does this automatically block kal va'chomer (a fortiori) inferences?
The Gemara proves that "statute" alone does not block kal va'chomer, because Yom Kippur also has the word "statute" and yet we derive laws from it. Each case needs a specific limiting factor (verse) to block the inference, not just the general label of "statute."
Question 2 of 5
How is the eglah arufah (broken-neck heifer) processed, and why can't we derive that shechitah would also work for it?
The eglah arufah's neck is broken (arifah) in a rough valley. The Gemara asks why we cannot derive via kal va'chomer that shechitah would also render it fit — but the Torah specifically restricts the method to arifah, closing this logical possibility.
Question 3 of 5
How does the Gemara reconcile the two different starting ages for Levite service (25 and 30)?
The two verses are reconciled beautifully: a Levite begins learning at age 25 and starts performing actual service at age 30. This means there are five full years of apprenticeship. During this time, the Levite learns the songs, the procedures, and the precision required for Temple service.
Question 4 of 5
According to R' Chanina, what physical sign indicates a Kohen has become too old for Temple service?
R' Chanina teaches that a Kohen is fit for service until his hands and feet begin to tremble. This is a practical, observable standard — trembling hands cannot perform the precise Temple service (sprinkling blood, handling offerings) with the required dignity and accuracy.
Question 5 of 5
What is the one-legged shoe test that the Gemara describes?
The Gemara describes this as a practical test for physical fitness. R' Yishmael b. R' Yose was famous for being able to do this even in old age. When he could no longer stand on one leg and remove his shoe, he declared himself unfit for service. It's a simple, elegant test of balance and strength.
