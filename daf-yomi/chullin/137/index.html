🐑 The Mitzvah of Reishit HaGez

A person shears his sheep at the start of the season
↓
Must give reishit hagez — the first portion of the shearing — to the kohen
↓
Minimum requirement: must own at least 5 sheep to incur the obligation
↓
The amount given must be sufficient to make a small garment for the kohen to benefit from
↓
Applies in Eretz Yisrael; Amoraim debate whether it extends to the diaspora

⚖️ How Much Wool Must Be Given?

Beit Shammai
The minimum weight for reishit hagez is 60 sela — a substantial amount, enough for a full garment worthy of a kohen.
Beit Hillel
A minimum of 5 sela weight suffices — the Torah's intent is benefit to the kohen, not a burdensome quantity.
Rabbi Elazar
The kohen must receive enough wool to make a small towel. The measure is defined by the recipient's practical need, not an abstract number.
Halakha The ruling follows Beit Hillel: 5 sela weight is sufficient. The kohen must receive an amount from which he can derive real benefit.

🔗 Reishit HaGez vs. Other Priestly Gifts

Reishit HaGez

  • First shearing of sheep given to kohen
  • Minimum: 5 sheep owned
  • Amount: 5 sela weight (Beit Hillel)
  • Disputed whether applies in diaspora
  • Given to kohen of owner's choosing

Matanot (Slaughter Gifts)

  • Foreleg, cheeks, and stomach
  • No minimum animal count required
  • Applies to every kosher slaughter
  • Applies in Israel and diaspora
  • Given to kohen of owner's choosing

Terumah

  • Portion separated from grain, wine, oil
  • No minimum quantity for obligation
  • 1/40 (generous) to 1/60 (minimal)
  • D'oraita only in Eretz Yisrael
  • Must be eaten by kohen in ritual purity

🌍 Does Reishit HaGez Apply Outside Israel?

רֵאשִׁית גֵּז צֹאנְךָ תִּתֶּן לּוֹ — The first shearing of your flock shall you give to him (Devarim 18:4)
Machloket Amoraim View A: Reishit hagez applies even in the diaspora — the Torah juxtaposes it with matanot (Devarim 18:3–4), and matanot applies everywhere. We derive the scope of one from the other via hekesh.

View B: Reishit hagez is more closely analogous to agricultural gifts tied to the Land. Like challah outside Israel (which is rabbinic), the diaspora obligation may be less certain.

Gemara's approach: The comparison to matanot is key — by analyzing whether the juxtaposition creates a full hekesh, the Talmud probes the extent of the diaspora obligation.
