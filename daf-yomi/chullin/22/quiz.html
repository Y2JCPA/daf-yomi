Chullin 22 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
The Torah says "young pigeons or doves." Which are valid for the altar — young or old doves?
The Torah says "young pigeons" (specifying young) but just "doves" (without specifying young). The Sages derive that the Torah's deliberate choice of words creates opposite rules: for pigeons, only young ones are valid; for doves, only adult ones are valid. The absence of "young" for doves is a positive exclusion of young doves.
Question 2 of 5
What physical sign indicates that a pigeon is transitioning from "young" to "old"?
Rava teaches that the yellow neck feathers are the visual indicator of a pigeon transitioning from the valid "young" category to the invalid "old" category. Once these golden feathers begin appearing, the pigeon is at the boundary. A bird with clearly visible yellow neck feathers is considered old and invalid for an offering.
Question 3 of 5
Why did the Torah write "doves" separately instead of just saying "young birds" to cover both?
Rava derives: if young doves were also valid, the Torah could have simply said "young birds." The separate mention of "doves" — without the qualifier "young" — teaches that for doves, the adult ones are required. The Torah's word choice creates a deliberate contrast between the two species' age requirements.
Question 4 of 5
A pigeon is brought to the Temple and it appears to be right at the transition point — neither clearly young nor clearly old. What is its status?
A borderline bird — at the exact transition moment — has a doubtful status. The Gemara engages with this: it is neither clearly valid nor clearly invalid. The Sages had to work out practical rules for these edge cases, including whether one may offer such a bird or must bring another one to avoid doubt.
Question 5 of 5
What is the difference in blood service between a bird Chatat and a bird Olah?
The Chatat bird requires blood to be sprinkled with the head and body still connected. The Olah bird's blood is squeezed against the altar wall — a different method. There is a debate about whether the Kohen must hold head and body together for the Olah, with different views derived from different verses in the Torah's altar service laws.
