🕊️ Doves vs. Pigeons — Age Rules

Torah verse: "A burnt offering of birds — from young pigeons or from doves" (Leviticus 1:14). The Torah uses different adjectives: "young" only for pigeons, not for doves.

Pigeons (Yonim)

  • 📜 Torah says "young pigeons"
  • 🐣 Only young pigeons are valid
  • ❌ Old pigeons: invalid for offerings
  • 📋 "Young" is the qualifier — without it: no

Doves (Torim)

  • 📜 Torah says just "doves" — no age qualifier
  • 🐦 Only adult doves are valid
  • ❌ Young (baby) doves: invalid
  • 📋 Absence of "young" means: adult only

📅 What Age Is "Valid"?

Q
What exactly is the cutoff age? When does a pigeon stop being "young"? When does a dove become an adult?
Rv
Rava: The signs are visible. For pigeons — when the golden neck feathers begin to appear, the bird is transitioning from young to old. Once the yellow shows, it's at the border.
Gm
The Gemara derives from Scripture: "Young" pigeons — clearly younger. The absence of "young" for doves means older. The Torah chose its words precisely to create opposite rules for the two bird types.
The boundary moment: A bird at the exact transition point — neither clearly young nor clearly old — is in a doubtful state. The Gemara works out the practical halachic consequences of this borderline case.

🔄 Why Opposite Rules for the Two Species?

Why are young pigeons valid but young doves invalid?
↓
Rava's derivation: The Torah never deviates from the natural order unnecessarily. Young pigeons are the norm for sacrifice — doves are mentioned without "young" to signal: take the older ones.
↓
If doves were also valid when young, the Torah would not need the word "doves" at all — "young pigeons" would cover both
↓
The separate mention of "doves" without "young" = a deliberate signal that adult doves (not young ones) are what is required

🐦 Bird Olah — Special Rules Continued

A bird Olah (burnt offering) is completely burned on the altar
↓
The Kohen pinches the nape and squeezes out the blood against the altar wall
↓
Must he hold the head and body together while sprinkling? (Like the Chatat?)
↓
Yes (one view): The verse "and he shall bring it" implies holding both together
No (another view): The Olah's blood is squeezed differently — head and body need not remain connected
