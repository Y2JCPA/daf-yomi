Chullin 42 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded

Question 1 of 5
Which of the following is NOT listed as a treifah condition in the Mishna of Chullin 42?
A broken leg bone is NOT a treifah condition in the Mishna. The Mishna specifies: perforated gullet, cut windpipe, perforated brain membrane, perforated heart, severed spine, removed liver, perforated/missing lung, perforated abomasum/gallbladder/intestines, torn inner rumen, fell from roof, fractured majority of ribs, clawed by predator. A simple broken leg is not on the list.
Question 2 of 5
From which verse does Reish Lakish derive the broad concept of treifah (beyond animals mauled in the field)?
Reish Lakish finds the allusion in Leviticus 11:2: "These are the living things which you may eat." The word "living" implies: you may eat only animals that are capable of living. An animal with an internal condition that prevents survival ("not living") is therefore excluded from what you may eat — this is the Torah source for all 18 treifot categories.
Question 3 of 5
According to the view that "a treifah cannot live," what would be the implication if an animal somehow survived despite a treifah wound?
According to the view that treifah is forbidden because the animal cannot live, if survival were demonstrated, the logical basis for the prohibition would be undermined. This is why the other view (treifah is a Sinai category regardless of survival) is important — it makes the prohibition unconditional. In practice, the halacha is that even a "surviving" treifah remains forbidden.
Question 4 of 5
Why does the Gemara engage in extensive counting to confirm the number 18 treifot?
Ulla (Daf 43) will teach that the 18 treifot were given to Moses at Sinai. This makes the number canonically fixed — not just a coincidence or a count of what we observe. If more cases seem to qualify, we must explain why they fall within an existing category; we cannot simply add new ones. The counting exercise ensures the traditional list is internally consistent.
Question 5 of 5
Rabbi Shimon disagrees with the Mishna about the lung treifah. What is his position?
Rabbi Shimon holds that a perforated lung is a treifah only if the perforation goes all the way through to the bronchi (the inner airway passages). A surface perforation that doesn't reach the bronchi — according to Rabbi Shimon — does not render the animal a treifah. The Mishna's first view counts any perforation as treifah, which is the more stringent position.
