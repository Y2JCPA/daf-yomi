בשני בצורת אין מעברין את השנה
🌾 Proof from Ba'al Shalisha (Amud A) Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi proves that one may not intercalate during a famine from the story of Elisha (II Kings 4:42). A man brought first-fruits from Ba'al Shalisha — the fastest-ripening land in all of Eretz Yisrael — yet only barley had ripened. Even in the prime growing region, the spring produce was barely sufficient after the omer. This shows a year may be ripe for intercalation, yet it is forbidden during famine because adding a month delays the omer offering and prolongs the prohibition on the new grain crop.

📋 Six Restrictions on Intercalation

Not before Rosh HaShanah — though if done under exigent circumstances, it stands
↓
Not from year to year — cannot declare the coming year a leap year while still in the current one
↓
Not three consecutive years — yet Rabbi Akiva reportedly did so from prison with the Nasi's blessing (Rabbi Shimon)
↓
Not in the Sabbatical Year — would unduly extend the Shemita prohibitions
↓
Not in the year after Shemita — produce is scarce; extra month causes hardship
↓
Not on the 30th of Adar — Shmuel: since it is fit to be Nisan, it is halakhically treated as Nisan already

🩸 Does Ritual Impurity Justify Intercalation?

Rabbi Yehuda
Yes — the court may intercalate due to impurity, to give the people time to purify before Passover. Proof: Hezekiah intercalated for this very reason (II Chr. 30:2). The frontplate of the High Priest only atones when it is on his forehead — so impurity without the frontplate is a real concern.
Rabbi Shimon
If they intercalated due to impurity, it is intercalated — but Hezekiah still prayed for forgiveness! Why? Because the frontplate atones whether or not it is worn. Therefore impurity alone was never a valid reason; Hezekiah sought forgiveness because he intercalated improperly.
Rabbi Yehuda
The Yom Kippur service refutes you — the High Priest wears linen garments and the frontplate is absent, yet the offerings are accepted. But that is an exception; ordinary Paschal offerings still need the frontplate worn!
Rabbi Shimon
Yom Kippur proves my point: even without the frontplate on the forehead, the service is valid. This shows the frontplate atones regardless of whether it is worn — so impurity is never grounds for intercalation.
Gemara's Note Each Tanna is internally consistent: Rabbi Yehuda requires the frontplate to be worn for atonement; Rabbi Shimon holds it atones even when not worn. Their dispute about intercalation for impurity flows directly from this underlying disagreement.

👑 Why Did Hezekiah Seek Forgiveness?

Gemara's Answer

  • Intercalation must be of Adar, not any other month
  • Hezekiah intercalated Nisan during Nisan itself
  • He thought it was still Adar on that day
  • We do not apply 'since it is fit' retroactively
  • His intercalation was therefore improper → he sought divine compassion

R. Shimon b. Yehuda's Answer

  • Hezekiah encouraged people to keep Second Pesaḥ
  • He mistakenly believed women are obligated in the first Pesaḥ
  • Minority impure → defer minority to Second Pesaḥ
  • He deferred the majority of men (impure) alongside the minority
  • Realizing his error about women's obligation, he sought forgiveness
