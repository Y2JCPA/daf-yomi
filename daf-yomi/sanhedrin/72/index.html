📖 Ben Sorer U'moreh — The Rebellious Son's True Verdict Rabbi Yosei HaGelili: The Torah does not condemn the rebellious son merely for eating meat and drinking wine. Rather, the Torah foresees his future — he will inevitably become a murderer to fund his habits. Better he die now while innocent than die later as a convicted murderer.

The death of the wicked is beneficial to them and to the world. The death of the righteous is harmful to them and to the world.

💰 Does the Burglar Acquire the Stolen Vessels?

Burglar breaks in, takes vessels, then flees and is caught later
↓
Rav: He is exempt from paying — he acquired them 'with his blood' (his life was forfeit at the moment of breaking in)
↓
Vessels still extant
Rav: Still exempt — no distinction between extant and destroyed; his forfeited life paid for them
Vessels destroyed (e.g., thrown in river)
Also exempt — his life was already pledged; the Torah released him from payment
↓
Rava's ruling in practice: When burglars returned his stolen rams, Rava refused to accept them — they acquired the animals 'with their blood'

⚔️ Objections to Rav's Ruling — Vessels and Shabbat

Rav
A burglar who took vessels and was caught afterward is exempt from payment. His life being forfeit at the moment of entry means he already 'paid' for them with his blood.
Rav Beivai bar Abaye
Objection from baraita: One who steals a purse on Shabbat is liable to pay even though Shabbat is a capital offense — a capital act doesn't wipe out monetary obligation!
Rava
The baraita speaks of a case where the purse was thrown into a river. The Merciful One released liability only for accidents — not for intentional destruction. The burglar case is different.
Mishna (objection)
If a burglar broke a barrel while breaking in: when there is blood-guiltiness he is liable; when there is no blood-guiltiness he is exempt. Why teach 'broke' — isn't 'took' more obvious?
Gemara
The mishna teaches that when there is blood-guiltiness, he is liable even for unintentional damage — this is the novel teaching, not the taking.
Key Principle A burglar whose life was forfeit 'acquires' stolen items — his forfeited life serves as payment, exempting him from monetary restitution

🌅 Blood-Guiltiness vs. No Blood-Guiltiness

No Blood-Guiltiness (May Kill)

  • Burglar breaking in — presumed dangerous
  • Applies on weekdays AND Shabbat equally
  • Anyone (not only the owner) may kill him
  • He is classified as a rodef — pursuer
  • If building collapses on him on Shabbat — no obligation to rescue
  • Burglar exempt from paying for damage to vessels

Blood-Guiltiness (May Not Kill)

  • 'Sun rose upon him' — intentions are clearly peaceful
  • Applies on weekdays AND Shabbat equally
  • Killing him is murder — owner is liable
  • A father breaking into his son's house: father has compassion — presumed peaceful
  • If building collapses on Shabbat — obligated to clear rubble and rescue him
  • Burglar liable for all damage he causes

🏃 The Pursuer (Rodef) Law — Can a Minor Be Killed?

General Rule: A pursuer (rodef) chasing someone to kill — any bystander may kill the pursuer to save the victim. No warning required per the burglar analogy.
↓
Rav Huna's ruling: Even a minor who is pursuing another may be killed to save the victim — his lack of legal responsibility does not protect him when acting as a pursuer.
↓
Rav Ḥisda's objection: A fetus endangering the mother may be destroyed to save her. But once the head has emerged, we may not kill it — 'one life does not supersede another.' If any pursuer could be killed, why does emergence matter?
↓
Resolution: The emerging fetus is a unique case — Heaven is the pursuer, not the fetus itself (natural birth process). The fetus has no agency and thus isn't a true rodef once born.
↓
Forewarning debate: A pursuer need not be warned before being killed to save the victim — supported by the burglar who is killed without warning. A baraita requiring warning is explained as referring to a case where the rescuer and pursuer are separated by a river and cannot physically stop him otherwise.
