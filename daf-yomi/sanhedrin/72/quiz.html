QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, why does the Torah permit executing the ben sorer u'moreh, even though his current crime seems minor?
A. Because eating non-kosher meat and wine is itself a severe transgression
B. Because the Torah foresees he will inevitably become a murderer, and it is better he die now while innocent
C. Because his parents have formally declared him lost to them
D. Because he has disgraced his family and community beyond repair
Correct: B — Rabbi Yosei HaGelili explains the Torah judges the ben sorer u'moreh based on his likely future trajectory — he will become a murderer to fund his appetites. The Torah says: better he die now while still technically innocent (zaka'i) than later as a convicted murderer.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is Rava's explanation for why a burglar breaking into a house may be killed with impunity?
A. The Torah gives blanket permission to kill anyone found trespassing at night
B. There is a presumption that a person does not restrain himself when his money is threatened, so the burglar must have resolved to kill the owner if resisted
C. A burglar automatically loses all his legal rights the moment he crosses the threshold
D. The owner is permitted to kill in defense of property alone, even without threat to life
Correct: B — Rava explains that since people do not restrain themselves when their money is at stake, we presume the burglar said: 'If the owner confronts me, I will kill him.' This makes the burglar a rodef, and the owner may preempt him.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Rav rules that a burglar who took vessels and was caught afterward is exempt from paying for them. What is his reason?
A. Stolen goods change ownership once physically removed from the premises
B. He acquired them 'with his blood' — his forfeited life during the break-in serves as payment
C. The owner implicitly waived his claim by not catching the burglar in the act
D. The vessels are considered lost property once taken during a capital act
Correct: B — Rav rules that because the burglar's life was forfeit at the moment of breaking in, he effectively 'paid' for the vessels with his life at that instant. This acquisition by life-forfeit exempts him from separate monetary restitution.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What does 'if the sun is risen upon him, there shall be blood shed on his account' mean according to the Gemara's interpretation?
A. The killing is only permitted during nighttime hours, not after sunrise
B. Once the sun rises, the burglar has had sufficient warning to leave
C. If the matter is as clear as the sun that the burglar's intentions are peaceful — such as a father entering his son's house — then killing him is murder
D. If the sun has risen and the burglar remains, he must be apprehended rather than killed
Correct: C — The Gemara interprets 'the sun rose upon him' figuratively: if the burglar's peaceful intent is as clear as the sun (the classic case is a father breaking into his son's house, since a father has deep compassion for his son), then killing him constitutes murder.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What was Rav Ḥisda's objection to Rav Huna's ruling that a minor pursuer may be killed to save the victim?
A. Minors cannot be considered pursuers because they lack legal intent (da'at)
B. A fetus endangering the mother may be killed, but once the head has emerged it may not be — suggesting being a pursuer alone is insufficient grounds to kill a minor
C. The rodef law requires the pursuer to have been warned, which a minor cannot process
D. Killing a minor is always forbidden regardless of circumstances in Jewish law
Correct: B — Rav Ḥisda notes: if any minor pursuer could be killed, why does the law change once the fetus's head emerges during difficult labor? The Gemara resolves this by distinguishing: in childbirth, Heaven is the pursuer (natural process), not the fetus — so the emerged fetus is not a true rodef and cannot be killed.
