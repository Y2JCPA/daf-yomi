☠️ Who Receives Death at the Hand of Heaven?

Food / Produce Violations

  • Eating untithed produce (tevel)
  • Impure priest eating pure teruma
  • Non-priest eating teruma (disputed — Rav says lashes only)

Temple Service Violations

  • Non-priest performing service
  • Impure priest performing service
  • Tevul yom (immersed, awaiting nightfall)
  • Priest lacking priestly vestments
  • Priest without atonement offering (mechussar kippurim)
  • Priest with unwashed hands/feet
  • Priest intoxicated with wine
  • Priest with overgrown hair

Prohibition Only — NOT Death

  • Uncircumcised priest performing service
  • Acute mourner (onen) on day of relative's death
  • Priest performing Temple service while seated

⚖️ From Which Source Do We Derive the Punishment for Tevel?

Tanna (first position)
Derive the punishment for eating tevel (untithed produce) from teruma: the word חִלּוּל (desecration) links them. Since an impure priest who eats pure teruma is liable for death, so too eating tevel incurs death at the hand of Heaven.
Challenger
On the contrary! Derive from notar (leftover offering): both tevel and notar involve the intrinsic unfitness of the food itself, and neither can become permitted to all people — unlike teruma which is permitted to priests!
Tanna (rebuttal)
Teruma is the better source: tevel and teruma both involve produce-based separation obligations. Both can become permitted through a corrective action (separating the tithes / priest becoming pure). These common features make teruma the stronger analogy.
Challenger (counter)
But notar is equally apt: both notar and tevel can never become permitted to a non-priest at all, and both relate to the intrinsic status of the food rather than the status of the person eating it!
Resolution The Gemara accepts the teruma derivation: tevel and teruma share more common features (produce-based, correctable by action, belong to the same halakhic family of separations). Eating tevel = death at the hand of Heaven.

🧪 Impure Priest — Three Contexts, One Principle

Impure Priest Eats Pure Teruma

  • Punishment: Death at hand of Heaven
  • Source: Leviticus 22 — חִלּוּל (desecration) term
  • Pure teruma → liable
  • Impure teruma → NOT liable (already desecrated)
  • Foundational case used to derive other punishments

Impure Priest Performs Temple Service

  • Punishment: Death at hand of Heaven
  • Source: 'Separate from the sacred items' (Lev. 22:2)
  • Further confirmed via gezerah shavah from teruma
  • Tevul yom performing service → also death
  • Mechussar kippurim performing service → also death

Non-Priest and Teruma (Dispute)

  • Baraita: death at hand of Heaven
  • Rav: flogged only, not death
  • Rav's reasoning: 'I am the Lord' ends the death clause
  • Baraita challenged Rav — unresolved tension
  • Non-priest Temple service: clearly death (Num. 18:7)

🌾 Deriving the Punishment for Eating Tevel

Question: What is the punishment for eating tevel (produce from which tithes have not been separated)?
↓
Shmuel in the name of Rabbi Eliezer: The word חִלּוּל (desecration) appears in the tevel passage (Leviticus 19:8 context) — trigger for verbal analogy
↓
Option A — Derive from Teruma
Both involve produce separation. Both can become permitted by action. Both are produce-based obligations. → Source: impure priest + teruma = death → ACCEPTED
Option B — Derive from Notar
Both involve intrinsic unfitness of food. Neither can become permitted to all people. → Would also imply death → REJECTED (teruma shares more features)
↓
Conclusion: Eating tevel = Death at the Hand of Heaven — derived via verbal analogy (gezerah shavah) from the teruma prohibition

📐 How the Gemara Uses Gezerah Shavah (Verbal Analogy)

Step 1 — Locate shared terminology: Identify the same Hebrew word (e.g., חִלּוּל / desecration) appearing in two separate Torah passages
↓
Step 2 — Anchor on a known ruling: One passage already has a clear halakhic outcome (e.g., impure priest + teruma = death at hand of Heaven)
↓
Step 3 — Transfer the ruling: Apply the same legal consequence to the second passage by virtue of the shared term — even without an explicit verse in the new case
↓
Step 4 — Test against competing analogies: If multiple passages share the term, each must be evaluated. The Gemara debates: which source is more similar to the target?
↓
Step 5 — Resolve by common features: The source that shares the most halakhic characteristics with the target wins (e.g., teruma beats notar for tevel because both are produce-based and correctable)
↓
Daf 83 applications: Tevel ← teruma | Impure priest in Temple ← teruma | Tevul yom in Temple ← earlier chain | Each derivation is debated and defended
