⚖️ Non-Priest Temple Service: Which Form of Execution?

Rabbi Yishmael
The verse says yamut (Num 18:7). I compare it to the false prophet who prophesied without hearing — also yamut — executed by strangulation. Therefore a non-priest who serves is strangled.
Rabbi Akiva
One derives yumat (passive) from yumat, not from yamut (active). I compare the non-priest's yumat to the false prophet in Deut 13:6 — also yumat — executed by strangulation.
Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri
I disagree with Rabbi Akiva's conclusion here. The non-priest who performs Temple service is executed by decapitation with a sword, not strangulation.
Gemara
The dispute between Akiva and Yoḥanan ben Nuri mirrors the broader tannaitic dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis. These are two tannaim disagreeing about Rabbi Akiva's own position.
Key Distinction Rabbi Akiva: derives yumat from yumat only (passive→passive). Rabbi Yishmael: one may also derive yumat from yamut (active→passive).

🪢 Mishna — Executed by Strangulation

אֵלּוּ הֵן הַנֶּחֱנָקִין: הַמַּכֶּה אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ, וְהַגּוֹנֵב נֶפֶשׁ מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, וְזָקֵן מַמְרֵא
מִשְׁנָה — 84b The following are executed by strangulation:

• One who strikes his father or mother
• One who abducts a Jewish person
• A rebellious elder who defies the Sanhedrin
• A false prophet
• One who prophesies in the name of an idol
• One who prophesies what he did not hear

Strangulation is the default execution method when Scripture does not explicitly specify another.

👊 When Is One Liable for Striking a Parent?

One strikes his father or mother
↓
Does the strike cause a wound with blood drawn?
↓
Yes — blood drawn
Liable — executed by strangulation
Derived by juxtaposing striking a person with striking an animal (Lev 24:21)
No — no blood drawn
Exempt from death penalty
Just as animal-striking without actual damage = no liability, striking a parent without wounding = no execution
↓
If parent is killed: Killing a stranger = decapitation. Killing a parent = strangulation (more severe view) — showing one is liable even before the parent dies

🩺 May a Son Perform Medical Treatment on His Father?

Dilemma raised: A son who lets blood (phlebotomy) for his father — is he liable for wounding a parent?
↓
Rav Mattana: Permitted — derived from "love your neighbor as yourself" (Lev 19:18). Treating a parent is an act of love and is permitted by the Torah.
↓
Rav's practice: Did not allow his son to extract a thorn from him — concern that his son might unwittingly wound him during the procedure.
↓
Mar son of Ravina: Did not allow his son to pierce his blister — same concern: even accidental wounding could violate the prohibition against striking a parent.
↓
Key distinction: If a stranger treats him and wounds accidentally — only a general prohibition. If a son wounds him accidentally — strikes his parent, a more severe violation.
↓
Conclusion: The juxtaposition of striking man/animal (Lev 24:21) teaches that just as one who strikes an animal for medical purposes is exempt, so too a son who wounds a parent for medical purposes is exempt from death.
