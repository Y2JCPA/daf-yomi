⚡ Two Halakhic Worlds: Blasphemy and Idolatry

Blasphemy — Amud A

  • Liable only for cursing the Tetragrammaton (יהוה)
  • Not liable for cursing the two-letter name (יה)
  • Judges must rise when the exact curse is repeated
  • All who hear blasphemy must tear their garments
  • Tears are never fully stitched closed again
  • 'I too heard as he did' is valid Torah testimony

Idolatry — Amud B

  • Four acts = death by stoning: slaughter, incense, libation, bowing
  • Capital even if not the idol's typical form of worship
  • Affectionate acts (hugging, kissing, etc.) = prohibition only
  • Ba'al Peor: defecating before it = death (that IS its worship)
  • Mercury/Markulis: throwing a stone = death (its worship)
  • Derived from Exodus 22:19 and Deuteronomy 17:3–5

⚡ Which Divine Name Triggers Blasphemy Liability?

Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov
One is liable for blasphemy only if he curses the Tetragrammaton (the four-letter name יהוה). One who curses only the two-letter name (יה, yod-heh) is not liable for capital punishment.
Gemara Challenge
Isn't that obvious? The mishna already uses the coded phrase 'יכה יוסי את יוסי — let Yosei smite Yosei,' which encodes a four-letter name. Why state this explicitly?
Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov (Answer)
Lest you say the mishna uses that name merely as a convenient example but one is actually liable even for the two-letter name — therefore it must be stated: only the Tetragrammaton obligates.
Alternative Reading
In an alternative version: Rav Aḥa teaches that the Tetragrammaton suffices — one need not curse the 42-letter name to be liable. Lest you say only a 'grand name' triggers capital punishment, the four-letter name is sufficient.
Bottom Line Capital blasphemy (חירוף וגידוף) requires cursing the Tetragrammaton (שם המפורש). Cursing a mere appellation, or the two-letter name, does not incur the death penalty.

👔 The Law of Tearing Garments Upon Hearing Blasphemy

Who must tear? Both one who hears blasphemy directly and one who hears it secondhand from someone who heard it — both are fully obligated (Baraita).
↓
Judges must stand when the exact curse is repeated in court. Source: Eglon king of Moab rose when Ehud announced 'I have a word of God for you' — one rises upon hearing the Divine Name invoked.
↓
Source for tearing: Eliakim, Shebna, and Joah tore their garments upon hearing Rabshakeh's blasphemy against God (Isaiah 36:22). This establishes the universal obligation.
↓
Witnesses are exempt from tearing a second time when they testify in court. The verse uses the past tense 'when they heard' (בשמעם) — they fulfilled the obligation when they first heard the curse; one tear per incident suffices.
↓
Tears are never fully stitched closed — derived via verbal analogy (גזרה שוה) with Elijah's mantle that was permanently split. Just as that cloak was never fully repaired, so too blasphemy-tears remain.
↓
Shmuel: Hearing blasphemy from a gentile does NOT obligate tearing. Although Hezekiah's servants tore upon hearing Rabshakeh, that was exceptional — a grave national-theological crisis.
↓
Shmuel further: One tears only for the ineffable Name (Tetragrammaton), not for an appellation. Rav Ḥiyya: Nowadays, one need not tear even for gentile blasphemy — or one's garment would be perpetually full of tears!

🗿 Idolatry: Capital Punishment vs. Prohibition Only

⚔️ Death Penalty — Stoning

  • 🔪 Slaughtering an animal as a sacrifice
  • 🕯️ Burning incense as an offering
  • 🍷 Pouring a libation
  • 🙇 Bowing down to the idol
  • 💩 Defecating before Ba'al Peor (its unique worship)
  • 🪨 Throwing a stone at Mercury/Markulis (its worship)
  • Capital even if not the idol's typical worship

🚫 Prohibition Only — No Death Penalty

  • 🤗 Hugging the idol
  • 💋 Kissing the idol
  • 🧹 Cleaning or dusting it
  • 💦 Spraying water before it
  • 🛁 Washing it
  • 🫒 Rubbing it with oil
  • 👗 Dressing it
  • 👟 Putting its shoes on it

📜 Deriving Capital Punishment for Idolatry from Scripture

Exodus 22:19: 'One who sacrifices to the gods shall be utterly destroyed, except to the Lord alone' — the Torah singles out slaughter
↓
Why mention slaughter specifically?
To compare all four Temple rites to it: slaughter, incense, libation, bowing — all carry death, even for non-standard worship of the idol
Why not include hugging/kissing?
The verse singles out sacrificial rites. Affectionate acts are not Temple rites — so 'utterly destroyed' does not apply to them
↓
Deuteronomy 17:3 + 17:5: 'Served other gods and bowed to them' → 'shall be stoned' — adds bowing as a separate capital offense
↓
Why is bowing singled out separately? Bowing is NOT a Temple rite. By mentioning it explicitly, the Torah teaches it carries death even though it falls outside the four Temple rites.
↓
Practical upshot: One who performs any of the four Temple rites (or bows) for any idol is executed — even if that idol is typically worshipped differently. Slaughter also teaches that intent for one rite applied to another still renders one liable.
