⚖️ Why Can Three Laymen Judge Monetary Cases?

Rava
Three laymen may judge monetary cases because the Sages enacted a leniency per Rabbi Ḥanina — to avoid locking the door in the face of potential borrowers, who would be deterred if ordained judges were always required.
Rav Aḥa bar Rav Ika
Three laymen may judge because three sitting together cannot be idle street-corner idlers — their very assembly implies legitimacy, unlike one or two who might be loitering.
Practical Difference Per Rava, laymen may judge even where ordained judges are available. Per Rav Aḥa, laymen may judge only where ordained judges are unavailable — and the concern is not borrowers but judicial propriety.

🔢 Deriving Three Judges from Scripture

Exodus 22:7–8 — The word אֱלֹהִים (the judges / the court) appears three times in the passage about a disputed bailment
↓
Rabbi Yoshiya
Tallies from the first mention onward — each of the three instances adds one judge → three judges required
Rabbi Yonatan
First mention is primary text and yields no tally. Second + third = two judges. Since two cannot decide alone, add one → three judges total
↓
Both conclude three judges are required — they disagree only on whether tallies are derived from the first mention of a term in the Torah

🗣️ Laymen as Judges: The Core Dispute

Questioner
If laymen (hedyotot) may adjudicate monetary cases, should they — unlike expert judges — be liable to compensate a party harmed by an erroneous verdict?
Gemara
If laymen were held liable, no one would ever agree to serve as a judge, since everyone is a layman in some respect. The Sages therefore ruled laymen are also exempt — just like ordained experts.
Rava
The mishna teaches two separate matters: (1) monetary cases need three judges, and (2) per Rabbi Ḥanina's leniency, laymen suffice — so as not to lock the door on potential borrowers.
Rav Aḥa bar Rav Ika
The reason laymen may judge is not Rabbi Ḥanina's leniency but judicial propriety: three people sitting together cannot plausibly be idle idlers — their gathering signals a proper court has convened.
Halakhic Consequence If Rava is right, the leniency is universal. If Rav Aḥa is right, laymen may judge only where no ordained judges are available — because the concern is appearances, not borrower welfare.

🏛️ Rabbi Yoshiya vs. Rabbi Yonatan

Rabbi Yoshiya

  • Derives tallies from the first mention of a term in the Torah
  • All three instances of אֱלֹהִים in Exodus 22 count → three judges
  • Does not require courts to have an odd number of judges
  • Follows Rabbi Yehuda: Great Sanhedrin = 70 (an even number)
  • A majority of 2-out-of-3 is still valid even without the odd-number rule

Rabbi Yonatan

  • Does not tally from the first mention — it conveys plain meaning
  • Only the second and third אֱלֹהִים add judges → then one more for decisions
  • Accepts the requirement for an odd number of judges
  • Follows the standard: courts must be odd to prevent hung verdicts
  • Uses idiomatic argument: 'Go to the regular court' = common-language usage, not a tally

📜 Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's Five-Judge Court — and Its Refutation

"אֶסְפָה לִּי שִׁבְעִים אִישׁ מִזִּקְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל" — במדבר י"א:ט"ז
רַבִּי — Rejected Opinion Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi held that monetary cases require five judges, so that a verdict can be rendered by a majority of three.

His reasoning: The verse 'he whom the court shall condemn' (Exodus 22:8) is in the plural, implying two judges condemn — but two cannot decide alone, so add one → three decide. Those three must be a majority of a larger body → five total.

Rabbi Abbahu's refutation: By this logic, the Great Sanhedrin would need 141 judges (for 71 to form a majority), and a lesser Sanhedrin would need 45 — both flatly contradict established tradition.

Resolution: The Torah's own language — 'Gather Me seventy men' (Numbers 11:16) — means that from the moment of gathering, the court must be at its full number. The court's size is fixed by its composition, not by the size of the majority needed for a verdict.
