⚖️ Two Great Unresolved Disputes of Daf 46

Rabbi Eliezer
Women ARE hung after execution — corpse faces the tree. Proof: Shimon ben Shatach hanged 80 women in Ashkelon!
Rabbis
No proof from Ashkelon — he acted as an emergency measure (hora'at sha'ah), hanging 80 in one day. Not standard halakha.
Position A
Burial is required on account of DISGRACE — so the body does not decompose shamefully in public view.
Position B
Burial is required on account of ATONEMENT — earth's decay is itself an expiation for the sins of the deceased.
Core Tension Post-execution rites — hanging, burial, eulogy — balance human dignity, divine compassion, and communal atonement. Both major disputes are left unresolved (תיקו).

⚖️ Rabbi Eliezer vs. Rabbis: Hanging Women

Rabbi Eliezer

  • Women ARE hung after execution
  • Corpse of a woman faces the tree
  • Proof: Shimon ben Shatach hanged 80 women in Ashkelon
  • 'Him' (אותו) excludes only a minor, not a woman
  • Stubborn and rebellious son excluded by 'man' + 'sin'

Rabbis

  • Women are NOT hung after execution
  • Only men — derived from 'a man' (אדם) in the verse
  • Ashkelon was an emergency measure, not precedent
  • One court cannot judge two capital cases in one day
  • 'Him' (אותו) teaches the corpse is hung without clothing

🪵 Mishna: Procedure for Hanging the Corpse

1. Construct the post: Sink a pole into the earth with a crossbeam jutting out — forming a T-shaped structure (כיוצא בו של חרשים)
↓
2. Position the hands: The court appointee places the dead man's two hands one upon the other, then hangs the corpse
↓
3. Untie immediately: The corpse hangs only for a brief moment — untied at once
↓
4. Bury before sunset: Leaving overnight violates: "His body shall not remain all night upon the tree" (Deut. 21:23)
↓
5. Special graveyard: Two court graveyards — one for those killed by decapitation, one for those killed by stoning. Not in ancestral burial plot.
↓
6. No public mourning: Relatives do not observe full mourning rites — unmourned death atones. But private grief in the heart is permitted.

🪦 Why Must We Bury the Dead?

Disgrace Theory
Burial prevents disgrace — the body should not decompose shamefully in public view. It is a matter of human dignity (כבוד הבריות).
Atonement Theory
Burial provides atonement — decomposition in the earth expiates the deceased's sins. Burial is a kindness to the soul.
Practical Difference
If someone says before dying: "I do not want to be buried" — if disgrace, honor his wish; if atonement, bury him anyway for his own spiritual benefit.
Challenge to Atonement
The patriarchs Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob were all buried — do the righteous need atonement? (The Gemara deflects: even the righteous have some minor need for atonement)
תיקו — Unresolved All proofs from Jeremiah, Ahijah the Shilonite, and the patriarchs are deflected. The dilemma stands: burial may be for disgrace or atonement — the Gemara does not decide.

📢 For Whom Is the Eulogy Delivered?

Is a eulogy (הספד) delivered for the honor of the living relatives, or for the honor of the dead?
↓
For the Living
Relatives receive comfort and honor; if no relatives survive, no eulogy required
For the Dead
The deceased deserves honor regardless of surviving relatives
↓
Proof attempt: Abraham traveled to eulogize Sarah (Gen. 23:2), delaying burial — suggests eulogy honors the dead
↓
Counter-proof: Jeremiah curses the wicked: 'They shall not be eulogized nor buried' (Jer. 16:4) — if for the living, why curse the wicked person with lack of eulogy?
↓
Dilemma remains unresolved — תיקו
All proofs are deflected; both reasons remain viable
