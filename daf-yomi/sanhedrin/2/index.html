📜 How the Mishna Derives 23 Judges

Numbers 35:24–25
"The congregation shall judge… the congregation shall save"
Two uses of 'congregation'
↓
Each congregation = 10 men
(from spies: "this evil congregation" = the 10 faithless spies)
2 × 10 = 20 judges
↓
Exod. 23:2 implies majority to convict AND separate majority to acquit
→ Add 2 more judges = 22
↓
22 is an even number — court may deadlock!
→ Add one more to ensure a majority is always possible
↓
Total: 23 Judges
The lesser Sanhedrin for capital cases

🤔 Why List Robbery Separately from Monetary Law?

Gemara
The mishna teaches both monetary law AND robbery require three judges. But isn't robbery simply a type of monetary law? Why list it separately?
R. Abbahu
Consider: even a single layperson's monetary verdict stands after the fact. So clearly admissions and loans have a lower bar — what then IS the meaningful distinction?
Gemara
The tanna must hold that a merging of Torah portions applies here — the word אֱלֹהִים in Exodus 22:8 teaches that robbery judges must be like the divine court: ordained experts!
Resolution
Precisely. For admissions and loans — any three laypeople suffice. For robbery and injury — three ordained, expert judges (מֻמְחִין) are required. The specification was essential!
The Core Distinction Admissions/loans → any three people | Robbery/injury → three ordained experts (mumḥin)

🏙️ Establishing a Lesser Sanhedrin

Minimum city population: 120 men (Tanna Kamma) — enough for 23 judges plus citizens to fill civic and military roles
↓
Rabbi Neḥemya disagrees: 230 men required — one judge per ten citizens, corresponding to Moses' ministers of tens (Exod. 18)
↓
The court of 23 must be ordained experts — drawn from those qualified in Torah law within the city or region
↓
Odd number is mandatory — a court of even judges risks deadlock with no majority verdict possible
↓
The court deliberates: majority of one acquits; majority of two convicts — requiring the extra judge to guarantee this asymmetry

⚡ The Word אֱלֹהִים — Judges as Divine Emissaries

וְנִקְרַב בַּעַל הַבַּיִת אֶל הָאֱלֹהִים — שמות כב:ז
Foundational Principle The requirement for ordained expert judges in cases of robbery and injury is derived from Exodus 22:8: "the owner shall come near unto the court [ha'Elohim]". The Talmud reads Elohim literally — the judges must embody divine justice, meaning they must be formally ordained (סְמִיכָה) and expert in law. This is why the tanna was compelled to list robbery separately from ordinary monetary law: the same number of judges (three) is required, but the qualifications differ fundamentally.
