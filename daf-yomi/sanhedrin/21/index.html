👑 How Many Wives May a King Marry?

Mishna
The king may marry no more than 18 wives, derived from David's 6 wives in Hebron multiplied through Nathan's prophecy: "like these and like those."
Rabbi Yehuda
He may add many wives — provided they do not turn his heart away from God. The verse itself states its own rationale, so we derive the law from it.
Rabbi Shimon
Even one wife who would turn his heart away is forbidden. If so, the verse should have simply said that — why state a number at all?
Ravina
"Like these" = 6 more; "like those" = all prior 12 — total 24 wives. A baraita confirms: he may not marry more than twenty-four.
Gemara's Resolution R. Yehuda normally does not derive law from a verse's stated rationale — but here the verse explains its own reason, so he uses it. R. Shimon counters that if the concern were only heart-turning wives, the verse would never have stated a number at all.

👸 18 vs. 24 Wives: Two Talmudic Counts

Tanna of the Mishna — 18

  • Nathan's rebuke: "like these and like those" (II Sam. 12:8)
  • Rav Kahana: both terms kahenna are equal — each = 6
  • David had 6 wives in Hebron × 3 iterations = 18
  • Eglah = Michal (6th wife, beloved as a calf/egel)

Ravina's View — 24

  • "Like these" = 6 more added to David's 6 (= 12)
  • "Like those" = all prior 12 added again = 24
  • Supported by baraita: "shall not add many" = not more than 24
  • Letter vav in וְכָהֵנָּה read expansively, not comparatively

📖 The King's Torah Scroll — Two Scrolls Required

Every Jew is obligated to write a Torah scroll — even one who inherited a complete scroll from ancestors (Rava: from "write for yourselves this song" — Deut. 31:19)
↓
The King writes two Torah scrolls: one as any Jew fulfills the mitzvah, and a second additional scroll specifically because he is king
↓
Scroll #1 — Treasury Scroll: Deposited in his treasury. He may not simply use his ancestors' scroll; he must write his own. It remains in the treasury at all times.
↓
Scroll #2 — Personal Scroll: Goes out and comes in with him — written very small, like an amulet, and hung on his arm at all times
↓
Source: "I have set the Lord always before me; He is at my right hand, that I shall not be moved" (Psalms 16:8) — the king keeps Torah literally at his side in every moment

🔤 The History of Hebrew Script — From Sinai to Ezra

Originally: Torah given to Israel in Ivrit (Libona'a) script and the Hebrew tongue at Sinai
↓
After Babylonian Exile — Ezra's Reform
Torah re-given in Assyrian script (Ktav Ashuri) and Aramaic tongue for the Jewish people
Who Retained Old Script?
Samaritans (Kutim) kept the Ivrit/Libona'a script; common people also retained it
↓
Rabbi Yosei: Ezra was worthy for the Torah to have been given through him, had Moses not preceded him — he is credited with establishing Assyrian script for Israel
↓
Why are Torah rationales hidden? Because the two commandments whose reasons were revealed — wives and horses — were the very ones Solomon violated, saying: "I know the reason, I will not fail." He did.

🐴 Solomon's Violations — Knowing the Reason Made It Worse

Torah Command
"He shall not accumulate many horses for himself, nor return the people to Egypt for horses" (Deut. 17:16). Reason given: so his heart not turn away.
Solomon
"I will accumulate horses, but I will not return the people to Egypt." — Yet Scripture states: "And Solomon's horses were exported from Egypt" (I Kings 10:29).
Torah Command
"He shall not accumulate many wives for himself, that his heart not turn away" (Deut. 17:17). Reason given explicitly.
Solomon
"I will multiply wives, but my heart will not turn away." — Yet Scripture states: "And his wives turned his heart away" (I Kings 11:3).
Rabbi Yitzḥak's Principle Torah rationales were deliberately hidden — for when the greatest king in the world was told the reason, he relied on his own judgment and failed. Two revealed rationales → two failures by Solomon. Lesson: follow the law, not your interpretation of its reason.
