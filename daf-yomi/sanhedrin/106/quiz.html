QUESTION 1 OF 5
What was wrong with Balaam's 'blessing' comparing Israel to a cedar tree?
A. A cedar is halachically considered an impure species in Jewish law
B. A cedar does not grow near water, has few roots relative to its height, and cannot regenerate when cut — making it a curse in disguise
C. The cedar blessing was actually directed at Midian, not Israel
D. Cedars were the sacred trees of Baal Peor and thus associated with idolatry
Correct: B — The Gemara contrasts Moses's palm blessing (near water, many roots, regenerates) with Balaam's cedar 'blessing' (no water, few roots, cannot regenerate). The cedar's weakness makes this a hidden curse, not a genuine blessing.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba citing Rabbi Simai, what were the fates of the three advisors in Pharaoh's court?
A. All three were eventually punished equally for their role in enslaving Israel
B. Job repented and was forgiven; Balaam and Yitro were both killed by Israel
C. Balaam was killed, Job suffered for his silence, and Yitro's descendants were honored with seats in the Sanhedrin
D. Balaam fled, Job advised drowning the males, and Yitro was killed
Correct: C — Balaam who advised drowning the males was killed by Israel. Job who was silent and did not protest suffered greatly. Yitro who fled was rewarded — his descendants merited seats in the Sanhedrin in the chamber of hewn stone.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What made Balaam's trap at Shittim particularly sophisticated according to the Talmud's account?
A. He used military intimidation to force Israelites into the enclosures
B. He bribed Israelite leaders with gold to betray their people to Moab
C. He used old women outside and young women inside, softening resistance with wine before the idol worship demand came last — when defenses were lowest
D. He convinced Israelites that worshipping Peor was permitted under their own Torah law
Correct: C — The scheme was staged: old women outside (non-threatening), young women inside, wine to lower inhibitions (Ammonite wine not yet forbidden), and only after the Israelite was fully comfortable did the young woman produce the idol and frame the request as trivial.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Yoḥanan say Balaam is called 'the diviner' (ha-kosem) in Joshua 13:22 rather than 'the prophet'?
A. The Torah uses different titles for stylistic variation with no deeper meaning
B. In Midianite culture, 'diviner' was actually a higher title than 'prophet'
C. After sinning, the spirit of prophecy was withdrawn from Balaam, leaving him with only the lesser power of divination
D. He was stripped of the prophet title by the Israelite court after his attempted curses failed
Correct: C — Rabbi Yoḥanan explains: Initially Balaam was a genuine prophet. But after he sinned, divine prophecy was withdrawn from him. By the time of Joshua 13:22, only the degraded status of 'diviner' remained — a step-by-step spiritual demotion.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What does the Gemara mean by concluding 'God seeks the heart' when comparing Doeg and Rav Yehuda?
A. That emotional prayer is always more valuable than intellectual Torah study for everyone
B. That Rav Yehuda could bring rain by merely removing his shoe while Doeg's encyclopedic Torah knowledge brought him no merit — sincere devotion outweighs brilliance
C. That God judges based primarily on family lineage and ancestry rather than deeds
D. That physical mitzvah observance matters less than having correct theological beliefs
Correct: B — Doeg raised 400 unresolved halachic dilemmas and knew every letter of Torah, yet was destroyed. Rav Yehuda, far less 'brilliant,' could bring rain by removing his shoe. The Gemara concludes: God seeks the heart — wholehearted devotion to God's will matters infinitely more than intellectual achievement alone.
