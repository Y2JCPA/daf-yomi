Sanhedrin 69 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Kruspedai citing Rabbi Shabbtai, what is the total window during which a stubborn and rebellious son can be judged and sentenced?
A. One month
B. Three months
C. Six months
D. Nine months
Correct: B — Rabbi Kruspedai teaches the window is only three months. This is because conception can be detected at one-third of a pregnancy (~3 months), and the time between the boy's two pubic hairs and his beard growing is essentially consumed by this gestational window.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Rabba compares a nine-year-old's sperm to what, in order to explain why he cannot actually father a child?
A. A spark that cannot ignite dry wood
B. Grain cut before reaching one-third of its growth
C. Rain that falls on salted earth
D. A river that runs but cannot reach the sea
Correct: B — Rabba says the nine-year-old's sperm is like grain that was cut before reaching one-third of its growth — even if planted, such grain will not sprout. The sperm exists but biologically cannot produce a child.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Ravina's proof that rov (majority rule) applies even in capital cases comes from which scenario?
A. A convert who died without known heirs
B. A designated maidservant and a nine-year-old boy
C. Two witnesses who disagree whether a crime happened on Monday or Tuesday
D. A kohen who married a divorcée
Correct: C — Ravina cites the mishna in Sanhedrin 40a: if witnesses disagree on whether the event was Monday or Tuesday, we do not acquit the defendant — we use the majority of the month's days. This demonstrates rov operates in capital cases.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yitzḥak, who is the biblical figure Iscah (Genesis 11:29)?
A. Rebekah
B. Leah
C. Sarah
D. Miriam
Correct: C — Rabbi Yitzḥak teaches that Iscah is Sarah. She was called Iscah (from sokha, 'to gaze') because she envisioned hidden matters through divine inspiration — i.e., she was a prophetess. This also confirms that Abraham and Sarah were siblings (children of Terah).
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does the Gemara ultimately conclude that a daughter cannot be judged as a stubborn and rebellious child, even though logic might suggest she should?
A. A daughter cannot own property and so cannot steal to fund her habits
B. The verse states 'a son' — it is a scriptural decree that excludes daughters
C. Women are not subject to capital punishment under Torah law
D. A daughter's rebellion is punished through her father's court instead
Correct: B — Rabbi Shimon acknowledges the logical argument for including daughters, but the Torah specifies 'son' (ben). This is a gezeirat hakatuv — a scriptural decree. No matter how compelling the logic, the verse's language is determinative.
