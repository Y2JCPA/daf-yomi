⚖️ Rabbi Meir vs. The Rabbis: Disqualification Rights

Rabbi Meir
Each litigant may disqualify the judge chosen by the other side — and each litigant may likewise disqualify the witnesses brought by the other.
The Rabbis
Disqualification is permitted only when one brings actual proof of unfitness: that a judge is related to a litigant, is personally wicked, or is otherwise halakhically disqualified.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Rabbi Meir's lenient rule applies only to laymen's courts in Syria — not to ordained expert courts, which cannot be freely refused.
Rav Pappa
Even among expert courts in the same city — e.g., the courts of Rav Huna and Rav Ḥisda — a litigant may say: 'Am I burdening you by requesting a different expert court?'
Core Question Does a litigant have inherent power to refuse a judge or witness selected by the opposing party, or must refusal be grounded in proven halakhic disqualification?

📋 How the Three-Judge Panel Is Selected

Step 1: Litigant A selects one judge before whom he wishes to be judged
↓
Step 2: Litigant B selects one judge before whom he wishes to be judged
↓
Step 3: The two chosen judges together select the third judge — both must consent to who joins them
↓
Rav Yehuda citing Rav: Witnesses do not sign a document unless they know who co-signs with them — the same principle of consent applies to judges
↓
Result: A three-judge panel whose formation reflects both litigant input and judicial consent

🏛️ Expert Courts vs. Laymen's Courts in Syria

Laymen's Courts — Syria

  • Either litigant may freely refuse the court chosen by the other
  • No proof of disqualification required
  • Rabbi Meir's rule of free disqualification applies here
  • Reflects informal, non-ordained judicial setting
  • Each side retains broad veto power over court choice

Expert / Ordained Courts

  • A litigant cannot freely refuse a fit, expert judge
  • Refusal requires proof: relation, wickedness, or legal disqualification
  • Fit judges treated like court-ordained experts
  • Rav Pappa: even same-city expert courts cannot be freely refused
  • Rabbis: such judges are effectively beyond litigant veto

📜 The Scrupulous People of Jerusalem

אין עדים חותמין על השטר אלא אם כן יודעין מי חותם עמהם
Rav Yehuda citing Rav — Confirmed by Baraita Witnesses do not sign a document unless they know who is signing with them. The scrupulous people of Jerusalem would not sit in judgment unless they knew who would sit with them. They would not recline at a meal unless they knew who else was dining. And they would not sign a document unless they knew the identity of their fellow signatories — ensuring fitness and integrity at every level.

👥 Can a Litigant Disqualify the Other's Witnesses?

Litigant claims the opposing witness is disqualified from testifying
↓
Rabbi Meir
Litigant CAN disqualify — but Reish Lakish reinterprets: this refers to a litigant who accepted one witness as equivalent to two, then seeks to retract that acceptance
The Rabbis
Litigant CANNOT disqualify — his testimony is tainted by conflict of interest. Only a family flaw claim (e.g., Canaanite slave lineage) may be raised
↓
Rav Dimi citing Rabbi Yoḥanan: Dispute concerns a litigant who claimed two sets of witnesses — must he substantiate their existence? Rabbi Meir: yes. Rabbis: no.
