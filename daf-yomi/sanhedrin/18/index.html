🔢 How Many Men for a Lesser Sanhedrin?

Rabbi Neḥemya
A city needs 277 men to be eligible for a lesser Sanhedrin. The court itself has 23 judges, but we add reserve men in case judges are disqualified — derived from Moses appointing ministers of thousands, hundreds, fifties, and tens: 78 + 155 + ... totaling 230, plus 47 for the court itself.
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi (Baraita)
A city needs 278 men — one more than Rabbi Neḥemya's number.
Gemara (Resolution)
No contradiction! Rabbi Neḥemya counts the Nasi (court president) within his total of 277. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi does not count the Nasi — therefore his total is 278. Same underlying calculation, different counting convention.
Biblical Source Exodus 18:21 — Moses's appointments of ministers of 1,000s, 100s, 50s, and 10s yield 78 senior officers + 155 mid-level + other tiers = 230 laypeople, plus the court of 23 needing 47 backups.

⚔️ High Priest Who Kills — What Is His Fate?

High Priest kills a person
↓
Intentional Murder
He is executed — same as any murderer. His sacred status grants no exemption from capital punishment.
Unintentional Killing
He is exiled to a city of refuge — same as any accidental killer. The baraita states this explicitly.
↓
When does the High Priest leave the city of refuge?
Normal rule: exile ends upon death of the serving High Priest
↓
⚠️ He NEVER leaves — since he himself is the High Priest, his own death is the only triggering event. The Gemara initially thought this might exempt him from exile entirely, but rules: he IS exiled, and simply stays forever.

🗓️ Why King and High Priest Are Excluded from Intercalating the Year

Intercalating the year (declaring a leap year by adding Adar II) requires objective sages to assess whether spring signs have arrived: early grain, late grain, and condition of oven-dried figs.
↓
⚔️ The King is excluded — soldiers receive their wages in grain. A leap year delays the new grain harvest, meaning the King has a direct financial interest in not adding a month. This disqualifies him from impartial judgment.
↓
🌿 The High Priest is excluded — on Yom Kippur he must immerse in cold water five times. If a leap year is added, Tishrei (and Yom Kippur) arrives later in the season when the water is colder. He has a bodily interest in keeping the year short.
↓
📐 Rav Pappa's inference: From the High Priest's concern about cold water, we learn that the seasons follow the months — weather tracks the calendar, not the reverse. This is a calendrical principle derived from this exclusion.
↓
🐄 Cattle herders offered signs — one said grain sprouting simultaneously signals Adar; another said an ox dying in morning snow means it is still Shevat. The Gemara concludes: the Sages relied on their own calculations; the herders merely corroborated them.

🤔 Can the High Priest Testify? A Talmudic Puzzle

כהן גדול דן ודנין אותו, מעיד ומעידין אותו
Gemara — Sanhedrin 18b The mishna says the High Priest testifies — but a baraita states that a person is exempt from returning a lost item (and by extension, from certain civic duties) if doing so compromises their dignity. Since a High Priest's dignity is supreme, can he really be required to testify? Rav Yosef: A High Priest testifies only about a king — such testimony befits his dignity. Gemara objects: But the mishna says the king does not testify and others do not testify about him! Resolution: Rav Yosef means the High Priest testifies before the king — it is not fitting for the High Priest to appear before a lower court where the king presides as judge. The baraita confirms: a king is not seated on the Sanhedrin at all.
