Sanhedrin 32 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
How many judges are required for capital cases (dinei nefashot) according to the Mishna?
A. 3 judges
B. 7 judges
C. 23 judges
D. 71 judges
Correct: C — Capital cases require 23 judges, versus 3 for monetary cases. The larger court reflects the gravity of life-and-death decisions.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In a capital case, what majority is required to convict versus to acquit?
A. Majority of 1 for both
B. Majority of 2 to convict; majority of 1 to acquit
C. Unanimous to convict; majority of 1 to acquit
D. Majority of 2 for both
Correct: B — Conviction requires a majority of 2; acquittal requires only a majority of 1. The asymmetry deliberately favors life.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rabbi Ḥanina resolve the contradiction between the Mishna (inquiry required) and the baraita (no inquiry for loans)?
A. The baraita is rejected as incorrect
B. The Mishna refers only to fines, not regular monetary cases
C. By Torah law both require inquiry; Sages waived it for monetary cases to enable lending
D. Inquiry was never required — the Mishna is speaking theoretically
Correct: C — Rabbi Ḥanina says Torah mandates inquiry for both case types, but the Sages removed the requirement from monetary cases to prevent 'locking the door' to potential borrowers.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What two teachings does the Gemara derive from the double language "Justice, justice shall you follow" (Deut. 16:20)?
A. Both daytime and nighttime courts are valid; and all judges must be equally qualified
B. One 'justice' means strict judgment; the other means compromise — and one should follow the best court of the generation
C. Monetary and capital cases must follow identical rules; and follow the majority opinion
D. Judges must always rule strictly; and witnesses must be interrogated twice
Correct: B — The doubled 'justice' teaches: (1) follow the best, most prestigious court of the generation, and (2) one instance refers to strict judgment while the other refers to compromise (peshara).
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Rav Kahana suggest fulfilling the Mishna's requirement to open capital deliberations with a claim to acquit?
A. The most senior judge speaks first in favor of acquittal
B. The witnesses are asked if they know any reasons to acquit the accused
C. The court issues a pro forma declaration that based on the testimony the accused could be acquitted
D. The accused is invited to present his defense before any testimony is heard
Correct: C — Rav Kahana: the court formally declares 'based on your statements, so-and-so is acquitted' as a structured opening statement — a pro forma acquittal before real deliberation begins.
