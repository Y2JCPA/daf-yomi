QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Abaye, why do the Rabbis exempt a father who abducts his own son from the death penalty?
A. A father has legal ownership over his minor son
B. Exodus 21:16 — 'if he is found in his possession' — excludes one already in the abductor's possession
C. The Torah only prohibits abducting strangers, not family members
D. Sons are considered partial slaves under their father's authority
Correct: B — Abaye derives the exemption from Exodus 21:16 — 'if he is found in his possession, he shall be put to death' — which excludes cases where the victim was already in the abductor's possession before the abduction, as a son is already under his father's authority.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
To which professional group does Rava apply the 'found in possession' exemption from abduction liability?
A. Scribes who copy Torah scrolls for their patrons
B. Judges who summon litigants to their courts
C. Teachers of children (dardekei) and mishnah reciters to scholars
D. Priests who supervise Levites in the Temple
Correct: C — Rava rules that teachers of children (dardekei) and those who recite mishnayot to Torah scholars are exempt from abduction liability regarding their students, since the students are considered permanently 'found in their possession.'
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does the baraita interpret Exodus 20:13 ('You shall not steal') as referring to kidnapping rather than property theft?
A. The verse uses the word נֶפֶשׁ (soul/person) rather than a word for property
B. A direct rabbinic tradition transmitted from Sinai states this explicitly
C. Its neighbors in the Ten Commandments — 'shall not murder' and 'shall not commit adultery' — are capital crimes
D. It appears in the same passage as laws governing Israelite slaves
Correct: C — The baraita reasons contextually: since 'You shall not steal' in Exodus 20:13 stands alongside 'shall not murder' and 'shall not commit adultery' — both capital offenses — it must refer to kidnapping, which is likewise punishable by death.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In the dispute between Ḥizkiyya and Rabbi Yoḥanan about conspiring witnesses in abduction cases, whose principle does Ḥizkiyya follow?
A. Rabbi Yehuda, who requires two separate acts to establish liability
B. Rabbi Akiva, who holds that testimony is valid only when it attests to an entire matter
C. Rabbi Shimon, who derives special rules from the 'brethren' verse
D. Reish Lakish, who limits conspiring-witness penalties to monetary cases
Correct: B — Ḥizkiyya follows Rabbi Akiva's principle that testimony is only actionable when it attests to a complete, standalone matter. Since the abduction witnesses' testimony alone cannot cause execution (a sale is also required), they are not liable as conspiring witnesses.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the punishment for a rebellious elder (zaken mamre) who defies the ruling of the Great Sanhedrin after going through all the court escalations?
A. Flogging and permanent removal from his rabbinic position
B. Execution by beheading as an instigator of rebellion
C. Execution by strangulation
D. Exile to a city of refuge for the remainder of the High Priest's life
Correct: C — The mishna teaches that a rebellious elder who returns home after the Great Sanhedrin's ruling and instructs others to act contrary to it is executed by strangulation. The crime is not the original disputed ruling but the defiance of the supreme court's authority (Deut. 17:12).
