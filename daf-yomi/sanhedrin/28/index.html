📜 Building the Web of Testimony Disqualification

Deuteronomy 24:16
"The fathers shall not be put to death for the children"
↓
Plural 'children'
Cousins (brothers' children) cannot testify about each other
Verse repeated
Relatives disqualified from co-witnessing — not just testifying about one another
'Fathers' appears twice
Extends disqualification to maternal relatives, not only paternal
↓
"Shall not be put to death" written twice → extends to testimony that benefits a relative, not only testimony that harms
↓
Leviticus 24:22: "One manner of law" → all rules apply equally in monetary law

🤔 Why Can't Brothers' Children Testify Together?

Rami bar Ḥama
This is logical reasoning, not derived from a verse! Conspiring witnesses are only condemned if both are condemned together. So if the children of two brothers co-testify, each depends on the other — they are mutually disqualified.
Rava
But consider the mishna: if three brothers each occupied land one year in sequence, each year counts separately as proof of ownership — yet they obviously cannot testify together. Your logic collapses here!
Rava
Rather, the verse itself teaches it: if relatives could co-testify, it would write 'a child shall not die for the father.' The fact it writes 'children' plural, and 'fathers' plural, teaches that related witnesses are disqualified from bearing witness together.
Rami bar Ḥama
— (no counter; Rava's verse-based reasoning is accepted)
Conclusion Rava's verse-based derivation stands: the plural language of Deuteronomy 24:16 — not logical inference — disqualifies relatives from co-witnessing.

🏛️ Rav's Ruling: How Many Generations Are Disqualified?

Mishna baseline: Relatives — themselves, their sons, their sons-in-law — are disqualified
↓
Rav's ruling: My paternal uncle will not testify about me; nor his son; nor his son-in-law. Nor will I testify about him; nor my son; nor my son-in-law.
↓
Problem raised
Rav's son and the brother of Rav's grandfather — that is 3rd generation vs. 1st generation. Is this really in the mishna?
Answer
Mishna's 'son-in-law' refers to son-in-law of one's son — a 3rd generation relative — teaching that 3rd ↔ 1st generation gaps are disqualified
↓
Final answer: Rav follows Rabbi Elazar's baraita — but disagrees on one sub-case. Rav's own derivation: "for the children, and the children" — disqualification is cast symmetrically in both directions

🔍 Rav vs. Rabbi Elazar: Generational Reach of Disqualification

Rav's Position

  • Uncle ↔ nephew: disqualified ✗
  • Uncle's son ↔ nephew: disqualified ✗
  • Uncle's son-in-law ↔ nephew: disqualified ✗
  • 3rd generation ↔ 1st generation: disqualified ✗
  • 3rd generation ↔ 2nd generation: PERMITTED ✓
  • Source: 'for the children, and the children' — symmetric bidirectional disqualification
  • Agrees with Rabbi Elazar on 3rd↔1st; disagrees on 3rd↔2nd

Rabbi Elazar's Position

  • Uncle ↔ nephew: disqualified ✗
  • Uncle's son ↔ nephew: disqualified ✗
  • Uncle's grandson ↔ nephew: disqualified ✗
  • 3rd generation ↔ 1st generation: disqualified ✗
  • 3rd generation ↔ 2nd generation: disqualified ✗
  • Source: 'for the children' — disqualification is projected forward onto subsequent generations indefinitely
  • Broader reach: each new generation inherits the prior generation's disqualifications

📋 The Brother-in-Law Deed of Gift — A Live Case

Situation: A deed of gift is brought to court. Two brothers-in-law are signed as witnesses on the document.
↓
Rav Yosef's initial ruling: The deed is valid. Shmuel ruled like Rabbi Yosei, who disqualifies only the brother-in-law himself — so two brothers-in-law testifying together should be permitted.
↓
Abaye challenges: Which Rabbi Yosei did Shmuel mean? Rabbi Yosei of the mishna (lenient) or Rabbi Yosei of the baraita (strict — disqualifies broadly)?
↓
Rav Yosef responds: Shmuel said explicitly — 'My brother Pineḥas and I cannot testify about each other' — showing he viewed himself as mutually disqualified with a brother, proving a lenient reading.
↓
Abaye's counter: Perhaps Pineḥas was Shmuel's brother-in-law, not his brother! This still doesn't settle which Rabbi Yosei Shmuel endorsed.
↓
Resolution: Unable to resolve the doubt, Rav Yosef instructs the recipient: acquire the gift via witnesses who personally observed the physical act of transfer (kinyan) — bypassing the disputed document entirely.
