📜 What Does 'That He Die' Teach?

R. Yosei b. R. Yehuda
The verse says: 'One witness shall not testify against any person that he die' — inferring: he may NOT testify on death-matters (conviction), but he MAY testify to acquit! Students are limited only on the side of conviction.
The Rabbis
We agree students may argue for acquittal — but we derive that from a baraita, not from this verse. The phrase 'that he die' addresses a different rule: if one of the witnesses says 'I have a reason to acquit,' we listen to him and bring him into court.
R. Yosei b. R. Yehuda
Then how do the Rabbis allow students to argue for acquittal at all? They must derive it from elsewhere — my reading of 'that he die' is more direct and economical.
The Rabbis
We derive student-acquittal speech from a baraita explicitly permitting it. The verse 'that he die' is reserved to teach the special case where a witness discovers a reason to acquit mid-trial and may enter the court to testify.
Verse (Devarim 17:6) לֹא יָקוּם עֵד אֶחָד בְּאִישׁ לְכָל עָוֹן וּלְכָל חַטָּאת… לָמוּת — "One witness shall not testify against any person for any iniquity… that he die"

📖 The Psalms 62:12 Rule — One Verse, Two Rulings

אַחַת דִּבֶּר אֱלֹהִים שְׁתַּיִם זוּ שָׁמַעְתִּי — "God has spoken once, twice I have heard this" (תהלים סב:יב)
Abaye's Derivation The Rule: Two judges in a capital case may not argue to acquit using the same logical reasoning derived from two different verses — this counts as only one valid argument, not two independent ones.

Why? Because the Psalmist teaches: "God has spoken once [אַחַת], twice I have heard this" — one divine speech yields two distinct halakhic rulings. Therefore a single ruling cannot be "doubled" by citing two verses for the same conclusion. The court needs genuinely independent arguments.

Practical Impact: Court scribes record each judge's reasoning precisely so the court can verify that acquittal votes rest on independent grounds, not on the same logic restated twice.

🔥 Examples: Same Conclusion, Different Verse

Rav Zevid's Example (Rejected)

  • Rabbi Yehoshua: Disqualified offerings that ascended the altar shall NOT descend
  • Source: "on the pyre" — whatever is on the pyre stays
  • Scope: Only items suited to be consumed by altar fire
  • Rabban Gamliel: Same ruling — anything that ascended stays
  • Source: "It is the burnt-offering" — it becomes like a burnt-offering
  • Scope: Even items NOT typically consumed by fire
  • ❌ Rejected: They actually dispute the scope — not truly the same ruling

Rav Pappa's Example (Better — Altar Sanctification)

  • R. Yosei HaGelili: Disqualified offerings do NOT become sanctified by touching the altar
  • Source: "lambs" — only suited items are sanctified
  • Rabbi Akiva: Same ruling — disqualified offerings not sanctified
  • Source: "offer a burnt-offering or sacrifice" — unsuited items excluded
  • ⚠️ Also challenged: Rav Adda bar Ahava finds a practical dispute between them regarding a disqualified bird burnt-offering

Rav Ashi's Example (Accepted — Outside Slaughter)

  • Rabbi Yishmael: Liable for slaughtering outside the Tent of Meeting
  • Source: Derives from the general prohibition verse
  • Rabbi Akiva: Same ruling — liable for outside slaughter
  • Source: Derives from a separate verse including strangers
  • ✅ Accepted: Abaye confirms both hold the same practical ruling — only one transgression, regardless of which verse is the source

👁️ Can a Blind Man Serve as a Judge?

Situation: A blind man lived near Rabbi Yoḥanan and was regularly serving as a judge in monetary cases. Rabbi Yoḥanan did not object or intervene.
↓
Challenge: An unattributed mishna in Niddah implies a blind person cannot judge — examination and perception seem to require sight.
↓
Counter-source: Rabbi Yoḥanan found this mishna (Sanhedrin 34): monetary cases may be concluded at night — when vision is irrelevant to everyone.
↓
Reasoning: If night-verdict is valid for monetary law, then sight is not a prerequisite for judgment. A blind judge who hears testimony and arguments is fully qualified.
↓
Why this mishna wins: An unattributed (stam) mishna in Sanhedrin — the tractate specifically governing courts and judges — carries greater authoritative weight on judicial qualification than an incidental ruling in Niddah.
↓
Rabbi Meir's dissent: Rabbi Meir derives from a juxtaposition of disputes and leprous sores that all adjudication must occur by day — which would exclude blind judges even in monetary matters.
