Sanhedrin 34 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yosei son of Rabbi Yehuda, what does the phrase 'that he die' (לָמוּת) in the verse about witnesses teach?
A. Capital cases require two witnesses to testify about the killing itself
B. A single student or witness may testify to acquit but not to convict in capital cases
C. The death penalty requires the unanimous agreement of all judges
D. Witnesses in capital cases must testify only during the daytime
Correct: B — Rabbi Yosei b. Rabbi Yehuda reads 'that he die' as limiting single-witness testimony to the side of conviction (death). One may freely testify or argue to acquit, since acquittal does not lead to death.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What ruling does Abaye derive from Psalms 62:12, 'God has spoken once, twice I have heard this'?
A. The Torah was given twice — once orally and once in writing — so each law has two valid forms
B. A majority of two judges is sufficient to acquit in capital cases
C. One verse may yield two halakhic rulings; therefore two judges citing different verses for the same acquittal argument count as only one independent argument
D. Each judge must offer two independent reasons before voting to convict
Correct: C — Abaye derives that a single divine utterance can produce two distinct legal conclusions. The flip side: one legal conclusion derived from two different verses is still just one argument — court scribes record each judge's reasoning to prevent this duplication.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
After a judge initially argues for acquittal in a capital case, what happens if he later wishes to argue for conviction?
A. He may freely reverse his position at any time before the final verdict
B. He may reverse only if a majority of other judges also wish to convict
C. He is forbidden to reverse — one who argued to acquit may never argue to convict
D. He may reverse only on the following day during the second deliberation session
Correct: C — The asymmetric rule protects defendants: a judge who initially argued for acquittal is locked in — he may not switch to argue for conviction. The reverse is permitted: one who argued to convict may always switch to acquit.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to the Gemara, when may the court issue a final verdict in a monetary case?
A. Only during the daytime, identical to the rule for capital cases
B. Only at night, to ensure deliberate and unhurried judgment
C. The trial must begin during the daytime, but the verdict may be issued even at night
D. At any time — day or night — from the very start of proceedings
Correct: C — Monetary cases must be initiated in the daytime, but unlike capital cases (which require daytime conclusion), a monetary verdict may be issued even at night. This distinction becomes the basis for Rabbi Yoḥanan's ruling on blind judges.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How did Rabbi Yoḥanan justify allowing a blind man to serve as a judge in monetary cases, despite a contrary mishna in Niddah?
A. He found a baraita explicitly permitting blind judges in all civil matters
B. He ruled that blindness is irrelevant since judges rely entirely on testimony, not personal observation
C. He derived from our mishna that since monetary verdicts may be issued at night (when nobody sees), vision is not a prerequisite — a blind judge is therefore qualified
D. He held that the Niddah mishna applies only to ritual purity rulings, not monetary adjudication
Correct: C — Rabbi Yoḥanan reasoned: our mishna permits monetary verdicts at night, when sight is impossible for everyone. This proves vision is not required for monetary judgment. Therefore a blind man who hears the arguments qualifies. He preferred this Sanhedrin mishna over the Niddah one as more authoritative on judicial qualification.
