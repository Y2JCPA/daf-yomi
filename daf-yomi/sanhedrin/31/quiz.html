QUESTION 1 OF 5
What do the Sages of Neharde'a rule about witnesses who disagree on whether a coin was black or white?
A. Their testimonies cannot be combined since they directly contradict each other
B. Their testimonies are combined, as the color discrepancy is irrelevant to the core claim
C. One witness is disqualified and the case is dismissed
D. The creditor must produce a third witness to resolve the contradiction
Correct: B — The Sages of Neharde'a hold that even a contradiction about the coin's color does not prevent combining testimonies — what matters is that both witnesses attest to the same underlying monetary obligation.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rabba bar Rav Huna, when a litigant explicitly said 'I have no witnesses' and later found witnesses, what is the halakha?
A. He may always bring new witnesses, following Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel
B. He may never bring them — his admission is binding, following the Rabbis in all cases
C. The halakha follows the Rabbis specifically in this case — he may not produce new witnesses
D. He may bring witnesses only if the case involves more than 200 dinars
Correct: C — Rabba bar Rav Huna rules that specifically when someone said 'I have no witnesses,' the halakha follows the Rabbis — he cannot now produce witnesses. However, on Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's other rulings (like the 30-day clause), the halakha does follow him.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why did Rav Naḥman allow the child's newly found witnesses to testify, even though the child had said 'I have no witnesses'?
A. Children are categorically exempt from the rule stopping litigants who disclaimed evidence
B. The witnesses appeared on their own initiative after hearing the child cry — the child had no way of knowing they existed
C. Rav Naḥman held that the halakha always follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel against the Rabbis
D. The child's tears demonstrated sincerity, which functioned as a legal exception
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman ruled that even the Rabbis agree when witnesses emerge independently — the child genuinely did not know they existed, and the witnesses came forward on their own initiative. This is not the scenario the Rabbis' rule was meant to address.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What was the outcome when Rava challenged Rav Naḥman regarding the woman who held a court-ratified promissory note and claimed the debt was repaid?
A. Rav Naḥman's ruling was upheld because possession of the note gave her full authority over it
B. Rava's refutation was dismissed since the woman's 'could have burned it' logic applies universally
C. Rava successfully refuted Rav Naḥman — a ratified document cannot be nullified by her word alone
D. Rav Naḥman accepted her testimony based on Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's ruling about document acquisition
Correct: C — When a promissory note was ratified in court, Rav Naḥman's reasoning that 'she could have burned it' no longer applies. Rava brought a baraita about receipts for ratified documents, and the Gemara declares this a conclusive refutation.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rabbi Elazar, if one litigant demands that the case be heard before the Assembly (Sanhedrin) rather than the local court, what happens?
A. The case is moved to the Assembly out of respect for its greater authority
B. The litigant demanding the Assembly is compelled to appear before the local court instead
C. Both litigants must travel to the Assembly at their shared expense
D. The local court decides whether the case merits referral to the Assembly
Correct: B — Rabbi Elazar argues that compelling someone to travel to the Assembly is unjust — why should one spend 100 dinars in travel costs to collect a 100-dinar debt? Therefore, the litigant demanding the distant court is actually compelled to appear locally.
