🪙 Combining Contradictory Testimonies

Sages of Neharde'a
Even if one witness says the coin was black and the other says it was white, their testimonies are combined — the defendant's underlying liability is established.
Beit Shammai
When one witness says the debt is 200 dinars and the other says 100, they cannot be combined at all — the contradiction undermines both testimonies entirely.
Beit Hillel
True, they cannot establish 200, but they can be combined for the lesser amount of 100 dinars on which both witnesses effectively agree.
Rabbi Ami (ruling)
One witness says barrel of wine, the other says barrel of oil. Since wine was cheaper, liability is established for the value of the wine — the minimum both can support.
Core Principle Witnesses who agree on the core obligation but diverge on peripheral details may be combined; irreconcilable contradictions on the substance of the claim prevent combination.

📋 New Evidence After a Verdict: Two Views

The Rabbis

  • Once a litigant explicitly says 'I have no witnesses' or 'I have no proof,' he is stopped — late evidence cannot overturn the ruling.
  • Finality of judgment requires a party who disclaimed evidence to be bound by that admission.
  • If he later found witnesses, it is his problem — the court needed certainty to proceed.
  • Halakha does NOT follow the Rabbis on the 'I have no witnesses' clause (Rabba bar Rav Huna).

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel

  • Even after saying 'I have no witnesses,' if he later finds witnesses or proof, the verdict can still be overturned.
  • We cannot fault a person for not knowing what witnesses or proofs he possesses — 'What should this person have done?'
  • Applies equally to the 30-day window: if he didn't find proof in time but found it after, the verdict is overturned.
  • Halakha follows RSbG on the 30-day clause but follows the Rabbis on the explicit disclaimer clause.

🧒 Rav Naḥman's Leniency for the Child

A child-orphan is brought before Rav Naḥman in a monetary dispute.
↓
Court asks: Do you have witnesses? Child: No.
Court asks: Do you have proof? Child: No.
↓
Rav Naḥman begins to rule against the child based on available evidence.
↓
Child walks out crying. Bystanders who heard him say: We know about your father's affairs — we can testify for you!
↓
Child brings these newly emerged witnesses back before the court.
↓
Rav Naḥman rules: Even the Rabbis agree in a case like this — the litigant genuinely had no knowledge the witnesses existed, and they came forward on their own.
↓
Conclusion: The Rabbis' rule stopping late evidence does not apply when the litigant had no reasonable way of knowing witnesses existed.

📜 Woman with Promissory Note — Two Versions

VersionScenarioRav Naḥman's RulingRava's ChallengeResolution
Version 1 (accepted)Woman holds a note and says: 'This debt was repaid — do not use it to collect.'Testimony is credible — since she could have burned the note, her claim to nullify it is trusted.Is this because documents are acquired by delivery (Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's view)?Rav Naḥman: No — it is simply that since she could have burned it, she is believed to nullify it.
Version 2 (rejected)Same scenario — woman with ratified note claims repayment.Rav Naḥman does not deem her credible.Rava: But she could have burned it! Why not trust her?Since the note was ratified in court, her word alone cannot nullify it. Rava's refutation stands conclusively.

📍 Where Must Litigants Appear in Court?

Dispute erupts over which court should hear the case
↓
One demands local court; other demands the Assembly
Rabbi Elazar: Compelled to appear locally
Why spend 100 dinars traveling to collect 100 dinars?
Ameimar's later ruling
The litigant who wants the Assembly compels the other to go there — authoritative judgment outweighs convenience
Yevama (levirate widow)
She follows her yavam even from Tiberias to Tzippori
Based on Deut. 25:8 — 'elders of his city'
↓
Letter to Mar Ukva: Judge him locally first; if he refuses, induce him to appear before us in Tiberias.
Rav Ashi: This was a fines case — Babylonian courts lack jurisdiction; only ordained judges may adjudicate.
