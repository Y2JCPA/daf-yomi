⚖️ The Great Compromise Debate

R' Eliezer b. R' Yosei HaGelili
Compromise is prohibited! The judge who mediates commits a sin — botze'a is negative, like Judah who sold Joseph for gain. Let true judgment pierce the mountain!
R' Yehoshua ben Korḥa
Compromise is a mitzvah! The verse says 'Execute judgment of truth and peace' — only through compromise can both truth and peace be achieved simultaneously.
Both Agree Once the verdict has been formally issued, compromise is no longer permitted — the judgment must stand.

🏛️ Two-Judge Courts — When Is the Ruling Valid?

Court of two judges adjudicates a monetary case
↓
Litigants accepted them
Ruling is binding post-facto — but judges are called beit din hazutzin (deficient court)
No prior acceptance
Ruling is invalid — a halakhic court requires a minimum of three judges
↓
Source: Rabbi Abbahu — 'they are called a deficient court,' and Shmuel agrees they are liable

📋 Types of Judicial Error and Their Consequences

Judge issues an erroneous ruling
↓
Error in Mishna (דבר משנה)
Reverse the ruling
Judge contradicted a clear, established halakha. No payment — ruling is simply undone.
Error in Deliberation (שיקול הדעת)
Cannot reverse; judge pays
Two authorities dispute a point and halakha was not established. Judge chose wrong — pays from own funds.
↓
Rav Pappa: 'Error in deliberation' = two tanna'im or amora'im dispute where the halakha was not explicitly decided

🤝 Strict Judgment vs. Compromise

Strict Judgment (דין)

  • Truth prevails absolutely
  • 'Let the judgment pierce the mountain'
  • Moses' model: unwavering application of law
  • R' Eliezer: the mediator is a sinner
  • 'Botze'a' = ill-gotten gain (like Judah selling Joseph)

Compromise (פשרה)

  • Achieves both truth AND peace
  • 'Execute judgment of truth and peace' (Zech. 8:16)
  • R' Yehoshua ben Korḥa: it is a mitzvah
  • Aaron's model: pursued peace among people
  • Only permitted before the verdict is issued

👑 David's Justice and Charity Explained

וַיְהִי דָוִד עֹשֶׂה מִשְׁפָּט וּצְדָקָה לְכָל עַמּוֹ
II Samuel 8:15 'David executed justice and charity to all his people'

How can strict justice also be charity? When David ruled that a poor defendant owed money and the debtor could not pay, David paid the creditor from his own royal treasury — rendering justice for the creditor (his money restored) and charity for the poor debtor (debt covered by the king).

R' Yehuda HaNasi objects: if only for the poor, the verse should say 'to the poor of his people' — not 'to all his people.'

📋 Sanhedrin 6 — Summary Map

Core Question: Is judicial compromise (פשרה) praiseworthy or prohibited?
↓
R' Eliezer: Prohibited — 'botze'a' is sin; pure justice must prevail like Moses
R' Yehoshua ben Korḥa: A mitzvah — only compromise achieves truth and peace together
↓
Unanimous rule: After the verdict is issued — compromise is forbidden for all opinions
↓
David's model: Justice + charity = paying poor defendants' debts from royal funds, serving both parties
