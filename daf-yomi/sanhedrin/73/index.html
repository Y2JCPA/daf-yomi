🆘 The Obligation to Save — Lo Ta'amod

Source: "You shall not stand idly by the blood of your neighbor" (Lev. 19:16) — a positive obligation to rescue
↓
Scenarios covered: One drowning in a river, being dragged off by a wild animal, or attacked by bandits
↓
Extends to: Hiring others to save — not only personal intervention is required
↓
Separate source: A different verse (Deut. 25:12 — "cut off her hand") derives the right to kill the pursuer beyond merely saving the victim
↓
A fortiori: If we may cut off an attacker's hand to stop a betrothed woman from being harmed, certainly we may kill to prevent worse harm

🤝 May the Victim's Words Spare the Pursuer?

Rabbis
Even if she says 'leave him alone' — she speaks only out of fear of being killed. This is not true consent. The obligation to kill the pursuer remains fully in force.
Rabbi Yehuda
If she explicitly says 'let him be so he will not kill me,' she has instructed the rescuers to stand down. He is exempt from being killed — her instruction counts.
Rava
They dispute the case where she is particular about degradation but allows it to save her life. Rabbis: her concern for degradation remains valid regardless. Rabbi Yehuda: her spoken instruction waives his liability.
Rav Pappa
Challenge to the Rabbis: If the reason the rapist may be killed is the woman's degradation, why can't a High Priest be killed for marrying a widow — he too degrades her by making her a ḥalala?
Resolution The rodef law is not about personal insult to the victim — it is about actively pursuing a transgression. A High Priest marrying a widow is not a rodef; he commits no act of pursuit.

📖 Na'ar vs. Na'ara — Why Both Terms Are Necessary

נַעַר — Na'ar (Male Youth)

  • Teaches: one pursued for sodomy may be saved at the cost of the pursuer's life
  • Cannot be derived from na'ara alone: his case involves an unnatural act
  • He does not lose virginity — so degradation reasoning does not apply to him
  • Unique feature: it is not the pursuer's natural way — a distinct stringency

נַעֲרָה — Na'ara (Betrothed Girl)

  • Teaches: a betrothed woman may be saved at the cost of the rapist's life
  • Cannot be derived from na'ar alone: she suffers a unique degradation
  • She loses her virginity — she is 'cheapened' in the eyes of her betrothed husband
  • Unique feature: irreversible personal dishonor — she is harmed in a way na'ar is not

🔬 Rav Ḥisda — When Does the Pursuer's Liability End?

"כִּי מָצָא אֹתָהּ בַּשָּׂדֶה" — "For he found her in the field" (Deut. 22:27)
Rav Ḥisda's Resolution The Problem: The mishna implies a brother pursuing his sister may be killed. But once typical intercourse begins she becomes a ḥalala (disqualified from Kohen marriage) — only a lav remains. Why can he still be killed at that stage? Rav Ḥisda's Answer: The case is where the brother first engaged in atypical intercourse (anal). This degraded her, but she is still a virgin and still forbidden to him under a death penalty — so he may still be killed to stop him. Rava's Alternative: The mishna follows Rabbi Yehuda — the sister told them 'do not harm him on my account,' and Rabbi Yehuda holds such an instruction exempts the pursuer from being killed.
