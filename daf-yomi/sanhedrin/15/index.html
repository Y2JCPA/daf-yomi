🏺 Three Opinions: Appraising Consecrated Movable Property

Rav Ḥisda / Avimi
Rav's ruling applies when the debtor redirected his vow-debt onto movable property — he had no cash, so his goods became the vehicle of payment.
Rabbi Abbahu
Rav speaks of one who declared: "My monetary valuation is upon me." When a priest collects via movable goods, 3 expert appraisers are required — since the debtor retains the right to redeem with cash instead.
Ravina
This is logically required: whether elevating property to sacred status or redeeming it downward, a formal court is needed. What difference does direction make? Both actions change consecrated status.
Bottom Line Movable property appraisal → 3 judges. Consecrated land valuation → 9 judges + 1 priest. The distinction tracks the severity and finality of the transaction.

📜 Deriving 9 Judges + 1 Priest from Leviticus 27

Shmuel's Derivation

  • The word כֹּהֵן (priest) appears 10 times in Leviticus 27
  • One mention = the actual priest performing the evaluation
  • Remaining 9 = judges who serve in a priestly-style role (Israelites)
  • Result: 9 lay judges + 1 priest = the tribunal for consecrated land

Objection: Rav Huna b. Rav Natan

  • A restrictive expression after a restrictive expression amplifies!
  • Maybe it means: 5 priests + 5 Israelites = 10 total?
  • The doubled emphasis could require more priests, not fewer
  • Challenge left unresolved in this sugya

Related Dilemma: Hair Ready to Be Shorn

  • Rabbi Avin asks: consecrated hair still attached to the head — how many judges?
  • Is it like shorn hair (movable) → 3 judges?
  • Or like land/person (attached) → 9+1?
  • Left unresolved — תיקו

Consecrated Slave (Baraita)

  • One who consecrates a slave: the slave's body itself cannot be truly consecrated
  • Benefiting from the slave is not misuse of sacred property
  • Only the slave's monetary value is consecrated
  • Parallels debate about attached vs. detached property

🔥 What Punishment Does the Ox's Owner Receive?

Gemara — Opening Question
The Torah says the owner "shall be put to death." Since the ox is stoned, does this mean the owner should also be executed by stoning?
Rava
Absurd! If he killed a person himself, he would only get the sword. Would negligence in owning an ox merit the harsher death of stoning? Logic alone refutes this.
Abaye
Had the Torah written only "stoned and also its owner" and stopped, I would have thought stoning applies to the owner too. The added phrase "shall be put to death" comes to exclude stoning.
Gemara — Counterproposal
Perhaps "shall be put to death" is a leniency — removing him from death-by-sword to the milder death-by-strangulation?
Final Resolution
No — Exodus 21:30 offers a ransom payment instead of death. This ransom option would be meaningless if he actually faced execution. Therefore he is subject to a monetary penalty, not death.
Key Principle The phrase "shall be put to death" in Exodus 21:29 does not impose execution — it opens the door to ransom. The owner's liability is monetary, not capital.

🦁 Dangerous Animals, Rabbi Akiva, and the Sinful Tribe

Mishna: Does a lion, wolf, bear, or serpent that killed a person require 23 judges before it can be put to death?
↓
Reish Lakish: The tanna'im only dispute when the animal has already killed. If it has not killed anyone, all agree it may be killed freely without a court — it is simply a dangerous animal.
↓
Rabbi Yoḥanan: The dispute applies even before any killing. Rabbi Eliezer holds a court of 23 is required regardless — even for a potentially dangerous animal that has not yet harmed anyone.
↓
"Merits" explained: Rabbi Eliezer says whoever kills them first "merits." According to Reish Lakish, this means he earns heavenly merit — a mitzvah — not property rights. According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, he acquires their hides (since condemned animals are ownerless).
↓
Rabbi Akiva: These five dangerous species — lion, wolf, bear, leopard, serpent — require a court of 23 judges. His ruling appears identical to the first tanna, but the Gemara notes a novelty: he adds the serpent explicitly.
↓
An Entire Sinful Tribe → 71 Judges: Even Rabbi Yonatan, who permits declaring large communities as ir hanidaḥat (idolatrous city), only allows a majority of a tribe — not the whole tribe. A fully apostate tribe is an unprecedented national crisis adjudicated only by the Great Sanhedrin.
