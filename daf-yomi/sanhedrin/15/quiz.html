QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Shmuel, how is the court of 9 judges + 1 priest for evaluating consecrated land derived?
A. Moses appointed exactly ten Levites to serve as the valuation court in the wilderness
B. The word 'priest' (כֹּהֵן) appears 10 times in Leviticus 27 — one is the actual officiating priest, the other nine indicate lay judges
C. A tradition from Sinai requires one priest for every nine Israelites in sacred monetary matters
D. The verse says 'the priest shall evaluate it' ten times, requiring ten priests in total
Correct: B — Shmuel counts the word 'priest' appearing 10 times in Leviticus 27. One instance refers to the actual priest performing the appraisal; the remaining nine indicate that nine non-priest judges serve alongside him, yielding a court of 9 + 1 priest.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the Gemara conclude that the owner of a goring ox pays ransom rather than faces execution?
A. The Sages ruled by majority that negligent owners cannot receive capital punishment
B. Exodus 21:30 explicitly offers a ransom option — which would be pointless if the owner were truly condemned to death
C. The phrase 'shall be put to death' is a Talmudic idiom meaning only monetary liability
D. Rava derives from the law of manslaughter that negligence never incurs the death penalty
Correct: B — Exodus 21:30 says 'if a ransom be placed upon him, he shall give for the redemption of his life.' This verse presupposes that ransom is an option — which would be legally absurd if the owner were genuinely sentenced to death.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the core disagreement between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish about dangerous animals?
A. Whether the five species can be killed on Shabbat without rabbinic dispensation
B. Whether 23 judges are required only when the animal has already killed, or even before it has killed
C. Whether the owner of a dangerous animal bears financial liability for future harm
D. Whether the court must witness the killing directly or may rule in absentia
Correct: B — Reish Lakish: the tanna'im only dispute about animals that have already killed — before killing, all agree they may be dispatched freely. Rabbi Yoḥanan: the dispute applies even before any killing; Rabbi Eliezer always requires a court of 23.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Reish Lakish, what does Rabbi Eliezer mean when he says one who kills a dangerous animal first 'merits'?
A. He acquires legal ownership of the animal's hide and carcass
B. He receives a formal commendation recorded in court documents
C. He merits reward before Heaven — he has performed a mitzvah by eliminating a threat
D. He is exempt from any liability if the animal belonged to another person
Correct: C — According to Reish Lakish, these animals have not yet killed (in his reading of the dispute), so they are not condemned property. 'Merits' therefore refers to spiritual/divine reward — performing a mitzvah — not acquiring material property.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does the Gemara require the Great Sanhedrin of 71 judges for an entire tribe that sinned idolatrously, rather than treating it as an idolatrous city (ir hanidaḥat)?
A. An idolatrous tribe is subject to the laws of a ben sorer u'moreh and requires a higher court
B. Even Rabbi Yonatan, who permits large communities to be declared idolatrous cities, limits this to a majority of a tribe — not the entire tribe; a fully apostate tribe is a unique national case
C. The Torah explicitly lists 'tribe' alongside false prophet as requiring 71 judges in Deuteronomy 17
D. A tribe has sovereign status and can only be judged by the court that has jurisdiction over all Israel
Correct: B — The Gemara notes that even the most lenient tanna (Rabbi Yonatan) permits the ir hanidaḥat law for at most a majority of a tribe. When the entire tribe is apostate, even he would agree it cannot be destroyed as a city — a wholly different ruling is required, adjudicated only by the Great Sanhedrin of 71.
