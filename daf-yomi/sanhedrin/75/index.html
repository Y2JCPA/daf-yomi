📖 Overview

סנהדרין עה
🔥 Two Major Themes of Daf 75 Part 1 (Amud A): A man fell dangerously ill with desire for a woman. The Sages ruled he must die rather than be permitted any contact — even conversation, even if she were unmarried. Rav Pappa cites family disgrace; Rav Aḥa cites licentiousness; Rabbi Yitzḥak adds that even marriage would not cure him.

Part 2 (Amud A–B): The Mishna lists those executed by sereifah (burning). The Gemara debates whether the Mishna follows Abaye's or Rava's reading of R. Yishmael vs. R. Akiva, then derives via gezeira shava on the word henna (הֵנָּה) which relatives are included — and why the mother of one's own mother cannot be included.

💔 Lovesick Man — Why Can't Even an Unmarried Woman Be Brought to Him?

Rav Pappa
Because of the family flaw (פגם המשפחה). It is not permitted to bring disgrace upon an entire family merely to save one lovesick man.
Rav Aḥa b. Rav Ika
So that the daughters of Israel do not become ownerless — permitting this act even once would open the door to licentiousness across all of Israel.
Rabbi Yitzḥak
'Since the Temple's destruction, sexual pleasure has been taken from the permitted and given to transgressors.' His mind would not be eased even by marriage — so there is no leniency to offer.
Final Ruling He must die rather than permit any contact. The doctors' recommendation — even speech, even seeing her from behind a fence — is rejected. Forbidden desire cannot be accommodated to save a life.

⚔️ Abaye vs. Rava — Whose Tannaitic Opinion Is in the Mishna?

Abaye
The dispute between R. Yishmael and R. Akiva concerns only the interpretation of a verse, not the practical law. Both agree that the mother-in-law is forbidden permanently. Therefore, the Mishna can represent both their opinions.
Rava
The dispute is whether the mother-in-law prohibition continues after the wife's death. R. Yishmael says it ends; R. Akiva says it is permanent. Since the Mishna includes the mother-in-law in the burning list, it follows R. Akiva alone.
What Is at Stake If Rava is right, the Mishna's inclusion of the mother's mother (a third-generation relative) follows R. Akiva's expansive view — the prohibition does not terminate and continues upward by analogy.

📜 Gezeira Shava — Deriving Relatives via 'Henna' (הֵנָּה)

Base prohibition (Lev. 18:17): Forbidden to have relations with a woman and her daughter — verse uses the word הֵנָּה (henna)
↓
His Relatives
Henna appears in Lev. 18:10 — 'the nakedness of your son's daughter or your daughter's daughter'
Her Relatives
Henna appears in Lev. 18:17 — 'her daughter and granddaughter'
↓
Verbal analogy (gezeira shava): His relatives are equated to her relatives — same sereifah penalty applies symmetrically to both sides
↓
Relatives 'above' equated to 'below': Just as his daughter's daughter is included, so too the mother of his mother-in-law — the analogy extends in both directions

⚖️ Can the Mother of His Own Mother Be Liable to Burning?

The Initial Argument to Include

  • Just as his wife's mother's mother is executed by burning…
  • …why not apply the same rule to his own mother's mother?
  • The gezeira shava seems to equate all relatives symmetrically
  • Rav Ashi: 'below' in the baraita means more distant relatives — so the analogy should extend

Three Decisive Refutations

  • 1️⃣ Abaye — explicit verse: 'She is your mother, do not uncover her nakedness' (Lev. 18:7) limits the specific prohibition — her mother is not covered by this verse
  • 2️⃣ Severity inversion: His own mother is punished by the less severe stoning — it is absurd that a more distant relative (her mother) would receive the more severe burning
  • 3️⃣ Asymmetry argument: For his wife's relatives, no distinction is drawn between her mother and her mother's mother — both receive burning. The same logic should apply to his relatives, yet we cannot extend burning to his own blood relatives beyond his mother
