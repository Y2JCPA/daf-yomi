⚖️ Who Receives Burning? The Central Dispute

Rabbis
Only the married daughter of a priest who commits adultery is executed by burning. A betrothed daughter of a priest is executed by stoning, like any other betrothed adulteress.
Rabbi Shimon
Both the betrothed and the married daughter of a priest who commits adultery are executed by burning. The Torah singles her out to render her punishment more severe in both states.
Rabbis
The gezerah shavah of 'her father' compares the priest's daughter to a betrothed adulteress — but only the married case is singled out from strangulation → burning.
Rabbi Shimon
The singling-out applies to both statuses. Just as the married daughter is elevated from strangulation to burning, so too the betrothed is elevated from stoning to burning.
Practical Difference If someone is convicted of two capital crimes, they receive the more severe punishment. Rabbis: betrothed priest's daughter convicted of adultery + a stoning-crime → stoned. Rabbi Shimon: burned. The scope of burning changes the outcome.

🏙️ Is Decapitation More Severe Than Stoning?

Objection
Decapitation should be more severe than stoning — it is given to the people of an idolatrous city (ir hanidachat), who undermine the entire foundation of Judaism, and their property is also destroyed!
Response
Consider: whose sin is greater — the subverter (meisit) who incited the city, or the subverted (nidachim) who were led astray? The subverter gets stoning. Since the instigator always receives the harsher punishment, stoning must be more severe than decapitation!
Conclusion
The ringleader receives stoning while the masses receive only decapitation. This asymmetry proves stoning is the harsher punishment. The severity of the subverter's punishment establishes the hierarchy.
Recurring Principle The meisit (subverter) argument appears multiple times on this daf: whenever decapitation is proposed as more severe than stoning, the Gemara redirects to the instigator who receives stoning — always the harsher penalty.

🔗 The Gezerah Shavah: 'Her Father' (אָבִיהָ)

Leviticus 21:9
"She profanes her father" — stated regarding the priest's daughter who commits adultery → punishment: burning
↓
Verbal Analogy Links To
Deuteronomy 22:21
"Her father's house" — stated regarding a betrothed woman who commits adultery → punishment: stoning
What We Learn
The priest's daughter is categorically linked to the betrothed adulteress — but the Torah singles her out, meaning she receives a more severe punishment
↓
Rabbis: Only the married priest's daughter is singled out → burning replaces strangulation (the married woman's default punishment)
↓
Rabbi Shimon: Both statuses singled out → betrothed elevated from stoning to burning; married elevated from strangulation to burning

📜 How the Gemara Establishes Severity Rankings

Step 1 — Propose: Claim Penalty A is more severe than Penalty B, because A is assigned to transgression X, which seems categorically more serious (e.g., blasphemy, idolatry).
↓
Step 2 — Object: Counter-argue that Penalty B is actually more severe, because B is assigned to transgression Y, which also seems very serious (e.g., destroys property, wounds parents, undermines all of Judaism).
↓
Step 3 — Subverter Principle: Compare punishment of the instigator (meisit) to those he led astray. The instigator always receives the harsher penalty — this resolves the stoning vs. decapitation debate conclusively.
↓
Step 4 — Singling-Out Principle: When the Torah singles out a category for a different punishment, it always elevates (makes more severe), never demotes. This drives the burning debates throughout the daf.
↓
Result: Burning > Stoning > Strangulation > Decapitation — both Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon agree on the hierarchy; they dispute only who falls into the burning category.
