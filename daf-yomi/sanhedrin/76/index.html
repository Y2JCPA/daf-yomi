⚖️ Core Dispute: The Daughter-in-Law Parallel

Gemara
Just as his daughter-in-law is forbidden to him, should her daughter-in-law also be forbidden to him — by verbal analogy?
Abaye
No — the verse states explicitly: 'she is your son's wife' (Lev. 18:15). The prohibition is due to the specific relationship with his son, not from any relational parallel. Her daughter-in-law is not included.
Rava
Abaye's verse-derivation is unnecessary. The verbal analogy method itself — whether derive from it and apply its details or derive from it but interpret in its own place — structurally cannot extend the parallel to her daughter-in-law, for independent reasons each way.
Conclusion Her daughter-in-law is NOT forbidden — the verbal analogy cannot be stretched that far, though Abaye and Rava offer different explanations for why.

👨‍👧 Deriving the Prohibition: Daughter Born from Rape

Problem: A man has a daughter by a woman he raped — where is her prohibition explicitly stated in the Torah?
↓
Abaye
Derive punishment by a fortiori: his wife's daughter (more distant) = burning; his own rape-daughter (closer) must be at least burning
Rava
Derive via verbal analogy (gezerah shavah) on the word henna — linking his descendants to the list of forbidden relations
Rav Avin's Father
Punishment derived from the verse of the priest's daughter who commits adultery (Lev. 21:9) — same burning penalty applies
↓
All agree: The prohibition exists and the punishment is burning — they differ only on the derivation method

🔥 Burning vs. Stoning — Which Is More Severe?

Stoning > Burning

  • His relatives include stoning cases — a notable severity
  • The verbal analogy refutation works: cannot extend to her daughter-in-law, who would be punished less severely than her daughter
  • Mother-of-his-mother IS derivable via the parallel structure
  • The third difficulty (mother-of-mother-in-law) does not block the derivation

Burning > Stoning

  • His relatives include burning cases — more severe notable feature
  • Mother-of-his-mother CANNOT be derived via parallel (third difficulty)
  • Different structural problem arises when trying to extend the analogy
  • Each position creates its own distinct logical difficulty for the derivation

🔥 'Him and Them' — Who Is Burned? (Lev. 20:14)

Rabbi Akiva
'Both him and them' — the man plus both women are burned. If he took both wife and her mother, all three are executed by burning.
Rabbi Yishmael
'Et'hen' (them) in Greek, heina, means one. Only one additional woman is burned — the mother-in-law with whom he had relations. His wife is NOT executed.
Abaye
The practical difference: Rabbi Akiva holds his wife is also executed when he violates her mother. Rabbi Yishmael holds the wife is entirely exempt.
Rava
Both Rabbis agree on the mother-of-mother-in-law prohibition being derived by interpretation. Their real dispute: is intercourse with mother-in-law after the wife's death punishable by burning?
Verse — Leviticus 20:14 "They shall be burned with fire, both him and them, that there be no lewdness among you"

💍 Rav Kahana / Rabbi Akiva — Marriage and Social Warnings

כָּל הַמַּשִּׂיא בִּתּוֹ לְזָקֵן — פּוֹגֵעַ בָּהּ | אֵין לְךָ עָנִי בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל אֶלָּא רָשָׁע
Those Who Cause Sin Rav (citing Rav Yehuda) — three who bring sin upon themselves:

1. One who marries his daughter to an old man — she may be driven to infidelity
2. One who takes a wife for his minor son — the marriage is unstable, leading to future sin
3. One who returns a lost item to a gentile unnecessarily

Rav Kahana citing Rabbi Akiva: "Beware of one who acts licentiously out of poverty" — one who delays his grown daughter's marriage becomes a conniving, wicked pauper through the pressure of his poverty.

📋 Sanhedrin 76 — Summary Map

Can verbal analogy extend forbidden relations?
↓
Her daughter-in-law: NOT forbidden — analogy structurally cannot extend this far (Abaye: explicit verse; Rava: analogy method fails both ways)
Rape-daughter: IS forbidden and punished by burning — confirmed by a fortiori (Abaye), gezerah shavah on henna (Rava), or priest's daughter verse (Rav Avin's father)
↓
New Mishna: Murderers + idolatrous city residents → decapitation. Holding victim in deadly situation = liable. Pushing victim who could self-rescue but didn't = exempt.
