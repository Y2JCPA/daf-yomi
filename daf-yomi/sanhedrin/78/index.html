⚖️ Liability When the Victim Is 'Already Dead'

Rava
One who kills a tereifa is exempt — it is as though he killed a dead person. All agree on this.
Rava
But a tereifa who kills another — if before the court, he is liable (eradicate evil from midst); if not before the court, he is exempt.
Rava
An ox that is a tereifa that killed is liable to be stoned. The animal's status is not reduced — only the owner's.
Rav Ashi
Even an ox that is a tereifa that killed is exempt. Since the tereifa owner would be exempt, the tereifa ox is also exempt — derived from the Torah's juxtaposition of ox and owner.
Core Tension A tereifa is legally 'already dead' — but does that status protect only the person, or extend to their ox? Rava and Rav Ashi dispute the scope of this principle.

🪦 Tereifa — Three Distinct Liability Cases

Kills a Tereifa

  • Killer is exempt
  • Tereifa is legally a dead person
  • All authorities agree (Rava)
  • No distinction between court/outside court

Tereifa Kills Another

  • Before court: liable — 'eradicate evil from your midst'
  • Not before court: exempt — witnesses cannot testify about what they did not see
  • Same rule applies to sodomy by a tereifa
  • Rava rules both cases identically

Ox (Tereifa) Kills a Person

  • Rava: ox is liable to be stoned
  • Rav Ashi: ox is exempt
  • Rav Ashi: derived from ox–owner juxtaposition in Torah
  • Fundamental dispute: does tereifa status transfer to one's animal?

⛓️ Incarceration Pending Outcome

Rabbis
The verse 'then he that struck him is absolved' (Ex. 21:19) teaches that the assailant is held in incarceration pending the victim's outcome — he is not executed until it is known whether the victim lives or dies.
R. Nechemya
That verse teaches something else: even if the victim recovers and then relapses and dies, the assailant is still executed. I derive incarceration from the blasphemer case (Lev. 24).
Gemara
The wood gatherer (Num. 15) was jailed because his guilt was certain but his mode of death unknown. The blasphemer was jailed because it was unknown whether he was guilty at all.
R. Nechemya
Exactly — the blasphemer case (unknown guilt) is the closer analogy to the assault case (unknown outcome). The Rabbis reject this because the blasphemer was only a provisional edict of Moses.
Key Distinction Wood gatherer: guilty + known capital crime, but which death-penalty method? Blasphemer: guilty? — unknown entirely. Each opinion uses the more analogous precedent.

📜 Mishna — Unintended Victim (End of Daf 78)

הַמְכַוֵּן לַהֲרֹג אֶת הַבְּהֵמָה וְהָרַג אֶת הָאָדָם — פָּטוּר
Mishna Principle One is exempt from execution in three cases of wrong target:

1. Intended to kill an animal, killed a person
2. Intended to kill a gentile, killed a Jew
3. Intended to kill a non-viable newborn (nefal), killed a viable infant

Capital punishment requires that the act, intent, and victim all align. Misalignment on any dimension breaks the chain of liability.
