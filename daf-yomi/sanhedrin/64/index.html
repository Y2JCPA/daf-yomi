Prophets cry out in the days of Nehemiah; people fast three days and pray for mercy
↓

↓
A fiery lion cub emerges from the Holy of Holies — Zechariah declares: this is the evil inclination for idol worship
↓
They seize it; a hair falls out and it cries out for 400 parasangs; cast into a lead container (lead absorbs sound)
↓

🪨 Ba'al-Peor: Gentile Revulsion vs. Israel's Attachment

Gentile Woman (Repulsion)

  • Vowed to worship every idol if she recovered from illness
  • Came to Ba'al-Peor to fulfill her vow
  • Became ill before the idol; involuntarily defecated
  • Accidentally fulfilled Ba'al-Peor's typical mode of worship
  • Even a devoted worshipper found it repulsive

Israel (Attachment)

  • "Hanitzmadim" — attached like a bracelet (loose) per one reading
  • Baraita reverses: attached like a ring through the nose (tight and degrading)
  • "But you who cleaved to the Lord" — always capable of return
  • Sabbeta ben Alas: stumbled at Ba'al-Peor accidentally, did not intend worship
  • Israel's bond to God endures even after grievous sin

👣 What Is the 'Typical Manner' of Passing Through Fire?

Abaye
The child is walked by foot along the ground between two bonfires — a procession through the flame.
Rava
The typical manner is like the leaps of children on Purim — jumping over bonfires lit in a pit. Passing on foot is exempt.
Baraita
Supports Rava explicitly: "If he passed him by foot, he is exempt" — the ritual requires leaping over the fire, not walking through it.
Rabbi Elazar b. R. Shimon
One who passes himself through the fire to Molekh is also liable — derived from the phrase "among you" (Deut. 18:10), which includes oneself.
Combined Ruling Liability for Molekh requires: (1) handing child to Molekh priests, (2) passing through fire by leaping — not on foot, (3) passing one's own descendant — not a parent or sibling.

⚖️ Three Mentions of Karet for Idol Worship

הִכָּרֵת תִּכָּרֵת הַנֶּפֶשׁ הַהִוא (במדבר טו:לא)
Rabbi Yosei son of Rabbi Ḥanina Karet appears three times in the Torah regarding idol worship:

1. Worshipping an idol in its typical manner of worship
2. Worshipping an idol not in its typical manner (e.g., bowing to Mercury)
3. The specific ritual of Molekh — needed because Molekh may not qualify as standard idol worship

A fourth karet appears for blasphemy — the double expression hikkaret tikkaret teaches excision in both this world and the next.

Rabbi Yishmael: But karet for blasphemy already appears earlier! Answer: the double form adds excision in the World-to-Come.
