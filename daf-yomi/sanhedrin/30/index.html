⚖️ Daf 30: Truth, Privacy, and Testimony

Rabbi Yoḥanan
Write simply: 'He is exempt' — do not reveal who dissented. The prohibition of talebearing (לשון הרע, Lev. 19:16) protects the minority judge.
Reish Lakish
Specify every judge's position — 'So-and-so deemed exempt, so-and-so deemed liable.' A document where not all agreed cannot simply say 'exempt' — that is marat sheker (appearance of falsehood).
Rabbi Elazar
Accepts both concerns → compromise formula: 'From the statement of the judges, so-and-so was deemed exempt.' Implies a ruling emerged from deliberation without revealing the dissenter.
Core Tension of the Daf Privacy of the dissenting judge (לשון הרע) vs. truthfulness of the legal document (מראית שקר) — and whether witnesses need to observe or testify together at all

📜 When Must a Court Document Specify Its Composition?

Document signed by only 2 judges — third judge not mentioned
↓
Ravina (initial view)
Apply Reish Lakish's principle: witnesses don't sign unless procedure was proper → presume valid court of 3
Rav Natan bar Ami (citing Rava)
Ravina's reasoning is rejected — still need to address the missing third judge explicitly
↓
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak: If document states 'We the court convened' — this implies 3 judges; no further specification needed
↓
Final confirmation: document mentions Rabbana Ashi — proves it was a proper, convened court of three

✍️ Writing the Verdict When Judges Disagree (2-1 Split)

Rabbi Yoḥanan
Write simply: פלוני פטור (so-and-so is exempt). Reason: lo telekh rakhil be-amekha — do not go as a talebearer. Revealing the dissenter embarrasses him publicly.
Reish Lakish
Write: 'Judges X and Y deem him exempt; Judge Z deems him liable.' Reason: a document stating only 'exempt' when one judge disagreed has the appearance of falsehood.
Rabbi Elazar
Accepts both valid concerns → writes: 'מדברי פלוני זכאי' — 'from the words of the judges, he was deemed exempt.' Implies a ruling emerged from deliberation without naming dissenters.
Gemara's Conclusion The practical difference is only wording — the Gemara rejects theories that the dissenting judge pays more or less, since he cannot be blamed for a majority outcome.

👁️ Must Witnesses Observe the Incident Together?

First Tanna

  • Witnesses must see the incident together as one
  • A saw on Monday, B saw on Tuesday → cannot combine
  • Logic: '100 dinars this one saw, that one did not see' — they are testifying about different events
  • Verse: 'witness' (singular) means they must act as one unit

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa

  • Witnesses need not see the incident together
  • Separate observations of the same event combine
  • Derives from: 'whether he has seen or known' — each alone suffices
  • Concedes to R. Natan: they also need not testify at the same time

Halakhic Rulings

  • Rav: Halakha follows R. Yehoshua ben Korḥa — both monetary & capital
  • Ulla: Only for land (unmovable); not movable property
  • Rav Yosef (citing Ulla): Applies to both land and movables
  • Ordination story: Rabbi Yosei b. R. Ḥanina ordained despite giving an 'obvious' answer — once ordained, one stays ordained

⚔️ Contradicting Witnesses — When Does It Invalidate Testimony?

Capital Law — strict standard: Any contradiction in examination questions (bedikot or ḥakirot) fully invalidates the testimony
↓
Rav Yehuda — monetary law: Contradictions on secondary details do NOT invalidate. Example: one witness says money was in a black purse, the other says a white purse → testimony stands
↓
Rava qualifies: Black vs. white purse is reasonable to forget — a legitimate secondary detail. But contradictions on core facts still disqualify even in monetary law
↓
Dream testimony (amud aleph): If someone dreams that his late father's hidden money belongs to 'so-and-so' — this has no halakhic weight whatsoever. Dreams cannot obligate or exempt anyone.
↓
Principle stated explicitly: 'If it is in the power of the witness to take the money, his statement stands; if it is not in his power, he has said nothing' — credibility requires the ability to have acted on the knowledge
