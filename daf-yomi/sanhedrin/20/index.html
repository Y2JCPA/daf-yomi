⚖️ Why Was Abner Punished?

Rav
Abner did not protest when Saul ordered the massacre of the priests of Nob. He had the power — his hands were unbound, his feet unfettered — yet he remained silent.
Rabbi Yitzchak
He did protest — but Saul would not listen. The verse praises him: "Your hands were not bound" means he was blameless. His protest simply went unheeded.
Rav Naḥman b. R. Yitzchak
If he protested Nob, why was he punished? Because he delayed David's kingdom by 2½ years by propping up the house of Saul after Saul's death.
Verse at Issue "Your hands were not bound, nor your feet put into fetters" (II Sam. 3:34) — read as praise by one opinion, as indictment by another

🛏️ What Is a Dargash?

דַּרְגָּשׁ — "מִטָּה שֶׁל גִּינּוּנִי" (Ulla) / מִטָּה שֶׁל עוֹר (Rav Taḥlifa)
Talmudic Mystery Object The mishna states the king reclines on a dargash during the meal of comfort. Three opinions on what it is:

Ulla: A "bed of fortune" — kept in homes for decoration and good luck, not for sleeping
Rav Taḥlifa (via Ravin): A leather bed
Rabbi Yirmeya / Rabbi Yoḥanan: A bed whose straps are woven inside the bedposts (unlike a regular bed whose straps are tied around the outside)

Practical stakes: A mourner must overturn his bed — must he overturn a dargash too?

📜 The Passage on Royal Powers (I Samuel 8): Permission or Warning?

Permission — Rav Yehuda / Shmuel

  • Everything listed in I Sam. 8:11–17 is permitted to the king
  • The passage defines the legitimate scope of royal authority
  • King may tax, conscript, and seize fields without sinning
  • Parallel tanna'im view: Rabbi Yosei agrees
  • A subject who resists the king transgresses

Warning Only — Opposing View

  • The passage is merely a prophetic warning of royal abuse
  • It grants no permission — a king who does these things sins
  • Said in response to Israel's demand, not as ideal law
  • Parallel tanna'im view: Rabbi Nehorai agrees
  • Israel's desire for a king was itself a complaint against God

🏛️ Three Mitzvot Upon Entering the Land — in Order

1. Appoint a King — "You shall surely set a king over you" (Deut. 17:15)
Must come first; all else depends on stable leadership
↓
2. Destroy Amalek — "Blot out the memory of Amalek" (Deut. 25:19)
Proof: God's throne (כֵּס) is incomplete while Amalek exists — only a king can wage that war
↓
3. Build the Temple — "Seek His dwelling and go there" (Deut. 12:5)
Proof: David could not build until God gave him rest from all enemies (II Sam. 7:1–2)
↓
Note: Rabbi Nehorai holds the kingship commandment was a concession to Israel's complaints — the ideal was direct divine rule; yet once asked, it is obligatory
