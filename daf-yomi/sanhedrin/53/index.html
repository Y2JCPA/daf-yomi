🪨 How is Stoning Derived for All Listed Offenders?

Rabbi Zeira
Necromancers and sorcerers have stoning explicitly written. But for the rest of those listed as stoned — whose punishment is derived by analogy — from where do we know their method of execution?
Abaye
It is derived from a verbal analogy: דמיהם בם ('their blood shall be upon them') appears in both passages. This links the derived cases to the explicitly stated punishment of stoning.
Rabbi Zeira
But then why does the Torah also write 'they shall be put to death' — if the verbal analogy is the source, that phrase seems redundant?
Abaye
That phrase is needed for a separate halakha: to teach that a murderer is executed only for intentional killing — warning is not required for capital liability in his case.
Key Principle When a phrase seems redundant, the Gemara finds the independent teaching it carries — preventing any word of Torah from being superfluous.

👨‍👩‍👦 Mother Who is Father's Wife: How Many Sin-Offerings?

Rabbi Yehuda

  • One who has intercourse with his mother who is also his father's wife…
  • …is liable for ONE sin-offering only
  • Reason: the two prohibitions merge — 'She is your mother' subsumes the father's wife prohibition
  • The verse singles out the mother-relationship as the operative one

The Rabbis

  • One who has intercourse with his mother who is also his father's wife…
  • …is liable for TWO sin-offerings
  • Reason: Two distinct prohibitions are violated simultaneously — mother AND father's wife
  • Each prohibition stands independently, creating dual liability

Mishna Rule

  • Daughter-in-law who is also one's son's wife → TWO sin-offerings
  • This supports the Rabbis: dual relationships create dual liability
  • One body can carry two distinct prohibitions
  • Used to challenge Rabbi Yehuda's position

📖 What is Rabbi Yehuda's Actual Source? Three Explanations

Abaye
Source: The verse 'She is your mother' (Lev. 18:7) teaches that liability attaches because she is his mother — not because she is his father's wife. Therefore: one prohibition, one offering.
Gemara Objects
But the parallel verse about father's wife (Lev. 18:8) says 'it is your father's nakedness' — by Abaye's logic, that should also exclude liability for the mother-relationship, leaving only one offering from that side!
Rav Aḥa b. Rav Ika
Alternative Source: The singular עֶרְוָתָהּ ('her nakedness') implies only one liability for intercourse with one's mother — even if she is simultaneously his father's wife.
Gemara Objects
But the same singular appears regarding daughter-in-law, yet the mishna rules two sin-offerings there! One body, two prohibitions, singular form — yet still dual liability.
Rava
Final Answer: Rabbi Yehuda holds that 'the nakedness of your father' (Lev. 18:8) itself refers to your father's wife. And this interpretation is derived from Deut. 23:1. Since both verses refer to the same prohibition, it merges into one — yielding only one sin-offering.
Method The Gemara tests each proposed source against mishna cases — a proposed source is rejected if it cannot account for rulings elsewhere.

🔗 Rav Aḥa of Difti & Ravina: Resolving the Stoning Derivation

Starting Point: The punishment of stoning for many transgressors is derived from an analogy to necromancers/sorcerers via the phrase דמיהם בם
↓
Rav Aḥa of Difti asks: Could we instead derive punishment from adultery with a married woman — which also says 'they shall be put to death'? That would yield strangulation, not stoning!
↓
Answer — Adultery case: Since the Torah explicitly specifies that a betrothed woman is stoned, we infer a married woman is NOT stoned — ruling out adultery as the source for stoning
↓
Rav Aḥa of Difti asks: What about deriving from 'one who wounds his father or mother'? Also says 'put to death' — could yield a different execution method
↓
Answer — Wounding parents: We derive that case from married-woman adultery (strangulation) rather than from necromancers (stoning) — because the 'put to death' language appears in both, and adultery is a closer analogy
↓
Ravina's Clincher: The very cases we are trying to derive cannot serve as the source for each other — the derivation must anchor in the explicit case of necromancers/sorcerers where stoning is written outright
