Sanhedrin 61 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava, what is the criterion for becoming liable for idol worship?
A. Performing any act of worship, regardless of intent
B. Worshipping in the idol's typical manner of service
C. Sincerely accepting the idol upon oneself as a god
D. Being explicitly commanded to worship by an inciter
Correct: C — Rava holds that the decisive criterion is sincere acceptance of the idol as one's god. One who bows out of love or fear for a person, without that inner acceptance, is exempt — the act alone is insufficient.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why is one who worships Mercury by bowing (rather than throwing stones) liable according to Rava bar Rav Ḥanan?
A. Bowing is Mercury's alternative typical manner of worship
B. The verse singles out bowing to teach that any honorable worship is liable
C. Mercury accepts all forms of service as equivalent
D. Bowing is prohibited because it is more severe than stone-throwing
Correct: B — Rava bar Rav Ḥanan derives from the verse on bowing that any honorable form of worship — even if atypical for that particular idol — carries the death penalty. Bowing was singled out precisely to extend liability across all honorable modes.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Abaye resolve the apparent contradiction between the two mishnayot on idol worship liability?
A. One mishna discusses typical worship, the other atypical worship
B. One mishna refers to a self-incited person; the other to one incited by another
C. One mishna is pre-Sinai law; the other is post-Sinai
D. One mishna discusses an apostate; the other a regular Jew
Correct: B — Abaye distinguishes: the mishna requiring actual worship refers to someone who incited themselves. The mishna holding verbal approval sufficient refers to one who was incited by another — in that case, saying 'yes' to the inciter is itself an act of liability.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What does the baraita teach about the difference between an individual who was incited and a subverted city?
A. An incited individual is stoned; a subverted city's residents are burned
B. A subverted city is judged more leniently — decapitation instead of stoning
C. An incited individual brings an offering; a subverted city is executed
D. There is no legal difference — both are judged identically
Correct: B — The Torah distinguishes the cases to render the punishment of a subverted city more lenient: residents are decapitated rather than stoned (the harsher punishment for individuals). This reflects a different mode of execution, not exemption.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rav Ashi offers yet another resolution to the contradicting mishnayot. What is his approach?
A. One mishna is the majority opinion; the other is a dissent
B. The mishna deeming verbal approval liable refers to an apostate Jew
C. The mishna requiring actual worship refers to a gentile, not a Jew
D. The contradiction is only apparent — both mishnayot require actual worship
Correct: B — Rav Ashi resolves the contradiction by noting that the mishna holding verbal acceptance sufficient refers to an apostate Jew. Since he has already abandoned Judaism, his statement of acceptance suffices — his status is already defined as one who worships idols.
