🐂 Benefit from Animals Sacrificed to Idols

Rabbi Yoḥanan
Benefit is prohibited from the moment of slaughter — even if blood was never sprinkled nor fat burned for the idol. The act of slaughter itself renders the animal forbidden.
Reish Lakish
Benefit is only prohibited once the service is completed — the animal remains permitted until blood is sprinkled or fat burned. Slaughter alone does not forbid it.
Rav Pappa
Challenge to Rabbi Yoḥanan: If the slaughter verse teaches only the slaughterer's liability, do you not need a separate verse for 'intention to complete from one rite to another rite'?
Rav Aḥa b. Rav Ika
Challenge to Reish Lakish: Even according to you, does one need a verse to teach the slaughterer is liable? You only permit benefit — but liability for the act itself was never in question!
Key Verse "And they shall no more sacrifice their offerings to the goat-demons" (Leviticus 17:7) — used to derive liability for sacrificing to Mercury

🗣️ Four Amora'im: When Does Verbal Acceptance Trigger Liability?

Rav Hamnuna

  • Mishna A: 'worships' = liable
  • Mishna B: 'accepts as god' = liable
  • Resolution: A = accepts deity only through worship
  • B = accepts deity via verbal declaration alone

Rav Yosef

  • Unnecessary — it is a tanna dispute
  • Rabbi Meir: liable even for verbal declaration
  • Rabbi Yehuda: liable only if says 'worship him'
  • Revised: both hold verbal approval = liable in some case

Abaye

  • Mishna A (needs action) = self-incited person
  • Mishna B (verbal = liable) = incited by another
  • Proof: 'You shall not approve nor listen' (Deut 13:9)
  • Approval of another's incitement is sufficient

Rava

  • Both mishnayot = incited by others
  • Mishna A: inciter described idol abstractly
  • Mishna B: inciter described idol vividly
  • 'It eats like this, drinks like this, does good' → approval = liable

⚖️ Abaye vs. Rava: The Core Dispute on Love/Fear Worship

Abaye
One who worships an idol due to love or fear is liable. The mishna lists 'one who worships' — it does not require sincere belief. The act itself creates liability.
Rava
Such a person is exempt. The criterion is sincere acceptance of the idol as god. If one bowed merely to please another, without internalizing it as deity, there is no prohibited act.
Abaye (proof from baraita)
From Exodus 20:5 — 'You shall not bow nor worship' are listed separately: bowing = even like Haman (bowed to as idol); worship = even in an atypical manner. No intent required!
Rava (rebuttal)
The verse teaches only that bowing to Haman as an idol-object is prohibited — not merely bowing to him as a person. Intent still matters; bowing without any divine acceptance is not idolatry.
Abaye's Mnemonic — Three Proofs עֶבֶד יִשְׁתַּחֲוֶה לַמָּשִׁיחַ — 'A slave will bow to the anointed' (Slave / Bow / Anointed = three scriptural sources for Abaye's position)
