🔍 'When Does This Apply' — Explain or Disagree?

R. Yehoshua b. Levi
Both phrases — 'when does this halakha apply' AND 'in what case' — indicate that Rabbi Yehuda is only explaining the Rabbis, not disagreeing with them.
R. Yoḥanan
Only 'when does this halakha apply' (במה דברים אמורים) signals explanation. The phrase 'in what case' signals genuine disagreement between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis.
Rami bar Ḥama
Even 'when does this halakha apply' can indicate disagreement. The baraita shows Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis truly dispute the dice player case.
Gemara
You are setting one Sage's view against another's — Rami bar Ḥama vs. Rava. No contradiction; they simply hold different positions on what these phrases signal.
Why It Matters If Rabbi Yehuda only explains the Rabbis, the dice player disqualification is the Rabbis' unanimous view. If he disagrees, the baraita reflects only Rabbi Yehuda's position citing Rabbi Tarfon.

🕊️ Two Interpretations of 'Those Who Fly Pigeons'

Babylonian View: Pigeon Racing

  • Gamblers who race pigeons against each other
  • Wager: 'If your pigeon arrives first, I pay you; if mine does, you pay me'
  • Classic asmakhta — relying on an uncertain outcome
  • Each party assumes he will win → no genuine intent to transfer money → robbery

Eretz Yisrael View: Ara (Luring)

  • Train pigeons to lure other people's pigeons home
  • The lured pigeons 'choose' to come — not seized by force
  • Still considered dishonest gain from others' property
  • Disqualified because the pigeons rest on others' property by inducement

🔄 Repentance Paths — How Each May Be Reinstated

🎲 Dice / Pispasim Gambler: Must break his dice completely and resolve not to play even for nothing — abandoning the occupation entirely
↓
💰 Interest Lender or Borrower: Must tear up all promissory notes for usurious loans and resolve never again to lend with interest — even to a gentile
↓
🕊️ Pigeon Racer / Ara Practitioner: Must break his instruments (lures/traps) and resolve not to engage in this even for free
↓
🌾 Sabbatical Year Merchant: Must wait until another Shemita year arrives and then refrain from trading in its produce — Rabbi Neḥemya adds: must also return the money
↓
🩸 Suspected Tereifa Slaughterer: Per Rav Idi bar Avin — must relocate to a place where no one knows him and demonstrate repeated honest behavior; verbal repentance alone is insufficient

📜 Rav Yehuda's Rule & Rabbi Zeira's Father

חזקת כשרות — גובה מכס כשר עד שיוודע שגובה יותר מן הראוי
Presumption of Fitness Rav Yehuda rules: An ordinary shepherd is presumed disqualified unless the court specifically knows he avoids others' fields — grazing in them is so common the presumption runs against him.

An ordinary tax collector is presumed fit unless the court specifically knows he takes beyond his due — proper collection is expected.

Story illustrating righteous conduct: Rabbi Zeira's father served as tax collector for 13 years. When the chief collector came to town, he would warn residents. When collecting, he spared Torah scholars. His careful conduct showed how an honest tax collector behaves.
