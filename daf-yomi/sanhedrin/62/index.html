📜 Rabbi Zakkai's Baraita: Rejected from the Study Hall

Rabbi Zakkai
If one sacrificed, burned incense, poured a libation, and bowed to an idol all in one lapse of awareness — he brings only one sin-offering for all.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Go out and teach that outside the study hall! The halakha is that one is obligated to bring a separate offering for each and every act of idol worship.
Rabbi Abba
Rabbi Zakkai's view mirrors Rabbi Yosei's position on kindling fire — it was singled out as an ordinary prohibition, not to divide the acts into separate transgressions.
Rav Yosef
You cannot compare! Rabbi Yosei derives division of Shabbat labors from 'from one of them' (Lev. 4:2) independently — kindling is irrelevant. And that verse cannot apply to idol worship at all.
Conclusion The suggestion to derive separate obligations for each act of idol worship from 'and performs from one of them' is groundless — that verse applies only to an anointed priest's unwitting sin, not to idol worship.

⚖️ Abaye vs Rava: Worship Out of Love or Fear

Abaye
One who worships an idol out of love or fear of a person IS liable — this constitutes genuine idol worship. His unawareness that it is forbidden makes him an unwitting transgressor.
Rava
One who worships out of love or fear of a person is EXEMPT — he never intended to accept the idol as a god. The act lacks genuine theological acceptance, so it is not idol worship at all.
Gemara
This dispute determines the very definition of 'unwitting' idol worship: According to Abaye it is love/fear worship; according to Rava it must be one who thought the practice was permitted.
Rava (unresolved dilemma)
If one thinks idol worship is generally permitted and performs multiple rites in one lapse — does he bring one sin-offering or one per rite? This dilemma was left without resolution (תֵּיקוּ).
Stakes The definition of 'unwitting' determines how many sin-offerings are owed and whether verse-derivations from Lev. 4:2 apply to idol worship at all.

🏛️ Reciprocal Stringencies: Shabbat vs Other Mitzvot (Amud B)

Stringency of Shabbat

  • Two distinct labors in one lapse of awareness → two sin-offerings
  • E.g., reaping AND grinding in one lapse each require their own offering
  • Each category of labor is an independent prohibition
  • Not so with other mitzvot: eating forbidden fat twice = only one offering

Stringency of Other Mitzvot

  • Acting without any intent still incurs an obligation
  • E.g., forbidden fat in mouth mistaken for spittle — swallowed → obligated
  • Shabbat requires at least awareness that it is Shabbat for liability
  • Other mitzvot: even fully unintentional acts require a sin-offering

📋 Rabbi Yoḥanan's Hermeneutic Principle

כָּל מַאן דְּמַפְרֵשׁ לִי מַתְנִיתִין דְּחָבִית כְּחַד תַּנָּא — אֲנָא אֵיזִיל בַּתְרֵיהּ לְבֵי מַסּוּתָא
Why Rabbi Yoḥanan Rejected the Baraita Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda's baraita presents two clauses: a stringency of Shabbat over other mitzvot, and a stringency of other mitzvot over Shabbat.

The problem: To make the clauses work, one must interpret the first clause as comparing Shabbat to idol worship, and the second as comparing other mitzvot (like forbidden fat) to Shabbat — mixing two different comparisons.

Rabbi Yoḥanan's rule: He never interprets the first clause of a baraita with one explanation and the second clause with a different explanation. The baraita must be internally consistent.

His famous declaration: 'Whoever explains to me the mishna of the barrel according to one Tanna, I will carry his clothes to the bathhouse' — demanding perfect internal consistency in tannaitic texts.

📋 Sanhedrin 62 — Summary Map

Core Question: Multiple acts of idol worship in one lapse of awareness — one sin-offering or one per act?
↓
Rabbi Zakkai: One offering suffices for all rites in one lapse
Rabbi Yoḥanan: Each rite = separate obligation — baraita rejected
↓
Rabbi Abba bridges to Shabbat: Rabbi Yosei (one offering per lapse) vs Rabbi Natan (each labor = separate offering) — same debate applies to idol worship
↓
Rav Yosef objects: Rabbi Yosei derives division from Lev. 4:2 independently — not from kindling. The bridge to idol worship collapses.
↓
Final conclusion: Lev. 4:2 cannot apply to idol worship (it addresses the anointed priest's sin). Rava's dilemma remains unresolved (תֵּיקוּ).
