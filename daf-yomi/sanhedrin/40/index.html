⚖️ Capital Case Witness Examination Procedure

Witnesses summoned and examined separately, one at a time
↓
Administer the seven chakirot (interrogations): jubilee, shemita, year, month, day, hour, place
↓
If any witness says 'I don't know' on a chakira → testimony immediately void
↓
Administer bedikot (examinations): Do you recognize him? Did you warn him? Details of the act?
↓
Court deliberates: open with acquittal arguments; any student may speak in favor of defendant
↓
Majority acquits → release; majority convicts → delay verdict to the following day

🔎 Chakirot (Interrogations) vs. Bedikot (Examinations)

Chakirot — Interrogations

  • Fixed questions about time and place
  • If ONE witness says 'I don't know' → entire testimony void
  • Failure on any single point invalidates both witnesses
  • Derived from the word heitev ('diligently') in three Torah verses

Bedikot — Examinations

  • Questions probing details of the specific act
  • If ONE witness says 'I don't know' → testimony still stands
  • More bedikot = more praiseworthy (may expose false witnesses)
  • Ben Zakkai examined witnesses about which type of fig tree stood nearby

📅 Time Discrepancy Between Witnesses — When Does Testimony Stand?

Two witnesses give different times for the same event
↓
Date: off by 1 day
Testimony stands — one witness may have miscounted the end of the previous month (29 vs. 30 days)
Date: off by 2+ days
Testimony void — too great a discrepancy to attribute to error
↓
Hour: off by 1–2 hours
Testimony stands — reasonable error estimating the sun's position
Hour: off by 3+ hours
Rabbi Yehuda: testimony void — sun position at those hours is unmistakably different

🗣️ How Many Interrogations Are Required?

Rabbis
Seven interrogations apply to ALL capital cases — idol worship, murder, and all four death penalties. We derive this from a verbal analogy (gezerah shavah) on the word 'diligently' (הֵיטֵב) appearing in all three relevant Torah verses.
Rabbi Yosei
Ask only: on which day, at which hour, and in which place. Too many interrogations make witnesses afraid to testify — they fear being tripped on technicalities. This would allow murderers to escape justice.
Rabbis
But if a witness says 'the accused killed the victim just now,' you still need to ask: which day? which hour? which place? So these questions always apply — you cannot selectively exempt witnesses.
Rabbi Yosei
The 'just now' case is common! Witnesses who saw an act and come immediately. The interrogations are calibrated for typical testimony — for this common case, the questions serve no purpose and should be waived.
Core Tension Rigorous examination protects the innocent from false witnesses — but excessive questioning may deter truthful witnesses, leaving criminals unpunished.

📜 Deriving the 7 Interrogations: Verbal Analogy on Heitev

Rav Yehuda
Three Torah verses each use the word heitev ('diligently'): Deut. 13:15 (idol worship), Deut. 17:4 (idolatrous city), Deut. 19:18 (conspiring witnesses). A gezerah shavah links them, requiring the same seven interrogations in all capital cases.
Gemara challenge
But these three cases are not similar! In an idolatrous city, the condemned lose their money too. In individual idol worship, money is spared. In conspiring witnesses, no city is executed. How can we derive one from another?
Rav Yehuda
We do not rely on conceptual similarity — a gezerah shavah is a textual link, not logical. The word heitev is superfluous in each verse (the Torah could have doubled the verb instead), freeing it to establish the linkage.
Rabbi Abbahu
Then why not derive EIGHT interrogations? Why not add a question about the specific year within the jubilee? Or treat the two-hour window as two separate questions?
Resolution Knowing the shemita cycle already determines the year in the jubilee — the question is redundant. The derivation yields exactly seven, no more.
