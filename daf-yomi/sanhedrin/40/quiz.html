QUESTION 1 OF 5
What happens if one of two witnesses says 'I do not know' in response to a chakira (interrogation)?
A. The testimony of the other witness alone is sufficient to proceed
B. The testimony of both witnesses is immediately void
C. The court asks additional questions to clarify the confusion
D. Only the uncertain witness is disqualified; the other may continue
Correct: B — The mishna states explicitly: with chakirot (interrogations), if even one witness says 'I do not know,' the testimony of both is void immediately. The two witnesses function as a single unit — one's failure collapses the entire testimony.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Two witnesses disagree — one says the event occurred on the 2nd of the month, the other says the 3rd. What is the ruling?
A. Testimony is void due to irreconcilable contradiction
B. The earlier date is preferred as more cautious
C. Testimony stands — one witness may have mistaken the last day of the prior month
D. A third witness must be summoned to resolve the dispute
Correct: C — A one-day discrepancy is acceptable: if the previous month had 29 days, one witness may have counted it as 30. This is a reasonable error. By contrast, a two-day discrepancy cannot be explained this way and voids the testimony.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
From what source does Rav Yehuda derive the requirement of seven interrogations?
A. A halakha l'Moshe miSinai (oral tradition from Moses at Sinai)
B. A verbal analogy (gezerah shavah) on the word heitev ('diligently') in three Torah verses
C. A rabbinic enactment enacted to protect innocent defendants
D. An a fortiori inference (kal va-chomer) from monetary cases
Correct: B — Rav Yehuda derives the seven interrogations from a gezerah shavah linking three verses — idol worship (Deut. 13:15), idolatrous city (Deut. 17:4), and conspiring witnesses (Deut. 19:18) — all using the superfluous word heitev ('diligently').
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is Rabbi Yosei's core argument against the Rabbis' position on the number of required interrogations?
A. Time and place are impossible to verify with certainty, making the questions useless
B. Requiring too many interrogations may deter witnesses, allowing guilty parties to go free
C. The Torah mentions only three interrogations in any single verse, not seven
D. Interrogations should only apply when the defendant denies the charge
Correct: B — Rabbi Yosei argues that multiplying the interrogations makes witnesses afraid they will be tripped up on obscure details — discouraging testimony and ultimately letting murderers escape justice. Fewer required questions encourages truthful witnesses to come forward.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does failing a chakira void testimony entirely, while a witness saying 'I don't know' on a bedhika (examination) does not?
A. Chakirot are biblical; bedikot are only rabbinic in origin
B. Chakirot test whether witnesses can account for the basic facts of when and where; a gap there reveals fundamental unreliability, while bedikot probe details the witness may simply not have noticed
C. Bedikot questions are optional and courts can waive them entirely
D. A witness who fails a chakira is presumed to be lying; a witness who fails a bedhika is merely forgetful
Correct: B — The chakirot establish the foundational framework of testimony — any witness who cannot account for when and where an event occurred cannot be deemed reliable. Bedikot probe specific details of the act; ignorance of a detail does not undermine the testimony's core reliability, and more such questions only add scrutiny that may expose perjurers.
