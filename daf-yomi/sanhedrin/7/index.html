⚖️ Two Views on the Timing of Compromise

First Tanna
A judge should offer the option of compromise before hearing the litigants' claims — while the case is fully open and no direction is apparent.
R' Shimon ben Menasya
Even after hearing their statements, compromise may be offered — as long as the judge does not yet know toward whom the case is leaning.
Gemara
Once a judge perceives which side will prevail, offering compromise is forbidden — it would pressure the losing side and pervert justice in their favor.
Proverbs 17:14 "The beginning of strife is as when one releases water" — once a dispute floods its banks, it cannot be recalled. Stop it early, before the dam breaks.

🔥 Aaron and the Golden Calf — A Calculated Sacrifice

וַיֹּאמֶר אֲלֵהֶם אַהֲרֹן פָּרְקוּ נִזְמֵי הַזָּהָב — Exodus 32:2
Why Aaron Complied Aaron's inner reasoning: If I refuse the people's demand, they will kill me as they killed Hur. The verse "Shall the priest and the prophet be slain in the Sanctuary of the Lord?" (Lamentations 2:20) will be fulfilled through my death — and Israel will bear the catastrophic sin of murdering a prophet inside the Temple.

Aaron chose to participate in building the calf, believing that delay through apparent compliance was less sinful than provoking that graver outcome. He acted hoping Moses would return in time.

🌳 Worthy vs. Unworthy Judges

The Worthy Judge

  • Judges by absolute truth — seen as if he repaired the entire world (R' Yonatan)
  • Rav gathered 10 scholars before ruling so each bore only a splinter of the responsibility
  • Humbles himself: Rav declared 'by his own will he goes out to danger of death'
  • Recuses himself from bias: Rav immediately stepped aside when his host appeared as litigant
  • Deliberate and careful: does not stride to the altar (Exodus 20:23 — Bar Kappara)

The Unworthy Judge

  • Appointing him is like planting an ashera (idolatrous tree) among Israel — Reish Lakish
  • Like an idol of gold or silver: impressive outside, no breath within (Habakkuk 2:19)
  • The Nasi's unlearned appointee was rebuked through Yehuda bar Naḥmani's public sermon
  • Ruins the land: 'He who exacts gifts [terumot] overthrows it' (Proverbs 29:4) — Rav Naḥman bar Kohen

📋 Duties of the Judge — Deuteronomy 1:16–17 Unpacked

"Hear the causes between your brethren" — listen patiently to both sides, even litigants who seem repetitive or foolish
↓
"Judge righteously between a man and his brother" — Reish Lakish: verify the judgment by meticulously examining every particular of the case
↓
"And the stranger [gero] who is with him" — gero resembles gur (dwell); distinguish even between the merits of an oven and a stove — every ownership detail matters
↓
"You shall not respect [takiru] persons in judgment" — do not favor the wealthy or powerful; hear the small and the great alike
↓
"You shall not be afraid [taguru] before any man" — do not suppress [tgor] your ruling out of fear; a judge who hides his ruling gathers sin (Rav Kahana)
↓
Practical application: Rav immediately recused himself when his host — who had shown him hospitality — came before him for judgment. Even indirect benefit disqualifies a judge.
