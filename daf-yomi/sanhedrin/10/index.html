👨 The Husband Cannot Testify Against His Wife

אין אדם משים עצמו רשע — a person cannot render himself wicked
Core Rule If a man testifies that another man committed adultery with his wife, his testimony is accepted to execute the accused man (combined with a second witness). However, the husband's testimony is inadmissible to execute his wife. The Gemara applies the principle: a person cannot testify about themselves — the husband is considered as one with his wife, making him effectively a party to the case rather than a neutral witness.

🗡️ Rava: Conspiring Witnesses — Two Parallel Cases

Rava — Case 1
Witnesses testify: 'So-and-so had relations with a betrothed young woman' — without naming the woman. They are then rendered conspiring witnesses (eidim zomemim). Ruling: they are executed (receiving the punishment they intended), but they owe no monetary payment, since the unspecified girl cannot come to collect.
Rava — Case 2
Witnesses testify: 'So-and-so committed bestiality with an ox' — without specifying which ox. They are rendered conspiring witnesses. Ruling: they are executed, but the ox is not killed, since no particular animal was identified. The secondary consequence falls away; the primary punishment stands.
Gemara
Why are both cases necessary? The ox case adds something: even though no monetary value can compensate for an animal's death, we might have thought the whole case collapses. Rava clarifies: conspiring witnesses always receive the intended punishment, even when secondary consequences are unenforceable.
Principle eidim zomemim receive the punishment they intended — even when secondary effects (payment, killing the animal) cannot be carried out due to vagueness.

👨‍⚖️ How Many Judges Are Required for Lashes?

Rav Huna
Three judges. Derived from Deut. 25:1: 'They shall justify the just, and they shall condemn the wicked.' Each plural verb implies two judges; add one to create a majority — total of three. The verse refers to financial disputes but applies equally to lashes.
Rabbi Yishmael / Abaye
Twenty-three judges. Abaye: a gezeira shava (verbal analogy) on the word 'wicked' (רשע) connects the lashes verse to capital cases — just as capital cases need 23, so do lashes.
Rava
Twenty-three judges — no verbal analogy needed. Conceptually, lashes stand in place of execution: the Torah substituted flogging for death in certain cases, so lashes must be judged by the same court of 23.
Rav Ashi (to Rav Aḥa)
The verse says 'your brother will be dishonored before your eyes' — you must strike a living person. Once dead, the obligation ends. The court must therefore evaluate the recipient's fitness carefully before administering each lash.
Tension The verse context (Deut. 25) suggests a small court; the severity of lashes — potentially lethal — argues for the gravitas of a capital court.
