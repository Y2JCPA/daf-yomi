Authentication of Documents: Signatures vs. Content

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
The witnesses are testifying about their handwriting — authenticating their own signatures. Each witness covers only his own signature.
Rabbis
The witnesses are testifying about the 100 dinars in the document. Two witnesses on the document suffice to ratify it.
Shmuel
The halakha follows the Rabbis: each signatory need only testify about his own signature to ratify the document.
Core Issue The nature of testimony in document authentication determines how many additional witnesses are needed — especially when a signatory has died.
3 / 9

What Are Witnesses Testifying About?

R' Yehuda HaNasi
Witnesses testify about their handwriting (ketav yadam). Since each authenticates only his own signature, you need an additional witness per signature.
Rabbis
Witnesses testify about the monetary content — the 100 dinars. The two signatories together constitute sufficient testimony to ratify.
Gemara
This distinction is not based on uncertainty. R' Yehuda HaNasi is certain they testify about handwriting — which matters when a signatory dies.
Key Distinction If R' Yehuda HaNasi were uncertain, he would need to rule stringently in both directions — requiring extra witnesses in every scenario.

When One Signatory Dies: How Many Extra Witnesses?

One of two signatories on a document has died. How do we ratify?
↓
R' Yehuda HaNasi (Lenient)
Only 1 additional witness needed for the deceased's signature. The surviving signatory + this witness authenticate it.
Rabbis (Stringent)
2 additional witnesses from the street required to authenticate the deceased's signature.
Rav Yehuda / Rav Since R' Yehuda HaNasi holds they testify about handwriting, the surviving witness + one outsider = two witnesses per signature. The Rabbis, who hold they testify about content, need two outsiders to avoid ¾ of the sum resting on a single witness.

Abaye's Earthenware Shard Solution

✍️ The surviving witness writes his signature on an earthenware shard (ḥeres)
↓
🏛️ He casts the shard before the court — the court sees it matches the document
↓
✅ His signature is now authenticated without his testimony
↓
🤝 He and the one additional witness then testify together about the deceased's signature
↓
⚠️ Must use earthenware, not parchment — a forger could write a false loan document above the signature!
Bava Batra 175b A document in the debtor's handwriting — even without witnesses — allows collection from unsold property. This is why a signature on parchment is dangerous.

Three Conclusions from Rav Huna — and Rav Ashi's Objections

Accepted Conclusion

  • A witness can become a judge (ed na'aseh dayyan)
  • The two judges who testified as witnesses also signed as judges
  • This applies to rabbinic matters (document ratification) but NOT Torah-level testimony (e.g., new moon)

Rav Ashi's Objections

  • Judges who recognize signatures may still require witnesses — Rav Huna's case only works because testimony WAS given
  • Witnesses testifying before each judge may not be required in general — here it was necessary only because otherwise there'd be NO testimony at all
Limitation Only the first conclusion — witness becoming judge — is firmly derived. The other two may be specific to Rav Huna's scenario.
