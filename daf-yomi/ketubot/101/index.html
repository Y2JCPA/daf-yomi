Ketubot 101 — Marriage Status & Property Rights

Central Question: What legal standing do marriages of minors, ailoniot, and shniyot have?
↓
Mi'un (Refusal)
Marriage annulled retroactively — she takes her property back
Shniyah (Forbidden Relative)
Both penalized — she loses melog, he pays tzon barzel
Ailonit
If he knew before marriage → ketubah; if not → no ketubah
↓
New Mishna: Obligation to sustain stepdaughter for 5 years stands independently per husband
Key Theme The daf systematically distinguishes marriage categories by their legal consequences for property, ketubah, and obligations.

Legal Standing of a Minor's Marriage

R' Eliezer
The marriage of a minor girl (not by her father) is nothing — her husband has no rights to her findings, earnings, or vows; he doesn't inherit her or become impure for her.
R' Yehoshua
The marriage of a minor girl is something — she has the full legal status of a wife in every sense, except she leaves through mi'un (refusal) rather than a get.
Gemara
Per R' Eliezer, all agree she has no ketubah. The Rav/Shmuel dispute is only per R' Yehoshua.
Rav
R' Yehoshua meant she has a wife's status only regarding her obligations to him, not his to her. Since the marriage is not biblical, the Sages cannot obligate him to pay her.
Shmuel
R' Yehoshua meant literally — she is his wife in every sense, including the ketubah payment.
Core Dispute Can the Sages impose financial obligations on a husband in a marriage that has no Torah-level standing?

Rav Pappa: Who Does Shmuel's Ruling About Worn Clothes Apply To?

Shmuel: She loses rights to worn nikhsei melog but keeps rights to worn tzon barzel
↓
Does this refer to a me'unenet (one who refuses)?
❌ No
If items exist — she takes all. If worn out — she takes nothing. No partial distinction needed.
↓
Does it refer to an ailonit?
❌ No
The logic would be reversed — melog stays in her possession, so she should keep that. Tzon barzel is in his hands, so she'd lose that.
↓
It must refer to a shniyah (secondary forbidden relative) — both are penalized!
Resolution The Sages penalized both parties for violating a rabbinic prohibition: she loses worn melog; he must reimburse worn tzon barzel.

Nikhsei Melog vs Tzon Barzel — Property Types in Marriage

Nikhsei Melog (Usufruct Property)

  • Remains in the wife's possession
  • Husband may benefit from its produce
  • Risk of depreciation falls on the wife
  • If worn out — Shmuel says shniyah loses this
  • Penalty to HER for the forbidden marriage

Tzon Barzel (Guaranteed Property)

  • Transferred to husband's possession
  • Husband bears responsibility for its value
  • He must return its assessed value at divorce
  • If worn out — Shmuel says shniyah keeps this claim
  • Penalty to HIM for the forbidden marriage
Rav Shimi's Inference From Shmuel's ruling we learn: a cloak brought into marriage after the wedding is treated as capital (tzon barzel) — the husband cannot simply use it until it wears out.

Does a Licentious Woman Lose Her Property Rights?

Tanna (before Rav Naḥman)
A woman who was licentious loses her right to her extant worn clothes upon divorce.
Rav Naḥman
If she was unfaithful, were her items also licentious?! Teach the opposite — she does not lose her property rights.
R' Yoḥanan (via Rabba bar bar Ḥana)
That strict teaching is the view of R' Menaḥem alone. The Rabbis hold she does not lose her extant worn clothes.
Principle Personal misconduct does not create a financial penalty on one's property — her items did nothing wrong.

Ailonit & Widow: When Is She Entitled to a Ketubah?

ScenarioRav HunaRav Yehuda
Ailonit — he knew before marriage✅ Entitled to ketubah✅ Entitled to ketubah
Ailonit — he did NOT know❌ No ketubah❌ No ketubah
Widow + Kohen Gadol — he knew✅ Entitled to ketubah✅ Entitled to ketubah
Widow + Kohen Gadol — he did NOT know✅ Entitled (she is 'entirely a wife')❌ No ketubah
Married without specification✅ Widow still gets ketubah❌ Neither gets ketubah
Conclusion Rav Huna is refuted by a baraita: both ailonit and widow require the husband's prior knowledge for ketubah entitlement. Rav Yehuda's view stands.
