Ketubot 56 — Tosefet Ketubah & Stipulations

Halakha follows R' Elazar ben Azarya:
Betrothed woman who is widowed/divorced collects main ketubah but not the tosefet
↓
New Question: What if she entered the chuppah but had no intercourse?
↓
Affection of Wedding
Chuppah alone entitles her to tosefet
Affection of Intercourse
She is like a betrothed woman — no tosefet
↓
Unresolved: Chuppah + niddah (no possibility of intercourse) — teku
Key Ruling Despite Rav Nachman's curse against judges who follow R' Elazar ben Azarya, the Gemara concludes: the halakha IS in accordance with R' Elazar ben Azarya.

Does the Halakha Follow R' Elazar ben Azarya?

Rav Ḥanina
The halakha follows R' Elazar ben Azarya — a betrothed woman collects the main ketubah but not the tosefet.
R' Yannai
Go read your verses outside! Your expertise is Tanakh, not halakha. The halakha does not follow R' Elazar ben Azarya.
R' Yitzḥak bar Avdimi
In the name of Rebbi (R' Yehuda HaNasi): The halakha follows R' Elazar ben Azarya.
Shmuel (via Rav Naḥman)
The halakha follows R' Elazar ben Azarya.
Rav Naḥman (own view)
The halakha does not follow R' Elazar ben Azarya. Any judge who rules like him — misfortune will befall him!
Gemara Conclusion
Even so, the practical halakha IS in accordance with R' Elazar ben Azarya.
Final Ruling Despite the fierce opposition, the Gemara emphatically concludes the halakha follows R' Elazar ben Azarya: a woman divorced or widowed after betrothal receives only the main ketubah, not the tosefet.

Chuppah Without Intercourse — Does She Get the Tosefet?

Ravin asks: Woman enters chuppah (wedding canopy) but is widowed/divorced without intercourse
↓
Affection of Wedding (חיבת חופה)
The chuppah ceremony creates the bond → she collects tosefet
Affection of Intercourse (חיבת ביאה)
Only consummation creates the bond → no tosefet, same as betrothed
↓
Baraita: 'He wrote the tosefet on account of the affection of the first night'
↓
Gemara attempts to prove from this baraita but concludes: no proof either way — 'first night' describes the ordinary mode of behavior for both wedding and intercourse
Teku Rav Ashi adds: If the bride entered the chuppah and then began menstruating (niddah), and the husband died — does she get the tosefet? The question remains unresolved.

Three Views on Reducing the Ketubah

R' Meir

  • Reducing below 200 (virgin) / 100 (widow) = licentious intercourse (בעילת זנות)
  • Stipulation is VOID even if she agrees
  • Ketubah is a Torah obligation (דאורייתא)
  • Stipulating against Torah law = void

R' Yosei

  • One IS permitted to reduce the amount
  • A verbal stipulation suffices
  • Provided the wife agrees to the reduction
  • Views ketubah as rabbinic

R' Yehuda

  • Write full 200 dinars in the document
  • She writes a RECEIPT (שובר) for part
  • E.g., 'I received 100 dinars from you'
  • Preserves dignity while reducing obligation
Core Dispute Is the ketubah a Torah or rabbinic requirement? R' Meir holds it is Torah law, making reduction stipulations void. R' Yosei and R' Yehuda treat it as rabbinic, allowing agreed-upon modifications.

Verbal vs. Written Stipulations — Abaye's Framework

CategoryExampleVerbal Sufficient?Reasoning
Common + Certain rabbinic matterKetubah amount❌ Must be writtenSages reinforced their decree
Uncommon rabbinic matterProduce of wife's property (פירות)✅ Verbal sufficesSages did not reinforce uncommon matters
Common + Uncertain rabbinic matterDonkey drivers / demai✅ Verbal sufficesDemai itself is a stringency — most amei ha'aretz tithe
Abaye vs. Rava Abaye distinguishes common vs. uncommon and certain vs. uncertain matters. Rava explains the donkey drivers case differently: the Sages were lenient about demai because most people DO tithe — it's a built-in leniency.

Can the Ketubah Depend on Movable Property?

First Tanna
A woman's ketubah may not be made dependent on movable property — this is a decree for the betterment of the world (תיקון העולם).
R' Yosei
What 'betterment'? The price of movable property is not fixed — it could lose value and she'd be left with less than her ketubah amount!
Gemara
The first tanna means: without a guarantee it's forbidden. With a guarantee, it's fine.
R' Yosei
Even with a guarantee — the price fluctuates! She could still be shortchanged.
Contradiction If R' Yosei is so protective of the ketubah here, how can he say one may verbally reduce the amount? This tension drives the Gemara's analysis.
