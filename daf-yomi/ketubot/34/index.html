Core Logic of Ketubot 34: Capital Liability vs. Payment

A thief steals an animal and then slaughters it under circumstances that also carry a capital crime
↓
Does capital liability exempt him from the monetary fine (4x or 5x)?
↓
Already Liable for Theft Before Capital Act
Pays the fine — theft obligation preceded the capital crime
Theft & Capital Act Are Simultaneous
Exempt from fine — no theft payment → no slaughter/sale payment
KEY PRINCIPLE The sequence of liabilities matters: if theft preceded the capital crime, the fine applies. If they are simultaneous, kim lei bid'rabba minei exempts payment.

Is Shabbat Slaughter Valid Slaughter?

R' Shimon
Improper slaughter — one that doesn't render meat fit to eat — is not legally 'slaughter.' Therefore no liability for the act of slaughter itself.
Gemara
This works for idolatry and ox sentenced to stoning — their slaughter is ineffective. But Shabbat slaughter is valid! (Ḥullin 14a)
Gemara
The tanna follows R' Yoḥanan HaSandlar: food produced by intentional Shabbat desecration may never be eaten — so the slaughter is effectively invalid.
RESOLUTION R' Yoḥanan HaSandlar's strict view renders Shabbat slaughter 'improper' — aligning it with idolatry and the stoned ox under R' Shimon's principle.

Cooking on Shabbat: Three Opinions

R' Meir

  • Unwitting: May eat the food
  • Intentional: May NOT eat
  • Most lenient for unwitting cases

R' Yehuda

  • Unwitting: May eat after Shabbat ends
  • Intentional: May NEVER eat
  • Penalizes even unwitting — delays benefit

R' Yoḥanan HaSandlar

  • Unwitting: Others may eat after Shabbat, not him
  • Intentional: NO ONE may ever eat it
  • Strictest view — treats product like hekdesh
SOURCE "And you shall observe Shabbat, for it is sacred to you" (Exodus 31:14) — R' Ḥiyya derives: just as hekdesh is forbidden to eat, so too food from Shabbat desecration.

Deriving the Shabbat-Product Prohibition

"It is sacred to you" (Exodus 31:14) — Shabbat is compared to hekdesh
↓
Just as hekdesh may not be eaten, Shabbat products may not be eaten
↓
Should benefit also be prohibited, like hekdesh?
↓
No
"Sacred to you" — it shall be yours, i.e., benefit is permitted
Only Intentional
"He who profanes it shall be put to death" — only intentional desecration triggers the prohibition
DISPUTE Rav Aḥa vs. Ravina: One holds the prohibition is d'Oraita (Torah law, from R' Ḥiyya's derivation). The other holds it is d'Rabbanan — 'It is sacred' implies the product is not sacred by Torah law.

Stole & slaughtered on ShabbatSimultaneousShabbat slaughter❌ Exempt — no theft → no fine
Stole kid previously, slaughtered during burglaryBefore burglaryBurglar may be killed✅ Liable — theft preceded
Stole during burglary & slaughteredSimultaneousBurglar may be killed❌ Exempt — no theft → no fine
WHY BOTH CASES? Shabbat is severe (eternal prohibition) but might not exempt from fines. Burglary needs no forewarning (he intends to kill) but is transient. Each case is necessary.

Rav Pappa's Addition: The Borrowed Cow

Rav Pappa
A stolen cow slaughtered on Shabbat — liable for 4x/5x. A borrowed cow slaughtered on Shabbat — exempt from the fine.
Rav Aḥa b. Rava
What's the novelty? Rabba already taught this with the kid! Stolen = liable, simultaneous = exempt!
Rav Ashi
The novelty is the borrowed cow. You might think: since Rav Pappa said a borrower is liable for sustenance from the moment of pulling, he should be liable for accidents from that point too.
Rav Ashi
Rav Pappa teaches: liability for unavoidable accidents attaches only when they actually occur. If that's on Shabbat — he's exempt.
INSIGHT Borrower's obligation for sustenance begins at meshikha (pulling), but liability for accidents is not retroactive — it arises only at the moment of the incident.
