Ketubot 34 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to R' Yoḥanan HaSandlar, what is the status of food cooked intentionally on Shabbat?
A. May be eaten immediately
B. May be eaten after Shabbat by the one who cooked it
C. May be eaten after Shabbat by others but not by him
D. May never be eaten by anyone
Correct: D — R' Yoḥanan HaSandlar holds the strictest position: food produced through intentional Shabbat desecration may never be eaten — not by the violator, not by anyone else, ever.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the biblical source for prohibiting food produced by Shabbat desecration, according to R' Ḥiyya?
A. 'Remember the Sabbath day to keep it holy'
B. 'It is sacred to you; he who profanes it shall be put to death'
C. 'You shall not do any work on the seventh day'
D. 'Six days shall you work, and on the seventh day you shall rest'
Correct: B — R' Ḥiyya derives from Exodus 31:14 — 'It is sacred to you' — that just as sacred/consecrated items (hekdesh) may not be eaten, so too food produced through Shabbat desecration may not be eaten.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rabba, if a thief already had a stolen kid and THEN slaughtered it on Shabbat, what is his payment obligation?
A. Exempt from all payment due to capital liability
B. Pays only the principal theft amount
C. Pays 4x or 5x the principal
D. Pays double but not 4x/5x
Correct: C — Since the theft obligation was already in place BEFORE the Shabbat violation, the thief must pay the 4x/5x fine. The capital crime of Shabbat only exempts simultaneous monetary obligations, not pre-existing ones.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why did Rabba need to state the exemption rule for BOTH Shabbat and burglary?
A. Because they have different Torah sources
B. Because one is d'Oraita and the other d'Rabbanan
C. Because each has a unique severity the other lacks, so neither case alone would teach both
D. Because R' Meir and R' Shimon disagree on which applies
Correct: C — Shabbat's severity is that it's an 'eternal prohibition' (always punishable), while burglary's severity is that it needs no forewarning. From Shabbat alone you'd think the transient nature of burglary wouldn't exempt; from burglary alone you'd think Shabbat (requiring forewarning) wouldn't exempt.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
If a father dies leaving his heirs a borrowed cow and they slaughter it thinking it was their father's property, how much must they pay?
A. Full value of the cow
B. Nothing, since they acted in good faith
C. A reduced assessment — the value of the meat they consumed
D. Four or five times the value of the cow
Correct: C — Rava rules that since the heirs didn't borrow the cow themselves and acted in good faith, they pay only for the benefit they received — a reduced assessment of the meat's value — not the full damage to the owner. (Unless the father left property as a lien, in which case they pay full damages.)
