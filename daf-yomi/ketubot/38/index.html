Execution vs. Payment & The Betrothed Divorcée's Fine

Rami bar Ḥama
The phrase 'any ḥerem' teaches that even if one blinded an eye with one blow and killed with a different blow, he is exempt from paying damages.
Rava
That's already derived from the School of Ḥizkiyya: 'An eye for an eye' — not an eye and a life for an eye!
Rav Ashi
Rather, 'any ḥerem' teaches that even a fine (kenas) — a novel Torah decree — is not paid when one is executed.
Rabba
I disagree! Since a fine is a novel element, one pays the fine even though he is killed.
Core Principle Kim lei bederabba minei: When a person incurs both a death penalty and a monetary obligation for the same act, the greater punishment (death) exempts the lesser (payment).

What Does 'Any Ḥerem' Teach?

Phrase: 'Any ḥerem shall not be redeemed' (Lev. 27:29)
↓
R' Ḥananya ben Akavya
One who is taken to execution — his monetary valuation vow is not binding
First Tanna
Valuations of one being taken to execution are not binding (Rabba follows this)
Rav Ashi
Teaches that a fine (kenas) is also exempt when liable for execution
Key Dispute Rav Ashi and Rabba disagree on whether the novel nature of fines exempts them from the rule of kim lei bederabba minei.

School of Ḥizkiyya: Smiting Person vs. Animal

Smiting an Animal

  • No distinction: unwitting vs. intentional
  • No distinction: with intent vs. without intent
  • No distinction: downward vs. upward motion
  • Result: OBLIGATED to pay money

Smiting a Person

  • No distinction: unwitting vs. intentional
  • No distinction: with intent vs. without intent
  • No distinction: downward vs. upward motion
  • Result: EXEMPT from paying money
Juxtaposition Leviticus 24:21 places these cases side by side — just as animal damage always requires payment, person damage (when fatal) always exempts from payment.

Mishna: Fine for a Betrothed & Divorced Na'arah

R' Yosei HaGelili
A na'arah who was betrothed and divorced and then raped — she does not receive a fine. The verse says 'who was not betrothed' — excluding her.
R' Akiva
She does receive a fine! 'Who was not betrothed' means: if she was betrothed, the fine goes to her, not her father.
Gemara
But by that logic, a non-virgin and a grown woman should also receive their own fine — which no one holds!
Gemara
R' Akiva uses 'who was not betrothed' for a different purpose: a gezeira shava (verbal analogy) between rape and seduction.
Dispute R' Yosei HaGelili reads the verse as an exclusion; R' Akiva reads it as establishing a verbal analogy and changing the recipient.
