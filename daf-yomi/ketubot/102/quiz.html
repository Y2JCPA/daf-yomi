QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Giddel, what makes verbal marriage financial agreements legally binding?
A. The parents perform a formal act of acquisition (kinyan)
B. The son and daughter perform the betrothal (kiddushin)
C. The agreements are written down in a contract
D. Witnesses observe the verbal negotiation
Correct: B — Rav Giddel teaches that once the couple actually performs the betrothal, the parents' prior verbal commitments become binding — these are 'things acquired through words alone' (דברים הנקנים באמירה).
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Ulla say a firstborn son is not redeemed even after the father pays 5 sela to the Kohen he designated in writing?
A. The father must pay more than 5 sela when using a written document
B. A rabbinic decree lest people think they can redeem with documents instead of money
C. The Kohen must verbally accept the redemption for it to take effect
D. Written designations of a specific Kohen are invalid by Torah law
Correct: B — Ulla explains this is a rabbinic decree (gezeira): if redemption were valid through a written commitment, people might think one could redeem a firstborn by giving the Kohen a debt document rather than actual money, which the Torah requires.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
In Ben Nannas's marketplace parable, why is the bystander who says 'Leave him, I'll pay' exempt from paying?
A. Because the bystander did not make his promise in front of witnesses
B. Because the creditor did not lend the money in reliance on the bystander's guarantee
C. Because verbal promises are never legally binding
D. Because the bystander was coerced into making the promise
Correct: B — Ben Nannas argues that since the original loan was not made in reliance on the bystander's promise (it came after the fact), there is no binding obligation — just as a guarantor who signs after the contract was not relied upon when the loan was made.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does Rava ultimately conclude that Rav Giddel's rule applies even when the bride is a bogeret (grown woman)?
A. Because a bogeret's father still legally controls her betrothal money
B. Because the father of the groom also has no direct monetary benefit, yet his commitment is still binding
C. Because the Rabbis extended the rule to all women regardless of age
D. Because a bogeret can voluntarily transfer her betrothal rights to her father
Correct: B — Rava reasons that if the rule only applied when a parent has direct monetary benefit, the father of the groom would also be exempt (since he receives nothing). Rather, the mutual benefit of the marriage itself makes both parents' commitments binding.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rav Ashi, what does the Mishna mean when it says 'the perspicacious ones would write' their marriage stipulations?
A. They would hire a scribe to draft formal contracts
B. They would verbally state (not literally write) their conditions explicitly
C. They would write the stipulations before the betrothal took place
D. They would record the stipulations in the ketubah itself
Correct: B — Rav Ashi interprets 'write' (כותבין) as 'say' (אומרין), citing R' Ḥiyya's parallel explanation of Mishna 83a. Verbal marriage agreements cannot actually be written into formal enforceable contracts.
