When Does a Fine Become a Regular Monetary Obligation?

Mishna
Payments for rape/seduction — humiliation, degradation, and fine — belong to the father.
Gemara
Why state this? It's obvious! If the money belonged to her, a seducer wouldn't pay — she consented!
Gemara
The Mishna addresses when she stood for judgment before the father died — do the sons inherit or does she collect?
R' Shimon
If she didn't collect before the father died, the payments belong to her, not the brothers.
Rabbis
Once the father stood in judgment, the payment is his monetary property — his sons inherit it.
Core Question Does a court verdict transform a knas (fine) into mamon (monetary obligation) so it can be inherited?

Father's Rights: What Belongs to Him?

Belongs to Father

  • Compensation for humiliation (boshet)
  • Compensation for degradation (pegam)
  • Torah-mandated fine (knas)
  • Pain compensation (for rapist)
  • Her earnings & found objects

Disputed After Father Dies

  • R' Shimon: Goes to the daughter
  • Rabbis: Sons inherit from father
  • Key factor: Did he stand trial?
  • Key factor: Did he collect before death?
  • Earnings/lost items → brothers (already his)
Distinction Earnings and found objects are already considered the father's property, so brothers inherit them even if uncollected. Fines are different.

Abaye's Dilemma: Oath Offering After Trial

Abaye
If a father says 'You raped my daughter, I took you to court, you were found liable' and the accused denies it under oath then admits — according to R' Shimon, what happens?
Abaye
Since he stood trial, is it now mamon — making him liable for a false oath offering? Or is it still a knas in essence?
Rabba
Since he already stood trial, it is considered a regular monetary payment — he is liable for the oath offering.
Abaye
But a baraita of R' Shimon lists rape, seduction, and similar cases as excluded from the oath offering — even after trial!
R' Shimon's Principle A person does not pay a knas on his own admission (modeh b'knas patur). Does a trial change this?

R' Shimon's Baraita: The Verse-Based Exclusion

Leviticus 5:21–22 lists oath liability for: deposits, pledges, robbery, oppression, lost items
↓
What do these have in common?
↓
All are monetary obligations equal in value to the actual loss caused
↓
Included
Regular monetary debts (mamon)
Excluded
Fines (knas): rapist, seducer, double payment, 4x/5x payment
Key Verse "V'khichesh ba'amito b'fikadon" — 'Deal falsely with his neighbor in a matter of a deposit' — limits the oath offering to claims that were originally monetary.

Abaye's PointBoth clauses deal with post-trialContradiction for Rabba!R' Shimon should exempt both

Abaye's Logic The first clause mentions double payment — which nobody pays on admission. So it must be discussing a post-trial scenario. This proves the latter clause is also post-trial!

Rabba's Refined Position

🏛️ A fine (knas) is imposed by the court after trial
↓
💰 After the verdict, the fine becomes a regular monetary obligation (mamon)
↓
📜 BUT — this does NOT create liability for a false oath offering (per the verse's limitation)
↓
👨‍👦‍👦 It DOES mean the payment can be bequeathed to one's sons as inheritance
↓
❓ But then R' Shimon in the Mishna says the daughter gets the money, not the sons — contradiction!
Rabba's Concession Rabba concedes to Abaye: post-trial fines are not subject to the oath offering. But he maintains they are inheritable — which creates a new problem with R' Shimon's Mishna.

📋 Ketubot 42 — Summary Map

Mishna: Rape/seduction payments belong to the father
↓
Gemara asks: What happens if father dies after trial but before collection?
↓
Rabbis: Sons inherit — post-trial it's mamon
R' Shimon: Daughter collects — still a knas
↓
Rabba vs. Abaye: Does post-trial status create oath offering liability? Rabba concedes it does not (per the verse), but maintains fines are inheritable
↓
Rav Yosef's Resolution: 'Shall give the father' — Torah conditions the right on actual giving. Father who dies before collection never acquired it. Daughter takes his place.
