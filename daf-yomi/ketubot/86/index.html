Ketubot 86: Debts, Documents & Oaths

Selling a Promissory Note
Can the original creditor forgive the debt?
↓
Shmuel
Yes — the debt is forgiven. The obligation is non-transferable.
Purchaser's Remedy
Pay the debtor to write a new note in the purchaser's name.
↓
If creditor forgives anyway → Is this garme (indirect damage)?
↓
Judges Garme
Creditor pays full value of the document
Doesn't Judge Garme
Creditor pays only value of paper
Key Theme This daf explores the enforceability of transferred debts, priority among creditors, and the limits of judicial authority.

Rav Naḥman's Regret: Judge or Advisor?

Rav Naḥman
Daughter should forgive her mother's ketubah debt to her father, then inherit the full amount when he dies — defeating the purchasers.
Daughter
Follows advice and forgives the ketubah obligation.
Rav Naḥman
"We have made ourselves like advisors of judges" — we acted as lawyers, not impartial judges.
Gemara
Initially: "Do not hide from your own flesh" (Isaiah 58:7) — help relatives. Ultimately: An important person must avoid any appearance of favoritism.
Judicial Ethics A judge must maintain impartiality even when halakha might permit helping a relative. The higher one's stature, the greater the standard.

Selling a Promissory Note: Shmuel's Rule

Creditor sells a promissory note to a purchaser
↓
Original creditor then forgives the debtor's obligation
↓
Shmuel: The debt is forgiven!
Even an heir can forgive the debt.
↓
What remedy does the purchaser have?
↓
Rav Huna b. R' Yehoshua
Jangle dinars (mekarkesh) — pay the debtor to write a new note in your name
If Forgiven Already
Seek compensation from the creditor for indirect damage (garme)
Practical Tip A savvy purchaser protects himself by having the debtor write a fresh promissory note directly to him, bypassing the original creditor entirely.

Creditor vs. Wife: Who Gets What?

Creditor

  • Gave the debtor money
  • Fitting to receive money in return
  • If only one plot of land — creditor gets it
  • Priority to encourage future lending

Wife (Ketubah)

  • Did not give money — relied on land lien
  • Receives payment in land
  • If only one plot — she does not receive it
  • Reason: Women want marriage more than the ketubah payment
Ameimar / Rav Ḥama Each party is settled according to their law (zeh k'dino v'zeh k'dinah). The creditor gets cash; the wife gets land. When assets are limited, the creditor takes priority.

PositionRulingReasoning

Core Question Are the tziddei reshut ha-rabbim (sides of the public domain) treated like the public domain itself, or like an ownerless field (agam)?

Compelling a Debtor: Repayment as Mitzva

💰 A debtor owes money and has land but says: "Pay yourself from the land"
↓
📖 Rav Pappa: Repaying a creditor is a mitzva (positive commandment)
↓
❓ Rav Kahana challenges: What if debtor says "I don't want to do the mitzva"?
↓
📜 Baraita: For positive mitzvot, the court strikes him until he complies
↓
⚖️ Just as one is forced to sit in a sukkah or take a lulav — one is forced to repay
↓
✅ Conclusion: The court can compel debt repayment by force
Rav Pappa → Rav Kahana A debtor cannot escape payment by claiming he doesn't wish to perform the mitzva. The court compels performance of positive mitzvot — including debt repayment.
