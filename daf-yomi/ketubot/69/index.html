Daughters' Rights to Estate Property & Third-Party Funds

Rav
Sends an ambiguous question to Rebbi: Can daughters collect dowries from mortgaged property?
R' Yehuda HaNasi
Whether sold or pledged, the court appropriates liened property for dowry but not for sustenance.
R' Yochanan
Disagrees: The court does not appropriate liened or sold property for either dowry or sustenance.
Rava
Final halakha: Collect from inheritors' real estate only — not movable property — for sustenance, ketuba, or dowry.
Core Theme This daf examines the limits of daughters' claims on estate property and the authority of women over funds designated for their benefit.

Rav's Strategic Question to Rebbi

Rav wants to know about both sold and pledged property
↓
If he writes 'sold' and Rebbi says yes → pledged is obvious.
But if Rebbi says no → pledged remains a dilemma.
↓
If he writes 'pledged' and Rebbi says no → sold is obvious.
But if Rebbi says yes → sold remains a dilemma.
↓
✅ Solution: Write 'mortgaged' — ambiguous enough to cover both cases and guarantee a complete answer
Insight Rav deliberately chose an ambiguous term (שעבדו) so that Rebbi's single answer would resolve both possible scenarios at once.

Can Daughters Collect Dowry from Liened Property?

R' Yehuda HaNasi
Whether brothers sold or pledged the property, the court appropriates it for dowry (פרנסה).
R' Yehuda HaNasi
But the court does not appropriate liened property for sustenance (מזונות).
R' Yochanan
The court does not appropriate liened or sold property for either dowry or sustenance.
R' Chanina
Challenges R' Yochanan: The Sages said the court does appropriate liened property for dowry — how can you deny even unliened property?
Key Distinction Dowry (פרנסה) = one-tenth of estate for marriage. Sustenance (מזונות) = ongoing support. Rebbi distinguishes; R' Yochanan does not.

Two Daughters & a Son Who Dies

Father dies leaving two daughters and one son
↓
First daughter collects her 1/10 dowry from the estate
↓
Before second daughter collects, the son dies
↓
R' Yochanan
Second daughter forfeits her separate 1/10 dowry. The sisters divide everything equally.
R' Chanina
Second daughter should still receive her 1/10 dowry — her brother's death shouldn't diminish her rights.
Abundance Logic R' Yochanan's reasoning: since the second daughter now inherits half the estate, there is 'abundance in the house' — she doesn't need the separate dowry.

R' Meir vs. R' Yosei: Daughter's Authority Over Transferred Funds

Rabbi Meir

  • From betrothal until marriage: agent executes his mission — daughter has no authority
  • From marriage onward: adult daughter may redirect the money to her husband
  • A minor girl has no authority at any stage

Rabbi Yosei

  • An adult woman has authority whether from betrothal or marriage
  • If it were a field, she could sell it — so she controls the money too
  • A minor girl has no authority at any stage
Practical Difference They agree about minors and married adults. The dispute is about an adult woman during the betrothal period — before the marriage is completed.
