When Can a Woman Sell Her Inherited Property?

Woman inherits property
↓
Before Betrothal
All agree: She may sell freely
After Betrothal
Dispute: Beit Shammai allow sale; Beit Hillel forbid ab initio (valid after the fact)
After Marriage
All agree: Husband repossesses from buyers
KEY PRINCIPLE A woman's ability to sell inherited property diminishes as her marital bond strengthens — from full autonomy before betrothal to no effective sale after marriage.

Why No Dispute Before Betrothal?

Gemara
Why do Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel agree when she inherited before betrothal but disagree after?
School of R' Yannai
Before betrothal — the property came when she had full rights. After betrothal — perhaps he now has rights to it.
Gemara
But if he has rights, why is the sale valid after the fact?
School of R' Yannai
It's a safek (uncertainty) — maybe she has rights, maybe he does. So l'chatchilah she can't sell, but b'dieved it stands.
PRINCIPLE The uncertainty of ownership during betrothal (אירוסין) creates a split ruling: prohibited initially, but valid after the fact.

The Sages Challenge Rabban Gamliel

Sages (per R' Yehuda)
Since he acquired the woman through betrothal, shouldn't he also acquire her property? The sale should be void!
Rabban Gamliel
With new property (inherited after marriage) we are already ashamed of our ruling — and you want to burden us with the old property too?!
Gemara
Was R' Yehuda's question about selling ab initio (Beit Shammai) or after the fact (Beit Hillel)?
Baraita
They argued the sale should be void — this proves R' Yehuda asked about the after-the-fact validity.
R' GAMLIEL'S LOGIC Rabban Gamliel resists expanding the husband's property rights: if we're uncomfortable restricting her new property, we certainly shouldn't restrict her old property.

R' Ḥanina ben Akavya's Version vs. R' Yehuda's Version

R' Yehuda's Version

  • Sages argue: betrothed = married → sale void
  • Rabban Gamliel: 'Ashamed of new, don't burden with old'
  • Applies to property inherited after betrothal
  • After the fact — sale is valid

R' Ḥanina ben Akavya's Version

  • Rabban Gamliel's real argument: husband has fewer rights over betrothed wife
  • No right to her finds, earnings, or vow nullification
  • Extends to property inherited before marriage, sold after marriage
  • She may sell even ab initio
DISTINCTION R' Ḥanina's version gives a substantive reason (fewer marital rights during betrothal), while R' Yehuda's version records only Rabban Gamliel's rhetorical pushback.

Rav & Shmuel's Final Ruling

Property inherited at ANY time — before betrothal or after
↓
Woman marries and then sells or gifts the property
↓
Husband may repossess from the buyers
↓
Source: Our Rabbis voted — a later authoritative ruling overriding earlier opinions
HALAKHIC CONCLUSION Rav and Shmuel follow 'Our Rabbis' (רבותינו חזרו ונמנו) who voted that after marriage, the husband always repossesses — disagreeing with both R' Yehuda and R' Ḥanina ben Akavya.
