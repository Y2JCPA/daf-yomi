Ketubot 99: Agency, Sales & the Widow's Ketubah

Widow sells estate property to collect her ketubah
↓
Did she reduce the price of the land she sold?
Her Own Portion
Sale is valid — she bears the loss herself
Orphans' Property
Sale is void — she cannot give away orphans' assets
↓
Broader question: When does an agent who deviates still act within authority?
Sells More
Adding to employer's words — first portion valid
Sells Less
Dispute — benefit or unwanted extra documents?
KEY PRINCIPLE An agent who errs is judged more strictly than an owner who errs — the owner can say: I sent you for my benefit, not my detriment.

The Widow's Reduced-Price Sales

Widow's ketubah = 400 dinars
↓
She sells property in installments of 100 dinars each
↓
Last sale: property worth 101 dinars sold for only 100
Last Sale
Void — she already collected her full ketubah; the extra dinar belongs to the orphans
Earlier Sales
Valid — sold at correct price from her own entitlement
MISHNA'S TEACHING Both clauses deal with reduced prices — but only reductions at the orphans' expense invalidate the sale.

Why Teach Both Clauses of the Mishna?

Gemara
The first clause already teaches that a widow who sold property worth 200 for 100 — her sale is valid. Why repeat in the last clause?
Answer
You might think: when she still has remaining claims, we should void even the first sale to prevent confusion with the invalid last sale.
Gemara
But in the first clause, she leaves the estate entirely — no further dealings with orphans' property!
Answer
Exactly — the last clause teaches: even with multiple partial sales, we don't decree against earlier valid sales because of a later void one.
PRINCIPLE We do not retroactively invalidate valid transactions merely because a later related transaction was void.

Agent Sells More vs. Less Than Instructed

Sells MORE (½ kor → 1 kor)

  • Agent was told to sell ½ kor, sold a full kor
  • He added to employer's words
  • First ½ kor sale is valid
  • No dispute — clearly extending instructions

Sells LESS (1 kor → ½ kor)

  • Agent was told to sell 1 kor, sold only ½ kor
  • Agent argues: 'I did you a favor' — you can decide if you need more money
  • Employer argues: 'More documents = bad reputation' as someone with many mortgages
  • Status: Unresolved dilemma
CORE TENSION Is selling less a benefit (preserving options) or a detriment (creating extra paperwork and reputational harm)?

Me'ila Case: The Gold Dinar, Robe & Cloak

ElementDetail
Employer's instructionBuy me a robe with this gold dinar (= 6 sela)
Agent's actionBought robe for 3 sela + cloak for 3 sela
DiscoveryThe gold dinar was consecrated property (hekdesh)
Who is guilty?Both employer and agent committed me'ila
Gemara's resolutionRobe was worth 6 sela but bought at a discount for 3 — agent fulfilled mission exactly
R' Yehuda's objectionEmployer can say: 'I wanted a large robe, you brought a small bad one'
Gemara's answerR' Yehuda refers to a case where the robe was truly worth only 3 sela
ME'ILA PRINCIPLE If the agent fulfilled the employer's wishes, the employer bears guilt for me'ila; the agent is guilty for spending consecrated money on the unauthorized cloak.

'Sell to One Person' — Does It Exclude Two?

Rav Huna
When he said 'sell to one', he meant: to one and not to two. The agent who sold to two has disregarded instructions.
Rav Ḥisda & Rabba b. Rav Huna
'To one' means even to two, even to a hundred! He wasn't specifying an exclusive number.
Rav Naḥman
I agree — 'to one' means even to one hundred. The number was not meant as a limitation.
Rav Ḥisda & Rabba
Even if the agent erred in the price — is the sale still valid as agency?
Rav Naḥman
No! I do not say this where the agent erred. An agent's error is different from an owner's error.
RULING Rav Naḥman: 'Sell to one' includes selling to multiple buyers — unless the agent made an error in pricing, which voids the agency.
