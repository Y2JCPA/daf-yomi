QUESTION 1 OF 5
When one presumption (chazakah) is opposed by two others, what does the Gemara rule?
A. The single presumption always wins because it is more specific
B. The single presumption cannot override the two opposing ones
C. We follow whichever presumption was established first in time
D. The court splits the disputed amount equally
Correct: B — The Gemara states explicitly that one presumption is not decisive when opposed by two. In our case, the wife's bodily presumption alone cannot overcome both the presumption of the husband's money and the presumption that people don't accept blemishes.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rav Ashi, why does the mishna distinguish between blemishes found before and after marriage?
A. Before marriage the blemishes are more severe; after marriage they are less relevant
B. Before marriage the ketubah goes to the father (claiming for another), while after marriage the wife claims for herself
C. Before marriage there is no ketubah obligation at all
D. The physical examination rules differ depending on the timing
Correct: B — Rav Ashi explains that the body's presumptive status can support one's own monetary claim but not a claim on behalf of another person. Before marriage, the ketubah would go to the father, so the body's chazakah doesn't help him. After marriage, the wife claims for herself, so her chazakah supports her claim.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
In the case of the extra toe (etzba yeteirah), what proof could the father bring?
A. Medical testimony that toes can grow after birth
B. Proof that the husband saw the extra toe before betrothal and was appeased
C. Witnesses that the daughter was born without an extra toe
D. Evidence that the blemish appeared only during the betrothal period
Correct: B — Since an extra toe is clearly congenital and cannot have developed after betrothal, the only relevant proof is that the husband was already aware of the blemish before betrothal and accepted it, meaning the betrothal was not made in error.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rami bar Yeḥezkel correct the version of Shmuel's ruling reported by Rav Yehuda?
A. The burden of proof is always on the seller in any transaction
B. The burden of proof rests on whichever party is wealthier
C. The burden of proof rests on the one in whose domain the uncertainty arose
D. The burden of proof is always on the one currently holding the money
Correct: C — Rami bar Yeḥezkel said to disregard his brother Rav Yehuda's version. Shmuel's actual rule was: in whose domain the uncertainty arose, upon that person rests the burden of proof. This harmonizes all the cases — the bride in her father's or husband's house, the cow-donkey exchange, and the needle in the stomach.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In the needle-in-the-stomach case, what does a scab forming over the wound indicate?
A. The animal is definitely kosher
B. The perforation happened at least three days before slaughter
C. The needle entered after the animal was slaughtered
D. The wound has healed and the animal is no longer tereifah
Correct: B — The baraita teaches that if a scab appeared over the wound, it is certain the perforation occurred at least three days before slaughter. This has practical significance for determining when the animal became a tereifah and whether a sale to a butcher can be voided as a mistaken transaction.
