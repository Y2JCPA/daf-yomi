Burden of Proof: Who Must Bring Evidence?

Dispute arises about blemishes, transactions, or animal status
↓
Identify the competing presumptions (chazakot)
↓
One chazakah vs. two
The single presumption cannot override two
Equal standing
Burden falls on the claimant or on the one in whose domain the uncertainty arose
Core Principle The burden of proof follows the uncertainty — whoever holds the disputed item or in whose domain the doubt arose must bring evidence.

One Presumption vs. Two: The Blemished Bride

Question
The wife claims her body's presumptive status (chezkat ha-guf) — she was unblemished before betrothal. Can one chazakah stand against two?
Gemara
We do not say one presumption is decisive when opposed by two others.
Objection
If the husband proves blemishes existed before betrothal, the wife can still claim: a person does not drink from a cup without examining it — he must have seen and accepted!
Gemara
On the contrary — there is a presumption that a person does not become appeased with regard to blemishes. The husband keeps his money.
Key Concept Two presumptions — (1) the money stays with its holder and (2) people don't accept blemishes — override the single presumption of the body's prior status.

Rav Ashi's Resolution: Father's Claim vs. Wife's Claim

First Clause: Father's House

  • Blemishes discovered before marriage
  • Ketubah payment would go to the father
  • Like saying: My father has 100 dinars in your possession
  • The body's presumption does not help claim money for another person
  • Father must bring proof

Latter Clause: Husband's House

  • Blemishes discovered after marriage
  • Ketubah payment belongs to the wife herself
  • Like saying: I have 100 dinars in your possession
  • Her body's presumptive status supports her own claim
  • Husband must bring proof
Rav Ashi's Principle The presumptive status of one's body (chezkat ha-guf) can support a claim only for oneself, not on behalf of another — even one's own father.

The Extra Toe Objection (Rav Aḥa b. Rav Avya)

R' Meir concedes: For blemishes that come from the father's house, the father must bring proof — even after marriage
↓
But according to Rav Ashi, she is married — shouldn't her own body's presumption support her claim?
↓
Answer: We're dealing with an extra toe (etzba yeteirah) — clearly a birth defect
↓
What proof could the father bring about a congenital condition?
↓
Proof that the husband saw the toe before betrothal and was appeased
Resolution When a blemish is clearly congenital (like an extra toe), the only relevant proof is that the husband knowingly accepted it before the betrothal.

Shmuel's Ruling: The Cow-Donkey Exchange

🐄 Owner of cow and 🫏 owner of donkey agree to exchange animals (chalifin)
↓
🤝 Donkey owner pulls the cow — this should acquire the donkey for the cow's owner
↓
💀 Before the cow's owner can pull the donkey, the donkey dies
↓
🐄 Cow owner claims: The donkey died before the pull — no transaction!
↓
📜 Shmuel rules: The donkey's owner must bring proof the donkey was alive at the time of the pull
↓
⚖️ Without proof, the cow owner retains his animal
Shmuel's Source Shmuel derived this halakhah from the mishna's ruling about a bride — the one seeking to maintain a claim must bring supporting evidence.
