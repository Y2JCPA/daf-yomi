Ketubot 23: Conflicting Testimony & Captive Women

Core Question: When witnesses conflict about a woman's status, what determines if she must leave her husband?
↓
Amud A
Resolving contradictions between betrothal and divorce testimony clauses
Amud B
Two captive women testifying about each other's purity — when is each believed?
Key Theme The Gemara explores how credibility, presumptive status, and self-interest interact when testimony conflicts.
3 / 9

Why Different Rulings for Betrothal vs. Divorce?

Gemara
In the betrothal clause, if she married another she need not leave. In the divorce clause, she must leave. Why the difference?
Abaye
Each testimony is from one witness. For betrothal — both agree she was unmarried. The one saying 'betrothed' is a single witness against two, so she may stay.
Abaye
For divorce — both agree she was married. The one saying 'divorced' is a single witness, so she remains presumed married and must leave.
Rav Ashi
Actually there are two witnesses on each side. Reverse the rulings: betrothal — must leave; divorce — need not leave.
Key Principle עד אחד בדבר ערוה — One witness has no standing in matters of marital status where two witnesses exist.

Rav Ashi: 'We Did Not See' Is Not Proof

Two witnesses: 'We saw her betrothed'
Two witnesses: 'We did NOT see her betrothed'
↓
Negative testimony ('we didn't see') has no evidentiary value — לא ראינו אינה ראיה
↓
But what if they all live in one courtyard?
↓
Betrothal
People are prone to betroth in private — neighbors may not know
Divorce
People also divorce in private — despite the assumption that marital strife is public
Chiddush Both betrothal (קידושין) and divorce (גירושין) can be performed privately, even among neighbors.

R' Oshaya vs. Rabba bar Avin: 'Need Not Leave'

R' Oshaya

  • Teaches 'need not leave' regarding the first clause — the divorcée
  • Holds Rav Hamnuna's presumption: a woman is not insolent before her husband
  • All the more so applies to the captive woman (Sages were lenient)

Rabba bar Avin

  • Teaches 'need not leave' only regarding the latter clause — the captive woman
  • Captive status involves mere suspicion/uncertainty, so Sages are lenient
  • Does NOT extend leniency to the divorcée claim
Underlying Dispute Do they disagree about Rav Hamnuna's presumption (חזקת רב המנונא) — that a woman would not be so brazen as to lie in her husband's presence?

Mishna & Tosefta: Captive Woman's Self-Testimony

She ClaimsAbout HerselfAbout Her Counterpart
'I am tainted, she is pure'✅ Believed (self-incrimination)✅ Believed
'I am pure, she is tainted'❌ Not believed❌ Not believed
'We are both tainted'✅ Believed (self-incrimination)❌ Not believed
'We are both pure'❌ Not believed about self✅ Believed about counterpart
Mishna Rule Each captive woman cannot testify for herself (when witnesses establish captivity), but can testify for the other.

Are All Cases With or Without Witnesses?

Gemara
The Tosefta's four cases seem inconsistent — some require witnesses of captivity to exist, others require no witnesses. How?
Abaye
The first and last clauses deal with cases where witnesses exist. The middle clause deals with no witnesses (so 'the mouth that prohibited is the mouth that permitted' applies).
Rav Pappa
All cases have witnesses of captivity, plus one witness who contradicts her claim. Her self-incrimination always works; her counterpart is freed by whichever source — her testimony or the witness.
Abaye vs. Rav Pappa Abaye reads the Tosefta as mixed scenarios; Rav Pappa creates a unified framework using a contradicting single witness.
