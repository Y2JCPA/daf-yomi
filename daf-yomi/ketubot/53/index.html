Ketubot 53 — Dowry Pressure, Ketubah Sales & Daughters' Sustenance

DAF OVERVIEW This daf explores three major areas: (1) The dramatic story of Yehuda bar Mareimar's silent visit that pressures a father into giving away everything as dowry; (2) Whether a wife who sells or forgoes her ketubah retains the ketubot bnin dikhrin (male children's inheritance clause); and (3) A series of debates and dilemmas about how long daughters receive sustenance from their father's estate — until marriage, betrothal, or physical maturity.

The Silent Visit: Pressuring a Father's Dowry

Rav Pappa
Come inside — it's a Rabbinic ordinance that a father provide a dowry for his daughter, equal to a son's inheritance share.
Yehuda bar Mareimar
That applies only when given willingly — should we force him too?
Rav Pappa
I said enter, not force him!
Yehuda bar Mareimar
My very entrance is an act of force — he will increase the dowry in my honor.
Narrator
Rav Pappa forced Yehuda to enter. He sat silent. Abba of Sura wrote all he had as dowry — then said: 'I've left nothing for myself!'
Yehuda bar Mareimar
What you wrote is not amenable to me. But don't retract — a man must keep his word.
ETHICAL LESSON Even passive presence can constitute undue pressure. Yehuda bar Mareimar tried to avoid causing Abba of Sura to give more than he intended.

Selling or Forgoing a Ketubah: Ketubot Bnin Dikhrin

Does a wife who sells/forgoes her ketubah retain the male children's inheritance clause (ketubot bnin dikhrin)?
↓
Sells to Others
✅ Retains the clause
Reason: The money forced her — she needed cash but didn't intend to renounce all rights
Forgoes to Husband
❌ Loses the clause
Reason: She has forgiven everything and intends no further claims
Sells to Husband
❓ Dilemma
Like selling to others? Or like forgoing?
↓
Rava resolves: Selling to husband = selling to others → she retains ketubot bnin dikhrin, as financial pressure is assumed
KEY PRINCIPLE When a woman sells her ketubah, we assume financial duress — she did not intend to surrender her children's future inheritance rights.

Daughters' Sustenance: Rav vs. Levi

Rav

  • Daughters sustained until married (nisuʾin)
  • Even a grown woman (bogeret) receives sustenance if unmarried? No — agreed she loses it at bogeret
  • Dispute is about a betrothed (arusah) minor
  • Betrothal = she has left father's domain → no more sustenance

Levi

  • Daughters sustained until becoming grown women (bogeret)
  • Even married daughters get sustenance until bogeret? No — agreed marriage ends it
  • Dispute is about a betrothed (arusah) minor
  • Betrothal alone doesn't end it — she's still under father's jurisdiction until bogeret
PARALLEL This dispute mirrors Tanna Kamma vs. R' Elazar: sustenance ends at betrothal (T"K) or at becoming a grown woman (R' Elazar).

Does a Betrothed Orphan Get Sustenance from Brothers?

Rav Ḥisda
Did you hear from Rav Yehuda whether a betrothed orphan receives sustenance from the brothers?
Rav Yosef
I didn't hear directly, but logically she does not — her husband wouldn't want her demeaned by asking brothers for food.
Rav Ḥisda
Logically she should have sustenance — he's not sure he'll marry her, so he won't spend his own money for nothing.
Narrator
Alternate version: Rav Yosef and Rav Ḥisda swap positions — each uses the other's reasoning!
CORE TENSION Is a betrothed husband responsible enough to sustain her (making estate support unnecessary), or too uncertain of the marriage to pay (making estate support essential)?
