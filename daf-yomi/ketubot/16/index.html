Ketubot 16 — Miggo, Marriage Publicity & Ketubah Collection

DAF OVERVIEW Amud A explores the principle of peh she'asar hu hapeh she'hitir (the mouth that prohibited is the mouth that permitted) as a form of miggo, and whether Rabban Gamliel's leniency applies when both parties have certain claims. Amud B examines the role of publicity in virgin marriages and tackles the practical problem: if a woman collects her ketubah via witnesses, how do we prevent double collection?

Peh She'asar — The Mouth That Prohibited Is the Mouth That Permitted

A man holds a field and admits: "This belonged to your father"
↓
He then claims: "I purchased it from him"
↓
No witnesses it was the father's
Believed — his own admission created the problem; without it, no one would have known
Witnesses exist that it was the father's
Not believed — the miggo is gone since we already know the facts independently
KEY PRINCIPLE This is the Mishnaic source for miggo: since he could have stayed silent and kept the field, his less advantageous admission lends credibility to his purchase claim.

Does Rabban Gamliel's Rule Apply When Both Claims Are Certain?

Gemara (Question)
The Mishna says she needs hinnuma witnesses — implying without them the husband is believed. Isn't this against Rabban Gamliel, who says the woman is always believed?
Gemara (Answer)
Even Rabban Gamliel only believed the woman where her claim is certain and his is uncertain. Here, both claims are certain — he insists he married a widow.
Gemara (Follow-up)
But since most women marry as virgins, shouldn't his certain claim really be treated as uncertain — like a minority position against a majority?
Gemara (Resolution)
That's precisely why the Mishna must teach this case — even with the majority supporting her, Rabban Gamliel concedes that when the groom is certain, she needs witnesses.
DISTINCTION Certain vs. Certain = neither party believed without proof, even where a statistical majority supports one side.

Marriage Publicity & the Role of Majority

Initial Assumption

  • Most women married as virgins have public celebrations
  • If this is a presumption (chazakah), then witnesses contradicting it would be false witnesses
  • Problem: If witnesses are automatically suspect, testimony about hinnuma is meaningless

Ravina's Resolution

  • It's a majority (rov), not a presumption
  • Most virgin marriages generate publicity — but not all
  • This woman's marriage lacked publicity, so the majority is undermined
  • Witnesses that she wore a hinnuma restore her virgin status
RAVINA The distinction between chazakah (presumption) and rov (majority) determines whether contradictory witnesses can be valid at all.

Preventing Double Collection of the Ketubah

Gemara (Problem)
If she collects 200 zuz via hinnuma witnesses in one court, she could then produce her actual ketubah document in another court and collect again!
R' Abbahu
This proves we write a receipt (kotvin shovair) — the husband keeps proof she already collected.
Rav Pappa
No — the Mishna deals with a place where no ketubah document is written. The obligation exists by custom alone, so there's nothing to produce twice.
PRACTICAL HALAKHA The dispute between R' Abbahu and Rav Pappa has major implications for whether courts must issue receipts in all monetary matters.
