QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why did the Sages institute that a virgin marries specifically on Wednesday rather than Sunday?
A. Courts only sit on Wednesday
B. So the groom has 3 days to prepare the feast and courts sit Thursday for claims
C. Wednesday is considered an auspicious day for marriage
D. So the couple can celebrate through Shabbat
Correct: B — The Gemara gives two reasons: (1) the Sages were assiduous (shakdu) for Jewish women, wanting the groom to have 3 days of feast preparation, and (2) Thursday courts are available for virginity claims. Sunday would satisfy the court requirement but not the feast preparation.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What was Rav Yosef's objection to Shmuel's original explanation?
A. Shmuel cited an incorrect mishna
B. Shmuel explained a mishna with an explicit reason using a mishna whose reason is not explicit
C. Shmuel contradicted a known halakha
D. Shmuel failed to account for the widow's wedding day
Correct: B — Rav Yosef exclaimed 'Lord of Abraham!' because Shmuel tried to explain our mishna (whose reason — court access — is stated explicitly) by referencing the mishna on 57a (whose reason is NOT explicit). This added nothing to our understanding.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What does the metaphor 'sadeihu hitzifa' (his field was inundated) mean in this context?
A. The groom's actual property was damaged by flooding
B. The bride's illness is like a natural disaster that befell the groom — his bad luck, not her fault
C. The wedding feast was ruined by rain
D. The ketuba document was water-damaged
Correct: B — When the bride falls ill and can't marry on time, she argues 'sadeihu hitzifa' — her illness is comparable to a natural disaster striking HIS field. It's his misfortune, not something she caused, so he should still provide her sustenance.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rav Ashi explain the mishna's use of passive language ('they were not married') rather than active ('the grooms didn't marry them')?
A. It proves the brides caused the delay
B. It proves unavoidable circumstances occurred
C. It's merely stylistic — matching the first clause's focus on the bride — and no inference should be drawn
D. It refers to both grooms and brides equally
Correct: C — Rav Ashi argues the passive form is purely stylistic: since the first clause of the mishna speaks of 'the virgin' (the bride), the latter clause maintains that framing. The mishna actually refers to cases where grooms voluntarily delay, and in cases of ones, she does NOT eat from his food.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rava, how do unavoidable circumstances (ones) affect a conditional bill of divorce differently than sustenance obligations?
A. Ones voids both the get and sustenance obligations equally
B. Ones exempts the groom from sustenance but does NOT prevent a conditional get from taking effect
C. Ones prevents the get from taking effect but does not affect sustenance
D. Ones has no effect on either — both obligations stand regardless
Correct: B — Rava distinguishes: for sustenance, ones may exempt the groom (he couldn't help the delay). But for gittin, ones has NO legal standing — if a man says 'this is your get if I don't return in 12 months' and he can't return due to illness, the get still takes effect. The condition was fulfilled regardless of why.
