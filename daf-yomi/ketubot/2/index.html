Ketubot 2: When Does a Virgin Marry?

Mishna: A virgin (betula) marries on Wednesday; a widow on Thursday
↓
Why Wednesday? Courts sit Monday & Thursday — if there's a virginity claim, he goes to court Thursday morning
↓
Additional reason: The groom needs 3 days (Sun–Tue) to prepare the wedding feast
↓
Wedding Delayed
If the designated time arrives and the wedding is postponed — does he owe her sustenance?
Get & Ones
Rava: Unavoidable circumstances (ones) don't prevent a get from taking effect
Key Theme The Gemara balances protecting the bride's rights with fairness to the groom when delays arise beyond anyone's control.

The Mishna's Rule: Wedding Days

Virgin (Betula)

  • Marries on Wednesday
  • Courts sit Thursday — virginity claim can be filed immediately
  • 3 days (Sun–Tue) to prepare the feast
  • She eats from his food starting Wednesday if he delays

Widow (Almana)

  • Marries on Thursday
  • No virginity claim — different concern
  • The Sages wanted the groom to rejoice with her for 3 days (Thu, Fri, Shabbat)
  • Courts available on Monday if needed
Mishna The Sages scheduled weddings to protect the bride while ensuring court access for legitimate claims.

Shmuel's Explanation & Rav Yosef's Challenge

Shmuel
Why does a virgin marry on Wednesday? Because another mishna (57a) says: if the designated time arrived and they didn't marry, she eats from his food. Wednesday sets the threshold — even if the date was earlier, he's not obligated until Wednesday.
Rav Yosef
Lord of Abraham! Shmuel explains a mishna whose reason is explicit (court access) using a mishna whose reason is not explicit! This adds nothing!
Gemara
Rather, Shmuel must have said it differently: A virgin marries on Wednesday so that if there's a virginity claim, he can go to court Thursday morning.
Gemara
But then let her marry Sunday — courts also sit Monday!
Gemara
The Sages were assiduous for Jewish women (shakdu) — the groom needs 3 days to prepare the feast: Sunday, Monday, Tuesday.
Shakdu The Sages proactively legislated to protect women's welfare — ensuring both a proper celebration and legal recourse.

The Chain of Dilemmas: Who Pays When the Wedding Is Delayed?

Designated wedding time arrives but wedding is postponed. Must he provide sustenance?
↓
He fell ill
He was forced by ones (circumstances beyond control) — is he exempt like when the time falls before Wednesday?
She fell ill
He says: I'm ready! Can she respond: your field was flooded (sadeihu hitzifa) — it's your bad luck?
↓
If she began menstruating (peirsa nidda) — is this ones or avoidable?
↓
At her set period (vesta)
This was predictable — she can't claim ones. She loses sustenance.
Not at her set period
Unresolved dilemma — is it like illness (ones) or still avoidable since periods can shift?
Principle The Gemara distinguishes between Rabbinic decree (Wednesday rule), true ones (illness), and foreseeable events (menstruation at her vesta).

Rav Aḥai vs. Rav Ashi: Reading the Mishna's Language

Rav Aḥai
The mishna says 'v'lo nissu' — 'they were not married' (passive). It does NOT say 'the grooms didn't marry them' (active). This implies the brides were delayed by ones — yet they still eat from his food. His bad fortune is responsible!
Rav Ashi
No! In any case of ones — whether his or hers — she does not eat from his food. The mishna refers to where grooms deliberately postponed. The passive language is just stylistic — matching the earlier clause about 'a virgin.'
Rav Aḥai
But if the groom chose to delay, why use passive language? The active form would be natural!
Rav Ashi
Since the reisha (first clause) speaks of 'the virgin', the seifa (latter clause) also frames it in terms of the bride. No inference may be drawn from passive wording.
Practical Difference According to Rav Aḥai: if she falls ill, he still pays. According to Rav Ashi: only if he voluntarily delays.
