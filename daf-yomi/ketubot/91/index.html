Ketubot 91 — Surplus, Liens & Creditor Claims

DAF OVERVIEW The Gemara resolves the dispute between the first Tanna and Rabbi Shimon about what constitutes a valid dinar surplus for ketubat b'nin dichrin. It then examines cases where creditors repossess property from purchasers and whether purchasers can force creditors to accept cash, applying the mishna's principle that one cannot inflate property values.

What Counts as a Dinar Surplus?

First Tanna
The surplus dinar must be in real estate (land) — movable property is insufficient
R' Shimon
Even a surplus in movable property (metaltelei) qualifies
Gemara
But R' Shimon himself said in the mishna that movable property is 'considered as nothing' — we must reinterpret!
Gemara (Revised)
Reverse the opinions: First Tanna accepts movable/liened property; R' Shimon requires free, unlienened land
CONCLUSION After multiple attempted explanations, the Gemara reverses the attribution: the first Tanna is more lenient about what counts as surplus

Mar Zutra's Ruling (in name of Rav Pappa)

A man marries two wives
↓
Wife #1 dies in his lifetime
Her sons are entitled to ketubat b'nin dichrin
Wife #2 dies after his death
Her son inherits normally from the estate
↓
One ketubah becomes surplus for the other — no additional dinar surplus needed beyond the two ketubot themselves
WHY BOTH HALAKHOT? Mar Zutra states both rules explicitly to teach that even when one wife's son might quarrel, we still apply ketubat b'nin dichrin

The Mishna: Can Sons Collect Ketubat B'nin Dichrin?

No Surplus Dinar

  • Estate = exact sum of two ketubot
  • Ketubat b'nin dichrin does NOT apply
  • All sons divide estate equally per Torah inheritance law
  • Prevents one set of sons from receiving more than their biblical share

Surplus of a Dinar Exists

  • Estate exceeds value of both ketubot by at least one dinar
  • Each group of sons collects their mother's ketubah first
  • Remaining estate divided equally among all sons
  • Torah inheritance is preserved through the surplus
KEY PRINCIPLE The dinar surplus ensures that biblical inheritance (yerusha d'oraita) is not entirely overridden by the rabbinic enactment of ketubat b'nin dichrin

Creditor Repossessing Two Mansions (1000 Dinar Case)

Setup
Debtor owed 1000 dinars. Sold two mansions for 500 each. Creditor repossessed one mansion, now wants the second.
Purchaser
Either this mansion is worth 1000 to you (keep it for the whole debt), or take 1000 cash and leave both mansions to me!
Rami bar Ḥama
This is like the mishna — you can't inflate property value. The purchaser cannot force this deal.
Rava
Not comparable! In the mishna, other orphans lose money. Here the creditor gets his full 1000 — it would be cruel to refuse!
HALAKHA The creditor must accept the cash. Unlike the mishna where inflating value harms others, here no one is harmed — middat Sedom (cruelty) applies

Authorization Document Amount — Ravina vs. Rav Avira

PositionAmount WrittenReasoning
RavinaFull amount (1000/100)Creditor forgave full debt for this property, so it effectively discharged that amount
Rav AviraPurchase price only (500/50)Creditor's personal decision to forgive doesn't change the property's actual value
HALAKHAPurchase price (500/50)The document reflects what the purchaser actually paid — the forgiveness was the creditor's choice
PRACTICAL IMPACT The purchaser uses this authorization document (harsha'ah) to claim compensation from the original seller who sold property that was liened
