Ketubot 55: The Tosefet Ketubah & The Great Pumbedita-Mata Meḥasya Debates

DAF OVERVIEW This daf establishes that the tosefet ketubah (additional marriage contract sum) follows all the same rules as the main ketubah — including collection from inferior land, the 25-year time limit, and the male offspring inheritance clause. It then presents a remarkable series of disputes between the Sages of Pumbedita and the residents of Mata Meḥasya on inheritance language, oath requirements, and deed procedures. The daf concludes with a discussion of umdena (assessing intention) and how it applies to deathbed gifts.

Ketubot B'nin Dikhrin: 'Inherit' vs 'Take'

Sages of Pumbedita
The stipulation reads: 'They will inherit' (יירתון) the money of their mother's ketubah.
Sages of Pumbedita
Since it is inheritance, they can only collect from property the father possessed at death — NOT from property he already sold.
Residents of Mata Meḥasya
The stipulation reads: 'They will take' (יסבון) the money of their mother's ketubah.
Residents of Mata Meḥasya
Since it is like a transfer/acquisition, the sons' claim predates other buyers and they may seize sold property.
HALAKHA The halakha follows Pumbedita: the text says 'inherit' — so the stipulation cannot seize liened property that was sold.

Four Pumbedita vs Mata Meḥasya Disputes

Pumbedita

  • 1. B'nin Dikhrin: 'Inherit' — no seizure of sold property ✅
  • 2. Movable property lost: Collects from other property WITHOUT oath ✅
  • 3. Land demarcated on ONE border: Takes WITHOUT oath ✅
  • 4. Deed of gift: Do NOT need to consult donor again ❌

Mata Meḥasya

  • 1. B'nin Dikhrin: 'Take' — may seize sold property ❌
  • 2. Movable property lost: Collects only WITH oath ❌
  • 3. Land demarcated on ONE border: Takes only WITH oath ❌
  • 4. Deed of gift: MUST consult donor again ✅
PATTERN Pumbedita wins 3 out of 4 disputes. Only on the deed of gift does the halakha follow Mata Meḥasya — the witnesses must consult the donor again if no formal acquisition was made.

Setting Aside Property for Ketubah: When Is an Oath Required?

Husband set aside movable property for wife's ketubah
Objects still intact
She takes them without an oath — all agree
Objects lost/changed
She collects from other property without oath (Pumbedita / Halakha)
↓
Husband set aside land for her ketubah
All 4 borders demarcated
She seizes without an oath — all agree
Only 1 border demarcated
She takes without oath (Pumbedita / Halakha)
KEY POINT The Gemara consistently rules leniently: once a husband designated property for the ketubah, we do not impose an oath on the widow even when the original designation is unclear.

R' Elazar ben Azarya: Marriage vs Betrothal Collection

R' Elazar ben Azarya
A woman who collects ketubah after marriage (nisuin) receives both the main sum and the tosefet.
R' Elazar ben Azarya
But one who collects after betrothal (eirusin) only receives the main sum — the addition was written only in anticipation of marriage.
R' Natan
The halakha follows R' Elazar ben Azarya — because the husband's intention (umdena) in adding the tosefet was clearly for the sake of marriage.
Rav
The halakha does NOT follow R' Elazar ben Azarya.
REASONING R' Natan consistently applies umdena (assessment of intention) — even without an explicit statement, we infer what the person must have intended and rule accordingly.
