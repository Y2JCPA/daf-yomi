Source for Father's Right to Daughter's Earnings

Gemara
From where do we derive that a daughter's earnings belong to her father?
Rav Huna / Rav
From "If a man sell his daughter as a maidservant" (Exodus 21:7) — just as a maidservant's earnings belong to her master, so a daughter's earnings go to her father.
Gemara
That works for a minor who can be sold. But a young woman (na'arah) cannot be sold — maybe her earnings are her own?
Gemara
If her earnings were her own, how could the father bring her to the wedding canopy? He'd be causing her to lose wages!
Rav Aḥai
He could compensate her for lost wages. Or bring her to the canopy at night, or on Shabbat/Festivals when she doesn't work.
Gemara
Rather, the verse about a maidservant itself teaches about a na'arah — for a minor, it's obvious her father owns her earnings since he can sell her entirely.
Key Logic The verse must be teaching something new — since a minor's earnings obviously belong to her father, the derivation must extend to a na'arah as well.

Father's Rights: Biblical Sources

RightBiblical SourceDerivation
Daughter's earningsExodus 21:7 — selling daughter as maidservantMaidservant's earnings → master; daughter's earnings → father
Nullifying her vowsNumbers 30:17 — "in her youth, in her father's house"Torah explicitly grants father power during this period
Accepting her get (divorce)Deuteronomy 24:2 — "she departs…and becomes another man's wife"Juxtaposition: just as father accepts betrothal, he accepts divorce
Not consuming her produceRabbinic reasoningUnlike a husband, a father will redeem his own daughter regardless
Note Three rights are derived from specific verses; the fourth — denial of produce rights — is based on logical reasoning about a father's natural motivation to redeem his child.

Does a Father Consume Produce of His Daughter's Property?

Tanna Kamma
A father may not consume the produce of his daughter's property during her lifetime.
R' Yosei b. R' Yehuda
A father may consume the produce during her lifetime.
Tanna Kamma (explained)
A husband gets produce rights so he'll be motivated to redeem her from captivity. But a father will redeem his own daughter regardless — she is his flesh and blood!
R' Yosei b. R' Yehuda (explained)
Even a father might reason: "She has money of her own — let her redeem herself!" So he too needs the produce incentive.
Underlying Question How strong is the natural bond of a father? Strong enough to guarantee he'll always pay ransom — or can even a father rationalize inaction?

Dowry During Betrothal: If She Dies Before Marriage

Father writes in the ketubah:
Produce, clothing, vessels as dowry
↓
Daughter dies during betrothal
(before nissu'in)
Tanna Kamma
Husband does not acquire the dowry — marriage never occurred
R' Natan
Husband does acquire the dowry — betrothal is sufficient
↓
Linked to dispute of R' Elazar ben Azarya vs. Rabbis
on collecting the ketubah addition from betrothal
↓
Gemara's Distinction
R' Elazar b. Azarya may agree: his gift to her requires marriage, but her father's gift to him takes effect at betrothal — the family bond is already formed
Resolution Even R' Elazar ben Azarya might concede the dowry belongs to the husband, since a father gives out of desire to join the families — and betrothal already accomplishes that.

Husband's Obligations Matched to His Rights

Husband's Obligations

  • 📌 Sustenance (mezonot) — daily, common obligation
  • 🔓 Redemption from captivity — uncommon but critical
  • ⚰️ Burial — final obligation

Husband's Rights (in exchange)

  • 💰 Her earnings (ma'aseh yadeha) — daily, common right
  • 🍇 Produce of her property — periodic income
  • 📋 Inheritance of her ketubah/dowry — collected once
Abaye's Principle Common obligations are matched with common rights (sustenance ↔ earnings), and uncommon obligations with uncommon rights (redemption ↔ produce, burial ↔ inheritance).
