Ketubot 105: The Incorruptible Judge

שׁוֹחַד — שֶׁהוּא חַד (Bribe — that he is one [with the litigant])
CORE THEME This daf explores the ethical foundations of judicial integrity — from how judges were compensated in Jerusalem, to the Torah's absolute prohibition on bribery, to extraordinary stories of Sages who disqualified themselves over the slightest favor. The key insight: bias is unconscious. Once a judge receives anything from a litigant, he becomes psychologically fused with that party's perspective.

Judges of Jerusalem: Resolving the Contradictions

SourceNumber of JudgesRole DescriptionNames Listed
Mishna2Judges who issue decrees (גְּזֵרוֹת)Admon, Ḥanan
Baraita #13Judges of theft (גְּזֵילוֹת)Admon, Ḥanan the Egyptian, Ḥanan ben Avishalom
Baraita #23Judges who issue decreesAdmon, Ḥanan, Naḥum
RESOLUTION Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak: They issued decrees concerning matters of theft — no contradiction. The Mishna listed only judges relevant to its topic. Rav Pappa: Naḥum is per R' Natan — the Sages didn't agree with him.

Judicial Compensation: From the Temple Treasury

Judges in Jerusalem received 99 maneh (9,900 dinars/year) from the Temple treasury
↓
Were the wages sufficient for the judge's needs?
Yes ✓
Judge serves with no changes to compensation
No ✗
Wages are increased even if the judge doesn't request it
RAV ASI'S PRINCIPLE The community must ensure judges can devote themselves fully to public service. A judge who is financially dependent is like a priest seeking out terumot — he 'overthrows the land' (Proverbs 29:4).

Karna's Practice: Payment from Both Parties

Karna's Practice
He would take an istera (small coin) from each party — both the innocent and the guilty — then judge the case.
Objection
But the Torah says "You shall take no bribe" (Exodus 23:8)! Even taking from both sides is prohibited!
Gemara
The prohibition applies to bribes. Karna took the money as a salary (אגרא), not a bribe.
Objection
But one who takes a salary to judge, his judgments are void! (Kiddushin 58b)
Resolution
Karna was compensated for lost wages (שְׂכַר בַּטָּלָה) — the income he forfeited by judging instead of working — which is permitted.
KEY DISTINCTION A judge may accept compensation for lost income (שְׂכַר בַּטָּלָה), but not payment for the act of judging itself.

Rava's Psychology of Bribery

The Torah's Prohibition

  • Applies even to bribes meant to acquit the innocent
  • Applies even to bribes meant to convict the guilty
  • Not limited to monetary bribes — includes verbal favors
  • The verse says שׁוֹחַד (bribe), not בֶּצַע (profit)

Rava's Insight

  • Once a judge accepts a bribe, his mind draws close to that party
  • The litigant becomes like the judge's own self
  • A person does not find fault in himself
  • שׁוֹחַד = שֶׁהוּא חַד — 'he is one' with the litigant
RAV PAPPA A person should not judge one he loves (he won't find fault) nor one he hates (he won't find merit).

Stories of Judicial Self-Disqualification

🚢 Shmuel — A man helped him step off a ferry. Shmuel refused to judge his case.
↓
🪶 Ameimar — A man removed a feather from his head. Ameimar disqualified himself.
↓
💧 Mar Ukva — A man covered spittle lying before him. Mar Ukva refused to judge him.
↓
🧺 R' Yishmael b. R' Yosei — His own sharecropper brought fruits a day early before a court date. R' Yishmael refused and appointed other judges.
↓
💭 Even after refusing, R' Yishmael caught himself mentally advocating for the sharecropper — proving how deeply bias penetrates.
R' YISHMAEL'S EXCLAMATION "Blast the souls of those who accept bribes! If I, who did not accept, and the fruits were legally my own, am still biased — how much more so those who actually take bribes!"
