Do Creditors Write Receipts?

Gemara
The mishna implies that when one repays a debt, the creditor writes a receipt — otherwise how could she collect with a get alone? She'd just reuse the ketubah!
Rav
No proof — we're dealing with a place where one does not write a ketubah. The obligation exists by rabbinic enactment, so there's no document to reuse.
Shmuel
The mishna applies even where one writes a ketubah. The system handles it through receipts and burden of proof.
Rav Anan
Shmuel clarified to me personally: the mishna addresses two separate cases depending on local custom.
Core Issue The dispute centers on whether the mishna proves that receipts are standard practice, or whether local custom regarding ketubah-writing resolves the concern.

Shmuel's Two Cases: Burden of Proof by Custom

Place That Does NOT Write Ketubot

  • Husband claims: 'I wrote a ketubah'
  • Burden on HIM to prove he wrote one
  • If no proof → she collects based on the get alone
  • Standard rabbinic sum applies automatically

Place That DOES Write Ketubot

  • She claims: 'He didn't write me one'
  • Burden on HER to prove he didn't write one
  • Only then can she collect without a ketubah document
  • Custom creates the presumption
Shmuel's Principle Local custom determines who bears the burden of proof — the party whose claim contradicts the norm must bring evidence.

Rav's Final Position: Preventing Double Collection

Rav retracts his initial position and rules for all places:
↓
Woman produces only a get
↓
She collects the main sum (ikar ketubah)
200 zuz (virgin) / 100 zuz (non-virgin)
↓
She later produces a ketubah with a larger amount?
Yes
She collects only the tosefet (additional sum), not the main sum again
No ketubah
Collection is complete — main sum only
Rav's Elegance By splitting main sum (via get) and additional sum (via ketubah), there is no possibility of deceit through double collection.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: The Time of Danger

מזמן הסכנה ואילך — האשה גובה כתובתה שלא בגט
Historical Context After the ruling authorities banned the performance of mitzvot, people would destroy their gittin and prosbol documents immediately after signing to avoid persecution.

Therefore: A woman collects her ketubah without a get, and a creditor collects without a prosbol — the assumption is that documents were written but could not be preserved.

The Gemara restructures the entire mishna as reflecting Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's view, distinguishing between cases with and without witnesses to the divorce.
