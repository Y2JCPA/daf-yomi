Ketubot 3: Conditional Divorce, Ones & Wedding Days

Core Problem: A husband sets a condition on a get — what if ones (unavoidable circumstances) prevents fulfillment?
↓
Virtuous Women
If ones doesn't count → she sits deserted forever, thinking he chose not to return
Licentious Women
If ones counts → she may remarry thinking it was ones when it wasn't → children are mamzerim
↓
Resolution: The Sages have authority to annul betrothal retroactively — kol hamekadesh ada'ata d'rabbanan mekadesh
↓
Second Topic: Why do virgins marry on Wednesday? And what happens in times of danger?
Key Principle The Sages' power to retroactively annul marriage undergirds their ability to free women from conditional divorces gone wrong.

The Dilemma of Conditional Divorce & Ones

Gemara
By Torah law, a condition unfulfilled due to ones is considered fulfilled. But the Rabbis say it is not fulfilled.
Gemara
But can we really say: by Torah law it's not a get, yet due to virtuous and licentious women we permit a married woman to remarry?
Gemara
Yes! Anyone who betroths a woman does so contingent upon the agreement of the Sages — and the Sages can invalidate the betrothal retroactively.
Ravina
That works if he betrothed with money — the court declares the money ownerless. But if he betrothed with intercourse, what then?
Rav Ashi
The Sages rendered his intercourse as mere licentious intercourse (be'ilat zenut), retroactively removing its legal effect.
Kol HaMekadesh This foundational principle — kol hamekadesh ada'ata d'rabbanan mekadesh — gives the Sages sweeping authority to annul marriages retroactively when necessary.

Does Ones Have Legal Standing in Gittin?

A man says: 'This is your get if I don't return within 12 months'
↓
He dies within 12 months — is it a valid get?
Mishna's Ruling
If he died → NOT a get (no posthumous divorce)
Implication?
If merely ill (other ones) → IS it a get?
↓
Gemara rejects: Illness is also ones — also NOT a get. Death was cited only to teach that divorce can't be posthumous.
Rava's View Rava maintains: Just as ones excuses the groom from providing sustenance, ones also has legal standing regarding gittin — the condition is NOT considered fulfilled.

The Ferry Case: Common vs. Uncommon Ones

The Case

  • Man tells agents: 'If I don't return within 30 days, give her the get'
  • He arrives on day 30 — but the ferry is on the other side
  • He shouts: 'See that I have come!'
  • Shmuel rules: It is NOT considered a return

Why Not Proof of Ones?

  • The ferry being unavailable is a COMMON, foreseeable obstacle
  • He should have stipulated this exception in advance
  • Since he didn't, he brought the failure upon himself
  • This does NOT prove ones has no legal standing generally
Distinction Ones that is common and foreseeable (ones d'shchiach) is treated differently — the person should have anticipated it and stipulated accordingly.

Why Wednesday? The Wedding Day Ordinance

FactorExplanation
Court AvailabilityCourts sit Monday & Thursday (Ezra's ordinance) — Wednesday wedding means Thursday court for virginity claims
Feast PreparationGroom prepares the feast Sunday, Monday, Tuesday — marries Wednesday
Pre-EzraWhen courts sat daily, a woman could marry any day
Today's ApplicationR' Shmuel bar Yitzchak: If courts sat daily today, any day would be permitted
Shabbat ConcernGroom is isolated from bride to prevent first intercourse on Shabbat (wound = forbidden labor)
Dual Rationale The Wednesday ordinance serves TWO purposes: court access for virginity claims AND ensuring proper feast preparation — both are required.

Time of Danger: The Prefect's Decree

⚠️ Government decrees: virgins married on Wednesday must submit to the prefect (hegmon) first
↓
📅 Custom shifts to Tuesday weddings — Sages do not reprimand
↓
🤔 Why not abolish Wednesday entirely? Because shemada (persecution decrees) are likely to be abrogated
↓
💪 Why not instruct women to submit? Virtuous women would rather die; licentious women would exploit the ruling
↓
👰 Wives of kohanim are forbidden to husbands even if raped — an additional concern
↓
📆 Tuesday works because the date is unfixed — the prefect won't come for an uncertain event
Principle We don't permanently abolish a rabbinic ordinance due to a temporary persecution decree — we issue a lenient temporary ruling instead.
