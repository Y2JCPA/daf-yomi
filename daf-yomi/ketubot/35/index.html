Lashes, Death Penalty & Monetary Payment: Who Pays?

R' Yochanan
One liable to lashes who was not forewarned must still pay damages — the Torah only juxtaposes death penalty with payment exemption, not lashes.
Reish Lakish
The Torah explicitly included those liable to lashes with those liable to death — they are unconditionally exempt from payment.
School of Chizkiyah
Smiting an animal → always pay. Smiting a person → always exempt. No distinction between intentional and unwitting — the exemption is absolute.
Rava
Everyone agrees: one who unwittingly commits a capital offense is exempt from payment. The dispute is only about lashes.
Core Principle When a single act triggers both corporal punishment and monetary liability, the more severe penalty takes precedence — but does this extend from death penalty to lashes?

The Dispute: Lashes + Payment

R' Yochanan
Those liable to death are juxtaposed with monetary cases and are exempt. But those liable to lashes are not juxtaposed — so if not actually flogged, one must pay.
Reish Lakish
The Torah explicitly included lashes-cases with death-cases. Even if not actually flogged, one is exempt from payment.
Gemara
Where does Torah include lashes with death penalty?
Key Question Does the principle of kam leih b'de-rabbah mineih (the greater penalty absorbs the lesser) extend from death to lashes even without actual flogging?

Two Sources for Including Lashes with Death

Abaye: Gezera Shava — "Wicked"

  • Numbers 35:31 — 'he is wicked and liable to die'
  • Deut. 25:2 — 'he is wicked and liable to be flogged'
  • The shared term rasha (wicked) links the two

Rava: Gezera Shava — "Smites"

  • Lev. 24:18 — 'one who smites an animal shall pay'
  • Lev. 24:19 — 'a man who places a blemish upon his counterpart'
  • Juxtaposition links lashes-liability to payment exemption
  • Not identical wording — a verbal analogy of concepts
Rav Pappa's Clarification Rava cannot derive from Lev. 24:21 ('smites a person shall die') — that verse is about death, not lashes. He must use the adjacent verses about injury and blemish.

The Silk-Tearing Case

בשעה שהכהו קרע שיראין שלו
Key Scenario If one strikes a person (incurring liability for lashes) and simultaneously tears the victim's silk garment — even though the blow itself caused less than a peruta of bodily damage, the property damage should require payment.

The juxtaposition teaches: one liable to lashes is exempt from payment even for simultaneous property damage, regardless of whether lashes are actually administered.
