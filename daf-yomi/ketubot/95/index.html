Ketubot 95: Cycles of Repossession & Spousal Waivers

Man married to two wives sells his field
↓
First wife writes waiver to purchaser: "I have no legal dealings with you"
↓
Second Wife
Repossesses field from purchaser (her ketubah predates the sale)
First Wife
Repossesses from second wife (her ketubah is earlier)
Purchaser
Repossesses from first wife (she waived her rights to him)
↓
🔄 Ḥalila — The cycle continues until they reach a compromise
Key Theme This daf explores circular chains of legal claims — when waivers, liens, and bequests create situations where no single party can hold property permanently, forcing compromise.

Is a Waiver of "No Legal Dealings" Valid?

Baraita
One who says "I have no legal dealings with this field" or "I have no connection to it" — has said nothing. Such declarations have no legal validity.
Gemara
Our Mishna deals with a case where a formal קנין (act of acquisition) was performed — validating her relinquishment.
Objection
Even with a kinyan, let the woman say: "I did it only to please my husband" (naḥat ruaḥ) — I didn't mean it seriously!
Gemara
This leads to a major dispute between R' Meir and R' Yehuda...
Principle A verbal declaration alone cannot transfer or relinquish property rights — a formal kinyan is required.

R' Meir vs. R' Yehuda: "I Did It to Please My Husband"

R' Meir

  • Wife who signed the second sale loses her ketubah claim
  • Cannot claim "I did it to please my husband"
  • First purchaser can say: "I left you a field to collect from — you brought the loss on yourself"
  • Our Mishna follows this view

R' Yehuda

  • Wife can claim she acted only for naḥat ruaḥ (to please husband)
  • Her signing the sale is not a genuine waiver
  • The Mishna in Gittin (55b) follows this view
  • Purchase from wife is nullified if she claims social pressure
Tension Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi (Rebbi) seems to rule like R' Meir here and like R' Yehuda in Gittin — how can both unattributed Mishnayot reflect different opinions?
6 / 9

Resolving the Contradiction: Three Answers

AmoraResolution
Rav PappaOur Mishna refers to a divorcée — she can't claim "to please my husband" since they're no longer married. Everyone agrees her waiver is binding.
Rav AshiBoth Mishnayot follow R' Meir. In Gittin there was only one purchaser (she can claim naḥat ruaḥ). Here, the husband sold to two purchasers — she signed only the second, so her claim fails.
R' Zeira / Rav ḤisdaOur Mishna = R' Meir; Gittin Mishna = R' Yehuda. Two different unattributed Mishnayot, two different opinions.
Halakha The key distinction: a wife's claim of 'naḥat ruaḥ' depends on whether she was still married and whether there were multiple purchasers.

Collecting Debts from Liened vs. Unsold Property

📜 Mishna (Gittin 48b): Creditor cannot collect from liened (sold) property when unsold property exists — even inferior quality
↓
❓ Dilemma: What if the unsold property became ruined or blighted?
↓
🌳 Case: Man mortgaged his orchard for 10 years; after 5 years it aged and stopped producing
↓
⚖️ Sages wrote a document of authorization (הרשאה) to collect from liened property sold to purchasers
↓
🛡️ Purchasers brought loss on themselves — they knew orchards age and should have anticipated this risk
↓
✅ Halakha: If unsold property becomes blighted, creditor may repossess liened property from purchasers
Rule The principle of collecting only from unsold property has an exception: when the remaining property is ruined or unproductive, the creditor may turn to sold (liened) property.

📋 Ketubot 95 — Summary Map

Wife Waives Rights
First wife writes to purchaser: "No legal dealings with you" (with kinyan)
↓
R' Meir: Waiver is binding — she cannot claim naḥat ruaḥ when two purchasers are involved
R' Yehuda: She can always claim she acted to please her husband
↓
Liened Property Rule
Cannot collect from sold property if unsold property exists — unless the unsold property is blighted
↓
Bequeathed Property
Husband = purchaser of wife's property. If she was unmarried when gifted → husband gets all. If married → cycle resolves for actual purchaser.
