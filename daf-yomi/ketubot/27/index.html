Captive Woman: Permitted or Forbidden?

A woman is taken captive
What determines her status?
↓
Monetary Offense
Permitted to husband
(when Jewish authority dominant)
Capital Offense
Forbidden to husband
(government abandons her)
↓
But if gentile authority is dominant, even monetary captivity requires witness testimony to permit her
↓
Witnesses — even a slave or maidservant — can testify she was not violated
Key Principle The permissibility of a captive woman depends on the type of offense, the political context, and the availability of witnesses.

Collateral vs. Imprisonment: Does It Matter?

Rava
The Ashkelon incident shows that a woman taken as collateral for a debt (monetary offense) still needed witnesses. This proves imprisonment is no different — when gentile authority is dominant, she is forbidden either way.
Gemara
No — collateral is different. Her husband stipulated the gentiles could take her, so they feel entitled to violate her. But in ordinary imprisonment, there is no such concern.
Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak (quoting Rav)
The real distinction is political context: When Jewish authority is dominant — monetary captive is permitted. When gentile authority is dominant — even monetary captive is forbidden without witnesses.
Resolution The Ashkelon case occurred when gentile authority was dominant, so witnesses were needed even for a monetary offense.

The Ashkelon Testimony — Eduyot

ElementDetail
WitnessesR' Yosei HaKohen & R' Zekharya ben HaKatzav
SubjectA Jewish woman taken as collateral for a debt in Ashkelon
Family's reactionThey distanced themselves from her, suspecting she was violated
TestimonyShe neither entered seclusion nor was violated
Sages' rulingIf you believe the witnesses she was taken captive, believe them that she wasn't violated. You can't accept half the testimony.
Principle You cannot selectively accept part of a testimony and reject the rest — the witnesses are either credible or they are not.

Capital Offense: Who Is Included?

Rav

  • Wives of thieves (gannavim)
  • When thieves are hanged, their wives are abandoned
  • Made available — not protected from potential rapists

Levi

  • Only the wife of ben Donai (a murderer)
  • Government specifically abandons a murderer's wife
  • Does not apply to mere theft

Chizkiyya vs. R' Yochanan

  • Chizkiyya: Only when husbands are sentenced to death
  • R' Yochanan: Even if not yet sentenced — capital charge alone suffices
Dispute The scope of 'capital offense' is debated — does it include all capital crimes, only murder, and must a death sentence be issued?

City Conquered by Siege — Mishna

🏰 A city is conquered by a besieging army
↓
All women married to Kohanim in the city are forbidden to their husbands
↓
Do they have witnesses?
Yes — even a slave or maidservant
She is permitted
Even normally disqualified witnesses are accepted
No witnesses / self-testimony only
She remains forbidden
A woman cannot testify for herself
Contradiction Raised Wine barrels in wartime are permitted (soldiers too busy to pour libations) — why aren't women also permitted (soldiers too busy to assault)? Rav Mari answers: Wine requires leisure; assault does not.

Miggo: 'Why Would I Lie?'

Rava
A captive woman who says 'I was captured but not defiled' should be believed via miggo — she could have made the stronger claim 'I hid and was never captured.'
Abaye
We do NOT apply miggo where there are witnesses. Just as in the donkey case — the renter said 'no water on the Pekod path,' but everyone knows there is water there. His miggo fails against established facts.
Gemara
The cases are different! The Pekod River certainly has water — witnesses confirm it. But the captive woman's defilement is only a concern, not an established fact. Where there is only concern, miggo applies.
Key Distinction Miggo is rejected against established facts (anan sahadei), but accepted against mere concerns (chashash).
