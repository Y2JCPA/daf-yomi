Ketubot 27 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak, what determines whether a woman taken captive for a monetary offense is permitted without witnesses?
A. Whether Jewish or gentile political authority is dominant
B. Whether her husband is a Kohen or Yisrael
C. Whether she was taken as collateral or imprisoned
D. Whether the captivity lasted more than 30 days
Correct: A — Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak (quoting Rav) explains that when Jewish authority is dominant, a woman taken for monetary reasons is permitted. When gentile authority is dominant, she requires witnesses even for a monetary offense.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the Gemara reject the comparison between the captive woman and the open wine barrels in wartime?
A. Wine barrels are muktzeh during wartime
B. Soldiers are too preoccupied for wine libations but not for assault
C. Wine libations require specific intent but assault does not
D. The wine case involves a rabbinic decree while the captive case is biblical
Correct: B — Rav Mari explains that pouring wine as a libation requires leisure and deliberate ritual action, so wartime preoccupation prevents it. Assault, however, can occur even when soldiers are busy, so wartime does not eliminate the concern.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does Abaye reject the application of miggo ('why would I lie') in the donkey rental case?
A. The renter admitted to violating the agreement
B. Miggo cannot be used in monetary disputes
C. There are witnesses that the Pekod River path has water, contradicting his claim
D. The donkey owner had already paid for a replacement
Correct: C — Abaye holds that miggo is not effective where there are witnesses contradicting the claim. Since everyone knows the Pekod River path has water, the renter's claim that it was dry is contradicted by established fact, and miggo cannot override that.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rav Ashi, why is a personal maidservant believed regarding a captive woman but not regarding conditional divorce?
A. A captive case is biblical law while conditional divorce is rabbinic
B. In divorce, her silence permits; in captivity, only active testimony permits — and she won't fabricate
C. The maidservant has no personal interest in the captive case
D. Conditional divorce requires two witnesses while captivity requires only one
Correct: B — Rav Ashi distinguishes: a maidservant may stay silent (concealing truth), but won't actively lie. In conditional divorce, her silence permits the woman — so we worry she saw intercourse and said nothing. In captivity, silence means forbidden — so if she actively speaks up to say her mistress wasn't defiled, she's believed, since she wouldn't commit two acts of dishonesty.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What logical principle did the Sages apply to the family who distanced themselves from the woman taken captive in Ashkelon?
A. Kal vachomer — if she's believed for a stricter case, certainly for a lenient one
B. You cannot selectively accept part of a testimony and reject the rest
C. Two witnesses override the testimony of one
D. A woman is always believed regarding her own personal status
Correct: B — The Sages told the family: if you believe the witnesses that she was taken as collateral, you must also believe their testimony that she wasn't violated. You cannot accept the part of the testimony that concerns you while rejecting the part that clears her.
