Ketubot 31 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Ḥisda, why must one who steals chelev and eats it pay for the chelev?
A. Because eating chelev is not a capital offense
B. Because the theft liability is incurred at lifting, before the eating prohibition is violated
C. Because kim lei biderabba minei only applies to Shabbat violations
D. Because chelev has monetary value that must always be repaid
Correct: B — Rav Ḥisda explains that the theft is complete when the thief lifts the chelev, which occurs before the prohibition against eating chelev is violated. Since the monetary liability precedes the capital liability, he must pay.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why is the case of shooting an arrow on Shabbat different from stealing chelev, according to the Gemara?
A. Arrows travel faster than a person can eat
B. Shabbat violations are more severe than eating chelev
C. Placement is impossible without lifting, but eating is possible without lifting
D. Arrows always cause damage but chelev might not be eaten
Correct: C — The Gemara distinguishes: in the arrow case, placement cannot occur without lifting — they are one inseparable act. But eating chelev can be done without lifting (by bending down), so lifting and eating are separable, making the theft precede the prohibition.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the practical difference (nafka mina) between the two reasons distinguishing the arrow case from chelev?
A. Whether one is liable for dragging vs. carrying
B. Whether one who carries a knife on Shabbat and rips silk while walking must pay for the silk
C. Whether chelev can be eaten raw or must be cooked
D. Whether the arrow was shot intentionally or accidentally
Correct: B — The case of one carrying a knife that rips silk: Under Reason 1 (lifting needed for placement), he is exempt because carrying requires lifting. Under Reason 2 (irreversibility), he is liable because he can stop carrying the knife, unlike the arrow.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What does Ben Azzai's principle 'מהלך כעומד דמי' (walking is like stopping) mean for stealing a purse on Shabbat?
A. Walking on Shabbat is itself a prohibited labor
B. Each step separates lifting from placement, so the theft is always a distinct act from carrying out
C. One who walks slowly is not liable for Shabbat violation
D. Stopping in the middle of the public domain creates a new domain
Correct: B — Ben Azzai holds that each step constitutes a pause between lifting and placement. Therefore, when one lifts a purse and walks it out, the lifting (theft) and the carrying out (Shabbat violation) are always separate acts, making the thief liable for payment.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does the Gemara resolve the problem of where the thief drags the purse to create simultaneous theft and Shabbat liability?
A. He drags it to another private domain owned by someone else
B. He drags it to the sides of the public domain (tzidei reshut harabim), which count as public domain for Shabbat but allow acquisition for theft
C. He drags it exactly to the boundary line between domains
D. He drags it to a karmelit, which has both public and private characteristics
Correct: B — Per R' Eliezer, tzidei reshut harabim have a dual status: for Shabbat, they are like public domain (crowds occasionally enter), but for acquisition, one can acquire there (the public isn't typically present). This allows both violations to occur simultaneously.
