Ketubot 22: The Mouth That Prohibited

הפה שאסר הוא הפה שהתיר
Core Principle This daf introduces the foundational principle of peh she'asar hu hapeh she'hitir — when the sole source of a prohibition is a person's own statement, that same person can also permit. The Mishna applies this to a woman who claims she was married but is now divorced, and to a captive who claims purity. The Gemara then explores what happens when contradictory witnesses testify about a husband's death or a woman's divorce, with a critical distinction drawn by R' Yochanan between the two cases.

❓ Objection: Perhaps it was an 'impudent court' of two (per Shmuel)?

✅ Resolution: It says 'the court of Rav Ashi' — his court follows protocol
Key Term Beit din chatzuf (impudent court) — Shmuel's term for two judges who rule as a court. Their verdict is binding but they violated rabbinic ordinance.

Mishna: Two Cases of Self-Reported Status

Married → Divorced

  • She says: 'I was married, now I am divorced'
  • No witnesses she was married → credible (peh she'asar)
  • Witnesses she was married → not credible
  • If she already remarried before witnesses came → need not leave

Captive → Pure

  • She says: 'I was taken captive but I am pure'
  • No witnesses of captivity → credible (peh she'asar)
  • Witnesses of captivity → not credible
  • If she married a Kohen before witnesses came → need not leave
Mishna's Rule Once she has already married based on her own testimony, even if contradicting witnesses arrive later, she need not leave her husband.

Providing a Rationale (Amatla) for Changing One's Statement

Scenario
A woman says 'I am married' and later says 'I am unmarried'
Gemara
But she already rendered herself an entity of prohibition! How can she retract?
Rava bar Rav Huna
She is credible if she provides an amatla — a rationale explaining why she initially said she was married
Baraita
A beautiful woman was being pursued by many men. She told them 'I am married' to ward them off. When a worthy man came, she retracted and explained her ruse
Shmuel
I learned this forty times to ensure I wouldn't forget it, yet I still did not rely on this leniency for myself
Practical Principle Amatla — if a person provides a plausible explanation for why their original statement was not truthful, they may retract it. But Shmuel's caution shows the gravity of relying on this leniency.
