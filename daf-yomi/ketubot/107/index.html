Does the Court Feed a Wife Whose Husband Went Overseas?

Rav
The court apportions sustenance (מזונות) for a married woman from her husband's estate — his property is legally mortgaged (משועבד) to her.
Shmuel
The court does not apportion sustenance. We cannot assume he left her with nothing.
Shmuel
Even Rav concedes: the court does not touch the estate for the first three months — a person does not leave his house empty.
Gemara
All agree: if they heard the husband died, the court sustains her. The dispute is only when his status is unknown.
Key Framework This daf revolves around Shmuel's position being challenged by multiple baraitot, each of which he deflects by reinterpreting as referring to a case where the husband was rumored dead.

Why Does Shmuel Exempt the Court?

Rav Zevid's Explanation

  • Perhaps he gave her a bundle of money (צררי) before departing
  • Assumes husband provided financially
  • Applies more strongly to an adult wife — he'd trust her with money
  • Does NOT apply to a minor wife — one doesn't leave money with a child

Rav Pappa's Explanation

  • Perhaps he told her: Spend your earnings to sustain yourself
  • He renounced her earnings in exchange for dropping sustenance obligation
  • Applies more strongly to a minor — even a child can earn something
  • Does NOT fully apply to an adult whose earnings may be insufficient
Practical Difference Adult wife whose earnings are insufficient: Rav Zevid says no court support (he left money); Rav Pappa says court must supplement. Minor wife: Rav Zevid says court must help (no money left); Rav Pappa says no support (she can use her earnings).

The Yevama Challenge — A Stronger Case Against Shmuel

Gemara
A yevama waiting for levirate marriage: first 3 months sustained from deceased husband's estate. After that, if yavam flees after court orders him to marry her — she is sustained from his property!
Gemara
His obligation is less than a husband's — yet the court still takes from his estate in his absence!
Shmuel
My concerns don't apply here: A yavam is not close enough to leave her money. And she doesn't owe him earnings yet — so he can't waive them.
Gemara
What about a wife who returned from overseas and said 'my husband divorced me'? She collects sustenance up to the value of her ketubah!
Shmuel
Again — this is where they heard he died. She gets only the ketubah amount because she caused her own loss by claiming divorce.
Logic The yevama case is powerful because Shmuel's usual defenses (bundle of money / earnings waiver) structurally cannot apply — forcing him to concede court intervention in that specific scenario.

The Mishna's Oath Dispute — Ḥanan vs. Sons of High Priests

👩 Wife's husband goes overseas; she demands sustenance from his estate
↓
📜 Both sides agree: the court does give her sustenance
↓
🤚 Ḥanan says: She takes an oath only at the end (when husband returns), not at the outset
↓
🏛️ Sons of High Priests say: She takes an oath both at the outset and at the end
↓
⚖️ Shmuel reinterprets: This mishna discusses only where they heard he died
Note The fact that the mishna's dispute is limited to the oath is the strongest textual argument against Shmuel — sustenance itself appears undisputed.
