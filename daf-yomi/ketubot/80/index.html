What Counts as 'Consumption' of Her Property?

R' Abba / School of Rav
Even a cluster of dates stuck together counts as dignified consumption of her property.
Rav Beivai
What about dough made of dates? Is that dignified consumption? (Unresolved — teiku)
Ulla
If he ate in a non-dignified manner, two Sages of Eretz Yisrael disagree: one says the amount of an issar, one says a dinar.
Rav Yehuda
Even taking a bundle of branches and feeding them to his animals counts as consumption of her property.
Key Principle Consumption (אכילה) of a wife's property includes not just eating but any beneficial use — even feeding branches to animals.

Expenditures on a Minor Wife's Property

A minor girl married off by mother/brothers
(not father — only rabbinic marriage)
↓

↓
She performs mi'un (refusal) upon maturity
↓
Rav Yaakov / Rav Ḥisda:
He is like one who spends on another's property — he takes the value of the improvement
Reason The Sages enacted this so the husband won't let her property depreciate. Without guaranteed reimbursement, he would neglect its upkeep.

Rabbi Ami's rulingWhat he spent — he spent; what he ate — he ate
Rabbis' objectionHe ate from the principal, not produce!
Rabbi Ami's revised viewThen he takes an oath on expenses and is reimbursed
Distinction A husband may freely consume produce of his wife's property, but consuming the principal (keren) triggers the rules of expenditure reimbursement.

Enhancement vs. Expense: How Much Can He Reclaim?

R' Asi
The husband takes an oath and reclaims expenses only if there is enhancement corresponding to his expense.
Abaye
If enhancement exceeds expense — he takes the expense amount without an oath.
Rava
No! He could deceive by claiming expenses just under the enhancement. Rather: if expense exceeds enhancement, he receives only up to the enhancement — and even that requires an oath.
Halakha Rava's view prevails: the husband can never reclaim more than the actual enhancement to the property, and must take an oath even for that amount.

Can a Husband Sell Produce Rights to His Wife's Land?

Yehuda Mar bar Mareimar

  • Said in Rava's name: What he did is done
  • The sale takes effect
  • Later shown to be inferred, not explicitly stated
  • Based on maidservant incident (misunderstood)

Rav Pappa

  • Said in Rava's name: He did not do anything
  • The sale is void
  • Halakha follows this view
  • Husband's usufruct is for the gain of the house only
Halakha A husband who sold produce rights to his wife's land — the sale is void. The Sages granted him usufruct only for the household's benefit, not for sale.
