QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav, why does a woman need a get when her husband betrothed her conditionally but married her without restating the condition?
A. Because marrying without specification means he waived the condition entirely
B. Because a person does not intend licentious relations, so consummation serves as new unconditional betrothal
C. Because the condition was fulfilled retroactively by the marriage
D. Because a rabbinic decree always requires a get after consummation
Correct: B — Rav holds that since a person does not intentionally engage in licentious intercourse (אין אדם עושה בעילתו בעילת זנות), the man intends the consummation to serve as a new, unconditional betrothal — but he does NOT waive the condition for ketubah purposes.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the Gemara say both the 'conditional betrothal' dispute and the 'minor who didn't refuse' dispute are necessary?
A. They involve different Amoraim and cannot be compared
B. One involves monetary law and the other involves ritual law
C. Each case has a unique factor — one has a condition, the other doesn't — so neither ruling can be derived from the other
D. The disputes actually reach different conclusions
Correct: C — The conditional betrothal case has an explicit tnai (condition) which might make Rav more lenient, while the minor case has no condition which might make Shmuel more stringent. Neither position in one case can be inferred from the other, so both must be stated explicitly.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rava explain why the woman needs a get but receives no ketubah payment?
A. The get is only rabbinic while the ketubah is Torah law
B. The Tanna is uncertain about the betrothal — lenient for money (no ketubah), stringent for prohibitions (requires get)
C. The husband explicitly waived the ketubah but not the betrothal
D. The condition invalidates the ketubah but not the betrothal
Correct: B — Rava says the Tanna is genuinely uncertain about the betrothal's validity. In monetary matters (ketubah), the burden of proof is on the claimant, so she cannot collect. But regarding prohibitions (adultery), we are stringent, so she needs a get.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is Rabba's revised distinction about the scope of the Rav-Shmuel dispute (after Abaye's objection)?
A. The dispute applies to all cases without exception
B. The dispute is only about cases involving two different women
C. The dispute is about a one-woman case resembling two women — where he betrothed conditionally, divorced, then remarried without specification
D. The dispute is only about cases where the condition was explicitly restated
Correct: C — After Abaye objected that the Mishna involves one woman, Rabba revised his statement: the dispute concerns a one-woman case similar to two women — he betrothed conditionally, divorced her, then remarried without specification. In a simple one-woman case (betrothal → marriage → discovery), all agree no get is needed.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In the sivlonot baraita, what does the First Tanna hold about a man who sent presents after an invalid betrothal and then consummated the marriage?
A. The presents validate the original betrothal retroactively
B. Neither presents nor intercourse create a valid marriage
C. The presents alone create a valid betrothal
D. The presents do not create betrothal, but intercourse does — because he intended it as new betrothal
Correct: D — The First Tanna holds that sending sivlonot (presents) does not create betrothal since they were sent based on the invalid original act. However, intercourse DOES acquire her as his wife because the man presumably intended it as a valid new betrothal — paralleling Rav's position.
