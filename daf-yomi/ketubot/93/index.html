Ketubot 93 — Dividing Limited Assets Among Competing Claims

CORE THEME When a deceased husband's estate is insufficient to pay all wives' ketubot, how do we divide? The Mishna presents a surprising formula. Shmuel explains it through a mechanism of mutual concessions (siluk), while the Gemara extends these principles to business partnerships — asking whether partners who invest unequally share profits equally or proportionally.

Shmuel Explains the Division Mechanism

Gemara
When the estate is 200, why does the wife with a 100-dinar ketubah get 50? She should only get 33⅓ — all three claim the first 100 equally!
Shmuel
The wife of 200 writes a document to the wife of 100: 'I have no legal dealings with you regarding the first hundred dinars.' She removes herself — leaving only two claimants to split the first 100.
Gemara
But then Wife C (300) should say to Wife B (200): You removed yourself from the first 100! You only claim the second 100. So C should get 100 total and B only 50!
Shmuel
Wife B responds: I only removed myself from litigation with Wife A — not from my rights against you! Against you, I still claim the first 100. So we split the remaining 150 equally: 75 each.
SILUK The concession (siluk) is personal, not absolute — Wife B waives her claim only vis-à-vis Wife A, not vis-à-vis Wife C.

Partnership Profits: Equal or Proportional?

Shmuel
Two people invest in a joint venture — one puts in 100 dinars, the other 200. The earnings are divided equally, not proportionally.
Rabba
Shmuel is correct only when they bought an ox for plowing and used it for plowing — each partner's share is equally necessary. But if they bought for plowing and slaughtered it, profits follow monetary investment.
Rav Hamnuna
Even if they bought an ox for plowing and used it for slaughter, the earnings are still divided equally. The partnership terms don't change based on outcome.
CORE DISPUTE Does the purpose of a joint purchase affect profit distribution? Rabba distinguishes plowing (equal) from slaughter (proportional); Rav Hamnuna treats both equally.
7 / 9

Ox for Plowing vs. Ox for Slaughter

Rabba's View

  • Plowing → equal split (each part of ox needed)
  • Slaughter → proportional to investment
  • Use determines distribution method
  • Tosefta's 'mixed oxen' case supports this

Rav Hamnuna's View

  • Plowing → equal split
  • Slaughter → ALSO equal split
  • Partnership terms remain constant
  • Outcome doesn't change the agreement
TOSEFTA CHALLENGE The Gemara attempts to refute Rabba from a Tosefta that says partners who deposited into a purse divide equally — but deflects by limiting it to the plowing-for-plowing case.
