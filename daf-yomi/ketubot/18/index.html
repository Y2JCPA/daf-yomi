Ketubot 18: Witnesses, Oaths & Self-Invalidation

↓
Case of "I borrowed & repaid" — rejected because witnesses who saw the loan don't require witnessed repayment
↓
Case of "Your father owed me" — rejected because it's a machlokes R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov vs. Rabbis

Your Father's Debt: Oath or Lost Article?

Borrower
Your father had 100 dinars with me, but I already repaid half.
R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov
He must take an oath on the half he claims to have repaid — this is an oath on his own claim (nishba al pi atzmo).
Rabbis
He is like one who returns a lost article (meshiv aveidah) — the son didn't even know about the debt! He is exempt from an oath.
Gemara
But R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov — doesn't even he agree that one who returns a lost article is exempt from oath?
Rav
The case is where the son (katan) has already made a claim — so it's not a voluntary admission.
Key Point The Gemara explains that katan here means one who reached majority but is a 'minor' regarding his father's affairs — he doesn't know the details of the dealings.

Rabba's Theory: Why Partial Admission Requires an Oath

A creditor claims: You owe me 100
↓
The borrower admits to part of the debt (modeh b'miktzat)
↓
Why didn't he deny everything?
↓
Rabba: A person would not be so insolent (me'iz panim) as to deny a debt to his creditor's face
↓
He wants to deny it all but can't — so he admits part to buy time
↓
Torah imposes an oath to extract the full truth
Rabba's Principle The oath on partial admission exists because the debtor's conscience prevents full denial, but his financial pressure prevents full admission — the oath resolves this tension.

Creditor vs. Creditor's Son: The Insolence Factor

R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov

  • No difference between creditor and his son
  • Borrower would NOT be insolent before either
  • Partial admission = regular modeh b'miktzat
  • Oath is REQUIRED
  • Not considered meshiv aveidah

Rabbis

  • Critical difference between creditor and son
  • Before creditor himself — would NOT be insolent
  • Before the SON — WOULD be insolent (son didn't lend)
  • Since he could have denied everything but chose to admit — he is meshiv aveidah
  • EXEMPT from oath
Underlying Logic The entire dispute revolves around whether Rabba's principle of insolence extends to the creditor's heir. If it does — oath required. If not — the admission is voluntary and praiseworthy.

Mishna: Witnesses Invalidating Their Own Signatures

Witness ClaimNo Independent VerificationIndependent Verification Exists
"We were compelled to sign"✅ Believed — peh she'asar❌ Not believed — cannot retract
"We were minors"✅ Believed — peh she'asar❌ Not believed
"We were disqualified witnesses"✅ Believed — peh she'asar❌ Not believed
Principle When the document's validity rests entirely on the witnesses' own testimony, they can also invalidate it. But once signatures are independently verified, the witnesses lose the power to retract — Reish Lakish: Signatories on a document are like witnesses whose testimony was already cross-examined in court.

Rami bar Ḥama & Rava: Types of Compulsion

Rami bar Ḥama
(Initially re: latter clause) If witnesses say they were compelled by monetary threat — not believed (self-incriminating). If by life threat — believed.
Rava
This can't apply to the latter clause! Once signatures are independently verified, witnesses cannot retract at all — Reish Lakish taught that signatories are like cross-examined witnesses!
Rami bar Ḥama
(Corrected — re: first clause) When there is no independent verification: life threat = believed; monetary threat = not believed.
Gemara
Why not believed for monetary compulsion? Because ein adam meisim atzmo rasha — a person cannot render himself wicked through his own testimony.
Distinction Life threat: Signing under duress is permitted — no self-incrimination.
Monetary threat: Signing falsely for money is a sin — admitting it is self-incriminating testimony, which is rejected.
