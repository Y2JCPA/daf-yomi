How Many Constitute 'Many' for an Irrevocable Oath?

Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
Three people — derived from the laws of zava: 'days' = 2 (minimum plural), 'many days' (Lev. 15:25) = 3
Rabbi Yitzḥak
Ten people — the verse about the Gibeonites uses the word assembly (עדה), and an assembly requires at least 10 members
Context The princes swore to the Gibeonites (Joshua 9:18). Though the oath was made in error, it could not be rescinded because it was uttered before 'many' people.

Vows, Remarriage & Degradation in Court

Rabbi Meir

  • Any vow requiring investigation by a halakhic authority — he may NOT remarry her
  • Reason: A man IS willing for his wife to be degraded in court
  • Concern: He might say 'Had I known the vow could be dissolved, I wouldn't have divorced her'
  • This casts doubt on the validity of the get

Rabbi Elazar

  • Prohibition on remarriage applies only due to a vow that does NOT require investigation
  • Reason: A man is NOT willing for his wife to be degraded in court
  • He cannot undermine the get by claiming ignorance of dissolution
  • Only with simple vows can he claim he didn't know it could be dissolved
Key Distinction The dispute hinges on whether a husband would tolerate his wife going through the public process of having a vow dissolved by a sage.

Women's Preference for Marriage — Four Maxims

💬 Reish Lakish: Women say — "Tav l'meitav tan du" — Better to dwell as two than to dwell alone like a widow
↓
🐜 Abaye: One whose husband is small as an ant still places her seat among the noblewomen
↓
🧶 Rav Pappa: One whose husband is a wool-comber (nafsa) calls him to sit at the entrance of the house — proud to be married
↓
🍲 Rav Ashi: One whose husband is lowly (kalsa) doesn't even require lentils for her pot — marriage itself suffices
↓
⚠️ Baraita: All these women who appear satisfied with lowly husbands — they commit adultery and attribute children to their husbands
Application These maxims explain why a woman cannot claim mistaken betrothal when her husband has blemishes — since women prefer any marriage to remaining single, she presumably accepted his flaws.

Burden of Proof: Where Were the Blemishes Discovered?

Blemishes discovered on the bride
↓
Where was she living when they were found?
Father's House
Father must bring proof they arose after betrothal
(Follows R' Yehoshua)
Husband's House
Husband must bring proof they existed before betrothal
(Follows Rabban Gamliel)
↓
Apparent contradiction: first clause = R' Yehoshua, latter clause = Rabban Gamliel?
R' Elazar
Mishna is disjointed — two different tannaim
Rava
"Discovered here = created here"
Single principle resolves both clauses
Principle Rava's presumption: where a blemish is discovered is presumed to be where it originated — shifting the burden of proof to the other party.
