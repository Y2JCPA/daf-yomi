Ketubot 66 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Pappa, why is a found object comparable to surplus produced through extraordinary effort?
A. Because both require physical exertion
B. Because neither is a regular occurrence
C. Because both are types of handiwork
D. Because the Torah equates them explicitly
Correct: B — Rav Pappa explains that finding a lost object, like surplus through extraordinary effort, is not a regular occurrence — making it subject to the dispute between R' Akiva and the Rabbis.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
If a bride brings 1,000 dinars in cash as a dowry, what does the groom write in the ketubah?
A. 1,000 dinars
B. 800 dinars
C. 1,500 dinars
D. 1,200 dinars
Correct: C — For cash dowries, the groom adds 50% to his ketubah obligation because he can invest and profit from the money. 1,000 × 1.5 = 1,500 dinars.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why can a father-in-law refuse to give the dowry he pledged to the yavam?
A. Because the yavam is always less worthy
B. Because the pledge was a personal commitment to the original son-in-law
C. Because yibum cancels all prior financial obligations
D. Because the daughter no longer wants the marriage
Correct: B — The dowry pledge was made to a specific individual. The father can say: 'To your brother I wanted to give, but to you I do not want to give.' This applies even if the yavam is a Torah scholar.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rav Ashi, why does a husband receive humiliation compensation when his wife is shamed, but family members do not when a relative is shamed?
A. The Torah explicitly grants this right only to husbands
B. Family members don't experience any emotional pain
C. A wife is considered 'gufo' — his own self — unlike other relatives
D. The ketubah includes a specific clause about humiliation
Correct: C — Rav Ashi distinguishes: a wife is considered the husband's own self (gufo), so her humiliation is as if he himself were humiliated. Other relatives, while affected, do not share this level of identity.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What was the fate of Nakdimon ben Guryon's daughter as described in the Gemara?
A. She became a successful merchant in Babylonia
B. She remarried a Torah scholar and was restored to wealth
C. She was reduced to gathering barley from animal dung, covering herself with her hair
D. She moved to Egypt and lived modestly but comfortably
Correct: C — The Gemara tells how the daughter of Nakdimon ben Guryon — who once had 400 gold coins allocated for daily perfume — was later seen by R' Yochanan ben Zakkai gathering barley from dung, wrapping herself in her own hair for lack of clothing.
