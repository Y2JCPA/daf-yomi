Ketubot 94 — Competing Claims & Judicial Discretion

Estate has a stolen field
One wife may lose her ketubah payment
↓
4th wife collects — then earlier wife's field is repossessed
↓
First Tanna
What she collected — she has NOT collected
Property returned to earlier wife
Ben Nanas
What she collected — she HAS collected
4th wife must swear an oath first
Core Theme This daf explores how we resolve competing claims — through oaths, division, or judicial discretion (shuda d'dayyanei).

The Stolen Field: Does the Later Creditor Keep What She Collected?

First Tanna
A later creditor who collected before the earlier creditor — what he collected, he has NOT collected. The 4th wife must return property to accommodate the earlier wife.
Ben Nanas
What the later creditor collected — he HAS collected. It is not taken from him. Therefore the 4th wife must take an oath before collecting.
First Tanna
No oath is needed — since whatever the 4th wife collects can be taken back, there is no risk of permanent loss to the earlier wives.
Ben Nanas
Since property cannot be taken from her, an oath ensures she collects legitimately and no other wife is harmed.
Key Dispute The disagreement about creditor priority directly determines whether the 4th wife needs an oath.

Rav Nachman's Alternative: Concern for Depleting the Field

Rav Nachman in the name of Rabba bar Avuh:
Everyone agrees — what the later creditor collected, he has NOT collected
↓
Then why does Ben Nanas require an oath?
↓
Ben Nanas
We ARE concerned she will deplete the field (machsha lei)
Knowing the land may be repossessed, she'll exploit it short-term
First Tanna
We are NOT concerned about depletion
We assume she'll maintain the field's value — no oath needed
Reframing Rav Nachman shifts the dispute from creditor law to a behavioral concern — will uncertain ownership cause the 4th wife to damage the land?

Abaye's Explanation: Connection to Abaye the Elder

First Tanna

  • Does NOT accept the ruling of Abaye the Elder
  • No special oath requirement when collecting from an estate
  • 4th wife collects without an oath

Ben Nanas

  • ACCEPTS the ruling of Abaye the Elder
  • Anyone collecting from orphans must take an oath
  • Even adult orphans may not know their parents' affairs
  • 4th wife must swear before collecting
Abaye the Elder's Rule When the Sages said one cannot collect from orphans without an oath — this includes even adult orphans, and certainly minors.

Rav Huna: One Partner's Court Loss Binds the Other

ScenarioRuling
Two brothers/partners have a lawsuitOne goes to court and loses
Can the absent partner say: 'I'm not bound by this'?No — the one who appeared acted as agent for both
Rav Nachman's proof from our Mishnah1st wife swears to 2nd — not separately to 3rd and 4th
Why not separate oaths?Because the 2nd wife acts as agent for those behind her
Gemara's distinctionAn oath to one = oath to 100. But in litigation, the absent partner can say: 'I would have argued better!'
Final ruleOnly applies if the partner was out of town. If in town and didn't come — he accepted the representation
Practical Rule A partner present in town who fails to appear in court is deemed to have consented to representation by the other partner.

Two Deeds on the Same Day: Rav vs. Shmuel

Rav
When two deeds bear the same date, the recipients divide the property equally. We cannot determine who acquired it first.
Shmuel
The property is awarded by shuda d'dayyanei — the discretion of the judges. The court determines the owner's likely intent.
Gemara
Rav follows R' Meir: signatory witnesses effect the transaction — same date means simultaneous validity. So divide.
Gemara
Shmuel follows R' Elazar: transmission witnesses effect the transaction — the deeds weren't delivered simultaneously, so one person truly acquired it.
Underlying Debate R' Meir: Eidei chatimah karsei — signing witnesses finalize the deed. R' Elazar: Eidei mesirah karsei — transmission witnesses finalize the deed.
