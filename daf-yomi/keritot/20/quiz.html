QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav, what is the status of a meal offering from which salt was omitted?
A. The offering is completely invalid and must be redone
B. The offering is fit (valid)
C. The priest is flogged but the offering is valid
D. The offering is valid only if frankincense was applied
Correct: B — Rav holds that only rites explicitly repeated in the Torah verses are indispensable. Since he does not count salt as truly repeated, omitting salt does not invalidate the meal offering — consistent with the tanna of the mishna.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In the klal-prat-klal derivation, what is the key characteristic that determines whether an offering requires salting?
A. The item must be eaten by priests after the service
B. The item must be brought during the daytime
C. Other items must come as a requirement for it (like wood for the meal offering)
D. The item must weigh at least one issaron
Correct: C — The klal-prat-klal principle limits inclusion to items similar to the detail (the meal offering handful). The defining similarity is that other items come as a requirement for it — specifically, wood is required for the burning of the handful.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
From which textual source does Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi derive that one may voluntarily donate wood as an offering?
A. The superfluous word 'korban' before 'mincha' in Leviticus 2:1
B. 'You shall sacrifice salt with all your offerings' (Lev. 2:13)
C. The phrase 'an everlasting covenant of salt' (Numbers 18:19)
D. The repetition of 'offering' in the wood-gathering passages of Numbers
Correct: A — The word 'korban' appearing before 'mincha' in 'korban mincha' is deemed superfluous — it teaches that korban alone (wood) can be brought as a voluntary offering to be burned on the altar.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why are the limbs of a burnt offering included in the salting requirement while blood is excluded?
A. A special verse explicitly requires salting burnt offering limbs
B. Limbs share multiple characteristics with the meal offering — wood requirement, burning on altar fire, me'ila, impurity prohibition — which blood lacks
C. Blood was exempted by a rabbinic decree to prevent waste
D. Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yehuda dispute this and blood is actually included
Correct: B — The Gemara provides a mnemonic listing characteristics shared by burnt offering limbs and the meal offering (requires wood, burned on altar fire, subject to me'ila, prohibited when ritually impure) that blood does not share — justifying inclusion of limbs and exclusion of blood.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rav Yosef raises an objection about a non-priest salting the handful. What does this argument establish?
A. Non-priests are permitted to approach the altar only for salting
B. The Sages enacted that salt must be applied before the handful reaches the altar
C. Since a non-priest cannot approach the altar, the scenario where a non-priest salts the handful is inconceivable — therefore the law about non-priests salting is merely theoretical
D. Rav's position is refuted and salt is in fact indispensable
Correct: C — Rav Yosef points out that a non-priest cannot enter the area near the altar, so the case of a non-priest salting the handful is not a realistic scenario. This clarifies that such a law exists only theoretically or for teaching purposes, not as a practical concern.
