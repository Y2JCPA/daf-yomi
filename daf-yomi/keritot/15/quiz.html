QUESTION 1 OF 5
Three Amoraim each bring independent proof that the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis is not about the tzitz (frontplate). Which of the following is NOT one of those proofs?
A. A baraita about the frankincense bowls, where Rabbi Yehuda agrees the tzitz works for altar-bound items
B. The Paschal offering mishna, where Rabbi Yehuda agrees the tzitz works when the whole community is impure
C. The two loaves of Shavuot — if the tzitz were the dispute, Rabbi Yehuda would have said 'both are burned'
D. A dispute about the daily tamid offering where Rabbi Yehuda explicitly invokes the tzitz
Correct: D — The three proofs come from Rav Huna b. Rav Natan (frankincense bowls baraita), Rav Ashi (Paschal offering mishna), and Ravina (two loaves of Shavuot). No tamid-based proof appears in this sugya.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the thanks offering render its accompanying loaves piggul, but the loaves cannot render the thanks offering piggul?
A. The loaves are consecrated after the offering, so they have less sanctity
B. The offering's blood is the matir (permitting agent) for the loaves — piggul flows only from the matir to what it permits
C. The loaves are of rabbinic origin, while the offering is biblical, and piggul only applies to biblical items
D. The loaves are consumed by priests while the offering is consumed by the owner — different domains
Correct: B — The fundamental piggul principle is that the matir transmits piggul to what it permits. The offering's blood is the matir for the loaves, creating a one-way dependency. The loaves have no such permitting function over the offering.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
In Rabbi Elazar's dilemma, what specific scenario does he pose to Rav that is genuinely novel?
A. A priest who expressed piggul intent silently rather than aloud
B. Combined intent: half an olive-bulk from the offering plus half an olive-bulk from the loaves, totaling one full olive-bulk
C. Intent to consume the offering on the third day, which is beyond its permitted time
D. A case where two priests each have partial piggul intent — does their intent combine?
Correct: B — Rabbi Elazar asks whether the priest's intent regarding half an olive-bulk from the offering and half from the loaves can combine to render the loaves piggul — since intent about the loaves alone would not suffice, can the offering's portion supplement it?
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is Rava's explanation for why Rabbi Meir holds that piggul intent during slaughter can render the libations piggul?
A. Rabbi Meir considers libations to be part of the offering itself, not separate accompaniments
B. Once libations are consecrated in a service vessel, they are irrevocably fixed to that offering and cannot be switched — creating the matir relationship
C. Rabbi Meir holds that any item purchased for an offering is automatically bound to it from the moment of purchase
D. The Rabbis misread Rabbi Meir — he actually agrees that libations cannot become piggul
Correct: B — Rava explains that according to Rabbi Meir, vessel consecration locks the libations to that particular offering. Unlike the Rabbis who note libations can arrive later, Rabbi Meir holds that once in the vessel, they are permanently designated — making them subject to piggul from the slaughter.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
The Gemara says both the thanks offering case and the Shavuot lambs case must be taught independently. Why?
A. They involve different Torah verses and cannot be derived from each other
B. Without the Shavuot case, one might think the thanks offering rule is unique to it; without the thanks offering case, one might think the rule only applies to Shavuot
C. The cases differ because the Shavuot loaves are leavened, requiring a separate ruling
D. The Gemara only says one case is necessary — the other is redundant
Correct: B — The Gemara explicitly notes that both cases are necessary: each case has a unique feature that might lead to an incorrect inference if only the other were taught. Together they establish the general rule of piggul asymmetry between offering and accompaniment.
