5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Reish Lakish, what is the result when oil is added to the handful of a sinner's meal offering?
A. The entire offering is unfit and must be brought again
B. The handful should not be burned, but the offering remains fit
C. The priest receives lashes and the offering is consumed
D. Only the added oil must be removed; then the handful is burned normally
Correct: B — Reish Lakish holds that 'he shall put no oil' creates a prohibition but does not invalidate the offering. The handful simply should not be burned — the offering itself remains fit.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Rav Ḥisda's nullification principle follows which substance to determine 'same type'?
A. The minority (potentially nullified substance)
B. The majority (potentially nullifying substance)
C. Whichever substance was present in the vessel first
D. The substance with the stricter level of sanctity
Correct: B — Rav Ḥisda holds we follow the majority: if the majority could ever attain the status of the minority, they are considered the same type and nullification does not occur.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the specific scenario posed in Rava's dilemma about contiguous altar substances?
A. Oil was poured on the flour before the handful was separated
B. The priest squeezed the handful's oil onto the wood, then placed the handful on top
C. Two handfuls from different owners were placed together on the altar fire
D. Frankincense fell onto the remainder instead of the handful
Correct: B — Rava asks: if the oil was squeezed off the handful onto the wood first, and the handful was placed afterward — does the oil still 'ascend' with the handful as a contiguous substance, or not?
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rabbi Zeira derive that handfuls and remainders cannot nullify each other?
A. He infers it from the general rule that min b'mino is never nullified
B. He uses a verbal analogy from the shared word 'burning' applied to both handful and remainder
C. He cites an explicit Mishnaic ruling that was transmitted from Sinai
D. He reasons that since both are from the same offering, they share identical status
Correct: B — Rabbi Zeira uses a gezerah shavah (verbal analogy): 'burning' appears by both the handful and the remainder, teaching that just as two handfuls don't nullify each other, two remainders don't either — and a handful with a remainder follows the same Torah edict.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to the mishna, when are two intermingled meal offerings (handfuls not yet removed) considered fit?
A. Always, as long as the priest intends to sacrifice them for their respective owners
B. Only if both offerings belong to the same person
C. Only if the priest can still remove a proper handful from each offering separately
D. They are never fit once intermingled — a new offering must be brought
Correct: C — The mishna rules: if the priest can still remove a distinct, valid handful from each offering on its own, both remain fit. If the intermingling prevents proper separation, both are unfit.
