QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the Torah need both 'upon the blood' (Lev. 14:17) and 'upon the place of the blood' (Lev. 14:28) for leper purification?
A. One verse applies to the thumb and the other to the big toe
B. Together they teach the oil is valid whether blood remains on the spot or has been wiped away
C. One is for the wealthy leper's guilt offering and one is for his sin offering
D. The second verse overrides the first, teaching only the wiped-clean case is valid
Correct: B — With only 'upon the blood' we'd think oil is invalid once blood is wiped; with only 'upon the place' we'd think oil is invalid while blood remains. Both verses together validate both scenarios.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rava, what does the word 'foot' in the wealthy leper purification passage teach via verbal analogy?
A. That the priest must stand on his right foot during the sprinkling rite
B. That the right foot is used in the ḥalitzah rite releasing a yavam from levirate marriage
C. That the leper's right foot must be washed before oil is applied
D. That walking to the Temple altar must be done leading with the right foot
Correct: B — The Gemara derives a gezera shava between 'foot' in the leper passage and 'foot' in the yavam passage, establishing that the right foot is used in ḥalitzah.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rava, when does the mention of 'priesthood' in a verse mandate use of the right hand?
A. Always, whenever priesthood is mentioned in any Temple context
B. Only when priesthood and finger are both mentioned in the same verse
C. Only when the rite is indispensable to atonement (מְעַכֵּב כַּפָּרָה)
D. Only for rites performed on the altar itself, not in the courtyard
Correct: C — Rava limits the rule to atonement-essential rites. Auxiliary acts like conveying burnt offering limbs to the altar ramp mention priesthood but are not mandatory for atonement, so either hand may be used.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rabbi Shimon's position on the right-hand requirement differ from Rava's?
A. Rabbi Shimon holds that the right hand is never required unless explicitly stated in the verse
B. Rabbi Shimon requires both 'finger' and 'priesthood' to appear together; Rava requires only one of them
C. Rabbi Shimon agrees with Rava but adds that it applies only on weekdays, not Shabbat
D. Rabbi Shimon holds that only 'finger' — not 'priesthood' alone — can mandate the right hand
Correct: B — The Gemara clarifies that Rabbi Shimon's rule is stricter: both conditions must appear together in the verse. This is why blood collection (where only priesthood appears) does not disqualify the left hand according to Rabbi Shimon.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does the Gemara need a verbal analogy from the leper passage to teach that the handful of a sinner's meal offering must be removed with the right hand?
A. Because the sinner's meal offering has no frankincense, suggesting reduced requirements
B. Because Rabbi Shimon says this offering generates no piggul, one might infer the right-hand rule is also relaxed
C. Because the meal offering is brought by a non-priest and the right-hand rule normally applies only to priests
D. Because this meal offering is the only one processed in the Outer Courtyard rather than the Inner Courtyard
Correct: B — Rabbi Shimon holds that the sinner's meal offering (מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא) does not generate piggul due to its lower sanctity. One might extend this leniency to the right-hand requirement as well — the verbal analogy forecloses that inference.
