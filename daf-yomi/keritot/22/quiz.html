Keritot 22 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua, why must wood for the altar be previously unused?
A. Unused wood burns more cleanly and efficiently on the altar
B. Just as the altar itself was dedicated solely to God and never used mundanely, the wood must likewise never have served ordinary purposes
C. Old wood risks impurity from contact with non-Jews or the dead
D. A specific verse explicitly prohibits previously-used wood on the altar
Correct: B — Rabbi Elazar draws a gezeira shava-like analogy: the altar was built exclusively for divine service, so its fuel (wood) must share that same quality of never having been used for mundane purposes.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does Rav Yehuda say the term 'morigim' (threshing sledges, 2 Sam. 24:22) actually refers to?
A. Heavy iron plows used to break hard soil
B. Clay threshing jars used for storing grain
C. Wooden boards studded with iron teeth, dragged over grain to separate it from chaff
D. Wooden yokes placed over the necks of oxen
Correct: C — Rav Yehuda explains that a 'turbal' (as Ulla named it) is a wooden threshing board with iron protrusions — used to separate grain. Its wooden frame made it usable as firewood, which is why Araunah offered it to David for the altar.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
When a handful (kometz) intermingles with the High Priest's meal offering, what is Rabbi Yehuda's ruling and his reasoning?
A. Fit — the High Priest's offering elevates the entire mixture
B. Fit — min b'mino (same type) cannot nullify either component
C. Unfit — the High Priest's offering is entirely burned (kalil) while the kometz is only partially burned; the two modes are incompatible
D. Unfit — the High Priest's offering has greater sanctity and overwhelms the ordinary handful
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehuda rules unfit because the two offerings have incompatible modes: the High Priest's offering must be entirely consumed on the altar (kalil), while a standard kometz is only partially burned. The mixture has no valid mode of sacrifice.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
From which Biblical event does Rabbi Yoḥanan say BOTH the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda derive their min b'mino nullification principles?
A. Moses's peace offering at Mount Sinai
B. The daily tamid offering and its accompanying libations
C. The Yom Kippur Temple service where the blood of the bull and goat are mixed together
D. The inaugural offerings brought by Aaron at the Tabernacle's dedication
Correct: C — Rabbi Yoḥanan's insight is that the same verse — describing the Yom Kippur mixing of bull blood (for the kohen) and goat blood (for the people) before sprinkling — teaches both positions. The Rabbis limit the principle to altar-bound mixtures; Rabbi Yehuda extends it broadly to all min b'mino cases.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the Gemara's response to the challenge that perhaps the min b'mino rule only applies when BOTH conditions are met — same type AND altar-bound?
A. The Gemara agrees and rules that both conditions are always required
B. The verse explicitly states that either condition alone is sufficient
C. The Gemara notes that the verse teaches each condition through a different example, proving each independently applies
D. Rabbi Yoḥanan himself later retracts and accepts the combined-condition theory
Correct: C — The Gemara answers that if the verse had only taught min b'mino in the context of altar-bound substances, we could have argued both conditions are required. But because the verse illustrates each principle through its own example, we learn they apply independently — each condition alone suffices.
