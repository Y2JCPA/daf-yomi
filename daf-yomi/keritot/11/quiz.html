QUESTION 1 OF 5
If a grain of salt is found in the priest's hand together with the handful of flour, what is the status of the meal offering, and why?
A. Fit — salt is a valid Temple ingredient and does not disqualify
B. Unfit — the salt diminishes the flour, leaving the handful lacking its full measure
C. Fit — only non-food objects like stones can disqualify the handful
D. Unfit — salt must never touch the handful, only the whole offering
Correct: B — The Gemara needs all three cases (stone, salt, frankincense) because each teaches a unique lesson: salt is a Temple ingredient yet still disqualifies, because it was not 'fixed' together with the whole meal offering at the outset — it is salted onto the handful separately.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rav (cited by Rav Zutra bar Toviyya), what is the precise physical technique for removing the handful?
A. Scoop with all five fingertips pressed together pointing downward
B. Press the flat of the palm against the flour and lift
C. Bend the middle three fingers until their tips reach the palm, filling the space with flour
D. Use only the thumb and forefinger to pinch exactly one handful
Correct: C — Rav teaches that the priest bends the middle three fingers until their tips reach the palm. The flour fills the space between the bent fingers and the palm — this constitutes exactly one handful, neither lacking nor overflowing.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the specific function of the little finger in the kometz removal, according to the Gemara's resolution?
A. It has no role — only the middle three fingers and thumb are used
B. It sets the lower boundary of the handful from beneath the hand
C. It first helps scoop a full handful, then wipes away overflow from above to level it
D. It holds the measuring vessel steady while the other fingers scoop
Correct: C — The Gemara resolves that the little finger participates in the initial removal (ensuring the handful is not lacking) and then serves as the leveling instrument, wiping excess flour from above (ensuring it is not oversized). Both roles are necessary.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Yosef says that Rabbi Yoḥanan notes all three tanna'im agree about frankincense that 'comes by itself.' What do they all agree upon?
A. Frankincense that comes alone is never disqualified by any decrease
B. A handful of standalone frankincense that became lacking may still be burned and is valid
C. Standalone frankincense requires the same rules as frankincense with a meal offering
D. Standalone frankincense is always burned in its entirety with no exceptions
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan teaches that the tannaic dispute only concerns frankincense that accompanies a meal offering. For frankincense that comes by itself (as an independent offering), all three tanna'im agree it may be burned even if lacking — the dispute about minimum measure does not apply.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
The Gemara infers from the Mishna's ruling on frankincense that 'if he increased the frankincense, it is fit.' What ruling does it then cite that appears to contradict this?
A. A baraita ruling that any change in frankincense — increase or decrease — disqualifies
B. Rav Pappa's ruling that increased frankincense renders the handful oversized
C. Rami bar Hama's ruling that extra separated frankincense retroactively disqualifies
D. Rabbi Eliezer's ruling that the 'it is not necessary' principle applies to frankincense as well
Correct: A — A baraita is cited that teaches any deviation — increase or decrease — from the proper frankincense measure disqualifies. The Gemara must then reconcile this with the Mishna's apparent ruling that only a decrease disqualifies. The answer involves reinterpreting the baraita's language.
