🫙 Does a Vessel Join Non-Touching Contents?

Rav Kahana
A vessel joins its contents only when the item requires a vessel to be sanctified — as with meal offerings. Items that don't need a vessel are not joined by one.
Sons of R. Ḥiyya
The mishna says a vessel 'joins' — implying in any case, touching or not. But if a finger is inserted between two non-touching half-tenths without touching either, are both rendered impure?
Rava
Proof from a baraita: 'his handful' (singular) means one may not bring a tenth in two vessels — yet one vessel with two separate portions allows removal from one part on behalf of the other.
Abaye
Not necessarily — the mishna may describe clumps like comb teeth, or a divided hen trough. These are 'one vessel that resembles two vessels,' not proof about genuinely non-touching portions.
Core Principle A vessel has the power to join (מצרף) separate portions of a meal offering for impurity and handful-removal — but exactly when and how far that power extends is vigorously debated.

🔄 Can Impurity 'Saturate' an Item? The Tevul Yom Problem

Rav (initial claim)
If one part of a meal offering was already rendered impure by a tevul yom, a second tevul yom touching it cannot add more impurity — it is 'saturated' with that level of impurity already.
Abaye
We do not say an item is saturated with impurity! Proof from mishna: a sheet made impure by a zav lying on it (midras) can still receive additional impurity when a zav touches it — two levels coexist.
Rava
That case involves two different types of impurity (treading vs. contact). Here we have the same minor impurity — tevul yom — applied twice. Perhaps identical-type impurity truly cannot accumulate.
Gemara (final proof)
A baraita (Rabbi Yosei) confirms: contact-impurity takes effect even after treading-impurity. And the two types are simultaneous there — not sequential. So saturation remains unproven either way.
Key Distinction Simultaneous impurity (two types at once) vs. sequential (one after the other) — the Gemara rejects a blanket saturation rule but leaves the exact sequential case unresolved.

👥 Three Half-Tenths: Rava vs. Abaye

Rava's View

  • Original half + Replacement half + Recovered lost half = 3 halves in one vessel
  • If REPLACEMENT becomes impure → joins with ORIGINAL (they were designated to complete each other)
  • If ORIGINAL or LOST becomes impure → only those two join; replacement stands apart
  • Handful from LOST half: permits ORIGINAL's remainder to be eaten; replacement remainder is not eaten
  • Handful from ORIGINAL: neither LOST nor REPLACEMENT remainders may be eaten

Abaye's View

  • All three are 'residents of one cabin' (בני ביתא חד) — permanently linked by shared destiny
  • If ANY one becomes impure → both others join and become impure as well
  • If handful removed from ANY one → the other two remainders are not eaten
  • Reason: we cannot determine whose consumption validates which remainder
  • Objected to by Rav Pappa (danka of handful) and Rav Yitzḥak bar Mesharshiyya (one-third of handful is non-sacred)

🏠 Abaye's Unifying Principle: 'Residents of One Cabin'

כולהו בני ביתא חד נינהו — They are all residents of one cabin (Menachot 24b)
בני ביתא חד | Bnei Veita Ḥad Abaye holds that once three half-tenths become associated as parts of the same intended tenth — even through the accident of losing and replacing one — they remain permanently bound to one another. The impurity or handful-removal of any one contaminates or restricts the others, because we cannot disentangle their fates. This extends the vessel's joining power into a principle of intentional association: items destined to form a single sacrificial unit are treated as one, regardless of physical proximity.

Rav Ashi's resolution: With regard to the handful itself, the matter depends on the priest's intention at the moment of removal — he removes it to permit the remainder of the tenth as constituted.
