QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Kahana, when does a vessel join the contents within it for purposes of ritual law?
A. Always, as long as both items are edible food
B. Only when the item requires a vessel in order to be sanctified
C. Only when the contents are physically touching each other
D. Only for dry items, never for liquid mixtures
Correct: B — Rav Kahana limits the vessel's joining power to items that require a vessel for sanctification — as meal offerings require a vessel to be sanctified. Items that do not need a vessel for sanctification are not joined by placing them in one.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is Abaye's 'comb teeth' reinterpretation in response to Rava's proof from the mishna?
A. That a comb-shaped vessel is a valid utensil for meal offerings
B. That the mishna may describe clumps arranged like comb teeth — touching at the base — so portions are actually connected
C. That Rava's baraita refers to a completely different type of offering
D. That the comb-teeth arrangement invalidates the entire meal offering
Correct: B — Abaye suggests the mishna's case may involve clumps shaped like comb teeth — separate at the top but connected at the base — meaning the portions do in fact touch. This would undermine Rava's proof that genuinely non-touching portions can be served by one handful.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does Rava reject Abaye's proof from the zav and the sheet regarding impurity saturation?
A. Because a zav cannot impart impurity to a sheet under any circumstances
B. Because the zav/sheet case involves two different types of impurity, whereas the meal offering involves the same minor impurity twice
C. Because Abaye misidentified the source mishna's tractate
D. Because Rava agrees with Abaye and withdraws his saturation argument entirely
Correct: B — Rava argues that the zav/sheet case involves two distinct types of impurity — midras (treading) and contact — coexisting on one item, whereas the meal offering involves the same minor impurity (tevul yom) applied a second time. Same-type impurity applied sequentially may still saturate.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What does Abaye mean when he rules that all three half-tenths are 'residents of one cabin'?
A. The three portions must be stored in the same Temple storage chamber overnight
B. All three half-tenths are permanently bound — impurity or handful-removal affecting any one affects all three
C. Only two of the three portions may be offered together at any time
D. The portions belong to the same priestly family and cannot be separated
Correct: B — Abaye holds that all three half-tenths, having been associated as parts of the same intended tenth of an ephah, are permanently intertwined regardless of physical separation. Any impurity or removal of the handful from one entails consequences for all three, since their fates cannot be disentangled.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What specific objection does Rav Pappa raise against Abaye's ruling on the three half-tenths?
A. That one cannot halve a tenth of an ephah three times under Temple law
B. That even the remainder of the half-tenth from which the handful was taken cannot be eaten, since one-sixth of that handful was needed to permit the extraneous half but was not properly sacrificed
C. That Abaye's principle contradicts an explicit Mishnaic ruling in Zevachim
D. That the replacement half-tenth was never consecrated and therefore cannot be joined
Correct: B — Rav Pappa points out that even the remainder of the specific half-tenth from which the handful was removed cannot simply be eaten — because a danka (one-sixth) of that handful was allocated to permit the extraneous half-tenth but was never actually sacrificed for that purpose, rendering the whole handful partially non-sacred.
