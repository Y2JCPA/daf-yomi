🧂 Does Blood Require Salt?

Tanna Kamma
Blood does not require salt — the verse explicitly excludes it. Wine libations and wood are likewise excluded.
Gemara Challenge
But if salting already disqualifies blood by drawing it out, why do we even need a verse to exclude blood from salting?
Resolution
Lest one think even a minimal amount of salt should be applied for fulfillment of the mitzva — the verse teaches that even a tiny application to blood is forbidden.
Core Principle Salt is mandatory for all meal offerings and sacrificial limbs on the altar. But blood, wine libations, wood, and incense are excluded by verse or logic — each for its own reason.

🔍 What Is Excluded from the Salt Requirement?

Verse: 'Every meal offering of yours you shall season with salt' (Lev. 2:13)
↓
🪵 Wood
Not susceptible to ritual impurity → excluded from salting requirement
🍷 Wine & Blood
Not placed on altar fire — sprinkled on corner of altar → excluded
🌿 Incense
Sacrificed separately on its own altar → excluded
↓
R. Yishmael b. R. Yochanan ben Beroka: Each exclusion is derived from the verse — 'every offering of yours' narrows the scope to only certain sacrificial items

🩸 Cooked/Curdled Blood — Liability for Consumption?

Ze'eiri (R. Ḥanina)
Blood that one cooked — one does NOT transgress the prohibition of consuming blood by drinking it. The prohibition no longer applies.
Abaye
Objection from a baraita! Curdled blood seems still subject to the blood prohibition — this contradicts Ze'eiri!
Rava
No contradiction: Ze'eiri speaks of blood curdled by FIRE (truly transformed in identity), the baraita speaks of blood curdled by the SUN (still blood-like in character).
Rav Ḥisda
Congealed blood of EXTERNAL sin offerings — if one ate it, he is liable. 'The priest shall take' implies it retains sacrificial blood status.
Rava (adds)
Even blood of INNER sin offerings — one is liable, since comparable blood of external sin offerings IS placed on the altar. Rav Pappa draws the practical implication from this.
Key Distinction Fire-curdled blood loses its blood identity entirely. Sun-curdled blood retains it. Inner vs. outer sin-offering blood differs in altar use but both can carry liability.

🏛️ Three Locations of Salt in the Temple

Chamber of Salt

  • Main storage location in the Temple
  • Salt kept perpetually available
  • Salt here IS subject to misuse of consecrated property (me'ila)
  • Source supply for all Temple needs

On the Ramp

  • Stationed for convenient priestly access
  • NOT subject to me'ila laws
  • Dedicated entirely to use on offerings
  • Positioned between storage and altar

On Top of the Altar

  • Applied immediately before burning
  • NOT subject to me'ila laws
  • BUT: salt already on a limb IS subject to me'ila
  • Final application point in the salting process

⚖️ The Salt Ordinance and Ben Bukhri's Testimony

The Court's Ordinance (Shekalim 7:7): The Sages instituted that the Temple provides salt for all offerings — neither priests nor Israelites must bring their own salt
↓
Shmuel's Interpretation: The ordinance permits priests to add Temple salt when eating the meat of their sacrificial portions in the Temple courtyard — the novel permission is eating with salt, not just salting before the altar
↓
Ravina's Support: If the ordinance only covered salting before burning, it would be obvious; the novelty is permitting salt with the priestly meal — hence Shmuel's reading is correct
↓
Ben Bukhri's Testimony at Yavne: A priest who contributes his half-shekel is not a sinner — priestly contribution is voluntary, not obligatory, since their offerings are subsumed in public ones
↓
Relevance to Salt Ordinance: According to ben Bukhri, priests' offerings merge with public communal offerings — thus the Temple's salt fully covers the priestly portion without any individual obligation
