⚖️ Can a Permitting Factor Render Another Permitting Factor Piggul?

Rabbis
If a priest removes the handful intending to burn the frankincense the next day, the meal offering is piggul — one who eats it is liable to karet. Just as in animal offerings!
Rabbi Yosei
The frankincense is not part of the meal offering — it is a separate, independent permitting factor (matir). One permitting factor cannot render another permitting factor piggul!
Rabbis
The 'no piggul between permitting factors' rule applies only when they are truly independent of each other. The frankincense accompanies the meal offering — it should render piggul!
Rabbi Yosei
Frankincense is not the preclusion (ikuv) of the meal offering. The handful permits the remainder for priests; the frankincense permits the shewbread. They are categorically distinct.
Outcome Rabbi Yosei: meal offering is pasul (unfit) but no karet. Rabbis: full piggul with karet liability. The Gemara ultimately links this to the shewbread frankincense bowls as well.

🔀 Abaye: Can Consume-Intent Join Burn-Intent?

Abaye
Why does the mishna need to teach the case of combining consume + burn intent for half an olive-bulk each? Can't we derive this from elsewhere?
Gemara
The mishna is still necessary! You might think: where each half is in accordance with its typical manner — one consumed normally, one burned normally — perhaps they do combine into a full olive-bulk!
Abaye
And where one intends half to consume and half to burn, with each half typical for its type — wouldn't this be stronger grounds to combine?
Gemara
No — intent to consume and intent to burn are qualitatively distinct acts. They cannot join. The mishna must teach this explicitly because it is not obvious.
Key Rule אכילה ועשן אינן מצטרפין — Intent to eat and intent to burn do not combine to form a prohibited olive-bulk of piggul intent.

🌿 Frankincense as Permitting Factor: Full Comparison

Rabbi Yosei

  • Frankincense is NOT part of the meal offering itself
  • It is an independent permitting factor (matir)
  • Principle: one matir cannot render another matir piggul
  • Same for two frankincense bowls of shewbread
  • Meal offering = pasul (unfit) but no karet liability
  • "Not part of the meal offering" = not its preclusion (ikuv)

Rabbis

  • The 'no piggul between matirin' rule applies only to truly independent permitting factors
  • Frankincense is bound to the meal offering — it is part of the same sacrificial act
  • Intent to burn frankincense next day = full piggul
  • Karet liability for one who eats the meal offering
  • Animal offering analogy: slaughter with next-day intent = piggul regardless
  • Karet requires olive-bulk of intent covering both loaves

🙏 Rav Mari: Proving Non-Priest Frankincense Collection Is Invalid

Rabbi Yannai's ruling: A non-priest who collects the frankincense from a meal offering — the act is invalid and disqualifies the offering
↓
Question: What is the source? The Torah only explicitly excludes non-priests from kemitzah (handful removal). Where is the prohibition on non-priest frankincense collection?
↓
Rav Mari's proof attempt: From the mishna on 12a — placing the handful in a service vessel renders piggul, just like blood collection into a vessel for animal offerings
↓
Implication: Blood collection requires a priest. If vessel-placement for handful is comparable, then frankincense collection must also require a priest — proving Rabbi Yannai's ruling
↓
Gemara's rejection: The comparison does not prove priestly requirement. Vessel-placement renders piggul for a different reason — it is indispensable (you cannot sacrifice the handful without it), making it a significant rite
↓
Conclusion: Both blood collection and vessel-placement involve a service vessel sanctifying a permitting factor. This structural equivalence is why both render piggul — but it does not establish priestly requirement for frankincense collection from the mishna alone
