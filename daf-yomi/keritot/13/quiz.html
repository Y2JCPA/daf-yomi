Keritot 13 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Gemara, why can't intent to consume and intent to burn combine into a prohibited olive-bulk for piggul purposes?
A. Burning intent always cancels consuming intent
B. They are qualitatively distinct acts that cannot join together
C. Only consuming intent matters for piggul; burning is irrelevant
D. The olive-bulk threshold applies only to consuming intent
Correct: B — The Gemara (following Abaye's analysis) establishes that consume-intent and burn-intent are categorically different. Even if each covers exactly half an olive-bulk, they do not combine — each must reach a full olive-bulk on its own.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In the mishna on 13a, Rabbi Yosei concedes that removing the handful with intent to eat the remainder the next day renders the offering piggul. Why does the mishna emphasize 'Rabbi Yosei concedes'?
A. To show Rabbi Yosei normally disagrees with all piggul rulings
B. Because the tanna wants to contrast it with the frankincense case where Rabbi Yosei dissents
C. To indicate this is a stringency Rabbi Yosei adopted late in his life
D. Because Rabbi Yosei usually holds the meal offering cannot become piggul at all
Correct: B — The tanna writes 'Rabbi Yosei concedes' specifically to set up the contrast with the latter clause: in the frankincense case Rabbi Yosei disagrees. Without the concession language, you might think Rabbi Yosei rejects piggul in meal offerings entirely.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What does Rabbi Yosei mean when he says the frankincense is 'not part of the meal offering'?
A. Frankincense is physically separated from the flour and oil
B. The frankincense is not the preclusion (ikuv) of the meal offering — it does not permit the meal offering's remainder
C. Frankincense belongs to the Kohen Gadol, not the offering itself
D. The frankincense is brought on a separate day from the meal offering
Correct: B — The Gemara clarifies that Rabbi Yosei means the frankincense is not the ikuv (preclusion) of the meal offering. The handful permits the remainder for priests; the frankincense only permits the shewbread. These are two separate permitting mechanisms — one matir does not render another piggul.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does Rav Mari's proof from the 12a mishna — that vessel-placement renders piggul therefore non-priests cannot collect frankincense — fail?
A. The mishna on 12a only discusses animal offerings, not meal offerings
B. Vessel-placement renders piggul not because it requires a priest, but because it is an indispensable step where a service vessel sanctifies the permitting factor
C. Rav Mari misread the mishna — vessel-placement does not render piggul
D. Frankincense collection is specifically excluded from the mishna's list of piggul-enabling rites
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that both blood collection (animal offerings) and vessel-placement (meal offerings) render piggul because a service vessel sanctifies the permitting factor — not because they require a priest. The structural equivalence proves ritual comparability, but cannot derive the priestly requirement for frankincense collection.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rav Huna applies Rabbi Yosei's logic to the two Shavuot loaves. What is his conclusion, and what is Rav Naḥman's objection?
A. Rav Huna: both loaves become piggul together; Rav Naḥman: only one loaf does
B. Rav Huna: piggul on one loaf does not affect the other; Rav Naḥman: the baraita shows the Rabbis require intent on both loaves for karet, implying the loaves are treated jointly
C. Rav Huna: the loaves cannot become piggul at all; Rav Naḥman: the baraita shows karet applies immediately
D. Rav Huna: piggul spreads from the lambs to the loaves; Rav Naḥman: the loaves and lambs are independent
Correct: B — Rav Huna holds (per Rabbi Yosei) that piggul intent on one Shavuot loaf does not spread to the other. Rav Naḥman objects from a baraita: the Rabbis say there is no karet unless one had piggul intent covering an olive-bulk of both loaves — implying the loaves are treated jointly, supporting the Rabbis over Rabbi Yosei.
