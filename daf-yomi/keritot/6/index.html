⚖️ Why a Verse Must Disqualify the Tereifa

Rav Sheisha b. Rav Idi
We can derive tereifa's disqualification by a fortiori: a blemished animal (permitted to eat, forbidden to altar) is unfit — surely a tereifa (forbidden to eat AND to altar) is also unfit!
Gemara
Refutation: What is unique about a blemished animal? The Torah equated those who sacrifice with what is sacrificed — a blemished priest also may not officiate. This uniqueness does not apply to tereifa.
Rav Ashi
The a fortiori fails from the very outset — every proposed base case carries a unique feature that collapses the inference. A verse is therefore indispensable.
Rav Aḥa Sava
A caesarean-born animal proves otherwise! The Torah did not equate its priests with its offering, yet it too is forbidden for sacrifice — so that uniqueness need not block the inference.
Rav Ashi
Caesarean-born has its own uniqueness: it is not sanctified as a firstborn, whereas a firstborn tereifa IS sanctified. The reversion still holds — only a verse can settle this.
Core Principle When every a fortiori base case possesses a unique feature that cancels the inference — and those features cannot be combined without creating a new reversion — a scriptural verse must provide the disqualification directly.

🔄 The Gilgul: Why Every A Fortiori for Tereifa Collapses

Goal: Prove tereifa unfit for sacrifice without relying on a verse
↓
Base Case A: Blemished Animal
Permitted to eat, forbidden to altar → tereifa should follow a fortiori
Base Case B: Fat & Blood
Come from a generally permitted animal (Pinchas incident) → tereifa should follow
↓
❌ Refuted
Unique to blemished: Torah equated blemished priest with blemished animal — tereifa has no such parallel
❌ Refuted
Unique to fat/blood: derive from item that is generally permitted — tereifa itself is forbidden
↓
Gilgul (Reversion): Each side's uniqueness cancels the other; combining them produces a new reversion — the inference collapses entirely
↓
✅ Three verses required: "of the herd" (Lev. 1:3) · well-watered pastures · "whatever passes under the rod" (Lev. 27:32) — each covers a distinct scenario

🤝 Rav Ashi vs. Rav Aḥa Sava: The Caesarean Animal Exchange

Rav Ashi
Common element of blemished + caesarean: their general prohibition was never permitted. Tereifa IS permitted — via pinching of a bird sin offering. So the common element refutation still stands!
Rav Aḥa Sava
Bird pinching applies only to birds — animal tereifot are never permitted. So the 'common element was never permitted' does not distinguish tereifa from blemished + caesarean.
Rav Aḥa b. Rava
Even bird burnt-offering pinching doesn't help: the bird becomes permitted through a unique mechanism for the altar — not because of its tereifa status. So tereifa's 'permission' is illusory here.
Gemara (Final Refutation)
Ultimate answer: the common element of blemished + caesarean is that their defect is visible and noticeable externally. Tereifa's internal injury may be hidden — yet another uniqueness, producing yet another reversion.
Conclusion Every attempted combination of base cases generates a new gilgul. The disqualification of tereifa can only be established through the explicit verses of Torah.

🌾 Mishna: Who Disqualifies the Meal Offering Handful?

Disqualifying Officiants

  • Non-priest (זָר — zar)
  • Acute mourner — אוֹנֵן (onen): relative unburied
  • Ritually impure priest — טָמֵא
  • Tevul yom — immersed but awaiting sunset
  • Priest lacking priestly vestments
  • Priest who did not wash hands and feet
  • Uncircumcised priest
  • Priest who sat while performing the rite

Left Hand — Ben Beteira's Rule

  • Handful removed with left hand: offering is unfit (Sages)
  • Ben Beteira: return handful to the vessel
  • Re-remove with the right hand
  • Offering is thereby rendered fit
  • Rav extends: applies to non-priest removal too

Physical Defects in Handful

  • Stone found in the handful
  • Grain of salt in the handful
  • Pinch (koret) of frankincense in handful
  • Outsized handful — חָפְנָיו יוֹתֵר
  • Lacking handful — חָסֵר
  • Rule: handful must be exactly full, no more, no less

🏛️ Is Slaughter a Sacrificial Rite? — The Red Heifer Challenge

Rav's baseline ruling: A non-priest who removed a handful returns it — because slaughter by a non-priest is valid, meaning unfit persons can sometimes perform rites without disqualifying.
↓
Challenge: Rav himself ruled that a non-priest's slaughter of the red heifer (פָּרָה אֲדֻמָּה) is invalid! Doesn't this contradict the claim that slaughter is not a sacrificial rite?
↓
Resolution: The red heifer verse employs the term עֲבוֹדָה (avoda/service), imported from the Tabernacle context — this uniquely imposes a priestly requirement on its slaughter. It is a textual exception, not a general principle.
↓
A fortiori challenge raised: If items sanctified for Temple maintenance (בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת) require priestly performance of their rites, surely altar-sanctified sacrifices should require it even more!
↓
Rebuttal: The red heifer's requirement rests solely on the word avoda in its verse — a purely local rule. It does not establish a universal principle that slaughter requires a priest.
↓
Key distinction confirmed: Removal of the handful is a sacrificial rite (hence non-priests disqualify it); slaughter is not (hence ben Beteira's return rule is needed to explain why non-priest removal can be corrected).
