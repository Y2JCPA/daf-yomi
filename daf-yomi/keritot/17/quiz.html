Keritot 17 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav (as transmitted by Rav Ḥisda), what is the ruling if one burns the handful with the intent to burn the frankincense the next day?
A. The offering is piggul and one who eats it incurs karet
B. The offering is not piggul, because the frankincense is not the matir for the remainder
C. The offering is valid, because the frankincense is the primary permitted factor
D. It depends on whether the handful was fully burned before the intent arose
Correct: B — Rav holds that burning does not render burning piggul because the frankincense is not the matir (permitting factor) for the remainder — only the handful is. Since piggul requires improper intent during sacrifice of the matir, intent involving the frankincense cannot create piggul.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What was the 'significant novelty' in Rabbi Ḥanina's teaching that Rav Hamnuna considered equivalent to all his other learning?
A. That burning the handful always renders the offering piggul regardless of intent
B. That frankincense functions as an independent matir alongside the handful
C. That burning the handful with intent for frankincense the next day makes the offering neither piggul nor valid — only pasul
D. That one who eats piggul is liable for karet even if unaware of the improper intent
Correct: C — Rabbi Ḥanina's novelty was the middle status: the offering is pasul (unfit) — not piggul (so no karet), but also not valid. Both facts combined in one ruling was the significant insight Rav Hamnuna found so compelling.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to the Mishna on 17a, what is the status of a meal offering if the priest removes the handful with intent to consume the handful itself (an atypical item) beyond its designated time?
A. Piggul — the priest has violated the piggul prohibition and karet applies
B. Pasul (unfit) — not piggul, so no karet liability
C. Valid — since the handful is not normally consumed, intent to consume it has no legal effect
D. The handful must be replaced and the offering may be re-offered
Correct: B — The Mishna distinguishes between typical and atypical items: intent regarding a typical item (normally eaten or normally burned) creates piggul; intent regarding an atypical item renders the offering pasul (unfit) but NOT piggul — no karet liability.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How do the Rabbis interpret the doubled expression 'he'akhol ye'akhel' (Vayikra 7:18), in contrast to Rabbi Eliezer?
A. It equates consuming typical items with burning atypical items for piggul purposes
B. It teaches that even atypical intent disqualifies the offering, just as Rabbi Eliezer holds
C. It teaches that piggul intent requires an olive-bulk measure, just as eating requires an olive-bulk
D. It indicates that piggul laws apply only on the day of sacrifice, not beyond
Correct: C — The Rabbis use the doubled verb form to derive that just as eating requires a minimum olive-bulk measure to be halakhically significant, so too piggul intent must concern an olive-bulk. They do not use the verse to extend piggul or pasul to atypical items — those remain valid.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rav Adda bar Ahava explains Rabbi Ḥanina's position as holding two things simultaneously. What are they?
A. Burning renders piggul when intent is to eat the remainder; and burning does not render piggul when intent is for frankincense
B. Burning the handful with frankincense intent is not piggul; AND such intent does render the offering unfit (pasul)
C. The frankincense is not the matir; AND the handful cannot create piggul through any intent
D. Rabbi Eliezer's ruling is Torah law; AND the Rabbis' ruling is only rabbinic stringency
Correct: B — Rav Adda bar Ahava clarifies Rabbi Ḥanina holds: (1) burning does NOT render burning piggul — so intent for frankincense next day creates no piggul; AND (2) such intent does disqualify the offering as pasul. This is the 'neither piggul nor valid' middle status that constituted the novel teaching.
