QUESTION 1 OF 5
If a priest removes the handful from a voluntary meal offering intending it for the sake of a different person's meal offering, what is the halakhic result?
A. The offering is entirely disqualified and may not be burned
B. The offering is valid and fulfills the owner's obligation
C. The offering is valid but does not fulfill the owner's obligation
D. The remainder is forbidden to the priests
Correct: C — A voluntary meal offering is valid even when the kemitza is performed shelo lishma, but the owner's obligation is not fulfilled — the vow persists per Deuteronomy 23:24.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the Gemara cite Deuteronomy 23:24 ('That which has gone out of your lips')?
A. To prove that only verbally stated vows are binding in Temple law
B. To prove that the owner's original vow persists even after a shelo lishma sacrifice
C. To show that a gift offering may substitute for a meal offering
D. To disqualify any offering sacrificed with improper intent
Correct: B — The verse establishes that even a gift offering — which is entirely voluntary — cannot be deviated from. Therefore, after a shelo lishma sacrifice, the original vow remains binding and the owner must bring another offering.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rav Ashi resolve the contradiction between Rabbi Shimon's two statements?
A. Statement 1 = shinui kodesh (fulfills); Statement 2 = shinui shem (does not fulfill)
B. Statement 1 = shinui shem (fulfills); Statement 2 = shinui kodesh (does not fulfill)
C. One statement was retracted by Rabbi Shimon; the other is the final ruling
D. The two statements refer to different stages of the kemitza rite
Correct: B — Rav Ashi (reversing Rabba) holds: when performed for the sake of a different individual's offering (shinui shem) it may satisfy; when performed for a different type of offering entirely (shinui kodesh) it does not satisfy. This reading aligns with the mishna.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does shelo lishma generally disqualify meal offerings but NOT slaughtered offerings?
A. Meal offerings are more sacred and therefore stricter rules apply
B. The Torah explicitly exempts slaughtered offerings from shelo lishma
C. All slaughtered offerings share identical rites so no intent is 'recognizably false'; meal offerings have distinct preparations
D. Slaughtered offerings are always brought voluntarily so intent is irrelevant
Correct: C — Since all slaughtered offerings are performed with identical slaughter, collection, and sprinkling rites, no 'change of offering type' intent is recognizable. Meal offerings have visually distinct forms, making improper intent detectable and therefore legally significant.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the status of the sinner's meal offering (mincha chatat) when its kemitza is performed shelo lishma?
A. Valid but does not fulfill obligation — same rule as a voluntary meal offering
B. Valid and still fulfills the obligation regardless of intent
C. Entirely disqualified, like a sin offering slaughtered shelo lishma
D. The handful may be burned but the remainder is forbidden to priests
Correct: C — Rabbi Shimon compares the sinner's meal offering to a sin offering (chatat). Just as a sin offering is entirely disqualified when slaughtered shelo lishma, so too the mincha chatat is entirely disqualified when its kemitza is performed shelo lishma.
