🤔 Rav Hoshaya's Unresolved Dilemma: Why Did Rabbi Shimon Validate the Offering?

Option A — Intent
Rabbi Shimon validates because sincere intent matters: the one removing the handful intended genuinely, even if for the wrong offering — and sincerity, though misdirected, preserves validity
Option B — One Law
Rabbi Shimon validates because of the verse 'This is the law of the meal offering' (Leviticus 6:7) — one uniform law governs all meal offerings, so any handful removal is valid regardless of intent
Consequence of A
If it's about intent: removing a handful from a vessel for the sake of a different vessel might still be valid — the intention was real, just misapplied
Consequence of B
If it's about 'one law': the verse would equally validate a different-vessel scenario — no distinction between offerings or vessels whatsoever
Rav Asi
Could not accept any of the proposed resolutions (Rabba's, Rava's, Rav Ashi's) — each was rejected as insufficient to conclusively resolve the dilemma
תֵּיקוּ — Unresolved This dilemma remains unresolved. The distinction between 'intent-based' and 'verse-based' reasoning would affect whether removing a handful for a different-vessel offering is valid.

⚖️ How the Gemara Derives that the Jealousy Offering Is Disqualified

Problem: The Mishna states the meal offering of jealousy is disqualified when not removed for its sake — but where is this derived from?
↓
Baraita clue: Surplus money from jealousy-offering funds goes to communal gift offerings — the same rule as surplus from a sin offering
↓
Rav Naḥman confirms: Numbers 5:15 uses "iniquity" (עָוֹן) for the jealousy offering; Leviticus 10:17 uses "iniquity" (עָוֹן) for the sin offering → gezera shava
↓
Conclusion drawn: Just as a sin offering is disqualified when slaughtered not for its sake, so too is the meal offering of jealousy — they share the same 'iniquity' law
↓
Challenge: Why not also derive that guilt offerings are disqualified? They too use 'iniquity' language (e.g., Leviticus 5:1)
↓
Answer: The verbal analogy is partial — it teaches only the surplus-money rule. The Torah itself blocked full derivation from sin offerings: "The Merciful One revealed that other offerings may not be fully derived from a sin offering"

🔤 The Word 'It' (הוּא) — Three Offerings, Three Outcomes

Sin Offering — חַטָּאת

  • 'It is a sin offering' (Leviticus 4:24) → 'it' excludes: only valid when slaughtered FOR its sake
  • Disqualified entirely if slaughtered not for its sake
  • This rule is specific to sin offerings — Torah explicitly blocked derivation to other offerings
  • Includes: meal offering of sinner (derived by same logic)

Regular Guilt Offering — אָשָׁם

  • 'It is a guilt offering' (Leviticus 7:5) — also has the word 'it'
  • NOT disqualified when slaughtered not for its sake
  • Rav Huna (Rav): 'it' teaches a different law — a guilt offering whose owner died or whose sin was already atoned is brought and proceeds go to the Dead Sea
  • The word 'it' here carves out a unique law, not an exclusion

Nazirite & Leper Guilt Offering — מַתִּירִין

  • Exception to the regular guilt offering leniency
  • These offerings 'render fit' (מַתִּירִין) — they enable the person to re-enter the camp or eat sacred food
  • Disqualified entirely if slaughtered not for their sake
  • Rabbi Yirmeya: offerings that render fit are subject to greater stringency than those that merely atone

🩺 Why Are 'Rendering-Fit' Offerings More Stringent? (Rabbi Yirmeya's Principle)

Question
Why are the nazirite's and leper's guilt offerings disqualified when slaughtered not for their sake — unlike regular guilt offerings, which remain valid?
Rabbi Yirmeya
The Torah treats offerings that render fit (מַתִּירִין) more stringently than offerings that merely atone. These offerings enable a status change — and status change requires precision.
Proof
Even atonement offerings (regular sin offerings) slaughtered not for their sake remain valid — yet the rendering-fit offerings of nazirite/leper are not. The Torah is more exacting about effect-on-status.
Objection — R. Yehuda b. R. Shimon b. Pazi
But rendering-fit offerings can come after death too! If a nazirite dies, his offering money goes to communal gifts — so they also apply post-mortem, like atonement offerings. What makes them different?
Rav Pappa
There is no instance of a fixed manner of rendering fit that applies after death. Post-mortem fund reallocation is not rendering-fit — the act of enabling status change is inherently limited to the living person.
Key Principle מַתִּירִין — Offerings that permit/render fit are subject to greater stringency than offerings that merely atone. No fixed rendering-fit exists post-mortem.
