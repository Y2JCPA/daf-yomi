QUESTION 1 OF 5
What are the two possible reasons for Rabbi Shimon's ruling that a meal offering from which the handful was removed for another offering's sake remains valid?
A. The priest's sincerity of intent, or the verse 'one law for all meal offerings' (Leviticus 6:7)
B. The offering's intrinsic holiness, or the size of the handful removed
C. The type of vessel used, or the time of day the handful was taken
D. The owner's explicit consent, or the availability of a replacement offering
Correct: A — Rav Hoshaya's unresolved dilemma: either Rabbi Shimon holds that sincere (if misdirected) intent validates, or he derives from 'this is the law of the meal offering' that one uniform law applies to all meal offerings — making any handful removal valid.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Through what verbal analogy (gezera shava) is the meal offering of jealousy derived to be disqualified when not removed for its sake?
A. The word 'fire offering' (אִשֶּׁה) shared with sin offerings
B. The word 'iniquity' (עָוֹן) shared between Numbers 5:15 and Leviticus 10:17
C. The word 'it' (הוּא) shared between the jealousy offering and the guilt offering
D. The word 'remembrance' (זִכָּרוֹן) shared with the omer offering
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman confirmed the baraita's logic: Numbers 5:15 uses 'iniquity' (עָוֹן) for the jealousy offering, and Leviticus 10:17 uses 'iniquity' for the sin offering — the gezera shava connects their laws of disqualification.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why is a regular guilt offering NOT disqualified when slaughtered not for its sake, even though its verse also says 'it is a guilt offering' (using the word 'it')?
A. The word 'it' in the guilt offering verse teaches a different law: if the owner died or the sin was already atoned, the offering is still brought and its value goes to the Dead Sea
B. The word 'it' in the guilt offering verse is considered weaker and carries less legal force than in the sin offering
C. Guilt offerings are always more lenient than sin offerings in all respects without exception
D. The guilt offering verse was written after the sin offering verse and therefore overrides it entirely
Correct: A — Rav Huna citing Rav: the word 'it' by the guilt offering teaches that if the owner died or his sin was atoned, the offering is still brought and consigned to pasture until blemished, then sold, proceeds to the Dead Sea — a completely different halakha than the sin offering's exclusion.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yirmeya, why are the guilt offerings of the nazirite and the leper disqualified when slaughtered not for their sake, unlike regular guilt offerings?
A. Because these offerings are more expensive and thus require greater care in their performance
B. Because nazirites and lepers are in a state of ritual impurity that demands extra stringency in all their sacrifices
C. Because the Torah treats offerings that 'render fit' (מַתִּירִין) more stringently than offerings that merely atone
D. Because these are communal offerings in nature, similar to the omer
Correct: C — Rabbi Yirmeya: the Torah differentiates between offerings that atone and offerings that render fit (enable status change — re-entry to camp, eating sacred food). The latter are held to a stricter standard because the act of enabling status change requires precision.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
The Gemara gives two reasons why the omer meal offering falls outside the Mishna's general rule ('fit but obligation not fulfilled'). What are they?
A. The omer uses barley not wheat, and it is offered only once a year
B. The omer is too small for a proper handful, and it belongs to a different tractate
C. The omer comes on behalf of the community (not individuals), and it has a fixed, time-bound obligation
D. The omer requires special Temple vessels, and it atones rather than renders fit
Correct: C — The Gemara explains: the Mishna's leniency applies only to (1) individual offerings, not communal ones, and (2) offerings with no fixed time. The omer fails on both counts — it is a communal offering and has a fixed time (16 Nisan), so it falls outside the Mishna's category entirely.
