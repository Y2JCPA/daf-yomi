🌾 Rav vs. Reish Lakish: The Omer Handful

Rav
An omer meal offering from which a handful was removed not for its own sake is disqualified — the handful may not be burned on the altar.
Reish Lakish
It is valid — the handful is burned on the altar. A second omer must be brought, but this handful is not disqualified.
Rav Adda bar Ahava
Reish Lakish's logic: an offering is not 'before its time' if it will become permitted that same day. The omer's new crop is permitted by dawn of 16 Nisan.
Rav Pappa
The true reason of Reish Lakish: the illumination of the eastern horizon permits the new crop — so when the handful is sacrificed, it is already fit.
The Crux Does 'before its time' apply when the offering's prohibition lifts that same morning? Rav says yes — disqualified. Reish Lakish says no — valid.

⏰ Is the Omer Handful 'Before Its Time'?

Omer handful removed not for its own sake
Remainder not yet permitted to be eaten — is the handful disqualified?
↓
Rav — Disqualified
Remainder cannot be eaten → handful is before its time → invalid for the altar
Reish Lakish — Valid
New crop is permitted at dawn of 16 Nisan (same day) → not 'before its time' → handful may be burned
↓
Rav Adda son of Rav Yitzḥak objects: The baraita lists meal offerings as a case where a 'general prohibition was permitted by sanctity.' But if the handful is always fit, there is nothing to permit!
↓
Gemara answers: The handful was initially fit — no prohibition was ever permitted. The baraita's formula simply does not apply here.

🩹 Leper's Purification — Required Order of Rites

Step 1: Slaughter the asham (guilt offering) — this must come first
↓
Step 2: Place blood of the guilt offering on the leper's right thumb, right big toe, and right ear (Leviticus 14:14)
↓
Step 3: Apply oil (from a log) over the blood — on right thumb and big toe
↓
⚠️ If oil was applied before blood: oil placement is invalid — must redo
↓
Rav Sheshet challenges: If the offering is not 'before its time' once the day has come, why must the oil be redone? The second placement should itself be invalid!
↓
Rav Pappa responds: The leper's laws are exceptional — the Torah says "This shall be the law of the leper" (Lev. 14:2), indicating the order is eternally fixed as written.

🐦 vs. 🫓 Birds vs. Meal Offerings — Distinct Halakhot

Birds Only

  • Bring atonement for zav, zavah, yoledet
  • Melikah (pinching the nape) serves as slaughter for the altar
  • Pinching a non-sacred bird creates a carcass — yet pinching is permitted by sanctity of the altar
  • Two birds offered: one as sin offering, one as burnt offering

Meal Offerings Only

  • Must be placed in a service vessel (kli shareit)
  • Require waving (tenufah) and bringing near (hagashah) to the altar corner
  • Some types require oil and frankincense
  • Handful (kometz) separated and burned; remainder eaten by priests

⚖️ Why Can't We Infer Tereifa's Disqualification by A Fortiori?

Baraita
A tereifa is disqualified from the altar. This cannot be derived by a fortiori from a blemished animal — a verse is required: 'from the herd' (Lev. 1:3).
Rav
The omer proves the a fortiori fails: omer's new crop is forbidden to all, yet offered on the altar. 'Forbidden to all' does not block the altar — so tereifa needs a verse.
Gemara
Rejected: The Sabbatical year omer does not permit the new crop (no valid planting), so the omer is not a consistent counterexample.
Reish Lakish
The incense preparer proves it: preparing incense is forbidden to a layperson yet permitted for the Most High — rejected: the prohibition is only rabbinic.
Mar b. Ravina
Shabbat proves it: forbidden generally, yet Temple service overrides Shabbat — rejected: circumcision also overrides Shabbat, so Shabbat is not purely a divine prohibition.
Conclusion Every proposed proof is refuted in turn. The verse 'from the herd' (Lev. 1:3) is indispensable to explicitly exclude a tereifa from the altar.
