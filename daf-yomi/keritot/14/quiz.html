Keritot 14 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, what is Rabbi Yosei's position on the two thighs of an offering?
A. They are two distinct bodies — piggul in one never affects the other
B. They are one body — piggul intent for one renders the other piggul as well
C. Piggul intent never applies to thighs, only to the meat portions
D. They are only one body if both are sacrificed in the same rite
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan clarifies that Rabbi Yosei views the two thighs as one body. Therefore, piggul intent regarding one thigh renders the other piggul as well (14b).
QUESTION 2 OF 5
If a priest slaughters the first Shavuot lamb with intent to consume half an olive-bulk from one loaf, and the second lamb with intent to consume half an olive-bulk from the other loaf, what is the ruling?
A. Both loaves are piggul — the half-intents combine across lambs
B. Only the first loaf is piggul — the first intent was sufficient
C. Neither loaf is piggul — the intents are separated and do not combine
D. The offering is disqualified (pasul) but not technically piggul
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi rules that intentions for half a measure from each of two different loaves do not combine. Since no single loaf received intent for a full olive-bulk, there is no piggul.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What does Levi teach in his baraita about intentions during the four sacrificial rites?
A. Intentions during all four rites combine to produce a single piggul
B. Only slaughter and sprinkling intentions can combine
C. Intentions during the four rites do not combine to render an offering piggul
D. Piggul requires identical intent during at least two of the four rites
Correct: C — Levi teaches explicitly that intentions during the four rites — slaughter, receiving the blood, carrying, and sprinkling — do not combine to render an offering piggul (14b).
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In the mishna at the end of Daf 14, what does Rabbi Yehuda rule when one of the two Shavuot loaves becomes ritually impure?
A. The pure loaf is eaten normally and the impure loaf is burned
B. Both loaves must go to the place of burning
C. The entire Shavuot offering must be repeated with new loaves
D. The impure loaf is buried and the pure loaf is eaten
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda rules that both loaves go to burning, based on his principle that a communal offering (korban tzibur) cannot be divided — it must be treated as one indivisible unit.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rav Pappa, what is the core of the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis regarding the impure loaf?
A. Whether ritual impurity can spread by contact from one loaf to another
B. Whether the blood sprinkling had already been completed before the impurity occurred
C. Whether the High Priest's frontplate (tzitz) effects atonement for communal ritual impurity in this case
D. Whether Shavuot loaves are considered a single offering or two independent offerings
Correct: C — Rav Pappa explains that the dispute hinges on the tzitz (frontplate): the Rabbis hold it does appease communal impurity (so the pure loaf is permitted), while Rabbi Yehuda holds it does not (so both loaves are forbidden).
