🔥 Core Dispute: Can Piggul Intent Cross Offering Bodies?

Rav Huna
Rabbi Yosei holds the two thighs are two distinct bodies — piggul intent for one does not affect the other. With the two loaves, intent must reach an olive-bulk within a single loaf.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Rabbi Yosei holds the two thighs are one body — piggul for one spreads to the other. Two loaves: if intent mixes them (olive-bulk combined), both are piggul; if separated, each stands alone.
Resolution Rabbi Yoḥanan's reading prevails. The two loaves are considered one body — mixing intent renders both piggul; separating intent keeps each independent. Rav Huna's position is refuted by multiple baraitot.

Full measure, single loaf → that loaf is piggul

Intent: olive-bulk from BOTH combined

⚔️ Rav Huna Challenged — Refutations from Baraitot

Rav Ashi
Refutation 1: A baraita — if one slaughters bulls/goats with intent to pour remaining blood the next day, the meat is piggul even though he had no intent regarding the meat itself. Piggul spreads across parts!
Ravina
Refutation 2: Mishna (13a): Removing a handful with intent to burn it tomorrow renders the remainder piggul. If the handful's intent affects the remainder, surely intent about one thigh affects the other!
Rav Huna
The baraita follows Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, not Rabbi Yosei. His opinion involves 'half and half' — you cannot infer Rabbi Yosei's view from there.
Verdict Both refutations stand. The Gemara accepts Rabbi Yoḥanan: piggul in one part of an offering spreads to related parts that share the same 'body.'

⏰ Do Intentions Across Different Rites Combine for Piggul?

Some Say: YES

  • Intent during slaughter (½ olive-bulk) + intent during sprinkling (½ olive-bulk) = piggul
  • Both rites are permitting factors (mattirim)
  • Therefore their half-intents combine into a full measure

Levi's Baraita: NO

  • Intentions during the four rites do NOT combine
  • The four rites: Slaughter, Receiving blood, Carrying, Sprinkling
  • Each rite's intent stands alone — no cross-rite combination

Abaye's Resolution

  • Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: no combining even across a full permitting factor + half measure
  • Certain disqualifications are rabbinic decrees (gezeirot) — not piggul proper
  • No general rule permits combining intents across separate rites

🏛️ Rabbinic Decrees — Disqualification by Association

Rabbi Yosei's Decree (13a): Intent to burn the handful of frankincense the next day disqualifies the meal offering — by analogy to intent regarding the handful of flour itself
↓
Rabbis' Decree (shewbread): Burning one bowl of frankincense with intent to eat both arrangements of shewbread the next day disqualifies — by decree based on the parallel case of the other bowl
↓
Two Lambs/Two Loaves (16a): Slaughtering one lamb with intent to consume both loaves disqualifies them — rabbinic decree extending from the parallel piggul case
↓
Abaye's Key Point: All these are gezeirot — extensions from similar cases. There is no underlying rule that half-intents across two separate rites combine to create piggul
↓
Proof from Mishna: The mishna calls these offerings piggul (not merely disqualified) — confirming the Rabbis extended the piggul label by decree, based on their resemblance to genuine piggul cases

📋 Keritot 14 — Summary Map

Question: Are the two thighs / two loaves one body or two separate bodies?
↓
Rav Huna: Two bodies → piggul in one does not spread to the other
Rabbi Yoḥanan (accepted): One body → piggul spreads; but separating intent keeps them distinct
↓
Practical Rule (Rabbi Yosei via Rabbi Yoḥanan): Mixed intent (olive-bulk across both loaves) = both piggul. Separated intent (half from each loaf independently) = neither piggul
↓
Rites Do Not Combine: Intent during slaughter + intent during sprinkling do not combine for piggul — each rite stands alone (Levi; Abaye)
