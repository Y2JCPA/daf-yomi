5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
Whose eyes streamed with tears in the story of Yosef the Babylonian, and why?
A. Yosef wept because Rabbi Elazar finally changed his ruling to 'unfit'
B. Rabbi Elazar wept with joy at Yosef's extraordinary dedication to the correct Torah ruling
C. Yosef wept because the offering was ultimately ruled unfit
D. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi wept upon hearing the account of their dispute
Correct: B — Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua's eyes streamed with tears — moved by how persistently Yosef pressed him to restore the correct ruling, he praised Torah scholars for their immense love of Torah.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to the Gemara's interpretation, what does the Mishna mean when it says 'if one did not pour the oil, the meal offering is fit'?
A. No oil was poured at all, yet the offering remains valid
B. A non-priest performed the pouring instead of a priest
C. The oil was poured after the waving rather than before
D. The oil was poured in insufficient quantity
Correct: B — The Gemara concludes the Mishna cannot mean no oil was poured at all (the Torah requires it). It must refer to a case where a non-priest performed the pouring — that is what the Mishna rules as fit.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Rabbi Zeira's explanation for why omitting the mixing of oil does not invalidate the meal offering?
A. Mixing is only a rabbinic ordinance, not biblical
B. For any flour measure suitable for mixing, the failure to mix is a missed mitzva but does not invalidate
C. The handful removal atones retroactively for any omitted preparatory rite
D. Mixing is only required for the sota meal offering, not others
Correct: B — Rabbi Zeira rules: for any flour measure that is suitable for mixing with oil, the failure to mix is a missed positive commandment but does not render the offering invalid. The mitzva and the indispensability are two separate questions.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rav Naḥman resolve the contradiction between the Mishna (non-priest pouring oil → fit) and the baraita (suggests it is not valid)?
A. The Mishna is a minority opinion; the baraita represents the accepted halakha
B. The Mishna refers to an Israelite's meal offering; the baraita refers to a priest's meal offering
C. The Mishna addresses intentional omission; the baraita addresses accidental omission
D. The Mishna applies before consecration; the baraita applies after
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman distinguishes the cases: the Mishna discusses an Israelite's meal offering (from which a handful is taken), while the baraita discusses a priest's meal offering (entirely burned on the altar, making each rite more integral).
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to the baraita on 18b, what is the consequence for a priest who does not admit to the validity of the meal offering rites?
A. He is disqualified from performing any Temple service permanently
B. He does not receive a share in the priestly gifts from that offering
C. His performed rites are retroactively invalid
D. He must bring a guilt offering for his denial
Correct: B — The baraita derives from the phrase 'among the sons of Aaron' that any priest who does not admit to the validity of the sacrificial rites entrusted to the sons of Aaron does not receive a portion in the priestly gifts — he forfeits his share, not his eligibility to serve.
