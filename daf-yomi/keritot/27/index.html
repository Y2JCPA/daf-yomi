🔗 The Principle of Ikuv (Indispensability)

מעכב — כל חלק מעכב את חבירו
Core Theme Menachot 27 systematically develops the halakha of ikuv — when an absent or deficient part of a mitzva invalidates the rest. The mishna establishes that for meal offerings, libations, and all paired Temple items, the absence of even a minority portion disqualifies the majority. This principle extends to pairs: two Yom Kippur goats, two Shavuot sheep, two shewbread arrangements, and — debated — the four lulav species. The daf concludes with a dispute about whether entering the Sanctuary and Holy of Holies carry different punishments.

  • Source: 'The priest shall take' implies full measure
  • Applies to the flour portion of the meal offering

Wine Libation (Nesech)

  • Source: 'So shall it be done' — complete act required

🌿 Must the Four Lulav Species Be Physically Bound?

Rav Ḥanan bar Rava
The mishna's rule that each lulav species prevents the others applies only when one lacks a species. If all four are present, they need not be physically bound together to fulfill the mitzva.
Baraita (Objection)
The four species — lulav, etrog, myrtle, willow — are bound together. Those that produce fruit bond with those that do not, teaching mutual unity. So too Israel must be bound together in repentance (Amos 9:6).
Rabbi Yehuda
Binding is required! Via gezerah shavah: 'taking' (לקיחה) of the lulav is compared to 'taking' of the hyssop bundle at Passover (Ex. 12:22) — just as hyssop must be bundled, so must the four species be bound.
Rabbis
Binding is not required — only preferred as hiddur mitzva (beautification), based on 'This is my God and I will beautify Him' (Ex. 15:2). A lulav, bound or unbound, is fit. The gezerah shavah is rejected.
Conclusion The baraita 'there is a mitzva to bind, but if one did not bind it is fit' follows the Rabbis — binding is hiddur mitzva, not l'ikuva (not indispensable).

⚠️ Entering the Holy of Holies — Two Punishments or One?

Rabbi Yehuda
Two distinct prohibitions: (1) Entering the Sanctuary (Heikhal) = lashes. (2) Entering within the Curtain (Holy of Holies) = death at the hand of Heaven. The verse 'he shall not come at all times... within the Curtain' (Lev. 16:2) adds a separate, stricter prohibition.
Rabbis
One prohibition, one punishment: death at the hand of Heaven for both Sanctuary and Holy of Holies. 'Within the Curtain' is not a separate prohibition — it describes the location of the Ark Cover for Yom Kippur sprinkling. The Merciful One wrote it to exclude entering by a roundabout path.
School of R. Eliezer ben Ya'akov
Paradigm: Wherever Torah says el penei ('before'), it means directly facing — not via a roundabout path. Applied to: 'he shall sprinkle before [el penei] the Ark Cover to the east' (Lev. 16:14).
Rabba bar Ulla
'He shall make atonement for the mikdash hakodesh' (Lev. 16:33) — interpreted: atone for the Sanctuary, meaning the sprinkling must be effective even if not perfectly aimed at the Ark Cover.
Key Textual Dispute Does 'within the Curtain' (Lev. 16:2) create a new, stricter prohibition (R. Yehuda) — or is it merely descriptive, telling us where blood is sprinkled on Yom Kippur (Rabbis)?
