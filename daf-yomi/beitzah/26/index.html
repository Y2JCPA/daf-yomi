⚖️ Core Dispute: Rabbi Yehuda vs Rabbi Shimon

Rabbi Yehuda
One may examine blemishes on a Festival. If an expert descends into the cistern and finds a permanent blemish, the firstborn may be slaughtered and eaten on Yom Tov.
Rabbi Shimon
Even if the animal now has a permanent blemish, it may not be slaughtered. It was not prepared before the Festival — it is muktze and remains forbidden.
Gemara
The cistern case was necessary to teach that even tzaar ba'alei chayyim (animal suffering) does not permit using an artifice to circumvent the muktze prohibition.
Resolution
The mishna addresses a case where the animal had a temporary blemish on erev Yom Tov, then fell and gained a permanent blemish — lest one think the mind was already set on it for slaughter.
Key Distinction The debate concerns not just blemish examination but whether animal suffering can override muktze — Rabbi Shimon says it cannot

🔍 Three Cases: When Did the Blemish Appear?

Born with Blemish

  • Animal blemished from birth — never fully sacred
  • Never had status of an unblemished firstborn
  • Rabba bar Rav Huna: experts may examine ab initio on Festival
  • Rav Nachman disputes — Abaye supports Rabba bar Rav Huna from 3-part baraita structure
  • Rav Oshaya's baraita: same leniency as erev Yom Tov case

Blemish on Erev Yom Tov

  • Blemish appeared before Festival began
  • Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: expert may examine on Yom Tov itself
  • If permanent blemish confirmed → may slaughter
  • Animal was notionally 'prepared' before Yom Tov onset
  • Middle ground between born-blemish and Festival-blemish

Blemish on Yom Tov Itself

  • Blemish appeared during the Festival
  • Rabbi Shimon: not considered prepared — forbidden even b'diavad
  • Animal lacked slaughter-eligibility at Yom Tov onset
  • Most stringent case — all opinions agree it is forbidden
  • Adda bar Ukhmei confused this ruling in a transmitted baraita

🤔 Hillel's Dilemma: Muktze for Half of Shabbat?

The Question (Hillel before Rava): Is there muktze for half of Shabbat? — i.e., if an item was fit, became unfit mid-Shabbat, then became fit again
↓
Rava's initial view: There IS muktze for half of Shabbat — once an item loses fitness during Shabbat, it remains forbidden even after recovering
↓
Hillel's objection: The baraita says a firstborn born with a blemish is 'prepared' — implying restored fitness removes the muktze prohibition
↓
Resolution (Abaye / Rav Safra): Judges sat watching at the birth and immediately saw the blemish — the animal never lost its eligible status, so no muktze was ever triggered
↓
Alternative Rava: There is NO muktze for half of Shabbat — once fit again it is fully permitted
↓
Counter-proof rejected: The baraita about born-blemished firstborns does not resolve the question, since judges witnessed the blemish immediately — a special circumstance

📜 Adda bar Ukhmei: A Confused Tradent

ההוא אדא בר אוכמי קתני לה, דמחלף ליה
Historical Note — Textual Criticism When two baraitot flatly contradict each other on blemish-examination rules, the Gemara resolves the conflict not through legal reasoning but through source criticism: one baraita was transmitted by Adda bar Ukhmei, who was known to regularly transpose the opinions of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon.

This illustrates a key Talmudic method: apparent contradictions between tannaitic sources may reflect unreliable transmission rather than genuine legal dispute — the identity of the tradent matters as much as the ruling itself.
