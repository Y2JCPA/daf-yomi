QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav, if one dammed a water channel on the eve of a Festival and found fish the next day, what determines whether the fish are permitted on Yom Tov?
A. Whether the fish were designated by name before Yom Tov
B. Whether the fish came from within the Shabbat/Yom Tov limit or from beyond it
C. Whether the fish were caught before or after sunrise on Yom Tov
D. Whether a rabbi inspected and approved them first
Correct: B — Rav rules that fish from within the Shabbat/Yom Tov limit are permitted — the owner's preparatory act (damming) applies to them. Fish that came from outside the limit are prohibited, as they were not within range on the eve of the Festival.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak reconcile the two contradictory baraitot about wild animals nesting in orchards?
A. One baraita discusses young animals; the other discusses adults capable of escape
B. One baraita applies to Shabbat; the other applies only to Yom Tov
C. Both discuss young animals unable to escape — but one involves a gentile-owned orchard, the other a Jewish-owned orchard
D. One baraita is lenient because it addresses animals already in a pen, not an open orchard
Correct: C — Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak resolves the contradiction by noting both baraitot involve young animals that cannot escape. The distinction: a gentile-owned orchard requires no designation (the gentile has no Yom Tov obligations), while a Jewish-owned orchard requires formal hachana.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
The Mishna on Daf 25 rules that if one slaughters an animal in the field on Yom Tov, it may not be brought home on a pole. What is the reason for this restriction?
A. Carrying a whole carcass involves forbidden labor (melacha) on Yom Tov
B. It appears like a weekday butcher's activity (uvdin d'chol) and violates the spirit of Yom Tov
C. The animal might not be fully dead, making transport dangerous
D. Poles are set aside (muktzeh) and may not be used on Yom Tov
Correct: B — The prohibition is due to appearance — carrying meat on a professional butcher's pole mimics weekday commerce and labor. The solution is a shinui (alteration): carry it limb by limb on one's shoulder, which signals the Yom Tov context.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What does Rav Huna rule regarding the presumptive status (chazakah) of an animal after it has been properly slaughtered?
A. It remains prohibited until a rabbi examines it for treifot
B. It retains its pre-slaughter forbidden status unless explicitly declared fit
C. After proper slaughter it is presumed permitted — no routine examination for treifot is required
D. Its status depends on whether it was slaughtered in the field or at home
Correct: C — Rav Huna rules that once an animal is properly slaughtered, it acquires a presumptive status of being permitted (mutar b'chezkat hetter). No automatic inspection for internal defects (treifot) is required — only if a specific problem is known does one need to investigate.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Under what circumstance did Rav Naḥman permit his wife Yalta to be carried in a chair on poles on Yom Tov, distinguishing her case from the general rule?
A. Because she was a woman and women are exempt from such Yom Tov restrictions
B. Because Rav Naḥman was a posek hador and his household had recognized leniencies
C. Because she was afraid of falling, making the supported chair a personal safety necessity
D. Because the chair did not rest on the bearers' shoulders but was low to the ground
Correct: C — The Gemara explains that Yalta was an exceptional case: she was afraid of falling, which made the chair-on-poles a personal medical/safety need. This distinguishes her from the general case, where such carrying is prohibited as it resembles weekday transport.
