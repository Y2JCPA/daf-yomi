🦌 Wild Animal in Orchard: Designation Required?

Rav Ḥisda
From Rav's ruling about dammed fish, we learn: an undomesticated animal that nested in an orchard near one's house does not require special designation — it is already considered prepared for the Festival!
Rav Naḥman
The cases are different! The fish are permitted because the person performed a preparatory action (damming the channel). But an animal that wandered into an orchard on its own — the owner did nothing at all!
Rav Naḥman
Moreover, a baraita explicitly states: 'A wild animal nesting in an orchard requires designation. A free bird must be tied in advance.' — and it does not require designation?!
Gemara
But Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's baraita implies orchard animals are permitted without designation! How do we reconcile these two contradictory baraitot?
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
Both baraitot discuss young animals unable to escape. The key difference: no designation needed = orchard owned by a gentile. Designation required = orchard owned by a Jew.
Key Principle A preparatory action performed on the eve of Yom Tov creates permitted status. Passive acquisition — where no action was taken — requires formal designation (הכנה) before the Festival.

↓
Next day (Yom Tov): fish are found trapped in the dammed channel
Fish came from within the Shabbat limit
✅ Permitted

Fish came from outside the Shabbat limit
❌ Prohibited
They were not within the permitted range on the eve of the Festival — no designation applies

🦌 Reconciling Two Baraitot on Orchard Animals

Baraita A — No Designation Needed

  • Young animal that cannot escape
  • Orchard owned by a gentile
  • Gentile is not subject to Yom Tov restrictions
  • Animal is considered automatically prepared
  • → Permitted without prior designation

Baraita B — Designation Required

  • Young animal that cannot escape
  • Orchard owned by a Jew
  • Owner is bound by Yom Tov laws
  • Animal must be verbally/mentally designated before Yom Tov
  • → Requires formal הכנה (hachana)

🔪 Slaughtering an Animal in the Field on Yom Tov

Situation: Animal is in danger of dying — owner slaughters it to avoid a total loss and benefit from the meat
↓
Permitted: Slaughter itself is allowed on Yom Tov since it is a need (ochel nefesh — food preparation)
↓
Prohibited manner: Cannot carry the carcass home on a pole (מוט) or on a set of poles carried by two people — this appears like a weekday butcher's work (uvdin d'chol)
↓
Required alteration (שינוי): Must carry limb by limb on one's shoulder — deviating from professional weekday manner signals the sanctity of Yom Tov
↓
Rami bar Abba's ruling: Torah law of the burnt-offering teaches proper butchering etiquette (starting from legs and hide) — this applies as a matter of proper conduct, not prohibition

🪑 Chair on Poles + Impudence of Israel

עַזִּין שֶׁבָּאֻמּוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל — Israel is the most brazen among the nations (Beitzah 25b)
Two Aggadic Teachings from Beitzah 25b Carrying in a Chair on Yom Tov:
A baraita prohibits a blind person from going out on a Festival with a guide's stick (resembles weekday). Litter/chair carried on poles was similarly debated.

• Rav Naḥman permitted his wife Yalta to go out in a chair — she feared falling, making it a personal safety need.
• Ameimar and Mar Zutra were carried to the study hall on students' shoulders on Shabbat Chol HaMoed — permitted due to fear of the crowd.
• Final ruling: a chair borne on poles is generally permitted on Yom Tov.

Impudence of the Jewish People (Rabbi Meir):
'Why was Torah given to Israel? Because they are impudent — Torah study weakens and softens their insolence.'

Resh Lakish: Three are most impudent — Israel among nations, the dog among animals, the rooster among birds.
