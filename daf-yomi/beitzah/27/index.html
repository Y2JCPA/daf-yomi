🍲 Is Food Being Cooked Considered Muktze?

Anonymous View
Food being cooked is temporarily inedible — it should be muktze during that time!
Abaye
But cooked dishes on Shabbat are still bubbling at twilight — by that logic, all pots on Shabbat would be muktze!
Resolution
No problem with foods whose completion is entirely in human hands — e.g., beans and lentils. The person controls when they become edible, so they are never muktze at all.
Principle When a person fully controls when food becomes edible (removing it from heat), its temporarily inedible state does not render it muktze.

🔍 Rabbi Meir vs. Rabbi Yehuda: Blemish Examination on a Festival

Rabbi Yehuda

  • Eye/mouth blemishes: PROHIBITED on Festival
  • Appearance may change after death — unreliable post-mortem
  • Body blemishes (ear, foreleg): PERMITTED to examine
  • These are prominent and do not change — no special expertise needed
  • Distinguishes between blemish types based on reliability

Rabbi Meir

  • Eye/mouth blemishes: PROHIBITED on Festival
  • Body blemishes: ALSO PROHIBITED
  • Decree: prominent blemishes follow the rules of non-prominent ones
  • Without Sage's prior permit, slaughter is invalid after the fact
  • Uniformly strict — no distinction between blemish types

קהל קדוש שבירושלים — The Holy Community of Jerusalem
🏛️ The Holy Community of Jerusalem Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya and his colleagues ruled that the halakha follows Rabbi Meir: examining a tereifa (clarifying physical facts) is permitted on a Festival, but formal blemish examination is not. The Gemara wonders — how could they rule about Sages far older and more senior than themselves? Rabbi Zeira adds: since the mishna specifies blemishes not perceptible before Shabbat begin, this implies visible blemishes are permitted — supporting a leniency.
