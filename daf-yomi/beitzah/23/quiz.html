QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why did Rabba prohibit placing spices on hot earthenware, even though there is no concern of kindling or extinguishing?
A. The spices might catch fire and spread to the earthenware
B. It creates a new scent in the earthenware, violating the rule against creating new things on Yom Tov
C. It wastes food, which is an unnecessary act on a Festival
D. Hot earthenware is considered muktzeh and may not be used
Correct: B — Rabba held that placing spices on hot earthenware produces a new scent absorbed into the vessel itself — and creating new things on a Festival is prohibited as a form of forbidden labor.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
The Gemara questions: 'Is it to say R. Elazar ben Azarya had only one cow?' What resolves this contradiction with his known wealth?
A. He had sold all his cows and kept only one for personal use
B. The mishna refers to a neighbor's cow that went out; R. Elazar did not protest, implying he ruled it permitted
C. He dedicated all but one cow to the Temple treasury
D. The 12,000 calves were goats, not cows, so there is no contradiction
Correct: B — The Gemara resolves that it was actually a neighbor's cow — R. Elazar ben Azarya saw it and did not object, effectively ruling the practice permitted. His silence was itself a halakhic ruling.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to the Gemara's conclusion (Rav Naḥman citing Shmuel), whose opinion does the halakha follow regarding unintentional acts on Shabbat?
A. Rabbi Yehuda — unintentional acts are prohibited because the outcome is inevitable
B. The Rabbis — we decree against similar-looking acts as well
C. Rabbi Shimon — unintentional acts, where the prohibited labor is not intended, are permitted
D. Rabba — each case must be evaluated on its own merits
Correct: C — Rav Naḥman citing Shmuel rules that the halakha follows Rabbi Shimon: an unintentional act (פסיק רישא דלא ניחא ליה in later usage, but here simply a non-intended outcome) is permitted on Shabbat.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why may one take animals from enclosures (beivarim) on Yom Tov, but NOT trap fish from ponds?
A. Animals are permitted for Yom Tov meals; fish require salting which is forbidden
B. Animals in enclosures are already considered captured; fish in ponds can still escape and are not pre-caught
C. The Torah only explicitly prohibits fishing on Yom Tov, while land-animal trapping is rabbinic
D. Fish are muktzeh; penned animals are not
Correct: B — The key distinction is 'already captured.' Animals in a proper enclosure cannot escape — taking them involves no hunting act. Fish in a pond can still flee, so catching them is genuine hunting, a forbidden Yom Tov labor.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why is the pepper mill susceptible to ritual impurity under three separate categories?
A. It was used for three different prohibited activities and each use created a new category of impurity
B. Three different rabbis issued separate decrees of impurity for different parts of its construction
C. Each of its three sections — receptacle, sieve, and general vessel — independently qualifies for its own impurity category
D. It is made of three different materials, each susceptible to a different type of impurity
Correct: C — The lower section collects pepper (receptacle = בית קיבול), the middle section filters it (sieve function), and the upper section is itself a complete vessel. Each part qualifies independently — the mill is susceptible to impurity three times over.
