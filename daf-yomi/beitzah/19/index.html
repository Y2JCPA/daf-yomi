⚖️ Beit Shammai vs. Beit Hillel: Festival Offerings

Beit Shammai
On a Festival: peace-offerings may be brought, but one may not lay hands on them (semikha = using animals, forbidden). Burnt-offerings may not be brought at all.
Beit Hillel
On a Festival: both peace-offerings and burnt-offerings may be brought, and one may lay hands on them — semikha is permitted for Festival-obligated offerings.
Ulla
The dispute applies only to Festival peace-offerings (re: semikha) and burnt-offerings of appearance. Vow-offerings and gift-offerings — all agree they may not be brought on the Festival day itself.
Rav Adda bar Ahava
Vow-offerings and gift-offerings are unanimously forbidden on the Festival. They are not part of the Festival's obligations and thus cannot override the holiday restrictions.
Core Principle Festival-obligated offerings may override certain restrictions (Beit Hillel: including semikha). Optional offerings — vows, gifts — may not override any Festival restriction. All agree on this boundary.

🌅 Does a Person Running to the Mikveh Know About Sunset?

Rabbis
If someone runs to the mikveh at twilight, his urgency proves he knows sunset is required. He will not mistakenly use the vessel before sunset. Twilight immersion is permitted on a weekday.
Rabbi Shimon Shezuri
Even on a weekday, twilight immersion is forbidden. He may be running because he is late from work — not because he understands the sunset requirement. He may mistakenly use the vessel too early!
Rava
Both agree that intent inferred from action (umdena) is a valid principle. Their real dispute: a vessel impure via midras, where the reason for running is genuinely ambiguous.
Rava's Resolution The principle of inferring intent from observable behavior (umdena) is universally accepted. Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon Shezuri disagree only where the person's motivation is unclear — e.g., midras impurity, where sunset requirement is less obvious.

🔄 Immersing 'From One Principle to Another'

Original intent: Immerse vessel to use with terumah (one level of purity)
↓
After immersion, he decides to use the vessel for kodashim — a higher purity level
↓
Ruling: He may re-immerse on the Festival for this higher purpose — it constitutes a genuine Festival need
↓
Pitcher example: Intended for winepress → redirected to another use → may immerse for the added purpose
↓
Group switching: Was eating Korban Pesach with Group A → switches to Group B → may re-immerse for the new group
↓
Key principle: Immersion on the Festival is justified only when there is a genuine, changed Festival need — not a pretext

📜 Which Offerings May Be Brought on a Festival?

✅ Permitted (all agree)

  • Festival peace-offerings (שלמי שמחה) — Beit Hillel: with semikha
  • Burnt-offering of appearance (עולת ראייה) — Beit Hillel: permitted
  • Thanks-offering on intermediate days of Sukkot (Abaye: all agree)
  • Any obligatory Festival offering — may be brought on the Festival

❌ Forbidden (all agree)

  • Vow-offerings (נדרים) — not Festival-obligated, forbidden on Festival day
  • Gift-offerings (נדבות) — not Festival-obligated, forbidden on Festival day
  • Thanks-offering on Pesach (requires leavened bread — impossible during Pesach)
  • Thanks-offering on Shavuot — per tanna who holds vows forbidden on Festivals

⚖️ Disputed

  • Semikha on Festival peace-offerings: Shammai forbids; Hillel permits
  • Burnt-offering of appearance: Shammai forbids; Hillel permits
  • Thanks-offering on Sukkot Festival day: Rabbi Elazar b. R. Shimon permits; others restrict to intermediate days
  • Whether Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's baraita reflects a unanimous or minority view
