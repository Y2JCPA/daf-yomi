⚖️ Weighing Portion Against Portion — Permitted?

Rabbi Yehoshua
One may weigh one portion against another on a Festival — just as we find regarding firstborn animals and consecrated property, where one portion may be weighed against another.
Abaye
Not necessarily! Perhaps Rabbi Yehoshua only permitted it on a Festival where it doesn't look like commerce — but in the case of consecrated animals he might forbid it. Or the Rabbis permitted it only for consecrated animals because it doesn't look commercial, but not on a Festival where it does look commercial!
Rav Pappa
In the incident with Rabbi Ḥiyya and Rabbi Shimon, a third party must have been present to justify the need for precision. Either it was Rabbi Ḥiyya with Rabbi Yishmael son of Rabbi Yosei, or Rabbi Shimon with another.
Bottom Line Weighing portion-against-portion on a Festival follows Rabbi Yehoshua — but the exact scope of the permission remains debated.

🍖 Acts with Meat and Utensils — Permitted vs. Prohibited

✅ Permitted on a Festival

  • Making a sign/mark on meat to identify it (Rav Huna)
  • Making a handle in meat by hand — not with a knife (Ravina)
  • Weighing meat against a vessel (Rabbi Yehuda, Mishna)
  • Sharpening a blunt un-nicked knife if it still cuts somewhat
  • Moving a skewer that still has an olive-bulk of meat on it

❌ Prohibited on a Festival

  • Placing meat on a scale even to protect from mice (Shmuel)
  • Expert hand-weighing — as precise as a scale (Shmuel)
  • Making a handle in meat with a knife — weekday act (Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi)
  • Repairing a bent roasting skewer — fixing utensils is forbidden
  • Moving a skewer with no remaining olive-bulk of meat on it

📜 'For You' — The Verse That Divides the Tannaim

אַךְ אֲשֶׁר יֵאָכֵל לְכָל נֶפֶשׁ הוּא לְבַדּוֹ יֵעָשֶׂה לָכֶם — שמות יב:טז
Exodus 12:16 "Save that which every person must eat — that alone may be done for you"

First Tanna: "That alone" limits Festival labor strictly to direct food preparation — preparatory acts like fixing skewers are excluded. "For you" teaches: for Jews, not gentiles.

Rabbi Yehuda: "That alone" limits (not for gentiles) AND "for you" expands — even indirect preparatory acts that enable food preparation are permitted on a Festival.

Result: Rabbi Yehuda permits repair of a roasting skewer because it enables food preparation — the first tanna does not.
