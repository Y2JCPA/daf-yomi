🥚 The Consecutive Holy Days Problem

Shabbat and Yom Tov fall on consecutive days
↓
A chicken lays an egg on one of the days — may it be eaten on the other?
↓
Rav
Egg is prohibited on the other day — each day is a separate sanctity; no cross-day preparation
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Egg is permitted on the other day — the two days form one extended sanctity

⚖️ Untithed Figs: Can Vessel Mouths Combine for Nullification?

R. Eliezer
The upper circles of untithed figs are treated as separate pieces. One hundred permitted mouths do nullify one prohibited mouth — they combine into a single mass.
R. Yehoshua
Even 100 mouths of different barrels cannot nullify one prohibited litra. Each vessel mouth is a distinct unit — they do not combine for nullification purposes.
R. Yehuda
Transmits a variant of R. Eliezer: If 100 permitted mouths are present in addition to the prohibited one, it is nullified. If not — it is not nullified.
Gemara Resolution If the prohibited litra was pressed into one vessel but it is unknown which one, all agree it is nullified — Rav Pappa clarifies this means the side of a single vessel is uncertain, not which vessel among many.

🗣️ Aḥerim's Ruling and the Concept of Guzma

גּוּזְמָא הוּא — It is an exaggeration (Beitzah 4a)
Aḥerim in the name of Rabbi Eliezer An egg laid on a Festival — it and its mother may be eaten.

The Gemara asks: in what case? Abaye explains: a chicken bought without specifying egg-use or eating — since it could be slaughtered, it is "prepared" as food, and its egg is also permitted.

Rav Mari objects: the phrase is a guzma (גּוּזְמָא) — a rabbinic exaggeration for emphasis. It does not literally mean the mother is eaten simultaneously. Proof: another baraita says "a chick and its shell may be eaten" — but a shell is inedible! Thus the expression overstates to stress permissibility.

📅 Egg on Consecutive Shabbat and Yom Tov: Rav vs. Rabbi Yoḥanan

Rav — Prohibited

  • Egg laid on this holy day is forbidden on the adjacent one
  • Follows R. Eliezer: consecutive days are two distinct sanctities
  • Ruled in accord with four elders who agreed with R. Eliezer
  • Holds that Rabba's preparation (muchan me'elav) is prohibited — an egg "prepared itself" on Day 1 cannot be used on Day 2
  • Rav Adda bar Ahava's host confirmed: Rav's view means the egg is prohibited

Rabbi Yoḥanan — Permitted

  • Egg laid on this holy day is permitted on the adjacent one
  • Holds the two consecutive days form one extended sanctity
  • Rejects Rabba's preparation as a binding prohibition
  • Supported by a baraita: an egg laid on Shabbat may be eaten on a Festival
  • Same baraita also states: one may not move such an egg to cover a vessel

🌍 Why Two Diaspora Festival Days? Rav vs. Rav Asi

We observe two days of Yom Tov in the Diaspora — but why?
↓
Rav — Uncertainty
Originally, messengers couldn't reach all communities in time → two days arose from calendar doubt. If certainty were restored, one day would suffice.
Rav Asi — Fixed Decree
The Sages made the second day a permanent ordinance — one sanctity — regardless of whether the calendar is now known.
↓
Abaye supports Rav: Samaritans disrupted fire-signal system → Sages switched to messengers → second day born from genuine doubt. Had interference ceased, one day would have been restored.
↓
Sent from Eretz Yisrael: "Preserve your ancestral custom" — even though the calendar is now fixed by calculation, the two-day practice continues as an irrevocable tradition.

📋 Beitzah 4 — Summary Map

Starting point: An egg laid on a holy day is muktzeh — not prepared before the day; it "came into the world" on the holy day itself
↓
Two Sanctities
Rav, R. Eliezer, four elders
Each day stands alone → egg on Day 1 is prohibited on Day 2
One Sanctity
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Days form a continuum → egg on Day 1 is permitted on Day 2
↓
Rosh HaShana exception: Uniquely, both Rav and Shmuel rule that an egg laid on either of the two days of Rosh HaShana is prohibited on the other — the two days have a special combined stringency
↓
Diaspora resolution: Rav Asi performed havdala between the days (implying two sanctities) but was uncertain whether the decree was permanent or doubt-driven. The ruling from Eretz Yisrael: "preserve your ancestral custom" — maintain both days regardless
