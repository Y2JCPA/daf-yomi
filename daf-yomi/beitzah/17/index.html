🍳 Eiruv Tavshilin: Gateway to Cooking for Shabbat

Festival falls on Thursday–Friday (Diaspora) or Friday (Israel)
↓
Eiruv prepared before Festival
May bake, cook, and insulate on Festival for Shabbat
Forgot eiruv
May not bake, cook, or insulate for Shabbat — food is prohibited
↓
Rava's solution: Place eiruv on first Festival day with a conditional stipulation covering the second day

🙏 Festival on Shabbat: How Many Amida Blessings?

Beit Shammai
Recite 8 blessings — the standard 7 of Shabbat plus a distinct Festival blessing inserted between the third and concluding blessings
Beit Hillel
Recite only 7 blessings — merge the Shabbat and Festival themes into a single middle blessing, concluding with one unified formula
Tanna before Ravina
He concludes the blessing with: מְקַדֵּשׁ יִשְׂרָאֵל וְהַשַּׁבָּת וְהַזְּמַנִּים — 'Who sanctifies Israel, Shabbat, and the Seasons'
Ravina
Does Israel sanctify Shabbat?! Rather: מְקַדֵּשׁ הַשַּׁבָּת וְיִשְׂרָאֵל וְהַזְּמַנִּים — Israel sanctifies Festivals (by setting the calendar), but God alone sanctifies Shabbat!
Halakha Beit Hillel: 7 blessings, concluding 'Who sanctifies Shabbat, Israel, and the Seasons'

🍳 How Eiruv Tavshilin Works

Before the Festival: Set aside bread and a cooked dish as the eiruv tavshilin — symbolizing cooking 'begun' before the Festival
↓
This preparation creates a legal fiction permitting further cooking on the Festival for Shabbat
↓
On the Festival: One may now bake, cook, and insulate food intended for Shabbat
↓
Rava's conditional formula: 'If today is a weekday and tomorrow is Festival — this is my eiruv; if today is Festival and tomorrow is Shabbat — this eiruv takes effect for tomorrow'
↓
Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi (Rav): Forgot before a Thursday–Friday Festival? Place eiruv on the first Festival day for the second day
↓
Logical inference: If eiruv for Shabbat boundaries may be placed on a Festival day, eiruv for cooked foods — all the more so

🍞 Baking on Festival: Permitted vs. Prohibited

✓ Permitted

  • A woman may fill the entire oven with bread — bread bakes better when the oven is full
  • Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar: extra bread in a full oven is permitted even if not all needed that day
  • Cooking for the Festival itself is always permitted; leftovers may be eaten on Shabbat
  • Cooking for Festival + leaving over for Shabbat — permitted, so long as no explicit artifice is declared

✗ Prohibited

  • Baking on the first Festival day explicitly and solely for the second Festival day
  • Employing artifice — saying 'I cook for today' while intending to set aside for Shabbat
  • Cooking on the Festival for Shabbat without having placed eiruv tavshilin beforehand
  • Sages were stricter about artifice than about intentional violation

⚖️ Transgressed and Cooked Without Eiruv — May He Eat?

One cooked on Festival for Shabbat without eiruv tavshilin
↓
Strict view
Food is forbidden — like intentional Shabbat violation; Sages penalized the transgressor
Lenient view
Food is permitted — the act was prohibited, but the food itself is not rendered forbidden
↓
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak: The strict baraitot follow the view of Ḥananya and Beit Shammai — not normative halakha
↓
Analogy from Shabbat: One who cooked on Shabbat intentionally — may not eat; unwittingly — may eat. A Festival violation is less severe than Shabbat.
