QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabba, why is an egg laid on one day of Rosh HaShana permitted on the other day after Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's ordinance?
A. A formal vote was taken by the Sages to explicitly permit eggs
B. The ordinance allowed witnesses to testify all day, removing the uncertainty that required two-day Rosh HaShana
C. Rav and Shmuel ruled that eggs are always permitted after the Temple's destruction
D. The egg prohibition was Biblical and Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai had authority to override Biblical law
Correct: B — Rabba reasons that Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's ordinance — allowing witnesses to testify throughout the day — removed the original uncertainty about which day was truly Rosh HaShana, thereby eliminating the cause of the egg prohibition.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does Rav Yosef prove from the Torah that a decree does not automatically lapse when its reason disappears?
A. From the case of the fourth-year vine near Lod requiring a formal court vote
B. From the prohibition to approach Mount Sinai, which needed the explicit divine command 'Return to your tents' before it lapsed
C. From Rava's ruling that halakha follows Rav in all three related cases
D. From the Levites who sang the wrong song because the law was unclear
Correct: B — Rav Yosef cites Deuteronomy 5:26 ('Return to your tents'), showing that Mount Sinai's prohibition — obviously inapplicable after the revelation ended — still required an explicit divine command to lapse. A decree never ends on its own.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Rava's unique argument that the egg remains prohibited even after Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's ordinance?
A. Rav and Shmuel are authorities whose ruling cannot be questioned by later Amoraim
B. Even Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai concedes that when witnesses arrive after mincha, both days remain fully sacred — proving two-day Rosh HaShana was never abolished
C. The Sages voted specifically to keep the egg prohibition as a stringency
D. Since the egg prohibition is Biblical in origin, only a Tanna can permit it
Correct: B — Rava argues that Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai himself acknowledges that witnesses arriving after mincha still render both days sacred. This proves two-day Rosh HaShana was never fully eliminated by his ordinance, so the egg remains prohibited.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What does the story of Rabbi Eliezer ben Hyrcanus's fourth-year vine prove about rabbinic decrees?
A. That fourth-year fruit must always be physically brought to Jerusalem, not redeemed
B. That a rabbinic decree requires an explicit court vote to be revoked, even after its reason no longer applies
C. That a student may not act leniently when his teacher is still alive
D. That the halakha always follows a majority vote among Sages
Correct: B — Although the ordinance requiring fourth-year fruit near Jerusalem to be brought there became unnecessary after Jerusalem was repopulated, it did not lapse on its own. Rabbi Eliezer's colleagues had to formally vote to annul it — demonstrating that decrees need explicit revocation.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why do Rav Adda and Rav Shalman maintain that the egg prohibition should remain even after Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's ordinance?
A. They rule that all decrees made in the Temple era are permanently binding
B. They fear that if the egg is permitted, people will also treat both days of Rosh HaShana as weekdays
C. They hope the Temple will be rebuilt speedily, after which we will need to revert to original practice — changing the egg status now would cause confusion
D. They hold that Rava's argument is conclusive and no other reasoning is necessary
Correct: C — Rav Adda and Rav Shalman reason: may the Temple be speedily rebuilt! When it is, the original two-day Rosh HaShana practice will need to be fully reinstated. Permitting the egg now could cause people to confuse past and future practice when the Temple is restored.
