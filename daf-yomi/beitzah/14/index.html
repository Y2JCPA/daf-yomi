🧂 The Salt Dispute: A Change Is Required

Beit Shammai
Spices: pound with a wooden pestle (slight change). Salt: pound only with an earthenware flask or wooden ladle (major change required).
Beit Hillel
Spices: pound in the usual manner. Salt: pound only with a flask or ladle — a change is still required even for Beit Hillel.
Shmuel
All foods that must be pounded before eating may be pounded in the usual manner — even salt. Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel dispute only whether salt may be pounded by itself in small quantities.
Bottom Line Everyone agrees salt requires some change on Yom Tov; the dispute concerns the degree of change and whether it can be pounded alone.

⚖️ Why Does Salt Require a Change? Two Reasons

Rav Huna's Reason

  • Different spices suit different dishes — you can't know in advance which you'll need
  • Salt is universal — it could always have been prepared the day before
  • Since he neglected to pre-prepare salt, he may only add it with a change
  • Practical difference: If he knew which dish he'd cook, spices that could have been pre-selected may now also require a change

Rav Ḥisda's Reason

  • Spices lose their flavor when ground early — they must be prepared fresh on Yom Tov
  • Salt does not lose flavor — it was always possible to prepare it beforehand
  • The change is a minor penalty for neglecting to pre-prepare
  • Practical difference: Even if he knew which dish he'd cook, salt still requires a change regardless

🌾 Groats (Tisanei) on a Festival

Baraita: One may not prepare tisanei (wheat crushed into quarters) on a Festival, nor grind grain with a mortar and pestle.
↓
Question: Why list both prohibitions? Isn't preparing groats simply a form of grinding?
↓
Answer: Groats require even greater effort than ordinary grinding — they are crushed into quarters. Without the explicit mention, one might think only ordinary grinding is forbidden but groats are permitted.
↓
Contradiction between baraitot: One baraita forbids grinding with a large mortar; another permits grinding with a small mortar.
↓
Rava resolves: Small mortar permitted in Babylonia (no servants to pre-prepare); large mortar forbidden in Eretz Yisrael (servants are available to prepare beforehand).
↓
Story — Rav Pappi: Refused porridge at Mar Shmuel's home on Yom Tov — he saw the husks were bright, indicating the wheat had been pounded that very day.

🫘 Selecting Legumes on Yom Tov

A pile of legumes mixed with refuse — may one sort them on Yom Tov?
↓
Beit Shammai
Select food only — take food and eat it immediately; leave the refuse in place
Beit Hillel
Select in the usual weekday manner — may remove refuse into a basket
↓
Rabban Gamliel clarifies: Beit Hillel's leniency applies only when food quantity exceeds refuse
↓
Food > Refuse
May select in usual manner ✅
Refuse > Food
Entire mixture is muktze — may not be moved at all ❌
↓
Rabban Gamliel's custom: Wash legumes in water — husks float to the top; pebbles and clods sink to the bottom

🧵 The Sha'atnez Problem

וילון של כלאים — מותר לתלותו
Can One Send Sha'atnez (Wool-Linen Mix) on Yom Tov? The mishna permits sending clothes of sha'atnez (prohibited wool-linen mixture, כִּלְאַיִם) if they serve Festival purposes. The Gemara raises a sharp question: how could such a garment be useful? • Sewn clothes: worn directly ✅ • Unsewn cloth: used as a covering ✅ • Sha'atnez: cannot be worn, even briefly — Rabbi Yannai ruled one may not even place it beneath oneself, as the verse states "shall not come upon you" (Lev. 19:19) • Cannot place beneath with a separator — Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi: even a temporary resting on sha'atnez is forbidden Resolution (Rava): The mishna refers to a vilon — a curtain of sha'atnez that may be hung up, since the prohibition applies only to wearing, not to hanging.
