QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Mishna (10a), what does Beit Hillel require in order to take fledgling doves from a dovecote on a Festival?
A. One must physically shake the desired birds before the Festival
B. One must stand and verbally declare 'I will take this one and that one'
C. No preparation is needed; doves are not considered muktze
D. One must mark the birds with a distinguishing sign before Yom Tov
Correct: B — Beit Hillel hold that a verbal declaration of intent ('I will take this one and that one') is sufficient preparation, without any physical act. Physical shaking is the requirement of Beit Shammai.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Rav Ḥanan bar Ami teaches that the dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel applies only to the first brood of doves. What is the ruling regarding the second brood according to both schools?
A. Both schools prohibit taking the second brood entirely, as it is fully muktze
B. Both schools require physical shaking for the second brood
C. Both schools agree the second brood is permitted with only verbal designation
D. The second brood requires no designation at all and may be taken freely
Correct: C — Rav Ḥanan bar Ami explains that the second brood is already 'in the owner's mind' for use, so both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel agree a verbal declaration suffices. The dispute is only about the first brood, where there is more concern the owner may change his mind.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
If one designated three fledglings before Yom Tov and the next day found only two in the nest, what is the ruling?
A. Prohibited — since one is missing, we suspect they are all different birds
B. Permitted — the two found are assumed to be the original ones; one hopped away
C. Prohibited — designation becomes void when the number changes at all
D. Permitted only if one can identify the birds by color or markings
Correct: B — The Gemara rules that the two are permitted, explaining 'these are the same ones, and one has gone to the outside world.' Fledglings are known to hop around, so finding fewer is not alarming — the remaining birds are still the designated ones.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the meaning of Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement 'הפוכי מטרתא' (the attribution of opinions is reversed)?
A. Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel both reversed their positions in later rulings
B. The opinions attributed to Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel in a given source are actually attributed to the wrong school and should be switched
C. The logical flow of the argument should be read from bottom to top
D. One should reverse his halakhic practice during a Festival
Correct: B — When contradictions arise within the rulings of a single school, Rabbi Yoḥanan resolves the problem by suggesting the opinions were mistakenly attributed — that is, what appears as a Beit Shammai ruling is actually Beit Hillel's, and vice versa.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
The Gemara debates whether Beit Hillel accept the principle of ברירה (breirah — retroactive designation). How does Rava resolve Beit Hillel's requirement to specify 'this one and that one' without relying on breirah?
A. Beit Hillel do accept breirah, and the specification is only a custom, not a legal requirement
B. The specification is required because doves belong to the owner's neighbor, not to him
C. The specification is a rabbinic precaution: without it, he might grab a fat bird, find it thin, and thereby move an undesignated (muktze) item
D. Beit Hillel require specification only in the case of doves, not for other muktze items
Correct: C — Rava explains that even if Beit Hillel reject breirah, they still require the specific verbal designation for a practical reason: without knowing exactly which birds he plans to take, he might end up handling birds that are not fit for his purpose, thereby transgressing the prohibition of moving muktze on a Festival.
