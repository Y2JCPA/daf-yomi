🕊️ Taking Fledgling Doves on a Festival

Beit Shammai
One may not take fledgling doves on a Festival unless he physically shook (הניד) the ones he wished to take while still day (erev Yom Tov), thereby preparing them.
Beit Hillel
No physical act is needed. It is sufficient if one stood and said verbally: 'I will take this one and that one' — verbal designation (מיגו דאמר) suffices.
Core Issue Doves are muktze (set aside, not prepared). Both sides agree preparation is required — they dispute whether a physical act or a verbal declaration accomplishes it.

🐣 First Brood vs. Second Brood — Rav Ḥanan bar Ami

First Brood (בכור גדול)

  • First pair of fledglings hatched in the year
  • Typically left in nest — not yet designated for use
  • Owner has not yet committed to taking them
  • Dispute applies here: Beit Shammai requires shaking; Beit Hillel requires verbal declaration
  • Concern: owner might change his mind (Beit Shammai) or not (Beit Hillel)

Second Brood (בכור קטן)

  • Fledglings born in the second cycle
  • Owner has already resolved to use these birds
  • No concern he will reconsider
  • Both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel agree: verbal designation suffices
  • No shaking or elaborate preparation required

🔢 Designated Fledglings Mishna — What If Things Change?

Erev Yom Tov: Owner designates specific fledglings for slaughter on the Festival
↓
Designated black → found white (or vice versa)
Prohibited — clearly different birds; original designation does not apply
Designated 2 → found 3
Prohibited — either the extras are undesignated strangers, or these are all new birds; either way, problem
Designated 3 → found 2
Permitted — these are the same ones; one simply hopped away. The remaining two retain their designation
↓
Found before the nest (not inside): Prohibited — may be outsiders. Found inside with only the designated number: Permitted — assumed to be the same

🔄 Does Beit Hillel Accept Breirah (Retroactive Designation)?

Objection
If Beit Hillel accept breirah (retroactive clarification of intent), why must one specify 'this one and that one'? Let him simply say: 'I will take from this dovecote tomorrow' — and whichever he takes will be retroactively designated!
Rava's Answer
Even Beit Hillel do not rely on breirah here. The reason specification is required is a rabbinic concern: he might pick a fat bird, find it thin, and then move a non-designated (muktze) object — thus the specific verbal designation prevents confusion.
Proof from Corpse Case
A mishna: if there are multiple doorways and one decides which door to remove the corpse through, Beit Hillel purify all other doorways retroactively — suggesting they do accept breirah!
Rabba's Rebuttal
Beit Hillel there only purify the entrances from that point forward (מכאן ולהבא) — not retroactively. No proof for breirah.
Bottom Line The daf leaves unresolved whether Beit Hillel truly accept ברירה. Rava's approach avoids the question by grounding Beit Hillel's requirement in a practical rabbinic concern about muktze.

🔁 Rabbi Yoḥanan's Reversal — Resolving Beit Shammai Contradictions

Problem: Beit Shammai appear lenient about Festival rejoicing (שמחת יום טוב) in the slaughtering case (allowing a hide before treaders), yet stringent in the store-shutters case — a contradiction within Beit Shammai!
↓
Initial Attempt: Distinguish based on whether a shovel was embedded before the Festival (מוכן): Beit Shammai lenient only when already prepared. But the Gemara rejects each attempt: 'From where do you know this?'
↓
Rabbi Yoḥanan's Bold Resolution: הפוכי מטרתא — the attribution of opinions is reversed. The lenient opinion in the slaughtering case is actually Beit Hillel, and the stringent opinion is Beit Shammai.
↓
Alternative Answer (without reversing): Beit Hillel state their lenient ruling only in the specific context discussed, but would agree with Beit Shammai's stringency in other contexts. The rulings are not contradictory — they address different scenarios.
↓
General Pattern: When Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel appear to trade positions, the Gemara first seeks a contextual distinction. Only when that fails does it resort to Rabbi Yoḥanan's reversal of attributions.
