🔥 Who May Benefit from Festival Labor?

Rav Ḥisda
A jointly-owned animal (half-gentile, half-Jew) may be slaughtered on a Festival — because you cannot extract even an olive-bulk of meat without slaughtering the whole animal.
R. Yosei HaGelili
The verse says 'for you' (לכם) — for you, but not for dogs. One may not perform Festival labor solely for the sake of animals.
Rabbi Akiva
The verse says 'every soul' (כל נפש) — this includes the soul of an animal. One may perform Festival labor even for the benefit of animals.
Rav Huna
Villagers obligated to supply flour to soldiers may bake on a Festival — provided the soldiers allow them to eat of the bread as well.
Core Tension Festival labor is permitted 'for you' (לכם) — but how broadly do we read 'you'? Gentiles? Dogs? Partners? Each case turns on whether a Jewish benefit is primary or incidental.

🤝 Jointly-Owned Animal on a Festival

Animal owned half by gentile, half by Jew
Can it be slaughtered on Yom Tov?
↓
Vow/Gift Offering
The gentile's portion is not separable — the whole offering belongs to the altar. Cannot be slaughtered.
Jointly-Owned Livestock
Rav Ḥisda: Permitted — impossible to extract even an olive-bulk without slaughtering. The act is indivisible.
↓
Why the difference?
A vow-offering belongs entirely to Heaven — no separation is possible. Jointly-owned meat is divisible in principle, and the Jew's share justifies the act.

🐕 Dough for Dogs — The 'Since' Principle (מִגּוֹ)

Rav Ḥisda
One who bakes all the dog-dough together on a Festival is flogged. He should divide it during kneading — baking only the human portion.
Challengers
But Rav Ḥisda himself permits baking the whole batch since (מִגּוֹ) guests might arrive! Does he not accept the 'since' principle?
Resolution
Two opinions among Amoraim: One holds we do say 'since guests may come, it is all permitted now.' The other holds we do not — and Rav Ḥisda follows the stricter view.
Final Answer
The case is where he already has a dead animal carcass to feed the dogs — so there is no 'since'; the dog-portion is clearly unnecessary on this Festival.
מִגּוֹ — 'Since' The principle that a permitted act extends to cover an otherwise-forbidden element, because circumstances might change to make the whole act necessary.

📜 Rabbi Yosei HaGelili vs Rabbi Akiva — Scope of 'For You'

Rabbi Yosei HaGelili

  • Verse: 'Only that which every soul must eat' — for you (לכם)
  • 'For you' — but not for dogs
  • Festival labor is permitted only for human benefit
  • Casting date-stones to animals would be forbidden
  • Gentiles excluded because Israel has no legal duty to sustain them

Rabbi Akiva

  • Verse says 'every soul' (כל נפש) — includes animal souls
  • Festival labor may be performed for animals
  • 'For you and not for gentiles' — gentiles still excluded
  • Dogs included because Israel bears responsibility for their sustenance
  • The Halacha follows this view in practice

🥂 Inviting a Gentile for a Meal — Shabbat vs Festival

Festival (Yom Tov): Rav Huna permits baking for gentile soldiers if Jews may also eat of the bread — the Jewish benefit makes the labor permissible.
↓
Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: One may invite a gentile for a meal on a Festival, but not on Shabbat — lest one come to cook extra food for the guest.
↓
Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov: Even on Shabbat one may not invite a gentile — because of wine remnants in the cups. Once a gentile drinks, the leftover wine becomes yayin nesech (forbidden wine) and is muktze.
↓
Objection: Move the remnant wine along with the cup — a vessel may be handled and nullifies the muktze! (cf. Rava: one may move a coal-pan on account of the ash inside it)
↓
Rejection: Broken sticks are merely muktze — but gentile wine-remnants are forbidden in benefit (אסורין בהנאה). The prohibition is stronger; the cup cannot override it.
↓
Final Ruling (Rava): One may invite a gentile on Shabbat — but not on a Festival, due to the risk of cooking extra food.
