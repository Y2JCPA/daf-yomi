QUESTION 1 OF 5
What was the central dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel regarding Yom Tov sacrificial practice?
A. Whether animals could be purchased on Yom Tov for sacrificial use
B. Whether semicha (laying hands on a sacrificial animal) is permitted on Yom Tov
C. Whether the kohen gadol could perform the service on Yom Tov
D. Whether burnt-offerings required inspection before Yom Tov
Correct: B — The central dispute was whether semicha — the ritual laying of one's hands on a sacrificial animal before it is slaughtered — is permitted on Yom Tov. Beit Shammai forbade it as a form of labor; Beit Hillel permitted it as part of the sacrificial service.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How did Hillel the Elder circumvent Beit Shammai's prohibition when bringing his burnt-offering on Yom Tov?
A. He brought it the day before the Festival and left it in the Temple courtyard
B. He performed semicha secretly before the courtyard opened to the public
C. He declared the animal ownerless (hefker) so there was no formal 'owner' performing semicha
D. He had a non-Jew perform the semicha on his behalf
Correct: C — Hillel cleverly declared his animal hefker (ownerless) before the Festival. Since it had no formal owner, there was technically no person 'performing semicha' — circumventing Beit Shammai's prohibition without directly violating it.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What was Beit Hillel's key argument for why the olat re'iyah (appearance burnt-offering) may be sacrificed on Yom Tov, unlike vow-offerings?
A. The olat re'iyah is more expensive and therefore receives special leniency
B. The Torah explicitly mentions it in the context of Yom Tov in Parshat Emor
C. Unlike vow-offerings, it has a fixed time — the Festival itself is its designated time
D. It was practiced this way throughout the forty years in the wilderness
Correct: C — Beit Hillel argued that the olat re'iyah has a zman kavua (fixed time) — the Festival is precisely when it must be brought. Vow-offerings have no such fixed time and could be brought any day. This distinction justifies permitting fixed-time offerings on Yom Tov.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Abba Shaul's version of the dispute, how does Beit Hillel's position differ from the standard account?
A. Abba Shaul holds Beit Hillel agrees with Beit Shammai that semicha is forbidden
B. Abba Shaul holds Beit Hillel permits even vow-offerings and gift-offerings on Yom Tov
C. Abba Shaul says the dispute applies only to peace-offerings, not burnt-offerings
D. Abba Shaul holds Beit Hillel permits sacrifice only on the last day of a Festival
Correct: B — Abba Shaul presents a more expansive Beit Hillel position: even nedarim (vow-offerings) and nedavot (gift-offerings) may be sacrificed on Yom Tov. This is broader than the standard formulation and reflects a deeper underlying disagreement about the nature of the restriction.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the practical halakhic difference between Rava and Rabba bar Rav Huna regarding vow-offerings improperly slaughtered on Yom Tov?
A. Rava says the animal must be buried; Rabba bar Rav Huna says it may be eaten
B. Rava requires a replacement offering; Rabba bar Rav Huna says none is needed
C. Both agree blood is generally sprinkled, but if the meat became impure first, Rava still sprinkles while Rabba bar Rav Huna does not
D. Rava says the offending kohen is lashed; Rabba bar Rav Huna says no lashes apply
Correct: C — Both Rava and Rabba bar Rav Huna agree that the blood of such an offering is generally sprinkled. Their dispute surfaces when the meat becomes impure before sprinkling: Rava (who holds the prohibition is rabbinic) still requires sprinkling; Rabba bar Rav Huna (who holds it is Torah-level forbidden) rules no sprinkling is required, as the impurity retroactively undermines the offering.
