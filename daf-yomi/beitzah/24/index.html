🐦 The Bird Contradiction: Mishna vs Tosefta

Mishna
One may catch a bird from within a house on Yom Tov — a house functions as a small enclosure where the bird is adequately trapped.
Tosefta
From enclosures of birds, one may NOT trap birds on a Festival at all — even catching from an enclosure is prohibited!
Rabba bar Rav Huna
The Mishna speaks of a domestic dove which accepts authority and is easily caught. The Tosefta speaks of a free sparrow (tzippor deror) which does not accept authority and evades capture even inside a house.
Gemara's Conclusion
No tannaitic dispute! The two sources address different types of birds. The nature of the animal — not just the enclosure — determines whether it is considered trapped.
Key Principle Adequate trapping depends on both the enclosure AND the animal's nature. A house cannot trap a sparrow because it refuses to submit to human authority.

🏠 Small vs Large Enclosure — Rav Ashi's Definition

Small Enclosure — Permitted

  • One can run after the animal and catch it in a single stoop
  • No real pursuit required — animal effectively subdued
  • Permitted to catch on Yom Tov
  • Examples: closet, small pen, dovecote
  • Rabbi Yehuda: trapping a bird into a closet = liable in Shabbat

Large Enclosure — Prohibited

  • Animal hides and must be actively pursued
  • Multiple attempts needed; animal evades capture
  • Prohibited to catch — the trapping act occurs on Yom Tov
  • Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's ruling applies
  • Even if enclosure is "enclosed," inadequate trapping = forbidden

🎁 Rav Pappa's Three-Factor Test: Gentile's Gift on Yom Tov

Scenario: A gentile brings a gift of fish or produce to a Jew on Yom Tov. Background dispute: Rav — permitted only to receive, not eat. Shmuel — permitted even to eat. Rav Pappa synthesizes a comprehensive ruling.
↓
Factor 1 — Is any of that species still attached? If fish of that species remain in the water, or produce remains in the ground — prohibited to eat. We assume it was harvested on Yom Tov specifically for the Jew.
↓
Factor 2 — None attached: Where was it brought from? If brought from within the Shabbat limit (techum) — permitted. No prohibited labor was performed on the Jew's behalf.
↓
Factor 3 — Brought from outside the Shabbat limit: Even if none of that species remains attached, the item was carried beyond the permitted boundary on Yom Tov — prohibited to eat on Yom Tov (permitted afterward).
↓
Rav's dramatic change of mind: Levi and Rav were both before Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. In the evening he said 'permitted'; in the morning he said 'prohibited.' Rav: 'A person should never miss the study hall even for a moment' — this very incident proves it!
