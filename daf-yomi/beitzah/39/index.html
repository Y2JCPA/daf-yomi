🔥 Coal, Flame & Cisterns: Substance, Ownership, and Bereirah

Mishna
A borrowed coal is as the feet of its owner — it may only travel where the owner may walk on Yom Tov. But a flame lit from it has no substance and no owner; it may go anywhere.
Tosefta
Five distinctions between coal and flame: (1) Festival resting-place, (2) Shabbat carrying liability, (3) idol-worship rules, (4) substance vs. no substance, (5) whether nullification applies.
Rava
Challenge to Rav Naḥman: The mishna in Nedarim lists the public cistern (olei Bavel) among jointly owned items. Partners may not wash in each other's cistern — so why is drawing water freely permitted?
Rav Naḥman
Answer: Bathing is indeed forbidden (one uses all the water). Drawing water is permitted via bereirah — we retroactively designate the drawn portion as always having been the drawer's share.
Resolution The Gemara ultimately concludes: everyone agrees the public cistern is ownerless (hefker). The dispute shifts to whether one can acquire ownerless property on another person's behalf.

🔥 Coal (גחלת) vs. Flame (שלהבת) — Five Halakhic Differences

Coal — גחלת

  • Has substance — treated as tangible property
  • Festival resting-place: as the feet of its owner
  • Carrying out on Shabbat: liable
  • Coal of idol worship: forbidden even after the fact
  • Subject to nullification rules (has measurable presence)

Flame — שלהבת

  • Has no substance — not treated as property
  • Festival resting-place: no restriction, may go anywhere
  • Carrying out on Shabbat: exempt
  • Flame of idol worship: permitted ab initio
  • Cannot be nullified (no measurable entity)

⚖️ Rava Challenges Rav Naḥman: Bereirah and the Public Cistern

Rava
The Nedarim mishna lists the public cistern among items owned jointly by all Israel. If so, it is like a partnership — and partners may not wash in each other's water. Drawing water freely seems to contradict this!
Rav Naḥman
Correct — bathing is forbidden (one uses all the water, encroaching on partners' shares). But drawing water is permitted: bereirah (ברירה) retroactively designates the drawn amount as always having been the drawer's destined portion.
Rava (presses)
But you yourself reject bereirah in the case of brothers dividing an inheritance! You hold their re-joined animals do not re-combine for animal tithe — because retroactive designation does not apply!
Rav Naḥman
The Gemara resolves: in fact, everyone agrees the public cistern is ownerless (hefker). The real dispute is whether one person can acquire ownerless property on behalf of another.
Key Principle Rav Sheshet: One cannot acquire ownerless property for another — so only the actual drawer gains the water's feet. Rav Naḥman: One can acquire for another — so even a messenger could draw and grant the water the owner's feet.

🐑 Brothers, Inheritance & Animal Tithe — Bereirah in Practice

Stage 1 — Undivided inheritance: Brothers inherit animals as a single joint estate. All animals form one unit → liable for animal tithe (one animal in ten must be tithed).
↓
Stage 2 — Brothers divide: Each takes his portion. The single unit is now split into smaller groups → each portion too small to tithe independently → exempt.
↓
Stage 3 — Brothers become partners again: The question: do the animals re-form a single unit, restoring tithe liability?
↓
Rav Anan's distinction: If divided kids against lambs (unequal kind) — estate is broken, no re-unification. If divided kids against kids (equal portions) — apply bereirah: 'this portion was always destined for him' → animals re-unify, liable for tithe.
↓
Rav Naḥman: Even equal division cannot invoke bereirah. The re-joined estate never re-unifies for tithe — Rav Naḥman categorically rejects bereirah as a halakhic principle.
