Beitzah 39 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Tosefta cited in Beitzah 39a, what is the rule regarding Shabbat carrying liability for coal versus flame?
A. Both coal and flame incur liability for carrying out on Shabbat
B. One who carries out coal is liable; one who carries out a flame is exempt
C. One who carries out a flame is liable; one who carries out coal is exempt
D. Neither coal nor flame incurs liability — both are considered ownerless
Correct: B — The Tosefta specifies that coal has substance and is treated as property, so one is liable for carrying it out. Flame has no substance, so one is exempt.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Abaye explains a case where carrying out a flame DOES incur Shabbat liability. What is that case?
A. When the flame is large enough to illuminate a room
B. When a vessel is smeared with oil and the flame is lit on it — liability is due to the oil
C. When the flame is carried in a container from one domain to another
D. When the flame was originally lit from a coal of idol worship
Correct: B — Abaye teaches that when oil is smeared on a vessel and lit, one who carries it out is liable due to the oil (which provides substance), not the flame itself.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
The mishna teaches that water from a private individual's cistern is 'as the feet of the individual.' What does this mean practically on Yom Tov?
A. The water may be carried to any location the Festival permits
B. The water may only be carried to places the cistern's owner is permitted to walk
C. The water is ownerless and may be used by anyone
D. The water reverts to the owner if carried beyond the Shabbat boundary
Correct: B — The water's permissible travel range is bound to the owner's Yom Tov boundary (techum). It cannot be carried further than the owner himself could walk on the Festival.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is bereirah (ברירה), and why is it relevant to the public cistern dispute?
A. A rule permitting drawing water from ownerless sources on Yom Tov
B. Retroactive designation — the claim that drawn water was always 'destined' for the drawer, resolving the partnership problem
C. A principle that partners must divide their cistern before Yom Tov
D. A leniency permitting acquisition of ownerless property on behalf of others
Correct: B — Bereirah means retroactive designation: we say the portion one drew was always 'his' portion of the jointly-owned cistern. Rav Naḥman invokes it to explain why drawing water is permitted despite joint ownership.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In the dispute about brothers who divide an inheritance and rejoin as partners, what is Rav Naḥman's ruling regarding animal tithe?
A. The animals re-unify and are liable for tithe, due to bereirah applying equally to all
B. The animals remain separate and exempt from tithe — Rav Naḥman rejects bereirah entirely
C. Tithe applies only if they divided kids against lambs (unequal portions)
D. Tithe applies only to animals born after the partnership was rejoined
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman categorically rejects bereirah. Even when brothers divided equal portions (kids against kids), he holds we cannot say 'this was always destined for him' — so the rejoined estate does not re-form a single titheable unit.
