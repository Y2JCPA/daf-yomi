⚖️ Rav vs Shmuel: Whose Feet Does Deposited Produce Follow?

Rav
כרגלי הנפקד — As the feet of the depositee (custodian). Produce deposited with another follows the holder's Shabbat boundary, not the owner's.
Shmuel
כרגלי המפקיד — As the feet of the depositor (owner). The owner's Shabbat rest determines where the deposited produce may travel.
Why It Matters If your produce is stored at a neighbor's house, whose eiruv governs how far it may be carried on Shabbat or Yom Tov? This dispute drives every case on this daf — guests, portions, and hung meat.

📦 The Amoraic Dispute in Full

Rav
Deposited produce is כרגלי הנפקד — it follows the custodian's feet.
Supported by: the mishna's eiruv clause ('if the owner placed an eiruv, his produce follows him') implies that without an eiruv it does not follow the owner.
Shmuel
Deposited produce is כרגלי המפקיד — it follows the owner's feet.
Supported by: the mishna's eiruv clause means that the eiruv merely extends the owner's reach — without it, the produce is already limited to the owner's default boundary.
Gemara
Rav Huna reports: Rav ruled like the Rabbis (against Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi); Shmuel ruled like Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. Does this mean they simply mirrored an earlier dispute?
No — the Gemara shows each ruling addresses a distinct case and the two disputes are independent.
Key Distinction Rav accepts Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's ruling only for items brought into a courtyard via an eiruv חצרות. Shmuel accepts the Rabbis' ruling only where the homeowner exercises active jurisdiction. The disputes do not collapse into one another.

🚶 Can Guests Carry Portions Back to Their Town?

Host invites guests from beyond his Shabbat limit; guests make an eiruv to reach him
↓
Host gives guests portions of food from his home
↓
No eiruv by host; no full transfer
Portions follow host's feet → guests CANNOT carry them beyond host's boundary
Host placed eiruv for the produce
Host's boundary is extended → guests may carry portions further
Host transfers full ownership to guests
Portions now follow guests' feet → guests' own eiruv governs the range

🥩 Rav Ḥana bar Ḥanilai — The Meat on the Door-Bar

נולד — lit. 'born,' an object whose usable status came into being on Yom Tov; treated like muktze
A Case Reinterpreted Rav Ḥana bar Ḥanilai hung meat on the door-bar of his host's house (outside his own town) and wondered: may I take it home, given that I made an eiruv? First reading: If he hung it → follows his feet (per Rav) → he can take it. If the hosts hung it → follows their feet → he cannot. Challenges: Rav Hillel cites Shmuel's ruling on an ox of a fattener (follows the fattener's feet, not the animal's prior owner); Ravina cites Rabbi Yoḥanan / Rabbi Dosa on towns with one authority — all complicating the simple reading. Final reinterpretation: The real problem is neither muktze nor Shabbat limits. It is the Yom Tov prohibition against slaughtering an animal that was not designated (הוכן) before the Festival — the issue of נולד, something that became available only on Yom Tov itself.

🐄 Desert Animals vs Domestic Animals on Yom Tov

🏜️ Desert Animals — Forbidden

  • Go out to pasture at Passover time
  • Graze in the field day and night
  • Return to town only at first winter rains
  • Owner has effectively removed them from regular use
  • Classified as muktze — may NOT be slaughtered on Yom Tov

🏡 Domestic Animals — Permitted

  • Graze outside during the day
  • Return to town every single night
  • Remain under the owner's ongoing daily use
  • Considered designated for potential Festival use
  • May be watered and slaughtered on Yom Tov
