From Dividing Property to Modes of Acquisition

Mishna (2a): Two claim a garment or animal — they divide it
↓
Does 'divide' mean the item itself or its monetary value?
Item Itself
Works for garment (children's clothes) or kosher animal (meat)
Monetary Value
Required for gold garment, non-kosher animal — dividing ruins item
↓
Conclusion: They divide monetary value, not the item itself
↓
This leads to deeper questions: How does each litigant acquire?
Core Theme Daf 8 explores what it means to 'divide' disputed property and then dives deep into the mechanics of acquisition — lifting for another, the miggo principle, and whether riding, leading, or holding reins effects kinyan.

Lifting a Found Item for Another — Does the Other Acquire?

Rami bar Ḥama
From the mishna we can infer: if one lifts a found item on behalf of another, the other acquires it.
Rami bar Ḥama
Otherwise, each litigant holding the garment would be as if it's still on the ground — neither acquires, and a third party could take it!
Rava
Not so. One who lifts solely for another — the other does not acquire.
Rava
Here, since each litigant acquires part for himself, he also acquires the other part for the other — this is the miggo principle.
Key Distinction Rava distinguishes lifting solely for another (ineffective) from lifting for oneself and thereby also for another (miggo — effective).

Rava's Miggo Principle — 'Since He Acquires for Himself…'

Person lifts a found item for himself
↓
Does he acquire part of the item for himself?
Yes
Miggo: Since he acquires for himself, he also acquires the other part for the other person
No
Neither party acquires anything — item remains ownerless
↓
Proof: Partners who stole together — one lifted the item, yet both are liable. Why? Because lifting for yourself also acquires for your partner.
Miggo The miggo (מיגו — 'since') principle: since a person's act of lifting is effective for himself, it extends to also acquire for the other party.

Deaf-Mute & Competent Person Lifting Together

Deaf-Mute (Cheresh)

  • Cannot perform legal acts for others
  • Acquires via the competent person's lifting (miggo)
  • Rabbis enacted that two deaf-mutes lifting together both acquire — to prevent quarrels

Mentally Competent Person (Pike'ach)

  • Intended to acquire for himself, not for the deaf-mute
  • Needs the deaf-mute to acquire for him — but deaf-mute cannot do so
  • Therefore does NOT acquire his portion
Result The deaf-mute acquires his portion (via the competent person's miggo), but the competent person does not acquire — because the deaf-mute cannot perform acquisition on his behalf.

Modes of Animal Acquisition — Riding vs. Leading vs. Reins

ModeAcquires Ownerless Animal?Reason
Leading (meshikha)✅ YesClassic mode of acquisition — animal moves because of him
Sitting on animal (riding)❌ No (per Shmuel/Rabbis)Sitting is passive — comparable to sitting in a wagon
Holding reins of ownerless animal❌ NoReins only work when handed over by another person (like a transaction)
Holding reins from another person✅ YesLike receiving (moser) — a valid transfer of ownership
Riding + driving with feet✅ YesActive driving (menaheg) makes it equivalent to leading
Etymology Rava explains: Moseira (reins) comes from moser — 'one who transmits/hands over.' Reins effect acquisition only when someone hands them to you as part of a transfer.

Rav Yosef's Proof — Kilayim and Sitting in a Wagon

Rav Yehuda
Shmuel taught me two halakhot — one about riding, one about leading — but I forgot which acquires and which doesn't.
Rav Yosef
We can analyze: In the Kilayim mishna, Shmuel reverses the opinions — the Rabbis exempt one sitting in a wagon. Since Shmuel follows the Rabbis, sitting = no acquisition.
Abaye
But Master — you told us this proof before as your own reasoning. Now you attribute it to Rav Yehuda? (Rav Yosef had suffered memory loss.)
Rav Yosef
I do remember — I even challenged Rav Yehuda: sitting on an animal isn't the same as sitting in a wagon, because on an animal you hold the reins!
Rav Yehuda
Rav and Shmuel both say: holding the reins of an ownerless animal does not effect acquisition.
Historical Note Rav Yosef suffered an illness causing memory loss. Abaye, his student, would sometimes challenge whether Rav Yosef's attributions were accurate or reconstructed.
