⚖️ Must a Creditor Leave the Debtor Enough to Live On?

R. Ya'akov / R. Yirmeya
No arrangements for debtor: A fortiori from valuations — in valuations the collateral is NOT returned yet arrangements ARE made; for a debtor collateral IS returned, so surely no extra protection is needed
Counter-position
Should make arrangements: A fortiori the other way — if in valuations (no collateral return) they still make arrangements, then for a debtor (collateral IS returned) we should certainly make arrangements!
Elijah
A verbal analogy (גזירה שוה) from the word עני (poor) links debtor to valuations: just as arrangements are made in valuations, so too for an ordinary debtor
Core Dilemma When collecting a debt, must the creditor leave the debtor assets sufficient to maintain his standard of living — or may he take everything owed?

🤔 The Dilemma of Arrangements — Two A Fortiori Arguments

Question raised before the Sages: Do we make arrangements (הורדה) for a debtor — leaving him enough to live on when collecting a debt?
↓
Ravin sent a letter from Eretz Yisrael: I asked all my teachers — none answered me. But a related dispute was heard:
↓
Argument A — No arrangements
In valuations: no collateral return → yet arrangements ARE made.
∴ For a debtor: collateral IS returned → no extra arrangements needed
Argument B — Yes arrangements
In valuations: no collateral return → yet arrangements ARE made.
∴ For a debtor: collateral IS returned → certainly make arrangements!
↓
Gemara resolves via limiting word הוא (he): the arrangement applies only when the person 'remains in his state' — excluding ordinary debtors

⚔️ The Vows–Valuations Juxtaposition and Its Implications

Gemara asks
What do R. Ya'akov and R. Yirmeya derive from the juxtaposition of vows and valuations in the Torah? They cannot use it for the debtor a fortiori.
Gemara answers
The juxtaposition teaches: a vow of consecration — even consecrated property can be subject to valuation rules. The debtor inference must come from elsewhere.
Gemara asks
Is consecrated property really in need of a blessing? Surely it belongs to the Temple treasury — why would the verse 'you shall eat and be satisfied and bless God' apply?
Gemara answers
Yes — even consecrated property requires a blessing! The verse 'you shall eat and be satisfied and bless the Lord your God' (Deut. 8:10) applies universally, including to Temple property.
Textual Key The word הוא limits the valuation arrangements to one who 'remains in his state' — preventing automatic extension to debtors via a fortiori

🪦 Rabba bar Avuh Meets Elijah — A Heavenly Encounter

רבה בר אבוה אשכחיה לאליהו דקאי בבית הקברות של עכו"ם
Aggadic Narrative Rabba bar Avuh found Elijah standing in a gentile cemetery and asked him the unresolved halakhic question about arrangements for a debtor.

Elijah's answer: Derive via verbal analogy (גזירה שוה) from עני/עני — the word 'poor' links debtor to valuations. Just as arrangements are made in valuations, so too for an ordinary debtor.

Rabba bar Avuh challenged: You are a priest — why are you standing in a cemetery?
Elijah replied: Have you not learned Seder Teharot? Gentile graves do not impart priestly impurity (טומאה)!

Elijah led Rabba bar Avuh through Gan Eden. He filled his cloak with fragrant leaves. Upon exiting, a heavenly voice declared: 'Who else has consumed his World-to-Come like Rabba bar Avuh?' — He had used his next-world merit for this world. He cast away the leaves, yet his cloak retained its heavenly fragrance.

💀 Collateral Obligation: During Life vs. After Death

During the Debtor's Lifetime

  • Return night garment before sunset each evening
  • Return day garment before sunrise each morning
  • The obligation is personal — tied to the living debtor's dignity
  • Torah basis: 'And it shall be righteousness to you' (Deut. 24:13)
  • Creditor may take the garment back each day as collateral

After the Debtor's Death

  • Creditor returned the collateral — then the debtor died
  • Creditor may retake collateral from the children
  • No obligation to return it nightly to the children
  • The personal obligation lapses at death — heirs are new parties
  • R. Yoḥanan's ruling (subject to further Talmudic discussion)
