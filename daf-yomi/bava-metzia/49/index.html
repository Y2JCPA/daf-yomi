Bava Metzia 49: Keeping Your Word & Fair Pricing

הן שלך יהא צדק, ולאו שלך יהא צדק
DAF OVERVIEW This daf transitions from the laws of shevi'it and collateral to the critical question: Is reneging on a verbal commitment an act of bad faith? Rav says no; Rabbi Yochanan says yes. The daf then introduces the Mishna of ona'ah (exploitation) — defining the one-sixth threshold for price fraud, and the dispute between Rav and Shmuel over whether the fraction is calculated from the item's value or the money paid.

Collateral & Shevi'it Cancellation

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel
A down payment (arravon) effects acquisition for the entire amount of the transaction. The Sabbatical Year does not abrogate any part of the loan backed by collateral.
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
A down payment effects acquisition only commensurate with its own value. The Sabbatical Year abrogates the uncollateralized half of the loan.
Gemara (Rejection)
Perhaps R' Yehuda HaNasi holds collateral is merely a reminder (זכרון), not a lien — and shevi'it cancels even the collateralized portion.
KEY QUESTION Does holding collateral create a legal claim that survives shevi'it, or is it merely a psychological motivator for repayment?

Rav Kahana's Linen Dilemma

Buyers give money to Rav Kahana for linen
↓
Price of linen increases before delivery
↓
Rav Kahana asks Rav what to do
↓
Rav's Ruling
Give linen equal to the money received. The rest was only a verbal commitment — not bad faith to renege.
Implication
A verbal promise without kinyan (formal acquisition) is not binding according to Rav.
PRACTICAL OUTCOME Rav Kahana only had to deliver linen worth the money already paid — not the full amount verbally promised.

Is Reneging on a Verbal Commitment Bad Faith?

Rav

  • Reneging on a verbal commitment is not an act of bad faith (מחוסרי אמנה)
  • Only formal acts of acquisition create binding obligations
  • Changed circumstances justify reneging

Rabbi Yochanan

  • Reneging on a verbal commitment is an act of bad faith
  • "Your yes should be just, your no should be just" (Lev. 19:36)
  • Moral obligation exists even without kinyan
ABAYE'S CLARIFICATION The verse 'a just hin' (הין צדק) means: don't say one thing with your mouth while thinking another in your heart. It prohibits insincere promises — not reneging due to changed circumstances.

Mishna: Ona'ah (Exploitation) Rules

📏 Exploitation threshold: 4 silver ma'a out of 24 in a sela = one-sixth
↓
⏱️ Buyer may return the item within time needed to show it to a merchant or relative
↓
📜 R' Tarfon in Lod: Exploitation = 8 ma'a (one-third) — merchants rejoiced
↓
🔄 R' Tarfon added: But buyer can return the entire day — merchants dismayed
↓
🏪 Merchants of Lod reverted to the Rabbis' original ruling (1/6 + limited return window)
IRONY The Lod merchants thought R' Tarfon's higher threshold favored them — until they learned his extended return window was far worse for their business.
