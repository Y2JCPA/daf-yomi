Spreading a Garment: For Whose Benefit?

Mishna
One who finds a garment spreads it for its sake to ventilate it, but not for his own prestige.
Gemara
Dilemma: What if he spreads it for both his sake and its sake?
First Inference
From 'for its sake' → implying: not for both his and its sake.
Counter-Inference
From 'not for his prestige' → implying: for both his and its sake is fine!
Gemara
Conflicting inferences — no proof can be drawn from the mishna.
Key Point The Gemara demonstrates how opposing inferences from a single mishna cancel each other out, leaving the dilemma unresolved from this source.

Proof from Guests & the Calf in the Yoke

Baraita: If guests visit, may not spread the garment — not for his sake nor its sake
↓
Does this prove spreading for both is forbidden?
Rejected
Guests are different — spreading before them is like burning it (evil eye / thieves)
↓
Proof from Eglah Arufah: Calf placed in yoke to suckle and thresh → unfit
↓
Proves dual-purpose intent disqualifies?
Rejected
Torah says 'has not been worked with' — stricter standard unique to eglah arufah
Result Both proofs are rejected. The dilemma of spreading a found garment for both one's own sake and its sake remains unresolved.

Rav Pappa: Reading 'Ubbad' vs. 'Avad'

If Written & Read as Ubbad (passive)

  • Even if the heifer performed labor by itself → disqualified
  • No owner intent required
  • Broadest disqualification

If Written & Read as Avad (active)

  • Only if owner intentionally used it for labor → disqualified
  • Owner must actively assign labor
  • Narrowest disqualification

Actual: Written Avad, Read Ubbad

  • We require ubbad to be similar to avad
  • Owner must be amenable to the labor
  • Bird resting on heifer → fit (not amenable)
  • Male mounting for mating → unfit (owner amenable)
Principle The Torah's spelling creates a middle ground: the heifer is disqualified not by any incidental labor, but when the owner is amenable to that labor being performed.

Dignity & the Obligation to Return Lost Items

Gemara
An elderly person for whom returning the item is beneath his dignity is exempt from the obligation.
Rabba
But if he began returning it — even striking it once to guide it — he is obligated to complete the return.
Abaye
Throws a clod at nearby goats to shoo them away...
Rabba
You've now obligated yourself! Arise and return them to their owner.
Rava
Rule: If you would recover your own item, you are also obligated to return another's.
Unresolved If a person's dignity permits returning the item in the field but not in the city — is he obligated at all? The Gemara leaves this teku (unresolved).
