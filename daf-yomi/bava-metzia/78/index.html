🗻 Mountain vs. Valley: Wrong Path Liability

Hired for Mountain → Took Valley

  • Even if both routes are 10 mil
  • Mountain air differs from valley air
  • If donkey dies: renter is liable
  • School of R. Yannai: 'air of the mountains killed it'
  • Cannot equally blame valley air — it was never the agreed route

Hired for Valley → Took Mountain

  • Even if both routes are 10 mil
  • Mountain terrain strains hooves differently
  • If donkey dies: renter is liable
  • R. Meir: diverging from agreement = robber
  • Liable even for oness — accidents beyond control

Donkey Ill or Seized (No Deviation)

  • Illness (vehivrika): owner not liable
  • Madness: owner not liable
  • Angarya: depends on Rav or Shmuel
  • Owner may say: 'That which is yours is before you'
  • Renter bears loss of the trip only

⚖️ Proving Rabbi Meir's Robber Rule

R. Yoḥanan
The mishna (renter on wrong path is fully liable) follows Rabbi Meir: one who diverges from the agreed terms is called a robber and bears liability even for unavoidable accidents.
Gemara
Which of Rabbi Meir's statements is the source? Three candidates: (1) the dyer mishna (Bava Kamma 100b), (2) the Purim collection baraita, (3) R. Shimon ben Elazar's baraita about charity clothing.
Gemara
❌ Dyer rejected: the dyer acquired the wool through a lien — a different mechanism. ❌ Purim rejected: the donor's intent was explicit from the start. ❌ Clothing rejected: perhaps the reason is only suspicion, not robbery.
Gemara
✅ Final proof: The baraita says 'because he diverges from the homeowner's intent' — not 'because of suspicion.' This language confirms Rabbi Meir holds such a person is literally called a robber.
Rabbi Meir's Core Rule Anyone who diverges from the intention of the one who hired him is called a robber — liable for all damage, including unavoidable accidents (אונס).

🏛️ Angarya: Rav vs. Shmuel

Rav
Temporary angarya: Owner need not replace — says 'yours is before you.' Permanent angarya: Owner must provide another donkey or reimburse the renter for the full trip.
Shmuel
The key is direction, not duration: If the donkey was seized and taken toward the destination, owner need not replace. If taken away from the destination, owner must compensate.
Gemara
Objection: A baraita states 'if seized for public service, the owner is obligated to provide another donkey' — contradicting the mishna which says owner need not. How does each Amora resolve this?
Rav
Mishna = permanent angarya (no obligation). Baraita = temporary angarya (must provide replacement). No contradiction.
Shmuel
Difficulty remains: the baraita's latter clause (Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar) uses my exact direction-based distinction — implying the first clause of the baraita follows a different opinion, not mine.
אַנְגַּרְיָא — Angarya Government commandeering of private animals or goods for state service. Whether the owner must compensate the renter turns on whether the seizure is temporary vs. permanent (Rav) or toward vs. away from destination (Shmuel).
