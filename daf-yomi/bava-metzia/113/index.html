🏛️ Can a Court Agent Enter the Debtor's Home?

Shmuel
A court agent may seize collateral from the debtor in the marketplace, but may not enter his house — just as the creditor must 'stand outside' per Deuteronomy 24:11.
Baraita (challenge)
'You shall stand outside' — this verse tells the creditor to wait. Why should the same restriction apply to a court-authorized agent? He acts on behalf of beit din!
Gemara (reply)
The court agent is treated like the creditor in this regard. He too must stand outside while the debtor brings forth the collateral on his own.
Rav Yosef
I raise an objection from a baraita: 'He may not take millstones as collateral — but he may take other things.' Doesn't this imply the creditor himself can freely seize non-exempt items?
Rav Pappa
The baraita adds a new prohibition on millstones beyond the general rule. The base assumption is already that collateral is taken via court — the baraita restricts what, not who.
Core Verse Deuteronomy 24:11 — 'You shall stand outside, and the man to whom you lend shall bring forth the pledge outside to you.'

🌙 Rules for Returning Collateral to the Debtor

Step 1: Court agent seizes collateral in the marketplace with beit din authorization
↓
Step 2 — Nightly return: A pillow must be returned each evening so the debtor can sleep on it
↓
Step 3 — Morning return: A plow must be returned each day at dawn so the debtor can work his field
↓
Step 4 — Death of debtor: If the debtor dies, the creditor is not obligated to return collateral to his heirs — the duty was personal
↓
Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: After 30 days, the creditor may sell the collateral outright rather than endlessly returning and reclaiming it

👤 Court Agent vs. Creditor: Collateral Rules

Court Agent (Shaliach Beit Din)

  • May seize collateral in the marketplace
  • May not enter debtor's home
  • Stands outside — debtor brings items out
  • Cannot take food-preparation utensils
  • Must leave debtor sufficient bedding (2 beds for wealthy; bed + mat for poor)
  • Acts with full court authorization

Creditor (Ba'al Chov)

  • May not seize collateral unilaterally
  • Must wait outside the debtor's home
  • Cannot enter even if debtor invites him
  • Cannot take millstones — explicit Torah prohibition
  • Must return pillow nightly, plow daily
  • If debtor dies — need not return to heirs

🔄 Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's Principle

כָּל הָרָאוּי לְבִילָה — אֵין בִּילָה מְעַכַּבְתּוֹ
Universal Tannaic Principle Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds: anything that is currently unfit for use is nonetheless legally considered 'fit' — we do not make exemptions based on transient unfitness.

Application to collateral: If a debtor wears a cloak worth 10,000 dinars but owes only 1,000 dinars, the court strips it and sells it, leaving him appropriate garments. No item is off-limits merely because the debtor happens to need it at this moment.

This principle is shared by Rabbi Shimon, Rabbi Yishmael, and Rabbi Akiva — demonstrated across multiple mishnayot involving Shabbat, rose oil, and valuations.
