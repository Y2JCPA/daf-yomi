Uncertain Tithe & Firstborn Animals

Torah Source
"The tenth shall be sacred" (Lev. 27:32) — a certain tenth, not an uncertain tenth
Rav Aḥa of Difti
What are these 'uncertain animals' subject to tithe? An uncertain firstborn is already sacred — tithe can't apply to it!
Ravina
It refers to uncertain firstborn donkey redemption — lambs used to redeem donkeys of uncertain firstborn status
Rav Naḥman / Rabba bar Avuh
An Israelite with 10 uncertain firstborn donkeys separates 10 lambs, tithes them — all belong to him, since the priest cannot prove entitlement
Principle An animal that is already sacred (even doubtfully) cannot receive the additional sanctity of ma'aser behemah (animal tithe).

Can One Consecrate Property Not in His Possession?

Rav Naḥman's Rule:
Property one cannot recover by legal process → consecration while in another's possession is not valid
↓
By implication: if one can recover it → consecration is valid?
↓
R' Yoḥanan objects:
A robbed item (owner not yet despairing) — neither robber nor owner can consecrate it!
↓
Resolution
The bathhouse case involves property attached to the ground — already in owner's possession; no separate act of acquisition needed
Movable Items
R' Yoḥanan's rule applies — must be in physical possession to consecrate
Distinction Immovable property (like a bathhouse dug in the ground) is considered in the owner's possession even without physical seizure — unlike movable objects.

Two People Grasping a Garment — Rav Taḥalifa's Baraita vs. the Mishna

Rav Taḥalifa's Baraita

  • Each one takes up to where his hand reaches
  • The remainder is divided equally
  • R' Abbahu adds: with an oath (shevu'ah)

The Mishna's Rule

  • They simply divide the garment
  • No mention of 'where each hand reaches'
  • Rav Pappa: the mishna discusses holding the fringes (karkashta), not the garment itself
Reconciliation When each grasps the garment itself, he keeps what he holds. When each holds only the fringes, neither has a stronger physical claim, so they divide equally with an oath.

Kinyan Sudar — Minimum Cloth for Acquisition

קנין סודר — Ruth 4:7
Rav Mesharshiyya's Inference From Rav Pappa's ruling (holding fringes counts as possession), we learn: Kinyan sudar (symbolic cloth acquisition) does not require the entire cloth to change hands. Once the recipient grasps 3×3 fingerbreadths — the minimum size to be considered a kli (utensil) — the acquisition takes effect.

This is based on: "A man drew off his shoe and gave it to his neighbor" (Ruth 4:7).

But the Gemara challenges: Rav Ḥisda taught that a get (divorce bill) with a string still in the husband's hand is not effective if he can pull it back — suggesting partial possession shouldn't work!
