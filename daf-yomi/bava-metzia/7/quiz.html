QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why can't animal tithe apply to an animal whose firstborn status is uncertain?
A. Because uncertain animals are too young for tithe
B. Because it may already be sacred as a firstborn, and tithe cannot apply to an already-sacred animal
C. Because the Torah only requires tithing herds, not individual animals
D. Because the priest already owns it
Correct: B — The Torah says 'the tenth shall be sacred' — tithe sanctity applies only to non-sacred animals. An uncertain firstborn already has potential sanctity, blocking additional tithe consecration.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rav Naḥman, when is consecration of property in another's possession valid?
A. Whenever the owner declares it consecrated
B. Only when the property is movable
C. When the owner can recover the property by legal process (and it is immovable/attached to ground)
D. Never — property must always be in physical possession
Correct: C — Rav Naḥman holds that property one cannot recover by legal process cannot be consecrated while in another's hands. For immovable property (like a bathhouse dug in the ground), legal recoverability suffices since it's considered already in one's possession.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
In the case of two people grasping a garment, how does Rav Pappa explain the mishna's rule of simple equal division?
A. The mishna only applies to garments of equal value
B. Neither person is actually holding the garment itself — they hold only the fringes (karkashta)
C. The mishna assumes both people are honest
D. The mishna was written before the oath requirement was enacted
Correct: B — Rav Pappa explains that the mishna discusses a case where both hold only the fringes (karkashta), so neither has a stronger physical claim to any specific part. Therefore they divide equally (with an oath), unlike the baraita where each grasps the garment itself.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is R' Yosei's general position regarding found promissory notes, and how does Rav Pappa explain his seemingly contradictory ruling about marriage contracts?
A. R' Yosei always suspects repayment; Rav Pappa says the cases are unrelated
B. R' Yosei never suspects repayment; his marriage contract ruling argues according to the Rabbis' own logic (l'divreihem)
C. R' Yosei always suspects repayment for marriage contracts but not promissory notes; no resolution needed
D. R' Yosei holds all documents should be returned; Rav Pappa reverses the opinions
Correct: B — R' Yosei holds documents retain their presumptive status (no suspicion of repayment). Rav Pappa explains that when R' Yosei distinguishes married from widowed wives regarding ketubot, he is arguing ad hominem — telling the Rabbis that even by their own logic, a married woman's ketubah should be returned.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What minimum amount of cloth must a recipient grasp for kinyan sudar (symbolic cloth acquisition) to take effect?
A. The entire cloth must change hands
B. One fingerbreadth is sufficient
C. Three by three fingerbreadths — the minimum size to be considered a utensil (kli)
D. Half the cloth, as in dividing a disputed garment
Correct: C — Rav Mesharshiyya derives from Rav Pappa's ruling that 3×3 fingerbreadths suffices — this is the minimum size for cloth to be considered a kli (utensil). It is treated as though severed from the rest, effecting the acquisition based on Ruth 4:7.
