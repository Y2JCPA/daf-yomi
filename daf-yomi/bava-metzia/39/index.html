Managing Property of Absent Owners

Owner leaves property
Captive (Shevuyim)
Taken by force — court authorizes relative to manage
Abandoned (Netushim)
Left under royal edict — dispute: Rabbis vs. RSBG on whether court removes occupant
Forsaken (Retushim)
Left voluntarily — court removes occupant entirely
↓
For all cases: court appraises labor like a sharecropper (per RSBG for netushim)
Key Principle The reason someone left their property determines the rights of the person who works it in their absence.
3 / 9

Three Categories of Absent Owners

Captive (Shevuyim)

  • Taken by force (e.g., war captivity)
  • Court authorizes relative to manage field
  • Worker is diligent and profits
  • Takes as much produce as he wishes

Abandoned (Netushim)

  • Left perforce — by royal edict
  • Source: "untashtah" (Exodus 23:11)
  • Rabbis: Court removes occupant
  • RSBG: Like captive's property

Forsaken (Retushim)

  • Left of own volition
  • Source: "rutasha" (Hosea 10:14)
  • Court removes occupant entirely
  • No sharecropper assessment at all
Biblical Sources The Gemara derives the distinction between netushim and retushim from their respective verses — one implies compulsion, the other voluntary departure.

Who Gets the Sharecropper Appraisal?

Gemara
The baraita says: "For all of them" the court appraises work like a sharecropper. To which case does this apply?
Gemara
Not captives' property — that worker takes even more than a sharecropper!
Gemara
Not forsaken property — the court removes the occupant entirely!
Gemara
It must be abandoned property (netushim), per Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.
R. Shimon b. Gamliel
Abandoned property is like captives' property in that court doesn't remove the occupant — but unlike it, as the worker gets only a sharecropper's share, not unlimited produce.
Resolution RSBG holds a middle position: the worker on abandoned property stays (unlike the Rabbis' view), but doesn't profit freely (unlike captive's property).

Captive's Perishable Crops — Emergency Procedure

Captive left behind standing grain, grapes, dates, or olives
↓
Court appoints a steward (apotropos) to harvest perishable crops
↓
Steward reaps, harvests, cuts, and picks
↓
Then court authorizes a relative to manage the property long-term
↓
Why not always use a steward? Ein maamidin apotropos l'gdolim — stewards are only for orphans, not adults!
Rav Yehuda / Shmuel The temporary steward is an emergency measure to prevent loss of perishable produce, not a standard management arrangement.

Who May Descend to a Captive's or Minor's Property?

RuleDetails
Minor cannot manage captive's propertyRav Huna: even if the minor is an heir
Heir-relative cannot manage minor's propertyRisk: the relative may claim presumptive ownership (ḥazakah)
Paternal brothers specificallyThey are potential heirs — creates conflict of interest
Maternal brothers?Initially distinguished, but Gemara concludes: no difference
Land vs. houses?Initially distinguished (neighbors can testify), but no difference
Document of division?Initially distinguished (generates publicity), but no difference
Final rulingNo relative may manage a minor's property — regardless of circumstances
Conclusion The Gemara rejects all proposed distinctions: no relative descends to a minor's property under any circumstances, to prevent false ownership claims.

The Old Woman's Estate — Dividing Uncertain Property

Narrator
An old woman had 3 daughters. She and daughter #1 were captured. Daughter #2 died, leaving a minor son. Only daughter #3 survives free.
Abaye
We can't give it all to the sister — maybe the old woman died and the minor inherits ⅓. We can't give it to the minor — maybe she's alive and he can't manage a captive's property.
Abaye
Solution: Give half to the surviving sister. Appoint a steward for the minor's potential half.
Rava
Once we need a steward for half, appoint a steward for all the property until the captives' fate is known.
Narrator
Later: the old woman died. The captive daughter's fate is unknown.
Abaye
Give ⅓ to the sister, ⅓ to the minor (from his mother), and of the captive's ⅓: ⅙ to the sister, steward for the other ⅙.
Rava
Appoint a steward for the entire captive's third until her fate is known.
Pattern Abaye divides property to the extent possible; Rava prefers to keep disputed property under a steward until certainty is achieved.
