🌳 The Orla Baraita

עָרְלָה — פֵּרוֹת שְׁלֹשׁ שְׁנִים הָרִאשׁוֹנוֹת
Tosefta Orla 1:4 A tree that sprouts new growth from its trunk is considered the same tree — no new orla period. Growth from the roots, however, constitutes a new tree and triggers a fresh three-year orla prohibition. The same ownership principle that governs garden disputes governs this case.

🏡 Upper Garden vs Lower Garden

Upper Garden Owner

  • Can stretch hand and reach the plant
  • Plant belongs to him
  • Rabbi Shimon's rule: physical reach = ownership
  • School of Rabbi Yannai adds nuance to this test

Lower Garden Owner

  • Plant grows on lower terrace
  • Gets all other vegetables out of reach
  • Default ownership when no reach possible
  • Receives bulk of contested border growth

🍃 Rav Anan's Dilemma — Leaves or Roots?

Rav Anan
If the upper owner can reach the leaves but not the roots — what is the law? Does partial reach confer ownership?
Rav Anan
Conversely, if he can reach the roots but not the leaves — is that determinative? The dilemma stands unresolved (תֵּיקוּ).
Rabbi Yirmeya
Some attribute this very dilemma to me. The question cuts to the heart of what 'reach' means: functional access to the edible part, or control over the plant's anchor point?
Status The dilemma is left unresolved — תֵּיקוּ (Teiku). Neither reaching only the leaves nor only the roots is definitively ruled sufficient.
