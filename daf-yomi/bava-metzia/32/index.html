Where was it found?

Core Theme This daf explores when we must help animals — from returning lost ones to relieving their suffering — and whether preventing animal pain is a biblical or rabbinic mandate.

Honoring Parents vs. Torah Mitzvot

אמר לו אביו: היטמא, או שאמר לו: אל תחזיר — לא ישמע לו
Halachic Principle If your father tells you: "Become tamei" (impure) or "Don't return the lost item" — you may not listen.

Both parent and child are equally obligated to honor Hashem's commandments. A parent cannot instruct a child to violate Torah law, even through the mitzvah of kibbud av va'em (honoring parents).

The Repeated Obligation to Unload

🐴 You see a donkey collapsing under its burden
↓
📦 You unload the burden from the animal
↓
🔄 Owner reloads the animal and it collapses again
↓
📦 You unload again
↓
🔄 This happens a third, fourth, even fifth time…
↓
✅ You are STILL obligated to unload — "עזֹב תַּעֲזֹב" (you shall surely release)
Source The doubled verb azov ta'azov (Exodus 23:5) teaches that the obligation applies repeatedly, not just once.

Must the Owner Participate?

Mishna
If the owner sat down and said: 'Since there's a mitzvah upon you — unload it yourself,' the passerby is exempt.
Torah Source
"You shall release it with him" (עִמּוֹ) — the owner must work together with the helper.
Exception
If the owner is old or infirm and physically unable to help, the passerby must unload alone.
Gemara
When the owner refuses to help, 'exempt' may mean exempt from doing it for free — but still must do it for pay (if tza'ar ba'alei chayyim is Torah law).
Key Nuance The Gemara reinterprets 'exempt' to preserve the animal's welfare — the helper may demand wages but cannot simply walk away if animal suffering is a Torah obligation.

Unloading vs. Loading: The Rabbis' Dispute

Rabbis (Tanna Kamma)

  • Unloading (פריקה) is a Torah obligation
  • Loading (טעינה) is NOT a Torah obligation
  • Unloading involves animal suffering + monetary loss
  • Derived from Exodus 23:5

Rabbi Shimon

  • Unloading IS a Torah obligation
  • Loading is ALSO a Torah obligation
  • Both are derived from the verses
  • Disagrees only because 'the verses are not clearly defined'

R' Yosei HaGelili

  • Only obligated for a normal burden
  • If burden exceeds the animal's capacity — no obligation
  • 'Under its burden' = a burden it can bear
  • Owner overloaded at his own risk
Implication The Rabbis and R' Shimon agree on unloading — their dispute about loading becomes the Gemara's springboard for analyzing whether tza'ar ba'alei chayyim is a Torah law.
