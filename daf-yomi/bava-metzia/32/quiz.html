Bava Metzia 32 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava, what kind of stable does the mishna refer to when it exempts the finder from returning a lost animal?
A. A stable that fully secures the animal
B. A stable that neither secures the animal nor encourages it to stray
C. A stable that encourages the animal to stray
D. Any stable belonging to the animal's owner
Correct: B — Rava explains the stable neither secures nor encourages straying — if it fully secured the animal, the exemption would be obvious and unnecessary to teach.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
If your father tells you not to return a lost animal, what should you do?
A. Listen to your father because kibbud av overrides other mitzvot
B. Return it only if the animal is in danger
C. Do not listen — you may not violate Torah law to honor your father
D. Ask a beit din to decide which mitzvah takes priority
Correct: C — The mishna explicitly states: if his father says 'do not return it,' he may not listen, because both parent and child are bound by Torah commandments.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
When the animal's owner sits idle and tells the passerby to unload alone, what does the Gemara suggest 'exempt' means if tza'ar ba'alei chayyim is Torah law?
A. Completely exempt — no obligation whatsoever
B. Exempt from unloading but must guard the animal
C. Exempt from doing it for free, but obligated to do it for pay
D. Exempt only if another person is available to help
Correct: C — The Gemara reinterprets 'exempt' to mean exempt from free labor — but if animal suffering is a Torah obligation, the passerby must still help, albeit for remuneration.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does the Gemara reject monetary loss as the basis for the a fortiori inference from loading to unloading?
A. Because loading never involves monetary loss
B. Because monetary loss applies to both loading and unloading scenarios
C. Because monetary loss is only a rabbinic concern
D. Because the Torah explicitly mentions only animal suffering
Correct: B — The Gemara argues that loading also involves monetary loss (marketplace delays, risk of thieves), so monetary loss cannot distinguish loading from unloading — the distinction must be animal suffering.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why must a Jew help unload a gentile's collapsed animal, according to the Gemara's alternative explanation?
A. Because tza'ar ba'alei chayyim is universally binding by Torah law
B. Because of dina d'malkhuta (law of the land)
C. To prevent enmity (eivah) between Jews and gentiles
D. Because the animal itself has inherent sanctity
Correct: C — The Gemara offers an alternative to the animal-suffering rationale: the obligation may exist to prevent hostility (eivah) that would arise if gentiles saw Jews helping only their own.
