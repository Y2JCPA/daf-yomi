Bava Metzia 70 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why did Rav Naḥman reject the idea that Shmuel permitted lending orphans' money at interest?
A. Because orphans are obligated in mitzvot like adults
B. Because we may not feed orphans prohibited items, even for their benefit
C. Because Shmuel never actually said this
D. Because interest is only prohibited by rabbinic law
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman argued that even though orphans are minors, the court managing their affairs cannot engage in prohibited conduct on their behalf. He harshly stated that orphans who consume what isn't theirs 'will follow their parent to the graveyard.'
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What was actually happening with Mar Ukva's kettle that Rav Anan misinterpreted as Shmuel permitting interest?
A. Shmuel was lending the kettle at interest to gentiles
B. Shmuel charged both rental fees and depreciation payments, which is actually permitted for everyone
C. Shmuel was using the kettle himself and paying the orphans interest
D. Shmuel was melting the kettle to invest the copper
Correct: B — Shmuel would weigh the kettle before and after rental, charging both a rental fee and for visible depreciation of the copper. Rav Naḥman explained this is permitted even for adults because the depreciation payment covers real, measurable loss — not interest.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why did Rabba specify buying 'scraps of gold' rather than finished gold items when investing orphans' money?
A. Scraps of gold are cheaper and more profitable
B. Finished items might be deposits whose true owner could reclaim them
C. Gold scraps are easier to divide among multiple orphans
D. The Torah specifically requires scraps for investment purposes
Correct: B — A finished gold item might be a deposit that someone left with the seller. The true owner could come, identify it with distinguishing marks, and reclaim it — resulting in a loss for the orphans. Scraps have no distinguishing marks and are thus safer.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to the Gemara, why is a 'guaranteed investment' (tzon barzel) considered a loan with interest?
A. Because the recipient must pay a fixed fee to the owner
B. Because the principal is guaranteed to be returned in full, making any profit the owner receives function as interest
C. Because the Torah explicitly categorizes it as interest
D. Because the animals always depreciate in value
Correct: B — In a tzon barzel arrangement, the recipient guarantees to return the full value of what was given regardless of loss. Since the principal is fixed (like a loan), any profit shared with the owner functions as interest on that 'loan.'
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Rava explain the exemption from firstborn laws when a Jew accepts an animal from a gentile as a guaranteed investment?
A. The gentile performed a kinyan that retains his ownership
B. The gentile's potential right to seize the offspring means his 'hand is in the middle'
C. The gentile accepted responsibility for accidents, retaining ownership
D. A rabbinic decree exempts all gentile-related animal transactions
Correct: B — Rava explains that if the Jewish recipient defaults, the gentile can seize the animal or its offspring. This potential claim means 'the hand of a gentile is in the middle,' and wherever a gentile has such involvement, the firstborn laws do not apply.
