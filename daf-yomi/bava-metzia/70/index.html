Daf 70: Orphans' Money, Guaranteed Investments & Interest with Gentiles

Can orphans lend money at interest?
↓
Shmuel (per Rav Anan)
Permitted — orphans are exempt from mitzvot
Rav Naḥman
Rejected — we don't feed orphans issurim
↓
Resolution: Invest orphans' money in ventures close to profit, far from loss (karov l'sakhar, raḥok m'hefsed)
↓
Mishna: Guaranteed investments (tzon barzel) are prohibited between Jews but permitted with gentiles
Core Theme The daf balances protecting orphans' assets against the prohibition of interest, then transitions to the laws of guaranteed investments and interest with gentiles.

Can Orphans' Money Be Lent at Interest?

Rav Anan (quoting Shmuel)
It is permitted to lend orphans' money at interest — they are minors exempt from mitzvot.
Rav Naḥman
Because they are orphans we may feed them prohibited items?! Orphans who consume what isn't theirs will follow their parent to the grave!
Rav Naḥman
Tell me — what was the actual incident you saw Shmuel do?
Rav Anan
Shmuel rented out Mar Ukva's orphans' kettle, charging both a rental fee and payment for depreciation of the copper.
Rav Naḥman
That's no proof! Even for adults this is permitted — the depreciation payment covers real, visible loss of copper from burning. It's not interest at all.
Key Point Shmuel never actually permitted lending orphans' money at interest. The kettle case involved legitimate rental + depreciation charges, not interest.

Three Types of Investors in Joint Ventures

Wicked (רשע)

  • Close to PROFIT for investor
  • Far from LOSS for investor
  • Similar to a loan with interest
  • The working partner bears all risk

Ordinary (כל אדם)

  • Equal distance to profit and loss
  • OR equally far from both
  • Standard halakhic partnership
  • Risk and reward are balanced

Pious (חסיד)

  • Close to LOSS for investor
  • Far from PROFIT for investor
  • Extra-stringent to avoid any hint of interest
  • Investor accepts additional risk
Exception For orphans' money, the Sages specifically permitted the 'close to profit, far from loss' arrangement — the one normally called wicked — because it is only rabbinic interest (avak ribbit), and protecting the orphans' estate takes priority.

How to Invest Orphans' Money Safely

💰 Rabba asks Rav Yosef: How do we manage orphans' funds?
↓
🏛️ Rav Yosef: Set up a court to distribute funds dinar by dinar
↓
📉 Rabba objects: The principal will be depleted!
↓
🥇 Rabba's method: Buy scraps of gold → give back as joint venture (close to profit, far from loss)
↓
❓ Rav Ashi objects: What if no gold scraps are available?
↓
✅ Rav Ashi's solution: Find a trustworthy man with undisputed property, who obeys Torah law and court authority → invest through him via court
Why Gold Scraps? Rabba specified scraps (not finished items) because a finished gold item might be a deposit — the true owner could identify it and reclaim it, causing the orphans a loss.

Mishna: Guaranteed Investment (Tzon Barzel) Rules

TransactionBetween JewsWith Gentiles / Ger Toshav
Accept guaranteed investment❌ Prohibited (= loan with interest)✅ Permitted
Borrow with interest❌ Prohibited✅ Permitted
Lend with interest❌ Prohibited✅ Permitted
Middleman: lend gentile's money to a Jew✅ With knowledge of the gentile—
Middleman: lend with own knowledge❌ Prohibited—
Definition Tzon Barzel (Iron Flock): The recipient guarantees to return the full value regardless of loss or depreciation, while sharing only part of the profit. Since the principal is fixed, it functions as a loan, and the profit share = interest.

Guaranteed Investment & the Firstborn Exemption

Abaye
The Bekhorot mishna (exempt from firstborn) is where the gentile owner accepted responsibility for accidents/depreciation — so the sheep still belong to him. Our mishna is where the owner did not accept responsibility.
Rava
If the owner accepted accident/depreciation risk, can you even call it a guaranteed investment? That's just a regular partnership!
Rava
Also — if Abaye is right, the Mishna should have distinguished within the Jewish case rather than switching to gentiles!
Rava
Rather: Both cases are where the owner did NOT accept responsibility. The firstborn is still exempt because if the Jew defaults, the gentile can seize the offspring — his 'hand is in the middle.'
Rava's Principle Wherever the hand of a gentile is in the middle — i.e., the gentile retains potential claim to the animal or offspring — the firstborn laws do not apply.
