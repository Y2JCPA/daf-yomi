⚖️ Whose Soul Is Taken?

Rav Huna
The soul of the worker is taken — he climbs ladders and risks his life to earn these wages. To withhold them is to take his very life.
Rav Ḥisda
The soul of the robber/employer is taken — as Proverbs teaches, 'it takes away the life of its owners.' Withholding wages is ultimately self-destructive.
📖 Source Verse "For he sets his soul upon it" (Devarim 24:15) — the laborer literally stakes his life on receiving these wages

🏪 Storekeeper Transfer: Is the Laborer Protected?

Rabba
The laborer can still collect from the employer even after storekeeper transfer. The mishna only says employer 'does not transgress the prohibition' — the underlying debt to the laborer remains fully intact.
Opposing View
Once the employer transfers payment instructions to the storekeeper, the laborer may only collect from the storekeeper. The employer is fully released — the transaction is complete.
⚠️ Practical Stakes This debate determines whether a laborer is left destitute if the storekeeper goes bankrupt or refuses to pay after the employer transferred the debt

🔨 Day Laborer vs. Contractor (Kablan)

Day Laborer (Poʿel)

  • Paid by time — hour, day, or night
  • Wages due at end of work period
  • Employer clearly violates bal talin if unpaid
  • No ownership question — works entirely for employer
  • Standard oath ordinance applies in disputes

Contractor (Kablan)

  • Paid per completed job, not by time
  • Rav Sheshet: prohibition still applies
  • Key dispute: does craftsman acquire item through enhancement?
  • If craftsman 'owns' the enhanced vessel, wages = sale price — different rules
  • Baraita of the garment supports: owner still violates bal talin after notification

📜 The Rabbinic Oath Ordinance

תַּקָּנוּ חֲכָמִים — שֶׁיִּשָּׁבַע הַשָּׂכִיר וְיִטֹּל
תַּקָּנַת חֲכָמִים When a laborer claims wages and the employer says 'I already paid,' the laborer takes an oath and collects — even though logically the employer (as defendant) should swear.

Why transfer the oath to the claimant?
• The employer is preoccupied with many workers and may genuinely have forgotten
• Without this protection, laborers would refuse to hire themselves out
• Requiring witnesses for every payment would be an inconvenience to both parties

Exception: If the dispute is about the agreed amount (not whether it was paid), normal rules apply — employer swears.

⚖️ How the Laborer's Oath Mechanism Works

Laborer completes work and claims wages within the proper time (day-worker by night; night-worker by day)
↓
Employer disputes: "I already paid you your wages"
↓
Normally (Torah rule): the defendant (employer) would take an oath to exempt himself from paying
↓
Rabbinic ordinance (takana): oath is transferred to the laborer — the claimant swears instead
↓
Laborer swears: "I did not receive my wages" — sworn before a court
↓
Laborer immediately collects his wages from the employer based on his oath
↓
Caveat: If dispute concerns a fixed contractual amount (not payment vs. non-payment), employer retains duty to swear
