⚖️ Core Disputes of Daf 115

Rav Huna
Taking a lower millstone as collateral incurs two sets of lashes: one for the millstone prohibition, one for 'he takes a man's life as collateral.'
Rav Yehuda
Lower millstone = one set; upper millstone = one set; taking both together = two sets. The second prohibition applies only when both are taken.
Rabbi Yehuda
One may never take collateral from a widow — whether poor or wealthy. The verse makes no distinction.
Rabbi Shimon
Only a poor widow is protected — because you must return it anyway, making seizure pointless. A wealthy widow's collateral may be taken.
The Underlying Question Do we derive halakhic limits from the rationale stated in a verse (ta'am dikra) — Rabbi Shimon says yes, Rabbi Yehuda generally says no.

🏠 Prohibition on Entering the Borrower's Home

כִּי תַשֶּׁה בְרֵעֲךָ מַשַּׁאת מְאוּמָה לֹא תָבֹא אֶל בֵּיתוֹ לַעֲבֹט עֲבֹטוֹ
Deuteronomy 24:10 "When you lend your neighbor any manner of loan, you shall not go into his house to take his collateral."

The lender must stand outside and wait for the borrower to bring the pledge out. This protects the borrower's dignity in his own home.

Key rule: A loan secured by collateral is not canceled by the Sabbatical Year — unlike unsecured loans. This is one reason a creditor might re-take collateral each day even though he must return it.

👩 Widow's Collateral: Rabbi Yehuda vs. Rabbi Shimon

Rabbi Yehuda

  • A widow — whether poor or wealthy — may never have collateral taken
  • The verse (Deut 24:17) makes no distinction based on wealth
  • Does NOT derive law from the verse's stated rationale
  • Prohibition is absolute regardless of circumstance
  • Parallel ruling: A king may multiply wives — provided they won't turn his heart

Rabbi Shimon

  • Only a poor widow may not have collateral taken
  • Reason: you must return it to her — making seizure oppressive and meaningless
  • A wealthy widow may have collateral taken
  • Does interpret law based on the verse's rationale
  • Parallel ruling: A king may never multiply wives — the verse's reason always applies

📜 Do We Derive Law from the Verse's Own Rationale?

Verse states a reason:
'He may not multiply wives — that his heart not turn away' (Deut 17:17)
↓
Rabbi Yehuda
We do not use the stated reason to limit the law. The prohibition is absolute — even 18 wives who won't cause corruption are forbidden.
Rabbi Shimon
We do use the reason to determine scope. If the reason doesn't apply (wealthy widow won't suffer), the prohibition may not apply.
↓
Gemara's resolution: Even Rabbi Yehuda acknowledges the rationale when the verse explicitly states it — but he requires this to be spelled out; Rabbi Shimon infers it wherever the logic leads.

🥩 Abaye vs. Rava: Paschal Lamb & Overlapping Prohibitions

Rava
Eating the Paschal lamb raw incurs two sets of lashes: one for 'do not eat of it raw,' and one for 'but roasted with fire.' Each phrase is an independent prohibition — paralleling Rav Huna's two-lash ruling on the lower millstone.
Abaye
'But roasted with fire' is a general prohibition (lav klali) — one is not flogged for a general prohibition. Only one set of lashes applies — paralleling Rav Yehuda's single-lash ruling.
The Structural Parallel Rava can hold his position even per Rav Yehuda: the Pesach phrase 'but roasted with fire' adds a new prohibition beyond the general rule — unlike the millstone case where the second prohibition fully overlaps. Abaye can hold his view even per Rav Huna: the Pesach phrase is inherently general, unlike the millstone's specific second violation.
