QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why would a creditor bother re-taking collateral each day if he must return it by nightfall anyway?
A. To embarrass the debtor publicly each day
B. Because the Torah requires daily renewal of all loan contracts
C. A loan secured by collateral is not canceled by the Sabbatical Year, unlike an unsecured loan
D. To ensure the item is not damaged or lost
Correct: C — By continuously maintaining collateral, the loan stays 'secured' and is therefore exempt from cancellation during the Sabbatical Year — a significant financial benefit for the creditor.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does Deuteronomy 24:10 specifically prohibit when a creditor collects collateral?
A. Taking more than one item as collateral at a time
B. Entering the borrower's house to take the collateral
C. Taking collateral from someone who is not yet in default
D. Sending a messenger instead of going personally
Correct: B — The verse says 'you shall not go into his house to take his collateral.' The creditor must stand outside; the borrower must bring the pledge out himself, preserving his dignity.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Rabbi Shimon's ruling regarding taking collateral from a widow, and what is his reasoning?
A. Always forbidden — the verse is absolute
B. Forbidden only for a poor widow, because you must return it to her and it causes needless hardship
C. Permitted for all widows — the prohibition was rescinded
D. Permitted only with a rabbinical court's authorization
Correct: B — Rabbi Shimon derives law from the verse's rationale: the prohibition exists because returning collateral causes hardship. This applies only to a poor widow. A wealthy widow's collateral may be taken.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rav Huna, how many sets of lashes does one receive for taking only the lower millstone as collateral?
A. No lashes — only a monetary fine is imposed
B. One set of lashes for the millstone prohibition
C. Two sets of lashes
D. Three sets of lashes
Correct: C — Rav Huna holds: one set for the millstone prohibition (Deut 24:6), and a second set for 'he takes a man's life as collateral' — since a lower millstone is essential for food preparation.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does Abaye hold that eating the Paschal lamb raw incurs only one set of lashes, not two?
A. Eating raw meat is not a Torah-level prohibition
B. The phrase 'but roasted with fire' is a general prohibition (lav klali), for which one is not flogged
C. The Pesach sacrifice has special leniencies compared to other offerings
D. One set of lashes can cover multiple violations of the same type
Correct: B — Abaye maintains that 'but roasted with fire' is a general (klali) prohibition encompassing many cases — and the halakha is that one is not flogged for violating a general prohibition. Only the specific 'do not eat it raw' yields lashes.
