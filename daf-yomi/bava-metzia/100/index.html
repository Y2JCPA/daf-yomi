⚖️ Scope of Sumakhos: Certain vs. Uncertain Claims

Rabba bar Rav Huna
Sumakhos divides property even when both parties assert certain claims — wherever genuine ownership uncertainty exists, we divide rather than preserve the status quo ante.
Rava
Sumakhos only divides when both claims are themselves uncertain — 'I don't know.' When each party asserts a definite, certain claim, standard halakha (oath / ḥazakah) governs.
Rava (proof from mishna)
Proof: In the certain-vs.-certain case (large vs. small slave), the mishna requires an oath, not division — demonstrating Sumakhos does not apply there.
Rabba bar Rav Huna (reply)
Sumakhos himself concedes that where Torah law independently mandates an oath, division is displaced. The oath case is an exception — Sumakhos still divides all other certain claims.
Agreed Principle Both opinions accept: where Torah law requires an oath, the disputed amount is not divided — the oath obligation overrides Sumakhos' division rule.

🔍 Four Interpretations of the Slave/Field Mishna

Rav

  • Dispute is about money paid
  • Buyer: paid price of a large slave
  • Seller: admits receiving price of small slave
  • Partial-admission oath (modeh b'miktzat) applies

Shmuel

  • Dispute is about a garment
  • Buyer claims large slave's garment
  • Seller admits selling small slave's garment
  • Rejected: no partial admission — claimed ≠ admitted

Rabbi Hoshaya

  • Buyer claimed slave + garment
  • Seller admits to garment of small slave
  • Garment 'binds' claim to oath requirement
  • Challenged: merely repeats Kiddushin 26a ruling

Rav Sheshet (accepted)

  • Follows Rabbi Meir's opinion
  • Slave = legal status of movable property
  • Oath applies directly to slave disputes
  • Dispute over slave alone triggers oath

🌿 Olive Trees Sold for Wood — Who Takes the Olives?

Setup: A seller sells his olive trees to a buyer for their lumber value. Before the buyer chops them down, the trees produce olives.
↓
Small yield — less than ¼ log of oil per se'a: The seller did not intend to retain the trees' productive value. The buyer takes all the olives.
↓
Large yield — ¼ log per se'a or more: Buyer says 'my trees produced them.' Seller says 'the nourishment from my land grew them.'
↓
Ruling on large yield: They divide the olives — the buyer holds the trees, the seller contributed the land's nourishment that produced the fruit.
↓
River swept trees: If a river uproots trees and deposits them in another's field where they take root and fruit — same division applies between the original tree-owner and the field-owner.

עֶבֶד הֲרֵי הוּא כְּמִטַּלְטְלִין — רַבִּי מֵאִיר
📜 Rabbi Meir: Slaves Have the Status of Movable Property Rabbi Meir holds that slaves (עבדים) share the legal status of movable property (מטלטלין). Practically: an oath can be administered over slave-related disputes, unlike the standard rule exempting slaves from oath liability alongside land. The Gemara proves this from a baraita about cow/maidservant exchanges — the Rabbis explicitly distinguish slaves from land in rebutting Rabbi Meir, confirming that Rabbi Meir himself treats slaves like movables. Separately, Rav Sheshet holds Rabbi Meir does not distinguish slaves from land for these purposes, relying on Rabba bar Avuh's reversal of attributions in Bava Kamma 96b.
