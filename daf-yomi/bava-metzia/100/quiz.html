QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the mishna rule that the calf's value is divided between buyer and seller? What is Shmuel's explanation?
A. The cow died during the exchange, making ownership retroactively unclear
B. The calf is standing in the marsh — in neither party's domain — so neither has presumptive ownership
C. Both parties signed identical documents claiming the calf
D. The calf was born before the exchange was legally completed
Correct: B — Shmuel answers that the calf is in the marsh, neutral territory belonging to neither party, so neither holds ḥazakah over the calf and they divide its value.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the core dispute between Rava and Rabba bar Rav Huna about Sumakhos?
A. Whether Sumakhos applies only to land disputes or also to movable property
B. Whether Sumakhos divides property only when both claims are uncertain, or even when both parties assert definite claims
C. Whether Sumakhos requires a beit din of three or twenty-three judges
D. Whether Sumakhos's rule is biblical or rabbinic in origin
Correct: B — Rava: Sumakhos only divides when both parties say 'I don't know' (uncertain vs. uncertain). Rabba bar Rav Huna: Sumakhos divides even when both parties assert certain, confident claims.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rav Sheshet explain why the seller must take an oath in the slave/field mishna?
A. Because a partial monetary admission triggers the modeh b'miktzat oath
B. Because the mishna follows Rabbi Meir, who holds slaves have the status of movable property — enabling oath obligations
C. Because the garment attached to the slave 'binds' the claim and triggers an oath
D. Because Sumakhos concedes that oaths are required in slave disputes
Correct: B — Rav Sheshet explains the mishna follows Rabbi Meir, who treats slaves as movable property. This means oath law — normally inapplicable to slaves — can apply directly to the slave dispute.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In the olive tree sale, what determines whether the buyer alone takes the olives or they divide them?
A. Whether the seller explicitly retained rights to the fruit at the time of sale
B. Whether the trees were chopped within 30 days of the sale
C. Whether the yield reaches a quarter-log of oil per se'a — below that the buyer takes all; at or above that they divide
D. Whether witnesses observed the olives growing before or after the sale
Correct: C — The mishna's threshold is a quarter-log of oil per se'a. Below that, the buyer takes all. At or above that amount, the buyer (tree owner) and seller (whose land nourished the trees) divide the olives.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Sumakhos concedes that in the 'certain vs. certain' slave case the parties do NOT divide — instead an oath is required. What is the reason for this exception?
A. Because Sumakhos never intended his rule to apply to slaves, only to monetary disputes
B. Because where Torah law independently requires an oath, the oath obligation takes precedence over Sumakhos's division rule
C. Because the seller's certain claim always defeats the buyer's certain claim under biblical law
D. Because ḥazakah (presumptive ownership) of the seller overrides Sumakhos in all disputed sales
Correct: B — The Gemara states: Sumakhos concedes that where Torah requires an oath (as in a definite claim vs. definite claim dispute), the oath displaces his division rule. Torah oath law is the stronger override.
