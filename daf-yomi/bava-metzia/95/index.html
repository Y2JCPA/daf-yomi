📜 Deriving Borrower Liability and the Sho'el Im Ba'al Exemption

Exodus 22:13: 'And it is injured or dies' — Torah explicitly makes borrower liable for injury and death
↓
Theft & Loss?
Attempted: Derive by similarity — injury/death are accidents, theft/loss are accidents
Refutation
Injury/death are distinguishable — owner cannot prevent them. Theft/loss are different.
↓
Baraita's Solution: A fortiori from paid bailee — he is still liable for theft/loss even though he can take an oath to exempt himself; borrower who has no such oath is certainly liable
↓
Sho'el Im Ba'al (שְׁאֵלָה בְּבַעְלֶיהָ): Borrower exempt when owner's services were borrowed together with the item — but scope of exemption is disputed
↓
Central Dispute: Rav Aḥa vs. Ravina — does sho'el im ba'al exemption cover negligence? And Rav Hamnuna — what exact conditions trigger the exemption?

📜 Step-by-Step: Deriving Borrower's Liability for Theft and Loss

Base verse: Exodus 22:13 — 'And it is injured or dies' gives borrower explicit liability for injury and death
↓
First attempt: Since borrower is liable for injury/death (accidents), derive by similarity (mah matzinu) that theft/loss also make him liable
↓
Refutation: Injury/death cases have a unique feature — owner cannot prevent them; theft/loss cannot be compared
↓
Baraita's a fortiori: Paid bailee is liable for theft/loss even though he can exempt himself by oath — borrower who cannot take such an oath is certainly liable for theft/loss
↓
Refutation sealed: Even armed bandit case does not break the inference — either the bailee pays principal without oath, or armed bandit = robber (double payment). Borrower's liability stands.

🤝 Rav Aḥa and Ravina: Negligence in Sho'el Im Ba'al

Rav Aḥa
A borrower who borrowed the owner's services IS LIABLE when the mishap is due to negligence. The verse 'And if he borrows' (Exodus 22:13) links the borrower's passage back to the PAID BAILEE — so the owner-exemption verse in the borrower's passage applies only to paid bailee cases (theft/loss), not to a borrower's negligence.
Ravina
A borrower who borrowed the owner's services is EXEMPT even for negligence. The verse 'And if he borrows' connects to the BORROWER's own passage — the owner's presence fully exempts him regardless of how the mishap occurred, including negligence.
Rav Aḥa
Corollary: An UNPAID BAILEE who borrowed the owner's services is NOT exempt for negligence — the relevant exemption verse was already repurposed to teach something else.
Ravina
Corollary: An UNPAID BAILEE who borrowed the owner's services IS exempt for negligence — the verse derives this through the borrower's passage, covering all bailee types.
Disputed Verse Exodus 22:13–14: 'And if he borrows… if its owner is with him, he does not pay' — which passage does this connect to?

⚖️ Four Bailees: Liability and Sho'el Im Ba'al Exemption

Unpaid Bailee (שומר חינם)

  • Exempt: theft & loss
  • Liable: negligence
  • Sho'el im ba'al: disputed (Rav Aḥa vs. Ravina) whether negligence is covered

Paid Bailee (שומר שכר)

  • Liable: theft & loss
  • Exempt: injury & death
  • Sho'el im ba'al: exempt for theft & loss (explicitly derived)

Renter (שוכר)

  • R. Yehuda: like paid bailee
  • R. Meir: like unpaid bailee
  • Baraita interpretation depends on which opinion is followed

Borrower (שואל)

  • Liable: ALL mishaps including injury & death
  • Sho'el im ba'al: exempt (biblical source)
  • Negligence with sho'el im ba'al: Rav Aḥa (liable) vs. Ravina (exempt)

📍 Rav Hamnuna: Narrow Conditions for Sho'el Im Ba'al

שְׁאֵלָה בְּבַעְלֶיהָ — Exodus 22:14: אִם בְּעָלָיו עִמּוֹ לֹא יְשַׁלֵּם
Rav Hamnuna's Ruling Rav Hamnuna severely limits the exemption to two precise conditions:

Condition 1 — Same work: The owner must be engaged in the same work as the animal — e.g., the owner plowing together with his cow that was borrowed. Working in a different place does not suffice.

Condition 2 — Continuous service: The owner must have been working for the borrower from the time of borrowing through the time of the mishap — no gaps allowed.

Rava's objection: The baraita implies the exemption applies even when the owner 'performed work in another place' — suggesting the owner need not work alongside the animal itself!

Gemara's resolution: 'Another place' means a different field — but still with the same animal and the same work.
