⚖️ The Central Dispute: Is the Collateral-Holding Lender a Paid Bailee?

Rabbi Eliezer
The lender holding collateral is a paid bailee. He benefits — his loan is secured by the item — so he bears greater responsibility for its safekeeping.
Rabbi Akiva
The lender holding collateral is an unpaid bailee. He is performing a mitzva by lending money; we do not burden a mitzva-doer with paid bailee liability.
Shmuel
If a creditor holds collateral equal to the loan and the item is lost, the loan is cancelled. This 'benefit' of debt-cancellation is what makes him a paid bailee — he gains from holding the object.
Gemara
Their dispute applies only when collateral was taken at the time of the loan. If taken later (to collect a debt), all agree with Rabbi Yitzchak — the creditor acquires collateral ownership by verse-derivation.
Core Question Does holding another's property for one's own benefit — whether financial security or mitzva merit — raise or lower the holder's level of liability?

📊 Three Opinions on the Lender's Bailee Category

Rabbi Yehuda

  • Lender of money = unpaid bailee
  • Lender of produce = paid bailee
  • Produce lender saves storage costs → greater benefit → higher duty
  • Anonymous mishna implies this position

Rabbi Akiva

  • Collateral-holding lender = unpaid bailee
  • He performs mitzva of lending — merit, not profit
  • Mitzva-doer should not bear extra burden
  • Most anonymous mishnayot follow Akiva's school

Rabbi Eliezer

  • Collateral-holding lender = paid bailee
  • His loan is secured — a tangible financial benefit
  • Greater benefit = greater halakhic responsibility
  • Shmuel's debt-cancellation principle supports this

📜 Rabbi Yitzchak's Verse Derivation + Abba Shaul's Ruling

הַשֵּׁב תָּשִׁיב לוֹ אֶת הַעֲבוֹט כְּבוֹא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ — דברים כד:יג
Two Important Halakhot Rabbi Yitzchak asks: From where is it derived that a creditor acquires his collateral — i.e., is considered its owner while holding it?

From the verse: "You shall return the pledge to him when the sun sets" (Devarim 24:13). The Torah commands returning it — implying the creditor currently owns it and must actively give it back.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Abba Shaul rules: A creditor may rent out a poor person's collateral, applying the rental income to progressively reduce the outstanding debt. This is permitted — and even praiseworthy — because it benefits the poor borrower. Without this justification, using another's property for profit would constitute forbidden ribbis (interest).
