QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Huna (the Master), if a buyer says 'Sell me your item for these coins' and underpays by one-sixth, what happens?
A. The buyer acquires the item and the seller has an ona'ah claim
B. The transaction is void because the price is unfair
C. The buyer acquires the item with no ona'ah claim since it is pure chalifin
D. Neither party acquires anything until the price difference is resolved
Correct: A — Rav Huna holds that the buyer acquires immediately (similar to chalifin since the seller isn't particular about the sum), but the use of sale language ('sell me') means ona'ah rules still apply and the seller can claim the difference.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In the cow-donkey exchange story, why doesn't the donkey owner acquire the cow even though he pulled it?
A. Because the cow owner never explicitly agreed to the exchange
B. Because the parties were particular about prices, making it like a sale rather than chalifin
C. Because pulling a cow is not a valid act of acquisition
D. Because the donkey died before any agreement was reached
Correct: B — Since both parties stated specific prices and were particular about equal value, the transaction functions more like a sale than standard chalifin. The acquisition is not complete until each party pulls their respective item. (Rava reinterprets this as involving an incomplete pull of cow + lamb.)
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the fundamental disagreement between R' Yochanan and Reish Lakish about meshikhah (pulling)?
A. Whether meshikhah applies to land transactions
B. Whether meshikhah must be performed by the buyer or seller
C. Whether meshikhah as a requirement for acquisition is Torah law or a rabbinic decree
D. Whether meshikhah can be performed by a third party agent
Correct: C — R' Yochanan holds that by Torah law money alone acquires movable property, and the Sages instituted meshikhah as a decree to protect buyers. Reish Lakish holds meshikhah is required by Torah law itself, derived from Leviticus 25:14 ('from your colleague's hand').
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to R' Yochanan, what is an asimon?
A. A bathhouse entry token
B. An unimprinted metal blank in the shape of a coin
C. A worn coin whose imprint has faded
D. A foreign coin not accepted as local currency
Correct: B — R' Yochanan defines an asimon as an unimprinted metal blank — shaped like a coin but not yet stamped. He rejects Rav's definition of a bathhouse token because a baraita lists asimon and bathhouse tokens as separate categories.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why did the Sages specifically worry about spontaneous fire (ones) rather than arson in their decree requiring meshikhah?
A. Because arson was extremely rare in Talmudic times
B. Because an arsonist is liable to pay damages, so the buyer would be compensated anyway
C. Because the Torah only discusses spontaneous fires
D. Because arson would void the entire transaction automatically
Correct: B — The Gemara clarifies: if someone deliberately sets a fire, the arsonist must pay damages — so the buyer is protected. The concern is specifically spontaneous fire (ones/circumstances beyond control) where no one is liable. Without meshikhah, the seller has no incentive to save goods that legally belong to the buyer.
