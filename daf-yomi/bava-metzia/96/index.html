🕰️ When Must the Owner Be Present — At Borrowing or at Injury?

Rabbi Yoshiya
The owner must be present at the time of the injury or death — that is the moment of liability, so that is the decisive moment for the exemption.
Rabbi Yonatan
The owner must be present at the time of borrowing — the borrowing is what creates liability in the first place, so presence at that moment exempts.
Rav Ashi
Proof from the verse itself: 'And when a man borrows' — the Torah places the owner's presence at the act of borrowing. The time of borrowing is the decisive moment.
Gemara
Counter: The time of injury/death is more significant — that is when the borrower actually becomes liable to pay for mishaps that occurred!
Rava
No — borrowing is more fundamental. Without the initial borrowing, there would be no liability at all. The injury alone cannot generate liability ex nihilo.
Conclusion The halakha follows Rabbi Yonatan: owner's presence at time of borrowing (שְׁאֵלָה) determines exemption, as Rav Ashi derives from the verse in Exodus 22:13.

🔍 Rav Ashi's Derivation from the Verse

Exodus 22:13: 'And when a man borrows from another… he shall pay'
↓
The verse places liability at the moment of borrowing — this is the act that creates the obligation
↓
Owner present at borrowing
Borrower is EXEMPT — שְׁאֵלָה בְּבְעָלִים
Owner absent at borrowing
Borrower is LIABLE even if owner present later
↓
Why do we still need 'if its owner is with him'? — To teach that presence at any subsequent point does NOT retroactively exempt

💍 What Is a Husband's Status Regarding Wife's Usufruct Property?

Like a Borrower?

  • Uses the property without paying
  • If so: borrowing + owner's services = exempt
  • Problem: wife is the owner, and she is always 'with him' — he should always be exempt!
  • Also: her presence is involuntary — she did not choose to lend

Like a Renter?

  • Pays value through ketubbah obligations
  • If so: renter + owner's services = still liable
  • Problem: still creates awkward result for all scenarios
  • Rava rejects both options for the basic case

Like a Buyer (Rava's View)

  • Husband acquires usufruct rights completely
  • Based on Rabbi Yosei b. Rabbi Hanina's statement
  • He is liable for depreciation like an owner
  • Resolves Rami bar Hama's dilemma entirely

🏛️ Misuse of Consecrated Property (מְעִילָה) via Marriage

Rami bar Hama
If a wife's usufruct property includes consecrated items and the husband acquires them through marriage — who has committed מְעִילָה (misuse of Temple property)?
Rava
Not the husband — he desires non-sacred items, not consecrated ones. Sacred property is not something he wants or expects to receive.
Rava
Not the wife — she did not want to transfer it; the rabbinic ordinance compelled the transfer against her wishes.
Rava
Not the court — when the Sages enacted the ordinance they meant it only for non-sacred items; the ordinance never applied to consecrated property.
Rava — Conclusion
The husband commits מְעִילָה only when he actually spends the money — just like one who spends consecrated coins inadvertently for personal use.
Principle מְעִילָה requires an affirmative act of personal benefit from consecrated property; passive acquisition alone does not trigger liability.

🪓 Borrower Liability for Animal Worn Down by Labor (96b)

Dilemma Raised: If the flesh of a borrowed animal was weakened (נִרְזָה בְּשָׂרָה) due to labor performed for the borrower — is the borrower liable?
↓
Rav Hilkiya's Challenge: Your question implies you assume the borrower IS liable when the animal dies from ordinary labor — but that is itself uncertain!
↓
Rava's Ruling: Not only is weakened flesh exempt — even if the animal dies from ordinary labor, the borrower is EXEMPT. He can tell the owner: 'I borrowed it to work with — it died doing exactly that work.'
↓
Practical Case — Borrowed Ax Broke: Borrower came before Rava. Rava said: Bring witnesses that you used it normally (לֹא שִׁינָּה) and you are exempt from liability.
↓
No Witnesses? A precedent from Rav's court: borrower came before Rav after ax broke with no witnesses. Rav required an oath — Rav Kahana and Rav Asi challenged this ruling...
