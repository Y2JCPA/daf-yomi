QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why did the Sages allow a hired laborer to take an oath and collect his wages — departing from the usual rule that oaths exempt rather than collect?
A. The Torah explicitly grants laborers this exceptional right
B. Employers are often busy and forget to obtain receipts, so the Sages empowered the laborer to protect him
C. Laborers are always presumed more credible than employers
D. The oath only works if the laborer has at least one witness
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that employers are occupied managing their workers and often neglect to write receipts. The Sages enacted the laborer's oath specifically to protect the worker in this structurally unequal situation.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
A landlord said 'I rent you this house' for ten years. The house falls down after three years. What is the halacha?
A. The landlord must rebuild the same house
B. The landlord must provide a comparable replacement house
C. The rental agreement terminates; the landlord owes no replacement
D. The tenant receives a pro-rated refund for the unused years
Correct: C — Since the landlord specified 'this house,' the rental was tied to that specific structure. Once it is gone, so is the agreement. Had he said 'a house like this' or specified dimensions, a replacement would be required.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Rav Naḥman permitted borrowing a utensil 'in good working order' indefinitely. What did Rav Mari (son of Shmuel's daughter) add as a requirement?
A. The borrower must pay a nominal fee each time he takes the utensil
B. The owner must verbally re-consent each time the borrower takes it back
C. The borrower must perform a formal act of acquisition (kinyan) to establish the ongoing right
D. The borrower may only take it during daylight hours
Correct: C — Rav Mari ruled that without a kinyan, once the borrower returns the utensil the open-ended borrowing right lapses. Only a formal act of acquisition locks in the indefinite borrowing arrangement.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rava, a person borrows a hoe saying: 'Lend it to me to dig up an orchard.' How many orchards may he use it on?
A. Only the orchard he happened to dig first
B. Any one orchard of his choosing
C. As many orchards as he owns
D. Only orchards with the same type of trees
Correct: B — Rava distinguishes: 'this orchard' limits use to that specific plot; 'an orchard' (indefinite) allows use on any one orchard. The borrower chose the general phrasing, so he picks which orchard — but only one.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
A tenant receives an irrigated field under a fixed-rent tenancy. Mid-lease, the side channel supplying the field dries up. What does the Mishna rule?
A. The tenant is fully exempt from further rent payments
B. The tenant subtracts proportionally based on lost irrigation
C. The tenant still owes the full agreed rental amount
D. The landlord must restore irrigation at his own expense
Correct: C — The Mishna rules that the tenant does not subtract from his rental. He assumed the natural risks of the specific field when accepting the tenancy. The Gemara notes this applies to a side channel; if the entire main river dried up, the analysis may differ.
