Bava Metzia 73: When Is a Discount Really Interest?

Core Question:
When does a favorable price constitute ribbit (interest)?
↓
Permitted
Seller bears risk of loss
OR legitimate business discount
OR gift for goodwill
Prohibited
Buyer bears risk = it's a loan
Extra payment = interest
No genuine risk involved
Key Principle The presence of genuine risk or legitimate business motivation transforms what might appear as interest into a permissible transaction.

Transporting Produce: Who Bears the Risk?

Someone says: "Give me your produce now,
I'll repay you with produce at your destination"
↓
He HAS produce there
✅ Permitted
It's an exchange, not a loan
He does NOT have produce
❌ Prohibited
It's a loan — price may change
↓
Similarly with packages:
If seller bears accident risk → Permitted
If buyer bears accident risk → Prohibited (= loan with interest)
Principle When the seller retains risk, it's a sale. When the buyer bears risk without ownership, it becomes a loan.

Why Can Donkey Drivers Sell at Discount Rates?

Gemara
Donkey drivers may accept money and set prices at expensive-city rates based on the cheap-city rate. Why is this permitted?
Rav Pappa
It benefits them because the gates of the new market are opened — they gain new customers in the area.
Rav Aḥa b"R Ika
It benefits them because sellers give them discounts — hearing the drivers must resell cheaply, suppliers lower their prices.
Gemara
Practical difference? A new merchant: Rav Pappa permits (he especially needs market access), but Rav Aḥa prohibits (suppliers won't trust him yet).
Nafka Mina A new donkey driver entering a market: Rav Pappa allows the discount (to open markets); Rav Aḥa bar Rav Ika does not (suppliers won't believe him).

Rav's Wheat Purchase: Sura vs. Kafri

DetailRuling
Price in Sura4 se'a per sela
Price in Kafri6 se'a per sela
Rav's arrangementPaid donkey drivers, accepted risk of accidents
What Rav accepted5 se'a per sela (not 6)
Why not 6?Adam chashuv shani — an important person must be stricter
PrincipleEven when technically permitted, prominent figures avoid the appearance of ribbit
Adam Chashuv Rav was entitled to 6 se'a per sela but took only 5. A prominent Torah scholar must hold himself to a higher standard to avoid even the appearance of impropriety.

Buying an Orchard's Produce in Advance

Rav
Prohibited. The produce will be worth more at harvest — the discount looks like ribbit for waiting.
Shmuel
Permitted. Since produce can spoil and the buyer accepts that risk, it's not interest — it's a gamble.
Rav Shimi b. Ḥiyya
Even Rav concedes it's permitted for young oxen — their risk of loss is so great, it's clearly an investment, not a loan.
Shmuel
To those buying vine shoots: Turn over some land yourselves — become partners, so it's not treated as a loan.
Resolution The greater the risk of loss the buyer accepts, the less the transaction resembles a loan with interest. Rav agrees when risk is substantial (e.g., raising oxen).

Rav Mari bar Rachel's Mortgaged House

Rav Mari's Position

  • Gentile mortgaged house to him for a loan
  • Standard mortgage = 1 year occupancy
  • Gentile could not evict him for 12 months
  • After 12 months: brought rent money to Rava

Rava's Response

  • "Had I known, I wouldn't have bought it"
  • Acts by dina d'nochrim (gentile law)
  • Under gentile law: no rent owed while mortgage active
  • "I won't take rent until the gentile repays you"
Dina D'Nochrim Rava assumed the gentile's legal position: under gentile law, a mortgagee owes no rent while the mortgage is active, and Rava honored that arrangement.
