Why Require an Oath? Sumakhos vs. the Mishna

Questioner
Sumakhos says uncertain property is divided without an oath. Our mishna has an even stronger case — both may own it! Why require an oath?
R' Yochanan
The mishna can agree with Sumakhos. This oath is rabbinic (תקנת חכמים) — so that everyone will not go and seize another's garment and say: 'It is mine.'
Questioner
So the oath is not Torah-mandated here, but a deterrent against false claims?
R' Yochanan
Exactly. Without the oath requirement, people would freely grab property and claim ownership with no consequence.
Key Insight The oath in the two-holders case is not from Torah law but a rabbinic enactment to prevent moral hazard.

R' Yosei vs. the Rabbis: The Deposit Case

The Rabbis

  • Two depositors: one deposited 100, one deposited 200 dinars
  • Each claims the 200 — one is certainly lying
  • Each receives 100 dinars (the undisputed minimum)
  • Remaining 100 held until Elijah comes

R' Yosei

  • If swindler still gets his 100 — what did he lose?
  • No deterrent for fraud!
  • ENTIRE deposit placed in safekeeping
  • All funds held until Elijah comes
Principle R' Yosei believes the ruling must penalize the swindler — 'מה הפסיד הרמאי' (what did the swindler lose?).

Can the Mishna Follow R' Yosei?

R' Yosei: Hold everything until Elijah comes when there's a certain swindler
↓
In the garment case — should we also hold until Elijah?
↓
Challenge
If R' Yosei holds everything even when one is certainly a swindler, kal va'chomer here where one may be entirely fraudulent!
Resolution
In the deposit — a swindler certainly exists. In the garment — maybe both lifted it simultaneously. No proven fraud!
Distinction The key: deposit = certain swindler (רמאי ודאי); garment = possibly both are truthful.

Modeh B'Miktzat — Psychology of Partial Admission

💰 Creditor claims: 'You owe me the full amount!'
↓
🧠 Debtor thinks: 'I want to admit everything, but I don't have the money right now'
↓
🤐 Debtor partially denies: 'I only owe you part' — to buy time until he can pay
↓
📖 Torah response: Impose an oath — this will pressure him to admit the full truth
↓
❓ But what about witnesses proving part of the debt? Debtor may be completely dishonest!
↓
⚡ R' Chiyya: Kal va'chomer — if self-admission triggers an oath, witnesses certainly should too
Psychological Insight The Torah assumes a partial denier has a conscience (אין אדם מעיז פניו) — he wants to repay but can't yet. The oath nudges him toward full honesty.
