QUESTION 1 OF 5
A creditor and debtor dispute the duration of a mortgage. The creditor consumed produce during the disputed extra year. According to Rav Kahana's ruling (as upheld by the Gemara), what happens to that produce?
A. It is returned to the debtor since the creditor's claim is unproven
B. The produce remains with whoever consumed it
C. The produce is split equally between both parties
D. The dispute is sent to a court of three judges to adjudicate
Correct: B — Rav Kahana rules that produce already consumed remains with whoever consumed it. The Gemara upholds this because the dispute involves a 'matter that will be revealed' — witnesses who signed the document can clarify — distinguishing it from unresolvable disputes.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
A day laborer finishes work at sunset on Monday. According to the Gemara, when does the employer violate Bal Talin?
A. Immediately at sunset if wages are not paid on the spot
B. At midnight Monday night if wages are still unpaid
C. At Tuesday morning (sunrise) if wages have not been paid
D. At Tuesday sunset — the employer has a full 24 hours
Correct: C — The verse in Leviticus 19:13 states 'shall not remain with you all night until the morning.' The day laborer's wage obligation arises at day's end; the employer may pay all night but violates Bal Talin if the wages remain unpaid through the following morning.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Orphans inherit a field mortgaged to a creditor and claim they enhanced the land. According to Rabbi Yoḥanan's ruling, who bears the burden of proof?
A. The creditor must prove no enhancement occurred, as he seeks to take the field
B. The orphans must prove they made the enhancement
C. Neither party — the court appoints an appraiser automatically
D. The burden shifts based on which party currently possesses the field's produce
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan ruled that the orphans bear the burden of proof. Although Rabbi Ḥanina initially reasoned that current possession favors the orphans, Rabbi Yoḥanan established that the creditor's prior lien is the presumptive claim; the orphans, as challengers, must substantiate their enhancement.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
When workers are not paid on time and it emerges that an agent hired them on an employer's behalf, who violates the prohibition of Bal Talin?
A. Only the employer, since he is ultimately the one who owes the wages
B. Only the agent, since he directly contracted with the workers
C. Both the employer and the agent are equally liable
D. Neither the employer nor the agent violates Bal Talin
Correct: D — The Gemara teaches that neither party violates Bal Talin: the employer did not directly hire the workers (the agent did), and the agent is not the one who owes the wages (the employer is the wage-debtor). The prohibition requires a direct debtor-creditor relationship between employer and laborer.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Shmuel (as cited by Rav Naḥman), when a creditor removes buyers from mortgaged land, how are the buyers compensated for improvements they made?
A. The buyers forfeit their improvements — this is the risk of buying encumbered land
B. The buyers receive a portion of the land equivalent in value to their enhancement
C. The buyers receive money equal to the value of their enhancement
D. The creditor and buyer divide the improved land equally by area
Correct: C — Shmuel rules that in the three cases where courts appraise enhancements, compensation is paid in money, not land. Rabbi Ḥanina had proposed giving orphans a parcel of land equal to their enhancement, but the Gemara corrects this: the established practice is monetary compensation, following Shmuel's ruling.
