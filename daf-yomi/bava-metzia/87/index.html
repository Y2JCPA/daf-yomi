🗣️ Righteous Say Little, Do Much — vs. Wicked

Abraham — Righteous

  • Said: 'I will fetch a morsel of bread'
  • Did: Ran to herd, selected the finest calf
  • Served: Curd, milk, tender veal — a lavish feast
  • Principle: Say little, do much

Ephron — Wicked

  • Said: '400 shekels — what is that between us?'
  • Did: Demanded and counted every single shekel
  • Initially offered the field as a free gift
  • Principle: Say much, do little

👴 Origins of Aging, Illness, and Recovery

Before Abraham: No visible aging — people confused Abraham with Isaac since they looked identical
↓
Abraham prayed for recognizable old age → God granted it; wrinkles and white hair distinguished elders from youth
↓
Before Jacob: No pre-death illness — people died suddenly without warning or time to prepare
↓
Jacob prayed for illness before death → God granted it; 'Behold, your father is ill' (Gen. 48:1) was the first such notice
↓
Elisha fell ill three times: for banishing Gehazi with both hands, for inciting King Jehoram's rage, and a third — the illness from which he died

☮️ God Altered Truth for the Sake of Peace

גָּדוֹל הַשָּׁלוֹם שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא שִׁינָּה בּוֹ
School of Rabbi Yishmael — Principle of Peace Sarah said 'And my lord [Abraham] is old' (Gen. 18:12), but when God reported her words to Abraham, He quoted her as saying only 'I am old' — omitting her reference to Abraham's age to prevent marital strife. Peace is so great that even the Holy One, Blessed be He, altered the truth to preserve it.

🌾 Deriving the Laborer's Right to Eat from Produce

Gemara
From where is it derived that a laborer may eat from produce? The Torah says: 'When you come into your neighbor's vineyard, you may eat grapes' (Deut. 23:25). But this covers only grapes in a vineyard — what about grain, olives, figs?
Challenge
We try comparing vineyard to standing grain. But each comparison can be refuted: a vineyard has olelot obligations; standing grain has ḥalla obligations. No clean analogy works!
Shmuel
The verse says 'you shall not wave a sickle over your neighbor's standing grain' (Deut. 23:26). The word 'sickle' extends the eating right to any produce requiring a sickle at harvest — meaning all field crops.
Rabbi Yitzchak
The unmodified term kama (standing) includes all standing produce of any kind. Together, 'sickle' and 'standing' cover every agricultural product.
Rava
The vineyard verse is still needed for its unique rules: the laborer must work for a Jewish employer, may not eat consecrated produce, and his rights parallel the owner's own relationship to the vineyard.
Conclusion Both 'sickle' and 'standing (kama)' expand the laborer's eating right to all produce. The vineyard verse supplies specific limiting conditions.

🍇 When May a Laborer Eat from Produce?

Laborer works with agricultural produce
↓
Attached to ground
May eat only at time of completion of work (e.g., during harvest)
Detached from ground
May eat after processing is complete (tithing stage reached)
↓
Additional conditions apply (Deut. 23:25–26)
↓
✅ Permitted
Eating until satisfied • Jewish employer • Non-consecrated produce • Eating while working
❌ Prohibited
Placing in vessel to take home • Excessive eating • Working for gentile (per one view) • Consecrated produce
