⚒️ Returning a Broken Borrowed Tool

Rav Kahana & Rav Asi
The borrower must return the broken tool to its owner AND pay the monetary difference between the tool's original value and its broken value.
Rav (initially)
Is it not sufficient to simply pay the difference in value between the tool before and after it broke? Why return the broken pieces at all?
Rav (after challenge)
Rav was silent — he could not answer. The halakha follows Rav Kahana and Rav Asi: return the broken item plus a monetary supplement.
📜 Halakha The borrower returns the broken object AND pays the monetary difference to make the lender whole — not merely a cash payment for the full loss.

👨‍🏫 She'ela B'Ba'alim — Exemption by Concurrent Work

🔓 Borrower EXEMPT

  • Say to lender: "Pour me water" before borrowing — lender serves borrower first
  • Borrow from the town teacher during teaching hours
  • Borrow from the town butcher during work
  • Borrow from the town gardener, bloodletter, or scribe at their work
  • During Kalla gatherings (Elul/Adar), teacher is 'lent' to students → borrowing from teacher = exempt

🔒 Borrower LIABLE

  • Lender says: "Borrow first, then I'll pour water" — loan precedes any service
  • Borrowing from a private individual not engaged in communal work
  • Borrowing from Rava outside Kalla — he is not 'lent' to his students at all times
  • Rava's retort to his students: "Am I your slave?" — Torah teaching ≠ servitude

❓ Does 'I Don't Know' Create Liability? — Rav Naḥman's View

Gemara (from Mishna)
The mishna shows that when someone says "I don't know" in response to a monetary claim, they may be held liable — proof against Rav Naḥman!
Rav Naḥman
One who says "I have 100 dinars in your possession" and the other says "I don't know" — the defendant is exempt. Uncertainty alone cannot compel payment.
Gemara (defense of Rav Naḥman)
The mishna is different: there is already a matter of an oath between them (one party is clearly obligated to swear), so the 'I don't know' functions differently — it creates liability in that specific context.
Rava
The principle applies when there is a pre-existing oath obligation — only then does 'I don't know' shift the burden. Rav Naḥman's rule stands for ordinary cases.
⚖️ Key Distinction 'I don't know' creates liability only where an oath is already in play between the parties — not as a standalone response to any monetary claim.

🐴 Mareimar's Mule — She'ela B'Ba'alim Applied to Rental

Mareimar bar Ḥanina rents his mule to residents of Bei Ḥozai for transport
↓
Mareimar himself goes out and helps load cargo onto the mule alongside the renters
↓
The renters are negligent and the mule dies — or: the mule is stolen and dies at the thief's
↓
Rava rules: Since the owner participated in the loading, this is a case of she'ela b'ba'alim
↓
Even though it was a rental (not a loan), the owner's active participation at the moment of use creates the exemption
↓
Caveat: This works for those who hold that she'ela b'ba'alim exempts even negligence — disputed by those who require the stricter reading
