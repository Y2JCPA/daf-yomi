⚖️ Four Types of Bailees — Liability Spectrum

Unpaid Bailee (Shomer Chinam)

  • Swears for all mishaps
  • Exempt even for theft & loss
  • Liable only for negligence

Paid Bailee (Shomer Sachar)

  • Pays for theft and loss
  • Swears for injury, capture, death
  • Exempt for true ones

Renter (Socher)

  • Same halakha as paid bailee
  • Not stated explicitly in Torah
  • Rabbi Meir: like paid bailee

Borrower (Sho'el)

  • Highest liability of all
  • Pays for nearly all damage
  • Exempt only if owner worked alongside

🌾 Watchmen of Produce: Who May Eat by Local Custom?

Rav
Only watchmen of gardens and orchards (attached produce) eat by local regulations — since they have no Torah right to eat, local custom steps in to grant them access.
Shmuel
Only watchmen of winepresses and detached piles eat by local custom. Watchmen of attached garden produce may eat neither by Torah nor by custom — Torah already defined who may eat.
Rav Ashi
The halakha follows Shmuel — the Mishna (87a) distinguishes between attached and detached produce for laborers, proving that detached produce is where custom operates.
Resolution Watchmen of detached produce (winepresses, piles) may eat by local custom. Watchmen of gardens eat neither by Torah law nor by custom, per Shmuel's ruling.

🐑 Paid Bailee's Duty: The Shepherd & the Lion

Shepherd (paid bailee) is guarding a flock
↓
Dangerous predator (lion, wolf) approaches
↓
Rabba's View
Normal safeguarding suffices. If shepherd entered city when others normally do → exempt. No obligation beyond ordinary care.
Abaye's Challenge
If predator was present, shepherd must hire extra guards (up to full value of flock). Failure to do so = negligence → liable.
↓
Conclusion: Paid bailee must hire guards up to the animals' full value — but cannot be required to risk his own life

⚔️ Rabba vs. Abaye: Armed Shepherd, Armed Bandit

Rabba
When a shepherd heard a lion roaring and fled to the city — this is ones. His initial action was not negligent; the circumstance forced him. He is exempt.
Abaye
But this logic applies equally to an unpaid bailee! You yourself ruled that an unpaid bailee who could face a wolf alongside other shepherds but didn't — is liable. Why distinguish paid bailee here?
Rabba
The paid bailee's extra duty: he must hire additional guards up to the full value of the flock. This obligation — not risk of his own life — is what sets him apart.
Rav Pappa
What benefit does the owner gain if the cost of extra guards equals the animals' value?
Abaye: The practical difference is quality and loyalty — the shepherd's own family members serve as better guards.
Unresolved Dilemma If a bandit is armed and the shepherd is armed — must the shepherd fight? Perhaps the bandit risks his life and the shepherd will not; the question is left open (תיקו).
