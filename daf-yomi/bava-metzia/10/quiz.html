QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Nachman and Rav Chisda, why can't one person lift a found item to acquire it on behalf of another?
A. Because lifting requires personal intent (kavvanah)
B. Because it's like seizing assets for a creditor at others' expense
C. Because the Torah requires the finder to keep it
D. Because agency doesn't apply to ownerless items
Correct: B — Rav Nachman and Rav Chisda compare this to seizing a debtor's assets for one creditor, disadvantaging other creditors. Since everyone has equal rights to ownerless property, one person cannot deprive all others of that right on behalf of a third party.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
When does a laborer's found item belong to the employer?
A. Always, since the employer is paying for his time
B. Only when the employer specified the exact task to perform
C. When the employer said 'Work for me today' without specifying the task
D. Never — a laborer always keeps what he finds
Correct: C — When the employer gives a general instruction ('Work for me today'), finding ownerless items falls within the general category of work, so the item belongs to the employer. But when a specific task is assigned ('Weed for me'), finding items is outside that scope.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to the Gemara, why doesn't the four-cubit rule help the person who fell on a found item?
A. The four-cubit rule only applies to items worth less than a perutah
B. By falling on it, he revealed his intent to acquire by falling — forgoing the four-cubit method
C. The four-cubit rule was abolished by the time of the Mishna
D. Falling on an item cancels the four-cubit zone entirely
Correct: B — By choosing to fall on the item, the person revealed that he wanted to acquire it through that physical act rather than through the Rabbinic institution of four cubits. Having chosen an invalid method, he forfeited the valid one.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the fundamental disagreement about how courtyard acquisition works?
A. Whether a courtyard must be enclosed or can be open
B. Whether the courtyard must be within four cubits of the owner
C. Whether a courtyard is an extension of one's hand or functions through agency
D. Whether courtyard acquisition is Biblical or Rabbinic in origin
Correct: C — R' Yannai holds a courtyard is like an extension of one's hand (so even a minor girl can acquire through it), while Abba Kohen Bardela holds it works through agency (so a minor, who cannot appoint an agent, cannot acquire through it).
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Ravina resolve the problem of courtyard acquisition implying 'agency for transgression'?
A. He argues that theft in a courtyard is not really a transgression
B. He says the rule against agency for transgression only applies when the agent is personally liable — a courtyard cannot bear liability
C. He rejects the concept of courtyard acquisition entirely
D. He distinguishes between movable and immovable property
Correct: B — Ravina explains that the principle 'there is no agency for transgression' only applies when the agent himself is subject to liability. Since a courtyard is inanimate and cannot bear liability, the owner (principal) remains liable — this is not a contradiction.
