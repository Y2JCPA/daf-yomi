The Great Debate: Ye'ush Shelo Mida'at

Abaye
Ye'ush shelo mida'at — unconscious despair — is NOT considered valid despair. The owner must know about the loss and actively give up hope.
Rava
Ye'ush shelo mida'at IS valid despair. Since we know the owner will despair when he discovers the loss, it takes effect retroactively.
Gemara
Multiple proofs are brought and deflected. Ultimately, Rava is conclusively refuted from a verse in Deuteronomy 22:3.
Gemara
This is one of the six cases (YA"L KGA"M) where the halakha follows Abaye against Rava.
Key Principle Unconscious despair is NOT valid despair. The owner must be aware of the loss before ye'ush takes effect.

Thief, Robber & River: Who Keeps the Item?

Robber (Gazlan)

  • Takes property by force, openly
  • Owner sees the robbery happen
  • Owner despairs immediately
  • Finder may keep the item

Thief (Listim)

  • Rav Pappa: refers to armed bandits
  • NOT a secret thief — owner is aware
  • Same practical result as a robber
  • Baraita lists two types of robbers

River (Nahar)

  • Natural force sweeps items away
  • Owner witnesses the loss
  • Owner despairs of recovery
  • Finder keeps what the river deposited
Key Point In all three cases, the owner is aware of the loss — that's why ye'ush is valid. The Gemara reinterprets 'thief' to avoid a case of unconscious despair.

Teruma Separated Without the Owner's Consent

Baraita
Someone enters another's field, gathers produce, and separates teruma without permission. Is the teruma valid?
Baraita
If the owner says 'You should have taken better quality!' — and better produce exists — the teruma is valid (he's sincere). If not, he's being sarcastic.
Challenge
But at the moment of separation, the agent didn't know the owner would agree! This seems to prove ye'ush shelo mida'at works — supporting Rava.
Rava
Actually, this is a case where the owner designated him as an agent but didn't specify which crops. The agent chose higher quality; the owner preferred even better.
Rava
Proof: Numbers 18:28 — 'you also' includes an agent. Without agency, teruma is invalid. So the baraita must involve a designated agent.
Bottom Line The teruma case is deflected: it involves a pre-appointed agent who chose the wrong quality, not an unauthorized stranger — no proof for ye'ush shelo mida'at.

Ki Yuttan vs. Ki Yitten: Susceptibility to Impurity

Written Form (Ketiv)Read Form (Keri)Reconciliation
כי יתן — ki yittenכי יותן — ki yuttanRav Pappa reconciles both
'When one places' — active'When it is placed' — passiveRequires owner's knowledge, not necessarily his hand
Implies owner must physically place waterImplies any wetting sufficesEven dew suffices if owner knows and approves
Why No Proof for Ye'ush? This Torah derivation specifically requires awareness (da'at) for impurity susceptibility. We can't generalize from a special scriptural requirement to the general law of ye'ush.

The Conclusive Refutation of Rava

📖 R' Yochanan cites R' Yishmael ben Yehotzadak: Deuteronomy 22:3 — 'which shall be lost from him and you have found it'
↓
🔍 'Lost from him' but available to all → ownerless, finder keeps it (e.g., swept by river)
↓
⚖️ The verse's prohibition (must return) applies whether or not there's a siman (distinguishing mark)
↓
🔗 Similarly, the permission (may keep) applies with or without a siman — until proven ye'ush
↓
❌ This conclusively refutes Rava: you must wait for actual, conscious despair before keeping a found item
↓
✅ Halakha follows Abaye — one of six exceptions in the mnemonic YA"L KGA"M (יע"ל קג"מ)
Mnemonic YA"L KGA"M (יע"ל קג"מ): The six disputes where Abaye's ruling stands against Rava. Ye'ush shelo mida'at is the Yod (י).
