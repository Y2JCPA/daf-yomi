Bava Metzia 2 — Two Holding a Garment

שנים אוחזין בטלית — זה אומר אני מצאתיה וזה אומר אני מצאתיה
OPENING MISHNA שנים אוחזין בטלית — Two people appear in court each grasping a garment, each claiming "I found it — all of it is mine." The Mishna establishes the foundational rule: each takes an oath that they own no less than half, and they divide it. The Gemara then probes why the Mishna states both claims, whether sight alone creates acquisition, and whether this ruling aligns with Ben Nannas and Sumakhos.

The Mishna's Core Case: Two Holding a Garment

👔 Two people come to court, both grasping a single garment
↓
🗣️ Each claims: 'I found it — all of it is mine'
↓
✋ Each takes an oath (שבועה) that they own no less than half
↓
✂️ They divide the garment equally between them
KEY PRINCIPLE The Mishna's oath is a rabbinic enactment — without it, anyone could seize another's property and walk away with half.

Why State Both 'I Found It' AND 'All of It Is Mine'?

Gemara
Why does the Mishna need two separate claims? Let it teach just one!
Answer
It's actually one compound claim: 'I found it and all of it is mine.'
Gemara
Then just say 'I found it' — that implies 'all is mine'!
Answer
'I found it' could mean 'I saw it' (ראיתיה). Adding 'all is mine' teaches that sight alone does not create acquisition.
Gemara
But Rabbenai taught that 'found' in the Torah means it came into his possession!
Answer
True in the Torah — but in colloquial speech, people say 'I found it' upon merely seeing it. The Mishna clarifies.
HALACHA אין מציאה נקנית בראיה — A found item is not acquired through sight alone. Physical possession is required.

Why Both 'Found Item' and 'Buying/Selling' Cases?

If Mishna taught only the found item case…
↓
One might rationalize: 'He didn't own it originally — I'll seize it and split.' But in a sale, maybe no oath is needed.
↓
If Mishna taught only the sale case…
↓
One might rationalize: 'We both paid — I'll take it, he can buy another.' But for a found item, maybe no oath is needed.
↓
∴ Both cases are necessary (צריכא)
PRINCIPLE Each scenario involves a different psychological rationalization for seizing the item, so both must be taught to show the oath applies universally.

Ben Nannas vs. the Rabbis on Oaths

Rabbis (Majority)

  • Both the storekeeper and laborer swear oaths
  • Even though one MUST be lying
  • Oaths are imposed to protect legitimate claims
  • Our Mishna follows this approach

Ben Nannas

  • How can we administer oaths when one is certainly false?
  • שבועת שוא — an oath in vain is prohibited
  • Would NOT impose oaths in the laborer/storekeeper case
RESOLUTION The Mishna can follow Ben Nannas: in our case, both may have lifted the item simultaneously, so both could be swearing truthfully — unlike the laborer/storekeeper case.
