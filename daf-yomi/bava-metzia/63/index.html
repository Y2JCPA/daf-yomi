Daf 63: Converting Debts, Market Pricing & the Limits of Interest

Core Question: When can a borrower repay a debt with produce instead of money?
↓
R' Oshaya's Baraita: Borrower converts debt → wheat → wine → oil at each market rate
↓
Has Produce
Permitted — it's a proper sale
No Produce
Forbidden — price appreciation = interest
↓
Further debate: Can lender accept money value of produce instead of produce itself?
Key Principle Rav Nachman: Any payment for waiting — any additional sum added because the lender delays claiming his money — is forbidden as interest.

R' Oshaya's Baraita: The Chain of Conversions

💰 Lender is owed 100 dinars
↓
🌾 Lender says: 'Give me money — I want wheat.' Borrower: 'Take my wheat at market rate'
↓
🍷 Later: 'Give me wheat — I want wine.' Borrower: 'Take my wine at market rate'
↓
🫒 Later: 'Give me wine — I want oil.' Borrower: 'Take my oil at market rate'
↓
✅ Permitted ONLY if borrower actually has each commodity at time of conversion
↓
❌ If he doesn't have it — price appreciation in the interim = forbidden interest
Rava's 3 Conclusions 1) One may establish loan repayment upon produce. 2) Only if borrower has produce. 3) The halakha follows R' Yannai.

Produce or Its Value? Rav vs. R' Yannai

Rav
One may arrange repayment with produce (peirot), but not with money representing the produce's value — it has the appearance of interest.
R' Yannai
מה לי פירות מה לי דמי פירות — What difference is it to me? Produce or its value are the same! The lender could sell the produce himself anyway.
Rav Huna (for Rav)
The baraita that permits is only where the lender pulled the produce (meshikha) — or at least the borrower designated a corner for him.
Rava
The halakha follows R' Yannai. Since we say produce = value, we can also set prices by market rate.
Practical Difference According to Rav, the lender must physically take the produce; according to R' Yannai, he may simply accept its monetary equivalent.

'Your Favor Is Thrown on the Thorns'

Rabba & Rav Yosef
Why may one set a price at market rate even without produce? Because the buyer says: טובתך שדיא אחיזרא — your favor is thrown on thorns! I could buy it myself in Hini and Shili!
Abaye
If so, lending a se'ah for a se'ah should also be permitted! The lender can say: 'My wheat would appreciate in my warehouse — will it burn in my granary?'
Rav Yosef
No — that case is a loan (halva'ah), so interest applies. Here it is a sale (zevina), which is fundamentally different.
Adda bar Abba
But the buyer does benefit — he saves the broker's fee (dinar) he'd otherwise pay!
Rava
He gives the broker's fee to the seller too. Problem solved.
Rav Ashi
No need — a person's dinars serve as his broker. Ready cash needs no middleman.
Key Distinction The 'thrown on thorns' argument works only for sales. In a loan, even if the lender claims no benefit, the prohibition of se'ah b'se'ah still applies.

Presenting at the Granary (שער הזול)

Buyer pays in advance for produce at the advance price (before market price is set)
↓
Must the buyer present himself at the granary during threshing?
↓
Not for Acquisition
Merely standing there doesn't effect meshikha
Not for 'Mi Shepara'
The curse applies regardless of buyer's presence
↓
Real reason: So the seller relies on him (samakh da'atei). Buyers often pay multiple sellers — seller needs assurance.
↓
Rav Ashi: Meeting in the marketplace and confirming the deal also suffices
Mi Shepara 'He Who exacted payment from the generation of the Flood will exact payment from one who does not stand by his word' — the moral obligation curse for reneging after payment.
