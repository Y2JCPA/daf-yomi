QUESTION 1 OF 5
What is the practical difference (nafka mina) between the two explanations of why a robber validates the sale?
A. When the purchaser dies
B. When the robber dies
C. When the robber gave the land as a gift rather than selling it
D. When the land was inherited by the robber
Correct: C — The Gemara rejects both 'purchaser died' and 'robber died' as differences. The real nafka mina is a gift: under 'not called robber,' the robber can say 'what did I rob you of?' But under 'maintain reliability,' he still wants to uphold his reputation.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
If the robber collected the stolen field as payment for a debt, when does the sale become validated?
A. Always — collecting a debt shows intent
B. Never — debt collection is passive like inheritance
C. Only if the victim has other land the robber could have collected instead
D. Only if the victim has no other land
Correct: C — If the victim has other land but the robber specifically chose THIS field, it shows intent to validate the sale. If the victim has no other land, the robber had no choice and was merely collecting his debt.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to the Gemara's conclusion, whose opinion does Rav follow regarding selling a field not yet in one's possession?
A. The Sages, who say it works only for unspecified fields
B. Rava, who distinguishes between specific and unspecified fields
C. Rabbi Meir, who holds one can transfer an entity not yet in the world
D. Shmuel, who limits it to promissory notes
Correct: C — The Gemara emphatically states ('By God!') that Rav follows Rabbi Meir, who holds adam makneh davar shelo ba la'olam — a person can transfer something not yet in existence — even for a specific field.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does Shmuel say a found deed of transfer should be returned to the creditor?
A. Because the court has already verified the debt
B. Because the debtor committed himself when signing, and if repaid he would have torn it up
C. Because the finder has no right to keep someone else's document
D. Because bills of foreclosure are never subject to repayment
Correct: B — Shmuel reasons: (1) the debtor is liable from the moment he signed, even if he hasn't yet borrowed, and (2) we're not concerned about repayment because the debtor would have torn up the note if he had repaid.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does Rava reject the mishna about returning court enactments as proof for Shmuel's ruling?
A. Because court enactments are different from promissory notes
B. Because the mishna refers only to foreclosure bills, which could have been repaid — but the debtor caused the loss to himself by not tearing up the document
C. Because Rava disagrees with Shmuel entirely
D. Because the mishna is discussing a different type of found object
Correct: B — Rava explains that court enactments are returned not because we're unconcerned about repayment (as Shmuel would need), but because if the debtor repaid and didn't tear up the document, he caused the loss to himself. This is a different rationale than Shmuel's.
