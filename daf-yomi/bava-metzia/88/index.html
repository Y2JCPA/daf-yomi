🏠 When Does Produce Become Subject to Tithes?

Rabbi Yannai
Produce becomes subject to tithes only when brought into the house — specifically through the main entrance through which people enter and exit. As it is written: "I have removed the consecrated from the house" (Deuteronomy 26:13).
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Even bringing produce into the courtyard renders it subject to tithes. The verse "And I have given it to the Levite" implies that a courtyard-level enclosure is the critical boundary — 'house' is not literal.
Gemara
Challenge to Rabbi Yoḥanan: But the verse says "from the house"! — Answer: 'House' is not taken literally; it indicates any significant private enclosure, including a courtyard.
Gemara
Challenge to Rabbi Yannai: But the verse says "in your gates"! — Answer: That term limits the obligation to bringing produce into one's own courtyard or city gates, not another's.
Key Verse "I have removed the consecrated from the house" (Deuteronomy 26:13) — the point at which produce crosses a defined private threshold determines when tithes are owed

🛒 Who Must Tithe? Owner vs. Laborer vs. Buyer

Owner

  • His eyes are on the fig tree — primary concern is the tree, not the courtyard
  • Courtyard location does not trigger his tithe obligation in the same way
  • Eats casually without triggering the 'completion of work' threshold

Laborer

  • May eat from produce without tithing — Torah-level right
  • Derived from kenafshekha: just as you yourself may eat, so may the laborer
  • Applies to both attached and detached produce (per Ravina, from Deut. 25:4)

Buyer

  • NOT obligated to tithe by Torah law
  • Derived from: "the produce of your planting" — one's own planting, not purchased produce
  • The shops of Beit Hino were destroyed for selling untithed produce — a rabbinic concern, not Torah

🐂 Ox vs. Person — A Fortiori Arguments Collide

Question
We have a source for a laborer eating detached produce and an ox eating attached produce. But where is the source for a laborer eating attached produce and an ox eating detached produce?
Gemara
Both are derived by a fortiori: If an ox (which need not be allowed to eat detached) must be allowed attached — then a person (who may eat detached) certainly may eat attached. And vice versa for the ox and detached produce.
Challenge
Shouldn't an employer be commanded against muzzling his laborer too? If for an ox — which you need not sustain — you are prohibited from muzzling, how much more so a person whom you are commanded to sustain!
Gemara
Refuted: Kenafshekha — the laborer is like your own self. Just as you yourself face no muzzling prohibition (you simply choose not to eat your own food), neither does your laborer face such a prohibition enforced on others.
Ravina's Final Resolution No additional verses are needed: "You shall not muzzle an ox in its threshing" (Deut. 25:4) itself encompasses both the ox's right to eat attached produce and the laborer's right to eat detached produce — all embedded in the one prohibition

🏠 The Tithing Threshold — Produce's Journey

Stage 1 — In the field: Produce is growing or freshly harvested. No tithe obligation exists yet. Owner, laborer, and ox may all eat freely without separation.
↓
Stage 2 — Through the city gates: The verse "in your gates" signals significance. Bringing produce through one's own city gates begins to mark a transition — but only into one's own gates, not another's.
↓
Stage 3 — Into the courtyard: Rabbi Yoḥanan holds this triggers the tithe obligation. The courtyard functions as a 'house' for tithes — produce is now considered 'finished.'
↓
Stage 4 — Through the main entrance of the house: Rabbi Yannai holds only crossing the main front entrance renders the production process complete and tithes mandatory.
↓
Special case (Rav Pappa): A fig tree in a garden with leaves leaning into a courtyard — the owner's eyes are on the tree (exempt), but a buyer eating in the courtyard may be obligated. Resolved: buyer's obligation is rabbinic, not Torah-level.
