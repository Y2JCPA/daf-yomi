5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the accepted ruling in the Mishna, what is the threshold of coin erosion that constitutes ona'ah?
A. 4 issar (1 issar per dinar)
B. 4 pundeyon (1 pundeyon per dinar)
C. 8 pundeyon (2 pundeyon per dinar, i.e. one-sixth)
D. 12 pundeyon (3 pundeyon per dinar)
Correct: C — The Mishna's accepted measure is 8 pundeyon, which equals one-sixth of the sela's value — the rate of 2 pundeyon per dinar across 4 dinars.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Abaye say people tolerate a larger disparity for garments than for coins?
A. Garments are easier to inspect than coins
B. People willingly overpay for clothing they wear but not for defective currency that won't circulate
C. The Torah sets a different ona'ah standard for clothing
D. Garments depreciate faster than coins
Correct: B — Abaye cites the folk saying: 'Overpay for your back, buy at cost for your stomach.' People waive overpayment for garments they desire, but a coin that doesn't circulate is immediately problematic.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to R' Elazar, when is it permitted to perforate an invalid coin and hang it as a child's ornament?
A. When perforated from the side
B. When perforated in the middle
C. Only after melting it down first
D. Only if it lost more than half its value
Correct: B — A hole in the middle makes the coin obviously invalid and prevents fraud. A hole on the side could be cut off, leaving a deceptive unperforated remainder.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In the villages, until when may one return a defective coin?
A. Until he shows it to a money changer
B. Until the next market day
C. Until Shabbat eves
D. Within twelve months
Correct: C — Villages lack money changers, so the deadline is Shabbat eves (Erev Shabbat), when people go to the market to purchase Shabbat needs and can discover the coin's true value.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What does Rav Ḥisda say about the Mishna's rule that one must accept back a recognized coin even after twelve months?
A. It is a strict legal obligation enforceable by beit din
B. It is middat chasidut — an attribute of piety, not a legal requirement
C. It applies only to coins that lost less than one-sixth
D. It applies only in cities where money changers are present
Correct: B — Rav Ḥisda explains that the Mishna teaches middat chasidut (the attribute of piety). One who refuses is not legally compelled, but the other party 'has a grievance' (tar'omet) against him.
