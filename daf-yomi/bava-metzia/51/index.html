Ona'ah: Who Can Claim, When, and How?

Transaction occurs with price deviation
↓
Was the seller a merchant or a homeowner?
Merchant
Ona'ah laws apply to both buyer and seller
Homeowner (personal items)
No ona'ah claim — buyer should expect inflated price
↓
Did the parties stipulate waiver of ona'ah?
"No claim of ona'ah against me"
Valid waiver — personal right relinquished
"Not subject to halakhot of ona'ah"
Void — cannot stipulate against Torah law
Key Principle Ona'ah protections depend on who is selling, what was stipulated, and whether the deviation was known or hidden.

Why Did the Merchants of Lod Prefer the Rabbis?

Gemara
If R' Tarfon extends renege time equally for both buyer and seller, why would merchants prefer the Rabbis' shorter window?
Gemara (Answer)
The merchants of Lod rarely erred in pricing — so they had little need for a long seller-renege period.
Gemara
They preferred limiting buyers' renege time over extending their own, since they seldom needed it.
Rav Naḥman
In fact, a seller may always renege — the time limit applies only to buyers, who have the item in hand to check the price.
Rationale A buyer can show the item to experts anywhere. A seller must wait until similar merchandise appears — so his window is unlimited.

The Landlord's Donkey: A Seller's Recourse

🫏 Rami bar Ḥama's landlord sells a donkey and errs on the price
↓
😔 The landlord is sad — he realizes his mistake too late
↓
💬 Rami bar Ḥama says: Go renege on the sale!
↓
⏰ Landlord: I waited longer than the time to show it to a merchant or relative
↓
⚖️ Rav Naḥman rules: The time limit was taught only for buyers — a seller may always renege
Key Distinction The buyer holds the item and can verify the price at will. The seller must wait for comparable goods to appear — hence no time limit for seller's ona'ah claim.

Buying from a Merchant vs. a Homeowner

Merchant Seller

  • Ona'ah laws fully apply
  • Buyer can claim exploitation if overcharged by 1/6
  • Seller can claim if undercharged by 1/6
  • Professional — expected to know market prices

Homeowner Seller (Personal Items)

  • No ona'ah claim for buyer (Rava, Rav Ḥisda)
  • Homeowner is reluctant to part with belongings
  • Buyer should expect inflated asking price
  • Exception: items crafted expressly for sale (tzadriyyata)
Rav Ḥisda The Mishna's 'layman' (hedyot) refers to a homeowner who crafts items for sale (e.g., linen garments) — not one selling personal possessions.

Can You Stipulate Against Ona'ah?

Rav
Even R' Yehuda agrees one cannot stipulate against Torah law in ona'ah cases — the buyer doesn't know for certain there will be exploitation.
Shmuel
Even R' Meir would agree the stipulation is valid here — unlike betrothal, who says any Torah law will actually be violated?
Rav Anan (citing Shmuel)
"No claim of ona'ah against me" = valid. It's a personal waiver of rights.
Rav Anan
"This sale is not subject to ona'ah" = void. This directly overrides Torah law itself.
Critical Distinction Waiving a personal right to claim is permitted. Declaring that Torah law doesn't apply is forbidden — even by mutual agreement.

When Exploitation Is Explicit

Seller says: "This is worth 100 but I'm selling for 200 — on condition you have no ona'ah claim"
↓
Does the buyer know the true value?
Yes — Explicit Ona'ah
Stipulation is valid — informed, conscious waiver
No — Ordinary Case
Rav holds stipulation is void per R' Yehuda — buyer cannot knowingly waive unknown exploitation
Rava's Resolution Rav and the baraita don't contradict: Rav invalidates the condition in ordinary cases; the baraita validates it when ona'ah is explicit and both parties know the true price.
