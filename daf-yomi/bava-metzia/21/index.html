R' Meir's View All these lack a siman (distinguishing mark), so the owner cannot prove ownership. R' Yehuda disagrees: an alteration (like a shard inside a fig cake) counts as a siman.

R' Yehuda vs. R' Shimon ben Elazar on Simanim

R' Yehuda
If a found item has an alteration (shinui), the finder must proclaim it.
R' Yehuda
Example: A fig cake with a pottery shard inside, or bread with coins inside — the owner inserted these as a siman!
R' Shimon b. Elazar
If one finds anpurya vessels (uniform manufactured goods), he need not proclaim them.
R' Shimon b. Elazar
Their shape is identical — no owner can distinguish his from another's.
Principle The dispute revolves around what constitutes a valid siman: intentional alterations (R' Yehuda) vs. inherent uniformity rendering identification impossible (R' Shimon ben Elazar).

Scattered Produce: When Does the Owner Give Up?

Kernels of wheat left on the threshing floor after gathering
↓
What is the dispersal ratio?
≥ 1 Kav / 4×4 Cubits
Gathering requires great exertion → owner renounces ownership → finder keeps
< 1 Kav / 4×4 Cubits
Gathering is manageable → owner returns for them → finder may not keep
R' Yirmeya's Dilemmas What about ½ kav in 2×4 cubits (same ratio, less exertion)? Or 2 kav in 8×4 cubits (same ratio, more exertion)? Is the key factor exertion or value?

Ye'ush Shelo Mida'at: The Core Dispute

Abaye
Unconscious despair is NOT valid despair. The owner doesn't even know the item is lost — how can he renounce it?
Rava
Unconscious despair IS valid. When the owner eventually discovers the loss, he will despair. Since the outcome is certain, it counts retroactively.
Abaye
But at the moment the finder picked it up, the owner had not yet despaired. The acquisition was prohibited at that moment.
Rava
The owner will say: 'I have no siman on this item.' His future despair is inevitable — it applies from the moment of loss.
Scope They only disagree about items without a siman. For items with a siman, even Rava agrees unconscious despair doesn't apply — the owner expects to reclaim via the siman.

Attempted Proofs Against Abaye — All Rejected

Proof Attempted

  • Scattered produce → belongs to finder
  • Scattered coins → belongs to finder
  • Round fig cakes / loaves → belongs to finder
  • Purple wool strips → belongs to finder
  • Coins in synagogues → belongs to finder

Rejection (Why Not Unconscious)

  • Threshing floor leftovers — owner knowingly abandoned
  • R' Yitzchak: person checks pouch constantly — aware quickly
  • Heavy items — owner feels them fall immediately
  • Valuable — owner checks for them constantly (like coins)
  • Same R' Yitzchak principle — pouch-checking
Pattern Every attempted proof from the Mishna is deflected: in each case, the Gemara shows the owner's despair was actually conscious, not unconscious.
