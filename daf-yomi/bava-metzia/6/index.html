Daf 6: Oaths, Seizure & Sanctity of Disputed Property

Core Question: Can one suspected of financial dishonesty be administered an oath?
↓
Three cases prove oaths are given despite suspicion:
1) Oath of inducement 2) Storekeeper & laborer 3) Unpaid bailee
↓
Conclusion
Suspected of dishonesty ≠ suspected of false oath
Abaye
Perhaps he has an uncertain old loan — property can be returned, oaths cannot
Key Principle People refrain from swearing falsely about uncertain matters but do not refrain from seizing property, because property can be returned — an oath cannot be retracted.

Can the Financially Suspect Take an Oath?

Gemara
If one is suspected of theft, shouldn't we say: suspected of dishonesty = suspected of false oath?
Rav Naḥman
I rule that one who denies a debt entirely still receives an oath of inducement (shevu'at hesset).
R' Ḥiyya
In the case of the storekeeper and laborer, both take an oath and collect from the employer!
Rav Sheshet
An unpaid bailee takes three oaths: not negligent, did not misappropriate, not in my possession.
Gemara
Conclusion: We do not say that one suspected of financial dishonesty is also suspected of swearing falsely.
Principle Financial suspicion does not automatically disqualify a person from taking a court-administered oath.

Abaye's 'Old Loan' Theory

Abaye: Perhaps the defendant has an old loan (milveh yeshanah) owed by the plaintiff
↓
He seizes property not as theft, but as self-help repayment
↓
But if so — why require an oath at all? Let him just keep the property!
↓
He is uncertain whether the loan exists — so the oath resolves the uncertainty
↓
But if he seizes on uncertainty, won't he also swear falsely on uncertainty?
↓
Rav Sheshet b. Rav Idi
People refrain from uncertain oaths but do not refrain from seizing property
Reason
Property can be returned; an oath cannot be retracted
Sevara The irreversibility of an oath creates a psychological deterrent that property seizure does not.

R' Zeira's Dilemma: Seizure Before the Court

👥 Two people grasp a garment before the court
↓
💪 One seizes the entire garment from the other in our presence
↓
🤐 The other is silent initially — then protests later
↓
❓ Was the silence an admission that was later regretted?
↓
💭 Or did he think: 'The court saw — no need to cry out'?
↓
📜 Rav Naḥman cites a baraita: if one holds the garment alone, burden of proof on the claimant
Unresolved? The Gemara ultimately reinterprets the baraita as referring to a case where they left court and returned — R' Zeira's exact dilemma remains open.

The Consecrated Bathhouse Dispute

The Case

  • Two people dispute ownership of a bathhouse
  • Neither is in possession
  • One arises and consecrates (hekdesh) it
  • Rav Ḥananya & Rav Oshaya refrain from bathing there

Rav Hamnuna's Proof

  • Uncertain firstborns: burden of proof on priest (claimant)
  • Yet prohibited from shearing and working the animal
  • Conclusion: Consecration can take effect even without possession

Rabba's Rejection

  • Firstborn sanctity is different — it emerges by itself at birth
  • It does not depend on an act of consecration
  • If priest seizes uncertain firstborn, court removes it
  • Cannot compare self-emerging sanctity to human consecration
Key Distinction Kedushat bekhor (firstborn sanctity) arises automatically — it is not comparable to hekdesh performed by a disputant.
