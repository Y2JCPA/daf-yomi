Returning Lost Items: Proof & Process

Lost item found — finder must determine rightful owner
↓
Are simanim (distinguishing marks) Torah-level proof?
Yes — Rava
Derived from derosh (Deut. 22:2): scrutinize the claimant → simanim are valid Torah proof
Alternative view
"Scrutinize" may refer to witnesses, not simanim — simanim could be rabbinic
↓
Hierarchy established: Witnesses > Simanim > Single witness (= nothing)
Core Principle Rava establishes that simanim (distinguishing marks) carry Torah-level authority for returning lost items, derived from Deuteronomy 22:2's command to scrutinize the claimant.

Are Simanim Torah Law?

Gemara
If returning by simanim is only rabbinic (by owner's tacit agreement), how do we explain returning a roll of documents by simanim?
Rava
Simanim are Torah law! The verse says 'until your brother doresh it' (Deut. 22:2) — meaning: scrutinize the claimant to see if he's a swindler!
Gemara
How does he prove he's not a swindler? Through simanim! — This proves simanim are Torah-level.
Gemara
But wait — Rava then says 'If you say simanim are Torah law…' — Didn't he just prove it?
Rava
I phrased it conditionally because one could reject my proof — perhaps doresh means scrutinize via witnesses, not simanim.
Key Verse "V'hayah imkha ad drosh achikha oto" — 'It shall be with you until your brother claims it' — Rava reinterprets drosh as scrutinize the claimant.

Rava's Hierarchy: Competing Claims to a Lost Item

ScenarioRuling
Both claimants provide simanimItem stays with finder — neither prevails
One provides simanim, other brings 2 witnessesGiven to claimant with witnesses
Both provide simanim, one also has 1 witnessSingle witness is as nothing — stays with finder
One has witnesses it was woven for him, other that it fell from himGiven to the one it fell from — the weaver may have sold it
One gives length, other gives widthGiven to the one who gave length — harder to approximate
One gives length + width, other gives gamma (combined)Given to the one with separate measures
One gives length + width, other gives weightGiven to the one with weight — hardest to approximate
Principle The more difficult a measurement is to approximate, the stronger it serves as a distinguishing mark. Weight > Length > Width > Combined (gamma).

The Found Get (Bill of Divorce): Husband vs. Wife

A get is found — both husband and wife claim it with simanim
↓
What type of siman does the wife provide?
Height & Width
❌ Not sufficient — she could have seen it while husband held it
Perforation near a specific letter
✅ Valid — she could only know this if the get was in her hand
↓
If wife describes the string binding the get — is it valid?
Color of string
❌ She could have seen the string from a distance
Length of string
✅ Valid — requires physical handling
Ruling The get is given to the wife — but only if her simanim prove she physically held the document, not merely observed it.

Evolution of Proclaiming Found Items

🏛️ Temple Era: Proclaim at three pilgrimage Festivals (Pesach, Shavuot, Sukkot) + 7 days after the last
↓
📐 7-day extension: 3 days to travel home + 3 days to return + 1 day to proclaim the loss
↓
🕍 After Temple destroyed: Proclaim in synagogues and study halls
↓
👑 After oppressors proliferated (who said 'lost items belong to the king'): Inform only neighbors and acquaintances
↓
🪨 Even HaTa'anot (Claimant's Stone) in Jerusalem: designated meeting point for finders and losers of items
Historical Note Rabbi Ami found a vessel of dinars. A Roman told him: 'Take it — we are not Persians who say lost items belong to the king.' This illustrates why proclamation methods had to change under oppressive rule.

How Should a Finder Proclaim?

Rav Yehuda
Proclaim: 'I found a lost item' — without specifying its nature. If you say 'I found a cloak,' a swindler might learn the owner's simanim and claim it!
Rav Nachman
Proclaim: 'I found a cloak' — specify the type. If you worry about swindlers guessing, there's no end to that concern — they could guess the type too!
Gemara
The mishna says: if one states the type but not simanim, we don't return it. This supports Rav Yehuda — the claimant must identify the type himself!
Rav Safra
No — the mishna can work for Rav Nachman too: the finder says 'cloak,' and the claimant provides simanim — but not clear-cut simanim, so we still don't return it.
Key Distinction The dispute turns on balancing swindler prevention (Rav Yehuda) vs. practical effectiveness of announcements (Rav Nachman).
