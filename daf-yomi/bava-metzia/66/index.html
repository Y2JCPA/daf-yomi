Key Principle Two major doctrines limit conditional obligations: pittumei millei (wrong party's condition is mere enticement) and asmakhta (penalty clauses are not binding because consent is not wholehearted).

Pittumei Millei: Who Must State the Condition?

Ameimar
When the seller says 'If I bring money, return the field' — these are pittumei millei (words of enticement). It's the buyer who should stipulate this condition.
Rav Ashi
But the baraita says if the buyer says 'When you have money I'll return it' — that's permitted! There the seller should stipulate, yet the buyer's words are valid?
Ameimar
Any condition stated by the wrong party is invalid. The baraita's case works because the buyer is considered as offering to return the field of his own accord — not as a binding condition.
Rava
Correct — the latter clause of the baraita works only because the buyer said he would return the field voluntarily (מדעתו). Otherwise it too would be mere enticement.
Principle A condition in a transaction must be stated by the party who benefits from it. If stated by the wrong party, it is pittumei millei — encouraging words with no legal force.

The Dying Husband's Divorce

A dying man writes a get (bill of divorce) to spare his wife from ḥalitza
↓
He moans and sighs in distress over divorcing her
↓
His wife says: 'If you recover, I am yours — I will remarry you'
↓
Is her promise legally binding?
Rav Zevid
These are pittumei millei — mere words of encouragement, not binding
Rav Aḥa of Difti
Even a real condition wouldn't matter — it's his prerogative to set conditions on the get, not hers
Why Rav Zevid's Ruling Matters Lest you say the husband relied on her promise and it's as if he stipulated the condition — Rav Zevid teaches us this is not the case.

Rav Huna vs. Rav Naḥman: Timing of the Condition

Rav Huna

  • Condition stated at the time of the loan → lender acquires the entire field
  • Condition stated after the loan → lender acquires only a portion corresponding to the loan amount
  • Timing of the condition determines the scope of acquisition

Rav Naḥman (Initial)

  • Even if condition stated after the loan → lender acquires the entire field
  • Ruled this way in the court of the Exilarch
  • Rav Yehuda tore up the resulting deed
Dramatic Exchange When told Rav Yehuda tore up his document, Rav Naḥman said either: 'A great man tore it — he must have had reason' or 'A child tore it — everyone is like a child relative to me in monetary law.'

Rav Naḥman's Dramatic Retraction on Asmakhta

1️⃣ Rav Naḥman initially rules: asmakhta effects acquisition — the lender gets the whole field
↓
2️⃣ He rules this way in the court of the Exilarch
↓
3️⃣ Rav Yehuda tears up the document
↓
4️⃣ Minyumi convinces Rav Naḥman that asmakhta does not effect acquisition
↓
5️⃣ Rav Naḥman fully retracts: even a condition stated at the time of the loan does not work
↓
6️⃣ Rava challenges from the mishna — Rav Naḥman explains the mishna differently (act of acquisition + 'from now')
Asmakhta An asmakhta (אסמכתא) is a conditional obligation one accepts as a penalty — e.g., 'if I don't pay, take my field.' The person never truly expects to forfeit, so consent is not wholehearted.
