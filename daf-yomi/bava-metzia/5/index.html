Oaths, Admissions & the Contested Garment

Gemara
If הילך (Here you are) exempts from oath, why does the Torah need to exclude land from oaths? Land is always 'here'!
Rav Sheshet
The verse is needed for land where pits were dug (damaged land isn't truly 'here'), or where one claims vessels and land together.
Gemara
Rami bar Ḥama teaches: All four bailees require partial admission for an oath. Doesn't this support R' Ḥiyya that הילך still requires an oath?
Gemara
No — the baraita refers to a case where the bailee says: one cow never happened, one died by accident, one by negligence. That's not הילך.
Core Tension Does saying 'Here you are' (הילך) exempt the defendant from a modeh b'miktzat oath, and how do we derive oath rules from 'זה הוא' (This is it)?

Why Exclude Land from Oaths if הילך Exempts?

Rav Sheshet: הילך (Here you are) = exempt from oath
↓
But land is always 'here' — why does the Torah separately exclude land?
↓
Answer 1
Defendant dug pits & caves in the land — it's damaged, not truly 'here you are'
Answer 2
Claim involved vessels AND land — admitted to vessels, denied land. Not הילך at all.
Key Point Even for Rav Sheshet, the verse excluding land from oaths has practical applications in damaged land or mixed claims.

What Does 'זה הוא' (This Is It) Teach?

R' Ḥiyya
One word teaches modeh b'miktzat (partial admission oath). The other teaches oath requires admission of the same type (מין הטענה).
R' Aptoriki's Father
One word teaches modeh b'miktzat. The other teaches that witnesses establishing partial liability do not trigger the oath.
Gemara
R' Aptoriki's father doesn't require 'same type' — he holds like Rabban Gamliel that claiming wheat and admitting barley still triggers an oath.
Gemara
R' Ḥiyya is a Tanna himself — he can dispute a baraita. So R' Aptoriki's baraita doesn't refute him.
Exegetical Dispute The phrase כי הוא זה (Exodus 22:8) contains a seeming redundancy — each sage derives a different halakha from each word.

Shepherds: Own Animals vs. Others' Animals

Herds Own Animals

  • Presumed to be a robber
  • Lets animals graze in others' fields
  • Disqualified from testimony (Rav Yehuda)
  • Sins for his own benefit

Herds Others' Animals

  • NOT presumed a robber
  • No personal gain from grazing violations
  • Otherwise giving him animals = 'stumbling block before the blind'
  • A person sins only for his own benefit
Principle אין אדם חוטא ולא לו — A person does not sin unless it benefits him personally (Leviticus 19:14).

Rav Huna's Oath Formula for the Two Claimants

🗣️ Each claimant states: 'All of it is mine'
↓
⚖️ Court cannot award all of it — so claimant cannot swear 'all is mine'
↓
❌ Cannot swear 'half is mine' — that contradicts his original claim
↓
✅ Formula: 'I swear I have a claim to it, and I do not have a claim to less than half'
↓
💡 Reframed: 'According to your ruling, I swear to at least half' — preserving original claim
Why This Formula? The oath must not contradict the claimant's position ('all is mine') while being honest about what the court will actually award.
