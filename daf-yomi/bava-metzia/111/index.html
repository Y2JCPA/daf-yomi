⏰ Rav vs. Shmuel: Reading the Mishna on Timing

Rav
A day laborer collects all day; a night laborer collects all night. The mishna must be read disjunctively — each clause applies to its own shift.
Shmuel
A day laborer may collect all night as well, and a night laborer may collect all day. The employer has the full following period to pay.
Gemara
"An hourly laborer collects all night and all day" — this appears to refute Rav! But Rav reads it disjunctively: night-laborer gets all night; day-laborer gets all day.
Baraita
A tanna'im dispute confirms: An hourly laborer by day collects all day; by night — all night. This is the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Thus Rav's reading is validated.
Practical Result Under Rav's view, each shift has its own distinct payment window. Missing it — even by one hour — triggers the biblical prohibition of delaying wages.

⛔ Five Prohibitions for Withholding Wages

לֹא תַעֲשֹׁק שָׂכִיר עָנִי וְאֶבְיוֹן (דברים כד:יד)
📜 Baraita (Amud A §9) Anyone who withholds the wages of a hired laborer violates five negative prohibitions and one positive mitzva:

1. "Do not oppress your neighbor" (Lev. 19:13) — applies by day
2. "The wages of a laborer shall not remain with you all night until morning" (Lev. 19:13) — applies by night
3. "You shall not oppress a hired laborer who is poor and needy" (Deut. 24:14) — applies by day
4. "You shall not withhold his wages" — applies by night
5. "For he sets his soul upon it" — an additional prohibition

✅ Positive mitzva: "On the same day you shall give him his wages" (Deut. 24:15)

Note: Not all five apply simultaneously — day and night prohibitions alternate

🤜 Oppression (עֹשֶׁק) vs. Stealing (גָּזֵל) — Four Opinions

Rav Ḥisda

  • Oppression: Employer tells laborer "Go and return, go and return" — repeatedly delays without denying the debt
  • Stealing: Employer takes wages already handed to him
  • Key distinction: oppression = delay; stealing = denial or active taking

Rav Sheshet (Objects)

  • Oppression requires the employer to first admit the debt, then deny it (modeled on deposit cases)
  • Simply saying 'go and return' is insufficient — there must be active denial
  • Raises objection only to oppression, not stealing

Abaye (Counter-Objects)

  • Stealing also requires denial modeled on deposit cases — why didn't Rav Sheshet object here too?
  • Answer: Rav Sheshet held that stealing requires an act of physical taking, which is absent in the laborer case
  • Oppression involves verbal denial — hence only oppression needed clarification

Rava (Final Ruling)

  • Oppression = Stealing — no practical halakhic difference
  • Torah listed both categories to allow double liability: the employer violates both prohibitions simultaneously
  • The distinction exists only to multiply the violation, not to define separate acts

📜 Who Authored the Mishna? Identifying the Tanna

Problem: The mishna includes animals, utensils, AND ger toshav under wage delay laws — which tanna holds this exact combination?
↓
Not the First Tanna of the baraita — he derives from "from your brothers" and treats the ger toshav differently, excluding him from the Leviticus prohibition
↓
Not Rabbi Yosei son of Rabbi Yehuda — he uses a verbal analogy (גְּזֵרָה שָׁוָה) of 'hired laborer / hired laborer' between the verses, producing a different scope
↓
Rava's Answer: The tanna is from the School of Rabbi Yishmael, who derives from "in your land" (Deut. 24:14) to include animal and utensil rentals beyond the hire of persons
↓
Resolution: Rabbi Yishmael's school reads "your stranger that is in your land within your gates" (Deut. 24:14) to include the ger toshav, unifying all three categories exactly as the mishna rules
