What Makes 'Bound' Money Unusable?

Gemara
Why can't the money changer use bound money? Everyone binds their money! Binding alone doesn't signal 'don't use.'
Rav Yehuda (via Rav Asi)
The mishna refers to money that is bound AND sealed (צרורין וחתומין) — a clear indication not to open it.
Rav Mari
It could also mean money tied with an atypical knot (קשר משונה) — signaling the owner doesn't want it opened.
Gemara
Rav Mari's dilemma: Is an atypical knot equivalent to a seal? Teku — the question stands unresolved.
Practical Point The manner of binding communicates the depositor's intent — ordinary binding is insufficient; it must be sealed or unusually knotted.

Ones: Is the Money Changer Liable for Force Majeure?

Rav Huna
Even if the unbound money was taken by force (ones), the money changer is liable — his right to use it makes him like a borrower.
Rav Naḥman
If taken by ones, he is NOT liable. He is not a borrower — he is a paid bailee (שומר שכר).
Rava (to Rav Naḥman)
If he's not a borrower, why isn't he just an unpaid bailee? What makes him a paid one?
Rav Naḥman
He benefits from the potential to use the money for profitable purchases — that benefit is his 'payment' as a bailee.
Dispute Rav Huna: Unbound deposit = loan → liable even for ones
Rav Naḥman: Potential benefit = paid bailee → exempt from ones, liable for theft/loss

Money changer acts as agent
↓
Rav Naḥman's challenge to Rav Huna: If unbound = loan, treasurer should be liable for me'ila immediately upon deposit — not only when spent!
↓
Rav Huna responds: Indeed, the treasurer IS liable immediately. The mishna mentions 'spent' only for stylistic symmetry with the first clause.
Insight The me'ila framework reveals the stakes: if depositing unbound money is tantamount to a loan, the transfer of ownership happens at deposit, triggering me'ila immediately.

Misappropriation: When Do We Assess Value?

Beit Shammai

  • Penalized for BOTH decrease and increase
  • If value drops — pays value at time of misappropriation
  • If value rises — pays value at time of repayment
  • Misappropriation does NOT require actual loss
  • Bailee = robber from moment of misappropriation

Beit Hillel

  • Pays value at time of REMOVAL (damage)
  • Until actual loss, decrease is owner's loss
  • Misappropriation REQUIRES actual loss
  • Bailee liable only when deposit is destroyed
  • More lenient — follows the robber less harshly

Rabbi Akiva

  • Pays value at time of the CLAIM (תביעה)
  • Halakha follows R' Akiva (per Shmuel)
  • Exception: if witnesses saw misappropriation — pays from that moment
  • R' Yoḥanan disagrees: even with witnesses, pays at time of claim
Final Reading The Gemara reinterprets 'decrease and increase' — not market fluctuations but fleece (גיזה) and offspring (ולדות), i.e., physical changes to the stolen item. Beit Shammai: enhancements belong to the victim. Beit Hillel: enhancements belong to the robber.
