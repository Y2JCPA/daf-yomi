🤝 What Triggers an Oath? Rami bar Ḥama vs. the Rabbis

Rami bar Ḥama
For a bailee to be required to take an oath, he must make a partial monetary admission — denying part and admitting part of the very same claim. Uncertainty alone ('I do not know') is insufficient.
Gemara
To satisfy Rami bar Ḥama's requirement in the first clause, we need three cows. Owner says: 'I lent you three cows — half day borrowing, half day renting each day.' Bailee says: 'I never took one of them' (denial) + 'one died during borrowing' (admission).
Rami bar Ḥama
The denial of one cow combined with admission about another constitutes a partial monetary admission — now the oath is required about the remaining cow of uncertain status.
Rabbis
We do not require a monetary partial admission. The bailee's statement 'I do not know' — expressing genuine uncertainty — is itself sufficient to impose a rabbinic oath even without a classical הוֹדָאָה בְּמִקְצָת.
Core Dispute Rami bar Ḥama: oath requires classical partial monetary admission. Rabbis: 'I do not know' alone can suffice.

🐄 Three Outcomes When a Borrowed and Rented Cow Die

Owner Certain: Borrowed Cow Died

  • Owner claims the borrowed cow died on his watch
  • Renter (bailee) insists the rented cow died instead
  • Renter takes an oath that the rented cow is the one that died
  • Bailee pays only the lower rental rate, not full borrower liability
  • Owner may also demand: 'swear it died naturally, not negligently'

Bailee Admits: But Owner Demands More

  • Owner's original claim may differ from bailee's admission
  • Owner can still impose oath: 'swear the cow died naturally'
  • The oath covers cause of death, not just identity of the cow
  • This satisfies Abaye: an oath can be imposed about connected claims
  • Bailee is not exempt merely because admission was partial

Both Say: I Do Not Know

  • Neither owner nor bailee can establish which cow died
  • No party has the certainty to take an oath
  • Resolution: divide the disputed amount equally
  • Each bears half the difference between borrower and renter rates
  • Neither can extract more from the other without proof

🔄 Rabbi Abba bar Memel's Four Unresolved Dilemmas

תֵּיקוּ — These dilemmas shall stand unresolved (בבא מציעא צח:)
תֵּיקוּ — All Stand Unresolved Core Question: When a transaction with owner's services (sho'el b'ba'alim) is followed by a new transaction without owner's services — or vice versa — does the later transaction inherit the exemption of the earlier one?

Dilemma 1: Borrowed cow with ba'alim → rented without ba'alim. Does rental inherit the borrower's exemption from liability?

Dilemma 2 (depends on #1): Rented with ba'alim → borrowed without ba'alim. Does borrowing inherit rental's exemption, even though borrowing is the stricter level?

Dilemma 3 (depends on #2): Borrowed → rented → borrowed again. Does the third period return to the original exemption of the first borrowing?

Dilemma 4 (depends on #3): Rented with ba'alim → borrowed without → rented again. Does the final rental revert to the original ba'alim exemption?

Each dilemma is conditioned on the prior one — forming an unresolvable chain.

⚖️ When Does Borrower Liability Begin and End?

Stage 1 — Agreement: Owner and borrower agree on loan terms. No liability attaches yet — the cow has not changed hands.
↓
Stage 2 — Dispatch (lender's choice): If the lender sends the cow via a messenger he chose without the borrower's request, the lender bears all transit risk. The borrower is not yet liable.
↓
Stage 3 — Dispatch (borrower's request): If the borrower said 'send it with your son / my slave / your agent' — the borrower has designated the messenger and liability begins at dispatch, even before arrival.
↓
Stage 4 — Active Use: Borrower is fully liable for any mishap during the period of use, including unavoidable accidents (oness) — the strictest liability of any bailee.
↓
Stage 5 — Return: Liability ends only upon actual receipt by the lender or the lender's designated agent. The same 'who requested the messenger' logic applies in reverse during return transit.
