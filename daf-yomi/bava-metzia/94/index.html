📜 Can Bailees Stipulate Against Torah Law?

Gemara
Why can the Mishna allow bailees to change their Torah liabilities? One who stipulates counter to Torah — his condition should be void!
Suggestion
The Mishna follows R. Yehuda: in monetary matters a condition against Torah is valid, since financial rights may be waived by the parties.
Objection
But the Mishna also states: 'Anyone who stipulates counter to Torah, his condition is void' — that is R. Meir's position, not R. Yehuda's!
Gemara
The entire Mishna is R. Meir. The condition is indeed void — but the bailee is exempt because at the outset the owner did not object to the arrangement. The exemption flows from prior consent, not from the condition.
Baraita
Just as an unpaid bailee can stipulate to be exempt, so too a paid bailee can stipulate to assume the full liability of a borrower — and this requires a formal act of acquisition to bind him to that higher standard.
Bottom Line R. Meir: void condition + valid act. The Mishna's exemptions rest on the owner's prior acceptance, not the conditions themselves.

🐄 Four Bailees: Liabilities at a Glance

שומר חינם Unpaid Bailee

  • Exempt: theft & loss (takes oath)
  • Exempt: injury, death, capture (oness)
  • Liable: negligence
  • Oath required for all outcomes

שומר שכר / שוכר Paid Bailee & Renter

  • Liable: theft & loss (pays principal)
  • Exempt: injury, death, capture (oness)
  • Liable: negligence
  • Oath: injury / capture / death

שואל Borrower

  • Liable: ALL outcomes — theft, loss
  • Liable: injury, death, capture
  • Liable: negligence
  • No oath — pays for everything

⚡ Why the Difference?

  • Borrower: all benefit is his, no cost
  • Paid bailee: compensated, so bears more
  • Unpaid bailee: no benefit → least burden
  • Severity tracks benefit from transaction

👥 שואל עם הבעלים — Borrowing With the Owner

Person borrows a cow from its owner — is he exempt if the cow dies? (Exodus 22:14: 'if its owner is with him, he shall not pay')
↓
Owner's services borrowed or hired SIMULTANEOUSLY with the cow
Exempt — biblical exemption applies: the owner was 'with him' at the moment of borrowing. No payment if cow dies.
Cow borrowed FIRST; owner's services borrowed or hired afterward
Liable — cow was not borrowed while owner was present. Exemption does not apply even if owner is working for borrower at time of mishap.
↓
Gemara proof: The Mishna's phrase 'afterward borrowed the cow' implies 'with it' in the first clause means literally simultaneous — same moment, not same period.
↓
Technical note: Simultaneous borrowing works even without formal acquisition if the cow is standing in the borrower's courtyard, which effects acquisition automatically.

⛓️ Impossible Conditions: R. Yehuda ben Teima

Mishna rule: Any condition that can ultimately be fulfilled — if stipulated at the outset, the condition is valid.
↓
Rav (cited by Rav Tavla): This is specifically the view of R. Yehuda ben Teima. If a condition is truly impossible, it is negated and the underlying act stands unconditionally.
↓
Proof case (Baraita): 'Here is your get on condition that you ascend to the heavens… or swallow a reed of 100 cubits.' R. Yehuda ben Teima: valid get — impossible condition is simply cancelled.
↓
Rabbinic dissent: Even an impossible condition voids the get — the husband clearly did not intend an unconditional divorce, so we cannot treat the condition as simply erased.
↓
Final ruling — Rav Naḥman: Halakha follows R. Yehuda ben Teima. The Mishna's precise wording ('any condition that can be fulfilled') implies impossible conditions are automatically negated and the act stands.
