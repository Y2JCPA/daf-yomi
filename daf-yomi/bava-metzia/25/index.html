When Must a Finder Proclaim?

You find a vessel and produce nearby
↓
Is the produce inside the vessel?
Yes — inside vessel
Must proclaim the find
No — outside vessel
Depends on circumstances: basket orientation, rim, remaining contents
Key Principle A siman (distinguishing mark) creates an obligation to return — but context determines whether items are connected or coincidental.

Produce Inside vs. Outside the Vessel

Produce IN the Vessel

  • Coins inside a pouch → must proclaim
  • Produce inside a vessel → must proclaim
  • The vessel serves as a siman for the contents

Produce OUTSIDE the Vessel

  • Coins before a pouch → belongs to finder
  • Produce before a basket → belongs to finder
  • No proof the items are connected

Partially Inside

  • Some produce in vessel, some on ground
  • Some coins in pouch, some outside
  • Must proclaim — evidence of falling out
Baraita The baraita explicitly teaches what the Mishna implies: produce before a vessel belongs to the finder, but produce partially inside must be proclaimed.

Resolving the Contradiction: Container & Flax vs. Basket & Produce

Gemara
A baraita says: an item without a siman found near an item with a siman — must proclaim both. This contradicts our inference!
Rav Zevid
The baraita refers to a container and flax — flax fibers are intertwined, so when some falls out, all falls out. The mishna refers to a basket and produce — individual units don't all fall together.
Rav Pappa
Both refer to a basket and produce. The baraita: some produce remains in the basket. The mishna: no produce remains in the basket.
Gemara
Or both have no produce remaining — the baraita: the basket's mouth faces the produce. The mishna: the mouth does not face it.
Gemara
Or both face the produce — the mishna: the basket has a rim (would have caught some). The baraita: no rim, so all could have spilled.
4 Resolutions The Gemara offers four increasingly nuanced distinctions: (1) type of contents, (2) remaining contents, (3) basket orientation, (4) presence of a rim.

Found Fledglings Behind a Fence

🐦 You find bound fledglings behind a fence or in field paths
↓
Someone likely concealed them there deliberately
↓
Can the knot serve as a siman?
No
Bound at wings — everyone ties them the same way (Rav)
Location as siman?
Birds hop — location is unreliable (Rav Ukva bar Chama)
↓
Result: Do not touch them — leave for the owner
Rav's Rule In any case of uncertainty whether placement was deliberate: ab initio, do not take it. If you already took it, you need not return it.

Vessel Concealed in a Garbage Dump

Mishna
Vessel concealed in a garbage dump → do not touch. Vessel exposed → take and proclaim.
Baraita
Vessel concealed in a garbage dump → take and proclaim, because garbage dumps are routinely cleared!
Rav Zevid
The mishna refers to large items (cups/containers) — clearly placed deliberately. The baraita refers to small items (knives/forks) — may have fallen.
Rav Pappa
Both refer to cups/containers. The baraita: a dump that is designed to be cleared. The mishna: a dump not designed to be cleared.
Gemara
Correction: even Rav Pappa's case refers to a dump not originally meant to be cleared, but the owner reconsidered — otherwise the owner already abandoned hope (ye'ush).
Ye'ush If someone hides an item in a dump meant to be cleared, they effectively abandoned it. The case must be where the owner's plans changed unexpectedly.
