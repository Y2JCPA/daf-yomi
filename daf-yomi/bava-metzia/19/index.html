The Lost Get in the Beit Midrash

The Finder
I lost a get (bill of divorce) that was entrusted to me for delivery.
The Finder
If you need a siman (distinguishing mark), I have one. If you accept tevi'ut ayin (visual recognition), I can do that too.
The Sages
We returned it to him — but which basis did we rely on?
The Finder
I don't know if they relied on simanim (Torah-level validity) or on tevi'ut ayin (scholar's recognition only).
Unresolved The Gemara leaves open whether simanim work by Torah law or only rabbinic law, and whether visual recognition is limited to Torah scholars.

The Nisan–Tishrei Backdating Problem

Husband writes a get in Nisan 📅
↓
He does not deliver it until Tishrei
↓
Between Nisan and Tishrei, husband sells produce from wife's property
↓
Wife produces the get dated Nisan — can she repossess the produce from buyers?
Opinion 1
Once he decided to divorce, he loses rights to her produce → wife repossesses lawfully
Opinion 2
Husband has rights until actual delivery → wife must prove when she received it
Resolution The court tells the wife: bring proof of when the get came into your possession, and only then may you repossess property.

Why Return a Get but NOT a Promissory Note?

Get (Bill of Divorce) ✅

  • Returned so wife won't remain an agunah (chained woman)
  • Purchaser knows it was returned for her personal status, not property claims
  • He will demand proof of the delivery date before surrendering property
  • Wife bears burden of proof → no harm to buyers

Promissory Note ❌

  • Only purpose of returning is to collect debts / repossess property
  • Purchaser assumes court validated the date when returning it
  • He will NOT demand proof — he'll assume the note is fully valid
  • Purchaser is harmed → cannot return the note
Key Distinction The difference is purchaser psychology: with a get, buyers know it serves a non-financial purpose; with a note, they assume the court endorsed it for collection.

Bills of Manumission: The Slave's Property Problem

Gemara
If master admits, return the shtar shichrur (manumission bill) to the slave. But what about the Nisan–Tishrei problem?
Gemara
A slave bought property between Nisan and Tishrei. While still a slave, his property belongs to the master, who sold it.
Abaye
Freedom is in the slave's interest (zakhin), so the witnesses' signatures acquire the bill for him from when it was written.
Gemara
This works for Abaye — but for those who disagree, the same problem applies as with a get: the slave must prove when he received it.
Parallel The manumission analysis mirrors the get: the document serves a personal status function (freedom), so returning it doesn't automatically validate property claims.
