QUESTION 1 OF 5
If a renter hires a donkey and it dies halfway through the journey, what formal legal remedy does the renter have against the owner for the disruption?
A. Full refund of any rental fees already paid
B. A ta'arumet (grievance) but no formal legal claim
C. The right to deduct replacement rental costs from his fee
D. A claim for consequential damages caused by the delay
Correct: B — The Gemara rules the renter pays for the completed half of the journey but retains only a ta'arumet — a grievance — against the owner. This is not a legally actionable claim; it is a moral acknowledgment that the owner disrupted the journey.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rav Pappa, in what precise circumstance does Rabbi Natan's boat ruling apply — that money stays where it is when the boat sinks?
A. Whenever a boat sinks before completing the contracted journey
B. Only when the owner was negligent in maintaining the boat
C. Only when both parties stipulated this specific boat AND that specific cargo
D. Only when the renter had already paid the full rental fee upfront
Correct: C — Rav Pappa clarifies that Rabbi Natan's 'money stays in place' ruling requires double specificity: THIS specific boat was designated by the owner AND THAT specific wine/cargo by the renter. With mutual specificity, neither can demand performance from the other, so loss is shared.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Rav's reason for ruling that the donkey's carcass proceeds cannot be used to pay the rental fee?
A. The carcass is ritually impure and legally non-transferable
B. The carcass constitutes the principal value, and one may not fully deplete the principal
C. Rental fees may only be paid in currency, not goods or their proceeds
D. The owner loses ownership of the carcass once the donkey dies in the renter's care
Correct: B — Rav holds that the carcass now represents the donkey's remaining principal value. His principle is that one may not fully deplete the principal — therefore using carcass proceeds to pay rental fees is prohibited. Shmuel disagrees and permits this deduction.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
If a man rents a donkey specifying that a man will ride, can a woman ride it instead?
A. Yes — women generally weigh less than men, so the donkey is not disadvantaged
B. No — women ride side-saddle, which is actually harder on the donkey than a man riding astride
C. Yes — the renter may always substitute a lighter rider without the owner's objection
D. Only if the owner grants explicit permission at the time of rental
Correct: B — The baraita rules that when a donkey is rented for a man, a woman may not substitute — because women ride side-saddle, a posture that places asymmetric pressure on the donkey's back and is harder on the animal than a man riding astride, even if the woman weighs less.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What broader halakhic principle does Abaye derive from the ruling that a pregnant woman may ride a donkey rented for a woman?
A. Pregnant women are legally identical to non-pregnant women for rental purposes
B. The weight of a fish is in its belly — weight is proportional to belly size
C. Any rider within the same gender category is automatically permitted
D. The owner waives objections to extra weight by specifying a female rider
Correct: B — Abaye derives the principle that 'the weight of a fish is in its belly' — just as a fish's mass is concentrated in its midsection, a person's weight increases in proportion to the size of their belly. This explains why a pregnant woman is heavier than average, yet she is still within the acceptable range for a donkey rented for a woman.
