🌾 Field Rentals, Partnerships & Shared Loss

מַכַּת מְדִינָה — regional disaster; חֲרָמָה — winnowed pile
Core Theme Daf 105 addresses three interconnected areas: (1) Iska partnerships — how joint ventures calculate combined profits/losses, and how partners may (or may not) divide assets unilaterally; (2) Field cultivation duties — what happens when a sharecropper neglects weeding or the crop falls below minimum threshold; (3) Regional disaster (מַכַּת מְדִינָה) — when a cultivator may deduct losses from what he owes the owner. Along the way, precise measurements emerge for piles, olives, and loads on the head from the school of Rabbi Yannai.

🤝 Rava on Iska Partnership Disputes

Rava
If a manager lost money in an iska venture, then replaced that loss on his own without informing the investor — he cannot later claim credit for that replacement when settling accounts.
Rava
Two managers who received goods together from one investor may not unilaterally divide the principal. Either partner can say: "The merchandise is jointly liened to both of us — you cannot force a split."
Combined Document Rule
Two joint ventures recorded in one document: profits and losses are combined. This is to the borrower's detriment — profits from one deal offset losses from the other before any gain is distributed.
Demand vs. Response
Demand: "Give me half the profits and half the principal now." Permitted response: "The merchandise is liened to us both — I can insist we remain partners until we divide everything together."
Key Principle An iska manager cannot take unilateral action — replacing losses secretly or demanding early division — and then claim its benefits when settling.

🌿 Sharecropper Who Refuses to Weed

Sharecropper refuses to weed the field
Offers: "I'll pay you the agreed produce anyway"
↓
Offer A
"I'll plow and remove weeds after harvest"
Owner rejects: grain grown among weeds is inferior quality
Offer B
"I'll buy you good grain from the market"
Owner rejects: I want my field's superior wheat
↓
Mishna stated reason: Weeds will spread — damages future crops
Gemara: this is a secondary reason
↓
Primary Gemara reason: Owner can always insist on superior quality grain, not weed-grown wheat
Quantity alone does not satisfy the obligation

📏 How Much Grain Counts as a Pile? (חֲרָמָה)

Rabbi Yosei b. R. Ḥanina

  • Pile must be large enough for the winnowing shovel to stand in it
  • The blade of the shovel must be covered by grain
  • Blade cannot 'see the face of the sun' — it is buried
  • Clarified by Rabbi Abbahu: this is a physical minimum standard

Rabbi Yehuda (Mishna B)

  • No fixed pile height required
  • Must equal the measure of seeds used to sow that field
  • = 4 kav per kor of sown land
  • Confirmed by both Rav Dimi and Ravin from Eretz Yisrael

Unruly Olives Parallel

  • Beit Shammai & Beit Hillel dispute unruly (peritzei) olives
  • Unruly = wicked olives barely yielding oil (Rav Huna)
  • Rabbi Elazar: only 4 kav of oil extractable per press
  • Different local practices (1 kor vs. 2 kor per press) explain divergent rulings

🦗 Regional Disaster (מַכַּת מְדִינָה) — Legal Framework

Setup: Cultivator rents a field; grasshoppers consume the crop or wind blasting strikes
↓
Requirement: Must be a regional disaster — affecting the whole area, not isolated to this one field
↓
Rav Yehuda's standard: Most of that valley in which the field sits was wind blasted → qualifies as regional disaster
↓
Ulla's standard: Four fields from four directions surrounding this field were also blasted → regional disaster
↓
Ulla's unresolved dilemma (West/Eretz Yisrael): One furrow blasted along its entire length, adjacent to other blasted fields — does it count toward regional disaster?
↓
Legal consequence: If regional — cultivator subtracts proportionally from the produce he owes the owner; loss is shared
