QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava, if two joint ventures (iska) are recorded in one document, how are their profits and losses treated?
A. Each venture is calculated independently
B. Only profits are combined; losses remain separate
C. Both profits and losses are combined — to the borrower's detriment
D. The investor chooses which calculation method to apply
Correct: C — Rava rules that combining two ventures in one document works against the borrower: profits and losses of both are totaled together, so profits from one deal offset losses from the other before any gain is distributed to the manager.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the Gemara say the owner may reject a cultivator's offer to pay compensation instead of weeding?
A. Because monetary substitutes are never valid for land obligations
B. Because the cultivator specifically contracted to weed
C. Because the owner can demand superior quality grain not grown among weeds
D. Because weeds will permanently contaminate the soil
Correct: C — The Gemara's primary reason is quality: even if the cultivator offers equivalent quantity or money, the owner may insist on 'superior wheat' — grain not grown among weeds. The mishna's stated reason (weed-spread) is secondary.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rabbi Yosei son of Rabbi Ḥanina define the minimum 'pile' (charamah) that obligates a cultivator to tend the crop?
A. Four kav of grain by volume
B. Enough grain that the blade of the winnowing shovel is covered and cannot see the sun
C. The amount equal to seeds used to sow the field
D. Any pile a single person can winnow in one day
Correct: B — Rabbi Yosei son of Rabbi Ḥanina (as clarified by Rabbi Abbahu) defines the pile as one large enough that the blade of the winnowing shovel, standing upright in it, is covered — it cannot 'see the face of the sun.'
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to the school of Rabbi Yannai, what must a person do when carrying a load of less than four kav and the time for prayer arrives?
A. Wait until he reaches his destination and sets down the load
B. Skip that prayer entirely since he is engaged in a mitzvah of livelihood
C. Lower the load, pray, then raise it again
D. Lean against a wall and pray with the load still on his shoulder
Correct: C — The baraita rules: a load under four kav — lower it to the ground, recite the prayer, then raise it. A load of four kav or more is too heavy to lower and raise, so one leans it against a wall and prays.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Ulla, what constitutes a 'regional disaster' (makat medinah) for a wind-blasted field?
A. The majority of the entire country experienced the same damage
B. Four fields surrounding the cultivated field from four directions were also blasted
C. The damage affected at least ten adjacent fields
D. The owner's other fields in the same village were also destroyed
Correct: B — Ulla defines makat medinah as four fields from four directions surrounding the cultivated field also being wind blasted. Rav Yehuda uses a different standard: most of that valley. Ulla separately raises an unresolved dilemma about a single furrow blasted along its full length adjacent to other blasted fields.
