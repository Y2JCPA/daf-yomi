QUESTION 1 OF 5
If most of the valley was wind blasted and the tenant's fields were also blasted, what is the halakha regarding the tenant's rent obligation?
A. The tenant owes the full amount since his specific fields were targeted
B. The tenant may subtract proportionally from the produce he owes
C. The tenant owes nothing at all due to the regional disaster
D. The owner must bear the cost of replanting
Correct: B — A regional disaster — where most of the valley is affected — allows the tenant to subtract proportionally from the produce he owes. The loss is communal, not personal. Only if the disaster is confined to the tenant's own fields does he bear full liability.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
The owner argues the tenant owes the full amount even when all the owner's fields were blasted, if the majority of the valley was NOT blasted. What is the owner's reasoning?
A. Because the tenant signed a fixed-sum contract
B. Because Scripture says 'we are left but a few from many' — even in personal misfortune, something would have remained
C. Because the mishap occurred after the crops were already harvested
D. Because the crop failure was due to the tenant's negligence
Correct: B — The owner invokes Jeremiah 42:2 — 'For we are left but a few from many' — arguing that even in extreme personal bad fortune, a remnant would have survived. If nothing remained from his fields while the valley was unaffected, it must be the tenant's misfortune, not his.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Shmuel, when does the regional-disaster rule (allowing the tenant to subtract) apply?
A. Whenever crops fail, regardless of cause
B. Only in the Sabbatical Year
C. Only when the crops sprouted and were then destroyed — not when they never sprouted at all
D. Only for wind blast, not mildew or locusts
Correct: C — Shmuel limits the rule: the tenant may subtract only if the field sprouted and then grasshoppers or locusts consumed it, or it sprouted and was then blasted. If the crop failed to sprout at all, the analysis differs — the tenant has not yet fulfilled any part of his obligation.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel about how many times a tenant must replant after successive crop failures?
A. R. Yehuda says once; Rabban Shimon says twice
B. R. Yehuda says twice; Rabban Shimon says three times — reflecting their dispute over when ḥazakah is established
C. Both agree on two times but dispute whether the owner pays for seeds
D. R. Yehuda says the tenant replants indefinitely; Rabban Shimon sets no limit
Correct: B — Two baraitot conflict: one requires replanting twice (not a third time), the other three times (not a fourth time). The Gemara resolves this as a tannaitic dispute: R. Yehuda HaNasi holds presumptive status (ḥazakah) is established after 2 occurrences; Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel requires 3.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to the mishna at the end of Daf 106, why may a tenant who received a field to plant wheat instead plant barley without liability?
A. Because barley and wheat command the same market price
B. Because the owner implicitly consented to any grain crop
C. Because barley weakens the soil less than wheat, so the owner suffers no loss
D. Because barley produces a higher yield, compensating the owner
Correct: C — The mishna permits substituting barley for wheat precisely because barley is less damaging to the soil than wheat. Since the landowner suffers no loss — and may even benefit — the change is permitted. Rav Ḥisda connects this to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's principle: where no actual loss occurred, no liability attaches.
