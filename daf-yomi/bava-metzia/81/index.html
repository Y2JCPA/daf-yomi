⚖️ After Completing Work: What Bailee Status?

Rav Ḥisda
An artisan who completed the work is a paid bailee — responsibility for the item continues even after finishing the job.
Objection
This contradicts the mishna! If artisan = paid bailee, why does the mishna separately teach the case of 'Take what is yours and bring money'?
Resolution
The mishna must teach 'take yours' explicitly — otherwise one might think that statement removes all bailee responsibility, even unpaid!
Rav Naḥman bar Pappa
Actually, the mishna supports Rav Ḥisda — comparing artisans to the 'take yours' case, both emerge as paid bailees.
Huna Mar bar Mareimar
There is a contradiction between mishnayot — resolved by noting each case involves distinct language about accepting responsibility.
Core Principle Completing work does not end responsibility — the artisan remains a paid bailee for the item until the owner retrieves it.

🐴 Borrower After Loan Period — Ameimar & the Baraita

During Loan Period

  • Status: Borrower
  • Liable for accidents (ones)
  • Full responsibility — even unavoidable loss
  • Highest level of bailee obligation

After Returning the Item

  • Status: Paid Bailee (Ameimar)
  • Exempt from unforeseeable accidents
  • Still liable for theft and negligence
  • Obligation reduced, not eliminated

Baraita Proof (Tosefta Bava Batra 6:5)

  • Vessels taken to father-in-law as gift
  • Broken going → liable (like borrower)
  • Broken returning → exempt (like paid bailee)
  • Return trip = paid bailee; a fortiori applies to borrower

🤝 Mutual Bailment & the Owner-Present Exemption

The Rule (Mishna): 'Safeguard my property and I'll safeguard yours' — each person is a paid bailee for the other, since the reciprocal benefit counts as compensation.
↓
Ice Plant Dealers — Rav Pappa: A group rotated baking duties. One said: 'Guard my cloak while I bake for you today.' The cloak was stolen. Rav Pappa ruled them liable.
↓
Rabbis' Objection: This is שאלה בבעלים — bailment while the owner is present! The owner was baking for them at the very moment the cloak was stolen. Rav Pappa was embarrassed.
↓
Resolution: It emerged that at the time of theft, the owner had already finished baking and left — he was not present. Liability was reinstated.
↓
Tall Man & Short Man — Rava: A tall man riding a donkey lent his sheet to a short man walking; in return, the short man covered them both with his woolen cloak. The sheet was lost.
↓
Rava initially ruled the short man liable. Rabbis protested: borrowing with the owner present! Rava was embarrassed. It then emerged the tall man was absent when the sheet was lost — liability confirmed.

🗣️ The Donkey Dispute: Does Miggo Apply?

The Case
Owner rented his donkey with explicit instruction: 'Do not go via Nehar Pekod (dangerous watery path) — go via Neresh instead.' The renter took the Nehar Pekod path. The donkey drowned.
Renter's Claim
I followed instructions and went via Neresh — the donkey died there from natural causes, not from water!
Rava
The renter is believed! Why would he lie? — מִגּוֹ: if he wanted to deceive, he could have said 'I went via Neresh' and that would be unverifiable. His weaker claim suggests truth.
Abaye
We do not apply miggo in monetary cases to exempt someone from payment. The renter violated instructions and must pay!
Rav Naḥman (re: vessels)
The return trip is treated like the trip to the recipient — because even on the way back, if an opportunity to sell arose, the agent would have taken it. The purpose persists.
מִגּוֹ — Credibility Principle When a litigant could have told a more advantageous lie but chose a weaker claim instead, that restraint lends credibility to the weaker claim.
