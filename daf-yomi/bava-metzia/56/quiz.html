QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why are documents (שטרות) excluded from the laws of ona'ah?
A. They are too fragile to be considered merchandise
B. They have no intrinsic value — they exist only for the proof within them
C. They are compared to land through a biblical juxtaposition
D. The Torah explicitly lists them as an exception
Correct: B — The Gemara derives from 'an item that is itself sold and itself acquired' that documents, which have value only for the proof they contain rather than intrinsic worth, are excluded from ona'ah.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Abaye, why is rental subject to the laws of ona'ah?
A. The Rabbis enacted a special decree extending ona'ah to rentals
B. Rental is considered a form of gifting, which is subject to ona'ah
C. The verse says 'an item that is sold' — not 'sold forever' — and rental is a sale for its day
D. Rental involves movable property, which is always subject to ona'ah
Correct: C — Abaye argues that since the verse does not say 'sell forever,' a rental qualifies as a temporary sale (שכירות ליומיה מכירה היא) and is therefore subject to ona'ah.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the Gemara's conclusion about the meaning of 'his hand' (יָדוֹ) in the Torah?
A. It always means general possession, never the literal hand
B. It always means the literal hand unless context makes that impossible
C. It means literal hand only in civil law, but possession in ritual law
D. The meaning depends on whether the subject is movable or immovable property
Correct: B — The Gemara concludes that every mention of 'his hand' defaults to the literal hand. Only where it is logically impossible (e.g., holding all his land) does it mean possession, and special textual expansions are needed to extend beyond the literal hand.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rav Yosef reconcile Rabbi Meir's seemingly lenient stance on demai?
A. Rabbi Meir is lenient on demai only when dealing with wholesalers
B. Rabbi Meir is lenient on redemption of demai but stringent on its consumption
C. Rabbi Meir treats all rabbinic law identically to Torah law
D. Rabbi Meir's leniency applies only to second-tithe, not to demai
Correct: B — Rav Yosef distinguishes between two aspects: Rabbi Meir is lenient regarding how one redeems demai (e.g., silver onto copper), but stringent about ensuring tithes are separated before consumption — requiring proprietors to tithe before selling.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does the Gemara reject Rav Kahana's opinion that there is no ona'ah for transactions involving perutot?
A. Because the Torah sets no minimum transaction value for ona'ah
B. Because the case of documents sold as wrapping paper to a perfumer — worth mere perutot — is subject to ona'ah
C. Because Rav Kahana's view contradicts an explicit Mishnah
D. Because perutot are still legal tender and count as real money
Correct: B — The Gemara teaches that documents sold to a perfumer as packaging paper are subject to ona'ah despite being worth only perutot, directly contradicting Rav Kahana's position that small-value transactions are exempt.
