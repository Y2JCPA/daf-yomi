QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Ashi, why does the baraita distinguish between collecting the principal from liened property and produce from unsold property?
A. The baraita is disjunctive: the robber consumed the produce (owed to victim) and sold the field (purchaser claims principal)
B. The produce was never legally owned by anyone
C. Liened property can only be used for land-based debts
D. The robber explicitly designated different assets for each debt
Correct: A — Rav Ashi reads the baraita as referring to two separate claims by two different parties: the purchaser claims the principal (field value) from liened property, while the robbery victim claims produce value from unsold property.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does the Gemara explain that a robbery debt — normally an oral agreement — can be collected from liened property?
A. Robbery debts are always treated as documented debts
B. The robber stood trial before selling the land, making the debt equivalent to a documented obligation
C. The Torah grants special collection rights for robbery victims
D. Witnesses to the robbery serve as documentation
Correct: B — Standing trial (omed b'din) transforms an oral debt into the equivalent of a documented one. Since the robber sold his land after being found guilty, purchasers of that land were on notice of the debt.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does Rav Yosef argue that paying a buyer for enhancement of stolen land does not constitute prohibited interest (ribbit)?
A. Because the Torah permits interest on stolen property
B. Because the transaction is classified as a sale, not a loan — buying low and selling high is legitimate commerce
C. Because the robber performed a kinyan that exempts him from ribbit laws
D. Because the enhancement was created by the buyer, not the seller
Correct: B — Rav Yosef distinguishes between loans and sales. In a loan, returning more than borrowed looks like interest. But in a sale context, paying more than the original price is normal commerce, not ribbit.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Shmuel, when a person knowingly buys a field from a robber, what happens to the money when the true owner reclaims the field?
A. The money is returned to the buyer as a deposit
B. The money is split between buyer and seller
C. The money is considered a gift to the seller and is not returned
D. The money is paid to the true owner as compensation
Correct: C — Shmuel holds that a person who knowingly enters an invalid sale clearly intended the money as a gift (matana). He didn't say 'gift' explicitly because it would have been embarrassing for the seller.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does the Gemara need BOTH the stolen-field case and the betrothing-one's-sister case to establish the Rav-Shmuel dispute?
A. Because each case involves a different amount of money
B. Because the stolen-field case alone might limit Rav's view to non-relatives (who don't give gifts), and the sister case alone might limit Shmuel's view to relatives
C. Because one case is biblical and the other is rabbinic
D. Because the sister case involves a different type of kinyan
Correct: B — Without both cases, we might think the dispute is limited: perhaps Rav only says 'deposit' for non-relatives (people don't give strangers gifts), or Shmuel only says 'gift' for relatives. Both cases show the dispute is universal.
