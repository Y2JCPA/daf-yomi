Three Readings of the Robbery Baraita

Rava
The field was legally appropriated from the buyer — and the robber damaged the produce before the field was taken
Rabba bar Rav Huna
Gentile thugs took the field by force — the field itself was undamaged (taken in its unadulterated form)
Rava
I reject Rabba bar Rav Huna's reading: 'appropriated from his possession' implies legal seizure, not thugs
Rabba bar Rav Huna
I reject Rava's reading: 'appropriated' implies the field was taken whole, not damaged
Rav Ashi
The baraita is disjunctive: robber consumed the produce AND sold the field — two separate claims by two different plaintiffs
Key Point All three agree: principal is collected from liened property (meshubadim), produce only from unsold property (bnei ḥorin).

Why Can Robbery Victims Collect from Liened Property?

Problem: Robbery debt is an oral agreement (milveh al peh) — cannot collect from liened property!
↓
Answer: The robber stood trial (omed b'din) before selling land
↓
A court judgment is equivalent to a documented debt (shtar)
↓
But then — why not collect produce from liened property too?
↓
He stood trial for the principal only, not yet for the produce
↓
Why? Normal practice: a person first claims the principal, then later claims produce
Legal Principle Standing trial (omed b'din) transforms an oral obligation into a documented one — but only for the specific claims adjudicated.

Enhancement, Sales & the Prohibition of Interest (Ribbit)

Gemara
Shmuel said deeds of sale should include a clause promising to reimburse enhancement and produce if the field is repossessed — doesn't this contradict his ruling?
Rav Yosef
The robber owns land, so paying enhancement looks like a sale transaction, not interest
Abaye
But borrowing a se'a for a se'a is prohibited even when the borrower owns land! How is this different?
Rav Yosef
That case involves a loan (halva'ah). This case involves a sale (mekher). Buying low and selling high is legitimate commerce!
Abaye
(Second version:) Even if an act of acquisition (kinyan) formalized the condition — a se'a for se'a is still prohibited with a kinyan!
Rav Yosef
Same answer: loan vs. sale. The sale framework prevents it from being classified as ribbit
Halakhic Distinction The loan-vs-sale framework is critical: the same economic result (paying more than received) can be prohibited interest in a loan context but permitted profit in a sale context.

Buying from a Known Robber: Rav vs. Shmuel

Rav — Money Returns (Deposit)

  • Buyer gets money back when owner reclaims field
  • But NO reimbursement for enhancement
  • Rationale: buyer knew sale was invalid → gave money as a pikadon (deposit)
  • Why not say 'deposit' explicitly? He feared the seller would refuse to hold it
  • The buyer wanted the seller to safeguard his money under the guise of a sale

Shmuel — Money Is a Gift

  • Buyer does NOT get money back at all
  • Rationale: buyer knew sale was invalid → gave money as a matana (gift)
  • Why not say 'gift' explicitly? It would be embarrassing for the seller
  • The buyer used the sale as a ploy to give a dignified gift
  • Complete loss — neither money nor enhancement
Core Dispute When someone knowingly enters an invalid transaction, did they intend their money as a deposit (Rav) or a gift (Shmuel)?
