When Is a Debtor Believed?

Court issues a verdict against the debtor
↓
"Go and give him"
Verdict is finalized — debtor was told to pay
"You are liable"
Verdict is not yet finalized — liability declared only
↓
Debtor later claims: "I repaid"
↓
Rav Papa
"Go give" → believed with oath
"You are liable" → not believed
Rav Zevid / R' Nachman
In both cases the debtor is believed with an oath
Key Principle The degree of court finality determines whether a debtor's repayment claim is credible

Court Verdicts: Two Formulations

Rav Papa
If the court said "Go and give him" (final verdict) and the debtor later says "I repaid" — he is believed with an oath
Rav Papa
But if the court only said "You are liable" (not finalized) and the debtor claims repayment — he is not believed, since he refused to pay voluntarily
Rav Zevid
In the name of R' Nachman: In both cases — whether "Go give" or "You are liable" — the debtor is believed when he says he repaid
Rav Zevid
The court does not write an authorization to seize property in either case
Authorization (הרשאה) If the debtor is not believed, the court writes an authorization for the creditor to seize the debtor's property

Chezkat Kafran — Becoming a Presumed Denier

Court says "Go and give him"
↓
Debtor says: "I repaid"
↓
Witnesses testify: He did NOT repay in their presence
↓
Debtor claims again: "I repaid"
↓
After "Go give"
He is a chezkat kafran (presumed denier) — not believed even with an oath
After "You are liable"
He is not a kafran — he was merely stalling, hoping the court would investigate further
Rav Zevid's Distinction Before the verdict is finalized, the debtor's denial is treated as delay tactics, not dishonesty

Denial Then Caught: Debt vs. Oath

Denying a Debt

  • Debtor says: "Nothing of yours is in my possession"
  • Witnesses prove the debt exists
  • Debtor then claims: "I repaid"
  • → He is a chezkat kafran — NOT believed (Rabba bar bar Channa / R' Yochanan)

Denying an Oath

  • Court obligates defendant to take an oath
  • He claims: "I took the oath"
  • Witnesses testify he did NOT take it
  • He claims again: "I took the oath"
  • → He is a kafran regarding that oath (R' Avin / R' Ela / R' Yochanan)
R' Abbahu's Qualification This applies only to court-imposed oaths. If one voluntarily offered to swear and then didn't, he is still believed — people casually promise oaths and change their minds

The Case of Shabbtai's Cloak

חזקת כפרן — presumptive status of a denier
מעשה — A Real Case Shabbtai son of R' Marinus pledged in his daughter-in-law's ketubah to give her an itztela demileta (fine wool cloak) and guaranteed the contract.

When the ketubah was lost, Shabbtai denied: "These matters never occurred."

Witnesses came and confirmed the pledge existed.

Shabbtai then claimed: "I already gave her the cloak."

R' Chiyya ruled: You are now a chezkat kafran — your repayment claim is rejected.
