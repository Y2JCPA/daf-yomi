QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why is muzzling an animal that threshes actual teruma generally NOT a Torah violation?
A. Teruma is not considered real food and the prohibition only covers grain
B. The animal is not legally permitted to eat teruma, so the prohibition of muzzling does not apply
C. Teruma is always threshed before separation, so no issue arises
D. The Torah's muzzling prohibition only applies in the Land of Israel
Correct: B — The prohibition of muzzling protects the animal's right to eat what it is working with. Since teruma may not be eaten by the animal (it is sacred and reserved for priests), the owner is not denying any lawful right by muzzling — hence no Torah violation. However, onlookers may not know the produce is teruma, so there is still a concern of marit ayin.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rabbi Meir, what is the legal status of second tithe?
A. It belongs to the Levite and must be given over entirely
B. It is the owner's property to use freely, including feeding to animals
C. It is property of the Most High — the owner may only consume it personally, not feed it to animals
D. It may be redeemed with money and used for any purpose without restriction
Correct: C — Rabbi Meir holds that second tithe is 'property of the Most High' (ממון גבוה). The owner has only the personal right to consume it in Jerusalem — he cannot authorize his animal to eat it. Therefore, muzzling an animal that threshes second tithe is not a violation, since the animal has no legal right to eat it.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What did Rav Sheshet derive from the baraita of Rabbi Shimon ben Yohai regarding an animal with diarrhea?
A. The owner may muzzle the animal to protect its health during illness
B. The animal must be removed from the threshing floor when ill
C. One may hang vetches for the animal, showing the obligation is based on the animal's benefit, not merely its right to the specific produce
D. The owner must provide double the normal food as compensation for the restriction
Correct: C — Rav Sheshet pointed to Rabbi Shimon ben Yohai's teaching that one can hang vetches (which are better for the animal than the threshed crop) for the animal. This proves the reason behind the prohibition is promoting the animal's wellbeing and benefit — not merely enforcing an abstract right to eat the specific produce it is threshing.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the ruling regarding one who muzzles an animal that belongs to someone else while it threshes?
A. He is fully liable, just as if he were the owner doing the threshing
B. He is exempt, because liability requires that the one who muzzles also be the one doing the threshing
C. He is liable only rabbinically, not by Torah law
D. He is exempt only if the act was accidental
Correct: B — The Sages taught in a baraita: one who muzzles a cow that someone else is using for threshing is exempt. The Torah verse links the muzzling prohibition to the one who is threshing — liability falls on the person conducting the threshing operation, not on a third party who interferes with the animal.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In the dispute between Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yoḥanan about muzzling with one's voice, what is Reish Lakish's position?
A. Voice-muzzling is entirely permitted and carries no prohibition whatsoever
B. Voice-muzzling violates only a rabbinic decree, not the Torah prohibition
C. One who muzzles an animal with his voice — berating it away from food — is liable under the Torah prohibition of לא תחסום
D. Voice-muzzling applies only to the kilayim prohibition, not to the muzzling prohibition
Correct: C — Reish Lakish holds that any effective act of preventing the animal from eating constitutes forbidden muzzling — including verbal intimidation (berating the animal whenever it tries to eat). He extends the same logic to kilayim: leading diverse kinds together by voice alone also incurs liability. Rabbi Yoḥanan disagrees on both counts, requiring a physical act.
