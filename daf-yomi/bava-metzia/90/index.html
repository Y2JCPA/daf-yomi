🐄 Muzzling, Sacred Produce, and the Limits of לא תחסום

לֹא תַחְסֹם שׁוֹר בְּדִישׁוֹ — You shall not muzzle an ox while it threshes (Devarim 25:4)
Core Question The Torah prohibits muzzling an ox while it threshes — but what if the produce is teruma or tithe, which the animal may not legally eat? The Gemara resolves contradictions between baraitot through distinctions between types of tithe, then extends the analysis to cases of animal illness, gentile agents, pre-threshing muzzling, and voice-based restraint.

⚖️ First Tithe vs Second Tithe — Who Owns It?

First Tithe (Ma'aser Rishon)

  • Belongs to the Levite
  • Owner has no rights over it once separated
  • Owner may not authorize animal to eat it
  • Muzzling during first tithe threshing = NOT a violation
  • Both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda agree on this

Second Tithe — Rabbi Meir

  • Second tithe = property of the Most High
  • Owner has only the right to consume it personally
  • Owner may not let animal eat second tithe
  • Muzzling during second tithe threshing = NOT a violation
  • Baraita permitting muzzling follows Rabbi Meir

Second Tithe — Rabbi Yehuda

  • Second tithe = owner's property
  • Owner retains full rights and may feed it to animals
  • Owner may authorize animal to eat second tithe
  • Muzzling during second tithe threshing = Torah violation
  • Baraita forbidding muzzling follows Rabbi Yehuda

🔇 Voice-Muzzling: Does Verbal Restraint Violate the Prohibition?

Reish Lakish
One who muzzles an animal with his voice — berating it whenever it tries to eat — is liable under the Torah prohibition. Any effective act of prevention counts, including verbal intimidation.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
One who muzzles with voice alone is not liable. The verse implies a physical act of muzzling. Liability requires doing something tangible to the animal or its environment.
Reish Lakish
The same logic applies to כלאים (diverse kinds) — if one leads an ox and donkey together using only his voice, without holding reins, he is liable. Effective direction = prohibited act.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
I raise an objection from a baraita: the language of these prohibitions implies physical involvement. Leading by voice or muzzling by voice alone — no liability in either case.
Status: Unresolved The Gemara ends with Rabbi Yoḥanan's objection outstanding — the dispute between Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yoḥanan on voice-based violations of both muzzling and kilayim remains live.

⚖️ Muzzling Before Threshing Begins — Rabbi Yonatan's Dilemma

Question (Rabbi Yonatan → Rabbi Simai): If one muzzled the animal before it entered the threshing floor, is one liable? The muzzling and the threshing are separated in time.
↓
Argument for exemption: The verse reads 'an ox in its threshing' — the muzzling must occur simultaneously with threshing. A pre-applied muzzle = no violation.
↓
Argument for liability: The Torah prohibits causing the animal to be in a muzzled state while threshing. If the muzzle is present during threshing, the owner is responsible regardless of when it was applied.
↓
Rabbi Simai's analogy: A priest is forbidden to drink wine before entering the Sanctuary. The prohibition covers the approach — similarly, muzzling before threshing that carries forward into threshing should be prohibited.
↓
Rejected: The priest-wine verse explicitly includes 'when you come into the Tent of Meeting' — it covers approach. The muzzling verse has no such extension. Dilemma remains unresolved (תיקו).
