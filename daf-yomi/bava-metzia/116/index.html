🏠 Two Worlds on Daf 116 Daf 116 closes the collateral law debates — how many lashes for taking millstones, and whether seized items can offset a debt — then pivots to two mishnas about a collapsed house. When a building shared by two owners collapses, how are the stones divided? When a tenant's floor breaks, who must repair it? The daf probes ownership evidence, possession presumptions, and the limits of rental agreements.

🔪 The Slaughtering Knife: Abaye vs. the Creditor

Creditor
I took a slaughtering knife as collateral from a debtor. What am I required to do?
Abaye
Return it immediately! A slaughtering knife is a vessel used in food preparation — it is included in the biblical prohibition against taking such items as collateral.
Questioner
But Abaye, didn't you once allow a creditor to keep goats that ate his barley, offsetting the debt owed to him? Why can't the knife-holder do the same?
Abaye
Goats are not usually lent or rented — whoever holds them has a presumptive right of ownership. But a knife is commonly lent out, so possessing it proves nothing about ownership.
Rava
Consistent with this: I myself removed wool-scissors and an aggada scroll from orphans — precisely because such items are commonly lent or rented and cannot ground a possession claim.
📌 Key Rule Items commonly lent or rented cannot establish presumptive ownership and must be returned. Items not commonly lent (e.g., goats) allow the holder to base a possession claim on them.

📋 Two Mishna Cases: Collapse Scenarios Compared

Two Owners — House Collapses

  • Lower story: Owner A | Upper story: Owner B
  • Both floors collapse together
  • Default: divide stones, timber, and earth equally
  • If stones identifiable: follow evidence of breakage
  • Recognized stones: claimant takes them (Abaye: vs. unbroken share only)
  • Dispute re: ownership → Rav Naḥman's oath rule applies

One Owner + Tenant — Floor Breaks

  • Owner rents upper story; lower story is owner's
  • Floor of upper story breaks or collapses
  • Owner refuses to repair it
  • Rav: majority of floor damaged → tenant may vacate
  • Rav Naḥman / Rav Ada: even 4 handbreadths → tenant may act
  • Rav Ashi: depends on exact rental language used

🪨 Rava vs. Abaye: What Does 'Count Toward His Amount' Mean?

Mishna
If one recognized some of his stones, he takes them for himself, and they count toward his amount of stones.
Rava (initial reading)
The recognized stones count toward his total share of all stones — broken and unbroken combined. He takes the identified ones and fewer from the shared pile overall.
Abaye
On the contrary — he is actually worse off! Since he recognizes these specific stones but nothing more, he evidently has no knowledge of any others. Therefore he has no claim to any additional stones beyond these.
Abaye (ruling)
The recognized unbroken stones count against his share of unbroken stones only. He takes his identified unbroken stones; the other party takes an equal number of unbroken stones; and the broken stones are divided equally between them.
⚖️ Principle Recognizing some property is not automatically advantageous — it can limit your claim to the rest by implying you have no knowledge of other items in the pile.
