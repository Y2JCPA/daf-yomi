🏚️ How Much of the Olive Press Roof Must Collapse?

Rav
Most of the roof must have collapsed before the obligation to provide replacement stones is triggered.
Shmuel
Even a hole of four handbreadths is sufficient — a majority collapse is not required.
Gemara
This same dispute applies to the upper story collapse case (116b). Both rulings must be stated — each carries unique reasoning that cannot be derived from the other.
Why both cases are necessary Had only the upper story case been taught, we might think the ruling is unique to that context. Stating both eliminates any such inference and establishes the principle broadly.

💸 Rav Naḥman's Evolving Resolution

Contradiction: Mishna says court does not listen to employer paying wages in straw; Baraita says court does listen
↓
1st attempt: Mishna = employer's own property; Baraita = ownerless property
↓
Rava objects: Baraita about a laborer's found item contradicts this distinction — laborer keeps found items even when working with ownerless property
↓
2nd attempt: Mishna = laborer working with employer's property; Baraita = ownerless property (Rava again objects)
↓
Final resolution: Both cases = ownerless property
Mishna = laborer not yet done → can still refuse
Baraita = laborer already finished → court accepts payment in kind

👁️ Does Viewing Ownerless Property Effect Acquisition?

Rabba: A Tannaitic Dispute

  • Based on the sefiḥim watchmen debate (Shekalim 9b)
  • First Tanna: viewing does effect acquisition — watchman acquires grain by sight, so he may volunteer as unpaid bailee
  • Rabbi Yosei: viewing does not effect acquisition — watchman does not acquire; the four-se'a stipend comes from communal directive
  • The phrase 'do you say so?' = challenge about volunteering as unpaid bailee

Rava: Two Alternative Explanations

  • Version 1: Everyone agrees viewing does effect acquisition; dispute is whether watchman will hand over grain wholeheartedly to the community
  • Version 2: Everyone agrees viewing does not effect acquisition; dispute concerns the communal omer offering directive and the four-se'a stipend
  • Both versions redirect the dispute away from acquisition mechanics to intent and communal obligation

🌿 Two Gardens: Ownership of Vegetables in the Dividing Wall

Scenario: Upper and lower garden separated by a soil wall (plateau edge); vegetables sprout from within the wall between them — who owns them?
↓
Rabbi Meir: Look at where the vegetable lives (draws nutrients). Roots in upper soil → the upper garden owner takes all. Since both parties can object, we follow the practical reality.
↓
Rabbi Shimon: Split ownership — roots belong to the upper garden owner; leaves hanging into lower airspace belong to the lower garden owner.
↓
Rava clarifies: Everyone agrees roots belong to the upper garden owner (his ground, his property). The dispute is only about the leaves.
↓
Baraita confirmation: For a tree growing on another's land — trunk and roots belong to the land owner beneath them; branches extending over another's property belong to that land owner. The tannaim are consistent.
