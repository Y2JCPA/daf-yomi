QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Shmuel, why is the borrower liable when the lender sends the cow via his slave?
A. Because any person designated by the lender transfers liability automatically
B. Because it is a Hebrew slave, whose person the master does not acquire — so items in his hand are not in the master's domain
C. Because the borrower explicitly consented to the specific slave as messenger
D. Because a Canaanite slave is considered legally independent in all matters
Correct: B — Shmuel holds the mishna refers to a Hebrew slave. Unlike a Canaanite slave, the master does not acquire a Hebrew slave's person. Therefore items in the slave's possession are not in the master's domain, and delivery to him shifts liability to the borrower.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rav Huna, when does a borrower of an ax first become liable for unavoidable mishaps?
A. From the moment he verbally agrees to borrow it
B. From the moment he lifts or pulls it (meshicha)
C. Only once he has actually chopped wood with it — the first use
D. When he carries it out of the lender's property
Correct: C — Rav Huna rules that borrowing is not activated — and liability does not begin — until the borrower makes first use of the ax (chopping wood). Until then, the borrowing relationship has not been realized.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does Shmuel rule that a robber pays only 49 perutot (not 50) when compensating a common person for a cake of 50 dates?
A. The robber receives a one-peruta discount for voluntarily returning the stolen item
B. Each individual date is worth less than one peruta, so the final date cannot be compensated — it is below the minimum unit
C. Rabbinic law requires the victim to forgive one peruta as a goodwill gesture
D. The rule applies specifically to food items that spoil during the theft
Correct: B — Since each date individually is worth less than one peruta, the last date falls below the minimum compensable amount (a peruta). Payment to a common person is therefore 49 perutot. The Temple treasury, however, receives the full 50.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rabbi Ami's ruling about a consecrated ax contradict Rav Huna?
A. Rabbi Ami says consecrated property may never be lent under any circumstances
B. Rabbi Ami says the lender commits misuse of consecrated property the moment he lends it, implying the borrower acquires it immediately — not only upon first use
C. Rabbi Ami agrees with Rav Huna but applies a stricter standard to Temple property
D. Rabbi Ami says only the borrower — not the lender — can commit misuse of consecrated property
Correct: B — For misuse of consecrated property (me'ila) to apply to the lender, the item must leave the Temple's domain at the point of lending. Rabbi Ami says this happens immediately when the ax is lent — implying acquisition and liability begin at that moment, contradicting Rav Huna's 'first use' rule.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Under Rava's ruling, when must porters pay the higher market-day price of 5 dinars for a broken barrel?
A. Whenever the barrel is physically broken on a market day, regardless of other circumstances
B. Only when the shopkeeper had no other barrels available and thus actually lost market-day sales
C. Only when the barrel was purchased at the market-day price originally
D. Whenever the shopkeeper demands the market-day rate within thirty days
Correct: B — Rava limits the 5-dinar rate to cases where the shopkeeper had no substitute barrels. If he had other wine to sell, he suffered no special market-day loss and only the standard rate applies. Additionally, the porters deduct saved costs (selling effort and tapping) from whatever rate applies.
