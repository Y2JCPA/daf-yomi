QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the Gemara reject deriving Rabbi Ḥiyya's oath obligation directly from gilgul shevua (extension of oath from one witness)?
A. Because gilgul shevua only applies to monetary claims, not oaths
B. Because in gilgul shevua, one oath leads to another oath — but witnesses cause monetary liability, not oaths
C. Because gilgul shevua requires the consent of the defendant
D. Because gilgul shevua only works in cases of deposit, not loans
Correct: B — The Gemara objects that gilgul shevua is unique in that 'one oath leads to another oath' — it extends oath obligations. But witnesses create payment liability, which is categorically different from extending an existing oath.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the 'tzad ha-shaveh' (common denominator) that ultimately supports Rabbi Ḥiyya's inference?
A. Both admission and one witness involve monetary payment
B. Both admission and one witness involve cases that come before the court with a claim and its denial, resulting in an oath
C. Both admission and one witness are subject to the laws of conspiring witnesses
D. Both admission and one witness require corroboration by a second witness
Correct: B — After each source individually fails due to unique features (pirka), the Gemara combines them: their shared element is that both involve a claim and its denial before the court, with the defendant being obligated to swear. This common denominator extends to Rabbi Ḥiyya's case of two witnesses.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Ḥisda, why is someone who denies receiving a loan (contradicted by witnesses) still fit to testify?
A. Because witnesses to a loan are inherently unreliable
B. Because loan denial is only a rabbinic prohibition, not biblical
C. Because he may have intended to repay later and denied only to buy time
D. Because the loan may have already been partially repaid
Correct: C — Rav Ḥisda distinguishes loans from deposits: a loan-denier may simply be stalling for time to acquire funds, so he is not a confirmed liar. A deposit-denier, however, has no such excuse and is considered a robber.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In the dispute about an unspecified promissory note where the debtor admits to owing 3 dinars, why does Rabbi Akiva exempt the debtor from an oath?
A. Because the promissory note has already been paid in full
B. Because admitting to more than the minimum implied amount makes him equivalent to one returning a lost item
C. Because the creditor failed to specify the amount at the time of the loan
D. Because three dinars is below the minimum threshold for oath obligations
Correct: B — Rabbi Akiva reasons that since the note only proves 2 dinars (the minimum plural), admitting to 3 is voluntarily returning more than required — like returning a lost item. One who acts honestly in this way should not be burdened with an oath.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
The Gemara offers two reasons why the promissory note case does not disprove Rabbi Ḥiyya's opinion that 'heilakh' requires an oath. What are they?
A. The debtor is a minor, and the note was written by a scribe
B. The note supports the debtor's claim, and land-secured debts are categorically exempt from oaths
C. The note was not properly witnessed, and the debt was already forgiven
D. The creditor waived his right to an oath, and the note was post-dated
Correct: B — Two independent answers: (1) The note itself corroborates the debtor's claim of owing only 2, distinguishing it from a standard heilakh case. (2) Promissory notes create liens on land (shibud karka'ot), and oaths are not administered for land-secured debts — a categorical exemption.
