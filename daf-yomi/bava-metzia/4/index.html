Rav Pappa's Foundation for the Kal Va-Chomer

Rav Pappa
Rabbi Ḥiyya's inference derives from gilgul shevua — once a single witness obligates an oath, the plaintiff can demand an oath on other claims as well.
Gemara
But gilgul shevua is notable in that one oath leads to another oath. That's not the same as witnesses causing monetary liability!
Gemara
Admission (hoda'at piv) can prove otherwise — admission is not an oath, yet it causes an oath obligation on the rest of the claim.
Gemara
But admission is notable in that it is not subject to contradiction — no witnesses can disprove what you yourself admitted!
Gemara
One witness can prove that even testimony subject to contradiction still generates an oath obligation.
Pattern The Gemara uses a classic 'pirka' (objection) cycle: each source is challenged for a unique feature, then another source fills the gap.

The Tzad Ha-Shaveh (Common Denominator) Argument

Source 1: Admission to part of a claim (modeh b'miktzat)
→ Oath on the rest
↓
Source 2: Extension of oath from one witness (gilgul shevua)
→ Oath on other claims
↓
Common denominator:
Both come before the court with a claim and its denial, and defendant is obligated to swear
↓
Therefore: Two witnesses to part of debt (Rabbi Ḥiyya's case) also involves claim + denial → oath required
Tzad Ha-Shaveh When two individual sources each fail due to unique features, their shared element can establish a principle — a foundational Talmudic method of derivation.

Denying a Loan vs. Denying a Deposit

Denied a Loan (הלוואה)

  • Witnesses prove he received a loan
  • Defendant denied it — but remains fit to testify
  • Reason: He may intend to repay later and denied only to buy time
  • Not considered a confirmed liar (kafran)
  • Rabbi Ḥiyya's oath inference survives

Denied a Deposit (פיקדון)

  • Witnesses prove he received a deposit
  • Defendant denied it — disqualified as a witness
  • Reason: No legitimate reason to deny holding someone else's property
  • Considered a robber (gazlan)
  • Clearly intends to steal the deposit
Rav Idi bar Avin / Rav Ḥisda This distinction rescues Rabbi Ḥiyya's inference: since denying a loan does not make one a 'kafran,' the defendant's oath retains credibility.

Does 'Heilakh' (Here You Are) Trigger an Oath?

Rav Sheshet
One who says heilakh ('here you are') while paying part of the claim is exempt from an oath — he is not truly 'denying' anything.
Gemara
From R' Shimon ben Elazar's ruling: the debtor is obligated to swear because he admitted to 3. Implying if he admitted to only 2 (= heilakh), he'd be exempt. This supports Rav Sheshet!
Gemara
Or perhaps: even admitting to 2 requires an oath. The baraita specifies 3 only to exclude Rabbi Akiva's 'returning a lost item' reasoning.
Gemara
If so, the baraita should say 'even this one takes an oath' — its phrasing implies 2 is genuinely excluded.
Gemara
The note supports him — that's why he's exempt at 2. Or: promissory notes create a lien on land, and oaths don't apply to land-secured debts.
Two Answers The Gemara offers two independent reasons the promissory note case doesn't disprove Rabbi Ḥiyya: (1) the note itself corroborates the debtor, (2) land-liens are categorically exempt from oaths.
