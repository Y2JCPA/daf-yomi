Bava Metzia 13 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Asi, for which type of document may a promissory note be written for a borrower without the lender present?
A. Any promissory note with witnesses
B. Only deeds of transfer (akarata)
C. Only notes without a property guarantee
D. Only notes verified by a court
Correct: B — Rav Asi limits writing in the lender's absence to deeds of transfer, where the debtor obligates his property from the date the document is written regardless of when the loan occurs.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Abaye say witness signatures acquire the lien even for ordinary promissory notes?
A. Because the court validates all documents automatically
B. Because the witnesses act as agents acquiring the lien on the lender's behalf
C. Because the debtor's verbal admission is sufficient
D. Because the Torah requires witnesses for all transactions
Correct: B — Abaye holds that the document's witnesses, with their signatures, acquire the lender's lien on the borrower's land on the lender's behalf — extending this even to non-deed-of-transfer notes.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the 'Nisan-Tishrei problem' that prevents returning found notes with a property guarantee?
A. The note may have been written in Nisan but the loan occurred in Tishrei, creating a false earlier lien date
B. The interest rate changed between Nisan and Tishrei
C. The debtor moved to a different jurisdiction between those months
D. The witnesses may have died between Nisan and Tishrei
Correct: A — If the note is dated Nisan but the loan actually happened in Tishrei, the creditor could unlawfully seize land sold between those dates — the lien appears to predate the sale when it actually doesn't.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to the confirmed view (R' Yochanan), when does the dispute between R' Meir and the Rabbis apply?
A. When the debtor denies the debt
B. When the debtor admits the debt
C. When neither party is present in court
D. When the note was found by a non-Jew
Correct: B — R' Yochanan says the dispute is when the debtor admits. R' Meir allows returning notes without achrayut (they only collect from unsold property), while the Rabbis forbid it (they collect from liened property too). The baraita confirms this reading.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is 'knuniya' (קנוניא) and why does it matter for found promissory notes?
A. A type of lien that attaches only to movable property
B. Collusion between debtor and creditor to defraud land purchasers
C. A special court procedure for validating old documents
D. A form of interest prohibited by Torah law
Correct: B — Knuniya is collusion: after repaying, the debtor pretends he still owes, enabling the creditor to seize land from innocent purchasers — they then split the proceeds. This concern prevents returning found notes with achrayut.
