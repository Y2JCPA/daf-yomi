Found Promissory Notes: Return or Not?

Someone finds a promissory note (שטר)
↓
Does it include a property guarantee (אחריות)?
Yes — Achrayut
Do NOT return to creditor — concern of unlawful repossession from purchasers or collusion
No Achrayut
Dispute: R' Meir says return if debtor admits; Rabbis say do not return
Core Issue A found document with a property guarantee could allow a creditor to seize land from innocent buyers — either through backdating or collusion with the debtor.

Writing a Note Without the Lender Present

Rav Asi
A promissory note may be written for the borrower without the lender present only for deeds of transfer (akarata) — the debtor obligates his property from the date of writing regardless of when the loan occurs.
Abaye
The document's witnesses acquire the lien on the lender's behalf with their signatures — this applies even to ordinary promissory notes, not just deeds of transfer.
Rav Asi
For ordinary notes, we don't write them without both parties. But if a note is found dropped, its credibility is compromised — perhaps it was written improperly without the lender.
Abaye
That reasoning is weak — if the rule is that we never write such notes without the lender, why suspect it happened? The witnesses acquire the lien automatically.
Key Distinction Rav Asi limits writing in the lender's absence to deeds of transfer; Abaye extends it to all promissory notes through the mechanism of witness-based acquisition.

Abaye's Witness Acquisition: When Does It Work?

Witnesses sign a document (שטר)
Lien acquired on lender's behalf?
↓
Did the document reach the creditor's hands?
Yes — Delivered
✅ Witnesses' signatures acquire the lien on the lender's behalf
No — Never Delivered
❌ Acquisition does not take effect — document may reflect reconsidered intent
Proof The Mishna (18a) rules that found bills of divorce, wills, and gift deeds should NOT be returned — because the writer may have reconsidered. If witness signatures always acquired, these should be returned. Therefore, acquisition requires delivery.

The Nisan-Tishrei Problem: Backdating Risk

The Scenario

  • Borrower writes note dated Nisan
  • Actual loan occurs in Tishrei
  • Between Nisan and Tishrei, borrower sold land
  • Note makes it look like lien predates the sale

The Danger

  • Creditor uses note to seize land from purchaser
  • Purchaser bought BEFORE the actual loan
  • But note's date makes it appear AFTER
  • Purchaser loses land unjustly

The Ruling

  • Found notes with achrayut: do NOT return
  • Even if debtor admits the debt
  • Date on note may not reflect actual loan date
  • Protects innocent third-party purchasers
אחריות נכסים A property guarantee (achrayut) means the creditor can seize liened property from subsequent purchasers — making the date on the note critically important.

R' Elazar vs. R' Yochanan: When Is the Dispute?

R' Elazar
The Mishna's dispute is when the debtor does NOT admit. R' Meir says a note without achrayut can't collect from anything; Rabbis say it collects from unsold property. But if the debtor admits — everyone agrees: return the note. We don't suspect collusion (קנוניא).
R' Yochanan
The dispute is when the debtor admits. R' Meir says without achrayut, collect only from unsold property; Rabbis say from liened property too. But if debtor denies — everyone agrees: don't return, as we suspect repayment.
R' Elazar
We do NOT suspect collusion — if the debtor says he owes money, we believe him.
R' Yochanan
We DO suspect collusion — a debtor might pretend to owe money to help the creditor seize land from purchasers, and they split the profits.
קנוניא Collusion (knuniya): The debtor repays, then claims he still owes — the creditor seizes land sold during the loan period, and they divide the proceeds.
