⚖️ Core Machloket: Enhancement of Sycamore Tree

Abaye
Although the sharecropper cannot take the sycamore beams, he does receive the value of the tree's enhancement — the growth that occurred during his tenure.
Rava
The sharecropper receives neither the beams nor the enhancement value. He contracted to cultivate land, not to acquire rights in permanent fixtures.
Practical Test Case When Rav Pappa claimed enhancement value for palm trees that sprouted during his hay-growing tenancy, the owner countered: 'I only caused you to lose a handful of hay — take it and go.' Rav Pappa replied that he had grown saffron there, claiming a higher standard of cultivation.

🌾 Sycamore Tree: Beams vs. Enhancement Value

Abaye
Beams: No — belong to field owner.
Enhancement value: Yes — the sharecropper invested labor; the tree's growth during his tenure belongs to him in value.
Rava
Beams: No.
Enhancement value: Also no — the sharecropper never acquired any interest in permanent trees. The owner gets all incidental growth.
Mishna Background (109a) A sharecropper holding a field for fewer than 7 years may not plant flax (weakens the soil), and has no rights to beams fashioned from branches of a sycamore tree already growing there.

💸 Voluntary Departure vs. Removal for Cause

Planter Leaves Voluntarily

  • Wants to ascend to Eretz Yisrael
  • Owner must compensate for enhancement
  • Rav Pappa bar Shmuel: pay 'one-quarter' of enhancement
  • Rav Ashi clarifies: one-quarter of owner's ⅔ share = ⅙ of total
  • Planter's portion: ½; owner's: ⅔ of remainder — complex formula prevents double loss
  • Alternative (Rav Aḥa): planter keeps his share; owner assigns his portion to new sharecropper

Planter Removed for Causing Damage

  • Stipulated: 'If I ruin the vineyard I will leave'
  • He caused loss but some enhancement remains
  • Rava: stipulation forfeits enhancement rights
  • Analogous to Mishna 104a: one who lets field lie fallow pays best-quality land
  • Runya's case: Ravina removed him; Rava upheld removal
  • Professionals (doctor, barber, town scribe) also forewarned — losses deducted from wages

🏡 Mortgaged Orchard That Ages Before Term

Setup: Debtor mortgages orchard to creditor for 10 years; profits repay the debt
↓
Crisis: After only 5 years, the orchard naturally ages and ceases to produce fruit
↓
Rava's ruling: The creditor suffers the loss. Natural aging is his misfortune (ones). He cannot extend the mortgage to recoup lost years.
↓
Baraita challenge: If a mortgaged tree dries up or is chopped down, neither party may take the wood — sell it and plant a new tree
↓
Resolution: The baraita refers to a tree chopped down or dried up before its time (abnormal). Natural aging mid-mortgage differs — the creditor absorbed that risk.
