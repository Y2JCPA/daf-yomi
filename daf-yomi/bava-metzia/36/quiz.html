QUESTION 1 OF 5
When a borrower takes a false oath that the cow died from labor (when it actually died naturally), why does he bring a guilt-offering rather than a sin-offering?
A. Because his oath denies a monetary obligation — he is liable for natural death but claims exemption
B. Because borrowers always bring guilt-offerings for false oaths
C. Because the court administered the oath
D. Because the cow was worth more than five shekalim
Correct: A — A guilt-offering (asham) is brought for a false oath that denies a monetary matter. Since a borrower is liable when the cow dies naturally but claims it died from labor (which would exempt him), his oath involves denial of a monetary debt.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rav, why is a paid bailee who transfers a deposit to an unpaid bailee exempt from liability?
A. Because the unpaid bailee accepted full responsibility
B. Because he transferred the deposit to a mentally competent person
C. Because the owner gave implicit permission to transfer
D. Because the Torah only obligates the original bailee for theft
Correct: B — Rav's reasoning is that transferring to any mentally competent person (bar da'at) constitutes adequate safeguarding, regardless of whether the new bailee has a higher or lower level of obligation.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What additional reason does Rava give (beyond R' Yochanan's reasoning) for why a bailee who transfers is liable?
A. The second bailee is presumed to be negligent
B. The owner can say: 'You are trustworthy to me under oath; that person is not'
C. Transferring violates a rabbinic decree
D. The first bailee becomes a guarantor for the second
Correct: B — Rava adds the dimension of oath-trustworthiness — the owner trusted the original bailee's oath specifically. Even if the second bailee provides better physical protection, the personal element of oath credibility cannot be transferred.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In the marsh dispute, why does Rava concede liability when the animal is stolen from the marsh and then dies naturally at the thief's house?
A. Because theft is always the bailee's fault
B. Because even if the Angel of Death would have killed it anyway, the animal is now wrongfully in the thief's possession due to the bailee's negligence
C. Because the thief's house is more dangerous than the marsh
D. Because Rava holds techilato b'peshia v'sofo b'ones is always liable
Correct: B — Even though the animal died naturally, the negligence placed it in a situation where it was stolen. The owner suffers a concrete loss — the animal is now with a thief — regardless of how it died. The 'Angel of Death' argument doesn't help when there's also theft involved.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What does R' Yirmeya's systematic presentation of oath cases come to exclude?
A. The opinion that borrowers are exempt from oaths entirely
B. R' Ami's opinion that court-administered oaths don't incur a sin-offering for false utterance
C. The opinion that renters and borrowers have the same oath obligations
D. The opinion that only guilt-offerings apply to bailment oaths
Correct: B — R' Ami derives from Leviticus 5:4 that only self-initiated oaths (not court-administered ones) incur sin-offering liability. R' Yirmeya's comprehensive cases demonstrate that sin-offerings apply even to court-administered oaths, rejecting R' Ami's position.
