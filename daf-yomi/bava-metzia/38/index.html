Deposited Produce: To Sell or Not to Sell?

First Tanna
If one deposits produce with another, even if it is spoiling, the bailee may not touch it.
Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel
He sells it before the court — doing so is like returning a lost item to the owner.
Rav Kahana
The reason not to sell: A person prefers a kav of his own to nine kav of another's.
Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak
The reason not to sell: Perhaps the owner rendered it teruma and tithe for produce elsewhere.
Core Question When a bailee holds spoiling produce, does respect for the owner's autonomy outweigh the economic logic of selling it?

The Mishna's Dispute: Spoiling Deposits

First Tanna (Rabbis)

  • Bailee may NOT touch deposited produce
  • Even if it is being lost to spoilage or vermin
  • Owner's autonomy over his property is paramount
  • The deposit belongs to the owner — hands off

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel

  • Bailee SELLS it before the court (beit din)
  • This is like returning a lost item (hashavat aveida)
  • Preventing total loss serves the owner's interest
  • Practical outcome trumps theoretical ownership
Halacha R' Yochanan rules like Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. Rav Nachman rules like the Rabbis.

Why Can't the Bailee Sell? Two Reasons

Rav Kahana
A person prefers a kav of his own produce to nine kav of another's. The owner would rather have his own diminished produce than money from a sale.
Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak
We are concerned the owner may have rendered this produce teruma and ma'aser for produce in another location. Selling it would cause the buyer to eat teruma improperly.
Gemara
A baraita says: the bailee may not touch it, and therefore the owner may render it teruma. This word 'therefore' supports Rav Kahana — the teruma right is a result, not the reason.
Rav Nachman b"Y responds
Read it: Now that the Sages said he may not sell (because of teruma concern), the owner can be confident the produce is still there — therefore he may designate it teruma even ab initio.
Key Distinction For Rav Kahana, the teruma right flows FROM the rule. For Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, the teruma concern IS the rule.

Standard vs. Accelerated Deterioration

Produce deposited with a bailee begins to deteriorate
↓
At what rate is it deteriorating?
Standard Rate
Dispute applies:
Rabbis say don't sell;
RSBG says sell in court
Accelerated Rate
All agree:
Bailee sells before the court
(R' Yochanan's ruling)
↓
If sold due to accelerated deterioration — sell to kohanim at teruma price to address concern owner designated it teruma
Principle Accelerated deterioration is uncommon (לא שכיח), so the Sages did not issue decrees for uncommon cases — but when it happens, all agree to sell.

Property of a Captive: Does Court Authorize a Relative?

A person is taken captive — who manages their property?
Heard captive died
All agree: Court authorizes the relative (heir apparent) to manage the property
Captive presumably alive
Dispute between Rav and Shmuel
↓
Does the court authorize the relative?
Rav: No
Relative may devalue the property, assuming the owner will return and reclaim it
Shmuel: Yes
Court authorizes relative to manage it — rules like RSBG on deposits AND independently on captive's property
Key Conclusion The Gemara concludes that the dispute about deposits and the dispute about a captive's property are based on TWO UNRELATED reasons — proven from Rav Nachman who rules like the Rabbis on deposits but still authorizes a relative for a captive.
