QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava's ruling, what happens when the price discrepancy is less than one-sixth?
A. The transaction is nullified
B. The exploited party receives the difference
C. The transaction is finalized immediately with no claim
D. The buyer has until he shows a merchant to decide
Correct: C — Rava rules that a discrepancy of less than one-sixth is waived (mechilah) and the transaction stands immediately — no claim is possible.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why did the merchants of Lod initially rejoice at Rabbi Tarfon's ruling?
A. He eliminated all exploitation claims entirely
B. His higher threshold (1/3) meant fewer transactions qualified as exploitation
C. He gave merchants the right to set any price
D. He ruled that only sellers could claim exploitation
Correct: B — Rabbi Tarfon's threshold of one-third meant that discrepancies between 1/6 and 1/3 — which the Rabbis considered exploitation — would be merely waived under his system.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why did the merchants of Lod ultimately revert to following the Rabbis?
A. Rabbi Tarfon's threshold was too low
B. Rabbi Tarfon allowed claims for the entire day, creating more uncertainty
C. The Rabbis offered a higher threshold
D. Rabbi Tarfon required all merchants to give refunds
Correct: B — Although Rabbi Tarfon's higher threshold reduced the number of exploitation claims, his full-day return window created far more business uncertainty than the Rabbis' shorter window.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the key difference between a discrepancy of exactly one-sixth and greater than one-sixth?
A. At 1/6 only the exploited party can renege; at >1/6 either party can renege
B. At 1/6 the sale is void; at >1/6 the difference is returned
C. There is no difference — both result in nullification
D. At 1/6 there is no time limit; at >1/6 there is a time limit
Correct: A — At exactly one-sixth, the transaction stands and the exploited party can demand the difference. At greater than one-sixth, either party — buyer or seller — can demand nullification of the entire transaction.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, when the seller is exploited, what advantage does the seller have?
A. He automatically receives double the difference
B. He can choose: reclaim his merchandise OR demand the price difference
C. He has unlimited time to make a claim
D. He can set a new price for the item
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi rules that the exploited seller has the upper hand (yado al ha-elyonah): he can either nullify the sale and get his merchandise back, or keep the sale and demand the exploiter return the difference.
