When Does Mi Shepara Apply?

A buyer and seller agree to a transaction
↓
Was money paid?
Yes — money paid
Reneging triggers Mi Shepara curse: He Who exacted payment from the generation of the flood…
No — verbal commitment only
Reneging means the Sages are displeased but no curse applies (Rava)
Key Principle Payment of money without meshikha (pulling) does not complete acquisition of movable property — but reneging after payment invokes a spiritual sanction.

Commitment vs. Curse: Rava's Distinction

R' Shimon
The Sages said about one who reneges after pulling a gold dinar: Mi Shepara — He Who exacted payment from the generation of the flood will exact payment from him.
Rava
One who reneges on a verbal commitment alone — the Sages are merely displeased with him, not subject to the curse.
Rava
But if there is a statement + money, then he stands subject to: He Who exacted payment…
Gemara
Verbal commitment alone → displeased. Verbal commitment + money → Mi Shepara curse.
Distinction The severity of the sanction depends on whether money accompanied the verbal commitment.

Reish Lakish's Proof from Leviticus

Leviticus 5:21 lists: deposit, pledge, robbery, oppression
↓
Leviticus 5:23 repeats: robbery, oppression, deposit — but omits pledge
↓
Why is pledge omitted?
Reish Lakish's Answer
Because the lender never pulled (meshikha) the pledged item — so he never truly acquired it. This proves money alone doesn't acquire movable property by Torah law.
Rav Pappa's Challenge
Maybe 'oppression' (wages) also lacks pulling — so the omission of 'pledge' proves nothing extra?
Resolution The Gemara explains 'oppression' refers to where workers took an item and then deposited it — so there was meshikha. The omission of 'pledge' still supports Reish Lakish.

Abaye vs. Rava: How Is Mi Shepara Applied?

Abaye — We Inform

  • We tell the reneging party about the punishment
  • Based on: "Nor curse a ruler among your people" (Exodus 22:27)
  • Cursing a fellow Jew is prohibited
  • Mi Shepara is a warning, not an active curse

Rava — We Curse

  • We actively pronounce the Mi Shepara curse upon him
  • "Among your people" — one who acts properly
  • One who reneges is NOT acting befitting 'your people'
  • Therefore the prohibition against cursing doesn't apply
Practical Difference Does the Beit Din merely notify the reneging party of divine consequences, or does it pronounce a curse upon him?

R' Ḥiyya bar Yosef and the Salt

DetailWhat Happened
TransactionBuyers gave R' Ḥiyya bar Yosef money for salt
ProblemSalt prices rose; R' Ḥiyya wanted to renege
RulingR' Yoḥanan told him: give the salt, or accept Mi Shepara
Rava's proofIf we only 'inform' — R' Ḥiyya already knew the halakha!
Gemara's rebuttalWould R' Ḥiyya accept a curse upon himself? Unlikely.
ResolutionIt was actually a down payment (arbon) — the dispute was how much it acquires
Reframed R' Ḥiyya held the down payment acquires salt commensurate with its value; R' Yoḥanan held it acquires the entire transaction.

Down Payment (Arbon): Rav vs. R' Yoḥanan

Rav
A down payment acquires merchandise commensurate with its value only.
R' Yoḥanan
A down payment acquires merchandise commensurate with the entire transaction.
R' Yehuda
It is sufficient that the down payment acquires commensurate with its amount. (Supports Rav)
R' Shimon b. Gamliel
If he paid 500 of 1000 dinars for land, he acquires the entire property and pays the rest later. (Supports R' Yoḥanan)
Note R' Yosei holds that even forfeiture/doubling conditions on a down payment are binding — because asmakhta (conditional commitment) effects acquisition.
