⚖️ The Core Dilemma: Whose Food Is It?

View A
The laborer eats from his own property — the Torah grants him early access to wages in the form of food, deducted from what he is owed.
View B
The laborer eats from the property of Heaven — the Torah grants him a gift entirely separate from and in addition to his wages.
Why It Matters If he eats from his OWN property, he can redirect the produce to his wife and children. If he eats from HEAVEN's property, the right is personal and non-transferable — the Gemara cannot resolve this dilemma.

📜 Isi ben Yehuda vs. Rav: Who May Eat From a Vineyard?

Isi ben Yehuda
By Torah law, even one who merely passes by a vineyard — without being hired — is permitted to eat from its grapes. The verse grants this broadly.
Rav
Isi has left no livelihood for vineyard owners! Countless passers-by would devour all the fruit. The eating right belongs only to hired workers.
Rav Ashi's Qualification Perhaps Isi meant only those who voluntarily enter a vineyard to perform labor in exchange for eating — not random passersby with no work obligation whatsoever.

🌿 Rav Asi's Two Rulings on When Eating Rights Activate

Question: How much labor triggers the right to eat?
↓
Ruling 1: Scope of Hire
Even if hired only to harvest one cluster of grapes, the laborer may eat while working.
Ruling 2: Actual Output
Even if he only actually harvested one cluster, he may eat from the produce.
↓
Why state both? Ruling 1: no other food available in field. Ruling 2: task is completable — can eat and still fill owner's vessel. Each case might have been ruled differently without the other teaching.

⚖️ Consequences: Own Property vs. Heaven's Property

Eats from OWN Property

  • Food is deducted from his wages
  • He CAN give produce to wife & children
  • Son eating = like worker eating; no tithe issue
  • He CAN stipulate higher wages in lieu of eating
  • Right is transferable to dependents

Eats from HEAVEN's Property

  • Food is a Torah gift beyond wages
  • He CANNOT give produce to wife & children
  • Son eating triggers tithe obligation separately
  • Stipulation waives a divine personal entitlement
  • Right is strictly personal and non-transferable

💰 Stipulation Rules: Who Can Trade Eating Rights for Higher Wages?

A worker may stipulate on his own behalf: 'I will not eat produce; give me higher wages instead.' — universally permitted.
↓
He may stipulate on his wife's behalf — since he provides her food and benefits from her labor.
↓
He may stipulate on behalf of adult Canaanite slaves per the mishna — since he provides their sustenance.
↓
Dispute: May he stipulate on behalf of his minor children and Canaanite slaves? The mishna permits it; Rabbi Hoshaya's baraita forbids it for slaves.
↓
Root of dispute: If laborer eats from his own property → stipulation is valid for dependents. If from Heaven's property → only works where he provides their food directly.
