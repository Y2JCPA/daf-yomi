How Does a Bailee Acquire the Double Payment?

Owner deposits item with bailee (shomer)
↓
Item is stolen from bailee
↓
Does bailee choose to pay rather than swear?
Yes — pays/chooses to pay
Bailee acquires rights to double payment (kefel) from the thief
No — takes an oath
Owner retains rights to the double payment
Core Question The Gemara explores the mechanism by which ownership transfers to the bailee and what level of commitment to pay is required.

How Is Ownership Transferred to the Bailee?

Challenge
How can the bailee acquire the double payment? At the time of deposit, who knows if the item will be stolen? If stolen, who knows the thief will be found? And perhaps the thief will confess and be exempt from kefel!
Rava
It is as though the owner said: When it is stolen and you choose to pay rather than swear — ownership of my cow is retroactively transferred to you from the time of deposit. The cow already exists in the world, so the transfer is valid.
R' Zeira
Objects! If ownership transfers from the time of deposit, the bailee should also get the fleece and offspring (gizah v'valadot)! But a baraita says he does not get those!
R' Zeira
Rather, the owner says: My animal is transferred to you from this time except for its fleece and offspring.
Key Distinction Profit from elsewhere (double payment from a thief) — a person is apt to transfer. Profit from the body of the animal (fleece, offspring) — a person is not apt to transfer.

Rava's Two Formulations of Transfer

Version 1: From Time of Deposit

  • Ownership transfers retroactively from the moment of deposit
  • R' Zeira's objection about fleece/offspring IS relevant
  • Must carve out exception for fleece & offspring
  • Problem if animal is in a marsh (agam) — not on bailee's property

Version 2: Adjacent to Theft

  • Ownership transfers immediately before the moment of theft
  • R' Zeira's objection does NOT apply — no time for fleece/offspring to accrue
  • Simpler mechanism, fewer exceptions needed
  • Still problematic if animal is in a marsh at time of theft
Nafka Mina The practical difference: (1) fleece & offspring during the bailment period, and (2) the marsh scenario where the animal isn't on bailee's property at the moment of theft.

Does 'I Choose to Pay' Equal Actual Payment?

R' Yochanan
When the mishna says if the bailee paid, it does not mean he actually paid. Once he says 'I choose to pay,' he acquires the double payment even before paying!
Objection
The mishna says 'if the bailee paid' — implying actual payment is required. If he didn't pay, he doesn't acquire!
Resolution
The latter clause implies: the reason the owner gets the kefel is that the bailee did not wish to pay. But if he wished to pay — even without actually paying — he acquires!
Gemara
The inferences from the two clauses contradict each other — so no inference can be drawn from this mishna.
Baraita
A renter who said 'I choose to pay and will not swear,' and then the thief was found — the thief pays the renter the kefel. This supports R' Yochanan.
Principle A baraita supports R' Yochanan: the mere declaration of intent to pay is sufficient to acquire the double payment.

Rav Pappa: When Does Each Bailee Acquire the Kefel?

Type of BaileeStatement That AcquiresReasoning
Shomer Chinam (unpaid)"I was negligent" (pashati)He could have claimed theft to exempt himself — admitting negligence = choosing to pay
Shomer Sakhar (paid)"It was stolen" (nignevah)He could have claimed ones (accident) to exempt himself — admitting theft = choosing to pay
Sho'el (borrower) — Version 1"I choose to pay" is NOT effectiveHe's liable regardless! His statement adds nothing, so it cannot confer rights
Sho'el (borrower) — Version 2"I choose to pay" IS effectiveHe could claim meisah machmas melakhah (died from normal use) to exempt himself
Dispute Rav Zevid cites Abaye: A borrower acquires the kefel only when he actually pays, because all benefit of the loan accrues to him — mere words are insufficient.
