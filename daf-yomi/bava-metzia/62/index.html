Bava Metzia 62 — Your Brother Shall Live With You

וחי אחיך עמך (ויקרא כה:לו)
CORE THEME This daf explores the verse "וחי אחיך עמך" — "And your brother shall live with you" (Lev. 25:36). R' Yoḥanan applies it to the famous two-travelers-in-the-desert dilemma, while others derive from it the obligation to return interest. The daf then examines when collected interest must be returned, children's duty to return a father's stolen property, and complex commodity transactions that may constitute disguised interest.

Two Travelers in the Desert

Setup
Two people walk in a desert. One has a kiton (jug) of water. If both drink — both die. If one drinks — he reaches civilization.
Ben Petora
It is preferable that both drink and die, and let neither see the death of the other.
R' Akiva
The verse says "וחי אחיך עמך" — your life takes precedence over the life of the other.
HALAKHA R' Akiva's view became the accepted ruling: the owner of the water drinks and survives. This is R' Yoḥanan's use of the verse.

Must a Lender Return Collected Interest?

A lender collected interest (ribbit) from a borrower
↓
Is the lender obligated to return the interest?
First Tanna
No obligation to return collected interest
R' Neḥemya & R' Eliezer b. Ya'akov
Must return it — this is a מצוה of "arise and take action" (קום ועשה)
KEY DISPUTE Because a corrective mitzvah exists (returning the interest), R' Neḥemya and R' Eliezer b. Ya'akov hold the lender and guarantor are exempt from lashes — the transgression is rectifiable.

Rav Safra's Principle: Interest by Gentile vs. Jewish Law

Fixed Interest (Ribbit Ketzutzah)

  • By gentile law: lender can collect from borrower
  • By our law: lender must return to borrower
  • Follows the opinion of R' Elazar

Prior & Subsequent Interest

  • Prior interest: gift before loan to induce lending
  • Subsequent interest: gift after repayment as thanks
  • Prohibited, but lender not compelled to return
  • By gentile law: not enforceable either
RAV ASHI Gentiles view the field transaction as a sale, not a loan — the field is temporarily sold to the lender and bought back when the debt is repaid.

The Wheat-Wine Transaction (Mishna)

🌾 Buyer acquires wheat at 1 kor = 25 dinars (current market price), to be delivered later
↓
📈 Wheat price rises to 30 dinars per kor
↓
🗣️ Buyer says: "Give me my wheat now" — wants to sell it and buy wine
↓
🍷 Seller says: "Your wheat = 30 dinars; collect wine from me instead"
↓
⚠️ Seller has no wine — this becomes a debt, not a sale = prohibited interest!
CRITICAL FACTOR If the seller had wine, it could be a valid exchange. Without wine in hand, the promise to deliver later at the higher value constitutes a disguised loan with interest.
