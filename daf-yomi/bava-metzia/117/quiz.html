Bava Metzia 117 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yosei, why does the lower-story resident bear responsibility for the plaster (מעזיבה)?
A. The plaster belongs to the upper resident since it is his floor
B. The plaster presses down on and burdens the ceiling structure below
C. The plaster strengthens the ceiling and thus benefits the lower resident
D. The Rabbis and Rabbi Yosei actually agree about plaster
Correct: B — Rabbi Yosei holds that the plaster is a weight pressing down on the ceiling, making it the lower resident's problem — it burdens his structure. The Rabbis disagree, holding that plaster strengthens the ceiling and so is the upper resident's obligation.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Rabbi Yoḥanan says Rabbi Yehuda's principle — that one may not benefit from another's property without consent — appears in three places. Which of these is NOT one of them?
A. Upper story resident temporarily occupying the lower story
B. A dyer who changes the color of wool
C. A creditor who takes a borrower's cloak as collateral overnight
D. A trustee who holds a partial-repayment promissory note
Correct: C — The three cases are: (1) upper resident in lower story, (2) the dyer case, and (3) the promissory note/trustee case. A creditor taking a cloak as collateral is not among Rabbi Yehuda's three instances cited here.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
When the lower-story resident wants to rebuild using different materials, in which case does the court NOT listen to him?
A. He wants to switch from bricks to girders
B. He wants to use untrimmed stones instead of hewn stones
C. He wants to reduce the number of windows
D. He wants to heighten the building
Correct: D — Heightening the building burdens the upper story resident, so the court does not listen to the lower resident's request. Switching to girders, using untrimmed stones, and reducing windows are all permitted as they strengthen or do not burden the shared structure.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
If the house and upper story collapsed and neither party has funds to rebuild, how does Rabbi Natan rule on dividing the land?
A. Each party takes half equally
B. Lower resident takes two-thirds; upper resident takes one-third
C. Lower resident takes three-quarters; upper resident takes one-quarter
D. The land is sold and proceeds split equally after court costs
Correct: B — Rabbi Natan rules that the lower resident takes two-thirds of the land and the upper resident takes one-third, reflecting the greater structural importance of the lower story. 'Others' go even further, giving the lower resident three-quarters.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Regarding a wall or tree that falls into the public domain and causes damage, when is the owner liable?
A. Always, since owners are responsible for their property
B. Only if the wall was built less than ten handbreadths high
C. Only after the court warned him the structure was unstable and he failed to repair it in time
D. Only if the wall fell during daytime hours
Correct: C — The mishna rules that if the wall or tree fell on its own it is an accident and the owner is exempt. However, if the court officially warned the owner that the wall was shaky or the tree was tilting — and gave him time to remove it — then his subsequent failure to act makes him liable for any resulting damages.
