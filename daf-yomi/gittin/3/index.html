Why Must the Agent Testify? — The Ongoing Debate

Rabba
The agent must say "written and signed in my presence" because a get must be written lishmah (for her sake), and overseas witnesses can't confirm this.
Rava
The declaration is needed because witnesses are unavailable overseas to ratify the document, so the agent serves as the ratifier.
Gemara
Each position is challenged: Is this really a leniency? With two witnesses the husband can't contest the get — with one agent, he can!
Gemara
Answer: The get must be delivered before 2 or 3 people (R' Yochanan / R' Chanina), so the agent will be careful not to deliver an invalid get.
Key Tension Both Rabba and Rava must explain why a single agent suffices when normally two witnesses are required — and why this is truly a leniency to prevent iguna (desertion).

Rava's Challenge to Rabba's Reason

Rava
If lishmah is the reason, the mishna should require the agent to say: "written in my presence for her sake" — but it doesn't!
Rabba
The Sages deliberately omitted 'for her sake' — if you increase the required formula, the agent may forget parts of it.
Gemara
But even now he might shorten the current formula by dropping one of the two clauses!
Rabba
A person may omit one of three terms, but will not omit one of two. The current two-part formula is safe from shortening.
Practical Insight The Sages calibrated the formula's length to balance completeness against the risk of human error.

Is the Single-Agent Rule a Leniency or Stringency?

Agent brings get with one witness (himself)
↓
Is this a leniency for the woman?
Challenge
With two witnesses, husband cannot contest the get — stronger protection!
Answer
Delivery before 2–3 people ensures agent is careful — effectively equivalent protection
Resolution The public delivery requirement compensates for having only one witness, so the rule remains a leniency preventing iguna.

Rabba vs. Rava — Why Must the Agent Testify?

Rabba: Lishmah Concern

  • A get must be written for her sake (lishmah)
  • Overseas scribes may not know this requirement
  • Agent confirms proper intent behind the writing
  • Declaration covers both writing and signing

Rava: Ratification Concern

  • Witnesses overseas are unavailable to ratify
  • Normally ratification requires two witnesses
  • One witness is credible for prohibitions (issur)
  • Reish Lakish: signed witnesses = examined testimony
Common Ground Both agree the rule was enacted due to the possibility of iguna — to prevent women from becoming 'deserted wives' with uncertain marital status.

Rabbi Meir vs. Rabbi Elazar on Lishmah

📜 Mishna (21b): Get written on something attached to the ground — if detached, signed, and given — valid
↓
✍️ Rabbi Meir: The essential act of 'writing' a get = the signing by witnesses → signing must be lishmah
↓
👁️ Rabbi Elazar: Witnesses sign only for tikkun ha-olam (betterment of the world) — signing is not essential
↓
📋 Rabbi Elazar: Get given before two witnesses without signatures is valid — she can even collect from liened property
↓
❓ Problem: Neither opinion requires both writing and signing lishmah — so whose view does the mishna follow?
The Dilemma R' Meir requires signing lishmah but not writing; R' Elazar requires writing lishmah but not signing. The mishna's declaration covers both — fitting neither perfectly.
