Gittin 86: The Three Invalid Bills of Divorce

CORE MISHNA Three types of gittin are invalid ab initio but if the woman remarries, the offspring's lineage is unflawed: (1) husband's handwriting with no witness signatures, (2) witness signatures but no date, (3) a date but only one witness. The Gemara analyzes why specifically these three — and not other invalid gittin — are listed, and debates whether the remarried woman must leave her second husband.

KEY RULING All three are invalid ab initio — she may not remarry with them. But if she does remarry, the offspring's lineage is unflawed (ha-vlad kasher).

R' Eliezer vs. the Sages on Witnesses

Sages
A get requires witness signatures on the document to be valid.
R' Eliezer
Even with no signatures on the document, if he hands it to her before two witnesses, it is a valid get!
R' Eliezer
She can even collect from liened property (meshu'abadim) with such a get.
R' Eliezer
Witnesses sign only for tikkun ha-olam — betterment of the world — not as an essential requirement.
PRINCIPLE R' Eliezer holds: עדי מסירה כרתי — the witnesses to delivery effect the divorce, not the witnesses who sign.

Why Only These Three? — Gemara's Elimination

Why not list an outdated get (get yashan)?
↓
An outdated get — she may remarry ab initio; these three — only after the fact
Why not list a bare get (missing a signature on a fold)?
↓
Per R' Meir: a bare get makes offspring a mamzer; these three do not
Why not list a get with wrong kingdom dating?
↓
There she must leave her husband; here (per one view) she need not leave
DISTINCTION The mishna lists specifically those gittin that are invalid yet produce unflawed offspring — each excluded case fails this criterion in some way.

Whose Handwriting? Rav vs. Shmuel

Rav
The mishna's case of 'his handwriting + one witness' means the husband wrote it himself. Only then is the offspring's lineage unflawed.
Shmuel
Even if a scribe wrote it and one witness signed, the offspring's lineage is still unflawed.
Rav
The later mishna (87b) about recognizable scribe handwriting being valid must mean the scribe also signed as a witness — that's ab initio.
Shmuel
No — 87b refers to an expert scribe (sofer mumḥeh); our mishna refers to a non-expert scribe. Both are scribe-written.
R' Yoḥanan
I agree with Rav — the mishna means his (the husband's) handwriting.
R' ELAZAR'S QUESTION R' Elazar challenged R' Yoḥanan: if witnesses signed, why does handwriting matter? R' Yoḥanan clarified he was referring to the latter clause (only one witness).

Must She Leave Her Second Husband?

Rav's Position

  • If she has children from second husband → need not leave (to avoid slander of mamzerut)
  • If she has no children → she must leave
  • The invalidity is only rabbinic — by Torah law, the get is valid

Levi & R' Yoḥanan

  • She never needs to leave her second husband
  • Whether or not she has children — she stays
  • R' Yoḥanan told the sons of R' Ḥalafta: your father held this view too
OBJECTION TO RAV Mar Zutra bar Toviya challenged Rav from a mishna in Yevamot: these three gittin create 'uncertain divorce' status for levirate law — implying the woman is validly divorced enough to stay with her second husband.
