Chezkat Chaim — When Do We Presume Someone Is Alive?

Core Principle: A person is presumed alive unless evidence suggests otherwise
↓
Agent brings a get, husband was elderly or sick
↓
Presumed Alive
Deliver the get — it is valid
Uncertain
Moribund or conditional get — complications arise
Key Concept The Gemara explores how far the presumption of life (חזקת חיים) extends across divorce, teruma, and sacrificial law.

Mishna: Three Cases of Chezkat Chaim

📜 Agent brings a get — husband was elderly or sick → deliver it, he is presumed alive
↓
🍞 Wife of a kohen whose husband went overseas → she may continue eating teruma
↓
🐦 One who sends a sin-offering from overseas → priests may sacrifice it on the altar
↓
✅ In all three cases we rely on the presumption that the person is still alive
Why All Three? Each case teaches a unique chiddush — get (necessity), teruma (she could eat chullin), sin-offering (don't bring chullin to the azara).

Rava vs. Abaye: Does Age Limit Chezkat Chaim?

Rava
The mishna's presumption applies only to one who has not reached 80 (שנות הגבורות) and to an ordinary sick person. A moribund person or one past 80 — no presumption.
Abaye
A baraita states: even if the husband is 100 years old, the agent delivers the get based on the presumption he is alive!
Gemara
This is a conclusive refutation (תיובתא) of Rava.
Gemara
Or perhaps: one who reached 100 has proven exceptional longevity. But an ordinary person reaching 80 — there is concern.
Outcome Rava's position is challenged but not definitively rejected — the Gemara offers a possible defense distinguishing exceptional longevity from ordinary aging.

The Teruma Contradiction

Mishna — She May Eat

  • Husband went overseas
  • Wife of a kohen continues eating teruma
  • We presume he is still alive
  • No concern that he has already died

Baraita — She May Not Eat

  • Husband says: 'This get takes effect one hour before my death'
  • She is immediately forbidden from teruma
  • Concern that he will die at an unknown future time
  • The prohibition is certain to arrive eventually
Resolution (Abaye) We are NOT concerned that perhaps he has already died. But we ARE concerned that perhaps he will die — these are two different types of uncertainty.

Resolving the Teruma Contradiction

Has the husband already died?
↓
No Concern
We do not worry he has already died → she eats teruma (Mishna)
Future Concern
We do worry he will eventually die → conditional get creates immediate prohibition (Baraita)
↓
Rava's conclusion: Everyone agrees we don't worry about past death. Whether we worry about future death — this is a tannaitic dispute
Key Distinction שמא מת (perhaps he already died) vs. שמא ימות (perhaps he will die) — two fundamentally different concerns.

R' Elazar ben Perata's Three Testimonies

SituationStill in DangerOutcome Determined
🏰 Town under siegePresumed aliveStringencies of living AND dead
🚢 Ship tossed at seaPresumed aliveStringencies of living AND dead
⚖️ Going to capital trialPresumed aliveStringencies of living AND dead
Practical Effect When outcome is determined (town conquered, ship lost, verdict issued): a bat kohen married to a Yisrael and a bat Yisrael married to a kohen both may NOT eat teruma — we apply stringencies of both life and death.
