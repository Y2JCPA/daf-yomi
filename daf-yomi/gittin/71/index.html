Can a Deaf-Mute Instruct a Divorce Through Writing?

Rav Kahana / Rav
A deaf-mute who can write — the court may write and give a get to his wife based on written instructions.
Rav Yosef
What is he teaching us? The mishna already says we examine a mute person's nods — this is not new!
R' Zeira
A mute is different — his mind is clearly intact. Rav Kahana's case is a deaf-mute (can neither hear nor speak), where writing proves competence.
Baraita Challenge
A deaf-mute's signals and writing are valid only for buying and selling movable property — not for gittin!
Resolution
It is a tannaitic dispute. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel distinguishes: deaf from birth vs. one who became deaf-mute later.
Core Issue Does writing by a deaf-mute demonstrate sufficient da'at (mental competence) to authorize a bill of divorce?

Halakhic Categories: Deaf vs. Mute vs. Deaf-Mute

Cheresh (חרש) — Deaf

  • Speaks but cannot hear
  • Considered halakhically competent
  • Source: Psalms 38:14 — 'like a deaf man, I do not hear'

Ilem (אלם) — Mute

  • Hears but cannot speak
  • Considered halakhically competent
  • Etymology: ishtakil millulei — 'his speech was taken'

Cheresh v'Ilem — Deaf-Mute

  • Cannot hear and cannot speak
  • Competence is disputed
  • Rav Kahana: writing can prove competence
Baraita One who speaks but cannot hear = cheresh. One who hears but cannot speak = ilem. Both are competent for all matters.

Can Written Testimony Replace Oral Testimony?

R' Zeira
Leviticus 5:1 exempts a mute from the oath-offering because he cannot 'utter' testimony. But why — he could write it! This proves writing is invalid for testimony.
Abaye
Testimony is different! The Torah says 'from their mouths' (Deut. 17:6) — testimony must come orally, not in writing. But divorce has no such requirement.
Baraita Challenge
But a baraita says we examine a mute regarding gittin, business transactions, testimonies, and inheritances!
Rav Sheshet
'Testimonies' here means only testimony that a woman's husband died — the Sages were lenient to permit remarriage.
Key Verse מפיהם — 'From their mouths' (Deuteronomy 17:6) — testimony must be oral, not written. But this limitation does not apply to gittin.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's Distinction

A person is a deaf-mute (cheresh)
↓
Deaf-mute from birth
Cannot give written instructions for a get. His marriage is only rabbinic — divorce is also only by intimation (remiza).
Became deaf-mute later
Can give written instructions to write and deliver a get. Writing proves his da'at (competence) remains intact.
R' Yochanan The Rabbis disagree with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel — they hold written instructions from a deaf-mute have no halakhic validity even if he became deaf later.

Narrowing the Baraita: What Can a Mute Do?

Category in BaraitaInitial ReadingGemara's Limitation
Testimonies (עדויות)A mute can testify in all casesOnly testimony that a husband died — Sages were lenient for agunot
Inheritances (ירושות)A mute can testify about others' estatesOnly identifying his own firstborn — it's dividing his own property
Business (משא ומתן)A mute can testify about others' dealsOnly for his own transactions — the court checks his competence
Pattern Each category is limited to matters where the mute person has personal standing, not general testimony on behalf of others.

Abaye's Proof: 'He Can Never Divorce Her'

נתחרש או נשתטה — לא יוציא עולמית
Yevamot 112b The mishna states: If a husband became a deaf-mute or imbecile, he can never divorce her. Abaye infers: the word 'never' (le'olam) means even though he can write, his written instructions are insufficient — supporting the Rabbis against Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.
