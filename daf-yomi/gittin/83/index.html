Can a Get Contain Conditions Limiting Remarriage?

Rabbi Eliezer
A husband may stipulate in a get that the wife may not marry a specific man — this is still a valid act of keritut (severance)
Four Sages
After R' Eliezer's death, R' Tarfon, R' Yosei HaGelili, R' Elazar ben Azarya, and R' Akiva each refute this position
Rava
All the refutations can be countered — except that of R' Elazar ben Azarya, which stands unrefuted
R' Yehoshua
אין משיבין את הארי לאחר מיתה — One does not refute a lion after his death
Core Issue Does a conditional divorce that restricts whom a woman may marry qualify as sefer keritut — a 'scroll of complete severance'?

Rav Ashi's Dilemmas: Retracting a Restriction

Husband first forbids marriage to Reuven & Shimon, then gives a second get permitting marriage
↓
He says: 'You are permitted to Reuven'
↓
Possibility A
He mentioned Reuven because he was listed first — she is permitted to both
Possibility B
He specifically meant only Reuven — Shimon remains forbidden
Teyku All of Rav Ashi's dilemmas — including whether 'also Shimon' means 'in addition to Reuven' or 'in addition to everyone else' — remain unresolved.

Four Refutations of Rabbi Eliezer

R' Tarfon

  • If she marries the brother of the forbidden man and he dies childless…
  • She cannot perform yibum with the forbidden man
  • The husband's stipulation uproots a Torah mitzvah
  • ∴ This is not valid keritut

R' Yosei HaGelili

  • Where do we find something forbidden to one and permitted to another?
  • What is forbidden is forbidden to all
  • What is permitted is permitted to all
  • ∴ This is not valid keritut

R' Elazar b. Azarya

  • ספר כריתות — Scroll of SEVERANCE
  • Must completely sever bond between husband and wife
  • A restriction means she is still bound to him
  • ∴ This is not valid keritut

R' Akiva

  • If she remarries, then marries the forbidden man — the get is retroactively void
  • Her children from husband #2 would be mamzerim!
  • Torah would not sanction such a divorce
  • ∴ This is not valid keritut
Rava's Verdict All refutations have counter-arguments — except R' Elazar ben Azarya's, which is unassailable. R' Yosei in a baraita agrees his is the strongest.

The Lion After Death

דברים כ״ד:א׳–ב׳
אין משיבין את הארי לאחר מיתה R' Yehoshua told the four Sages: One does not refute a lion after his death. After a Sage has passed away, one cannot reject his opinion based on a difficulty — he may have provided an answer had it been raised in his lifetime.

The Gemara reveals that R' Yehoshua himself had a refutation of R' Eliezer, based on Devarim 24:1–2: just as before her first marriage she was not bound to any man, so too before her second marriage she should not be bound to any man.

R' Akiva's Second Argument: The Kohen Problem

R' Akiva
If the forbidden man is a Kohen and the ex-husband dies — she is a widow regarding him but a divorcée to all others
R' Akiva
Kal va-chomer: If the minor prohibition of a divorcée marrying a Kohen applies to him, surely the major prohibition of eishet ish should apply to all men!
Rava
This can be refuted: the prohibition of marrying into Kehunah is unique and cannot be used for a kal va-chomer to other prohibitions
Gemara
Rava follows R' Yannai, who says R' Eliezer derives his view from Devarim 24:2, not from the Kohen verse — so the Kohen comparison is irrelevant
Exception vs. Stipulation R' Akiva raised objections to both versions of R' Eliezer — one for those who understood it as an exception, another for those who understood it as a stipulation.
