Why List All Three Cases in the Mishna?

Rav
Mixing teruma with non-sacred produce is the same category as rendering food impure — both involve intermingling forbidden with permitted.
Shmuel
One who mixes teruma with another's produce is liable for a fine. We cannot derive a fine in one case from a fine in another case.
Gemara
Even according to Rav who says we can derive fines — all three cases are still necessary because each has a unique element.
PRINCIPLE אין לומדין קנס מקנס — We do not derive one fine from another (according to Shmuel).

Is Hezeik She'eino Nikar Real Damage?

One renders another's food ritually impure
(no physical change visible)
↓
Is this categorized as real damage (hezeik)?
Chizkiyya
✅ Yes — damage not evident is damage
Liable by Torah law
R' Yochanan
❌ No — damage not evident is not damage
Liability is rabbinic fine only
↓
Refutation of Chizkiyya: A robber who returns a coin later invalidated by the government can say 'yours is before you' — proving non-evident damage ≠ real damage
CONCLUSION תיובתא — Chizkiyya's position is conclusively refuted. Hezeik she'eino nikar is NOT categorized as damage.

R' Meir vs R' Yehuda: Liability for Unintentional Damage

Rabbi Meir

  • Unintentional or intentional → liable
  • Penalizes shogeg (unintentional) due to meizid (intentional)
  • Applies to rabbinic prohibitions
  • Exception: also penalizes for idolatry (Torah) due to its severity

Rabbi Yehuda

  • Unintentional → exempt
  • Intentional → liable
  • Does NOT penalize shogeg due to meizid
  • Fine applies only when deliberate harm is proven
NOTE Both may agree that hezeik she'eino nikar is NOT real damage. Their dispute is whether the Sages imposed a fine even on unintentional offenders.

Resolving the Contradiction in R' Meir's Rulings

Gemara
R' Meir penalizes the unintentional offender for rendering food impure — but says one who unintentionally cooks on Shabbat may eat the food! Isn't that contradictory?
Resolution
R' Meir distinguishes: for rabbinic prohibitions (e.g., causing impurity), he penalizes even shogeg. For Torah prohibitions (e.g., Shabbat), people are already careful — no need to penalize shogeg.
Gemara
But pouring wine for idolatry is a Torah prohibition, and R' Meir still penalizes the unintentional offender!
Resolution
Idol worship is uniquely severe — its gravity warrants penalizing even unintentional acts as an exception to the general rule.
DISTINCTION Rabbinic law → penalize shogeg (people are lax) · Torah law → don't penalize shogeg (people are careful) · Idolatry → exception, always penalize.
