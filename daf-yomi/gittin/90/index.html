When May a Man Divorce His Wife?

Beit Shammai
Only for ervat davar — a matter of forbidden sexual intercourse (adultery).
Beit Hillel
Even for a minor matter — she burned his dish or over-salted his food.
Rabbi Akiva
Even if he simply found another woman more attractive — 'if she finds no favor in his eyes.'
Gemara
The dispute hinges on how to parse ervat davar: Is it one phrase or two separate grounds?
KEY VERSE Deuteronomy 24:1 — 'Because he has found ervat davar (some unseemly matter) in her'

Parsing 'Ervat Davar' — The Core Dispute

Beit Shammai
The word ervat (unseemly) controls — divorce requires finding sexual misconduct.
Beit Hillel
The word davar (matter) is also stated — any shortcoming is sufficient grounds.
Beit Hillel
If only ervat were written, a woman divorced for adultery couldn't remarry. Davar teaches she can remarry.
Beit Shammai
We use davar for a gezeira shava — linking to Deut. 19:15: adultery must be established by two witnesses.
TEXTUAL KEY Both schools agree the verse contains two words — they disagree on whether ervat and davar create one concept or two.

Three Views on Grounds for Divorce

Beit Shammai

  • Only for adultery or sexual misconduct
  • Reads 'ervat davar' as one unified phrase
  • 'Ki' means 'because' — no favor because of erva
  • Uses 'davar' for gezeira shava with two-witness rule

Beit Hillel

  • Any deficiency — even burning a dish
  • Reads 'ervat' and 'davar' as two separate grounds
  • Both erva AND any other matter justify divorce
  • 'Ervat' teaches even an adulteress may remarry

Rabbi Akiva

  • Even finding a more attractive woman suffices
  • Focuses on 'if she finds no favor in his eyes'
  • Reads 'ki' as 'if/or' — broadest interpretation
  • No objective fault required at all
REISH LAKISH The word ki has four meanings: if, perhaps, rather, and because. The dispute turns on which meaning applies here.

Is a Divorce Valid Without Grounds?

A husband divorces his wife with no grounds — neither erva nor any other matter
↓
Rav Pappa asks Rava: Is the divorce valid?
↓
Rava derives from the rapist's law (Deut. 22:29): only there must a man remarry his divorcée
↓
Rapist
Must remarry — Torah explicitly commands it
Ordinary Divorce
Valid — 'what he did, he did'
PRACTICAL HALAKHA Even according to Beit Hillel, a divorce without grounds is valid after the fact, though not ideal.

The Fly in the Cup — Rabbi Meir's Parable

Type of HusbandReaction to a Fly in His CupAttitude Toward Wife
Overly JealousThrows out the entire cup — won't drink itLike Pappos ben Yehuda who locked his wife inside to prevent her seeing any man
ModerateRemoves the fly and drinks the wineTolerates minor faults but expects basic decency — the standard husband
Morally LaxRemoves the fly, but would drink even after a fly was in itIgnores his wife's immodest behavior — bathing where men bathe, garment open on both sides
HALAKHA For the third type — a wife who behaves immodestly — it is a mitzva to divorce her.

The Wicked Wife and Her Two Husbands

📜 First husband discovers wife's immodest behavior
↓
✅ He divorces her — fulfills the mitzva to send her away
↓
💍 Second husband (called 'aḥer' — other) marries her
↓
⚠️ He is 'not a peer' of the first — he introduced a wicked woman into his house
↓
📤 If he merits — he too will divorce her (Deut. 24:3)
↓
⚰️ If not — she will bury him, as the verse states 'or if the latter husband dies'
MORAL The Torah calls the second husband aḥer (other/different) — implying he is morally inferior for knowingly marrying a woman of bad character.
