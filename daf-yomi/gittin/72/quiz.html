QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yosei, why can't verbal directives be delegated to an agent for writing a get?
A. Because agents might alter the husband's instructions, e.g., letting the scribe sign as witness out of shame
B. Because only a beit din can authorize a get
C. Because the Torah requires the husband to physically hand the get to his wife
D. Because agents are not trusted as reliable witnesses
Correct: A — The Gemara explains that if delegation were allowed, a pitfall could arise: agents might let the scribe sign as a witness (due to the scribe's shame at being excluded), contrary to the husband's instructions, producing an invalid get they mistakenly consider valid.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
If a husband says 'This is your get if I die' without adding 'from today,' what is the status of the get?
A. Valid, since death is inevitable
B. Invalid — it is as if he said nothing, since a get cannot take effect after death
C. Uncertain — she requires ḥalitza
D. Valid only according to Rabbi Yosei
Correct: B — The mishna explicitly states that 'if I die' without 'from today' is treated like 'after my death' — the get would only take effect post-mortem, which is impossible, rendering it invalid.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What happens when a husband says 'This is your get from today and after my death'?
A. The get is completely valid
B. The get is completely invalid
C. She must perform ḥalitza but may not enter levirate marriage
D. She may choose either ḥalitza or levirate marriage
Correct: C — Because the husband's intent is ambiguous — he may have meant 'from today' (valid) or 'after my death' (invalid) — the get has uncertain status. She performs ḥalitza (in case it's invalid and she's bound by levirate) but cannot do yibum (in case it's valid and she's a forbidden divorcée).
QUESTION 4 OF 5
If a husband conditioned his get on dying 'from this illness,' recovered and walked in the marketplace, then fell ill again and died, what determines the get's validity?
A. The get is automatically invalid because he recovered
B. The get is automatically valid because he eventually died
C. The court assesses whether death resulted from the first or second illness
D. The wife decides whether to accept the get
Correct: C — The mishna teaches that the court must assess (omdin) whether death was caused by the original illness (get valid) or a new illness (get invalid). Walking in the marketplace suggests recovery but is not conclusive.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How do Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Rabbi Yosei differ regarding 'from today and after my death'?
A. R' Yehuda HaNasi says it's valid with no ḥalitza needed; R' Yosei considers it uncertain
B. R' Yosei says it's valid; R' Yehuda HaNasi says it's invalid
C. They agree it requires ḥalitza but disagree on yibum
D. R' Yehuda HaNasi invalidates it entirely; R' Yosei validates it through his date principle
Correct: A — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds that 'from today and after my death' produces a fully valid get (no ḥalitza needed). Rabbi Yosei, despite his date principle, treats this specific formulation as uncertain. Each uses the word 'like this' (כזה) to exclude the other's position.
