Conditional Get: When Does It Take Effect?

Husband gives wife a get with a condition tied to his death
↓
Did he say 'from today' or 'from now'?
No — just 'if I die'
Get is invalid — takes effect only after death, when he can no longer divorce
Yes — 'from today if I die'
Get is valid — retroactively applies from the day of the statement
Mixed — 'from today AND after death'
Uncertain — requires ḥalitza but cannot do yibum
Core Principle A get must take effect while the husband is alive. Conditions tied to death are valid only if the get retroactively applies from a time before death.

Can Verbal Directives Be Delegated?

Rabbi Meir
Verbal directives can be delegated — agents can relay the husband's instructions to a scribe
Rabbi Yosei
Verbal directives cannot be delegated to an agent (מילי לא מימסרן לשליח)
Gemara
If delegation were allowed, a pitfall would result: the scribe might shame agents into letting him sign as witness, contrary to the husband's instructions
Rav Ashi
The entire mishna follows Rabbi Yosei, using the style of 'lo tzirikha' — 'it is not necessary to state…'
Baraita Support 'Until they hear his voice' — the scribe and witnesses must hear the husband directly, excluding delegation of verbal instructions.

Conditional Get Formulations & Their Validity

Husband's StatementStatusReason
"This is your get if I die"❌ InvalidTreated as 'after my death' — get cannot take effect post-mortem
"This is your get after my death"❌ InvalidA dead man cannot divorce his wife
"This is your get from today if I die"✅ ValidRetroactively takes effect from today upon his death
"This is your get from now if I die"✅ ValidSame as 'from today' — retroactive effect
"This is your get from today and after my death"❓ UncertainAmbiguous intent — ḥalitza required, yibum prohibited
Key Distinction The words 'from today' or 'from now' anchor the get's effect to a moment during the husband's lifetime, making the death condition a mere trigger rather than the moment of effect.

Illness, Recovery & Relapse

Husband says: 'This is your get from today if I die from this illness'
↓
He recovers and walks in the marketplace
↓
He becomes ill again and dies
↓
The court must assess (אומדין): which illness caused death?
First illness
Get is valid — his condition was fulfilled
New illness
Get is not valid — he did not die from 'this illness'
Practical Impact Walking in the marketplace suggests recovery, but the court must determine whether the original illness was truly the cause of death or merely in remission.

Rabbi Yosei's Date Principle: Does It Apply to Verbal Conditions?

Rav Huna — Uncertain

  • Uncertain if R' Yosei's principle applies when the husband adds a verbal condition
  • Result: Wife needs ḥalitza due to doubt
  • Perhaps the date proves effect only for written terms, not oral ones

Rava — Certain

  • Holds the halakha follows R' Yosei even with verbal conditions
  • 'If I die' / 'when I am dead' = valid get (date supplies 'from today')
  • 'When I die' / 'after my death' = invalid get
  • Teaches new phrasings not found in the mishna
R' Yosei's Principle זמנו של שטר מוכיח עליו — The date written in a document proves when it takes effect, potentially making an explicit 'from today' unnecessary.

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi vs. Rabbi Yosei on 'From Today and After Death'

R' Yehuda HaNasi
"From today and after my death" — a case like this is a valid get. No ḥalitza needed.
Rabbi Yosei
"A case like this" — the date proves when it takes effect, but "from today and after death" remains uncertain.
Gemara
Each says 'like this' (כזה) to exclude the other's position — they do not agree.
Rav Huna
R' Yehuda HaNasi does not hold like R' Yosei, and R' Yosei does not hold like R' Yehuda HaNasi.
Subtle Distinction Both appear lenient, but R' Yehuda HaNasi treats ambiguous language as fully valid, while R' Yosei's date principle creates certainty only when no contradictory verbal condition exists.
