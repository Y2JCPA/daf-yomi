Gittin 60 — The Written & the Oral

עת לעשות לה׳ הפרו תורתך — תהלים קיט:קכו
CORE THEME This daf explores the boundaries of sacred writing: the order of Torah readers, whether partial scrolls may be used publicly, the dramatic debate over whether most of Torah was given in writing or orally, and the principle of עת לעשות לה׳ — sometimes we must bend the rules to preserve Torah. It closes with mipnei darkhei shalom enactments about eiruv placement and irrigation rights.

Reading from Incomplete Scrolls (Chumashim)

People of the Galilee
May one read from a chumash (single book of Torah) in the synagogue publicly?
R' Shmuel bar Nachmani / R' Yochanan
A Torah scroll missing even one sheet may not be read publicly — so an incomplete scroll is invalid.
Gemara
That proof fails! A missing sheet means it lacks part of its content. A chumash contains a complete book.
Rabba & Rav Yosef
One may not read from chumashim in the synagogue — out of respect for the community (kavod ha-tzibbur).
ALSO They likewise prohibit reading the haftara from a scroll containing only haftarot, since such a scroll may not be written — prophetic books must be complete.

How Was the Torah Given?

Scroll by Scroll (מגילה מגילה)

  • Moses taught one portion at a time
  • Wrote each section down after teaching it
  • Eventually joined into one complete scroll
  • Maybe today one may still write partial scrolls — as originally done
  • Or: once joined, it can never be separated again

Complete Book (חתומה)

  • Moses taught the entire Torah orally first
  • Then wrote it all down at once as a complete work
  • Maybe one may never write partial scrolls — it was always whole
  • Or: since people can't always afford a full scroll, partial writing is permitted
  • Rabba rules: One may not write partial scrolls
OBJECTION But Queen Helene wrote the sota passage on a golden tablet for the Temple! Reish Lakish / R' Yannai answers: it was written as individual letters, not a proper text.

Majority Written or Majority Oral?

R' Elazar
The majority of Torah was given in writing. As it says: "I wrote for him the greater part of My Torah" (Hosea 8:12).
R' Yochanan
The majority was given orally. As it says: "For al pi (on the basis of) these matters I made a covenant" (Exodus 34:27).
Gemara on R' Yochanan
Read Hosea as a rhetorical question: Did I write the greater part? If so, Israel would be like strangers — no different from the nations!
Gemara on R' Elazar
"Al pi" doesn't mean more numerous — it means the Oral Torah is more difficult to learn, hence the covenant emphasizes it.
R' YEHUDA BAR NACHMANI Written matters may not be recited orally; oral matters may not be committed to writing. The school of R' Yishmael: "These" — these you may write, but not halakhot.
