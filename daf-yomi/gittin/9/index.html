Gittin 9: Dividing Statements & Equating Documents

DAF OVERVIEW This daf explores whether a single legal document can be partly valid and partly invalid (pelaginan dibbura), examines the parallel halakhot governing bills of divorce and manumission, and clarifies procedures for contested documents and witnesses who cannot sign.

Emancipation: 'All My Property to My Slave'

R' Shimon
The slave always becomes a freeman — unless the master reserved 1/10,000, which might include the slave himself
R' Meir
Even when the master reserves some property, the document does not fully sever the master-slave bond — so no emancipation
Rav Naḥman
Despite R' Yosei praising R' Shimon — 'He kisses the lips' (Proverbs 24:26) — the halakha follows R' Meir
KEY VERSE R' Yosei quoted: "שְׂפָתַיִם יִשָּׁק מֵשִׁיב דְּבָרִים נְכֹחִים" — praising R' Shimon's reasoning, yet the halakha still follows R' Meir

Deathbed Gift: Can He Retract?

A shechiv mera (person on deathbed) writes all property to his slave
↓
He recovers from his illness
Property Gift
CAN retract — gift of a shechiv mera is cancelled if he survives
Emancipation
CANNOT retract — it has been publicized that the slave is a freeman
PROOF This ruling of Rav Naḥman proves he does accept dividing the statement (pelaginan dibbura) — one document is partly valid, partly void!

Why Does Rav Naḥman Follow R' Meir?

Rava
Rav Naḥman rejects dividing the statement — that's why he follows R' Meir
Rav Ashi
No! Rav Naḥman follows R' Meir because the document doesn't fully sever (keritut) the master-slave bond — not because of dividing the statement
Rav Ashi
Rav Naḥman actually agrees with Rava that we do divide a statement — the deathbed case proves it
PRINCIPLE כְּרִיתוּת (keritut) — A bill of manumission must completely sever the relationship. Reserving property that might include the slave fails this requirement.

Contesting a Get in Eretz Yisrael

One Witness Contests

  • R' Yoḥanan: Contestation requires at least TWO people
  • A single ordinary witness cannot invalidate a get
  • But if the HUSBAND himself contests — that suffices

Two Witnesses Contest

  • Two say it's forged vs. two who ratify = equal testimony
  • Problem: 'What did you see to rely on these over those?'
  • Therefore the Mishna must refer to the husband contesting
RESOLUTION The Mishna's case: The husband himself claims the get is forged — he has standing as a single contestant
