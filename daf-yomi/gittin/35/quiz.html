QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why did the widow in the gold dinar story suffer punishment even though she believed her oath was truthful?
A. She had secretly spent the dinar on herself
B. She benefited from the space the dinar occupied in the bread, using less flour
C. She had lent the dinar to someone else before returning it
D. The oath itself was considered blasphemous regardless of truth
Correct: B — The Gemara explains she benefited (ishtarshi) from the place of the dinar — it displaced flour in the bread, giving her a small material benefit she was unaware of, making her oath technically false.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why is the concern about oaths specific to widows and not divorcées?
A. Divorcées are generally more trustworthy than widows
B. Widows live with the orphans and may rationalize that their household work entitles them to partial payment
C. Divorcées have witnesses to their financial arrangements but widows do not
D. The Torah explicitly exempts divorcées from oath requirements
Correct: B — The widow continues living with the orphans and performing household services. This ongoing relationship creates a temptation to rationalize that she has earned compensation, leading her to inadvertently swear falsely about whether she has already collected part of her ketubah.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Huna, why can't a remarried widow take a vow to collect her ketubah?
A. Her new husband inherits the obligation and it becomes his debt
B. Remarriage signals she has already collected her ketubah
C. Her new husband can nullify her vows, rendering the vow meaningless as a safeguard
D. The orphans lose standing to demand a vow once she remarries
Correct: C — Rav Huna's concern is that a husband has the power to nullify his wife's vows. If the widow remarries first and then takes a vow, her new husband could nullify it, removing the protection the vow was meant to provide for the orphans.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is Rav Pappa's concern about NOT requiring a person to detail their vow before a sage dissolves it?
A. The sage might charge more for dissolving an unspecified vow
B. The person might forget what they vowed entirely
C. The vow might concern a prohibited matter that the sage would not dissolve if he knew the circumstances
D. Undetailed vows cannot be dissolved according to Torah law
Correct: C — Rav Pappa argues that some vows serve essential legal functions — like a widow's vow protecting orphans, or a kohen's vow to divorce forbidden wives. If the sage doesn't know the circumstances, he might dissolve a vow that must remain in force, undermining the entire system.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
When the widow spontaneously swore before Rav Huna that she had not benefited from her ketubah, what did Rav Huna rule?
A. He rejected the oath because Rav forbids all oaths for widows
B. He accepted her oath — even Rav concedes that a widow who 'leaps' and swears on her own initiative is believed
C. He required her to take a vow instead of the oath she had sworn
D. He referred her case to Rav for a final ruling
Correct: B — Rav Huna ruled that although Rav generally does not collect a ketubah for a widow, Rav concedes that when a widow voluntarily 'leaps' and takes an oath on her own initiative (rather than having it administered by the court), her oath is accepted and she may collect.
