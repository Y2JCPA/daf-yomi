Widow Collecting Her Ketubah: The Oath Problem

Widow seeks to collect her ketubah from orphans' estate
↓
Normally requires an oath that she hasn't already collected
↓
But Sages stopped administering oaths to widows — why?
↓
Reason
The gold dinar incident showed even a nearly-truthful oath can bring severe punishment
Solution
Rabban Gamliel instituted that a vow (neder) replaces the oath
Key Principle The Sages prioritized protecting widows from the spiritual danger of oaths while still safeguarding the orphans' inheritance through vows.

The Gold Dinar Incident

אם נהניתי מדינרך — יהנה סם המות באחד מבניה של אותה אשה
Rav Kahana's Teaching During a famine, a man deposited a gold dinar with a widow. She placed it in a flour jug and unknowingly baked it into bread she gave to a poor person.

When the owner returned, she swore: "May poison take effect on my children if I benefited from your dinar!"

Shortly after, one of her children died. The Sages concluded: if one who swears essentially truthfully is punished this way, how much more so one who swears falsely!
4 / 9

Why Was She Punished If She Didn't Benefit?

Gemara
She didn't knowingly benefit — why was she punished?
Answer
She benefited from the space the dinar occupied (ishtarshi) — it displaced flour, saving her a small amount.
Gemara
If she did benefit, her oath was actually false! How can we say she swore truthfully?
Answer
She was like one who swore truthfully — the oath was true to the best of her knowledge. Yet she was still punished.
Implication This is why courts stopped administering oaths to widows — the spiritual danger is too great even for unintentional inaccuracy.

Widow vs. Divorcée: Why the Distinction?

Widow (אלמנה)

  • Court does NOT administer an oath
  • Lives in the house with the orphans
  • Performs many services for them
  • May rationalize: "I earned what I took through my work"
  • Higher risk of inadvertently false oath

Divorcée (גרושה)

  • Court DOES administer an oath (R' Zeira/Shmuel)
  • Does not live with the ex-husband's heirs
  • No ongoing relationship creates rationalization
  • Lower risk of self-deception about what she received
  • Standard oath procedure applies
R' Zeira says Shmuel says The leniency of not administering oaths applies only to widows, not divorcées, because the widow's close relationship with the orphans creates unique temptation to rationalize.

Can a Remarried Widow Take a Vow to Collect?

Widow remarries and seeks to collect ketubah from first husband's orphans
↓
Can the court administer a vow (neder) to her?
↓
Rav Huna
No. Her new husband can nullify vows she takes during marriage. The vow provides no protection for the orphans.
Rav Naḥman
Yes. The court administers the vow in public, and a public vow cannot be nullified by the husband.
Underlying Dispute The Tannaitic dispute: Does a vow taken in public have the possibility of nullification by the husband? One Tanna says yes (supporting Rav Huna), another says no (supporting Rav Naḥman).
