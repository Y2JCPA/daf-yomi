Resolving the Contradiction: Lost Get vs. Bava Metzia

Gemara
Our Mishna says a lost get found after a delay is invalid. But Bava Metzia 18a implies: if the writer says 'Give it,' we give it — even after time has passed!
Rabba
No contradiction! Our Mishna deals with a place where caravans are frequently found (שיירות מצויות) — many people pass through, creating identity confusion.
Rabba
Bava Metzia deals with a place where caravans are NOT frequent — so the found document likely belongs to the known owner.
Gemara
And even where caravans ARE frequent — we only worry if it's established (muḥzaq) that two men share the same name in that town.
Key Principle Rabba introduces a two-factor test: (1) frequency of travelers and (2) established identical names in the locale.

Rabba vs. Rabbi Zeira: Same Question, Different Sources

Rabba

  • Raises contradiction from Mishna in Bava Metzia
  • Prefers Mishna because Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's language is more precise
  • Only concerned when two identical names are established in the town
  • Ruled practically in the flax house of Pumbedita

Rabbi Zeira

  • Raises contradiction from a baraita about finding a get in the marketplace
  • Baraita: if husband admits he wrote it → return it to the wife
  • Some say he agrees with Rabba (need established names)
  • Some say he is stricter: caravans alone suffice to block return
Dispute According to one version, Rabbi Zeira holds that in a caravan area, we do not return the get even without established identical names — stricter than Rabba.

Rabba bar bar Ḥana's Lost Get

רבה בר בר חנה אירכס ליה גיטא בי מדרשא
Story Rabba bar bar Ḥana lost a get in the study hall. He said: 'I have a distinguishing mark AND I can recognize it visually.' They returned it — but he remained uncertain which basis they relied upon:

• If by siman → they hold simanim work by Torah law
• If by visual recognition (tevi'ut ayin) → they relied on him specifically as a Torah scholar (tzurva me-derabbanan), but an ordinary person might not be trusted
