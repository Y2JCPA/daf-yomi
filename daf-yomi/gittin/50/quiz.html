QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why is a guarantor for a ketubah exempt from payment, even if the husband has property?
A. Because the ketubah amount is too large to guarantee
B. Because his intent is to perform a mitzva, not to truly obligate himself, and the woman suffers no loss
C. Because the Rabbis never recognized guarantors for any marriage obligation
D. Because the woman can always collect from the husband's orphans instead
Correct: B — The Gemara gives two reasons: the guarantor intends to facilitate the mitzva of marriage, not to create a binding financial obligation, and the woman does not actually lose anything (unlike a lender who parts with money).
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Ulla, what is the Torah-law basis for a creditor collecting only inferior-quality land?
A. The verse about returning a stolen object in its original condition
B. The verse about the debtor bringing out a pledge — implying the least valuable item
C. A tradition from Moshe at Sinai about property assessment
D. The principle that we follow the more lenient ruling in monetary law
Correct: B — Ulla derives from Devarim 24:11 ('the man shall bring forth the pledge out to you') that a debtor would naturally bring out his least valuable vessel, teaching that creditors are entitled only to ziburit.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How was Mar Zutra's opinion — that ketubah collection from ziburit applies only to orphans — refuted?
A. A baraita explicitly states the woman collects ziburit from her husband
B. The Mishna's reason ('a woman wants to be married') wouldn't apply if the rule were only about orphans
C. Rav Naḥman testified that he saw a woman collect ziburit from her living husband
D. The Torah explicitly requires ketubah payment from ziburit
Correct: B — Ravina argued that if the rule applied only to orphans, the Mishna should have given 'because they are orphans' as the reason, not 'because a woman wants to be married.' This was accepted as a conclusive refutation (teyuvta).
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is Rava's challenge to Abaye's kal va-ḥomer argument about collecting from orphans?
A. Rava argues orphans have no legal standing to be sued
B. Rava shows that a stipulation for idit creates a Torah-level right, unlike the Rabbinic upgrade to beinoni
C. Rava proves that idit and beinoni are the same quality in most cases
D. Rava argues that promissory notes override all Rabbinic protections for orphans
Correct: B — Rava distinguishes: the standard creditor's right to beinoni is only Rabbinic (Torah law is ziburit), so reverting to ziburit for orphans makes sense. But a note stipulating idit creates a Torah-level right, so the kal va-ḥomer from beinoni doesn't apply.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In a deathbed distribution using the phrase 'after him,' from whom does a creditor collect first?
A. From the first recipient listed, as he received the gift earliest
B. From all recipients equally, regardless of order
C. From the last recipient listed, working backwards if insufficient
D. From whichever recipient holds the highest-quality land
Correct: C — When the donor specifies 'after him' (aḥarav), a priority order is established. The creditor collects first from the last recipient, then works backward — because later gifts are treated as liened property relative to earlier ones.
