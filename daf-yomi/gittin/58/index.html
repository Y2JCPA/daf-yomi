Gittin 58: Churban Beitar & Captive Children

בְּנֵי צִיּוֹן הַיְקָרִים הַמְסֻלָּאִים בַּפָּז — Lamentations 4:2
THEMES Amud A is one of the most emotionally devastating passages in the Talmud — a series of aggadic accounts of the destruction of Beitar and the suffering of Jewish captives, especially children. The Gemara mourns the scale of death, the beauty of Zion's sons, and tells heartbreaking stories of captive children, including the ransoming of the future R' Yishmael ben Elisha. Amud B returns to the halakhic discussion of Sicarius laws — purchasing property from violent occupiers — including disputes between Rav and Shmuel, and the rule that courts may advance claims on behalf of buyers and heirs.

The Scale of Destruction at Beitar

R' Yannai b. R' Yishmael
Three baskets of 40 se'ah each of tefillin boxes were found on the heads of those killed at Beitar
Baraita
Forty baskets of 3 se'ah each were found
Gemara
They do not disagree: one refers to tefillin shel rosh (head), the other to tefillin shel yad (arm) — they differ in size
R' Asi
Four kav of children's brains were found on a single stone
Ulla
Nine kav were found on one stone
VERSE Psalms 137:8–9 — "Happy is he who shall seize and dash your little ones against the rock" — the Gemara sees this as foretelling the cruelty of Beitar

The Beauty of Zion's Children

בְּנֵי צִיּוֹן הַיְקָרִים הַמְסֻלָּאִים בַּפָּז
LAMENTATIONS 4:2 "The precious sons of Zion, comparable to fine gold (מְסֻלָּאִים בַּפָּז)"

The school of R' Sheila taught: Only two istira-weights of fine gold (piza) exist in the entire world — one in Rome, one everywhere else. So "comparable to fine gold" cannot mean they were covered in gold.

Rather: the people of Jerusalem were so beautiful that they would disgrace fine gold by comparison. Roman noblemen would tie Jewish children to their beds so they could gaze upon their beauty.

Two Captive Children Discuss Torah

🏛️ Two Jewish children are held captive by Romans
↓
❓ One asks: 'Where is this affliction written in the Torah?'
↓
📖 The other answers: Devarim 28:61 — 'Every sickness and plague not written in this Torah'
↓
📏 'How far was I from reaching that verse?' — 'Only one and a half columns (pusta)'
↓
💡 'Had I reached it, I would not have needed you' — showing remarkable Torah knowledge even in captivity
INSIGHT Even in unimaginable suffering, these children sought meaning through Torah — finding their own fate encoded in Deuteronomy's curses

R' Yehoshua Ransoms a Child in Rome

R' Yehoshua ben Chananya travels to Rome and hears of a captive Jewish child with beautiful eyes and curly hair
↓
He stands at the prison entrance and recites: "Who gave Jacob for a spoil?" (Isaiah 42:24)
↓
The child completes the verse: "Did not Hashem, He against Whom we have sinned..."
↓
R' Yehoshua swears: "I will not move until I ransom him!" — He pays a great sum
↓
Within days the child issues halakhic rulings — this was R' Yishmael ben Elisha
PIDYON SHVUYIM This story illustrates the supreme importance of ransoming captives — R' Yehoshua recognized greatness in a child and spared no expense

The Tragic Tale of R' Yishmael's Son & Daughter

The Son

  • Son of R' Yishmael ben Elisha the Kohen Gadol
  • Taken captive and sold as a slave
  • His master boasted: 'I have a male slave of unmatched beauty'
  • Sat in the corner and declared: 'I am a Kohen — shall I marry a slave?'
  • Wept all night — at dawn recognized his sister

The Daughter

  • Daughter of the Kohen Gadol
  • Taken captive and sold to a different master
  • Her master boasted: 'I have a female slave of unmatched beauty'
  • Sat in the corner and declared: 'I am a Kohen's daughter — shall I marry a slave?'
  • They fell upon each other weeping until their souls departed
LAMENT Lamentations 1:16 — "For these things I weep; my eye, my eye runs down with water" — Yirmiyahu mourns such tragedies
