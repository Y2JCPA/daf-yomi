When Is a Slave Emancipated?

Jewish master's slave ends up
in gentile possession
Sold Willingly
Emancipated — owner is penalized
Seized for Debt
Not emancipated — owner didn't profit
Sold to Parhang
Emancipated — should have appeased official
Core Principle The Sages penalize a Jewish master who allows his slave to fall under gentile authority, but distinguish between voluntary and involuntary loss of the slave.

Debt Seizure vs. Sale: Is the Slave Emancipated?

Tosefta
If a gentile collected a slave for debt, or a Sicarius (violent extortionist) took him — the slave is not emancipated.
Gemara
But a baraita says: If the king's household seized a threshing floor for debt, one must tithe — treating it like a sale! So why not emancipate the slave?
Gemara Answer
In the tithe case, the owner profits — he pays debt with untithed grain. With the slave seizure, he didn't profit, so no penalty.
Key Distinction The penalty of emancipation applies only when the owner derives some benefit or could have prevented the outcome — not when he is a pure victim.

Sale to a Government Official (Parhang)

Rav: One who sells his slave to a parhang (gentile official) —
the slave is emancipated
↓
But the official forced him! What could he do?
↓
He should have appeased (פייס) the official
another way — and he didn't
Rav's Ruling Even a coerced sale triggers the penalty if the owner had alternatives. The obligation to protect the slave's religious status demands exhausting all options.

Slave Fled to Army: Can Master Take Payment?

House in Eretz Yisrael

  • Gentile seized a Jew's house by force
  • Owner cannot recover it by any law
  • He MAY accept payment from the gentile
  • Considered 'rescuing money from their hands'
  • He may even register the sale in gentile courts

Slave Fled to Army

  • Slave gave himself to foreign army
  • Master cannot retrieve him by any law
  • Should he be permitted to accept payment?
  • Analogy to the house case suggests YES
  • But Gemara rejects: perhaps only houses, not slaves (people differ)
Unresolved The comparison between a seized house and a fled slave is challenged — selling a house to a gentile is prohibited but less severe than selling a person.

Do Penalties Pass to Heirs?

Question
If the master who sold the slave died — do the Sages penalize his son as well? Did they penalize the person or the property?
R' Asi
A field improved with forbidden Sabbatical-year labor: if the owner dies, his son may sow it. Penalties apply to the transgressor, not the heir.
R' Yosei b. R' Ḥanina
We have a tradition: if one improved his field in a forbidden manner and died, his son may sow it.
Abaye
Similarly: one who defiled another's pure items and died — his son does not pay. Invisible damage (טומאה) is only a rabbinic penalty, and penalties don't transfer to heirs.
Principle Rabbinic penalties (קנסות) are personal: they apply to the transgressor but do not extend to his heirs after death.
