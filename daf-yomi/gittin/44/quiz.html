QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why is a slave seized by a gentile creditor NOT emancipated, unlike produce seized for debt (which must be tithed)?
A. Because the owner profits from paying debt with untithed grain, but doesn't profit from the slave seizure
B. Because the slave was not formally sold through a legal transaction
C. Because debt seizure is considered temporary, not permanent
D. Because the gentile creditor did not intend to keep the slave permanently
Correct: A — The Gemara distinguishes based on profit: with grain, the owner benefits by paying his debt with untithed produce (saving himself the cost of tithing). With the slave, the owner derives no such benefit, so the Sages did not impose the penalty.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rav, why is a slave sold to a parhang (government official) emancipated even though the sale was coerced?
A. Because all sales to gentiles automatically trigger emancipation
B. Because the owner should have appeased the official in some other way
C. Because government officials are considered worse than ordinary gentiles
D. Because the sale to an official is always permanent
Correct: B — Rav holds that even in cases of pressure from a government official, the owner had the option to appease him through other means. His failure to do so makes him responsible, and the penalty of emancipation applies.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the general principle the Gemara derives about rabbinic penalties (kenas) after the transgressor dies?
A. Penalties are doubled for heirs who benefit from the transgression
B. Penalties transfer to heirs only if the property still exists
C. Penalties apply only to the transgressor himself, not to his heirs
D. Penalties are suspended for one year after death, then apply to heirs
Correct: C — From multiple cases — Sabbatical year field labor, defiling pure items, and Festival labor — the Gemara concludes that rabbinic penalties are personal: they target the one who transgressed, not his children or heirs.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel distinguish whether a slave sold to 'so-and-so of Antioch' is emancipated?
A. By whether the sale price was above or below market value
B. By whether the buyer actually resides outside Eretz Yisrael or merely originates from there
C. By whether the slave consented to the sale
D. By whether the buyer is Jewish or gentile
Correct: B — Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says 'of Antioch' alone doesn't prove the buyer lives there — he may have been born there but now lives in Eretz Yisrael. Only when it's clear the buyer actually resides abroad is the slave emancipated. The Gemara further distinguishes between having a house (permanent residence) vs. an inn (temporary stay).
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In R' Yirmeya's dilemma about a Babylonian who marries a woman with slaves in Eretz Yisrael, what is the core legal uncertainty?
A. Whether Babylonia is considered 'outside Eretz Yisrael' for this law
B. Whether the slaves are considered the husband's property (triggering emancipation) or the wife's (no emancipation)
C. Whether the marriage itself constitutes a formal acquisition of slaves
D. Whether the husband must pay the wife for the slaves before leaving
Correct: B — The dilemma hinges on ownership: if the slaves are considered the wife's, no sale occurred and they aren't emancipated. If the husband's rights to their labor make them effectively his, then taking them to Babylonia would be like selling them outside Eretz Yisrael — triggering emancipation. The Gemara leaves this unresolved (teiku).
