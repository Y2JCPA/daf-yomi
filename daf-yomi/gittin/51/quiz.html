QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the Gemara say a daughter's dowry can be collected from liened property even though it is not written?
A. Because it generates publicity (pirsuma) and is treated as if written
B. Because daughters always have priority over buyers
C. Because the Torah explicitly states dowries can be collected from any property
D. Because the Rabbis enacted a special takanah for orphan daughters
Correct: A — The Gemara explains that when a father dies leaving daughters, everyone knows a portion of the estate is pledged for their dowries. This widespread knowledge (pirsuma) makes the obligation equivalent to a written one.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to the Gemara, why can a stepdaughter collect sustenance from liened property but the husband's own daughter cannot?
A. The stepdaughter is older and therefore has priority
B. The stepdaughter's right was confirmed by a kinyan, while the own daughter's right comes from a court stipulation
C. The husband loved his stepdaughter more
D. The stepdaughter's mother had a larger ketubah
Correct: B — The stepdaughter's sustenance was established through the husband's contractual stipulation confirmed by an act of acquisition (kinyan), making it like a written obligation. The own daughter's sustenance derives from court stipulation (tenai beit din), which only collects from unsold property.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Rabba's explanation for why partial admission of a claim requires an oath but total denial does not?
A. Total denial is more believable because it is simpler
B. The Torah only discusses partial admission, not total denial
C. A person wouldn't be brazen enough to deny everything to his creditor's face — so partial admission suggests evasion
D. Partial admission is more common and therefore more suspect
Correct: C — Rabba reasons that a debtor would be too ashamed to completely deny a known debt to his creditor's face. If he admits part, it suggests he wanted to deny everything but couldn't bring himself to — and is likely delaying payment of the rest. The oath pressures him to admit the full amount.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How do R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov and the Rabbis differ regarding a debtor who voluntarily admits owing money to the creditor's son?
A. R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov exempts him from the oath; the Rabbis require it
B. Both agree he must take an oath
C. R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov requires an oath (brazenness applies to the son too); the Rabbis exempt him (he's like one returning a lost item)
D. Both agree he is exempt since it's voluntary
Correct: C — R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov holds the brazenness principle applies equally before the creditor's son, so partial admission requires an oath. The Rabbis hold the debtor would be brazen before the son, so choosing to admit shows good faith — making him like one returning a lost item, exempt from oath.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does Rav say the 'minor' in R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov's case is actually an adult?
A. Because minors cannot own property
B. Because a minor's claim has no legal standing to impose an oath, so the case must involve an adult who is 'like a minor' regarding his father's affairs
C. Because the Mishnah never discusses cases involving actual minors
D. Because the inheritance had already been transferred to the adult son
Correct: B — The Gemara notes that according to Shevuot 38b, one does not take an oath in response to a minor's claim. Therefore, Rav clarifies the 'minor' is actually an adult son who is called a 'minor' because he lacks precise knowledge of his father's financial dealings.
