Gittin 51: Collecting from Liened Property & Oaths of Partial Admission

CORE THEME This daf explores two major questions: (1) When can obligations be collected from liened property sold to third parties — must the debt be written, fixed, or publicly known? (2) When does voluntary admission of a debt trigger an oath, and does Rabba's brazenness principle apply to a creditor's heir?

Can the Second Daughter Claim Her Dowry?

Setup
A man dies leaving two daughters and a son. The first daughter collected her 1/10 dowry. Before the second could collect, the son died — the entire estate now falls to both daughters.
R' Yochanan
The second daughter forfeited her 1/10 dowry claim. They simply divide the estate equally.
R' Chanina
She did NOT forfeit! We already established that a daughter's dowry can be appropriated from buyers of the estate — surely she can collect from the estate before it is divided!
KEY DISTINCTION R' Chanina argues: if the dowry obligation is strong enough to collect from third-party buyers, it's strong enough to collect before dividing the estate.

Can a Fixed but Unwritten Obligation Be Collected from Liened Property?

An obligation exists that is fixed in amount (e.g., 1/10 of estate) but not written in a document
↓
Can it be collected from liened property sold to third parties?
↓
Dowry Case
A daughter's dowry generates publicity (pirsuma) — everyone knows daughters are owed a dowry → treated as if written
General Rule
Other unwritten obligations may still require both a fixed amount and a written document to collect from liened property
PRINCIPLE Publicity (pirsuma) can substitute for a written document — if everyone knows about the obligation, it is as if it were documented.

R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov vs. The Rabbis: Voluntary Admission of Debt

R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov

  • One who says: 'I owed your father 100 dinars but repaid half' must take an oath
  • This is an 'oath on one's own claim' — his own admission creates the obligation
  • Not treated as returning a lost item
  • R' Yitzchak applies this logic to finders who return only part of what was lost

The Rabbis (Chachamim)

  • One who voluntarily admits a debt is like someone returning a lost item
  • Since no one claimed against him, he had no obligation to speak up
  • Therefore he is exempt from an oath
  • We reward honesty — imposing an oath would discourage voluntary admissions
PRACTICAL IMPACT R' Yitzchak extends R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov's view to a finder: if someone claims they lost 2 items and the finder says he found only 1, the finder takes an oath — he is not simply 'returning a lost item.'

Rabba's Reasoning: Why Partial Admission Requires an Oath

💰 A creditor claims: 'You owe me 100 dinars'
↓
😤 The debtor wants to deny everything — but cannot be so brazen (chutzpa) before his creditor who knows the truth
↓
🤏 So the debtor admits to part (e.g., 50 dinars) — hoping to delay paying the rest until he has funds
↓
📖 The Torah therefore imposes an oath on the partial admitter — to pressure him into admitting the full debt
↓
🔑 One who denies everything gets no oath — the presumption is he truly doesn't owe, since he'd be too ashamed to deny outright if he did
RABBA'S PRINCIPLE Ein adam me'iz panav bifnei ba'al chovo — A person would not be so brazen as to completely deny a debt to his creditor's face.
