Gittin 40: Paths to Emancipation

A master takes action suggesting
his slave is free
↓
Is the master's intent to emancipate
clearly demonstrated?
Yes — Clear Intent
Master placed tefillin on slave;
master provided wife for slave;
explicit emancipation formula
No — Ambiguous
Slave wore tefillin on his own;
master lent money from slave;
slave appointed steward
Core Theme This daf explores which actions and words by a master constitute proof of emancipation — and what happens when intent is ambiguous or the master dies before completing the process.

Betrothing One's Own Maidservant

R' Zeira
If a slave marries a woman in his master's presence, he is emancipated — the master's silence is consent to freedom.
R' Yochanan
That's extreme! I teach: if a master writes a betrothal document to his own maidservant — R' Meir says she is betrothed, the Rabbis say she is not.
Gemara
R' Zeira's case is where the master himself provided a wife for the slave — that clearly proves emancipation.
R' Nachman b. Yitzchak
The betrothal case: master said "Become free with this and betrothed with this." R' Meir holds the betrothal formula contains emancipation language; the Rabbis disagree.
Key Point All agree a master is not suspected of marrying his maidservant without first freeing her — the debate is whether the document itself achieves both emancipation and betrothal.

Tefillin on a Slave — R' Yehoshua ben Levi

A slave dons tefillin
in his master's presence
↓
Who placed the tefillin
on the slave?
Master placed them
✅ Slave is emancipated
(Rabba bar Rav Sheila's reading
of R' Yehoshua ben Levi)
Slave put them on himself
❌ Slave is NOT emancipated
(per the baraita: tefillin, Torah reading,
or stewardship alone is insufficient)
Resolution The baraita listing activities that do not free a slave refers to the slave acting on his own. R' Yehoshua ben Levi's ruling applies only when the master actively participates.

Deathbed Instructions About a Maidservant

Rav Dimi's Version

  • Master says: "My heirs should not treat her as a slave"
  • Court compels heirs to write a bill of manumission
  • R' Ami & R' Asi object: her children remain slaves — he only meant to ease her burden

Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda's Version

  • Master says: "She gave me satisfaction; do something that satisfies her"
  • Court compels heirs to give her satisfaction
  • If only emancipation satisfies her — they must emancipate
  • Reason: מצוה לקיים דברי המת — mitzva to fulfill the dead's wishes
Practical Difference In the first version, the instruction explicitly references not enslaving her. In the second, emancipation is only required if that is what would satisfy her.

Renouncing Ownership of a Slave (Hefker)

Ameimar
If a master renounces ownership (hefker) of his slave, there is no remedy. The slave no longer belongs to the master, but the prohibition against marrying a Jewish woman remains — and it cannot be transferred.
Rav Ashi
But Ulla says in R' Yochanan's name: both betrothing one's slave and renouncing ownership — the slave requires a bill of manumission. This implies one can be written!
Ameimar
They meant he requires one — but there is no remedy, because the master can no longer issue one for a slave he doesn't own.
Second Version
If the master renounced and then died — the heirs certainly cannot write one, as the master could not bequeath the prohibition to his son.
Legal Paradox The slave is free in practice but cannot marry a Jewish woman — he needs a document from someone who no longer has the legal standing to issue one.

Formulations of Emancipation (40b)

Statement by MasterEffect
"I made so-and-so a freeman"✅ Emancipated
"My slave was made a freeman"✅ Emancipated
"Behold he is a freeman"✅ Emancipated
"I will make him a freeman"R' Yehuda HaNasi: ✅ Acquired himself
Rabbis: ❌ Not acquired
"I made him a freeman" (verbal only)❌ Not effective — R' Yochanan: must be in a written document
R' Yochanan's Rule All these formulations are effective only when written in a document (שטר) and transferred to the slave. Verbal declarations alone do not emancipate.
