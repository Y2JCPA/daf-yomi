Writing a Get, Rumors, and the Bare Bill of Divorce

Beit Shammai
Even writing a get — without giving it — disqualifies a woman from marrying a kohen.
Beit Hillel
Even if he gave it conditionally and the condition wasn't fulfilled, she is not disqualified. Only an effective divorce disqualifies.
Shmuel
If a rumor circulates that a kohen wrote a get for his wife — she must leave, but the matter requires investigation.
Rav Ashi
We are not concerned for any rumor that circulates after marriage.
Gemara
Shmuel means she must leave a second husband (a kohen she married after the first husband died), not the first.
Core Tension How seriously do we treat incomplete divorce actions and rumors of divorce when it comes to priestly sanctity?

Writing a Get Without Giving It

Mishna
A man wrote a get to divorce his wife but reconsidered and did not give it to her.
Beit Shammai
He has disqualified her from marrying into the priesthood (pesulah li-khehunah). The act of writing alone creates a blemish.
Beit Hillel
Even if he gave it conditionally and the condition was not fulfilled, she is not disqualified. Only effective divorce disqualifies.
R' Yehuda bar Elai
The earlier generations (Beit Shammai) were more conscientious about maintaining unflawed priestly lineage than the later generations.
Key Principle Beit Shammai treat the intent to divorce as tainting the marriage, while Beit Hillel require the divorce to actually take effect.

Investigating the Rumor of a Priest's Divorce

Rumor circulates: A kohen wrote a get for his wife, but she still lives with him
↓
Shmuel: She must leave, but the matter requires investigation
↓
What investigation is needed?
Option 1 (Rejected)
Can we abolish the rumor?
❌ In Neharde'a (Shmuel's city), they do not abolish rumors
Option 2 (Accepted)
Do locals use 'writing' to mean 'giving'?
✅ If so, the rumor may mean he actually gave the get
Linguistic Precision The investigation hinges on local dialect — if people say 'wrote a get' when they mean 'gave a get,' the rumor is far more serious.

Rumors Before vs. After Marriage

Shmuel's Ruling

  • She must leave her husband based on the rumor
  • Gemara clarifies: she must leave a second husband (kohen she married after the first died)
  • She does NOT have to leave the first husband
  • This avoids casting aspersions on children from the first marriage

Rav Ashi's Principle

  • We are not concerned for rumors that circulate after marriage
  • A rumor of betrothal before marriage — we are concerned
  • A rumor after marriage — she need not leave
  • Shmuel's case applies only to a subsequent marriage to a second kohen
Reconciliation The Gemara resolves the contradiction: Shmuel agrees she stays with the first husband. 'She must leave' refers only to a second kohen she marries after the first dies.

Seclusion After Divorce — Licentious Intercourse?

Mishna
A divorced couple was seen together. Do we assume he re-betrothed her through intercourse, requiring a second get?
Beit Shammai
A person does engage in be'ilat zenut (licentious intercourse). We assume no betrothal intent — no second get needed.
Beit Hillel
A person does not engage in licentious intercourse. He intended to betroth her — she needs a second get.
R' Shimon b. Elazar
They only disagree when witnesses saw intercourse. If not seen, all agree — no second get.
Mishna's View
They disagree about witnesses of seclusion — Beit Hillel equate seclusion with intercourse; Beit Shammai do not.
Beit Hillel's Concession Even Beit Hillel agree: if divorced from betrothal (not marriage), seclusion doesn't imply intercourse — because he is not accustomed to her.
