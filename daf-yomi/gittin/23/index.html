Who Can Write & Deliver a Get?

Mishna
A deaf-mute, shoteh, or minor may write a get if an adult stands over him and supervises.
Rav Naḥman
If so, what about a gentile with a Jew supervising? A baraita teaches a gentile is disqualified in any event!
Rav Huna
A gentile acts on his own will (da'ato). Incompetent persons follow the supervisor's intent; a gentile does not.
Rav Naḥman
I retract — the Mishna later disqualifies a gentile only as agent, implying he is qualified to write.
Core Issue The lishma (for her sake) requirement in writing a get depends on whether signing or writing effects the divorce.

Meir vs. Elazar: What Effects the Divorce?

R' Meir
Signatory witnesses (edei ḥatima) effect the divorce. Therefore, lishma applies to signing, not writing.
R' Elazar
Witnesses of transmission (edei mesira) effect the divorce. The verse 'and he writes her' means the writing must be lishma.
Rav Naḥman / Shmuel
Per R' Meir: Even a get found in the garbage dump with matching names is valid — just have it signed lishma!
Rava
But the verse says 'v'katav lah' — 'and he writes her' — this refers to writing, not signing!
Rav Naḥman
No — 'v'katav' can refer to the signing. R' Meir reads it as: the signatures must be for her sake.
Practical Difference Per R' Meir: the body of the get need not be written lishma — only signed lishma. Per R' Elazar: the actual writing must be lishma.

Essential vs. Standard Parts of the Get

Toref (Essential Part)

  • Names of husband and wife
  • Date of the document
  • The expression: 'You are permitted to any man'
  • MUST be written lishma (per R' Elazar)
  • Shmuel: incompetent person must leave this unwritten

Tofes (Standard Part)

  • Boilerplate formulaic language
  • General conditions and terms
  • Need not be written lishma
  • Can be written by anyone
  • Incompetent person may write this portion
Shmuel & Ulla The Mishna permitting incompetent writers applies only when the toref (essential part) is left for a competent person to complete.

Ubar Yerech Imo — The Fetus Is Part of Its Mother

עובר ירך אמו הוא
R' Yehuda HaNasi's Principle A master tells his pregnant maidservant: 'You remain a slave but your child is free.' How can the mother receive freedom for the child from their common master?

Answer: The fetus is considered part of the mother's body (ubar yerech imo). The master is effectively freeing a portion of her — not using her as an agent for a separate person. This avoids the rule that a slave cannot receive a bill of manumission for a fellow slave from a shared master.
