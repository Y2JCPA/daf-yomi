↓

Exception is meaningless

Core Principle A get must grant complete freedom to remarry. Exceptions that limit her to eligible men she could otherwise marry render the divorce incomplete.

Conditions Written Before vs. After the Essential Part

Rava
The dispute between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the Rabbis is about a condition written after the essential part of the get.
R' Yehuda HaNasi
We decree it invalid even after the essential part — lest people confuse it with a condition written before.
Rabbis
We do not issue such a decree. A condition after the essential part does not invalidate.
Rava
But if written before the essential part — everyone agrees the get is invalid.
Key Point The placement of a condition relative to the essential text (toref) determines validity, with the core dispute centering on whether we issue preventive decrees.

Rabbi Zeira Corrects the Baraita

הכל מודין
Textual Emendation The father of R' Avin taught: "If one wrote a conditional get, everyone agrees it is invalid."

R' Zeira challenged: But they disagree about this!

He emended: Say rather — everyone agrees it is valid, referring to a condition written after the essential part.

Why not simply remove 'everyone agrees' and attribute it to R' Yehuda HaNasi? Because the tanna clearly stated 'everyone agrees' — a conspicuous phrase unlikely to be in error.

Exceptions in Divorce: Valid vs. Invalid

Exception: "Permitted to any man except..."StatusReason
My father / your father✅ ValidCannot betroth her (עריות)
My brother / your brother✅ ValidCannot betroth her (עריות)
A slave or gentile✅ ValidBetrothal has no legal effect
Anyone with whom kiddushin cannot take effect✅ ValidGeneralization for all arayot
A Kohen Gadol (she is a widow)❌ InvalidKiddushin takes effect (transgression)
A mamzer or Gibeonite❌ InvalidKiddushin takes effect despite prohibition

Principle If the excluded man could legally betroth her — even sinfully — the exception creates a partial, incomplete divorce.

Rava's Dilemma: Excluding a Minor Boy

Husband says: "Permitted to any man except this minor boy"
↓
A minor boy currently cannot perform betrothal — but eventually he will be able to
Focus on the present
He is currently ineligible
→ Exception is meaningless
→ Get is VALID
Focus on the future
He will become eligible
→ Exception is meaningful
→ Get is INVALID
↓
Rav Naḥman resolves from a baraita: A minor girl can receive a get even though she cannot yet receive her own betrothal — showing we focus on current status
Resolution We assess eligibility at the time of the divorce, not based on future potential.

Essential Formulas: Get vs. Bill of Manumission

Bill of Divorce (Get)

  • "You are hereby permitted to marry any man" (הרי את מותרת לכל אדם)
  • R' Yehuda adds: "And this you shall have from me is a scroll of divorce and a letter of leave"
  • "You are hereby your own" — valid? Ravina argues yes, a fortiori from manumission
  • "You are hereby a free woman" — NOT valid for divorce (wrong formula)

Bill of Manumission (Shtar Shiḥrur)

  • "You are hereby a free woman" (הרי את בת חורין)
  • OR: "You are hereby your own" (הרי את לעצמך)
  • "You are hereby permitted to any man" — NOT valid for manumission
  • "I have no business with you" — valid, derived from case of slave sold to a gentile
Logic Each document has its own essential formula. Cross-applying the wrong formula renders it meaningless — a free woman is not a divorced woman.
