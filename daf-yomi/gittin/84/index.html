Gittin 84: Conditions in Divorce

CORE THEME When a husband attaches conditions to a bill of divorce, the Gemara examines which conditions are valid, which are void, and what happens when impossible or forbidden acts are required. The daf also addresses how to correct a get that contains a disqualifying restriction.
3 / 9

Condition: 'Marry So-and-So' — Who Can She Marry?

Rav Naḥman (1st reading)
She may not marry the specified man (lest people say husbands are trading wives like gifts). But she may marry another man.
Rava (challenging)
If she marries another man first, how will she ever fulfill the condition? Her new husband may refuse to divorce her!
Rav Naḥman (revised)
She may not marry him (the specified man) due to the decree. But if she does marry him, we don't remove her — ein motzi'in.
Rava (final ruling)
She may marry neither him nor another man. If she marries him — she stays. If she marries another — she must leave.
HALAKHA A baraita is cited supporting Rava: she may marry neither him nor another, but if she marries the specified man she need not leave.

Impossible Conditions in a Get

Husband says: 'This is your get on condition you fly to the sky / cross the sea by foot / swallow a 4-cubit reed'
↓
Can this condition ever be fulfilled?
Rabbanan: No → Invalid Get
The condition stands. Since it cannot be fulfilled, the get never takes effect.
R' Yehuda ben Teima: Void condition → Valid Get
He is merely hyperbolizing (מגזים). The condition is not meant seriously and is disregarded.
RULING Rav Naḥman in the name of Rav: Halakha follows R' Yehuda ben Teima. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak finds support from a mishna's precise language.

Forbidden Condition: 'Eat Pig Meat'

Abaye

  • Eating pig meat = impossible condition
  • Since it is forbidden, it is as if she cannot fulfill it
  • Condition is void → get is valid
  • Falls under R' Yehuda ben Teima's principle
  • Excluded case: 'have relations with so-and-so' — possible in a permitted way (if she marries him)

Rava

  • Eating pig meat = possible condition
  • She physically CAN eat it — it's just forbidden
  • Condition is binding → get depends on fulfillment
  • A forbidden act is not the same as an impossible act
  • Supported by a baraita listing pig meat as a valid condition
KEY DISTINCTION Abaye: forbidden = impossible (she shouldn't do it). Rava: forbidden ≠ impossible (she can do it, even if she shouldn't).

Stipulating Against Torah (מתנה על מה שכתוב בתורה)

ChallengeResolution
If the condition requires a sin, isn't it 'stipulating against Torah' and automatically void?This principle applies only when fulfilling the condition uproots a Torah obligation
Example of void condition:Man betroths woman on condition he need not provide food, clothing, and conjugal rights — he uproots Torah law
Rav Adda b. Rav Ika:Here she uproots Torah law, not him — so the principle doesn't apply
Ravina objects:She only eats the pig to fulfill his condition — so he is effectively uprooting Torah law!
Ravina's answer:He's not forcing her to sin. She can choose: eat and be divorced, or don't eat and remain married. No Torah law is definitively uprooted.
PRINCIPLE A condition only counts as 'against Torah' when it necessarily uproots a mitzvah. If the person has a choice, the condition stands.

Correcting a Get with a Qualification

📜 Husband wrote a qualification inside the get (e.g., 'permitted to all except so-and-so')
↓
❌ This get is invalid — even if the qualification is later erased
↓
🔄 He must take the get back from her
↓
📝 Hand it to her again and say: 'You are permitted to marry any man'
↓
✅ Now the divorce takes full effect
DISPUTE R' Shimon ben Elazar: He must physically re-give the get. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: Normally just telling her suffices — but here even he agrees re-giving is needed, since she already acquired the get for disqualification from kehunah.
