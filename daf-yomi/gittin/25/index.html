The Great Bereirah Debate: Does the Future Clarify the Past?

Ze'eiri
Only the final case of the mishna (get written for 'whichever wife I choose') disqualifies the woman from marrying a Kohen
Rav Asi
I agree — only the final case disqualifies, because bereirah is uncertain
R' Yochanan
Even the final case does not disqualify her — that get is completely invalid. There is no bereirah at all
R' Yochanan
Brothers who divide an inheritance are like purchasers — they must return land in Yovel. This proves: no retroactive clarification
Core Question Can a future event retroactively determine that a legal act was valid from the start? This is the principle of bereirah (בררה) — retroactive clarification.

R' Yochanan's Double Proof Against Bereirah

R' Yochanan rules: There is no bereirah
↓
Proof 1: Get
A get written for 'whichever wife' is completely invalid — Torah requires 'he writes for her' (לשמה)
Proof 2: Inheritance
Brothers dividing land are purchasers, not heirs — land returns in Yovel
Why Both? Each ruling alone could be explained by a reason specific to that case. The get ruling might be about lishmah; the inheritance ruling might be a stringency. Both together prove R' Yochanan categorically rejects bereirah.

Rav Hoshaya Challenges Rav Yehuda

Rav Hoshaya
If a husband says 'write a get for whichever wife exits first,' what is the law?
Rav Yehuda
The mishna already teaches this — a get for 'whichever I choose' is unfit. No bereirah!
Rav Hoshaya
But the mishna about Korban Pesach says: 'whichever child reaches Yerushalayim first' — and it works! That's bereirah!
Rav Yehuda
Hoshaya, my son! What do Paschal offerings have to do with gittin? R' Yochanan explains: the father registered all children from the start — it was just to encourage zeal in mitzvot!
Key Distinction The Paschal offering case is not bereirah at all — all children were included from the outset. The 'competition' was merely motivational.

Bereirah: Dependent on Self vs. Others

Dependent on One's Own Decision (בידו)

  • Example: Wine from Samaritans — designating which log is terumah later
  • R' Meir: Bereirah works — may drink immediately
  • R' Yehuda: NO bereirah — prohibited
  • R' Shimon: NO bereirah — prohibited

Dependent on Others' Decision (בידי אחרים)

  • Example: 'This is your get if I die from this illness'
  • R' Yehuda: Bereirah WORKS — get takes effect retroactively upon death
  • R' Shimon: Bereirah WORKS — betrothal valid if father later consents
  • Death/others' will is not in one's own control
Pattern R' Yehuda and R' Shimon both distinguish: when the outcome depends on your own future choice, no bereirah. When it depends on others or circumstances beyond your control, bereirah applies.

The Samaritan Wine Procedure (R' Meir)

🍷 Purchase 100 logs of wine from a Samaritan (tithes uncertain)
↓
📣 Declare: 'Two logs I will later separate are ma'aser rishon; one log will be terumat ma'aser; two logs are ma'aser sheni'
↓
💰 Deconsecrate the future ma'aser sheni onto coins
↓
🥂 Drink the wine immediately, relying on the future separation
↓
⏳ Later, physically separate the designated logs — bereirah clarifies retroactively
Dispute R' Meir permits this because he accepts bereirah even when dependent on one's own decision. R' Yehuda and R' Shimon forbid it — since you choose which logs to designate, there is no retroactive clarification.

📋 Gittin 25 — Summary Map

Question: Can a future event retroactively validate a past legal act?
↓
R' Meir: YES — bereirah works even when outcome depends on your own choice (e.g., Samaritan wine)
R' Yehuda & R' Shimon: ONLY when dependent on others — not when you control the outcome
R' Yochanan: NO — never. Get for 'whichever wife' is void; inheritance division = purchase
↓
Practical Impact: A get written for an unspecified wife is invalid according to all views — the dispute is whether it even creates a safek (doubt) that disqualifies her from Kehuna
