QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why is a scribe believed when he says the parchment was not processed with proper intention, but not believed about God's names?
A. Because parchment is more important than names
B. Because claiming improper parchment processing causes him to lose his entire wage, so he has no motive to lie
C. Because the scribe has witnesses for the parchment claim
D. Because the laws of parchment are rabbinic while names are Torah law
Correct: B — When claiming the names were written without intent, the scribe only loses partial wages and may be lying to distress the buyer. But claiming improper parchment processing costs him everything — so his admission is credible.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rava, if a husband tells witnesses 'this is a get' but tells his wife 'take this promissory note,' what is the status?
A. The divorce is void because the wife was deceived
B. The divorce is valid — consent is not required, and he spoke from embarrassment, not cancellation
C. The divorce depends on whether the wife later discovers the truth
D. The witnesses must inform the wife before it takes effect
Correct: B — Rava learns from R' Yochanan ben Gudgeda that divorce doesn't require the woman's consent. The husband called it a 'promissory note' out of embarrassment — had he meant to cancel, he would have told the witnesses.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the dispute between Ulla and Rav Yehuda about a publicly-known stolen sin-offering?
A. Whether it must be returned to the owner or destroyed
B. Whether it effects atonement when the theft is known vs. unknown
C. Ulla says the concern is the Kohanim's distress; Rav Yehuda says it's the honor of the altar
D. Whether the thief or the original owner receives atonement
Correct: C — Both agree a publicly-known stolen offering does not atone. They disagree on why: Ulla focuses on the Kohanim being distraught at eating forbidden meat, while Rav Yehuda focuses on people saying the altar consumes stolen property.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In Rava's dilemma about a thief who consecrates a stolen animal, why does he conclude ownership begins at the time of consecration rather than theft?
A. Because consecration is the more significant halakhic act
B. Because the Torah requires a formal kinyan for acquisition
C. So that the sinner does not profit — he must return wool and offspring from before the consecration
D. Because the owner has not yet despaired at the time of theft
Correct: C — If ownership were retroactive to the time of theft, the thief would keep the wool and offspring — profiting from his crime. The Sages instituted the decree to increase his liability, not to benefit him.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Under the law of Sicarii, why is a purchase void if one buys from the Sicarius first and only then from the original owner?
A. Because the Sicarius never had legal title to transfer
B. Because the original owner's subsequent 'sale' is presumed to be under duress or not genuine consent
C. Because the buyer is considered an accomplice to the Sicarius
D. Because wartime transactions are automatically void
Correct: B — When the buyer already holds the field via the Sicarius, the original owner's agreement to 'sell' is presumed coerced — the owner didn't truly consent. But buying from the owner first shows genuine willingness.
