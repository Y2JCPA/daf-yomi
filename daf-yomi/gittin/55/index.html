Gittin 55: Testimonies, Stolen Property & the Altar

DAF OVERVIEW R' Yochanan ben Gudgeda testifies on four topics: divorcing a deaf-mute woman, a minor eating teruma, returning value for a stolen beam (takkanat hashavim), and a stolen sin-offering's validity. The Gemara then explores when the altar may "consume" stolen property, whether ye'ush alone transfers ownership, and introduces the law of Sicarii regarding land seized by violence.

When Is a Scribe Believed?

Not Believed (R' Ami)

  • Scribe claims God's names written without proper intent
  • He only loses wages for the names, keeps rest of payment
  • Possible motive: distress the buyer (mistake of R' Yirmeya)

Believed (Our Case)

  • Scribe claims parchment not processed with proper intent
  • He loses his entire wage for the whole scroll
  • No motive to lie — self-incrimination is credible
PRINCIPLE A person is believed when their admission causes themselves a total loss — migo d'i ba'i reasoning

Rava: Get Disguised as a Promissory Note

Husband tells witnesses: 'See this get I am giving my wife'
↓
Husband tells wife: 'Take this promissory note'
↓
Is the divorce valid even though she doesn't know it's a get?
YES — Valid
We learn from R' Yochanan ben Gudgeda: divorce does not require the woman's consent
Why 'promissory note'?
He said it out of embarrassment, not to cancel the get — otherwise he would have told the witnesses
KEY LOGIC If he intended to cancel the get, he would have informed the witnesses. Silence to witnesses = intent stands.

Why Doesn't a Publicly-Known Stolen Offering Atone?

Ulla
Because the Kohanim who eat the sin-offering would be distraught learning they ate a forbidden offering
Rav Yehuda
Because people would say the altar consumes stolen property — it's about the honor of the mizbe'ach
Gemara
According to Ulla, the Mishna's focus on sin-offering (eaten by Kohanim) makes sense
Gemara
According to Rav Yehuda, why only sin-offering? A burnt-offering is entirely on the altar!
Rav Yehuda's Defense
The Mishna uses lo zu af zu: even a sin-offering where only fat/blood go on the altar — still decreed invalid if publicly known stolen
PRACTICAL DIFFERENCE If not publicly known as stolen → both agree it effects atonement. Dispute is about the reason behind the public case.

Ye'ush & Consecration of a Stolen Animal

A thief steals an animal
↓
He consecrates (makdish) it as a sacrifice
↓
He slaughters it outside the Temple
↓
Is he liable for karet for slaughtering outside?
Rav Sheizevi
Yes — 'karet by rabbinic law': the Sages placed the animal in his possession so the consecration is valid
Rava Defends
Don't laugh! He means karet that comes through the Sages' decree granting him ownership
RAVA'S DILEMMA Did the Sages place the animal in the thief's possession from the time of theft (he keeps wool & offspring) or from the time of consecration (he must return them)? Rava concludes: from consecration — so the sinner doesn't profit.
