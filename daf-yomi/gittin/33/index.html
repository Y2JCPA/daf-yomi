Voiding a Get: From Problem to Solution

Husband sends get via agent to wife
↓
Can husband void the get before a different court?
Before Decree
Yes — husband could void get in any court, even without wife's knowledge
After Decree
Rabban Gamliel instituted: Cannot void a get in absentia — for betterment of the world
Key Issue This daf explores why voiding a get remotely is dangerous, whether the decree is enforceable by Torah law, and how nullification works when multiple agents are involved.

How Many Judges for a Prosbol?

Rav Naḥman
Two judges also constitute a court (beit din). The prosbol's language of 'so-and-so and so-and-so the judges' implies two suffice.
Rav Sheshet
The tanna isn't counting judges — he merely used 'so-and-so' twice to teach that judges' names must be written. Three judges are still required.
Rav Naḥman
Proof: The mishna says 'judges sign or witnesses sign.' Judges are compared to witnesses — just as witnesses are two, so too judges are two.
Rav Sheshet
The cases are not comparable — each has its own requirement. Judges need three; witnesses need two. The mishna simply lists who may sign.
Practical Point The mishna teaches that the precise wording of the prosbol doesn't matter — whether written in terminology of judges or witnesses, either group may sign it.

Why Can't a Husband Void a Get in Absentia?

R' Yoḥanan

  • Concern: Mamzerim (illegitimate children)
  • Wife doesn't hear about the voiding
  • She remarries thinking she's divorced
  • Children from second marriage are mamzerim
  • Follows Rav Naḥman: can void before 2 people
  • 2 people = no publicity → wife won't find out

Reish Lakish

  • Concern: Deserted wives (agunot)
  • Wife does hear about the voiding
  • She fears any get might be voided
  • So she refrains from ever remarrying
  • Follows Rav Sheshet: must void before 3 people
  • 3 people = generates publicity → wife hears
Connection The debate about 2 vs. 3 judges from the prosbol discussion directly determines whether the concern is mamzerim or agunot.

If Husband Voids the Get Anyway — Is It Void?

Despite Rabban Gamliel's decree, husband voids the get in absentia
↓
Is the get actually rendered void?
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
Yes — the get is void. The decree does not override Torah law.
Rabban Shimon b. Gamliel
No — the get remains valid. Otherwise, what advantage does the court have? (מה כח בית דין יפה)
Key Principle Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel argues the court's authority must be meaningful — if its decrees can be ignored, מה כח בית דין יפה (what power does the court have)?

Afkinhu — The Sages' Ultimate Power

כל דמקדש אדעתא דרבנן מקדש ואפקעינהו רבנן לקידושין מיניה
Foundational Concept The Gemara asks: Can a Rabbinic decree really override Torah-level marriage?

Answer: כל דמקדש אדעתא דרבנן מקדש — Anyone who betroths does so contingent upon the will of the Sages.

When the husband defies their ruling, the Sages retroactively expropriate (אפקעינהו) the betrothal money, nullifying the marriage from its inception.

Ravina's challenge: This works for betrothal with money — but what about betrothal via intercourse?

Answer: The Sages declare the intercourse to be licentious (בעילת זנות) — not creating a marital bond.
