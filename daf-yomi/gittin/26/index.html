Pre-Writing Bills of Divorce: The Tofes Question

Can a scribe write the standard part (tofes) of a get in advance?
R' Yehuda
Invalidates ALL pre-written documents — gittin, loans, and sales alike
First Tanna
Valid — but must leave blank: names, date, and key phrase
R' Elazar
Valid for loans & sales, invalid for gittin — "he writes for her"
Key Debate The mishna's first clause follows R' Elazar per Shmuel — witnesses of transmission effect divorce, so the writing must be for her sake.

Three Tannaitic Views on Pre-Written Documents

First Tanna
A scribe may write the tofes (standard part) of any document in advance, as long as the essential details are left blank.
R' Yehuda
I invalidate all these documents if the standard part was pre-written — gittin, loans, and sales alike.
R' Elazar
All are valid except gittin. The Torah says "וכתב לה" — he must write it for her. Even the standard part of a get cannot be pre-written.
Shmuel
The first clause of our mishna actually follows R' Elazar — who holds that witnesses of transmission effect the divorce, so the writing must be lishmah.
Contradiction? The first clause permits pre-writing the tofes of a get, while R' Elazar in the last clause forbids it. Answer: Two tannaim disagree about R' Elazar's opinion.

What Is the "Ordinance" (Takana)?

R' Shabbetai / Ḥizkiyya: Prevent a Quarrel

  • Follows R' Meir — signatory witnesses effect divorce
  • Writing need not be lishmah
  • By right, even the essential part could be pre-written
  • But a wife might hear the scribe writing her name by chance
  • She would think her husband ordered the get — causing a quarrel

Rav Ḥisda / Avimi: Remedy for Deserted Wives

  • Explained per both R' Meir and R' Elazar
  • Per R' Meir: Don't let husband have a ready get to throw in anger
  • Making him wait gives time to calm down and reconsider
  • Per R' Elazar: Allow tofes so a get can be completed quickly
  • Otherwise a husband traveling overseas may abandon his wife
Takana The ordinance balances two risks: a get that's too easy to deliver (impulsive divorce) vs. too hard to obtain (deserted wives).

Deserted Wives: Two Directions of the Same Concern

Husband wants to divorce his wife
↓
Is a scribe readily available to write a complete get?
Yes — Risk of Impulsive Divorce (R' Meir)
If the essential part is pre-written, husband might throw the get in anger during a fight → wife left divorced and deserted
No — Risk of Abandonment (R' Elazar)
If even the tofes can't be pre-written, husband traveling abroad won't find a scribe → wife left as an agunah
↓
Solution: Allow pre-writing the tofes only — fast enough to prevent abandonment, slow enough to prevent impulsive divorce
Balance The Sages crafted a middle path: pre-write the boilerplate, but require the personal details to be written fresh each time.

Why Must the Date Be Written — Even for Betrothal?

Gemara
The mishna requires a date on all gittin — whether divorcing from marriage or from betrothal. Why?
View 1
The date prevents a husband from protecting his adulterous wife with an undated get (the "daughter of his sister" case). This applies even to betrothal — adultery law begins at betrothal.
View 2
The date protects the wife's right to produce from her property after divorce. But a betrothed woman's husband has no rights to her produce yet!
Gemara
So according to the "produce" reason, why require a date for a betrothal divorce?
Ulla
The date is for the benefit of the child — to establish that the get was written after marriage, not before.
Ulla's Principle A get written for a betrothed woman to be used after marriage is invalid. The date proves the get was written at the proper time, protecting the legitimacy of any children.
