Can a Public Vow Be Nullified?

Initial View
A vow taken in public (b'rabbim) has no possibility of nullification.
Challenger
But according to the one who says a public vow can be nullified — what then?
Ameimar
A vow taken al da'at rabbim (with public consent) — even the lenient view agrees it cannot be nullified.
Limitation
This applies only for optional matters. For a mitzvah, even such a vow can be nullified.
Example
Rav Aḥa imposed a vow on a teacher who hit children excessively. Ravina nullified it because no better teacher could be found.
Principle Public-consent vows are the strongest form — nullifiable only for mitzvah purposes.

Why Do Witnesses Sign a Get?

Rabba — following R' Elazar

  • Witnesses of transmission (mesirah) effect the divorce
  • Signatures are NOT required by Torah law
  • Sages added signatures so the get can be ratified later
  • Concern: witnesses may die or travel overseas

Rav Yosef — following R' Meir

  • Signatory witnesses effect the divorce
  • Signatures ARE required by Torah law
  • The takanah: witnesses must write their full names
  • Previously they wrote only 'I, so-and-so, signed' — identity unclear
Rabban Gamliel Instituted that witnesses write: 'So-and-so son of so-and-so' — with identifying details — for the betterment of the world.

How the Prosbol Works

📉 People refrain from lending as Shemittah approaches — violating Devarim 15:9
↓
💡 Hillel sees the problem and institutes the prosbol
↓
📝 Creditor declares: 'I transfer to you, judges of [place], the right to collect my debts'
↓
👨‍⚖️ Judges or witnesses sign below the prosbol document
↓
✅ Shemittah does not cancel the debt — creditor may collect whenever he wishes
Mechanism The prosbol effectively transfers the debt to beit din. Since Shemittah only cancels personal debts, court-held debts remain collectible.

Can the Sages Cancel Debts That Torah Law Preserves?

Question
If Shemittah debt cancellation is only rabbinic today, the Sages are essentially telling debtors to keep money the Torah says they owe!
Abaye
It's shev v'al ta'aseh — the Sages instruct one to sit passively and not pay. Passive violation of Torah law is permitted.
Rava
The Sages can do this because hefker beit din hefker — property declared ownerless by the court is ownerless.
R' Yitzḥak
Source: Ezra 10:8 — 'All his property shall be forfeited' — the court can confiscate property.
R' Eliezer
Source: Yehoshua 19:51 — Just as fathers transmit to children, so heads of the nation transmit to the people as they wish.
Key Dispute Abaye: passive non-compliance | Rava: court has direct power over property (hefker beit din hefker)
