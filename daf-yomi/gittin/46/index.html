Why Can't He Remarry Her? Two Frameworks

Rav Naḥman (Version 1)
The prohibition exists to prevent potential harm — he might later cast aspersions on the get, making her children mamzerim.
Rav Naḥman (Version 2)
The prohibition exists as a deterrent — so Jewish women won't be promiscuous or lax with vows. He must say 'Be aware' to publicize.
Baraita — R' Meir
Supports Version 1: He might later say 'Had I known, I wouldn't have divorced her' — invalidating the get retroactively.
Baraita — R' Elazar b. R' Yosei
Supports Version 2: The purpose is to deter women from acting improperly. Therefore he must announce the reason publicly.
Key Dispute Both versions agree he cannot remarry — they disagree on why, which affects whether he must explicitly declare 'Be aware' to the wife.

Two Versions of Rav Naḥman's Statement

Version 1 — Preventing Harm

  • He must state the reason for divorce
  • Concern: He may later claim the get was given under false pretenses
  • This could retroactively invalidate the divorce
  • Children from second marriage would be mamzerim
  • If he stated the reason, his later claims are dismissed

Version 2 — Deterrence

  • He must say 'Be aware (הוי יודעת)'
  • Purpose: Deter women from forbidden relations or reckless vows
  • Public announcement serves as a warning to others
  • The penalty itself (no remarriage) is the deterrent
  • Publicizing the reason amplifies the deterrent effect
Practical Difference Version 1 requires merely stating the reason; Version 2 requires a formal 'Be aware' declaration directed at the wife.

Rabbi Yehuda's Distinction: Public vs. Private Vows

R' Yehuda
If the vow was known to the public — he may not remarry her, because such a vow cannot be dissolved.
R' Yehuda
If the vow was private — he may remarry her, because a sage can dissolve it.
R' Yehoshua b. Levi
Source: The oath to the Gibeonites (Joshua 9:18) — a public vow cannot be dissolved.
Rabbis (Dissenting)
The Gibeonite oath was based on false pretenses — it never took effect! The Jews honored it only to avoid desecrating God's name.
Gibeonites The Gibeonites lied about coming from a far land. The Jewish leaders' oath never technically took effect, yet they honored it for kiddush Hashem.

How Many People Constitute 'The Public'?

AuthorityNumberSource
Rav Naḥman3 peopleYamim rabbim (Lev. 15:25) — 'days' = 2, plus 'many' = 3
R' Yitzḥak10 peopleGibeonite verse: 'leaders of the congregation' — an edah requires 10 (derived from the spies)
Why It Matters A vow made before the requisite number of people cannot be dissolved by a sage, and therefore the husband may never remarry her according to R' Yehuda.

The Husband's Vow (Konam)

Husband vows: 'All produce in the world is forbidden to me if I don't divorce you'
↓
He divorces her to fulfill his vow
↓
May he remarry her?
Possible Objection
R' Natan: Vowing = building a private altar; fulfilling = sacrificing on it. Perhaps penalize him!
Halakha
He may remarry her — the prohibition applies only when she took the vow
Betterment of the World Ravina: The tikkun ha-olam reason for prohibiting remarriage applies only to her vow. When he vows, there is no such concern.

The Ailonit Case — 'Your Silence Is Preferable'

👫 Man marries woman, later discovers she may be an ailonit (unable to bear children)
↓
📜 He divorces her, claiming the marriage was a mistaken transaction
↓
💍 She marries another man and has children — proving she is NOT an ailonit
↓
💰 She demands her ketubah payment from husband #1
↓
🤐 R' Yehuda: He may say 'שתיקותיך יפה מדיבורך' — your silence is preferable to your speech
↓
⚠️ If she persists, he can claim the get was given in error, endangering her children's status
Implicit Threat The husband can claim: 'I only divorced you because I thought you were an ailonit — the get was a mistake.' This would retroactively endanger her children from the second marriage.
