Rewriting Over Existing Ink (Kesiva al Gabei Kesiva)

Rav Ḥisda
If a get was written not lishmah and the scribe passed over it with a reed pen lishmah — this is the dispute of R' Yehuda and the Rabbis.
R' Yehuda
If a scribe writing a Torah scroll accidentally wrote God's name without intent, he may pass over it with a reed pen and sanctify it. ✅
Rabbis
Even adding a second layer of ink — the name was not written in the optimal manner. ❌
Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov
Perhaps the Rabbis only object for a Torah scroll due to zeh Eli v'anvehu (beautification of mitzvot). A get has no beauty requirement — even the Rabbis may agree it's valid!
Core Dispute Can a second layer of ink supply the required lishmah intent? R' Yehuda says yes; Rabbis say the writing is not optimal — but perhaps only for sacred items.

Why a Torah Scroll Cannot Be a Get

Could a husband use a Torah scroll to divorce his wife?
↓
❌ Lishmah — 'He writes her' requires intent for this specific woman
↓
What if he paid the scribe to write the divorce verses for her sake?
↓
❌ A get requires his name, her name, his city, her city — these aren't in the Torah
↓
So what does Rav Yosef teach us?
↓
Novel teaching: gall water on gall water is not permanent — no concern about writing a get on top of a Torah scroll
Rav Yosef's Chiddush The real novelty is that mei gallin (gall ink) applied over existing gall ink does not create a permanent bond — so there's no concern a get was secretly written atop the scroll.

Rav Ḥisda's Challenge: Can All Gittin Be Invalidated?

Rav Ḥisda's Claim

  • "I can invalidate ALL gittin in the world"
  • Torah says 'he writes' — but the wife pays the scribe
  • Torah says 'and gives' — yet the get has no monetary value

Rava's Refutations

  • The Sages transferred ownership of the get to the husband — it counts as 'he writes'
  • 'And gives' = the act of giving, not monetary value
  • Proof: A get on forbidden-benefit items (no value at all) is still valid!
Ruling A get need not have monetary value. The Sages of Eretz Yisrael confirmed: a get written on items from which benefit is forbidden is valid.

Ownership vs. Condition: The Paper of the Get

📜 Husband says: 'This is your get but the paper is still mine'
↓
❌ She is NOT divorced — he only gave the writing, not the document
↓
📜 Husband says: 'This is your get on condition you return the paper'
↓
✅ She IS divorced — the get belongs to her; returning is just a condition
↓
❓ Rav Pappa's dilemma: What if the paper between lines/words remains his but the written parts are hers?
↓
🤷 Teiku — unresolved. Is this one scroll or multiple scrolls?
Critical Distinction Retaining ownership of the paper invalidates the get entirely. Imposing a condition to return it does not — the get is fully hers, she just must fulfill the condition.
