QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why can't a Torah scroll serve as a bill of divorce?
A. Because Torah scrolls are too sacred to use for divorce
B. Because a get must be written lishmah with the specific names of husband and wife
C. Because a Torah scroll is written on parchment, not paper
D. Because the Torah does not contain language about divorce
Correct: B — The Gemara gives two reasons: a get must be written for the specific woman's sake (lishmah), and it requires the specific names and cities of husband and wife — details not found in a Torah scroll.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov, why might even the Rabbis accept rewriting a get with a reed pen for lishmah purposes?
A. Because the Rabbis only reject rewriting when there is no ink available
B. Because the Rabbis' objection about rewriting applies only to Torah scrolls, which require beautification (zeh Eli v'anvehu), but gittin have no such requirement
C. Because the Rabbis changed their position after further deliberation
D. Because rewriting a get is considered a different category of writing entirely
Correct: B — Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov distinguishes: the Rabbis' objection was based on the mitzvah of beautification ('This is my God and I will glorify Him'), which applies to sacred items like Torah scrolls but not to bills of divorce.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the halakha if a husband says 'This is your get but the paper is mine'?
A. She is divorced and must return the paper
B. She is divorced but cannot remarry until she returns the paper
C. She is not divorced — he gave only the writing, not the document
D. She is divorced because the writing is the essential part
Correct: C — If the husband retains ownership of the paper, the wife has not truly received the get. However, if he gives it on CONDITION that she return the paper, she IS divorced — because the get fully belongs to her and the return is merely a condition.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does Rav Naḥman require the husband to explicitly state that the remainder of the paper is for ketubah payment?
A. Because the remainder has no monetary value
B. Because without explicit designation, the blank space is considered merely the margin of the scroll
C. Because the wife might not realize the paper has value
D. Because the ketubah must be paid in coins, not paper
Correct: B — Without explicit designation, the blank remainder of the paper is simply viewed as the gilyono (margin) of the get — part of the divorce document itself, not a separate payment for the ketubah.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What proof does the Gemara bring that a get need not have monetary value?
A. The Torah uses the word 'scroll' which implies any material
B. A get written on items from which benefit is forbidden is valid, per the Sages of Eretz Yisrael
C. Rav Ḥisda ruled that value is irrelevant to all documents
D. The mishna states that a verbal declaration suffices for divorce
Correct: B — The Sages of Eretz Yisrael sent the ruling that a get written on items from which benefit is forbidden (issurei hana'ah) — which have absolutely no monetary value — is still valid. This proves 'and gives' refers to the act of giving, not to giving something of value.
