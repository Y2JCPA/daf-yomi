Gittin 39: Consecration of Slaves & Paths to Freedom

Master consecrates his slave — what is the effect?
↓
R' Meir
Slave is consecrated (monetary value) — a person doesn't speak for naught
Rabbis / Rav
Slave is not consecrated — statement has no effect
↓
If consecrated → does me'ilah (misuse) apply?
↓
First Tanna
No me'ilah — slave = land
R' Shimon b. Gamliel
Me'ilah on hair ready to cut
Core Tension The daf explores how consecration, bodily status, and emancipation interact — from the metaphysics of speech acts to the legal mechanics of freedom.

Does Consecrating a Slave Have Effect?

Rav
One who says 'My slave is consecrated' — there is no effect. The slave simply continues working and eating.
Baraita
One who consecrates his slave — the slave works and eats from his labor, since only his monetary value is consecrated.
Gemara
This baraita follows R' Meir, who says: A person does not make a statement of consecration for naught (אין אדם מוציא דבריו לבטלה).
Gemara
Rav holds like the Rabbis — the consecration has no meaningful interpretation, so it takes no effect at all.
Proof The baraita's second clause — a freeman who consecrates himself works and eats — only makes sense under R' Meir, since a person doesn't 'exist for monetary value.'

Me'ilah (Misuse) of a Consecrated Slave

First Tanna

  • Using the consecrated slave = NO me'ilah
  • Slave is equivalent to LAND (קרקע)
  • Me'ilah does not apply to land
  • Hair still attached = part of the slave = land

Rabban Shimon b. Gamliel

  • Me'ilah applies to the slave's HAIR
  • Hair ready to be cut = as if already detached
  • Detached hair = movable property (מטלטלין)
  • Movable property IS subject to me'ilah
Key Agreement Both tannaim agree the slave IS consecrated (against Rav) and that a slave is equivalent to land. They disagree only about hair ready to be cut.

Grapes ready to harvestConsidered already harvested → movable propertyStill attached → status of land
Oath on partial admissionDefendant must swear (movable property rules)No oath (land rules apply)
Underlying principleעומד ליגזז כגזוז דמי — ready to be cut = as if cutStill attached = still land until actually cut
R' Yosei b. R' Chanina The dispute about vines is specifically about grapes READY to harvest — paralleling hair READY to be cut. The principle of 'ready to detach = as if detached' is the common thread.

Slave Emancipation upon Master's Death

Gezeira Shava: 'לה' (maidservant) ↔ 'לה' (wife)
A slave is compared to a wife
↓
Master dies — is the slave freed?
↓
Abba Shaul
Adult slave: freed entirely (can acquire himself)
Minor slave: freed from prohibition, NOT from monetary bond
Opposing View
Both adult and minor are freed completely — just as both adult and minor wives are released by husband's death
↓
R' Yehoshua b. Levi
Halakha IS like Abba Shaul
R' Yochanan
Halakha is NOT like Abba Shaul
The Analogy Wife freed by husband's death from the marital prohibition but not monetary claims → slave freed from prohibition of servitude but monetary bond on a minor remains (Abba Shaul).

Explicit Statement or Inference?

R' Zeira
Did you hear R' Yehoshua b. Levi say this explicitly, or did you infer it from another statement?
R' Yaakov bar Idi
What inference could I have drawn?
R' Zeira
From R' Yehuda HaNasi's gezeira shava comparing slave to wife — you could infer either direction!
R' Zeira
If by inference, deduce the opposite: wife freed at husband's death whether adult or minor → slave too should be freed!
R' Yaakov bar Idi
I heard it explicitly — the halakha is like Abba Shaul.
Methodology R' Zeira systematically verifies whether rulings are based on direct tradition (שמיעה) or inference (שמועה), since inferences from analogies can be drawn in opposite directions.
