⚖️ Blood Stain Found: Does She Render Him Retroactively Impure?

Rav
A woman who finds a blood stain DOES render her husband retroactively impure — in accordance with Rabbi Meir, who equates stain impurity with actual menstrual bleeding.
Shmuel
She does NOT render him retroactively impure. She is only forbidden to him going forward — in accordance with the Rabbis, who distinguish stains from actual flow.
Reish Lakish
I agree with Rav — retroactively impure, per Rabbi Meir. The stain is treated like observed bleeding.
Rabbi Yochanan
I agree with Shmuel — only from this point forward, per the Rabbis. The stain does not reach back in time.
Why the mishna needed to state this Lest you say: since both retroactive impurity AND stain impurity are rabbinic decrees, they cancel each other out — the mishna teaches they do NOT cancel, because a stain IS 'something here' (yesh kan), a tangible, present reality.

🏠 Presumptive Purity: Husband Returning from a Journey

Husband returns from journey — may he assume his wife is tehora (pure) without checking?
↓
General rule: All women have presumptive purity for their husbands — even if he has been away
↓
He found her in a state of purity
Permitted — presumptive purity fully applies
He found her 'occupied with her needs' (ambiguous state)
Forbidden — presumptive purity does not apply
↓
Reish Lakish in name of Rabbi Yehuda Nesia: Presumptive purity for a returning husband applies only when he came and found her in an explicitly pure state

📅 Fixed Cycle Day Arrived While Husband Was Away — Permitted or Forbidden?

Rav Huna
Presumptive purity applies ONLY when her projected veset day had NOT yet arrived. If the cycle day came while he was away, she is FORBIDDEN — the concern for impurity at veset time is Torah law, creating real doubt.
Rabba bar bar Chana
Even if her veset day arrived, she is PERMITTED. The concern for impurity at a projected cycle time is only RABBINIC — and we do not apply a rabbinic stringency against the presumptive purity she already holds.
Rav Ashi (reformulating Rav Huna)
Rav Huna actually said: presumptive purity applies only to a woman whose cycle is determined by BOTH fixed days AND physical sensations — not days alone. Days alone creates Torah-level uncertainty.
The Core Dispute Is the concern that a woman will become impure on her projected veset day a Torah-level obligation (Rav Huna) or a rabbinic safeguard (Rabba bar bar Chana)? This determines whether presumptive purity can override it.

🔁 Two Rabbinic Impurities: Do They Cancel Each Other Out?

Retroactive Impurity (24-Hour Rule)

  • Rabbinic decree
  • No blood visible at the moment of concern
  • Retroactive — reaches back in time
  • Nothing tangible 'here' to point to
  • Argument: maybe this rabbinic and the stain rabbinic cancel?

Blood Stain (Kesem) Impurity

  • Rabbinic decree
  • There IS something here — a visible stain
  • Present and concrete evidence
  • 'Yesh kan' — a real, discoverable object
  • Ruling: these do NOT cancel — the stain is substantive enough to stand

📐 Rabbi Yochanan: Husband Returns 7 Days After Wife's Expected Period

Situation: Woman has a fixed menstrual cycle. Her husband was away for 7 days after the expected onset of her period, then returned.
↓
Step 1: Husband calculates: she presumably experienced her period on the expected day. She is niddah for 7 days.
↓
Step 2: After 7 days of niddah, she presumably counted 7 clean days and then immersed in the mikveh.
↓
Step 3: Husband calculates whether his return falls within the presumed clean window. If so, she is permitted.
↓
Critical limit (Rav Shmuel bar Yeiva's challenge): Does this apply even to a young girl too embarrassed to immerse? Rabbi Abba: Rabbi Yochanan only addressed women with a presumed onset — not women who definitely saw blood.
↓
If she definitely saw blood: FORBIDDEN — 'who is to say she immersed?' Certainty of impurity cannot be overridden by mere uncertainty of purification.
