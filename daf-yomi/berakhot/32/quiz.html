QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Meir forbid minor boys and girls from entering levirate marriage (yibum)?
A. The Torah explicitly requires adults for yibum, just as it does for ḥalitza
B. Minors cannot give legally valid consent under rabbinic law
C. A minor might later be found to be a saris or ailonit, retroactively invalidating the yibum
D. The Rabbis and Rabbi Meir both agree — yibum is forbidden for minors
Correct: C — Rabbi Meir fears that a minor boy could grow into a saris (sexually underdeveloped male) or a girl into an ailonit (sexually underdeveloped female). If yibum was performed and this emerged later, the marriage would be retroactively invalid — a serious halakhic problem Rabbi Meir seeks to prevent.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to the Gemara, from what age does a girl become subject to the laws of niddah (menstrual impurity)?
A. From age 12, when she becomes a halachic adult (bat mitzvah)
B. Only from the time she actually begins menstruating
C. From age 3, by halacha transmitted to Moses from Sinai
D. From one day old, derived from the verse 'and if a woman' (Lev. 15:19)
Correct: D — The Gemara derives from the word 'and' (vav) in 'and if a woman' (Lev. 15:19) that even a one-day-old girl is included in the laws of niddah. This is a scriptural derivation — not a Sinaitic tradition.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What distinction does Rava draw regarding the age of sexual maturity (3-yr girl / 9-yr boy) versus the inclusion of newborns in purity laws?
A. Both are derived from biblical verses in Leviticus
B. Both are halacha transmitted to Moses from Sinai
C. The age of sexual maturity is Sinaitic; inclusion of newborns in niddah/zav is derived from verses
D. The age of sexual maturity is derived from verses; inclusion of newborns is Sinaitic
Correct: C — Rava clarifies the apparent contradiction between baraitot: the halakha that a 3-year-old girl's intercourse is legally significant and that a 9-year-old boy's seminal emission is significant are halacha l'Moshe miSinai. The inclusion of one-day-old infants in niddah and zav laws is derived from biblical verses — each fills a different role.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does the Gemara say that Jewish girls do NOT need to be treated as menstruating from the cradle, unlike Samaritan girls?
A. Jewish girls are biologically less likely to menstruate early than Samaritan girls
B. The verse 'and if a woman' implies awareness and intent, which infants lack — so Jewish infant discharge is not halachic niddah
C. Rabbinic decree specifically exempts Jewish girls from early purity laws
D. The incidents of early menstruation occurred only among Samaritans, not Jews
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that the verse 'and if a woman has an issue' implies the woman is aware of her discharge. A Jewish infant's incidental discharge does not meet this standard. Samaritan girls are treated strictly because Samaritans do not observe the laws of niddah, so all their girls are presumed impure.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What does the mishna mean when it says a Samaritan man imparts impurity to 'the lower bedding like the upper bedding' of a zav?
A. All mattresses under a Samaritan man become equally and severely impure, rendering anyone who touches them impure
B. The lowest mattress under a Samaritan man assumes first-degree impurity, rendering food and drink impure — just like bedding above a zav
C. The bedding becomes second-degree impure, rendering other vessels impure but not food
D. The Samaritan man's bedding has no special impurity since he is not a halachic zav
Correct: B — The Gemara explains: bedding above a zav conveys first-degree impurity, rendering food and drink impure (but not people or vessels). The lowest mattress beneath a Samaritan man who had intercourse with a menstruating woman has this same status — derived from Lev. 15:10: 'whoever touches anything that was under him shall be impure.'
