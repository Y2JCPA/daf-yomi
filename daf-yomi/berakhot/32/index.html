⚖️ Core Dispute: Minors, Minority Concern & Purity Thresholds

Rabbi Meir
Minors may NOT perform ḥalitza or enter levirate marriage — we are concerned even for a minority that they may be sexually underdeveloped (saris / ailonit), rendering the act retroactively invalid.
Rabbis
We follow the majority: most minor boys and girls will develop normally. Yibum is therefore permitted; only ḥalitza requires an adult, since the verse says 'man.'
Gemara
Rabbi Meir's concern also applies to girls who menstruate early — and this is a common minority, confirmed by incidents in Ein Bul and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's household.
Rava
Age thresholds for sexual maturity (3-yr girl / 9-yr boy) are Halacha L'Moshe MiSinai. Inclusion of newborns in niddah and zav laws is derived from verses. There is no contradiction.
Bottom Line Samaritan girls are treated as menstruating from the cradle (Rabbi Meir's view); Jewish girls need not be treated this way, because the verse 'and if a woman' implies awareness and intent — which infants lack.

⚖️ Ḥalitza & Yibum for Minors — The Core Dispute

Baraita
A minor boy and a minor girl may not perform ḥalitza, nor may they enter into levirate marriage (yibum).
Rabbis
We agree regarding ḥalitza — the Torah writes 'man' (Deut. 25:7), requiring adults, and we derive the woman from the man. But why forbid yibum? Follow the majority who develop normally!
Rabbi Meir
A minor boy might become a סָרִיס (saris); a minor girl might be an אַיְלוֹנִית (ailonit). If yibum was performed and this emerges later, it was retroactively a grave violation.
Gemara
But Rabbi Meir is only proven to be concerned for a common minority. Is saris/ailonit common enough? And is early menstruation in girls common enough to justify treating Samaritan girls as menstruating from the cradle?
Resolution The Gemara establishes: early-menstruating girls ARE a common minority (incidents at Ein Bul and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's household confirm it). Rabbi Meir's concern is therefore justified for Samaritan girls.

🔢 Common Minority vs. Uncommon Minority — Rabbi Meir's Concern

Common Minority (מיעוט המצוי)

  • Rabbi Meir IS concerned for this
  • Saris and ailonit — real if rare conditions
  • Girls who menstruate before expected age — confirmed by rabbinic incidents
  • Incident at Ein Bul: baby girl immersed before mother's menstrual cycle appeared
  • Incident at Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's household: same pattern
  • → Samaritan girls treated as menstruating from cradle

Uncommon Minority (מיעוט שאינו מצוי)

  • Gemara challenges: is Rabbi Meir concerned even for this?
  • If not, then early menstruation must be 'common' to justify his ruling
  • The Gemara establishes: it IS common, based on historical incidents
  • Therefore no contradiction: Rabbi Meir acts consistently
  • His concern for early menstruation is not an exceptional leniency

The Rabbis' Counter-Position

  • Follow the majority (רוב) — most minors develop normally
  • No need to restrict yibum on account of saris/ailonit minority
  • Jewish girls: verse teaches us not to worry about early menstruation
  • 'And if a woman' implies awareness — not applicable to infants' incidental discharge
  • Samaritan stringency not required for Jews

🛏️ Samaritan Men & Bedding — Upper vs. Lower Mattresses

כָּל אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע בְּכָל אֲשֶׁר יִהְיֶה תַחְתָּיו יִטְמָא — וַיִּקְרָא טו:י
Mishna Explained The mishna states: Samaritan men impart ritual impurity to the lower bedding like the upper bedding of a zav.

What does this mean?
The lowest mattress beneath a Samaritan man who had intercourse with a menstruating woman assumes first-degree ritual impurity — equivalent to bedding placed above a zav (not second-degree).

Why first-degree?
Samaritan men are presumed to be zavim. Bedding above a zav carries first-degree impurity, rendering food and drink impure — but NOT people or vessels.

Source (Lev. 15:10): 'And whoever touches anything that was under him shall be impure' — this teaches that the item beneath a zav is impure, but only conveys impurity to food and drink, not to humans.
