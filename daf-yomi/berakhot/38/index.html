⚖️ The Central Dispute: When Does Birth Exempt from Ziva?

Rav (via Rav Adda)
Birth purifies a woman from ziva even if she experienced bleeding during the seven days of menstruation — that blood is attributed to labor pains, not ziva.
Levi
Birth purifies only if she bled during the eleven days fit for greater ziva. Menstrual-day blood does not count for this exemption at all.
Abba Shaul (citing Rav)
Birth purifies even if she bled during days fit for lesser ziva (1–2 days). The Torah writes both "days" and "many days" — one for lesser, one for greater ziva.
Rabbi Levi
Only bleeding during eleven days fit for greater ziva qualifies — lesser ziva days alone are insufficient to trigger the birth-exemption.
📌 Background A זָבָה (zava) sees blood outside her normal menstrual cycle. A greater zava sees blood on 3 consecutive ziva days; a lesser zava on 1–2. Birth can retroactively attribute prior bleeding to labor pains — but only under disputed conditions. This daf explores those conditions across multiple tannaitic and amoraic disputes.

📅 How Far Before Birth Can Labor Pains Precede Delivery? Four Views

Rabbi Meir

  • Even 40–50 days before birth
  • Allows very long pre-birth exemption from ziva
  • Basis for his famous 150-day calculation
  • Requires viable birth to apply

Rabbi Yehuda

  • Only within one month (30 days) of birth
  • Blood must span the ninth month of pregnancy
  • Rav Adda: shofar-blast of the 9th month purifies the blood
  • If 2 days in 8th month + 1 day in 9th month → she is pure

Rabbi Yosei & Rabbi Shimon

  • Only within two weeks (14 days) before birth
  • Most restrictive tannaitic opinion
  • Derived from biblical language about childbirth
  • Shmuel derives their reasoning from a verse

Rabbis' Counter to Rabbi Meir

  • If non-viable births counted, labor pains could last a lifetime
  • Rabbi Meir: non-viable births don't grant the exemption
  • This limits the exemption to viable newborns only
  • Prevents an infinite theoretical chain of exemptions

🔄 Rabbi Eliezer vs. Rabbis — Dayyo and Blood During Purity Days

Rabbis
Kal vachomer: If blood emitted while resting (without labor pains) renders her a zava, surely labor-pain blood during her days of purity should also render her a zava!
Rabbi Eliezer
דַּיּוֹ (Dayyo): The conclusion of a kal vachomer cannot exceed the stringency of its source. Since labor-pain blood during niddah days is not ziva, the inference cannot produce a stricter ruling for purity days.
Rabbis (counter-inference)
New inference: if labor-pain blood during menstruation → niddah, then labor-pain blood during the eleven ziva days → she should be a zava!
Rabbi Eliezer
Even if you repeat this argument all day — dayyo still applies. The conclusion can only equal, never surpass, its source's level of stringency.
Rava
Rabbi Eliezer triumphed: The verse "And if a woman…" independently exempts labor-pain blood from ziva — this is a direct scriptural ruling, not merely a kal vachomer conclusion.
⚖️ Key Principle — דַּיּוֹ (Dayyo) The דַּיּוֹ principle (Bava Kamma 25a): the conclusion of a kal vachomer (a fortiori inference) is sufficient when it equals — not exceeds — its source. Rabbi Eliezer applies this to prevent extending ziva-impurity beyond what the Torah explicitly states.

📅 The Pious Men's Wednesday Rule — Reasoning and Calculation

The Practice: The early generations of pious men (חֲסִידִים רִאשׁוֹנִים — chasidim rishonim) engaged in marital relations only on Wednesday nights.
↓
The Concern: They wished to avoid their wives giving birth on Shabbat, which would require a midwife to desecrate Shabbat — a serious transgression they sought to prevent.
↓
Mar Zutra's Derivation: A full-term pregnancy lasts at least 271 days from insemination. This is derived from the verse about Boaz and Ruth — numerical encoding implies 271+ days minimum.
↓
The Arithmetic: Conception on Wednesday night + 271 days = Thursday or later. Conception Sunday → birth day 271 = Shabbat. Conception Monday → day 272 = Shabbat. Conception Tuesday → day 273 = Shabbat. Only Wednesday is safe.
↓
Mar Zutra's Caveat: Even those who hold that a 9-month pregnancy requires a complete nine months acknowledge that birth can occur at the very start of the last day — so Wednesday remains the only reliably safe night.
↓
Connection to Shmuel: Shmuel's ruling that a woman does not give birth before 271 days aligns with and validates the practice of the early pious men — linking halachic science with pious stringency.
