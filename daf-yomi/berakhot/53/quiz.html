Berakhot 53 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's resolution of the two baraitot, to which case does Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra's stringent ruling apply?
A. She examined herself during both Rabbi Yehuda's and Rabbi Yosei's twilight
B. She examined herself during neither Rabbi Yehuda's nor Rabbi Yosei's twilight
C. She examined herself only during Rabbi Yosei's twilight
D. She examined herself only during Rabbi Yehuda's twilight
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi resolves the apparent contradiction by assigning each ruling to a different scenario. Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra's stringent view (she must be concerned) applies when she did not examine herself at all — covering neither twilight window.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the force of Rava's objection to the study-hall explanation about examining oneself during twilight?
A. He proves that Rabbi Yosei's twilight is entirely daytime and irrelevant
B. He argues that even a full twilight examination is inconclusive since she may have bled after removing her hands
C. He rules that only the Rabbis' lenient view is halakhically correct
D. He demonstrates that examining during Rabbi Yehuda's twilight is sufficient and covers Rabbi Yosei's window too
Correct: B — Rava challenges the explanation by noting: unless her hands were continuously in her private parts throughout the entire twilight period, she may have begun bleeding after removing them — so a mid-twilight examination does not actually confirm she was clean.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does the Gemara correct Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's statement about stains and retroactive impurity?
A. He holds stains are more stringent than actual bleeding, requiring impurity from laundering for consecrated items
B. He holds stains render neither herself nor consecrated items impure retroactively from the time of laundering
C. He holds stains render only herself impure retroactively, not consecrated items
D. He holds stains are entirely lenient and do not create retroactive impurity at all
Correct: B — The Gemara rewrites Rabbi Shimon's baraita because the original phrasing created an internal contradiction (stain more stringent than actual blood for consecrated items). The corrected version: he is lenient — she does not render even consecrated items impure retroactively from laundering.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Under what condition does Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar permit a woman to attribute her stain to subsequent bleeding?
A. If the bleeding occurs within 24 hours of discovering the stain
B. If the bleeding occurs within seven days of discovering the stain
C. If the bleeding occurs on the same calendar day she discovered the stain
D. If she examined herself (bedika) between the stain and the bleeding
Correct: C — Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar is more restrictive than the Rabbis on this point: attribution is only permitted if the actual uterine bleeding occurred on the very same day as the discovery of the stain — not merely within a 24-hour window spanning two different days.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is Rabbi Yehoshua's objection to the Mishna's twilight-bleeding cases?
A. He disputes that twilight can ever create both a zava and niddah concern simultaneously
B. He holds twilight is always nighttime, so these cases can never arise in practice
C. He argues the Rabbis should focus on guiding knowledgeable women with common real doubts, not on theoretical edge cases of the confused
D. He disagrees with the 11th-day zava status calculation underlying the Mishna's cases
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehoshua criticizes the Mishna for addressing the rare hypothetical of a woman who lost count and bled at twilight. He urges the Rabbis instead to provide halakhic guidance for competent women who know exactly when they bled but face genuine uncertainty about their status.
