🌅 Two Twilights, One Ruling

Rabbi Yehuda
Twilight (bein hashmashot) is an extended period between sunset and when three stars appear — the entire span is uncertain day or night.
Rabbi Yosei
Twilight is a mere instant — once the sun sets it is immediately night. The extended period Rabbi Yehuda calls twilight is in fact already nighttime.
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
The two twilight periods may not overlap. The gap between when Rabbi Yehuda's twilight ends and Rabbi Yosei's twilight begins creates its own halakhic uncertainty about blood seen then.
Rava
Even if she examined herself during Rabbi Yehuda's twilight, perhaps she saw blood after removing her hands from her private parts — so that examination alone cannot be conclusive!
Core Question When a woman examines herself at twilight, does the examination cover the relevant uncertain window — and which twilight definition governs zava vs. niddah status?

🩸 Stain on a Garment — Scope of Retroactive Impurity

The Rabbis

  • She renders herself retroactively impure from laundering
  • She renders consecrated items she touched retroactively impure from laundering
  • Full retroactive impurity — stain is treated like actual blood-sighting

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar

  • She does not render herself retroactively impure from laundering
  • She also does not render consecrated items impure from the time of laundering
  • Only items she touched close to the time of discovery are retroactively affected

Gemara's Correction

  • The Gemara rewrites Rabbi Shimon's baraita to avoid self-contradiction
  • His original phrasing implied stain is more stringent than seeing blood — an impossibility
  • Corrected: he is actually more lenient on retroactivity, limiting it to items near discovery

📅 Attributing a Stain to Subsequent Bleeding

Step 1: Woman discovers a ketem (red stain) on her garment
↓
Step 2: She subsequently experiences actual uterine bleeding
↓
Rabbis: If bleeding occurs within 24 hours, she may attribute the stain to the bleeding — the stain does not count as an independent zava day
↓
Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar: She may attribute the stain only if bleeding occurred on the same calendar day — not merely within 24 hours across two days
↓
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: Rabbi Shimon 'fixes' her ziva count in easy cases but 'ruins' it in edge cases — e.g. blood seen on day 8 after the stain becomes an additional zava day
↓
Conclusion: Two competing concerns — retroactive ziva counting vs. benefiting from attribution — pull in opposite directions across different scenarios

🔍 Do Stains (Ketamim) Require a Bedika?

Rabbi Zeira
Are stains halakhically equivalent to actual blood-sighting, such that she must perform a clean bedika before she can immerse and return to purity?
Reish Lakish
Yes — the fact that she must examine herself at the end of the seventh day from discovering the stain proves that the stain triggered a full counting period requiring a closing bedika.
Rabbi Zeira
Does that mean, by inference, that stains truly require a bedika? (asked in surprise)
Rabbi Asi
Yes — that is the halakha. A ketem triggers the same purification process as actual blood, including the examination step at the end of the seven clean days.
בְּדִיקָה — Bedika A self-examination confirming no active bleeding, required at the end of the seven clean days before immersion. The Gemara establishes that stains (ketamim) trigger this same requirement, not merely niddah status.
