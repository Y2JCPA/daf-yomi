⚔️ Rav vs. Levi: The Two-Source Debate Resolved

Rav
There is one source of uterine blood. Blood discharged during the days of purity after childbirth can count toward rendering a woman a zava gedola — the womb is a single channel.
Levi
There are two sources of uterine blood. Blood from the days of purity flows from a different channel and cannot render her a zava — the two streams are distinct and do not cross-contaminate.
Rav Ashi
Halakha follows Rav — whether as a leniency or as a stringency. Mareimar ruled identically.
Why It Matters Per Rav: blood in the days of purity that bleeds into the 11 days of ziva can count toward making her a zava gedola (requiring 7 clean days). Per Levi: it cannot — different source, different legal track.

Pregnant woman experiences labor pains accompanied by an emission of blood

During Days of Menstruation (נידה)
She is a niddah — impure as a menstruating woman; all agree
During 11 Days of Ziva
According to Rava: she is pure — labor blood is categorically excluded from ziva status

🩸 Three Amoraic Opinions: Blood During the 11 Days of Ziva

Rav
She is not a zava (not even a lesser zava), but she is like a niddah for that day — forbidden to her husband until she immerses in the evening. (Stricter than Rabbi Yitzchak)
Rabbi Yitzchak
She is completely pure — labor blood during the 11 days of ziva renders her nothing at all, and she is immediately permitted to her husband.
Rava
The mishna means: during days of menstruation → niddah; during days of ziva → pure. This reading is confirmed by a baraita, and it resolves the apparent contradiction.
Baraita Confirmation The baraita explicitly states: if she rested from labor for one day then labored two days (or similar patterns), she is not a zava — confirming that labor attribution protects against ziva status.

📖 Three Verse Derivations — Why Labor Blood ≠ Ziva Blood

The Exclusion — Lev. 15:25

  • "And if a woman has an issue of her blood"
  • The word "her blood" excludes blood emitted due to her child
  • → Labor blood does not render her a zava during the 11 days
  • Both an inclusion and exclusion require logical resolution of which blood is pure/impure

Reish Lakish — Lev. 12:5

  • "She shall observe the blood of purity"
  • Points to another instance where blood is 'observed' but does not render her a zava
  • → Blood during the days of purity after birth follows this pattern
  • A gezerah shavah-style inference

Shmuel's Father — Lev. 12:5

  • "She shall be impure two weeks, as in her menstruation"
  • Not: as in her ziva — the Torah specifies the menstruation track
  • → Labor blood CAN render her a niddah (in days of menstruation)
  • → But it cannot render her a zava — only the niddah track applies

🔥 The Rav Asi Controversy — Did Rav Retract His Ruling?

Rav's original ruling: A woman who emits blood due to labor during the 11 days of ziva is like a niddah for that day — forbidden to her husband until evening immersion.
↓
Sheila bar Avina ruled leniently based on Rav's opinion, permitting such women to their husbands — apparently understanding Rav differently or following a lenient track.
↓
After Rav died, Rav Asi confronted Sheila: "Retract your ruling — Rav himself retracted his opinion before he died!"
↓
Sheila's counter: "If Rav had retracted, he would have told me — I was his close student. You have no standing to override his known ruling." He refused to retract.
↓
Rav Asi's bold claim: "I am Isi ben Yehuda, who is Isi ben Gur Arye... a copper mortar [asita de-nachsheta] that does not forget" — invoking an unbroken chain of reliable tradition to assert his transmission is trustworthy.
↓
Divine consequence? Rav Asi subsequently fell severely ill — alternating between burning fever and chills. The Gemara implies a connection to the aggressive nature of the dispute.
