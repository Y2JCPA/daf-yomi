😤 Mashed-Face Fetus: Is the Mother Impure?

Rabbi Yoḥanan
A woman who discharges a fetus whose face is mashed but not flat — her mother is impure. The basic human form is present.
Reish Lakish
If the face is mashed, the mother is pure. Without discernible features, it does not qualify as a human birth.
Rav Pappi
Clarification: When the face is merely mashed, all agree → impure. The real dispute is a face that is completely flat with no features at all.
Rabbi Ḥiyya
My sons ruled impure — thinking stringency was safe. But ruling impure without cause is itself a leniency: it forbids the woman to her husband unnecessarily!
Paradox of Stringency Ruling 'strictly' (impure) can be a hidden leniency — needlessly prohibiting the husband-wife relationship is itself a halakhic violation.

🔢 Two Backs & Two Spines: Rav vs. Shmuel

Rav — Mother is Pure

  • Such a creature cannot survive in the living world
  • The Torah's prohibition on 'swarming things with two backs' was taught about a fetal anomaly only
  • No living entity actually has two backs — the verse is theoretical
  • A priest with two backs is disqualified as blemished, but none exists in practice
  • Fetus not fit for circumcision on the eighth day → mother pure

Shmuel — Mother is Impure

  • Such a creature can survive and live in the world
  • The Torah's prohibition applies to a real, living entity
  • Rav Ḥanin bar Abba's verse refers to an actually existing being
  • Fit for circumcision on the eighth day → mother impure
  • Rav Yirmeya bar Abba initially ruled in accordance with Shmuel before Rav Huna corrected him

🐍 The Snake-Shaped Fetus: A Forgotten Ruling Recovered

A woman discharges a mass shaped like a snake. Ḥanina (nephew of Rabbi Yehoshua) rules: Mother is impure — a snake's eyes resemble human eyes.
↓
Rabbi Yosef reports the ruling to Rabbi Yehoshua. Rabbi Yehoshua is troubled — he cannot recall what the law should be.
↓
En route to consult Rabban Gamliel, Ḥanina's daughter-in-law greets Rabbi Yehoshua and asks the identical question about a snake-shaped fetus.
↓
Rabbi Yehoshua answers spontaneously: Mother is impure — the snake's eyes resemble human eyes. He immediately realizes this is the very ruling he had forgotten.
↓
Abaye's lesson: A Torah scholar must always state the reason for his ruling — so that when others remind him of his reasoning, he will recognize and recall his own position.
