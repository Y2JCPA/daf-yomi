🏠 Is a House Containing an Afterbirth Impure?

Rabbi Meir
The house is impure. An afterbirth is presumed to contain an offspring. If moved to an outer room in a basin, that room becomes impure.
Sages
If the afterbirth is moved to the outer room, that room is pure — because the offspring presumably disintegrated in the womb before birth.
Rabbi Meir
The outer room is pure because the offspring is not there. But the inner room is impure — the offspring was present there when the afterbirth discharged.
Sages
If the offspring doesn't exist in the outer room, it doesn't exist anywhere. It disintegrated in the womb — it never entered the house at all.
Rabbi Shimon
The house is pure. The offspring is nullified (batel) by the blood of childbirth — it has no independent halakhic existence.
Rav Pappa's Insight Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei agree with Rabbi Shimon because any item whose measure is incomplete is nullified — the afterbirth alone cannot constitute a full human offspring.

🌫️ Rabbi Shimon: When Does Corpse-Dust Convey Impurity?

Base rule: A tefaḥ (ladleful) of dust from a corpse conveys tent-impurity (ohel) — it is treated like the corpse itself
↓
Scenario 1: Dirt fell into the tefaḥ of corpse-dust — Rabbis say impure; Rabbi Shimon says pure
↓
Rabba's explanation: Corpse-dust in its ultimate state follows the law of its initial decomposition state — just as fresh decomposing matter mixed with other material is nullified, so is dust
↓
Source (baraita): Corpse has the halakha of dust only if buried directly in earth — not wrapped in a cloak, wooden coffin, or on brick (foreign matter would mix in, preventing pure dust)
↓
Scenario 2: Corpse-dust became scattered (less than tefaḥ remains intact) — Rabbis say impure; Rabbi Shimon says pure
↓
Rabbi Shimon's principle: An item that left its designated measure is nullified — both scenarios require stating because each has a distinct logical basis

🧬 Mashed Fetus in Gestational Sac — Impurity Status?

Reish Lakish
A fetus mashed in its amniotic fluid by violent shaking is like a deformed corpse — it does not convey tent-impurity (tum'at ohel).
Rabbi Yoḥanan
From where do we derive that a deformed corpse is pure? You cannot simply cite Rabbi Shabbtai's ruling without first establishing the underlying principle.
Reish Lakish
The source is Rabbi Yitzḥak of Migdala — remains that have lost their recognizable form no longer convey impurity in the same manner as an intact corpse.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Rabbi Shimon concedes that the mother is impure with the impurity of childbirth — because the verse states 'if a woman bears seed and gives birth', she is impure seven days regardless.
Key Tension The dispute turns on whether loss of form (through mashing or deformation) strips a body of its corpse-impurity status — and whether a fetus in a sac is even counted as a "corpse" in the first place.
