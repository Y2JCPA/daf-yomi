Berakhot 27 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
After Rav Yosef's correction, who reported the case where one twin remained 33 days in the womb after the other was born?
A. Rabbi Yoḥanan
B. Rav Aḥa son of Rav Avira
C. Shmuel
D. Rabbi Avin bar Rav Adda
Correct: B — Rav Aḥa son of Rav Avira reported the 33-day case (twins). Rav Yosef corrected Rabba bar bar Ḥana, noting that Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rav Aḥa's statements had been transposed — the Gemara reverses them.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Shimon rule that a house containing an afterbirth is pure?
A. An afterbirth is not halakhically considered part of the offspring
B. The offspring is nullified by the blood of childbirth
C. The afterbirth must be removed within 24 hours to convey impurity
D. A gestational sac cannot survive outside the womb
Correct: B — Rabbi Shimon holds that the offspring — which would make the house impure as a corpse — is nullified (batel) by the blood of childbirth. It therefore never achieves independent existence and cannot convey impurity.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Rabba's explanation for Rabbi Shimon's ruling that corpse-dust mixed with dirt is pure?
A. The dirt outnumbers the dust, causing nullification by majority
B. The dust-state follows the law of the corpse's initial decomposition state
C. Dust from a cemetery is treated differently than dust from a single corpse
D. Only an intact corpse can convey impurity — dust is never impure
Correct: B — Rabba explains: just as a freshly decomposing corpse mixed with other matter has its impurity nullified, so too dust (the corpse's final state) follows that same rule. The final state mirrors the initial state.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rabbi Meir, what is the status of the room to which an afterbirth is moved in a basin?
A. That room is impure because the afterbirth is now present
B. That room is pure, because the offspring is not present there
C. The entire house and courtyard become impure
D. It depends on whether the basin has a lid
Correct: B — Rabbi Meir concedes that the room to which the afterbirth is moved is pure — because the offspring is not there. He uses this to argue that the original room is impure precisely because the offspring was once there.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Regarding a fetus mashed in its amniotic fluid, what position does Reish Lakish take?
A. It conveys full corpse-impurity like an intact fetus
B. It does not convey tent-impurity, being like a deformed corpse
C. The mother is pure but the house is impure
D. It depends on whether the gestational sac was intact
Correct: B — Reish Lakish rules that a fetus mashed in its amniotic fluid is like a corpse that was deformed — it does not convey tent-impurity (tum'at ohel). Rabbi Yoḥanan challenges him to provide a source for this principle.
