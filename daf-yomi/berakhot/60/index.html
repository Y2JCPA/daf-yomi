🧥 Gentile Borrower: Is Rav's Ruling an Error?

Rav
If a woman loaned her garment to a gentile woman who already experienced blood, she may attribute the stain to the gentile — just as she could to a menstruating woman.
Rav Sheshet
Rav said that in his sleep! The baraita clearly permits attributing the stain to any gentile — not only one who previously experienced blood.
Rava
You misread Rabbi Meir's intent. He is being lenient — permitting attribution to any gentile because all gentile women are presumed to experience blood regularly.
Baraita
A woman who loaned her garment to a gentile woman may not attribute the stain to the gentile. But Rabbi Meir says: she may attribute it to her.
Resolution Rava: Rabbi Meir permits attribution to any gentile because all gentile women are presumed to experience blood regularly — this is a leniency, not a stringency.

👗 Borrowers: May the Lender Attribute the Stain?

✅ May Attribute

  • Lesser zava observing one clean day for each discharge day
  • Woman counting seven clean days who has not yet immersed
  • Woman after childbirth in days of blood of purity
  • Zava on her first day of discharge (all agree)
  • Any gentile woman — according to Rabbi Meir (Rava's reading)

❌ May Not Attribute

  • Gentile woman with no history of blood (Rav's original view)
  • Woman already impure from a prior blood stain — per Rabbi Yehuda bar Livai
  • A woman with no known susceptibility to discharge

⚖️ Disputed

  • Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: Only the borrower's status is ruined
  • Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: Attribution ruins both women's status when borrower observes day-for-day
  • Already-impure borrower: Rav Ashi and others resolve the baraita differently

🛤️ Two Travelers, Two Paths — Rav Ḥisda's Analogy

Case: One impure person + one pure person each walked one of two paths — one pure path, one impure path
↓
Neither knows which path they took
↓
Rav Ḥisda
Analogous to dispute about blood attribution — parallels Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel vs. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
Rav Adda's Objection
Not comparable! The zava can immerse to become pure — she has a clear path to purity unlike the traveler
↓
Rav Ḥisda's response: Even the zava is not fully pure until she immerses — and yet Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi is already stringent with her. The analogy holds.
↓
Rabbi Yosei son of Rabbi Ḥanina: Even if one person is impure only by doubt, the ruling still applies — both travelers are considered doubtfully impure

🪨 Rabbi Neḥemya: Non-Susceptible Items

כָּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְקַבֵּל טֻמְאָה — אֵינָהּ מִטַּמְּאָה בִּכְתָמִים
Rabbi Neḥemya's Principle Any item that is not susceptible to ritual impurity does not cause a woman to become impure from a blood stain found upon it.

Applications ruled by Rabbi Neḥemya:
• Blood on a stone bench or bathhouse bench → pure (stone not susceptible)
• Blood on the exterior of an inverted earthenware vessel → pure (only interior airspace is susceptible)
• Blood on small rags under 3×3 fingerbreadths → pure (too small to be susceptible to impurity)

Rav's ruling: The halakha follows Rabbi Neḥemya.
But: Rav Naḥman cites a baraita: actual incidents brought before the Sages were ruled otherwise — indicating a tannaic dispute.
