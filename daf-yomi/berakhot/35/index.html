🦠 Rava vs Rav Yehuda — Leper's Ziva and Carrying-Impurity

Rava
The verse mentions the word 'issue' twice. This extra mention teaches that a leper's second sighting of ziva conveys impurity through carrying — just like a full-fledged zav. A special verse was required because a kal va-chomer could be refuted.
Rav Yehuda of Diskarta
How do you know the verse refers to a zav who is also a leper? Perhaps it speaks of an ordinary zav alone — and the a fortiori inference you assumed was flawed is actually valid, so no verse was needed for the ordinary zav!
Abaye
The verse 'This is the law of the zav' (Lev. 15:32) already covers the ordinary full-fledged zav completely. Therefore the doubled 'issue' must be adding the leper-zav — otherwise the verse is entirely redundant.
Key Verse (35a) וְזֹאת תּוֹרַת הַזָּב — 'This is the law of the zav' (Leviticus 15:32) already accounts for the ordinary zav; the doubled expression 'issue' must therefore add the leper who is also a zav.

🔍 Rav Huna — First Sighting of Ziva Is Always Impure

זֹאת תּוֹרַת הַזָּב וַאֲשֶׁר תֵּצֵא מִמֶּנּוּ שִׁכְבַת זָרַע (ויקרא טו:לב)
Rav Huna (Amud A) The first sighting of ziva imparts ritual impurity even when the discharge occurred due to circumstances entirely beyond one's control.

Derivation: The verse juxtaposes the zav with one who experienced a seminal emission — just as seminal emission always conveys impurity regardless of circumstances, so too does the first sighting of ziva.

Gemara's challenge from Zavim 2:2: The mishna says one examines a man after his first sighting — implying it might be exempt if involuntary?

Resolution: The examination after the first sighting is not to determine impurity (which exists regardless) but rather to determine his liability for an offering. The Rabbis and Rabbi Eliezer dispute whether even the third sighting requires examination for offering purposes.

🤱 Post-Partum Blood — Beit Shammai vs Beit Hillel

Beit Shammai

  • Woman completes her days of post-partum impurity but has not yet immersed
  • Her blood is like saliva or urine — secondary impurity only
  • Imparts impurity only while moist; once dry it is inert
  • Reasoning: Purity depends on days alone — once days expire, blood is weakened
  • Exception: woman who gave birth as a zava — her blood remains fully impure whether moist or dry

Beit Hillel

  • Woman completes her days of post-partum impurity but has not yet immersed
  • Her blood retains full menstrual impurity status
  • Imparts impurity whether moist or dry
  • Reasoning: Purity depends on both days and immersion — without immersion, full impurity remains
  • Proof: A niddah who did not immerse after 7 days and saw more blood is fully impure — same principle applies here

🔬 Rav vs Levi — One Source or Two Sources of Blood?

Woman's body during and after childbirth
Are pure blood (days of purity) and impure blood (days of impurity) physically distinct?
↓
Rav — One Source
Pure and impure blood both emerge from the same anatomical source. The Torah renders blood impure during days of impurity and pure during days of purity — a legal transformation, not a physical one.
Levi — Two Sources
There are two distinct sources in the body. The impure-blood source is active during days of impurity; the pure-blood source is active during days of purity. The two do not operate simultaneously.
Practical Difference: Woman continuously bleeds from within her days of impurity into her days of purity without stopping
↓
Rav → Stringent
Continuous flow = blood is still from the single source; treat as impure during the days of purity as well
Levi → Also Stringent
Continuous flow indicates the impure-source is still active; treat as impure during the days of purity as well
