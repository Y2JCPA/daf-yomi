QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Huna (35a), does the first sighting of ziva impart ritual impurity if the discharge occurred due to circumstances beyond the person's control?
A. No — uncontrolled discharge never imparts impurity for any type of emission
B. Yes — the first sighting of ziva imparts impurity regardless of whether it was voluntary or involuntary
C. Only if it is followed by a second sighting within three days
D. Only if the person is also afflicted with leprosy
Correct: B — Rav Huna derives from Leviticus 15:32 — which juxtaposes the zav with one who experienced a seminal emission — that just as seminal emission always imparts impurity regardless of circumstances, so too does the first sighting of ziva, even when it is entirely beyond one's control.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the central dispute between Rav and Levi regarding blood emitted by a woman during her days of purity after childbirth?
A. Whether such blood can make a person a zava or only a niddah
B. Whether the days of purity are counted from birth or from the end of bleeding
C. Whether pure and impure blood come from one shared source (Rav) or from two anatomically distinct sources (Levi)
D. Whether immersion is required at the end of the days of impurity or only at the end of the days of purity
Correct: C — Rav holds that both pure and impure blood emerge from the same single source; the Torah legally renders blood impure during days of impurity and pure during days of purity. Levi holds that the two types of blood have physically separate, alternating sources in the body.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What do Beit Shammai say about blood seen by a woman who completed her days of post-partum impurity but did not yet immerse in a mikveh?
A. It imparts full menstrual impurity whether moist or dry, since she has not yet immersed
B. It imparts impurity only while moist — like saliva or urine — since the days of impurity have expired
C. It imparts no impurity at all, since her days of impurity are complete
D. It imparts impurity to food and drink but not to people or vessels
Correct: B — Beit Shammai hold that purity of post-partum blood depends on the days alone. Once the days of impurity have passed, the blood is legally 'weakened' and imparts impurity only while moist — similar to saliva or urine — not when dry.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rav Huna (35b), what physical characteristic distinguishes the discharge of ziva from the discharge of semen?
A. Ziva is odorless; semen has a distinctive smell
B. Ziva is thick like paste; semen is thin like water
C. Ziva emerges from dead (flaccid) flesh; semen emerges from living (erect) flesh
D. Ziva occurs only at night; semen can occur day or night
Correct: C — Rav Huna teaches that ziva discharge comes from 'dead flesh' — i.e., when the penis is flaccid — whereas semen emerges from 'living flesh' — i.e., when the penis is erect. This physical distinction is central to understanding the different legal categories.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Eliezer (35a) rule that one examines a zav even after his third discharge of ziva?
A. To determine if the discharge was involuntary, which would exempt him from the impurity of a full zav
B. To determine if the discharge was involuntary, which could exempt him from the obligation to bring an offering
C. To determine whether he must count seven clean days before immersion
D. To determine whether his discharge renders his bedding and seat impure as a primary source
Correct: B — Rabbi Eliezer interprets the word 'et' in the verse as adding a third sighting that can still be examined. If the third discharge occurred due to circumstances beyond one's control, he may be exempt from the obligation to bring an atonement offering, even though his zav-status and impurity are already established.
