🤰 Why Is a Pregnant Woman's Time Sufficient? Competing Reasons

Rav Pappi
A miscarriage is not considered a proper birth. Therefore, blood emitted during labor pain is not birth blood — the Merciful One declares it pure, and it is not treated as zava blood.
Rav Pappa
The reason is that a pregnant woman's head and limbs feel heavy. This compromised physical state disrupts her normal menstrual function, so blood seen during pregnancy is not typical niddah.
Rabbi Yirmeya
What if she saw blood, and only afterward her pregnancy became known? At the moment of bleeding, was she already in the exempted physical state — or not yet?
Rabbi Zeira
Per Rav Pappa's logic: if she saw blood before her pregnancy was evident, her time is not sufficient. The heavy-limbs state had not yet set in at the time of bleeding.
Practical Difference Rav Pappi's reason (type of blood) exempts only labor-related blood. Rav Pappa's reason (physical state) exempts all pregnancy bleeding — but only once the bodily change is established, creating a dispute about early pregnancy.

🍼 Why Don't Nursing Women Menstruate? Three Opinions

Rabbi Meir

  • Menstrual blood spoils and is converted into milk
  • Nursing actively transforms the blood — no surplus for menstruation
  • When nursing ends, blood returns and normal cycles resume
  • Verse interpreted as referring to menstrual blood itself

Rabbi Yosei

  • Her limbs become dislocated at childbirth
  • Her spirit does not return for twenty-four months
  • Physical disruption — not a blood conversion — prevents menstruation
  • Verse interpreted as referring to semen or sprinkling water

Rabbi Yehuda & Rabbi Shimon

  • Time is sufficient for all 24 months as a fixed window
  • After 24 months, normal retroactive liability resumes
  • Even nursing for 4 or 5 years: revert to full liability after month 24
  • Agree with Rabbi Yosei's physical disruption framework

⚖️ Rabbi Eliezer vs. The Rabbis — Three Skipped Cycles Rabbi Eliezer holds: any woman for whom three typical menstrual cycles (90 days) pass without bleeding acquires the status of an elderly woman — when she next sees blood, her time is sufficient.

The Rabbis disagree, arguing that the cases Rabbi Eliezer cited involved exigent circumstances — years of famine, or women who handled many ritual objects. Proof from exceptional situations cannot establish standard halakha.

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi initially ruled like Rabbi Eliezer. Upon recalling the Rabbis' dissent, he annulled his ruling. The Gemara's conclusion: the halakha was stated neither in accordance with Rabbi Eliezer nor in accordance with the Rabbis — the matter remains undecided.
