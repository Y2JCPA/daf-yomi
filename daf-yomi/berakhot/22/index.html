🩸 Core Dispute: Can a Woman Be Pure While Her Uterus Is Impure?

First Tanna (R. Eliezer)
It IS the manner of a woman to discharge blood in tissue — she is impure as a menstruating woman.
Rabbis
It is NOT normal to discharge blood embedded in tissue. The blood resembles a wound discharge — she is pure.
Rava
Both sides actually agree it is not normal. The real dispute: can a woman be pure while the uterine location is impure? R. Eliezer says yes — split status is possible.
Rava's Reframing Rava shifts the entire dispute from 'is this blood normal?' to a deeper question: can personhood and bodily location have separate impurity statuses? Rabbi Eliezer permits this split; the Rabbis do not.

💧 Rabba vs. Rav Huna: Semen Extracted With a Wood Sliver

Rabba
If a man extracts semen from his penis with a wood sliver — does he become impure as a ba'al keri?
Rav Huna
He is pure. Impurity requires enough semen to block the tip of the penis. A sliver cannot extract that minimum measure.
Rabba
But the baraita compares a zav to one with seminal emission — a seminal emission negates the seven clean days! If the reason for impurity is merely contact with emitted semen, why should it negate the count?
Rav Huna
A zav's emission always contains traces of ziva — that is why it negates the count. But pure semen negates only one day; full ziva negates all seven. This is a Torah edict.
Torah Edict Pure ziva emission negates the entire seven-day clean count. Semen mixed with ziva negates only one day. These are distinct halakhic categories by divine decree.

📖 R. Elazar b. R. Tzadok's Two Real-Life Cases

תלמוד בבלי, מסכת נידה כב
Historical Precedent Case 1 — Red Shell-Like Items: A woman repeatedly discharged items resembling red shells. The Sages consulted doctors, who said: "She has a wound in her womb that produces these shells." The Sages ruled her pure.

Case 2 — Red Hair-Like Items: A woman discharged items resembling red hairs. Doctors again explained it was a wound, not blood. The Sages ruled her pure.

These real cases confirm the mishna's categories are not theoretical — and that the water-dissolution test adjudicates ambiguous red items when medical explanation is unavailable.
