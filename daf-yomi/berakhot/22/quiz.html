Berakhot 22 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava's reinterpretation, what is the true core of the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis regarding blood found in a piece of discharged tissue?
A. Whether it is normal for women to discharge tissue containing blood
B. Whether a woman can be ritually pure while the uterine location is impure
C. Whether dry blood can transmit impurity at all
D. Whether a verbal analogy applies to blood found outside the body
Correct: B — Rava reframes the dispute: both sides agree it is abnormal for blood to emerge embedded in tissue. The real question is whether personhood and bodily location can have split impurity statuses — Rabbi Eliezer permits this split (woman pure, place impure); the Rabbis do not.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Rav Huna rule that a man who extracts semen with a wood sliver is ritually pure?
A. Only involuntary seminal emissions cause impurity, not deliberate ones
B. The minimum measure required for impurity — enough to block the tip of the penis — cannot be extracted with a sliver
C. Semen touched by a foreign object loses its impurity-transmitting status
D. The baraita comparing a zav and ba'al keri excludes artificially extracted semen
Correct: B — Rav Huna rules that seminal emission impurity requires a minimum measure: enough semen to block the tip of the penis. A wood sliver cannot extract this amount, so no impurity is incurred.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How is the status of a red shell-like or hair-like item discharged by a woman determined?
A. Red color alone renders the woman impure — no further testing required
B. The item is soaked in lukewarm water: if it dissolves it is blood (impure); if it remains intact she is pure
C. A doctor must examine the womb for wounds before any ruling is made
D. The item is compared against known blood samples by the local beit din
Correct: B — Reish Lakish teaches (confirmed in a baraita) that such items must be soaked in lukewarm water. Dissolution indicates it is blood, rendering the woman impure; if the item remains intact, she is pure.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is Rabbi Meir's reason (as explained by Rav Yehuda in Shmuel's name) for why a woman who discharges an animal-form fetus has the same birth-impurity as one who gives birth to a human?
A. The Torah states that any birth — human or animal — causes impurity for the mother
B. A verbal analogy on the shared term יצר ('formed') links land animals and humans, making their halakhot equivalent
C. Doctors confirmed that animal-form fetuses are genuine human pregnancies gone wrong
D. The mishna explicitly equates animal-form fetuses with human births in all respects
Correct: B — The word יצר ('formed') appears in Genesis 2:7 for man and in Genesis 2:19 for land animals, and both usages are 'free' (superfluous) — enabling a valid gezerah shavah that equates their halakhic status regarding birth impurity.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why cannot a woman who discharges a sea-monster-form fetus be compared to human birth via verbal analogy?
A. Sea monsters are not mentioned anywhere in the laws of impurity in the Torah
B. The term ברא ('created') used for sea monsters is not free on both sides, unlike יצר ('formed') for man and animals
C. The Rabbis categorically excluded sea creatures from all impurity discussions
D. Sea monsters are considered pure animals, so no impurity can derive from their form
Correct: B — According to Rabbi Yishmael's principle, a verbal analogy requires the term to be 'free' (superfluous) on both sides. The term יצר is free on both sides for man and animals, making their analogy valid. The term ברא for sea monsters is free on only one side — insufficient for a binding gezerah shavah.
