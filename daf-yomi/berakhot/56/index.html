🦎 Dried Sheretz — Does Skeletal Integrity Preserve Impurity?

Reish Lakish
A sheretz that dried out but whose skeleton is intact still transmits impurity. The verse teaches impurity applies when the carcass is in a state similar to death — and an intact skeleton qualifies as that state.
Baraita (R. Shimon b. Yoḥai)
"Whoever touches them when they are dead" — 'of them' would give impurity from partial contact; 'them' requires complete wholeness. The same logic supports Reish Lakish: wholeness is the criterion, not moisture.
Rava
Practically: the lizards of Meḥoza — when their skeleton is intact, they are impure. This confirms that skeletal integrity is the operative test, even after drying.
Gemara objection
But a burnt sheretz on olives renders them impure (Teharot 9:9)? That baraita speaks of a scorched but form-intact carcass — not one reduced to ash. Consistent with Reish Lakish!
Principle A dried sheretz retaining its skeletal structure is treated as whole — and transmits impurity. Complete incineration that destroys form removes impurity entirely.

💧 Bodily Fluids — The Saliva Rule (R. Shimon b. Yoḥai via R. Yoḥanan)

✅ Transmits Severe Impurity

  • Saliva of a zav — paradigm case: gathers in mouth, expelled by will, would return if not expelled
  • Ziva of a zav — issues from a place of inherent impurity
  • Blood (niddah/zava) — from the uterus, classic source of impurity

❌ Does NOT Transmit Severe Impurity

  • Blood from mouth of a zav — does not first gather and is not expelled voluntarily
  • Blood/fluid from the opening of the penis of a zav — fails the saliva analogy
  • Urine of a zav — excluded by verse: 'it' (ziva only), not urine

⚖️ Disputed — Breast Milk

  • Gathers in the ducts before release ✓
  • Once expelled, does NOT return to the body ✗
  • Masters of the Mishnah: milk is pure — fails the 'return' criterion
  • R. Shimon b. Yoḥai's formulation requires all three conditions

🧺 Retroactive Impurity — Alleyway and Garment Rules

Scenario A — Sheretz found in an alleyway: All items that passed through are rendered impure retroactively
↓
Retroactivity runs back to: the last time the alleyway was examined and found clean, or the last time it was swept
↓
Core dilemma: Does sweeping = examining? A sweeper may have swept without looking carefully.
↓
If sweeping = examining: sheretz in a hole in the ground → examiner would not have seen it → items from that time are pure
↓
If sweeping ≠ examining: the hole does not change anything — items may still be impure from that time
↓
Scenario B — Blood stain found on garment: Retroactive impurity from last laundering or last examination
↓
If laundered but not examined — same dilemma: does laundering create a presumption of having examined?
↓
R. Aḥa's remedy: Launder again! R. Yehuda HaNasi agrees: a pre-laundering stain looks different from a post-laundering stain — inspect the appearance to determine timing
↓
R. Meir's rationale: The retroactive impurity rule was instituted to prevent women from being careless about inspecting their garments

🏚️ Samaritan Houses and Credibility — The Governing Principle

כָּל דָּבָר שֶׁהֵם חֲשׁוּדִין עָלָיו — אֵין מְקַבְּלִין מֵהֶם עָלָיו
כּוּתִים (Samaritans) — Trusted or Not? Tent (ohel) impurity of Samaritan houses: A Samaritan home transmits corpse-impurity by means of a tent — because they bury their stillborn children inside their homes.

Rabbi Yehuda dissents: They bury stillborns outside, not inside — therefore no tent-impurity applies to their houses.

Where Samaritans ARE believed:
• "We buried stillborn children here" — that place is impure
• "We did not bury them there" — that place is pure
• Testifying about which of their produce is demai

Where Samaritans are NOT believed:
• Overhanging boughs (ohel over a grave)
• Protrusions jutting into a public domain
• Any matter they are suspected of not observing
