5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yoḥanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai, what three criteria must a bodily fluid meet in order to transmit severe ritual impurity?
A. It must be blood-red, it must emerge from an impure body part, and it must be at least an olive-bulk in volume
B. It must first gather together inside the body, then come out voluntarily, and if not expelled it would return inside
C. It must come from a zav or zava, it must be warm to the touch, and it must be expelled through normal bodily pressure
D. It must be a liquid, it must originate from a living person, and it must be emitted during a state of tumah
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan derives the rule from saliva as the paradigm: (1) it gathers in the mouth, (2) is expelled by will, and (3) if not expelled it returns inside. Blood from the mouth or penis fails these criteria — it does not gather or return — and is therefore pure.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Reish Lakish rules that a dried sheretz carcass still transmits impurity under what specific condition?
A. Only if it was immersed in lukewarm water for 24 hours and restored to a moist state
B. Only if a halakhic expert examined it and declared it to still resemble a living creature
C. As long as its skeletal bone structure remains intact, even if completely desiccated
D. Only if it was found in a place frequented by Jews and within three days of drying out
Correct: C — Reish Lakish rules that skeletal integrity is the operative criterion: a dried sheretz whose bone framework is intact is still considered 'whole' and retains its impurity. Rava applies this practically to the lizards of Meḥoza. Complete destruction (e.g., burning to ash) is required for the impurity to lapse.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to the Gemara's resolution of the dilemma about retroactive impurity, from what point in time does a blood stain discovered on a garment render previously-handled items impure?
A. From the moment the stain was actually created, which must be established through testimony
B. Only from the moment of discovery — retroactive impurity does not apply to blood stains at all
C. From the time the woman last examined the garment and found it clean, or from the last laundering
D. Only from the beginning of the woman's most recent menstrual cycle
Correct: C — The mishna and baraita of Rabbi Meir establish that retroactive impurity runs back to the last verified point of purity — either a direct examination that found the garment clean, or the last laundering. This rule was instituted to ensure women examine their garments carefully.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
The mishna rules that blood stains from garments originating in the town of Rekem are ritually pure. What is the governing reason for this ruling?
A. Rekem was a Levitical city whose residents were known for extraordinary purity observance
B. The Sages issued a blanket decree that blood stains found on garments outdoors are always pure
C. Most residents of Rekem are gentiles, and blood stains of gentile women do not create niddah impurity
D. Rekem's water supply had purifying properties that neutralized blood stains on contact
Correct: C — The principle is: follow the majority. Since most residents of Rekem are gentiles, a blood stain found on a garment there is attributed to a gentile woman. The blood of a gentile woman does not create niddah or zavah impurity under Torah law.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Regarding the credibility of Samaritans (Kutim) in matters of ritual purity, what is the governing principle stated at the end of Daf 56b?
A. Samaritans are fully credible in all matters of purity because the Sages ruled they are true converts
B. Samaritans are never credible in matters of purity because they are suspected of violating all of the Torah
C. Samaritans are credible only when two witnesses corroborate their testimony
D. In any matter of halakha that Samaritans are suspected of not fulfilling, they are not deemed credible to testify about it
Correct: D — The mishna states the governing principle explicitly: Samaritan credibility is matter-specific. Where they are suspected of non-compliance (e.g., overhanging boughs, protrusions into the public domain), their testimony is rejected. Where they comply (e.g., burying stillborns, demai tithing), they are believed.
