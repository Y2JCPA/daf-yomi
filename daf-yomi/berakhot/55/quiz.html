QUESTION 1 OF 5
What is the practical halakhic difference between Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yoḥanan regarding corpse flesh impurity?
A. Reish Lakish says only moist flesh is impure; Rabbi Yoḥanan says both moist and dry flesh are impure
B. They disagree about whether detached flesh transmits impurity or only flesh attached to the corpse
C. Reish Lakish holds that flesh crumbled to dust is pure; Rabbi Yoḥanan holds it remains impure
D. Reish Lakish derives the law from Numbers 19; Rabbi Yoḥanan derives it from Leviticus 11
Correct: C — Both agree dried flesh is impure. The dispute concerns flesh so desiccated it crumbles: Reish Lakish (deriving from Leviticus on creeping things) holds it becomes pure like dust; Rabbi Yoḥanan (from Numbers 19:16, equating flesh to bone) holds it remains impure as long as recognizable.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rav Adda bar Ahava's final ruling, what two conditions must a detached body part satisfy to transmit corpse impurity?
A. It must be larger than an olive-bulk and still moist
B. It must be created with the person at birth AND its root must not renew itself if removed
C. It must be a hard substance that does not decompose within 30 days
D. It must be connected to bone and contain blood at time of death
Correct: B — Rav Adda bar Ahava's criterion: like bone — (1) present at birth, and (2) if removed, does not grow back. Teeth fail condition 1 (they erupt later); hair and nails fail condition 2 (they regrow). Flesh satisfies both — Mar bar Rav Ashi clarifies that regrown tissue is new flesh, not restoration.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why did the Sages decree that human skin is ritually impure, despite Ulla ruling it pure by Torah law?
A. Because skin contains blood vessels that themselves transmit impurity
B. Because the skin of a corpse does not renew its root, placing it in the same category as bone
C. Lest a person fashion a mat or seat cover from human skin — a disrespectful use of human remains
D. It is actually impure by Torah law; Ulla's statement is a minority opinion rejected by the Gemara
Correct: C — Ulla explicitly states skin is pure by Torah law. The rabbinic decree was enacted as a protective measure (גזירה) against the disgraceful act of tanning human skin for use as a mat — connecting the decree to practical concern for human dignity.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why is the scapegoat cited as a counter-argument in the derivation of ziva's impurity?
A. The scapegoat's handler becomes impure, proving indirect transmission is possible
B. The scapegoat is listed alongside a zav in Leviticus 15, creating a verbal analogy
C. The scapegoat causes impurity to those who handle it yet is itself pure — refuting the a fortiori argument that ziva must be impure since it defiles the zav
D. The scapegoat is used to show that only three emissions of ziva cause impurity
Correct: C — The a fortiori argument runs: ziva causes impurity to the zav, so surely the discharge itself is impure. The Gemara rejects this: the scapegoat (Leviticus 16:26) causes impurity to its handlers yet is itself pure — proving that causing defilement does not imply the cause is itself defiled.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Of the nine fluids of the zav listed in the baraita, which three are considered ritually pure?
A. Tears, sweat, and nasal fluids
B. Urine, mucus, and phlegm
C. Sweat, foul secretion from an infected wound, and excrement
D. Milk of a zava, wound blood, and saliva
Correct: C — The baraita enumerates nine fluids and explicitly states that sweat, the foul discharge from an infected wound, and excrement are ritually pure. The other six — ziva, saliva (plus phlegm/mucus), urine, tears, wound blood, and milk of a zava — carry varying degrees of impurity.
