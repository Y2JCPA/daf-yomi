🔍 Niddah 8 — Counting Rabbi Eliezer's Rulings

הלכה כרבי אליעזר בארבעה מקומות
Central Tension Rabbi Elazar declares halakha follows Rabbi Eliezer in exactly four cases. The Gemara mounts three successive challenges — mi'un, balsam/Shevi'it, and havdala in the Amida — each resolved by distinguishing speakers, sub-cases, or attributions. The daf then opens the mishna's definition of 'virgin' status and a pregnant woman's presumptive halakhic standing.

🗣️ Can We Count Beyond Four? Three Challenges to Rabbi Elazar

Rabbi Elazar
Halakha follows Rabbi Eliezer in four cases — all within Seder Teharot. No more, no less.
Gemara (Challenge 1)
But the mishna says Rabbi Eliezer rules the court instructs a minor to refuse her husband for levirate purposes — that's a 5th case!
Resolution
That ruling belongs to Rabbi Elazar (the amora), not Rabbi Eliezer (the tanna). Two different sages — no contradiction.
Gemara (Challenge 2)
What about balsam having Sabbatical Year sanctity? Rabbi Eliezer holds it is fruit — another case in Seder Zera'im, not Teharot!
Rabbi Pedat
That mishna may reflect the Rabbis' own view — they agree sap of a fruit is like the fruit itself. No proof it follows Rabbi Eliezer specifically.
Gemara (Challenge 3)
Rabbi Eliezer rules havdala goes in the thanksgiving blessing — that's a halakhic ruling in Seder Moed. A 5th case again!
Rabbi Abba
Rabbi Eliezer stated that ruling in the name of Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel — it is not his own independent opinion and does not count.
Critical Distinction Rabbi Eliezer (Tanna, 1st–2nd c.) ≠ Rabbi Elazar (Amora, 3rd c.). Confusing them generates false contradictions throughout this sugya.

🙏 Where Does Havdala Belong in the Amida?

Rabbi Akiva

  • Recite havdala as a separate, independent blessing
  • Inserted into the 4th blessing: 'Who graciously grants knowledge'
  • Rationale: knowledge and distinction (havdala) are thematically linked

Rabbi Eliezer

  • Recite havdala within the thanksgiving blessing (Modim)
  • Gratitude for Shabbat's departure belongs with general thanks
  • Cited in name of Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel — not his own ruling

Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel

  • Pray the complete 18 blessings even on Shabbat/Yom Tov
  • Havdala cannot be squeezed into another blessing — it requires the full Amida
  • Cited in the name of his forefathers

👧 Mi'un (Refusal) Enabling Yibbum — Step by Step

Deceased brother dies childless, leaving two wives:
an adult widow and a minor wife
↓
Problem: The surviving brother wants to perform yibbum (levirate marriage) with the adult widow
↓
Without Mi'un
Minor wife remains formally married to surviving brother — performing yibbum with adult sister is forbidden (marrying two co-wives)
Rabbi Eliezer's Ruling
Court instructs the minor to perform mi'un (refusal), which retroactively dissolves her marriage
↓
Surviving brother may now perform yibbum with the adult widow — the legal obstacle is removed

🤰 Pregnant Woman's Presumptive Status (Chazakah)

Starting point: A woman has an established chazakah (presumptive status) that she is pregnant — recognized once her pregnancy is visible
↓
Sumakhos (citing Rabbi Meir): The fetus becomes 'known to all' — i.e., publicly visible — after three months
↓
Rabbi Meir calls the scriptural support (Isaiah 26:18) a mere allusion, not a definitive proof — because some women give birth after only 7 months, making the 3-month rule imprecise
↓
She sees blood during pregnancy — ordinarily this triggers niddah concern. But the chazakah of pregnancy changes the analysis.
↓
Miscarriage of 'air' (non-viable matter): She retains her pregnant chazakah — the blood seen was dam leida (birth blood), not niddah blood
↓
Proof text: 'We have been with child, we have been in pain, we have as it were brought forth wind' (Isaiah 26:18) — even a pregnancy ending in nothing is treated as a real birth
↓
Counter-case: If she had labor pains for two days with blood discharge, then miscarried on day three — she is treated as one who gave birth (stricter ruling)
