QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Ḥisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna, when a woman discharges a shaped hand or foot, which halakhic consequence does NOT apply to her?
A. She is impure with the impurity of a woman after childbirth
B. She receives the days of purity that normally follow childbirth impurity
C. The discharge is considered to have come from a real complete fetus
D. She observes the impurity period for either male or female birth
Correct: B — The shaped limb establishes yoledet (post-childbirth) impurity, but Rav Ḥisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna rule she does not receive the subsequent yemei taharah (days of purity), because we cannot confirm a complete birth took place — only a limb emerged.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Abaye explains how a burned corpse can still retain its tzurah (form). What is his answer?
A. The corpse was buried in dry salt before burning
B. Only the skull remained, which preserves identity
C. It was burned atop a hard leather spread (katavla) that prevented the body from sinking in
D. It was burned at low heat so only the surface was charred
Correct: C — Abaye teaches that if the corpse was burned on a katavla — a hard leather spread — the spread does not absorb or collapse the body, so the corpse's external form is preserved even after burning, allowing it to impart roof impurity.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Rav Huna rules that a fetus who extends its hand out of the womb and retracts it renders its mother a yoledet. What is his biblical proof?
A. The binding of Isaac — Abraham raised his hand before the act was completed
B. Zeraḥ, son of Tamar — he extended his hand and a scarlet thread was tied on it before he was born
C. Moses — his mother hid him for three months after a partial disclosure
D. The midwives of Egypt — they recognized birth the moment any part emerged
Correct: B — Rav Huna derives from Genesis 38:28–29: Zeraḥ extended his hand and the midwife tied a scarlet thread on it, indicating legal recognition of birth at the moment of hand-extension — even before the full body emerged.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
The Gemara asks why the Mishna explicitly states that a woman who gives birth to a tumtum observes both male and female strictures — isn't this obvious? What is the answer?
A. Some Sages held that a tumtum is definitively classified as male
B. Lest one say: since Rabbi Yitzḥak holds sex is determined at conception, we can retroactively resolve the tumtum's sex and apply only one set of strictures
C. The Mishna needed to rule that a tumtum's mother is not exempt from impurity entirely
D. To clarify that the ruling applies even if the tumtum later has its covering removed (nikkur)
Correct: B — Rabbi Yitzḥak teaches that sex is determined at conception based on which seed is emitted first. One might therefore argue we can retroactively determine the tumtum's sex and impose only one set of strictures. The Mishna teaches that despite this theoretical possibility, observable indeterminacy requires full stringency in both directions.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rav derives from Numbers 5:3 ('both male and female you shall send out') that an impure tumtum is exempt from the Torah prohibition of entering the Temple. Why then is teruma they touch still burned?
A. Teruma is always subject to stricter standards than Temple entry in all cases of doubt
B. The verse limits the Temple prohibition to definitively male or female persons; the ruling to burn teruma stems from a separate application of impurity stringency
C. Tumtum is considered definitively male for teruma purposes based on a different verse
D. The Rabbis enacted a special fence (seyag) around teruma that does not apply to Temple entry
Correct: B — The verse 'both male and female you shall send out' specifically limits the Temple-entry prohibition to persons of definite sex, excluding tumtum and hermaphrodite. However, the rabbinic principle of applying impurity stringency under doubt — supported by the verse about zavim and zavot — still requires burning any teruma they contact.
