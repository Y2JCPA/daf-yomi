QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Gemara, why are two separate verses needed regarding a caesarean-born animal in the context of sacrificial law?
A. One verse addresses Torah law and the other is a rabbinic fence
B. One verse bans consecrating a non-sacred flawed animal; the other teaches its offspring cannot inherit the mother's sanctity
C. One verse applies to firstborns; the other applies to peace offerings
D. One verse applies before slaughter; the other applies after
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that two verses are needed for each disqualification: one to prevent a non-sacred flawed animal from being consecrated, and a second to teach that even a consecrated animal's offspring born by caesarean does not inherit the mother's sanctity.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yoḥanan citing Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai, what is the precise moment a woman becomes impure as a niddah?
A. When blood forms anywhere within the uterus
B. When blood passes the cervical opening
C. When blood emerges from her nakedness — i.e., exits through the vaginal opening
D. When she experiences physical symptoms of menstruation
Correct: C — Rabbi Yoḥanan derives from Ezekiel 16:36 ('your nakedness was poured out') that impurity only begins when blood exits through the vaginal opening — internal blood, even in the uterine cavity, does not yet trigger niddah.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rav Yosef reinterpret the 'latter clause' of the baraita about a pregnant woman and caesarean birth?
A. It addresses blood that emerged vaginally in the three days before delivery
B. It addresses a woman who gave birth normally but had spotting beforehand
C. It addresses a case where both the infant and blood emerged through the abdominal incision
D. It addresses ziva discharges during a normal pregnancy
Correct: C — Rav Yosef explains that the latter clause concerns a case where both the infant and blood exit through the caesarean incision — not the vagina. The dispute is then whether the uterine location itself is impure, not about birth-blood vs. ziva-blood.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
A woman's uterus became dislodged and fell out of her body. What impurity, if any, does she incur according to the Gemara's conclusion?
A. No impurity — no blood was discharged
B. Seven-day niddah impurity
C. Seven-day ziva impurity
D. She is impure, but not as a niddah or zava — more like contact with a carcass-like object
Correct: D — The Gemara rules out niddah (requires blood flow) and ziva (requires multiple-day discharge). The woman is nonetheless impure — the uterus as flesh falls under a carcass-like category, generating a lesser form of impurity.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does the Gemara reconcile the two contradictory baraitot about a woman's impurity after intercourse?
A. One baraita follows Rabbi Shimon and the other follows the Rabbis
B. One applies to married women and the other to unmarried women
C. One addresses the act of intercourse itself (seven-day impurity); the other addresses post-coital semen discharge after prior immersion (evening suffices)
D. One is a Torah-level ruling; the other is rabbinic stringency
Correct: C — The Gemara distinguishes the two baraitot: seven-day impurity applies to the act of intercourse itself. Rabbi Shimon's leniency (immersion until evening suffices) applies only when the woman already immersed and later discharges semen — a distinct, lesser form of impurity contact.
