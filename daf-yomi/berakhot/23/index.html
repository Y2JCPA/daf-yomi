⚖️ Gezerah Shavah: Three Levels of Freedom

Free on Both Sides

  • Neither verse needs its shared word to teach its own halakha
  • Strongest form — derive freely in both directions
  • Cannot be refuted by any logical argument (teshuvah)
  • Torah made man-animal analogy free on both sides for this reason

Free on One Side

  • One verse is 'free'; the other needs its shared word
  • Rabbis: can derive halakhot — but can also be refuted
  • If two analogies compete and one has a logical refutation, prefer the other
  • Man-sea monster analogy rejected on this basis

Not Free at All

  • Neither verse is free — both need their shared word
  • Rabbi Elazar (via Rav Aḥa b. Rava): still valid but refutable
  • Rabbis treat it same as free on one side
  • Weakest form of verbal analogy

🔗 Gezerah Shavah: The Competing Analogies

Gemara
Why did the Torah make the man-animal verbal analogy free on both sides? So no logical refutation could ever challenge it.
Rav Aḥa b. Rava (R. Elazar)
A verbal analogy not free at all can still derive halakhot — but it can also be refuted. This is more lenient than the standard rule.
Rabbis
When two analogies compete — one free on one side, one not free at all — prefer the stronger (free on one side), unless neither has a logical refutation.
Gemara
The man-sea monster analogy fails: it is only free on one side, and there IS a logical refutation — an animal is a complete creature, but a sea monster is not.
Core Rule Free on both sides = irrefutable. Free on one side = refutable. Not free = weakest. The Torah engineered the man-animal analogy to be strongest.

👁️ Three Amoraic Explanations for Rabbi Meir's Ruling

Rabbi Yoḥanan (via R. Ḥiyya b. Abba)

  • The form (tzurah) of animals resembles a human
  • Challenged by Rabbi Ami: mountains also have 'creation' language
  • Also challenged: amorphous 'ruaḥ' has creation language too
  • Reply (R. Ḥiyya): derive Torah from Torah, not from oral tradition

Rabba bar bar Ḥana / Rabbi Yoḥanan

  • The eyes of these animals are similar to human eyes
  • Challenged: a snake's pupil is also round like a human's
  • Response: if the mishna included snakes, we'd think only snakes are disputed
  • Leaving out snakes teaches the Rabbis dispute more broadly

Rabbi Yannai

  • The eyes are fixed in the front of the head, like a human's
  • Excludes birds (eyes on sides) and snakes
  • Includes owls: their eyes face forward like land animals
  • Supported by Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus's distinction

🦉 Owl vs. Other Birds — Which Discharge Renders Impure?

Woman discharges a bird-shaped fetus
↓
Little Owl or Great Owl
Eyes face forward like a human — Rabbi Meir rules impure
Other Bird Species
Eyes on sides — not like a human — Rabbi Meir rules not impure
↓
Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus: agrees with Rabbi Meir for land animals and owls; does not accept Rabbi Meir's ruling for other birds
↓
Abaye's reconstruction: The baraita is incomplete — Rabbi Ḥanina endorses Rabbi Meir for owls; his rejection applies only to other species of birds. This proves Rabbi Meir distinguishes between owls and other birds.

🔒 Rava's Rulings: Esophagus, Sealed Body, and Partial Limbs

Rava: A fetus with one eye in the normal eye position, or one thigh at the normal hip location — mother is impure. Placement in the correct location is what matters.
↓
Rava: Fetus with a punctured esophagus — although it is a tereifa (will die within 12 months), the mother is impure. It still had the structure of a living being.
↓
Rava: Fetus with a sealed esophagus (closed at one end, no functional passage) — mother is NOT impure. It was never formed as a viable creature.
↓
Baraita: A woman who discharges a sealed body (guf sasum) — mother is not impure. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: a sealed body lacks the lower bodily passage necessary for life.
↓
Rabbi Zakkai asks: How much of the lower body, if removed from a living person, would cause death? (Daf ends mid-discussion — to be continued on Daf 24)
