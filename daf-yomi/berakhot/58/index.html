🗣️ How Did Shmuel Rule? Two Readings of His Leniency

Rav Pappa
Shmuel rules: if she examined the ground, sat on it, and found blood — she is pure by Torah law but impure by rabbinic law, since she sensed no emission. The ground was merely examined, not her body.
Rav Ashi
Shmuel rules she is pure even by rabbinic law, following Rabbi Neḥemya: any surface not susceptible to ritual impurity (like the ground) cannot generate an impurity ruling — blood found there is simply not attributed to her.
Key Principle The debate turns on whether the ground's insusceptibility to impurity matters here. For Rav Ashi, it is decisive — the ground cannot function as an 'examination cloth,' so the stain is legally inert.

📍 Where Was the Stain Found? Location Determines Status

Inner Side — Impure

  • Inner surface of leg or foot
  • Heel (blood can travel downward)
  • Tip of the large toe
  • Blood from the uterus reaches these areas
  • Even distant from vagina — impure by mishna

Outer Side — Pure

  • Outer surface of leg or foot
  • Outer side of foot and ankle
  • Uterine blood cannot reach these areas
  • Stain attributed to external contact
  • Standard ruling: she is pure

The Joint — Disputed

  • Rav Ketina: inner side extends until the joint
  • Joint itself: borderline, dilemma unresolved
  • Rabbi Yirmeya: bracelet-shaped stain — inner or outer?
  • Straight-line stain — inner or outer?
  • Gemara raises the questions but leaves them open (תיקו)

🐛 Louse Attribution: How Far Does the Leniency Go?

The Rabbis
A woman may attribute a blood stain to a louse even without having killed one — lice are ubiquitous on any sheet. Result: No woman is ever impure from stains alone! Every sheet has lice.
Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel
She may only attribute a stain to a louse if she actually killed one. Without that act, there is no basis for attribution. Result: Every tiny stain makes her impure — no woman is ever pure!
Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's Own Conclusion He acknowledges both rulings lead to absurdity. He endorses Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus: attribution to a louse is valid up to the size of a גריס הפסול (split bean), even without killing — a practical middle ground.

⚖️ Rabbi Akiva's Lenient Ruling — The Case of the Healed Wound

A woman comes before Rabbi Akiva — she discovered a blood stain and feared she was ritually impure.
↓
Rabbi Akiva asks: 'Did you have a wound anywhere on your body?' She replies: 'Yes — I had a wound, and it has since healed.'
↓
Rabbi Akiva asks further: 'Was the wound capable of reopening and producing blood?' She confirms it was.
↓
Rabbi Akiva declares her pure — the stain can be attributed to the healed but re-openable wound. External attribution is sufficient.
↓
To his puzzled students: 'The Sages instituted the law of stains as a leniency for women, not a stringency. Where attribution is possible — we apply it.'
