📅 Rav vs Shmuel: Does a Skipping Pattern Create a Veset?

Rav
If a woman bleeds on the 15th, then the 16th (next month), then the 17th (month after), she has established a veset b'dilug — a fixed cycle by skipping. She must anticipate the 18th the following month.
Shmuel
A skipping pattern creates no veset at all. Only a consistent, repeating interval — the same calendar day each month or the same gap between flows — constitutes a fixed cycle.
⚖️ Core Question Does halacha recognize a progressing (dilug) pattern as a veset, or must a veset reflect strict repetition of the identical day or interval?

📅 Rav vs Shmuel — The Skipping Veset Debate

Rav
15th → 16th → 17th establishes a veset b'dilug. The pattern itself is recognizable and obligates the woman to examine herself on the 18th of the following month.
Shmuel
No veset arises from a skipping pattern. Shmuel objects from a baraita: a woman with an existing fixed veset who deviates on consecutive days — all intermediate days are prohibited, because her original veset persists until fully displaced.
Gemara
Both Rav and Shmuel agree that three occurrences are required (like Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel). The sole dispute: whether a progressing pattern across those three occurrences qualifies as a veset.
Gemara
The Gemara defends Shmuel's baraita: the case involves a woman who already had a veset on the 20th — so the baraita addresses displacement, not dilug. The dispute between Rav and Shmuel remains intact.
📜 Practical Ruling Rambam and most poskim follow Shmuel: a veset b'dilug alone does not create a prohibited day. The debate is nonetheless preserved l'halacha.

🩸 Three Categories of Brides — Hymeneal Blood Rules

Pre-Pubescent Girl

  • Has not yet reached the age of menstruation
  • All blood after marriage attributed to the hymen wound
  • Applies even if she previously saw menstrual blood (Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak)
  • Attribution continues until wound ceases to be noḥeret
  • Most lenient category — broad wound-blood attribution

Pubescent Girl (Never Menstruated)

  • Has reached puberty but never yet menstruated
  • Beit Hillel: blood attributed to hymen for four nights (until end of Shabbat)
  • She does not lose her nights — each of four nights permits intercourse (Rav)
  • Shmuel: she does lose a night once intercourse occurs
  • Dispute: Rav (nights not lost) vs Shmuel (night consumed)

Woman Who Already Menstruated

  • Saw menstrual blood before marriage while in father's house
  • Only the very first act of intercourse is permitted (mitzva act)
  • Immediately thereafter she is treated as niddah
  • No four-night leniency — prior cycle is established
  • Strictest category — even Beit Hillel grants only one act

💧 Noḥeret — Determining When Wound Blood Attribution Ends

Rav's rule: Blood is attributed to the hymeneal wound all the time she is noḥeret — meaning the wound is still actively dripping.
↓
Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak clarifies: Noḥeret means she sees blood when standing but does not see blood when sitting. The postural difference proves blood is dripping from an active wound — not from a uterine (menstrual) source.
↓
While noḥeret persists: All blood — regardless of timing or volume — is treated as hymeneal wound blood, not niddah. Intercourse remains permitted within the allotted period.
↓
Once noḥeret ceases: The wound is considered healed. Any further blood, even in a pre-pubescent girl, is treated as menstrual (niddah) blood and full niddah restrictions apply.
↓
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak extends the rule: Even a girl who has previously menstruated benefits from noḥeret attribution after marriage — prior menstruation does not eliminate the wound-blood leniency while the wound is still active.
