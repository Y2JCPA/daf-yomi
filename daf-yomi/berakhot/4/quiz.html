QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Gemara's third resolution, what distinguishes the case debated by Ḥizkiyya and Rabbi Yoḥanan from the case debated by Shammai and Hillel?
A. Shammai/Hillel debate a covered basket; Ḥizkiyya/Yoḥanan debate an uncovered one
B. Shammai/Hillel debate produce stored in the corner of a house; Ḥizkiyya/Yoḥanan debate a basket physically moved to a new corner
C. Ḥizkiyya/Yoḥanan debate an examined basket; Shammai/Hillel debate an unexamined one
D. Shammai/Hillel debate Torah-level impurity; Ḥizkiyya/Yoḥanan debate rabbinic impurity
Correct: B — The third resolution distinguishes produce stored in the corner of a house (Shammai/Hillel) from a basket moved from corner to corner (Ḥizkiyya/Yoḥanan). The first two options describe resolutions 1 and 2 respectively.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
The Gemara cites the baraita about the loaf on the shelf. Why does the Gemara conclude that no proof can be drawn from it about traveling impurity?
A. The baraita deals only with creeping animals, not impure garments
B. The baraita explicitly states its own reason — a pure person entered and placed the loaf on the ground, not that impurity traveled
C. Rabbi Elazar's ruling overrides the baraita in all cases
D. The baraita addresses a public domain, not a private one
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that the baraita gives its own explicit rationale: we say a ritually pure person entered, took the loaf off the shelf, and placed it on the ground — the ruling is not based on a principle that impurity traveled.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What did Rav Yehuda say in the name of Shmuel regarding the Rabbinic ordinance for women's examinations?
A. Women must examine themselves once a week on Erev Shabbat
B. Women must examine themselves at the start and end of every ona
C. The Sages ordained twice-daily examinations — morning (to permit teruma) and evening (to prepare pure bedding)
D. Women with a fixed veset need not examine themselves between cycles
Correct: C — Rav Yehuda in Shmuel's name: the Sages ordained twice-daily self-examinations. The morning examination permits her husband to eat teruma; the evening examination prepares ritually pure bedding for the night.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why did the Rabbis set the retroactive impurity period at a fixed twenty-four hours rather than just one additional ona (half-day)?
A. To match the biblical requirement of a full day and night
B. Because it is physically impossible to bleed for less than twenty-four hours without noticing
C. So that the sinner should not profit — a woman who neglects her examinations should not benefit from a shorter retroactive period
D. To align with Rabbi Dosa's ruling that time is always measured in full days
Correct: C — One answer in the Gemara: fixing the retroactive period at twenty-four hours ensures a woman who deliberately skips her required examinations cannot reduce her impurity window to just one additional ona — the sinner does not profit.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
On what specific point do the Rabbis ultimately agree with Rabbi Dosa, according to the Gemara's final resolution?
A. That all women, regardless of regularity, are impure only from the moment of discovery
B. That a woman who finds blood at the fixed time of her veset (menstrual cycle) is impure only from that moment — her time is sufficient
C. That the twenty-four-hour rule applies even to women who have a fixed veset
D. That women need never examine themselves if they have a predictable and regular cycle
Correct: B — The Gemara concludes that the Rabbis agree with Rabbi Dosa for a woman who finds blood at her fixed veset time — her time is indeed sufficient. They only disagree when blood is discovered at an irregular time.
