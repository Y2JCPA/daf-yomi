🧺 The Central Dispute: Retroactive Impurity

Ḥizkiyya
A basket that was examined before use and found clean of creeping animals — we may presume it remained clean. Retroactive impurity is not imposed.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Even an examined basket: when moved to a new corner, we cannot presume the creeping animal was there from the start. Impurity is imposed only from when the basket arrived in the new location.
🔑 Key Question Do we presume ritual impurity (טומאה) moved from place to place, or is the presumption fixed to the location of discovery?

🧺 Three Resolutions: Reconciling the Basket Cases

Problem: The basket dispute (Ḥizkiyya/Yoḥanan) seems to contradict Shammai and Hillel's parallel case involving a niddah (menstruating woman)
↓
Resolution 1
Covered vs. Uncovered: Shammai/Hillel = covered basket (like a woman — no outside blood enters); Ḥizkiyya/Yoḥanan = uncovered basket
Resolution 2
Examined vs. Unexamined: Ḥizkiyya/Yoḥanan debate an examined basket (checked before use); Shammai/Hillel debate an unexamined one
Resolution 3
Corner of House vs. Moved Basket: Shammai/Hillel debate produce stored in the corner of a house; Ḥizkiyya/Yoḥanan debate a basket physically moved to a new corner

📍 Does Impurity Travel? Gemara Challenge

Gemara (Teharot 5:7)
Someone touched a person at night; in the morning found him dead. The Rabbis rule him impure — we presume the person was already dead when he was touched. So impurity is presumed retroactively!
Baraita Response
The presumption of impurity applies only in the place where it was discovered — not to other locations. So Rabbi Yoḥanan cannot presume the creeping animal traveled from the basket's first corner.
Gemara Counter
But this 'place-only' rule applies to definite impurity (burning teruma). For uncertain impurity — the rule of doubt in a private domain (טומאה ברשות היחיד) should render the item impure!
Final Answer
That rule applies to Torah-level impurity. Here we deal with rabbinic impurity — the Rabbis were lenient and did not extend the private-domain rule to rabbinic cases.
⚖️ Principle חזקת טומאה — the presumption of impurity — is anchored to the place of discovery, and the private-domain doubt rule applies only at the Torah level.

🕐 Rabbis' Ruling: 24-Hour Period & Examination-to-Examination

The Rabbis' Rule: When a woman finds blood, she retroactively impures items for whichever is shorter — a twenty-four-hour period or the time since her last self-examination (עונה)
↓
24 hours limits examination interval: She examined Sunday (pure) → skipped Mon–Tue → found blood Wed morning. Retroactive impurity: back to Sunday (less than 24 hrs before Wed morning).
↓
Examination limits 24-hour period: She examined at Hour 1 (pure) → sat Hours 2–9 → found blood at Hour 10. Retroactive impurity: only from Hour 1 — well under 24 hours.
↓
Rav Yehuda in Shmuel's name: The Sages ordained twice-daily examinations — morning (to permit teruma to her husband) and evening (to prepare ritually pure bedding)
↓
Penalty for neglect: A woman who skips her required examination loses the purity status of all items she touched during the missed ona. A fixed 24-hour maximum ensures the sinner does not profit from negligence.

📅 Fixed Menstrual Cycle: Rabbi Dosa vs. The Rabbis

Rabbi Dosa

  • Her time is sufficient (דייה שעתה) — impure only from moment of discovery
  • Applies when blood is found at the fixed veset (her expected cycle time)
  • Reason: a woman can sense her flow within herself (מרגשת בעצמה)
  • Gemara extends: Rabbi Dosa may hold this even for irregular discovery

The Rabbis

  • Agree with Rabbi Dosa when blood is found at the fixed veset time
  • Disagree only for irregular discovery — not at her expected cycle
  • For irregular cases: apply the ona rule (24 hrs or last examination)
  • A baraita supporting 'time sufficient': the Rabbis can accept it — it refers to the fixed veset
