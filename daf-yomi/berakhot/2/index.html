⚖️ Three Opinions on Retroactive Impurity

Shammai
דַּיָּהּ שַׁעְתָּהּ — Her time is sufficient. Impurity begins only from the moment she notices blood. No retroactive impurity whatsoever.
Hillel
Impurity is retroactive to her most recent self-examination (bedikah). All items she touched since that examination are ritually impure.
Rabbis
A twenty-four-hour period reduces the window. Retroactive impurity extends back no more than 24 hours, or to the last bedikah — whichever interval is shorter.
Practical Ruling The Rabbis adopt a middle position: retroactive impurity spans at most 24 hours (or the last bedikah), balancing stringency with practical leniency.

📅 Two Types of Women: With and Without Veset

Has a Fixed Cycle (Veset)

  • Examines herself at the expected time of her cycle
  • If blood is found: impurity begins from that moment only
  • דַּיָּהּ שַׁעְתָּהּ — Her time is sufficient
  • No retroactive impurity even by Rabbinic ruling
  • Counts menstrual days only from the moment of discovery

No Fixed Cycle (No Veset)

  • Cannot predict when menstrual flow will begin
  • Rabbinic ruling: retroactive impurity up to 24 hours
  • Or retroactive to last bedikah — whichever is sooner
  • All items handled in that window may be impure
  • More stringent due to body's unpredictability

🛁 Challenge from the Ritual Bath (Mikveh)

Gemara
A mikveh found lacking in water: all items purified in it are retroactively definitely impure. Why is a menstruating woman treated differently — only doubtful retroactive impurity?
Answer
The mikveh case has two weakening factors against purity: (1) it lacks the required water now, and (2) the immersed item has a presumptive status of impurity (חזקת טומאה). The woman has only one factor — she is bleeding now.
Second Answer
With a mikveh, water can trickle out gradually — it may have been invalid much earlier. With a woman, menstruation does not seep gradually; it flows suddenly. Therefore no presumption that onset was earlier.
Key Distinction (Gemara's Resolution) Two concurrent weakening factors (חזקת טומאה on the item + the mikveh's present lack) yield certain impurity. A single factor alone yields only doubt.

📜 Rabbi Shimon Reconciles the Mikveh and the Barrel

רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — טָהוֹר; בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד — תְּרוּמָה תִּשָּׂרֵף
Machloket Tanna'im: Certain vs. Doubtful Impurity Rabbis hold: A mikveh found lacking renders immersed items definitely impure — the mikveh was invalid from the last time it was measured as complete.

Rabbi Shimon holds: Even the mikveh creates only doubtful impurity. In a public domain the items are pure; in a private domain, teruma that touched them is burned but not with certainty.

The Gemara concludes: the baraita about the wine barrel follows Rabbi Shimon, who treats the mikveh the same way — both as cases of doubt, not certainty. This resolves the apparent contradiction between the two rulings.
