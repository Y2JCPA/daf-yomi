QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Shammai, when does a woman's menstrual impurity begin?
A. From the time of her most recent self-examination (bedikah)
B. Twenty-four hours before she notices the blood
C. From the moment she discerns the blood — no retroactive impurity
D. From the beginning of that calendar day
Correct: C — Shammai holds דַּיָּהּ שַׁעְתָּהּ — 'her time is sufficient.' Impurity starts precisely from the moment she notices the blood, with no retroactive component. This is the most lenient of the three opinions.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the Rabbinic ruling for a woman without a fixed menstrual cycle (veset)?
A. Her time is sufficient — impurity begins at discovery only
B. Retroactive impurity extends back 24 hours or to her last bedikah, whichever is more recent
C. Retroactive impurity extends back a full seven days
D. She follows the same rule as a woman with a veset
Correct: B — The Rabbis establish a 24-hour retroactive window (or back to the last examination, whichever is shorter). This middle position overrides both Shammai's leniency and Hillel's broader retroactivity.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why do items immersed in a ritual bath (mikveh) found lacking water become *definitely* impure, while a menstruating woman creates only *doubtful* retroactive impurity?
A. The mikveh has only one weakening factor; the woman also has only one
B. The mikveh has two weakening factors (present lack + item's presumptive impurity), while the woman has only one
C. There is no difference — both create certain impurity
D. Rabbi Shimon rules both are definitely impure in a private domain
Correct: B — The Gemara's key answer: the mikveh case combines two factors against purity — it lacks water now AND the immersed item carries a presumptive status of impurity (חזקת טומאה). The woman only has one factor. Two factors yield certainty; one factor yields only doubt.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
A woman with a fixed cycle (veset) examined herself at the expected time and found blood. What is the halakhic ruling?
A. Retroactively impure for 24 hours — the standard Rabbinic rule applies
B. Retroactively impure from her last bedikah
C. Her time is sufficient — impurity begins from that moment only, items touched before are pure
D. She must consult a rabbi before any determination can be made
Correct: C — A woman with a veset who examines at the right time benefits fully from דַּיָּהּ שַׁעְתָּהּ. Because she examined herself at the expected moment, impurity begins only from discovery. The Mishna explicitly excludes her from the Rabbinic 24-hour retroactive rule.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Rabbi Shimon reconcile the barrel of wine (which creates doubtful impurity) with the standard ruling on a mikveh (which creates certain impurity)?
A. He rules the barrel creates certain impurity, aligning it with the mikveh
B. He rules the mikveh also creates only doubtful impurity, making both cases consistent
C. He holds the two cases are governed by entirely different principles with no reconciliation needed
D. He rules both create certain impurity only when found in a private domain
Correct: B — Rabbi Shimon holds that even a mikveh found lacking creates only doubtful (not certain) impurity — items immersed in a public-domain mikveh are pure; in a private domain, teruma is burned but not with full certainty. The barrel baraita follows his view, resolving the apparent contradiction.
