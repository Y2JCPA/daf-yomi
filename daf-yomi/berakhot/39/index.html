🔍 When Does Failing to Examine Herself Matter?

Woman fails to examine herself — does she retain purity?
↓
During 11 Days of Ziva
Presumes pure — no examination required to maintain status
During Menstrual Days (veset arrived)
Presumes impure — it is typical to discharge at this time
During 7 Clean Days (zav/zava)
Status undermined — must examine or clean-day count is void
↓
Rabbi Meir's stringency: a woman without a fixed cycle is forbidden to her husband even during menstrual days, lest she emit blood during intercourse

💬 Three Amoraim Explain the Mishna's First Clause

Rav Yehuda
The ruling that ziva days preserve purity without examination permits her husband to continue living with her — she remains fully permitted and need not be separated.
Rav Ḥisda
The first clause is necessary only for Rabbi Meir's opinion: even a woman without a fixed cycle has days — the 11 ziva days — when she is permitted to her husband. This answers: why does Rabbi Meir require divorce? Only because she is forbidden on menstrual days, not because she is forbidden always.
Rava
The mishna teaches that blood emitted during the 11 ziva days does not impart retroactive impurity beyond 24 hours — unlike a menstruating woman who imparts impurity retroactively for a full day-and-night (עונה).
📌 The Underlying Question With regard to what halakha does the mishna state that a woman retains presumptive purity throughout the 11 days of ziva?

📊 Seven Days of Menstruation vs. Eleven Days of Ziva

7 Days of Menstruation (נִדָּה)

  • Failure to examine when veset arrives → presumes impure
  • Typical for a woman to discharge blood at that time
  • A fixed menstrual cycle (וֶסֶת) can be established here
  • Rabbi Meir: woman without fixed cycle is forbidden to husband during these days
  • Retroactive impurity covers a full onah (day or night)

11 Days of Ziva (זִיבָה)

  • Failure to examine → presumes pure (even willfully, per mishna)
  • A fixed menstrual cycle cannot be established during these days
  • Rava: retroactive impurity only up to 24 hours, not beyond
  • Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya / Shmuel: sightings here do not create a veset
  • Rabbi Meir implicitly permits relations on these days

🧠 No Veset is Fixed During Ziva Days

אֵין הָאִשָּׁה קוֹבַעַת לָהּ וֶסֶת בְּיָמֵי זִיבָתָהּ
🧠 Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya — In the Name of Shmuel The mishna's first clause comes to teach that a woman does not fix a menstrual cycle (וֶסֶת) during her days of ziva. A sighting that occurs during the 11 ziva days cannot anchor a repeating pattern — even if she sees blood on the same calendar day in two successive months, the ziva sighting is halakhically different from a niddah sighting.

Abaye challenges: Rav Yehuda had already taught this ruling in the name of Shmuel regarding a different mishna (63b) — why repeat it here?

🐔 Counting a Deviated Cycle — Rav Pappa vs. Rav Huna b. Rav Yehoshua

The Case: A woman normally bleeds on day 15 from immersion (= day 22 from her previous sighting). She deviates and bleeds on day 20 from immersion (= day 27 from previous sighting).
↓
Rav Pappa's View: Count 22 days of menstrual cycle from the usual expected start (day 22). Even though she actually bled on day 27, her internal cycle is anchored to day 22.
↓
Rav Huna b. R. Yehoshua's Challenge: Count 22 days from the actual bleeding event (day 27). The cycle resets from reality — just as a chicken that resumes its routine picks up from its last actual state, not from where it was expected to be.
↓
The Chicken Analogy: A chicken lays an egg one day, withholds one day. If it skips a cycle, when it resumes, does it follow the schedule ahead of it (withhold), or retake from its last actual event (lay)? — It follows its last actual event.
↓
Conclusion: The 22-day cycle is counted from when she actually bleeds. The organism resets from lived experience, not from scheduled expectation.

⚖️ Reish Lakish vs. Rabbi Yochanan — Fixing a Cycle During Ziva

Reish Lakish
A woman fixes a menstrual cycle during the days of her ziva. Consistent sightings during ziva days can establish a veset.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
A woman does not fix a menstrual cycle during her days of ziva.
Rav Pappa (initial reading)
The dispute concerns a case where she saw blood: 1st of Month 1 (as zava), then 5th of Month 1 (as niddah), then 1st of Month 2 (as zava), then 5th of Month 2 (as niddah). Does this mixed pattern fix a cycle?
Rav Huna b. Rav Yehoshua (corrected reading)
No — the dispute is: she saw blood on 1st of Month 1, then 1st of Month 2 (as a full niddah, not zava), then 1st of Month 3. Reish Lakish: the two ziva sightings flanking the niddah establish a veset. Rabbi Yoḥanan: they do not. Ravin confirmed this explanation upon arriving from Eretz Yisrael.
📌 Key Issue Does a niddah sighting 'interrupt' or 'anchor' the ziva pattern? Rabbi Yochanan says the interrupted ziva sightings cannot combine to form a fixed veset.
