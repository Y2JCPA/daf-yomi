QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the school of Rabbi Yishmael, why does the bird carcass NOT require prior contact with liquid to be susceptible to ritual impurity as food?
A. The Torah explicitly exempts bird carcasses from all liquid requirements
B. Only items that will never transmit impurity severe enough to defile a person require prior liquid contact
C. Rabbi Yishmael held that no food ever requires liquid for susceptibility
D. Bird carcasses are treated as inherently pure and need extra stringency to become impure
Correct: B — The school of R. Yishmael taught that seeds need liquid first because they will never cause severe (person-level) impurity. Since the bird carcass CAN cause severe impurity (defiling garments in the throat), it is exempt from the liquid prerequisite.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does Rav Shimi bar Ashi prove that the mishna on 50a ('any food obligated in tithes becomes impure with food impurity') refers only to general categories of food, not every circumstance?
A. He proves it from a case of ownerless (hefker) vineyard produce — pe'a applies but tithes do not, showing pe'a and tithes can separate
B. He proves it from the case of Shabbat, where neither tithes nor impurity apply
C. He cites a baraita where Rav Sheshet rules that the mishna explicitly says 'categories'
D. He shows that dill is obligated in tithes but the Sages ruled it cannot become impure
Correct: A — Rav Shimi bar Ashi notes that in a hefker vineyard, pe'a applies (per Rabbi Akiva in Pe'a 3:2), yet tithes do not apply (hefker produce is tithe-exempt). Since the mishna says pe'a-obligated produce is tithe-obligated, but here it is not — the mishna must be about general categories only.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
The Gemara asks: since every scaled fish also has fins, why does the Torah write BOTH signs? What is Rabbi Abbahu's answer?
A. To teach that fins alone are sufficient to permit eating a fish even without visible scales
B. To add a stringency: both must be visually verified before eating the fish
C. To teach that if you find a fish with scales in the sea you may eat it even without seeing its fins
D. Because the Torah always writes two signs when only one is truly required, as a mnemonic
Correct: C — Rabbi Abbahu (and the school of R. Yishmael) teach that writing 'fins' in addition to 'scales' tells us a practical rule: if you find a fish in the sea bearing scales, you may eat it even if you cannot see its fins — scales alone suffice as the identifying kosher sign.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What case does the mishna about blessings add when it rules that some items require a blessing before but NOT after?
A. Bread — one says HaMotzi but Birkat HaMazon is optional
B. Vegetables — one recites a blessing before but not after
C. Wine — the after-blessing was abolished by a later rabbinic decree
D. Meat — a blessing before is required but Birkat HaMazon is only for bread
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that the mishna's clause about items requiring a blessing before but not after serves to include vegetables: one recites Borei pri ha'adamah before eating vegetables, but no after-blessing (or only a minimal rabbinic one) is recited.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rava states that Rabbi Yoḥanan concedes that intent for tithing purposes counts even while produce is still attached to the ground. What is Rav Ashi's primary challenge to Rava's proof from the mishna about hyssop?
A. Hyssop is exempt from tithes entirely, so the mishna proves nothing about intent
B. The hyssop in the mishna grew on its own in a courtyard — unspecified plants there default to human use regardless of intent
C. Rabbi Yoḥanan himself explicitly rejected this reading of the hyssop mishna
D. The mishna about hyssop deals only with Sabbatical Year produce, not regular tithes
Correct: B — Rav Ashi argues that the hyssop mishna is not a proof about intent while attached. The hyssop grew on its own in a courtyard, and unspecified plants in a courtyard are simply assumed to be for human consumption by default — not because of any expressed intent at planting time.
