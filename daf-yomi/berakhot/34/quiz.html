QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Beit Hillel, when does the blood of a gentile niddah impart ritual impurity?
A. Always — identical to Jewish niddah blood
B. Only when moist, not when dry
C. Never — it is completely pure
D. Only if witnessed by two people
Correct: B — Beit Hillel rule that gentile niddah blood is impure only when moist (לח), serving as a conspicuous marker. Jewish niddah blood is impure even when dry; this distinction signals that gentile impurity is rabbinic, not Torah-level.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rava, what is the purity status of a gentile man's semen, even according to Beit Hillel?
A. Impure by Torah law
B. Impure by rabbinic law, like his ziva
C. Ritually pure
D. Impure only when still warm
Correct: C — Rava rules that a gentile man's semen is ritually pure even according to Beit Hillel. Its purity serves as the conspicuous marker: knowing his semen is pure, everyone understands his ziva is only rabbinically impure, not Torah-impure.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the core of Rav Pappa's unresolved dilemma?
A. Whether a gentile woman can become a zava at all
B. Whether Jewish semen in a gentile womb is still impure after 3 days
C. Whether a minor's semen imparts impurity
D. Whether a gentile man's ziva is impure per Beit Hillel
Correct: B — Rav Pappa asks: does Jewish semen deposited in a gentile woman become pure after 3 days? The question turns on whether her body's 'coolness' (linked to lack of mitzvah concern) keeps the semen viable longer, or whether location determines status.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rava resolve the question of whether a minor's first ziva sighting imparts impurity?
A. It does not — since his semen is pure, his ziva follows suit
B. It imparts impurity, based on a verse equating minor and adult
C. It depends on whether he has shown signs of puberty
D. The Gemara leaves it unresolved
Correct: B — Rava cites a baraita deriving from 'This is the law of the zav — whether adult or minor' that Torah equates them. Just as an adult's first ziva sighting is impure, so is a minor's. Ziva-impurity and semen-impurity are independent legal categories.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why do Beit Hillel say the verse 'whether it be a male' (Lev. 15:33) must teach that a female leper's blood imparts impurity?
A. Because female leprosy is categorically more severe than male leprosy
B. Because her saliva and urine cannot be derived from any other source
C. Because her blood cannot be derived by analogy to the male leper, so the verse must teach it explicitly
D. Because the verse mentions blood immediately before this phrase
Correct: C — Beit Hillel argue: if the verse only taught saliva/urine of a female leper, we'd derive that by analogy to the male leper. The verse would be superfluous. It must therefore come to teach her blood — the one thing not derivable from the male leper.
