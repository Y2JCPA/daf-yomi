Berakhot 3 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
From which case do both the Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon derive their positions on ritual bath uncertainty?
A. A kohein who contracted impurity inside the Temple
B. A nazir who unknowingly came into contact with a corpse
C. A sotah — a woman suspected of marital infidelity
D. A person who ate food of uncertain tithing status
Correct: C — Both the Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon derive the rules for ritual bath uncertainty from the case of the sotah (suspected adulteress), differing only in how they apply her public/private domain distinctions.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rava, what is the PRIMARY reason Shammai rules that a woman is impure only from the time she discovers her flow — not retroactively?
A. Women always sense their menstrual flow at the moment it begins
B. Prior menstrual blood would have physically flowed out before going unnoticed
C. Retroactive impurity would cause dereliction of the mitzva to be fruitful and multiply
D. The sota analogy requires leniency in cases of public-domain uncertainty
Correct: C — Rava holds that Shammai's core reasoning is concern for p'ru u'rvu: if women were always deemed retroactively impure, scrupulous husbands would avoid intercourse entirely rather than risk the possibility, causing them to fail the mitzva.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Hillel challenges Shammai by comparing a menstruating woman to produce stored with a creeping animal. What is Shammai's rebuttal?
A. A creeping animal causes a stricter grade of impurity than menstrual blood, so the cases are incomparable
B. The basket case is also only impure from the moment of discovery, consistent with Shammai's ruling
C. A creeping animal may lurk unnoticed, but prior menstrual blood would have already flowed out — the analogy fails
D. The basket case is governed by rabbinic law, while menstrual impurity is biblical — different standards apply
Correct: C — Shammai distinguishes the cases: a creeping animal can remain in a basket undetected for a long time, but if menstrual blood existed earlier it would have already emerged naturally and been noticed. The basket analogy therefore does not apply.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the practical halakhic difference between Abaye's version of Shammai's reason (bodily sensation) and Rava's version (blood flows out)?
A. Abaye's version applies only to biblical impurity; Rava's applies to rabbinic impurity
B. They differ in a case where a woman uses a contraceptive absorbent cloth during intercourse — Abaye: prior blood may still be retained; Rava: even then, blood would flow out around it
C. Abaye's version requires the woman to testify about her sensation; Rava's does not
D. Rava's version applies only to menstrual blood, while Abaye's applies to all categories of niddah
Correct: B — The Gemara explicitly identifies the contraceptive cloth case as the key practical divergence: according to Abaye (sensation), the cloth might prevent the woman from feeling a flow; according to Rava (blood flows out), the cloth does not hermetically seal the womb, so prior blood would still have escaped.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In the basket cases discussed at the end of the daf, why does a basket with an inward-curved rim or base lead to retroactive impurity for its contents?
A. The rim sanctifies the basket's interior, making any impurity inside more stringent
B. A rimmed basket legally constitutes a 'private domain' so private-domain stringency applies
C. The rim could have retained a creeping animal inside the basket undetected, meaning it may have been there all along
D. Baskets with a base are used only for ritually pure produce, creating a stricter presumption
Correct: C — An inward-curved rim creates a physical mechanism by which a creeping animal could remain trapped inside unnoticed. Since we cannot rule out that the animal was present throughout, the produce stored inside is deemed retroactively impure — unlike a flat basket where the animal would simply fall out.
