📜 Niddah 26 — Afterbirth, Sandal Fetuses & the Handbreadth

נידה כ״ו
Core Theme Three interlocking inquiries dominate this daf. First, a dispute between Rav Pappa and Rav Huna bar Taḥlifa over how a sandal fetus is physically oriented relative to its twin — a question with real consequences for firstborn inheritance and a mother's birth-offering. Second, a tannaitic debate about whether an afterbirth (שליה) in a house causes tent-impurity. Third, a wide-ranging survey of items requiring a minimum of one handbreadth, followed by the rule that afterbirth is attributed only to a viable offspring within three days.

⚔️ How Does the Sandal Fetus Lie? Rav Pappa vs. Rav Huna bar Taḥlifa

Rav Pappa
The sandal and its twin do not lie side by side. The full-fledged offspring's head presses into the middle (half the height) of the sandal. Therefore — firstborns: sandal exits first → twin inherits as firstborn (but not redeemed). Offerings: full-fledged exits first through regular birth → sandal follows → mother brings offering for the sandal birth.
Rav Huna bar Taḥlifa
(citing Rava)
Reverse Rav Pappa's conclusion entirely. The two lie precisely side by side. Firstborns: the full-fledged offspring exits first (from the lower half) → it is the firstborn for inheritance. Offerings: the sandal exits first → the full-fledged offspring that follows triggers the offering obligation.
Why it matters Identical anatomy yields opposite legal outcomes depending on which fetus is considered to exit first — affecting both bekhor inheritance and a mother's post-birth korban obligation.

🏠 Does Afterbirth in a House Cause Tent-Impurity? (Mishna)

Sages — House is Impure

  • Afterbirth in a house renders the house ritually impure
  • Not because the afterbirth itself is a corpse
  • Rather: there is no afterbirth without an offspring
  • The offspring may have dissolved — but it once existed
  • The house becomes an ohel (tent) over what was effectively a corpse
  • Everything under the roof contracts corpse-impurity

Rabbi Shimon — House is Pure

  • The house does NOT become a tent over a corpse
  • The offspring disintegrated and turned entirely to blood before exiting
  • It was nullified by the majority of blood in the womb
  • No identifiable offspring-substance remains
  • The afterbirth exits alone — it has no corpse-power
  • Therefore: no tent-impurity applies

⏳ Attributing Afterbirth to a Prior Birth — The Three-Day Rule

Statement (Rav, cited by Rav Yehuda): For all of the first three days after a woman gives birth, if she subsequently discharges an afterbirth, we attribute the afterbirth to the prior birth.
↓
Challenge (Rav Kahana): But didn't Rav himself say that an offspring does not remain in the womb after another offspring was already born? How then does afterbirth appear days later?
↓
Resolution (Rav Yitzḥak bar Shmuel): Rav's statement refers to a case where the woman first discharged a non-viable newborn and later discharged an afterbirth — the afterbirth is attributed to that earlier birth.
↓
Key limit (Rav, via Rav Yosef of D'vil): Afterbirth is attributed only to a viable offspring. A non-viable discharge does not draw afterbirth attribution to it.
↓
Practical outcome: Afterbirth within 3 days + prior viable birth → attributed. Afterbirth after 3 days, or prior birth was non-viable → no attribution. The birth of a second viable offspring interrupts attribution entirely.

🐦 Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul Questions Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi

Question 1 (Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul): A woman discharges something in the form of a crow (עורב) — and there is also an afterbirth present. Is the afterbirth attributed to this crow-form discharge?
↓
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi replies: Yes — the afterbirth is attributed to the crow-form discharge (implying it is treated as a genuine, if malformed, offspring).
↓
Question 2 (Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul): What if the afterbirth is physically tied to the crow-form item — does that change the ruling?
↓
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
"You asked about a matter that does not exist."
A crow-form discharge cannot be physically attached to an afterbirth — the scenario is anatomically impossible.
Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul
Raised his hands, brought fingers together
— gesturing emphatically that such a case does exist — insisting the question is valid and unanswered.
