QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does a zav who moves a ritually pure person using a pole held in his own body folds NOT render that person impure?
A. Because the pole is too far from the zav's body to carry his impurity
B. Because movement-impurity of a zav is compared to his hands, and body folds are not hands
C. Because body folds are considered 'interior of the interior' and are fully exempt
D. Because only living creatures — not objects — can transmit zav impurity
Correct: B — The verse (Lev. 15:11) states 'without having rinsed his hands,' equating zav movement-impurity to hand-contact. Just as hand-impurity requires direct hand contact, movement-impurity must flow through the zav's hands — not objects stored in his folds.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the key difference between Abaye's and Rava's explanations of why the priest may hold his penis while swallowing teruma?
A. Abaye says it is a rabbinic emergency leniency; Rava says it is a biblical permission
B. Abaye says he holds with a coarse cloth; Rava says even soft cloth is fine because quaking makes the emission inevitable
C. Abaye requires the priest to spit out the teruma first; Rava says he may swallow it
D. Abaye permits it only at night; Rava extends the permission to daytime
Correct: B — Abaye limits the mishna to a coarse cloth (no stimulation possible). Rava permits even a soft cloth, reasoning that quaking limbs signal the emission is already physiologically inevitable — holding at that stage cannot increase it.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the practical halakhic difference between Shmuel's two versions of his ruling on semen impurity?
A. There is no practical difference — the two versions are identical in outcome
B. Version 1 applies to men; Version 2 applies to women
C. When semen is uprooted with sensation but emitted without sensation: Version 1 = impure; Version 2 = pure
D. Version 2 is stricter and renders impure in more cases than Version 1
Correct: C — When semen was uprooted with full-body sensation but exited without force, Version 1 ('felt by whole body') renders him impure since the uprooting was felt; Version 2 ('shot like arrow') renders him pure since the emission itself was not forceful.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Rava raises a dilemma about a gentile whose semen was uprooted before conversion and emitted after. What is one reason the Gemara considers he might be pure even if we generally follow the moment of uprooting?
A. Because gentiles are entirely exempt from all purity laws under the Torah
B. Because the rule of following uprooting may only apply as a stringency for a born Jew, not for a new convert
C. Because conversion retroactively purifies all prior states of impurity
D. Because the Rabbis did not extend semen-impurity to cases involving gentiles
Correct: B — Even granting that we follow the moment of uprooting, the Gemara considers that this stringency may apply specifically to a born Jew. For a gentile who becomes Jewish, one could argue the conversion creates a new legal status that overrides prior-state calculations.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Shmuel (citing the baraita of Rabbi Natan / Rabbi Yishmael), what is the minimum discharge that renders a zav ritually impure?
A. Any amount whatsoever, even a single drop
B. A revi'it (quarter-log) of discharge
C. Enough discharge to cause a blockage at the tip of the penis
D. Three separate emissions on three consecutive days
Correct: C — Shmuel rules in accordance with Rabbi Natan's baraita (aligned with Rabbi Yishmael) that the discharge must be substantial enough to block the tip of the penis. This contrasts with the Mishna, which states the emission of a zav renders impure in any amount.
