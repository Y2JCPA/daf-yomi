🩸 Does Labor-Pain Blood Negate the Seven Clean Days?

Rava
Only a substance that causes one to become a zava can negate the count. Labor-pain blood does not cause zavut — therefore it does not negate the seven clean days.
Abaye
Proof from ziva beyond one's control: it does not render one a full zav, yet it negates the count! So any impurity-imparting substance should negate the count.
Rava
La'ei — not so! An involuntary ziva emission does contribute to zavut: even involuntary sightings count toward the three needed. It is fundamentally different from labor blood.
Abaye
We have a tradition (mekubbalani): labor-pain blood does not negate the seven clean days — in accordance with the Rabbis. Any tanna who says otherwise follows Rabbi Eliezer's minority view.
Halakhic Ruling Abaye rules: blood from labor pains does NOT negate the seven clean days of a zava (per the Rabbis, not Rabbi Eliezer).

⚖️ Does Birth Count Toward the Seven Clean Days? Rava vs. Abaye

Rava
Birth counts toward the seven clean days. Proof (Lev. 15:28): 'she shall count for herself seven days' — the counting continues through the birth without interruption. Days must be consecutive; a gap would violate the requirement.
Abaye
Birth does not count toward the seven clean days. The verse compares birth-impurity to menstruation — those are impure days, not clean. She needs 'seven days clean from birth-impurity'; birth days are therefore excluded.
Rava
The baraita reads: 'From her ziva and not from her leprosy' — since we needed to teach the leprosy exclusion, the verse must intend that birth does count. Otherwise there was no reason to derive both exclusions.
Abaye
Both the zav verse and the zava verse are necessary — each teaches a unique leniency. The zava verse teaches that three sightings on one day do not combine; neither verse implies birth days count.
The Dispute Rava: birth days count toward seven clean days. Abaye: birth days neither negate the count nor count toward it.

👶 Rabbi Marinus & the Zava Who Gives Birth Mid-Count

Rabbi Marinus's Ruling

  • A zava who gives birth in the middle of counting seven clean days —
  • The birth does NOT negate her count
  • She continues counting from where she left off
  • Source: Lev. 15:28 — 'she shall count for herself seven days'
  • The counting is not interrupted by an act of birth

Rava's Proof for This Ruling

  • 'From her ziva' (Lev. 15:28) — counting begins from cessation of ziva
  • If birth days did not count, the sequence of seven would be broken
  • The Torah requires the seven days to be consecutive
  • Birth days count as 'clean' once ziva has stopped
  • Consistent with: 'not from her giving birth' = birth doesn't restart the clock

Abaye's Counter-Position

  • Verse compares birth-impurity to menstrual impurity (Lev. 12:2)
  • Menstrual days are impure — they cannot be 'clean days'
  • She needs seven days clean from birth-impurity
  • Birth days are excluded from the count entirely
  • But: birth also does NOT negate days already counted

📜 Deriving the Halakha: The Possible from the Impossible

Problem: Can one derive the halakha of a yoledet (woman who gave birth) from the halakha of menstruation regarding ziva?
↓
The baraita: Verse (Lev. 12:2) compares birth-impurity to menstrual impurity — just as menstruation-impurity does not create ziva, so too birth-impurity does not create ziva.
↓
Objection (Gemara): But this derives the possible (birth-impurity, which can combine with ziva) from the impossible (menstrual-impurity, which cannot cause ziva by definition). Is such a derivation valid?
↓
Rav Aḥadevoi bar Ami: This baraita follows Rabbi Eliezer, who permits deriving the possible from the impossible.
↓
Rav Sheshet: Even those who reject such derivations agree here — because the verse explicitly compares the two cases, making the derivation textually grounded rather than purely logical.
↓
Conclusion: The comparison between birth-impurity and menstruation is valid. Birth days during the eleven-day period are treated like menstrual days — not as ziva days.
