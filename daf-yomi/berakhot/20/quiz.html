QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabba bar Rav Huna and a supporting baraita, the black impure blood (ḥeret) described in the mishna is as dark as what?
A. The garments worn by inhabitants of Siva'a
B. Ink (דיו)
C. Bath attendants' cloths from overseas
D. Black wax (kekir)
Correct: B — Rabba bar Rav Huna explicitly identifies the ḥeret of the mishna as ink (דיו), confirmed by a baraita. The other options — Siva'a garments (Ulla), bath attendants' cloths (Rabbi Yoḥanan), and black wax (Rav Yehuda in Shmuel's name) — are alternative comparisons offered by other Sages for the same shade.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Abaye reconciles four contradictory baraitot about saffron-colored blood by explaining the crocus structure. Which part of the flower is the halakhic standard?
A. The top leaf of the top layer
B. The bottom leaf of the bottom layer
C. The middle leaf of the middle layer
D. Any leaf from any of the three middle positions
Correct: C — Abaye explains that the crocus has three layers, each with three leaves. The baraitot referring to 'upper,' 'lower,' and 'middle' each describe a different layer. The binding standard is the middle leaf of the middle layer — not too pale and not too dark.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Bar Kappara agrees with the general rule (deeper=impure, lighter=pure) but makes an exception for one blood color. Which color and why?
A. Black — deeper black has changed character entirely
B. Saffron — lighter saffron is still within the impure orange range
C. Diluted wine — if deeper, it resembles undiluted wine, itself an impure (red) category
D. Red earth water — only the exact match is impure
Correct: C — Bar Kappara holds that for diluted wine blood, a shade deeper than diluted wine begins to resemble undiluted wine — which is its own impure color (red). Therefore even a deeper-than-standard diluted-wine color is impure, unlike the other four colors where deeper shades are pure.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why did Rav Yehuda stop examining blood samples, and what was his one exception?
A. He moved to Babylonia and lacked familiarity with local conditions; he kept examining birth blood
B. His wife told him he was regularly contradicted by Rabbi Ami; he continued examining only post-birth purity blood
C. Rabbi Ḥanina publicly overruled him; he only examined black blood thereafter
D. Rabbi Zeira persuaded him the matter was too complex; he deferred entirely to Rava
Correct: B — Rav Yehuda reports that his wife (mother of his son Yitzḥak) observed that when he ruled impure, Rabbi Ami ruled pure, and vice versa. Recognizing his unreliability, he stopped — except for blood emitted after the completion of a woman's post-birth purity days, which he continued to examine.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How did the Gemara ultimately explain Rav Yitzḥak's reversal in Yalta's case — ruling pure after Rabba bar bar Ḥana had ruled impure?
A. A second halakhic authority is always permitted to reverse an impurity ruling if shown the same sample
B. Rav Yitzḥak relied entirely on Yalta's testimony about Rabba's usual rulings
C. Rav Yitzḥak ruled on his own independent judgment; Yalta's claim only overcame his reluctance to contradict Rabba
D. He sent the sample to a third expert who confirmed it was pure
Correct: C — The Gemara clarifies that Rav Yitzḥak did not simply defer to Yalta's word. He had his own learned view that the blood was pure but was reluctant to contradict Rabba bar bar Ḥana. Yalta's claim that Rabba usually ruled such blood pure gave him the confidence to follow his own assessment — the ruling was independently his.
