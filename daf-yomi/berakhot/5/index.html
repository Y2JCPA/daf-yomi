📜 Mishna or Baraita — Which Reflects the Majority View?

Questioner
The Gemara reads the mishna as following Rabbi Dosa (lenient: fixed time = no retroactivity) and the baraita as everyone's view. Why not flip it — mishna = everyone, baraita = Rabbi Dosa?
Gemara
Since the text can be read as a leniency AND as a stringency, we prefer assigning the lenient reading to the anonymous mishna. The anonymous mishna follows the majority — and here the majority is lenient.
Principle
When two valid interpretations exist, prefer the one that produces a leniency for the mishna. This guides which opinion we attribute to the stam (anonymous) mishna.
Hermeneutic Rule An anonymous mishna (סתם משנה) is attributed to the majority opinion. When the majority position is also the lenient one, the interpretive preference for leniency reinforces the attribution.

📅 Women With vs. Without a Fixed Cycle

Fixed Cycle (בעלת וסת)

  • דייה שעתה — 'her time is sufficient'
  • Blood renders items impure only from moment of discovery
  • No retroactive 24-hour period of impurity
  • Bed and items touched before discovery remain pure
  • Rabbi Dosa's lenient position — accepted as halakha
  • Blood stains likewise impure only from point of discovery (R. Hanina b. Antigonus)

No Fixed Cycle (אין לה וסת)

  • מטמאה למפרע — retroactive impurity
  • Items touched up to 24 hours prior are impure
  • Includes: pregnant, nursing, elderly, and minor women
  • Blood stains carry same retroactive ruling as sighting
  • Stricter standard — uncertainty traced back in time
  • Examination cloths can reduce the retroactive window

🧵 Examination Cloths (עדים) During Intercourse

Mishna states: A woman who uses examination cloths during intercourse — this act counts like a valid examination (בדיקה) and reduces retroactive impurity
↓
Shmuel disagrees: This does NOT count as a valid examination — she is too excited (מהומה) and hurried for intercourse to perform a proper check
↓
Objection raised: Even if excited, the cloth still contacts the area and would detect blood — why does excitement invalidate the examination?
↓
Answer: Because she is excited, she does not insert the cloth deeply enough — a proper bedikah requires thorough insertion, which she skips
↓
Why 'cloths' (plural)? One cloth used before intercourse, one used after — both are obligatory. The post-coital cloth ensures blood is not hidden beneath semen
↓
Result: Two valid examinations reduce retroactive impurity — not from 24 hours, but only from examination to examination (a shorter window)

🤲 Rabbi Yohanan: Uncertain Impurity by Human Hand

ספק טומאה הבאה בידי אדם — טמא
Rabbi Yohanan (רבי יוחנן) In a case of uncertain ritual impurity that comes about through human involvement — the owner of the vessel must consult a Sage. It is treated as impure, because a person is aware of their own actions and cannot claim pure ignorance. This differs from doubt arising without any human involvement, which remains pure. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel adds a practical solution: have the person repeat the action (e.g., wrapping in a cloak) to observe whether contact with impure items actually occurs — if uncertain, he can clarify through repetition.
