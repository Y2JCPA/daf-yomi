🪨 Rabbi Meir vs. Rabbis — Impure Pile of Stones

Rabbi Meir
Any item with a presumption of impurity forever retains it. Even if all three piles are examined and found pure, all three remain impure.
Rabbis
Search until you reach bedrock or virgin soil. If no impurity is found there, presume an animal carried off the olive-bulk — the piles may then be declared pure.
Rabbi Meir
But you concede that all three women are impure when blood is found! Why do you treat piles of stones differently from women?
Rabbis
Blood cannot be carried away — no raven takes blood. But an olive-bulk of a corpse can be dragged off by an animal, providing a valid reason to declare the piles pure.
Key Distinction The physical possibility of removal is what separates the two cases — blood is immovable, a corpse-piece is not.

⚠️ Lashon Hara: What Is Permitted? (Rava)

Forbidden

  • Accepting the report as established fact
  • Shaming or punishing someone based on rumor alone
  • Treating the speaker as a reliable legal witness

Permitted — Rava

  • Being concerned and taking personal precautions
  • Protecting yourself from the potential danger
  • Lesson from Gedaliah: he dismissed Johanan's warning and was killed — one must act on it

🧵 Locating Lost Impurity or Forbidden Mixture in a Garment

Lost menstrual blood stain: examine the garment section by section — each part examined separately will reveal any remaining stain
↓
Lost seminal emission: if the garment is new, insert a needle into every part — wetness on the needle identifies the location
↓
Lost sha'atnez (wool + linen): dye the entire garment — wool and linen absorb dye differently, making the forbidden thread visible
↓
If dyeing fails to reveal the thread, the garment must be burned — it may not be worn while doubt remains
↓
Mar Zutra's leniency: a single flax thread lost in a wool cloak is permitted to wear — sha'atnez requires combing, spinning, and weaving together; a loose lost thread lacks this combined character

👴 Why Moses Feared Og — Abraham's Hidden Merit

וַיֹּאמֶר ה' אֶל מֹשֶׁה אַל תִּירָא אֹתוֹ (במדבר כ״א:ל״ד)
Rabbi Yoḥanan citing Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai Moses feared battling Og because Og was the one who escaped to inform Abraham that Lot had been captured (Genesis 14:13). Though Og's secret motive was selfish — hoping Abraham would die in battle so he could take Sarah — Og nonetheless earned a lasting merit from Abraham's household.

God's reassurance — "Do not fear him" — revealed that this merit had finally expired. The magnitude of the reassurance tells us the magnitude of what Moses feared.
