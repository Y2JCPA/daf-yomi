🩸 Fixed Cycles, Daily Exams & Key Exceptions

כל הנשים בחזקת טהרה לבעליהן — All women have presumptive status of purity for their husbands
Core Theme Niddah 11 asks: who must examine themselves, how often, and why? The daf opens by establishing that a veset kavua (fixed menstrual cycle) can be set even through unusual physical triggers like jumping. It then mandates twice-daily examinations for women with fixed cycles, requires examination cloths during intercourse, and explores crucial debates — Rav vs. Levi on the source of post-birth blood, and Beit Hillel's nuanced rules on virgin bleeding.

🦘 Can Jumping Establish a Fixed Menstrual Cycle?

Rav Huna
If a woman jumped and saw blood three times, she has established a veset kavua caused by jumping. Even if the third jump occurred on a Sunday, she has not thereby established a Sunday cycle — jumping alone is the cause.
Rav Ashi
Why must Rav Huna specify this? Lest you say the day caused the bleeding and she now bleeds every Sunday regardless of jumping. Rav Huna therefore clarifies: the jumping is the operative trigger, not the calendar day.
Gemara
A woman may also establish a combined veset — neither days alone nor jumps alone, but a specific day plus jumping. Only when both conditions coincide does her period begin.
⚖️ Ruling Three consistent occurrences of any trigger — even an unusual act like jumping — establishes a veset kavua. The cause is the trigger, not incidental circumstances (like the day of the week).

👶 Post-Birth Blood: One Source or Two?

Rav — One Source

  • Post-birth blood (impure) and blood of purity (pure) flow from the same physiological source
  • The Torah simply declared: post-birth blood is impure; purity-period blood is pure
  • Therefore: a woman in her purity period needs no examination — all blood is presumptively pure
  • Explains the mishna's exemption straightforwardly

Levi — Two Sources

  • Post-birth blood and purity-period blood originate from two distinct physiological sources
  • The impure source and the pure source operate independently
  • Challenge: at the end of the impure period she should require examination — lest the impure source has not yet ceased
  • Requires additional reasoning (Beit Shammai / alternate reading) to justify the mishna's blanket exemption

📋 The Twice-Daily Examination Requirement

All women — including those with a fixed cycle — must examine themselves daily to maintain ritual purity and protect their husbands
↓
⏰ Morning Examination — to determine whether she menstruated during the night
↓
🌆 Twilight Examination — to determine whether she menstruated during the day
↓
💏 Before Intercourse — a third examination is also required before relations with her husband
↓
Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel: The twice-daily exams apply specifically to handling ritually pure items (taharot). For her husband, she retains presumptive purity — no prior examination needed.
↓
Exemptions from daily exam: A menstruating woman (status already impure), a woman in the blood-of-purity period, and a virgin whose examination would be inconclusive

🧵 Are Examination Cloths Required During Intercourse?

Woman about to engage in intercourse
↓
Has fixed cycle (veset kavua)
✅ Must use examination cloths during intercourse to detect onset of flow
Menstruating woman
🚫 Exempt — impure status is already certain; intercourse is prohibited entirely
Post-birth purity period
✅ Exempt from cloths — blood during this period is presumptively pure (Rav: one source)
Virgin (hymen wound active)
✅ Exempt per Beit Hillel — blood attributed to torn hymen; examination would be inconclusive
↓
Beit Hillel's limit: Virgin becomes impure if (a) she stopped bleeding during intercourse and then saw blood again, or (b) one night passed without intercourse and she then saw blood
