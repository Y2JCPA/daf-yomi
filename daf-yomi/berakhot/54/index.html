📊 Short Cycles vs. Long Cycles — Structural Differences

Short Cycles (1:1 – 5:5)

  • 🔵 1:1 — 4 nights in 18-day frame; niddah and ziva days interleave so 3 consecutive ziva days never accumulate
  • 🔵 2:2 — Days 8 and 12 permitted; day 8 ends niddah, day 12 follows clean ziva days
  • 🔵 3:3 — Only days 8 & 9 ever permitted; from day 10 onward the pattern locks her out permanently
  • 🔵 4:4 — Day 8 only; pure days bridge niddah exit to ziva accumulation
  • 🔵 5:5 — Days 8, 9, 10 permitted; completes niddah with three clean ziva days before new blood

Long Cycles (7:7 – 10:10)

  • 🔴 7:7 — 7 of every 28 days (¼ of her life); pure days coincide exactly with days 8–14
  • 🔴 8:8 — 15 of 48 days claimed; Gemara objects: should be only 14, since menstrual days cannot count as clean ziva days
  • 🔴 Rav Adda bar Yitzḥak: Reject the premise — menstrual days on which she does not see blood DO count toward her seven clean days of ziva
  • 🔴 9:9 — 8 of 36 days; baraita resumes after the zava-who-gave-birth digression
  • 🔴 10:10 — Permitted days equal her days of flow; the cycle perfectly balances

📅 Should Day 19 Be Permitted? — The Rav Ashi Resolution

Gemara Objects
In the 2:2 pattern, day 18 is the last of the eleven ziva days. Why not also permit intercourse on day 19? The ziva period has already concluded!
Initial Answer
Day 19 is the eve of renewed menstrual days — the Rabbis decreed a prohibition against intercourse on the eve of expected menstruation.
Rav Ashi
In fact, day 19 is NOT rabinically forbidden at all! It is already the beginning of her new menstrual-day count, not merely its eve. The rabbinic decree does not apply here.
True Reason
The real reason day 19 is omitted: this woman's fixed pattern causes her to experience bleeding on day 19 regardless — it is impure due to her cycle, not due to any decree.
Rav Adda bar Yitzḥak
On the 8:8 debate: menstrual days during which she sees no blood DO count toward her seven clean days for ziva — therefore 15 permitted days is correct, not 14.
Halakhic Principle The transition between niddah days and ziva days requires careful tracking — a woman's individual pattern determines which days are structurally impure independent of any rabbinic decree.

👶 Zava Who Gave Birth — Uncertain Status & Offering

Woman saw blood on 2 consecutive days during ziva period
On day 3 she miscarries — does not know what she miscarried
↓
Scenario A: Gave birth to a human fetus
Birth retroactively cancels ziva status → she is a yoledet (new mother), NOT a zava gedola
Scenario B: Did not give birth to a viable fetus
The 2 ziva days + day 3 = confirmed zava gedola; must count 7 clean days and bring offering
↓
She brings one offering — but it is NOT eaten by the priests
Doubt: is it a ziva offering? A birth offering? Or entirely non-sacred?
↓
Rav Pappa: This baraita cannot resolve the general dilemma of a zava who gives birth — the baraita involves a unique variable: uncertainty of male vs. female birth
↓
Rav Huna b. Rav Yehoshua refutes Rav Pappa: Both male-birth and female-birth possibilities create real uncertainty — neither can be dismissed. The dilemma stands in full.

📜 Mishna — Blood of Niddah: Moist and Dry

דַּם הַנִּדָּה וּבְשַׂר הַמֵּת — מְטַמְּאִין לַחִין וִיבֵשִׁין
MISHNA · Niddah 54b The blood of a menstruating woman (niddah) and the flesh of a corpse transmit impurity by contact AND by carrying — both when moist AND when dry. By contrast, the gonorrhea-like discharge (ziva) of a zav or zava, the spittle of a zav, the urine of a zav, and doubtful blood of a zava — these transmit impurity ONLY when moist, NOT when dry. To determine if a dried substance can be restored to its moist state: soak it in lukewarm water for a full twenty-four-hour period. Rabbi Yosei disagrees: Cold water suffices — as long as it soaks overnight.

💧 Gemara: Deriving That Dry Menstrual Blood Transmits Impurity

Step 1 — Source for moist blood (Ḥizkiyya): A verse explicitly calls menstrual blood 'her flow' (נִדָּתָהּ) — clearly referring to liquid blood. Impurity when moist is straightforward.
↓
Step 2 — Source for dry blood (Rabbi Yitzḥak): The phrase 'with her impurity' (בְּנִדָּתָהּ) extends the ruling — even dried menstrual blood retains full impurity status.
↓
Objection: Perhaps 'dry' applies only to blood that was once moist and later dried — not blood that was dry from the very outset?
↓
Further Objection: If menstrual blood can transmit impurity by lying/sitting contact like a zava, one might wrongly conclude the niddah renders those items impure to a person and that person renders others impure — but she does not!
↓
Baraita Answer — 'The Zav' (הַזָּב) is exclusive: Leviticus 15:4 says 'Every bed upon which the zav lies shall be impure.' The definite article excludes a stone afflicted with leprosy AND menstrual blood from rendering lying/sitting items impure.
↓
Conclusion: Menstrual blood transmits impurity by contact and carrying (moist and dry) — but does NOT render lying/sitting items impure with the zav-level force. Scripture carefully distinguishes these categories.
