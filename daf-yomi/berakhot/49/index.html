🏺 Three Methods for Testing Vessel Holes

The Question: How do we determine if a broken earthenware vessel's hole is large enough for liquid to enter?
↓
First Tanna — Tub Method: Place vessel right-side-up in a tub filled with water. If water enters the vessel, the hole meets the entry-measure.
↓
Rabbi Yehuda — Inverted Tub Method: Turn the vessel upside-down into an empty tub, then cover the whole tub with water. Observe whether water seeps in from above.
↓
Rabbi Yehuda — Fire Method: Fill the vessel with liquid and place it on fire. If the fire 'holds back' the liquid (it does not drip out), the hole is only exit-size, not entry-size.
↓
Rabbi Yosei objects to the fire method: Heat causes liquid to contract — even a hole large enough for entry might not drip on fire. This makes the test unreliable!
↓
Ulla's distinction between the tanna methods: First tanna — vessel upright, water pressure pushes in from outside. Rabbi Yehuda — vessel inverted, water weight presses down from above. Different pressures yield different results.

💅 Extra Finger: Bone + Nail Combinations

Has Both Bone AND Nail

  • Full limb status
  • Transmits impurity through contact
  • Transmits impurity through movement
  • Transmits impurity in a tent (ohel)
  • Even if smaller than an olive-bulk
  • No dispute — complete limb

Has Bone, NO Nail

  • Rav (via Rav Ḥisda): transmits contact + movement impurity
  • Does NOT transmit tent (ohel) impurity
  • Rabbi Yoḥanan qualifies: only if the finger cannot be counted alongside the hand's other fingers
  • If it CAN be counted as a normal finger → treated as full limb
  • Disputed threshold between Rav and Rabbi Yoḥanan

Neither Bone nor Nail

  • Not considered a halakhic limb
  • No impurity of any kind transmitted
  • Mere flesh has no independent limb status
  • Completely disregarded for impurity purposes

🔩 Zav Treading vs. Corpse Impurity — Where They Overlap

Mishna
Any vessel susceptible to zav-treading impurity is also susceptible to corpse impurity. Zav-treading ⊆ corpse impurity.
Gemara
But the reverse is not true! There are vessels that receive corpse impurity but do NOT receive zav-treading impurity. What examples?
Baraita
A wooden trough (beit hashitin) or a soldier's shield — these are wooden vessels that receive corpse impurity, but are NOT designated for sitting or lying.
Verse (Lev. 15:6)
'He who sits on any object whereon the zav sits' — the verse specifies objects designated for sitting. A shield or trough is not used for sitting!
Conclusion
Therefore: zav-treading impurity applies only to sitting/lying vessels. Corpse impurity applies to all vessels. The categories overlap but are NOT equal.
מדרש הפסוק 'כל הכלי אשר ישב עליו הזב' — only designated sitting-objects, excluding functional tools like shields and troughs

👁️ Fit to Testify vs. Fit to Adjudicate

Mishna Rule: Fit to adjudicate → automatically fit to testify
(The greater qualification includes the lesser)
↓
But: Fit to testify does NOT always mean fit to adjudicate
↓
Added Case
One blind in one eye
✅ Can testify
❌ Cannot adjudicate capital cases
Rabbi Yoḥanan's Reasoning
Capital adjudication demands full perceptual capacity to 'judge righteously' — partial blindness disqualifies
↓
This final mishna elegantly echoes the daf's theme: adjudicating ⊃ testifying, just as entry-holes ⊃ exit-holes and bones ⊃ nails
