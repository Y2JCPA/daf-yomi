The Anointed Priest: Like the Public or Like the King?

The anointed priest (כהן משיח) is removed from the category of an individual
↓
To whom is he compared for liability?
Like the Tzibbur (Public)
Bull offering, no provisional asham → requires absence of awareness + unwitting action
Like the Nasi (King)
Goat for idol worship, brings definite asham → liable for unwitting action alone
↓
Verse resolves: "so as to bring guilt upon the people" (Lev. 4:3) — priest is like the tzibbur
KEY PRINCIPLE The anointed priest brings an offering only for absence of awareness of the matter together with unwitting performance of the action — just like the general public.

Who Is the Anointed Priest Like?

Like the Tzibbur (Public)

  • Both bring a bull as sin-offering
  • Neither brings a provisional guilt-offering (asham talui)
  • Both require absence of awareness + unwitting action
  • Verse: "so as to bring guilt upon the people" links them

Like the Nasi (King)

  • Both bring a female goat for unwitting idol worship
  • Both bring a definite guilt-offering for certain sins
  • King liable for unwitting action alone
  • This analogy is ultimately rejected
RESOLUTION Since either analogy is logically valid, the verse is needed to decide: the anointed priest is like the tzibbur.

What Does 'Sinned By Himself' Mean?

Abaye
The priest 'sinned by himself' means he and the court were in two different places issuing rulings about two different prohibitions.
Rava
Does being in two places make it 'by himself'? Even in one place — if they rule on different prohibitions, he sinned by himself!
Rava
The subject of the ruling determines independence, not the location of the ruling.
PRINCIPLE Rava holds: the determining factor is whether the priest and court ruled on the same prohibition, not whether they were in the same place.

Why the Priest Doesn't Bring an Asham Talui

Verse: "The priest shall atone for him for his unwitting act that he performed unwittingly" (Lev. 5:18)
↓
This applies only to one whose transgression and unwitting action are equal
Ordinary Person
Unwitting act = the transgression itself → brings provisional guilt-offering
Anointed Priest
Unwitting act = erroneous ruling, transgression = the action → they are not equal
↓
Therefore the anointed priest is excluded from the provisional guilt-offering (asham talui)
KEY DERIVATION The priest's "unwitting act" is his erroneous ruling, while the sin is the action — since these are not the same, the asham talui does not apply.

The Priest Bears Only His Own Sin

על חטאתו אשר חטא — הוא מביא על חטאו ואינו מביא על חטא אחרים
VERSE From "then he shall offer for his sin that he has sinned" (Lev. 4:3) — he brings an offering for the sin he himself committed based on his ruling, but not for sins that others committed based on his ruling. Unlike the court, whose offering atones for the public's transgression, the priest's offering is personal.
