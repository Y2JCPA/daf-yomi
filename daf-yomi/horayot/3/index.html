Who Bears Liability for Erroneous Court Rulings?

Shmuel
The mishna follows Rabbi Meir: an individual who sins based on the court's ruling is exempt.
Rabbis
An individual who sins based on a court ruling is liable to bring his own offering.
Rav Pappa
Perhaps everyone agrees the individual is exempt — the dispute is whether court members combine with the congregation to form a majority.
R' Yonatan
Even 100 judges must rule unanimously for the court to be liable — "the entire assembly".
Rav Huna b. Rav Hoshaya
This is logical — throughout Torah, majority equals entirety. Here it says "entire" — so unanimity is required.
Core Question When a court issues an erroneous ruling, does the individual who acted on it bear personal responsibility, or does the court's ruling shield him?

Rabbi Meir vs. The Rabbis — The Minority Who Sinned

Shmuel
The mishna is Rabbi Meir's view: an individual who sins based on the court's ruling is exempt.
Rabbis
An individual who sins on the basis of a court ruling is liable to bring his own offering.
Rav Pappa
Alternative 1: All agree the individual is exempt. The dispute is whether court members combine with the congregation to complete a majority.
Rav Pappa
Alternative 2: When a majority sinned, Rabbi Meir exempts individuals; the "Rabbis" (= R' Shimon) say both court and congregation bring offerings.
Rav Pappa
Alternative 3: The case involves a tribal court. Rabbi Meir says no liability for tribal court rulings; the "Rabbis" (= R' Yehuda) disagree.
Rav Pappa's Point There is no conclusive proof that the Rabbi Meir / Rabbis dispute relates to the mishna's case — multiple alternative readings exist.

Rabbi Yonatan's Unanimity Requirement — Refuted

R' Yonatan's Position

  • Even 100 judges must ALL agree for liability
  • Derived from: "the ENTIRE assembly" (Lev. 4:13)
  • If even one judge dissents — no offering
  • Supported by Rav Huna b. Rav Hoshaya: normally majority = entirety, but here 'entire' is explicit

Refutation (Conclusive)

  • Rabban Shimon b. Gamliel & R' Elazar b. R' Tzadok: don't decree unless majority can withstand
  • Derived from: "the ENTIRE nation" (Malachi 3:9)
  • Yet they treat majority as equivalent to entirety
  • Proves: 'entire' does NOT always require unanimity

Conclusion After Refutation

  • "Entire assembly" means: all judges must be PRESENT
  • But the ruling follows the MAJORITY among those present
  • If not all judges are present — it is not a valid ruling
Teyuvta (תיובתא) Rabbi Yonatan's opinion is conclusively refuted. The verse requires full attendance, not unanimous agreement.

The Dissenting Judge — A Special Case

שלא תלה בבית דין — He did not rely on the court
Mishna Principle A judge who knew the court erred, or a qualified student, who nevertheless sinned based on the ruling — is liable to bring his own individual offering.

This applies whether he sinned with the other judges, after them, or alone.

Reason: he did not associate his action with the court's ruling — since he knew it was wrong, the court's ruling cannot exempt him.
