Who Brings What? Sliding-Scale Offering Logic

Unwitting transgression requiring a sliding-scale offering (korban oleh v'yored)
↓
Individual
Wealthy → sheep
Poor → birds
Extreme poverty → meal-offering
King
Excluded from sliding-scale entirely — cannot become poor
Anointed Priest
R' Akiva: exempt from all sliding-scale offerings
R' Shimon: exempt only from extreme poverty tier
Core Principle The sliding-scale offering applies only to one who can experience both poverty and wealth — excluding the king and (according to some) the anointed priest.

Why King & High Priest Are Excluded from Sliding-Scale

👑 The King

  • Cannot come to a state of poverty
  • "He has only the Lord his God upon him" (Lev 4:22)
  • Greater than the entire nation
  • Not dependent on others

⛪ Anointed Priest

  • "Greatest among his brethren" (Lev 21:10)
  • Greater in beauty, power, wisdom, and wealth
  • If he lacks wealth — brethren make him wealthy
  • "Make him great from the property of his brethren"
Key Verse The Torah's language of "if his means suffice not" presupposes the possibility of poverty — which cannot apply to a king or High Priest.

A Leprous King: Still Exempt?

Ravina
If a king is afflicted with tzara'at and unfit to serve — what is his status regarding the sliding-scale offering?
Ravina
Was he completely eliminated from the obligation (permanently exempt)? Or merely exempted during his reign (now obligated)?
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
Does he bring the offering from your (public) property? No — he brings it from his personal treasure (gazza).
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
Since he still has his personal wealth, he remains exempt from the sliding-scale offering.
Principle The exemption follows the person's financial reality, not merely their title — since a leprous king retains his personal fortune, he remains exempt.

R' Akiva: Anointed Priest Exempt from All Sliding-Scale

Rava's reasoning for R' Akiva:
Leviticus 6:13 — "One-tenth of an ephah… on the day he is anointed"
↓
"This tenth of an ephah comes as his obligation — and no other"
↓
Challenge: Maybe only the meal-offering (extreme poverty) tier is excluded?
↓
Leviticus 5:13 — "from one of these"
One who gains atonement with every tier gains atonement with any; one who doesn't — doesn't.
↓
Result
Since the anointed priest cannot bring the meal-offering tier, he is exempt from all tiers of the sliding-scale offering
Abaye & Rava R' Akiva derives this from "from one" (מֵאַחַת, Lev 5:13) but not from "of one" (מֵאַחַת, Lev 5:5) — the placement at the end of the passage, by the meal-offering, is what teaches the all-or-nothing rule.

Mishna Summary: Who Brings What for Unwitting Karet-Violations?

PersonOffering for Unwitting SinNotes
IndividualEwe or female goatStandard fixed sin-offering
KingMale goat (שָׂעִיר)Unique offering; never sliding-scale
Anointed PriestBull (פָּר)Only for ruling-based errors
Court (Sanhedrin)Bull + male goatFor erroneous communal rulings
Note These are for sins whose intentional violation carries karet — the sliding-scale offering applies to a different set of transgressions (hearing of a voice, oath of utterance, tum'at Mikdash).

Why Is the High Priest Exempt from Tum'at Mikdash?

R' Shimon
The anointed priest is not liable for defiling the Temple or its sacrificial foods.
Ḥizkiyya
Numbers 19:20 says the impure person is "excised from the congregation" — this applies to one whose offering equals the congregation's.
Ḥizkiyya
The High Priest's Yom Kippur offering (a bull) differs from the congregation's (goat) — so he is excluded.
Gemara
But then the king should also be exempt — he brings a goat, not the same as the congregation!
Gemara
The king's Yom Kippur atonement is achieved through the same offerings as the congregation — he is included.
Rava
Better answer: "One whose sin-offering equals that of individuals" — the High Priest brings his unique sin-offering only for ruling-based errors, unlike anyone else.
R' Shimon's View The High Priest's unique sin-offering mechanism (bull for his own erroneous ruling) sets him apart from both individuals and the congregation, exempting him from tum'at Mikdash liability.
