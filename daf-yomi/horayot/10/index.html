Horayot 10 — Status, Repentance & The Same Path

DAF OVERVIEW This daf concludes the discussion of how appointment and removal affect sin-offering obligations for kings and High Priests. It then pivots to powerful aggadic teachings: R' Yoḥanan ben Zakkai praises a generation whose king brings offerings for unwitting sin, Rava reinterprets the suffering of the righteous, and R' Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish debate how the same action can be righteous or sinful depending on intent.

A Fortiori Reasoning: Why Both Verses Are Needed

❓ Can we derive the priest's rule from the king's?
↓
King loses his special offering after leaving office → Kal va-ḥomer: Priest (whose standard is stricter) should also lose his!
↓
Torah writes: "ḥattato asher ḥata" — he brings his offering even after leaving priesthood
↓
❓ Now reverse: Can we derive the king's rule from the priest's?
↓
Priest keeps his offering after leaving → Kal va-ḥomer: King (whose standard is lighter) should also keep his!
↓
Torah writes: "asher nasi yeḥeta" — only when he is king
PRINCIPLE Neither rule can be derived from the other — both verses are necessary because the kal va-ḥomer works in both directions.

"Ashrei" — Happy Is the Generation Whose King Repents

R' Yoḥanan ben Zakkai
The verse says "asher nasi yeḥeta" — read it as ashrei (happy)! Happy is the generation whose king brings an offering for his unwitting sin.
R' Yoḥanan ben Zakkai
If the king brings an offering, how much more so will a commoner atone! If he atones for unwitting sin, how much more will he repent for intentional sin!
Rava b"R Rabba
But asher appears elsewhere — "asher ḥata min ha-kodesh" and regarding Jeroboam "asher ḥata va-asher heḥeti" — is that also 'happy'?!
Gemara
Here it is different — the Torah altered its formulation from the usual "im" (if) to "asher" (when/that), signaling the homiletic reading.
INSIGHT True leadership is measured by willingness to acknowledge error. A generation is blessed when its ruler models accountability rather than impunity.

Righteous Suffering: Rav Naḥman bar Ḥisda vs. Rava

Rav Naḥman bar Ḥisda

  • Interprets Ecclesiastes 8:14
  • "Ashrei" the righteous who suffer in this world like the wicked in the World-to-Come
  • Woe to the wicked who enjoy this world like the righteous in the World-to-Come
  • Suffering here = reward later

Rava

  • Challenges: Would two good worlds be bad for the righteous?!
  • "Ashrei" the righteous who enjoy this world like the wicked enjoy this world
  • Woe to the wicked who suffer in this world like the righteous suffer in this world
  • Prosperity here is also a blessing
RAV PAPPA & RAV HUNA Rava asked his students: Did you master tractates? Yes. Did you become somewhat wealthy? Yes. He proclaimed: Ashrei the righteous who enjoy this world too!

"The Righteous Walk in Them" — One Path, Two Outcomes

R' Yoḥanan
Hosea 14:10 — "The ways of the Lord are right; the righteous walk in them and transgressors stumble in them." Like two people eating the Paschal lamb: one for the mitzva, one for gluttony.
Reish Lakish
You call the glutton wicked?! He still fulfilled the mitzva of eating the korban Pesaḥ! He's not a transgressor.
Reish Lakish
Better analogy: Two men in dark rooms — one's wife comes to him, one's sister comes. Same action, vastly different outcomes.
Gemara
But those are two paths, not one! The verse speaks of the same path. Better: Lot and his daughters — they intended a mitzva; he intended transgression. One act, one path, two intents.
HOSEA 14:10 The deepest teaching of the daf: the same action can be righteous or sinful. What determines the outcome is not the deed but the intention (kavvanah) behind it.
