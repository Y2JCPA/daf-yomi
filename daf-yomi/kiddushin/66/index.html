One Witness, Silence & Credibility

One witness testifies about a prohibition
↓
Does the accused remain silent?
Yes — Silent
Abaye: Silence = admission → witness is credible
No — Denies
Witness is not credible against denial (generally)
↓
But what about matters of forbidden relations (עריות)?
Abaye
Even in עריות, one trusted witness + silence = credible
Rava
Forbidden relations always require two witnesses — no exceptions
Core Issue Does silence create credibility for a lone witness, and does this extend to the most serious areas of halakha?

Abaye's Three Cases: Why Each Is Necessary

CaseWitness Says…Why Silence Might NOT Be AdmissionWhy It's Still Needed
Forbidden Fat (חלב)"You ate ḥelev"He wouldn't bring a non-sacred animal to the Temple unless he believed the witnessStrongest case — silence clearly = conviction
Ritual Impurity (טומאה)"Your pure food was made impure"He may stay silent because he can still eat the food on impure days — minimal lossTeaches credibility even when accused suffers little
Bestiality with Ox"Your ox was used for bestiality"Owner thinks: only matters if I wanted to sacrifice it; one witness can't kill the oxTeaches credibility even when consequence seems remote
Principle Each case addresses a unique reason the accused might stay silent without actually admitting — Abaye must specify all three to close every loophole.

Can One Witness Testify About Adultery?

Abaye
If a husband is told by one witness that his wife committed adultery, and the husband remains silent — the witness is credible.
Rava
The witness is not credible. This involves עריות (forbidden relations) — testimony about sexual prohibitions requires two witnesses, no exceptions.
Abaye
I have a proof: Mar Shmuel told a blind man — if the messenger is trusted by you, divorce your wife based on his lone testimony about adultery!
Rava
Mar Shmuel meant: trusted by you like two witnesses — a personal standard of trust, not a halakhic rule about one witness.
Key Dispute Abaye extends his silence principle to adultery; Rava draws a firm line at עריות, requiring two witnesses regardless of silence.

The Deficient Mikveh of Diskim

R' Tarfon
The mikveh had presumptive status (ḥezkat kashrut) of being complete. You cannot retroactively invalidate immersions performed when we presumed it was valid!
R' Akiva
The person who immersed had presumptive status of being ritually impure. You cannot purify him based on an uncertain immersion!
R' Tarfon
A parable: A priest sacrificing on the altar is found to be the son of a divorcée — his prior service is valid. So too, prior immersions are valid!
R' Akiva
Better parable: A priest found to be blemished — his prior service is disqualified. The mikveh's deficiency is intrinsic, like a blemish!
R' Tarfon
"Akiva — anyone who separates from you, it is as though he has separated from life itself." I accept your reasoning.
Ruling R' Akiva prevails: the mikveh's own deficiency (intrinsic) parallels a blemished priest — all prior immersions are retroactively invalid.

Blemished Priest vs. Son of a Divorcée

Blemished Priest (בעל מום)

  • Disqualification by one witness
  • Defect is intrinsic — in the priest himself
  • Can be verified: "Remove your clothes and show us"
  • Prior service: invalid retroactively

Son of Divorcée/Ḥalutzah

  • Disqualification requires two witnesses
  • Defect is external — through his mother
  • Cannot be verified by examining the priest himself
  • Prior service: valid after the fact
R' Akiva's Logic Intrinsic, verifiable disqualifications (blemish, deficient mikveh) need only one witness. Disqualifications through others (mother's status) require two.
