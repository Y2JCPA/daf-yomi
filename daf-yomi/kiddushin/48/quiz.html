QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Shmuel, what happens when someone sells a promissory note and then forgives the debtor?
A. The forgiveness is invalid because the note was sold
B. The debt is forgiven because the obligation is non-transferable
C. The buyer can sue the seller for fraud
D. The debtor must pay both parties
Correct: B — Shmuel rules that a promissory note represents a personal obligation to the creditor. Even after selling the note, the original creditor (or his heir) can forgive the debt, because the debtor's obligation runs to the creditor alone.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In Rav Huna's rule about transferring money through three parties (ma'amad sheloshtan), what is the dispute between R' Meir and the Rabbis?
A. Whether all three must be present
B. Whether the rule applies to loans or only deposits
C. Whether women can be the third party
D. Whether the transfer requires a kinyan
Correct: B — Both agree the rule works for deposits (where the item is intact). The Rabbis limit it to deposits only, while R' Meir extends it to loans as well, since he sees no difference between loan and deposit for this purpose.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the concept of 'kinyan umman b'shevach kli' (craftsman acquires through enhancement)?
A. A craftsman can keep any item he repairs
B. A craftsman gains partial ownership of an object by improving it
C. A craftsman's tools become part of the object
D. A craftsman can charge whatever he wants for improvements
Correct: B — R' Meir holds that a craftsman acquires ownership rights through the enhancement he adds to a vessel. This matters for betrothal because if the craftsman partly owns the improved item, he transfers real property rather than merely forgiving a debt (loan).
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In the Mishna's case where a man says 'Be betrothed with this silver dinar' and it turns out to be gold, why does the Tanna Kamma rule she is NOT betrothed?
A. Gold is worth less than silver
B. She was deceived even though the item is more valuable — there was no meeting of minds
C. Gold cannot be used for betrothal
D. The man committed fraud and betrothal requires honesty
Correct: B — The Tanna Kamma holds that any discrepancy between what was stated and what was given invalidates betrothal, even if the actual item is more valuable. The betrothal requires that both parties agree on the same terms — a 'meeting of minds.'
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Reish Lakish resolve whether a betrothal document must be written 'for her sake' (lishma)?
A. He compares it to betrothal by money, which needs no lishma
B. He leaves the question unresolved (teiku)
C. He derives from Deuteronomy 24:2 that 'becoming' is juxtaposed to 'leaving,' requiring lishma
D. He rules it is a rabbinic requirement only
Correct: C — Reish Lakish resolves his own dilemma from Deuteronomy 24:2: 'She departs...and becomes another man's wife.' The verse juxtaposes becoming (betrothal) to leaving (divorce) — just as a get must be written for her sake, so must a betrothal document.
