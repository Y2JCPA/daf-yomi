Kiddushin 6 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Shmuel, why is 'I am hereby your man' not a valid betrothal?
A. The Torah requires formulas directed at the woman's status change
B. It was not said in front of witnesses
C. The man did not give her money
D. It is only valid in Judea
Correct: A — Shmuel derives from 'when a man takes a woman' that valid betrothal language must address the woman's changed status, not the man's declaration about himself.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the baraita split the six valid betrothal expressions into two groups of three?
A. The first three are biblical and the second three are rabbinic
B. The Tanna heard them as two separate teachings from his teachers
C. The first three work without witnesses, the second require witnesses
D. The first three are for betrothal and the second for marriage
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that the Tanna received each set of three as a separate halakha from his teachers, so he preserved the original format of two groups.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why can't a man betroth a woman with a loan he previously gave her?
A. Loans between potential spouses are prohibited
B. The loan is an obligation, not present money — nothing is given at the time of betrothal
C. She must first repay the loan before accepting betrothal
D. A loan can never have the status of kesef kiddushin
Correct: B — Abaye rules that betrothal requires actual value given at the time of kiddushin. A loan is merely an outstanding obligation — the money was given previously and is no longer present.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Ravina prove that 'You are hereby for yourself' is a valid divorce formula?
A. From a verse in Deuteronomy about sending away a wife
B. From a kal va-ḥomer: if it frees a slave whose body is owned, it certainly divorces a wife
C. From the practice in Judea where this phrase was commonly used
D. From R' Yosei's ruling that context makes intent clear
Correct: B — Ravina argues a fortiori: if 'you are for yourself' can free a Canaanite slave — whose very body belongs to the master — it certainly suffices to divorce a wife, whose body is not owned by the husband.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rava, if one gives 100 dinars as terumah on condition the priest returns them, what is the result?
A. It is completely invalid — the terumah obligation is not fulfilled
B. It is valid and fully permitted
C. The obligation is technically fulfilled, but it is prohibited to do this
D. It is valid only if the priest agrees in advance
Correct: C — Rava rules that unlike betrothal or purchase, the terumah obligation is technically fulfilled because it requires only the act of giving. However, it is prohibited because the priest appears to be 'assisting at the threshing floor' — giving a favor in exchange for being chosen.
