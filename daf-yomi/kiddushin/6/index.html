The Language of Kiddushin: From Clear to Ambiguous

Man says a betrothal formula to a woman
↓
Clear Expressions
"You are my wife / betrothed / acquired to me" → Betrothed
Secondary Expressions
"You are mine / under my authority / bound to me" → Betrothed
Ambiguous Expressions
"You are my unique one / helper / rib..." → Dilemma
↓
Key question: Was he already speaking with her about betrothal?
↓
Yes — Context exists
Even silence + money suffices per R' Yosei. Ambiguous words may be for labor, not betrothal.
No — No context
She doesn't know what he means → Not betrothed
Core Principle The Torah says "when a man takes a woman" — the man must act upon the woman's status, not declare his own. Valid formulas address her new status.

Shmuel's Ruling: Man-Directed vs. Woman-Directed Language

Shmuel
If he says "I am your man" or "I am not your man" — these are nothing. No betrothal, no divorce.
Gemara
Why not? Because the Torah writes "when a man takes a woman" — he acts on her status.
Gemara
And for divorce: "and sends her" — he sends her, not himself from her.
Shmuel
The novelty is that these man-directed formulas have no concern at all — not even a doubt (safek).
Principle Valid betrothal language must be directed at the woman's status, not the man's. The Torah's phrasing sets the template.

Two Sets of Valid Betrothal Expressions (Baraita)

Set 1 — PrimarySet 2 — Secondary
"You are hereby my wife" (ishti)"You are hereby mine" (sheli)
"You are hereby my betrothed" (mekudeshet li)"You are hereby under my authority" (birshuti)
"You are hereby acquired to me" (knuyah li)"You are hereby bound to me" (tzrurah li)
Why Two Groups? The Tanna heard them as two separate teachings from his teachers, so he preserved them as two sets of three — though all six are equally valid.

The Ambiguous Expressions: Betrothal Dilemmas

Resolved

  • "You are my taken one" (lekuḥati) → Betrothed, from "when a man takes"
  • "You are my espoused one" (ḥarufati) → Betrothed, from Lev. 19:20 neḥerefet
  • In Judea: ḥarufa = betrothed woman (local usage confirms)

Unresolved (Teyku)

  • "You are my unique one" (meyuḥedet)
  • "You are my designated one" (mezumenet)
  • "You are my helper / counterpart" (ezer / neged)
  • "You are my rib" (tzela)
  • "You are my closed one / beneath me"
  • "You are my seized one" (tefisat yadi)
Key Factor The dilemma: did he say these words for betrothal or to hire her for labor? Context alone may not clarify intent.

Context Requirement: R' Yosei vs. R' Yehuda

He was speaking with her about betrothal matters
↓
He then gives her money or a document
↓
But he says nothing — or says an ambiguous phrase
↓
R' Yosei
Sufficient — context makes intent clear. Halakha follows R' Yosei (per Shmuel).
R' Yehuda
Insufficient — he must explicitly clarify his action.
So Why the Dilemmas? If context + silence suffices, why ask about ambiguous words? Because his words might indicate labor, not betrothal — actively contradicting the context.

'You Are For Yourself': Divorce or Just Earnings?

Gemara
If a husband says "You are hereby for yourself" — is she divorced? Or did he just mean she can keep her earnings?
Ravina
A baraita teaches: the core of a bill of manumission is "You are a freeman" or "You are for yourself."
Ravina
Kal va-ḥomer: A slave whose body is owned — "for yourself" frees him. A wife whose body is not owned — certainly divorced!
Rav Naḥman
Similarly: "I have no business with you" = valid manumission, proven from the case of selling a slave to a gentile.
Proof from Slavery Rav Sheshet: When a master sells a slave to a gentile, the document reads "When you escape, I have no business with you" — confirming this as a valid emancipation formula.
