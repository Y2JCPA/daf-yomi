Convert Who Married a Mamzeret

R' Shimon b. Lakish
There is betrothal without transgression — a convert may marry a mamzeret — yet offspring follows the flawed lineage (mamzer).
R' Yosei (Baraita)
A convert who married a mamzeret — the offspring is a mamzer.
R' Yochanan
The Mishna follows R' Yehuda, who says a convert may not marry a mamzeret — so there is a transgression!
Gemara
If so, why doesn't the Mishna list this case explicitly?
R' Yochanan
The phrase "Any case" (kol she...) in the latter clause serves to include this case.
Dispute R' Yosei: convert + mamzeret is permitted but offspring is mamzer. R' Yehuda: the marriage itself is forbidden.

Why the Mishna Cannot List the Ḥalala

היכי ליתני? בת כהן ובת לוי ובת ישראל וחללה שנישאו לכהן — חללה לכהן מי שריא?
Structural Problem The Mishna lists: "A daughter of a Kohen, Levi, or Israelite who married a Kohen, Levi, or Israelite."

To add ḥalala to this list would require saying she married a Kohen — but a ḥalala is forbidden to a Kohen! The sentence structure cannot accommodate this case without implying a permitted union that is actually forbidden.

Therefore the Mishna uses the phrase "Any case where there is betrothal and no transgression" to include this implicitly.

R' Yosei vs. R' Yehuda: Reading the Mishna

R' Yehuda

  • Convert may NOT marry a mamzeret
  • "Any case" (latter clause) → includes convert + mamzeret
  • "In which case" (latter clause) → stylistic parallel only
  • All phrases in the Mishna serve a distinct legal function

R' Yosei

  • Convert MAY marry a mamzeret
  • "In which case" (first clause) → excludes convert + mamzeret
  • "Any case" (latter clause) → stylistic parallel only
  • Some phrases exist for consistency, not legal derivation
Resolution The Gemara concludes that stylistic consistency (lo zu af zu) can explain extra phrases — since the first clause uses "Any case," the latter clause mirrors it.
