QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to R' Yehuda, why does the offspring of a convert and mamzeret follow the flawed lineage?
A. Because there is betrothal AND a transgression — a convert may not marry a mamzeret
B. Because there is no valid betrothal at all between them
C. Because converts always produce mamzer offspring
D. Because the mamzeret's status overrides all other lineage rules
Correct: A — R' Yehuda holds that a convert may not marry a mamzeret, so this is a case of betrothal with a transgression, and the offspring follows the flawed lineage.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why can't the Mishna explicitly list the case of a ḥalala marrying a Kohen, Levi, or Israelite?
A. Because a ḥalala is forbidden to a Kohen, so the sentence structure would imply a permitted union that is actually forbidden
B. Because the ḥalala case was unknown to the Tanna
C. Because a ḥalala has no lineage status at all
D. Because ḥalala status only applies to daughters of Kohanim
Correct: A — The Mishna's sentence lists women who married a Kohen, Levi, or Israelite. Adding ḥalala would incorrectly imply she could marry a Kohen, which is forbidden.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Ravin's ruling, what happens when an Ammonite man and an Egyptian woman (both converts) have children?
A. All children follow the father and are Ammonite
B. A male child follows the father (Ammonite); a female child follows the mother (2nd-gen Egyptian)
C. All children follow the mother and are Egyptian
D. Children have no national status restrictions after conversion
Correct: B — After conversion, we follow the flawed lineage. A male is cast after his Ammonite father (permanently barred); a female is cast after her Egyptian mother (2nd-generation, temporarily restricted).
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the significance of the term "another" (ish aḥer) in Deuteronomy 24:2?
A. It teaches that a woman may remarry after divorce
B. It derives that betrothal with forbidden relatives does not take effect at all
C. It limits divorce to cases where the husband is still alive
D. It establishes that only men can initiate betrothal
Correct: B — The emphasis on "another" teaches that she can become the wife of "others" but not of relatives — meaning kiddushin with forbidden relatives is legally void.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does the Gemara resolve the objection that both the "shall not take" verse and the "another" verse might both refer only to a father's wife (husband's son)?
A. It shows that the two verses are in different books of the Torah and cannot refer to the same case
B. It uses a kal va-ḥomer from wife's sister to prove ab initio invalidity, freeing "another" to address all relatives
C. It cites a separate baraita that explicitly lists all forbidden relatives
D. It demonstrates that the husband's son case is only rabbinic, not biblical
Correct: B — The Gemara derives ab initio invalidity for the son's case from a kal va-ḥomer: if wife's sister (karet) cannot be betrothed, certainly a father's wife (capital) is void ab initio. This frees "another" to teach about all relatives.
