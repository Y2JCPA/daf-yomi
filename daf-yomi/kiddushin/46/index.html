Can a Minor Girl Refuse Her Own Betrothal?

Rav
A minor betrothed without her father's consent — either she or her father can prevent it from taking effect.
Rav Asi
Only her father can prevent it — she cannot, since she initially agreed to the betrothal.
Rav Huna
Objection to Rav Asi! The verse "ma'en yema'en" (Exodus 22:16) teaches that even a seduced woman can refuse marriage — surely a minor can too!
Rav
Don't cite this proof — Rav Asi can answer that the baraita discusses seduction not for the sake of marriage.
Abaye
Even if he seduced her for marriage, she still requires a new betrothal with her father's consent, since she is a minor.
KEY DISPUTE Does a minor's initial agreement lock her in? Rav says no — consent can be withdrawn by either party. Rav Asi says her agreement is binding, only the father can object.

The Loan Problem: Eating Dates One by One

He says: "Be betrothed with this, and this, and this"
(combined betrothal)
↓
She eats each date as she receives it
↓
Until all dates are given, each one is considered a loan
(she'd have to return them if he retracts)
↓
By the time the last date is given,
the earlier ones are already consumed
↓
R' Yochanan: "There is a table, meat, and a knife —
and we have nothing to eat!"
The mishna is clear but unexplainable.
↓
Rav & Shmuel: Money given for a betrothal
that doesn't take effect must be returned — it's a loan, not a gift.
PRINCIPLE One cannot betroth a woman with a loan (halva'ah). Consumed items that were part of an incomplete betrothal revert to loan status.

Betrothing One's Sister: What Happens to the Money?

Rav — It's a Deposit

  • A person knows betrothal cannot take effect with his sister
  • He gave the money intending it as a deposit (pikadon)
  • Why not say so explicitly? He feared she wouldn't accept a deposit
  • Result: The money must be returned

Shmuel — It's a Gift

  • A person knows betrothal cannot take effect with his sister
  • He gave the money intending it as a gift (matana)
  • Why not say so explicitly? He feared it would embarrass her to accept a gift
  • Result: She may keep the money
UNDERLYING QUESTION When someone performs a legally impossible act, do we interpret their hidden intent as generosity (gift) or caution (deposit)?

Why Flour Separated as Challah Must Be Returned

📜 Torah requires challah from dough, not flour (Numbers 15:20)
↓
🚫 One separates flour as challah — separation is invalid
↓
🤔 Shmuel's logic: He knew it's invalid, so it should be a gift to the priest!
↓
⚠️ Gemara: No — the priest might combine this flour with his own
↓
🧮 If combined total exceeds 5/4 kav, he'll think his dough is exempt from challah
↓
🍞 He'll eat untithed bread — a serious violation!
↓
↩️ Therefore: the flour must be returned to prevent this error
DISTINCTION Unlike betrothing a sister, the challah case has downstream consequences — a ruinous situation (takkala) may emerge if the priest keeps the flour.
