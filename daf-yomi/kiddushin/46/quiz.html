5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav, who can prevent a minor girl's betrothal (done without her father's consent) from taking effect?
A. Only her father
B. Only the girl herself
C. Either she or her father
D. Neither — it takes effect automatically
Correct: C — Rav holds that either the minor girl or her father can prevent the betrothal from taking effect, since it was done without proper consent.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In the Mishna, if a man says 'Be betrothed with this, and this, and this' while giving dates — what is the peruta requirement?
A. Each date must be worth a peruta
B. Only the last date must be worth a peruta
C. All dates together must equal a peruta
D. No peruta is required at all
Correct: C — When using combined language ('with this, and this, and this'), the dates are treated as one act of betrothal, so their combined value must reach a peruta.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does R' Yochanan say 'there is a table, meat, and a knife, and we have nothing to eat' about the mishna?
A. He disagrees with the mishna's ruling
B. The mishna is clear but its reasoning cannot be explained
C. He thinks the mishna is corrupted
D. He is making a joke about the dates
Correct: B — R' Yochanan means that all the elements of the mishna are explicit and visible, yet he cannot find a satisfactory explanation for how the case of eating dates one by one works, given the loan problem.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Shmuel, why is money given to betroth one's sister considered a gift rather than a deposit?
A. Because betrothal money is always a gift if it fails
B. Because he knows betrothal can't work and intended it as a gift, but was too embarrassed to say so
C. Because the Torah requires it to be treated as a gift
D. Because the sister earned it by agreeing to the betrothal
Correct: B — Shmuel reasons that a person knows he cannot betroth his sister, so he must have intended the money as a gift. He didn't say so explicitly because he thought she'd be embarrassed to accept an outright gift.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why must flour separated as challah be returned to its owner, unlike the case of teruma from a perforated pot for a non-perforated pot?
A. Because challah is more stringent than teruma
B. Because the priest might combine it with his own flour and mistakenly think no further challah is needed
C. Because flour has no monetary value
D. Because the owner never intended it as a gift
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that a 'ruinous situation' (takkala) may emerge: the priest might add this flour to his own, exceed the minimum amount requiring challah separation, and eat untithed bread — thinking it was already properly tithed.
