Can One Acquire What Doesn't Yet Exist?

R' Yehuda HaNasi
"You shall not deliver a slave to his master" — this refers to one who buys a slave in order to free him. The manumission takes effect even before acquisition!
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
How? The master wrote: "When I purchase you, you are acquired to yourself from now." This proves Rebbi holds one can transfer a davar shelo ba la'olam.
R' Meir
If a man says to a woman: "Be betrothed to me after I convert" or "after your husband dies" — she is betrothed.
Sages
She is not betrothed. One cannot betroth a woman who is currently forbidden — neither after conversion nor after a husband's death.
R' Yehuda HaNasi
By Torah law she is betrothed, but the Sages said no — due to enmity (mishum eivah). Betrothing a married woman pending her husband's death breeds hatred.
Key Concept Davar shelo ba la'olam — R' Meir and R' Yehuda HaNasi both hold that one can transfer or create legal status regarding something not yet in existence.

Conditional Betrothal: Services as Payment

Man says: "Be betrothed to me on condition I speak to the authorities for you"
↓
Did he fulfill the condition?
Yes — he spoke/worked
✅ She is betrothed
No — he did not
❌ She is not betrothed
↓
Reish Lakish: He must also give her a peruta at the time of betrothal. The service is merely a condition, not the betrothal money.
↓
Gemara challenges: Baraita says "by the monetary value of the ride" — implying the service itself can be the betrothal money!
Dispute Can the monetary value of future labor serve as kiddushin? The Gemara brings two baraitot against Reish Lakish, showing cases where service value alone creates betrothal.

Past vs. Future Services as Betrothal

ScenarioPast ServiceFuture Service
Riding a donkey❌ Not betrothed (it's a loan)✅ Betrothed (new benefit)
Sitting on a wagon (karon)❌ Not betrothed✅ Betrothed
Riding in a boat❌ Not betrothed✅ Betrothed
Dancing / entertainingCourt appraises — if worth a peruta, betrothed—
Building work (dimus)Court appraises value—
Principle A debt from a past service is like a loan (milveh) and cannot serve as betrothal money. But the value of a future service is a new benefit that can serve as kiddushin.

"On Condition My Father Wants" — Three Interpretations

Initial Reading

  • Father's silence = consent
  • Problem: If son dies, why instruct father to object? He already was silent!

R' Yannai's Reading

  • Condition means: father will not object at any point
  • Mishna has two different cases (two reasons)
  • Reish Lakish: R' Yannai prefers unified authorship even at the cost of multiple scenarios

Rav Yosef bar Ami

  • One unified case throughout the mishna
  • Condition means: father won't object within 30 days
  • Silence for 30 days = acceptance
Methodological Principle Reish Lakish derives from R' Yannai: it is preferable to force a mishna into two scenarios under one author rather than attribute it to two different tannaim.

Father Says: 'I Betrothed My Daughter but Forgot to Whom'

Mishna
A man comes forward and says: "I betrothed her." He is deemed credible (ne'eman).
Rav
Credible to give a get — no one sins without personal gain. But not credible to marry her — his desire may have overcome him (yitzro tolkfo).
Rav Asi
Credible even to marry her. A man would not dare lie directly to the girl's father (lo mechatzif inish).
Gemara
The mishna says if two claim her, one gives a get and the other may marry her. Refutation of Rav?
Rav
When two people claim her, each is afraid to lie because the other might expose him. That's different from a single claimant.
Baraita supports Rav Asi A baraita rules that a single claimant is believed even to marry her. But if a woman says she was betrothed and a man claims her — he is not believed to marry, because she may be protecting him.
