QUESTION 1 OF 5
What principle does the Gemara repeatedly attempt to prove on Amud A?
A. Devarim shebalev einam devarim — unspoken thoughts are not legally significant
B. Ein adam meisim atzmo rasha — a person does not incriminate himself
C. Rov — we follow the majority
D. Umdena — we assess a person's likely intent
Correct: A — The Gemara brings five attempted proofs that דברים שבלב אינם דברים — unspoken matters in the heart are not significant matters — each time rejected with an alternative explanation.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why is coercing someone to say 'I want to bring my offering' NOT a proof for devarim shebalev?
A. Because the Torah doesn't require verbal consent
B. Because deep down he genuinely wants atonement
C. Because coerced statements are always invalid
D. Because offerings don't require intent
Correct: B — The Gemara rejects the proof because the person truly desires atonement (כפרה) deep down, so his verbal consent reflects genuine inner will — it's not a case of words contradicting thoughts.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does the mishna need to teach all three cases (two women/one peruta, less than a peruta, minor)?
A. Each involves a different type of invalid betrothal
B. Each progressively strengthens the argument that later gifts are NOT for betrothal
C. The three cases correspond to three different Tannaitic opinions
D. Each applies in a different geographic region
Correct: B — Each case is progressively stronger — with a minor, everyone knows the betrothal is void, so you'd most expect later gifts to be intended as new betrothal. The mishna teaches that even so, the gifts are attributed to the original failed betrothal.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rav Pappa, when should we be concerned that gifts may constitute betrothal?
A. Always, regardless of local custom
B. Only when the gifts are worth more than a peruta
C. In a place where the custom is to betroth first, then send gifts
D. Only when witnesses are present at the time of sending
Correct: C — Rav Pappa rules based on local custom: where men typically betroth first and then send gifts, gifts imply a completed betrothal. Where gifts precede betrothal, there is no such concern.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In the me'ilah case, why can't the owner simply claim his misuse of consecrated property was intentional to avoid bringing an offering?
A. Because intentional misuse requires a more severe offering
B. Because the court would not believe such a claim
C. Because a person does not make himself out to be wicked
D. Because witnesses already testified to his intent
Correct: C — The Gemara invokes the principle אין אדם משים עצמו רשע — a person does not place himself in the category of a wicked person. Claiming intentional misuse of hekdesh would be self-incriminating, so this escape route is not psychologically realistic.
