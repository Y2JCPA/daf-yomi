Kiddushin 50: Thoughts vs. Words

CORE QUESTION The Gemara launches a sweeping inquiry: Are unspoken thoughts in the heart legally significant (דברים שבלב)? Through five attempted proofs — from coerced offerings, forced divorce, mistaken betrothal, conditional kiddushin, and misuse of consecrated property — the sugya tests whether inner intent can override explicit statements. Amud B shifts to whether gifts sent after a failed betrothal can themselves effect kiddushin.

The Paradox of Coerced Consent

A man pledged a burnt-offering (עולה) but refuses to bring it
↓
The verse says both "he shall offer it" (coerce him) AND "according to his will"
↓
Resolution: They coerce him until he says 'I want to'
↓
Proof?
His heart disagrees → proves unspoken thoughts are not significant
Rejection
He truly wants atonement deep down — his consent is genuine
LEVITICUS 1:3 "He shall offer it... according to his will before the Lord" — the Torah itself embeds the tension between coercion and willingness.

Five Attempts to Prove Devarim Shebalev Einam Devarim

Gemara
Attempt 1: Coerced korban — he says 'I want' but doesn't mean it. Proof that unspoken thoughts don't matter?
Rejection
No — deep down he wants atonement. His consent is real.
Gemara
Attempt 2: Coerced gittin and manumission — same logic applies?
Rejection
No — it's a mitzvah to listen to the Sages. He truly wants to comply.
Rav Yosef
Attempt 3: Betrothal with mistaken assumptions — she's betrothed despite his wrong beliefs.
Abaye
No — she only requires a get as a stringency, not definitively betrothed.
Abaye
Attempt 4: Mishna says woman who says 'I intended to be betrothed' is still not betrothed.
Rejection
No — her thoughts can't uproot his explicit condition. Different principle.
Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin
Attempt 5: Agent brings consecrated item from the specified place — owner liable for me'ilah despite claiming different intent.
Gemara
Perhaps he's lying to avoid an offering? But he could say it was intentional...
KEY PRINCIPLE דברים שבלב אינם דברים — Unspoken matters in the heart are not significant matters. The Gemara struggles to find a clean, unrejectable proof.

Why All Three Mishna Cases Are Needed (Amud B)

Two Women, One Peruta

  • He thinks one peruta can betroth two women
  • Since money 'goes out' — he genuinely erred
  • Gifts sent later are NOT for betrothal

Less Than One Peruta

  • Maybe he KNOWS less than a peruta doesn't work
  • You'd think later gifts ARE for betrothal
  • Mishna teaches: still NOT betrothed

Minor Who Betroths

  • EVERYONE knows a minor's betrothal is void
  • Strongest case that later gifts = new betrothal
  • Mishna teaches: STILL not betrothed
LEGAL LOGIC Each case is progressively stronger — if the Gemara only taught the weaker cases, we might have thought the stronger ones come out differently.

Must We Be Concerned About Gifts (שושבינות)?

Rav Huna
If she agreed to betrothal and he sent gifts with witnesses — we must be concerned that the gifts constitute betrothal.
Rabba
I agree. But we raised an objection from the mishna: 'Even if he sent gifts, she is not betrothed.'
Abaye
The mishna is different — there, he sent gifts on account of the first betrothal. Where there's no prior betrothal, gifts may indeed effect kiddushin.
Rabba (v.2)
Alternative version: The mishna itself proves my point — it's only because he thought the first betrothal worked that gifts don't count.
Abaye (v.2)
Or maybe the mishna uses the style of 'לא מיבעיא' (it is not necessary) — you can't derive the general rule from it.
TWO VERSIONS The Gemara presents two versions (lishna'ot) of the Rabba-Abaye exchange, each deriving opposite conclusions from the same mishna.
