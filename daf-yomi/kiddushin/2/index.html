Why Does the Mishna Say 'Acquired' (Nikneit)?

Gemara
Why does our Mishna use the language of acquisition (nikneit), while the next chapter (42a) uses betrothal (mekadesh)?
Gemara Answers
Because the tanna wanted to teach about betrothal through money — the standard means of acquisition.
Gemara
How do we derive that money effects betrothal?
Gemara Answers
A gezera shava (verbal analogy): 'When a man takes a woman' (Deut. 24:1) ↔ 'I will give money for the field; take it from me' (Gen. 23:13)
Gemara Answers
Just as Ephron's field was acquired with money, so too a woman can be acquired (betrothed) with money.
Gezera Shava The shared word 'kicha' (taking) links the marriage verse to Abraham's purchase of the Cave of Machpelah from Ephron the Hittite.

Why Is Betrothal Called 'Kiddushin' (Consecration)?

קידושין — שהוא אוסרה על כל העולם כהקדש
Key Concept The Sages call betrothal kiddushin (קידושין) — literally consecration — because through betrothal, the husband renders her forbidden to all other men, like consecrated property (hekdesh) that is set apart from ordinary use.

The Torah's language is kicha (taking); the Sages' language is kiddushin (sanctification).

Why 'A Woman Is Acquired' — Not 'A Man Acquires'?

Answer 1: Structural

  • The latter clause teaches: 'And she acquires herself' (release from marriage)
  • Since the end of the Mishna focuses on her — the beginning does too
  • Keeps the woman as subject throughout for consistency

Answer 2: Consent

  • 'A man acquires' would imply he can betroth her against her will
  • 'A woman IS acquired' implies: with her consent — yes; without — no
  • The passive voice signals that her agreement is required

Why Not 'He Transfers'?

  • Because one method of release is the husband's death
  • In that case, it is Heaven — not the husband — that 'transfers' her freedom
  • So the Mishna cannot generalize with active male language
Practical Halakha A woman's consent is required for betrothal to be valid. This is derived from the Mishna's passive phrasing.
