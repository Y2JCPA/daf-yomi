Kiddushin 73: Congregation, Converts & Foundlings

KEY THEME The Gemara examines who may enter the congregation of God (kahal Hashem) — resolving the status of mamzerim, converts, shetukei, and foundlings (asufim). It establishes practical rules for determining lineage based on majority presumptions, location, and timely testimony.

Rabbi Yehuda vs. Rabbi Yosei: Can a Convert Marry a Mamzeret?

Rabbi Yehuda

  • Priests & Levites derived from ONE 'kahal'
  • Extra 'kahal' refers to congregation of converts
  • Mamzer PROHIBITED from marrying a convert
  • Derives from: 'One statute for you and the stranger' (Num. 15:15)

Rabbi Yosei

  • Priests & Levites each have their own 'kahal'
  • Congregation of converts is NOT called 'kahal'
  • Mamzer PERMITTED to marry a convert
  • 'One statute' interrupts the matter — converts excluded from kahal
HALAKHA The law follows Rabbi Yosei: a convert may marry a mamzeret, and the congregation of converts is not called a 'kahal.'

The Etrogim Incident in Meḥoza

R' Zeira
Taught publicly in Meḥoza: A convert may marry a mamzeret.
Crowd
The many converts of Meḥoza were insulted — they stoned him with their etrogim!
Rava
Is there anyone who teaches such a matter in a place full of converts? He should have been more careful!
Rava
Taught diplomatically: A convert may marry a kohen's daughter. They carried him on silk!
Rava
Then added: A convert may also marry a mamzeret. They said: You ruined the first teaching!
Rava
I did you a favor — a convert may marry from here (pure lineage) or from here (mamzeret). Maximum choice!
HALAKHA A convert may marry both a bat kohen (women of unflawed lineage were not prohibited) and a mamzeret (per R' Yosei).

Shetuki: Is He Fit to Enter the Congregation?

Shetuki — knows his mother, not his father
↓
Rava: By Torah law, a shetuki is fit — most men are of valid lineage
↓
Father came to her (parish)
Kol d'parish — mei-rubba parish
Anything that separates from a group is presumed from the majority → FIT
She went to him (kavua)
Kol kavua — k'meḥetza al meḥetza
Anything in its fixed place is treated as 50/50 → UNCERTAIN
KEY PRINCIPLE The Torah-law fitness of a shetuki depends on the rov (majority) principle: most potential fathers would produce legitimate offspring.

SituationSubject to Foundling Laws?
Baby found circumcised❌ No — parents cared for him
Limbs adjusted / anointed / adorned❌ No — evidence of parental care
Note or amulet hanging on baby❌ No — parents intended retrieval
On palm tree — animal can reach✅ Yes — abandoned
On palm tree — animal cannot reach❌ No — placed safely
In thicket near town (not frequented)✅ Yes — abandoned
In synagogue near town (frequented)❌ No — given to community
In public domain (could be trampled)✅ Yes — abandoned
At sides of public domain❌ No — placed safely
Middle of river with boats passing❌ No — will be found
Side of river✅ Yes — abandoned
RAVA'S ADDITION In famine years, even a child collected from the marketplace — parents are still credible to claim him, since even fit families abandon children in desperation.

Credibility of Parents Claiming a Foundling

👶 Baby is found abandoned in the marketplace
↓
⏰ While child is STILL in marketplace — parents are credible to claim him (Rav)
↓
📢 Once child is COLLECTED — he is publicized as having flawed lineage
↓
🚫 After collection — parents are NO LONGER credible (Rava: status is fixed)
↓
🌾 EXCEPTION: In famine years — parents remain credible even after collection
REASONING In famine years, even parents of unflawed lineage commonly abandon children — so their later claim is believable.
