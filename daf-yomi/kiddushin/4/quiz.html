QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Ravina, what does the extra yod in 'ein kasef' (Exodus 21:11) teach?
A. No money at all is involved when a maidservant goes free
B. No money for this master, but there IS money for a different master — the father
C. The maidservant must repay her purchase price before going free
D. Money can never be used for betrothal
Correct: B — Ravina argues that 'ein' could have been written 'en' without the yod. The full spelling teaches that while there is no money for the current master upon her release, there IS money for a different master — her father — when she is betrothed.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why can't the halakha that a woman is betrothed with money be derived solely through a kal va'chomer from a Hebrew maidservant?
A. A Hebrew maidservant cannot be acquired with money
B. A yevama disproves it, and Rav Ashi adds that a maidservant can be released with money but a wife cannot be divorced with money
C. The Torah never uses kal va'chomer for marriage laws
D. A maidservant's acquisition is more stringent than betrothal
Correct: B — Rav Ashi teaches that the a fortiori has a fundamental flaw: a Hebrew maidservant can be RELEASED with money (paying her remaining value), but a wife cannot be divorced with money. This structural dissimilarity breaks the inference from the outset.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does the Torah need BOTH the verse about betrothal money and the verse about a daughter's earnings belonging to her father?
A. They refer to different age groups — one for a minor, one for a na'arah
B. One applies to a firstborn daughter and the other to subsequent daughters
C. Each has a unique rationale the other lacks: betrothal money requires no toil, while earnings are offset by the father's support
D. The repetition is for emphasis only, not to teach separate halakhot
Correct: C — If only betrothal money were mentioned, one might say earnings belong to her since she toiled for them. If only earnings were mentioned, one might say betrothal money is hers since it comes from an external source. Neither can be derived from the other.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What does the yod in 'ein lah' (Leviticus 22:13, regarding a priest's daughter) ultimately teach?
A. Only biological children prevent her from eating teruma
B. Even children of flawed lineage (mamzerim) count as her children and prevent her from eating teruma
C. She must have at least two children before the rule applies
D. Adopted children are considered like biological children
Correct: B — The Gemara concludes that since grandchildren being like children is an established principle (requiring no verse), the yod in 'ein lah' — read as 'ayyein lah' (examine her) — teaches that even children of flawed lineage prevent her return to teruma.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What do the two verses 'ein kasef' and 'when a man takes' teach when combined about the mechanics of betrothal?
A. Betrothal requires both money AND a document simultaneously
B. The father gives money to the groom as a dowry
C. The man must give the money (not the woman), and while she is a na'arah, the money goes to her father
D. Either the man or the woman may initiate betrothal with money
Correct: C — 'Ein kasef' teaches that betrothal money belongs to the father. 'When HE takes' (not 'when she takes') teaches that the man must be the one giving the money. Together they establish: the man gives betrothal money, and it goes to her father during her youth.
