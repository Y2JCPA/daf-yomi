From 'She Shall Go Out' to the Three Modes of Kiddushin

Exodus 21:11
"She shall go out for nothing, ein kasef"
↓
Why is ein spelled with a yod?
Could have written en
↓
Ravina's Reading
Ein kasef = No money for this master,
but money for a different master — her father
Baraita's Reading
"For nothing" = days of adulthood
"Without money" = days of youth
↓
Separate verse needed: Deut 24:1
"When a man takes a woman" = taking is with money
↓
Both verses together establish:
Father receives betrothal money & man gives it
Key Theme This daf traces how multiple verses work together to establish the father's right to betrothal money and the three modes of kiddushin — money, document, and intercourse.

Does 'Ein Kasef' Teach About the Father's Right?

Gemara
The verse says "she shall go out for nothing, without money" — this teaches a Hebrew maidservant leaves upon adulthood and youth. The verse is used up!
Ravina
If that were the only teaching, the verse should say en kasef without the yod. The full spelling ein kasef teaches: no money for this master, but money for a different master — her father.
Gemara
How do we know the yod in ein is expounded this way?
Ravina
We see that me'en (refuses) in Numbers 22:14 and Deuteronomy 25:7 is written without a yod. Here the yod is extra — it must be for exposition.
Principle A superfluous letter in the Torah is never accidental — it generates new halakhic derivations.

The Yod in 'Ein Lah' — Priest's Daughter Returns to Teruma

Leviticus 22:13
"She has no child (ein lah)…
she may eat of her father's bread"
↓
Why is ein spelled with a yod?
Read as: ayyein lah — examine her!
↓
First Derivation
Grandchildren count as children — if she has descendants from a non-priest, she cannot eat teruma
Second Derivation
Even children of flawed lineage (mamzerim) count as her children for this halakha
↓
Gemara resolves: Grandchildren = children is an established principle,
so the verse actually teaches about flawed lineage
Parallel The same technique of expounding the yod in 'ein' applies both to the Hebrew maidservant and the priest's daughter — showing a consistent hermeneutical method.

Why Two Verses? Betrothal Money vs. Earnings

Betrothal Money → Father

  • Derived from 'ein kasef' (Exodus 21:11)
  • She did NOT toil for it
  • Comes from an external source (the husband)
  • Without this verse: maybe earnings alone go to father

Earnings → Father

  • Derived from a separate verse
  • She DID toil for these wages
  • Father sustains her from his property
  • Without this verse: maybe only betrothal money goes to father
Necessity Neither halakha can be derived from the other — each has a unique rationale that the other lacks. Both verses are indispensable.

Deriving Betrothal by Money from 'When a Man Takes'

Baraita
"When a man takes a woman" (Deut 24:1) — taking means with money, as we find: "I will give money for the field; take it from me" (Gen 23:13).
Gemara
But can't we derive this by kal va'chomer from a Hebrew maidservant? She can't be acquired through intercourse, yet she can be acquired through money!
Objector
A yevama disproves this! She IS acquired through intercourse but NOT through money.
Gemara
A yevama is different — she can't be acquired through a document either. A wife CAN be acquired by document, so the refutation fails.
Rav Ashi
The verse is still needed! The kal va'chomer has an initial flaw: a maidservant can be released with money, but a wife cannot be divorced with money. So the a fortiori fails from the start.
Rav Ashi's Method When an a fortiori inference has a fundamental structural weakness from the outset, the verse is not redundant — it is the actual source of the halakha.
