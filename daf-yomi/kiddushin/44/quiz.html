QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to R' Yochanan (following the Rabbis), why can only the father accept kiddushin for a na'arah, but either she or her father can receive a get?
A. Kiddushin requires consent (father's domain); a get does not require her consent
B. The Torah explicitly grants the father exclusive betrothal rights
C. A na'arah lacks legal competence for any transaction
D. The father owns the kiddushin money but not the get document
Correct: A — R' Yochanan's reasoning: Since betrothal requires the consent of the accepting party, only the father — who holds authority — can accept it. A get, which takes effect without her consent, can be received by either her or her father.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is R' Yehuda HaNasi's rationale for ruling that levirate betrothal can be performed against the yevama's will?
A. He derives levirate betrothal from standard kiddushin
B. He derives levirate betrothal from yibum intercourse — which acquires even against her will
C. He holds the zikah bond automatically creates marriage
D. He rules that a yavam has the same rights as the original husband
Correct: B — R' Yehuda HaNasi derives yevama-related law from other yevama-related law: just as yibum intercourse acquires her even against her will, so too levirate betrothal (ma'amar) can be performed without her consent.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does the Gemara conclude that levirate betrothal does NOT disprove R' Yochanan's position about standard kiddushin?
A. Levirate betrothal is only rabbinic in origin
B. The yevama's pre-existing zikah bond to the yavam makes levirate betrothal fundamentally different
C. The baraita is rejected as unreliable
D. R' Yochanan conceded his position after the challenge
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that levirate betrothal is different because the yevama is already 'bonded and standing in wait' (zekukah v'omdah) for the yavam, so her accepting betrothal money suffices — this doesn't apply to standard kiddushin.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In Rava's dilemma about a na'arah, what is the practical difference between being considered her father's 'hand' versus his 'courtyard'?
A. A hand can receive a get but a courtyard cannot
B. A hand can appoint an agent; a courtyard can only receive directly
C. A courtyard requires witnesses but a hand does not
D. A hand creates immediate acquisition but a courtyard delays it
Correct: B — If the na'arah is like her father's hand, she has active legal power and can potentially delegate (appoint an agent). If she is like his courtyard, she can only passively receive items placed into her possession — she cannot delegate to an agent.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does Shmuel (confirmed by Rav) require BOTH a get and mi'un for a minor betrothed without her father's consent?
A. The get covers Torah-level doubt; mi'un covers rabbinic-level doubt
B. Perhaps the father desired the betrothal (needing a get) or perhaps he didn't (needing mi'un) — we don't know
C. One dissolves the betrothal and the other dissolves the marriage
D. Mi'un is required by Torah law and get is required by rabbinic law
Correct: B — The uncertainty is whether the father desired the betrothal. If he did, it was a valid kiddushin requiring a get. If he didn't, it was only a rabbinic-level betrothal requiring mi'un. Since we don't know, both are required to cover both possibilities.
