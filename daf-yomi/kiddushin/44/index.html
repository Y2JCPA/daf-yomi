Who Can Accept Betrothal for a Young Woman?

R' Yochanan
According to the Rabbis, only the father can accept betrothal (kiddushin) for a na'arah. A bill of divorce, however, can be received by either her or her father.
Reish Lakish
According to the Rabbis, a na'arah can also accept her own betrothal — not just her father.
Gemara
A baraita on levirate betrothal distinguishes it from standard betrothal — implying only the father can accept standard kiddushin. Is this a conclusive refutation of Reish Lakish?
Reish Lakish
That baraita follows R' Yehuda, who says two hands cannot acquire as one — so it doesn't refute my reading of the Rabbis.
Core Dispute Does a na'arah have independent legal capacity to accept kiddushin, or is that power exclusively her father's?

R' Yochanan vs. Reish Lakish on a Na'arah's Betrothal

R' Yochanan

  • Only the father can accept kiddushin for a na'arah
  • A get can be received by either her or her father
  • Betrothal requires consent → father's exclusive right
  • Supported by baraita distinguishing levirate from standard betrothal

Reish Lakish

  • A na'arah can also accept her own kiddushin
  • The Rabbis grant her independent capacity
  • Problematic baraita is attributed to R' Yehuda
  • The mishna listing only father/agent also follows R' Yehuda
Key Logic R' Yochanan: Betrothal needs consent → only father controls it. Get doesn't need consent → either can receive it.

Levirate Betrothal Against Her Will

R' Yehuda HaNasi
If a yavam performs levirate betrothal (ma'amar) against her will, he acquires her. We derive ma'amar from bi'ah (intercourse): just as intercourse acquires even against her will, so too ma'amar.
Rabbis
He does not acquire her. We derive ma'amar from standard kiddushin: just as kiddushin requires her consent, so too ma'amar requires consent.
Gemara
Rabbi derives yevama-law from yevama-law. The Rabbis derive betrothal-law from betrothal-law.
Principle The dispute turns on which analogy controls: Is levirate betrothal more like yibum intercourse, or more like regular kiddushin?

The Zikah Defense — Why Levirate Betrothal Is Unique

A yevama is bonded (zekukah) to the yavam
↓
She is already "standing and waiting" for him
↓
Her acceptance of betrothal money suffices for levirate betrothal
↓
This is why even R' Yochanan's position is not contradicted by the na'arah accepting levirate betrothal herself
Resolution Levirate betrothal is fundamentally different from standard betrothal because the zikah bond already partially connects the yevama to the yavam.

Rava's Dilemma: Hand or Courtyard?

Rava
Can a na'arah appoint an agent to receive her get? Is she like her father's hand (and can appoint an agent), or like his courtyard (and can only receive directly)?
Rava (resolving)
A sleeping slave in her possession can receive a get for her, but an awake slave cannot — because he's an unsecured courtyard. If she were merely a courtyard, she too would be unsecured!
Rava
She must be like her father's hand. The remaining question: is she strong enough as a hand to appoint an agent?
Rav Nachman
No — she cannot appoint an agent to receive her get.
Analogy Hand = active legal power (can delegate). Courtyard = passive receptacle (can only receive directly). Rava proves she's a hand, but Rav Nachman limits her power.
