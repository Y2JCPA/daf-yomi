Kiddushin 47: Betrothal Language, Loans & Deposits

Core Question: How does the language of betrothal affect validity when items are consumed?
↓
"With these"
Items treated as one unit — combined value counts, even if eaten
"With this & this"
Each item is separate — one alone must be worth a peruta
↓
Second Question: Can a loan (מִלְוֶה) serve as betrothal money?
↓
Rav
One cannot betroth with a loan — the money already belongs to the borrower
R' Meir
One can betroth with a loan — forgiving debt has monetary value
Key Principle The precise wording a man uses when offering betrothal items determines whether they combine or stand alone — and whether a loan can serve as betrothal money is a fundamental dispute.

"With These" vs. "With This and This"

בְּאֵלּוּ — "With These"

  • All items treated as a single unit
  • Combined value must equal one peruta
  • If she eats them one by one — still betrothed
  • She is eating her own food (Rava)

בָּזוֹ וּבָזוֹ — "With This & This"

  • Each item is a separate act of betrothal
  • One single item must be worth a peruta
  • If eaten one by one — not betrothed unless last one = peruta
  • Earlier items become a loan, not betrothal money
Rava's Principle When he says "with these," once she accepts them they become her property — so eating them is eating her own food and the betrothal stands.

Which Clause Does the Eating Case Refer To?

Rabbi Ami
The eating case refers to the latter clause — where he said "with this and with that." "One of them worth a peruta" means the last one must be worth a peruta.
Rav & Shmuel
The eating case refers to the first clause — where he made separate betrothal statements. Despite her immediate benefit from eating, one date must independently be worth a peruta.
Gemara
The baraita works well for Rabbi Ami. But for Rav and Shmuel — the baraita lists only groupings, not individual betrothal statements. How do they explain it?
Gemara
The baraita follows Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, who holds that even without the word "and," each statement is treated separately — as with the laws of piggul.
Resolution Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's principle from piggul law: listing items without "and" still treats each as a separate statement.

Can One Betroth a Woman with a Loan?

A man has an outstanding loan (מִלְוֶה) with a woman
↓
He says: "Be betrothed to me with this loan"
↓
First Tanna
A loan is in the debtor's possession — the lender cannot retract it, so it cannot serve as betrothal
R' Shimon b. Elazar / R' Meir
An unspent loan is like a deposit — still in lender's domain, so it can serve as betrothal
Rav's Position Rav holds one cannot betroth with a loan at all. Rava defends Rav by arguing the baraita is corrupted and cannot serve as proof against him.

Loan vs. Deposit: Rabba's Analysis

Scholars of Rav
The tanna'im disagree about both retraction rights and accident liability — who bears the loss if the loan money is destroyed?
Rabba
No! Regarding accidents, everyone agrees the debtor is liable. A loan is no worse than a borrowed item — and a borrower is always liable for accidents.
Rabba
The only dispute is about retraction (חֲזָרָה): Can the lender demand the money back before the borrower spends it?
First Tanna
He cannot retract — the loan passed to the debtor's domain.
R' Shimon b. Elazar
He can retract an unspent loan — it still stands in the lender's possession.
Key Distinction A loan (spent and repaid with other money) differs from borrowing an item (returned intact) — this distinction limits the scope of the dispute.
