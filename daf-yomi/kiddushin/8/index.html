Must Betrothal Use an Item of Set Value?

Rav Yosef
An item used for betrothal must have a clearly defined value (shaveh kesef katzuv), just like money itself.
Rabba
No — any item worth a peruta suffices. The baraita about grain/vessels refers to symbolic exchange (chalipin), not set value.
Rav Yosef
Proof from Hebrew slave: grain and vessels worth a peruta still can't redeem because their value isn't set.
Gemara
Rejected — the baraita may teach that items under a peruta can't acquire even though their benefit is immediately available.
Core Issue Does betrothal require an item with a fixed, appraised value — or is any item worth at least a peruta sufficient?

Set Value Requirement: Rav Yosef vs. Rabba

Rav Yosef
From Leviticus 25:51 — a Hebrew slave is redeemed with money, not grain or vessels. This proves an item must have a set, defined value.
Rabba
The verse 'the price of his redemption' actually includes all equivalents of money. The exclusion of grain/vessels means only that chalipin (symbolic exchange) doesn't work for slaves.
Gemara
But according to Rav Naḥman, produce can't effect chalipin at all — so why does the baraita mention grain?
Gemara
Revised answer: The grain and vessels are worth less than a peruta. The baraita uses the style of 'lo tzrikha' — it's not necessary to teach about money, but grain's immediate benefit might mislead us.
Key Term Shaveh Kesef (שווה כסף) — 'worth money.' The debate is whether this must be a known, set amount or merely any positive value.

↓
Gemara asks: Is this proof that set value is required?
↓
Rejected — the first case is where the item is not actually worth 5 sela. The second case is where the Kohen accepts it at that value.
Practical Case Rav Kahana (a Kohen) accepted a cloth for pidyon haben, saying: 'For me, this is worth 5 sela.' Rav Ashi limits this to distinguished individuals who actually need such a cloth.

Betrothal with Collateral (Mashkon)

Rav Naḥman's Ruling

  • Man says: 'Be betrothed with 100 dinars' and gives collateral
  • There is no 100 dinars here — money not yet given
  • There is no collateral here — it's security, not a gift
  • Result: She is NOT betrothed

Baraita's Exception

  • Collateral belonging to OTHER people in his possession
  • Based on R' Yitzḥak: a creditor acquires collateral
  • Proof: Returning a pledge is called 'righteousness' (Deut. 24:13)
  • He has rights to it — so he CAN betroth with it
Distinction His OWN collateral = merely a security, not a transfer of value. Someone ELSE's collateral in his hands = he has acquired rights to it and can use it for betrothal.

Woman's Response to Betrothal Money

Her ActionBetrothed?Reasoning
Throws money into the sea or fire❌ NoMoney is destroyed — no acquisition took place
Throws money before him (not destroyed)❌ NoShe is saying: 'I don't want your betrothal'
Says: 'Give to my/your father'❌ NoDirecting money elsewhere = not accepting it for herself
Says: 'Give to my/your father on condition he accepts for me'✅ YesFather acts as her agent — she is accepting through him
Says: 'Give to so-and-so'❌ NoUnrelated party — clearly not accepting for herself
Says: 'Give to so-and-so on condition he accepts for me'✅ YesEven a stranger can serve as her agent if stipulated
Why Both Cases? The Gemara needs BOTH rulings: we might think a father would hold money on her behalf (so no betrothal without stipulation is novel), and we might think giving to a stranger = a gift from her (so betrothal with stipulation is novel).

The Rock Dilemma

הניחם על הסלע — אם הסלע שלה, מקודשת
Halakhic Puzzle If she says 'Place them on a rock' — she is not betrothed. If the rock is hers — she is betrothed, because placing money in her domain is like giving it to her hand.

Rav Beivai's unresolved dilemma: What if the rock belongs to both of them? Is placing money on shared property sufficient for acquisition? Teiku — the question stands.
