Ein Shaliach L'Dvar Aveira — No Agency for Sin

Question: Can we derive from me'ilah (misuse of hekdesh) that agency applies to transgressions?
↓
Me'ilah + Slaughter/Sale = two verses that come as one → don't teach other cases
↓
"Hahu ha-ish" (Lev. 17:4) — that man is liable, not his agent
↓
Principle: Ein shaliach l'dvar aveira — the agent, not the sender, bears liability
Core Principle When someone sends an agent to sin, the agent is liable — because the agent should have listened to God rather than the sender (divrei ha-rav v'divrei ha-talmid, divrei mi shom'in?).

Can Agency Apply to Transgressions?

Gemara
Let's derive from me'ilah (misuse of consecrated property) that an agent can be appointed for a transgression!
Gemara (Answer)
Me'ilah and slaughter/sale of a stolen animal are shnei ketuvim ha-ba'im k'echad — two verses that come as one — and they do not teach other cases.
Gemara
What is the verse for slaughter/sale?
Gemara (Answer)
"And he slaughters it or sells it" (Ex. 21:37) — selling requires another party, so slaughter can also be done via an agent, and the thief is liable.
Key Concept Shnei ketuvim ha-ba'im k'echad — When two separate verses teach the same principle, we do not generalize from them to other cases (according to one opinion).

Three Derivations: Agent Liable for Slaughter/Sale

Beit R' Yishmael

  • The word "or" in "he slaughters it or sells it"
  • Serves to include the agent — the thief is liable even through an agent

Beit Chizkiyah

  • Repetition of "for" in "five oxen for an ox, four sheep for a sheep"
  • The extra word includes the agent — sender is liable

Simple Reading

  • Selling inherently requires another party
  • Juxtaposition with slaughter teaches slaughter can also be via agent
Note All three approaches reach the same conclusion: for slaughter/sale of a stolen animal, the thief who sent the agent pays the penalty.

Deriving Ein Shaliach L'Dvar Aveira

According to the view that two verses do teach other cases — why not derive agency does apply to sin?
↓
Torah writes "hahu ha-ish" (Lev. 17:4) re: slaughtering offerings outside the Temple
↓
First "hahu"
That man is liable — not his agent. Excludes agency for this sin.
Second "hahu"
If not needed here, apply to entire Torah: no agency for transgression anywhere.
Hermeneutic "Im eino inyan" — if a verse is not needed for its own context, apply it to another context. The double hahu teaches the universal rule.

Shammai vs. the Sages: Sending an Agent to Kill

Baraita
One who tells his agent: "Go kill a person" — the killer (agent) is liable, and the sender is exempt.
Shammai HaZaken
In the name of Haggai the Prophet: The sender is liable! As it says regarding David and Uriah: "Him you have slain with the sword of the children of Ammon" (II Sam. 12:9).
Sages
Ein shaliach l'dvar aveira — the agent should have listened to God, not to the sender. The agent bears the liability.
Shammai's Source Shammai invokes a tradition from Haggai the Prophet — David was held responsible for Uriah's death even though Joab carried out the act.
