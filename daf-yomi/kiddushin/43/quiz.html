QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why can't we derive from me'ilah (misuse of consecrated property) that agency applies to transgressions generally?
A. Me'ilah and slaughter/sale are two verses that come as one and don't teach other cases
B. Me'ilah applies only to kohanim, not to ordinary people
C. Me'ilah is only a rabbinic prohibition, not a Torah one
D. The agent in me'ilah cases is always exempt
Correct: A — The Gemara explains that me'ilah and the slaughter/sale of a stolen animal are shnei ketuvim ha-ba'im k'echad — two verses teaching the same principle — and according to the majority view, such paired verses do not establish a precedent for other cases.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does Shammai the Elder derive from the verse about David and Uriah?
A. The sender of an agent to kill is liable, not just the agent
B. David was exempt because Joab acted independently
C. Murder cannot be performed through an agent at all
D. A king has special liability that ordinary people do not
Correct: A — Shammai, citing a tradition from Haggai the Prophet, holds that the sender IS liable when he sends an agent to kill, as shown by the verse blaming David for Uriah's death even though Joab carried out the killing.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does Rav Nachman need to teach that agents can serve as witnesses in all three domains (betrothal, divorce, and money)?
A. Each domain has a unique reason to suspect or trust the agents, so none can be derived from the others
B. Each domain has different numbers of required witnesses
C. The Torah explicitly requires witnesses in each domain separately
D. Rav Nachman was simply being thorough for pedagogical reasons
Correct: A — In betrothal, we might think agents are trusted only because she becomes forbidden to them too. In divorce, a woman can't marry both agents. In money, they might split it. Each case has a unique concern, so all three rulings are necessary.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to R' Yochanan, why can a na'arah receive her own get but NOT accept her own betrothal?
A. Divorce returns her to her father's authority (as if he received it through her), but betrothal removes her from it
B. Betrothal requires a higher level of mental competence than divorce
C. The Torah explicitly grants only the father the right to accept betrothal
D. A na'arah lacks the legal capacity to form a new relationship
Correct: A — R' Yosi b. R' Chanina explains R' Yochanan's reasoning: divorce restores the father's authority, so it's as if the father received the get via his daughter. But betrothal removes her from his authority — she cannot effect that removal herself.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does the opinion that splits 'hahu' (הַהוּא) into 'ha' + 'hu' use this to derive multiple halakhot from a single word?
A. From 'hu' he derives ein shaliach l'dvar aveira; from the extra 'ha' he derives exclusions for compelled/unwitting acts
B. From 'ha' he derives agency for sin; from 'hu' he derives liability for the sender
C. He uses 'ha' for Amud A topics and 'hu' for Amud B topics
D. From 'hu' he excludes women; from 'ha' he excludes minors
Correct: A — By treating 'hahu' as composed of two parts, this opinion derives both the principle of ein shaliach l'dvar aveira AND the exclusions (two who slaughter together, one who acts under compulsion, unwittingly, or by mistake) — all from the same word.
