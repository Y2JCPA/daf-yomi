Father's Power: Firstborn vs. Disqualification

R' Yehuda
Just as a father is believed to say "This is my firstborn," a Kohen father is also believed to say "This son is from a divorced woman or ḥalutza" — disqualifying him from kehuna.
Rabbanan
He is not believed to disqualify his son. His credibility extends only to identifying the firstborn — a matter of giving rights, not removing them.
Key Principle The dispute hinges on whether the father's Torah-granted credibility for firstborn extends to disqualifying his children from priestly status.

Abba Shaul's Beduki: Examining the Mother's Claim

A shetuki (one who doesn't know his father) — Abba Shaul calls him beduki
↓
We examine (bodekin) the mother's claim about the father
↓
Does she say: "I had relations with a man of unflawed lineage"?
↓
Yes
She is believed — child has valid lineage
(Extends even to her daughter's fitness to marry a Kohen)
No / Silent
Child retains shetuki status — uncertain lineage
Rava's Ruling The halakha follows Abba Shaul — even where most potential fathers would disqualify the child, the mother is still believed.

Rabban Gamliel vs. Abba Shaul on Mother's Credibility

Rabban Gamliel

  • Pregnant single woman is believed about the father's identity
  • She herself is fit to marry a Kohen
  • Unclear if her daughter is also fit

Abba Shaul (Beduki)

  • Adds: daughter is also deemed fit to marry a Kohen
  • Applies even when most potential fathers would disqualify
  • Broader scope: covers betrothed women too
  • Rava rules halakha follows Abba Shaul
Why Both Needed? One ruling establishes the mother's fitness; the other extends credibility to her daughter's fitness to marry into the priesthood.
