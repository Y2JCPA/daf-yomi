Eglah Arufah: Source of Prohibition

כַּפֵּר לְעַמְּךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל
SCHOOL OF R' YANNAI The verse states "Atone for Your people Israel" (Deut. 21:8) regarding the heifer whose neck is broken.

Since an expression of atonement (kaparah) is written — the same language used for sacrificial animals — benefit from the eglah arufah is prohibited, just as one may not benefit from an offering.

Leper's Birds: The Derivation Chain

Inside the Temple:
Enabling rite = Leper's asham (guilt-offering)
Atoning rite = Other offerings
↓
Enabling rite is forbidden like atoning rite ✓
↓
Outside the Temple:
Enabling rite = Leper's birds
Atoning rite = Eglah arufah
↓
So too: leper's birds are forbidden like the eglah arufah
SCHOOL OF R' YISHMAEL The parallel structure of enabling/atoning rites inside and outside the Temple creates an analogy that extends the prohibition of benefit to the leper's birds.

When Are Leper's Birds Forbidden?

R' Yochanan
From the moment of slaughter — that is what prohibits them, since they are not consecrated beforehand.
Reish Lakish
From the moment they are taken — derived from eglah arufah, which is forbidden during its lifetime.
R' Yochanan
Objection: The verse says 'of all clean birds you may eat' — this includes the freed bird. 'These you shall not eat' — this excludes the slaughtered bird. If forbidden while alive, why would we need a verse for after slaughter?
Reish Lakish
The verse is needed lest you say: like sacrificial animals, slaughter permits — here too slaughter might permit the bird.
KEY DISPUTE The core question: Does the prohibition attach at designation (taking) or at the ritual act (slaughter)?

The Freed Bird: Why Is It Permitted?

School of R' Yishmael

  • Verse: 'Let go the living bird into the open field'
  • Just as the field is permitted, so the bird is permitted
  • 'The field' (with article) teaches TWO things:
  • 1) Must release to an actual field
  • 2) The bird itself is permitted for benefit

Rava's Approach

  • Logical argument, not textual
  • The Torah would not command sending the bird free if it were forbidden
  • That would create a 'stumbling block' — people might unknowingly eat it
  • Torah does not set traps for Israel
RESULT The slaughtered bird is forbidden; the freed bird is permitted for benefit — but they disagree on the source.
