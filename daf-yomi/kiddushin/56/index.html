Kiddushin 56: Forbidden Items & Betrothal

Can second tithe money or forbidden items be used for kiddushin?
↓
Second Tithe
Depends on intent and whether the woman is a ḥavera who knows the law
Forbidden Items (Orla, Kilayim, etc.)
Betrothal is invalid — no benefit may be derived
↓
If forbidden items are sold first, betrothal with the proceeds is valid
Key Theme This daf explores which items carry benefit prohibitions and how those prohibitions affect the validity of betrothal.

Second Tithe: Unwitting vs. Intentional Purchase

Tanna Kamma
If one unwittingly purchased an animal with ma'aser sheni money, the money returns to its place — the transaction is void.
Tanna Kamma
If purchased intentionally, the animal is brought up and eaten in Jerusalem.
R' Yehuda
That applies only when the buyer intended it as a peace-offering. But if the intent was to desacralize the money — whether unwitting or intentional — the money returns to its place.
Gemara
But R' Yehuda said in the mishna that intentional betrothal with ma'aser sheni is valid! How does this fit?
Tension R' Yehuda seems to contradict himself — penalizing intentional desacralization in one context but validating kiddushin in another.

R' Elazar's Resolution: The Ḥavera

R' Elazar: The woman in the mishna knows ma'aser sheni is not desacralized by her acceptance
↓
She effectively agrees to ascend and eat it in Jerusalem
↓
R' Yirmeya objects: Non-kosher animals, slaves, and land — the seller also knows the money is still sacred, yet we don't apply the same rule!
↓
Resolution: The mishna deals with a ḥavera — a woman devoted to mitzvot who knows tithe law. An ordinary person assumes the money was desacralized.
Ḥavera A ḥavera is someone meticulous in observing halakhot of ritual purity, teruma, and tithes — her informed consent makes the kiddushin valid.

The Mouse & The Hole: Who Gets Penalized?

The Mouse (Buyer)

  • Initiated the improper purchase
  • Used sacred money for forbidden items
  • Should we penalize him to take equivalent money to Jerusalem?

The Hole (Seller)

  • Enabled the violation by accepting the money
  • "It is not the mouse that steals, but the hole"
  • Wherever the forbidden item (money) rests — there we penalize
Principle When the seller fled with the money, we penalize the buyer to eat equivalent food in Jerusalem. But normally, the seller who holds the sacred money bears the penalty.

Rule All these items carry a prohibition against deriving benefit (issur hana'ah). Kiddushin with them is invalid, but kiddushin with proceeds from their sale IS valid.

Deriving the Kilayim Prohibition: Ḥizkiyya vs. Rav Ashi

Ḥizkiyya
"Pen tikdash" (פן תקדש) — read as pen tukad esh (פן תוקד אש) — 'lest it be burned by fire.' Diverse kinds must be destroyed.
Rav Ashi
"Pen tikdash" means 'lest it become kodesh' — consecrated. It is forbidden like hekdesh (Temple property).
Gemara
If it's like hekdesh, then it should be redeemable — transferring sanctity to money. But the mishna says sale proceeds are permitted!
Gemara
Therefore Ḥizkiyya's derivation — 'lest it be burned' — must be correct.
Conclusion Rav Ashi's comparison to hekdesh fails because diverse kinds don't transfer their prohibition to sale proceeds, unlike true consecrated property.
