Gilgul Shevua: Extending Oaths

Sota Oath (internal, in Temple)
Requires 2 witnesses for seclusion
Yet oath can be extended
↓
Kal va-chomer: If sota oath (needing 2 witnesses) can be extended...
monetary oath (needing only 1 witness) certainly can be extended!
↓
But this only proves extension for definite claims.
What about uncertain claims?
↓
R' Shimon ben Yoḥai: Internal oath (sota) treats uncertainty like certainty → External oath does too
Key Principle Just as the Torah rendered uncertainty like certainty for the sota oath inside the Temple, so too for monetary oaths outside the Temple — gilgul shevua applies even to uncertain claims.

How Far Does Oath Extension Reach?

Rav Yehuda / Rav
One can even say: 'Swear you are not my Canaanite slave' — oath extension has no limit!
Gemara
But one who calls another a slave shall be ostracized (nidui)! The court won't enforce this.
Rava
Rather: 'Swear you were not sold to me as a Hebrew slave' — no insult to lineage, just a property claim.
Gemara
But that's just a monetary claim! What's novel?
Rava
A Hebrew slave himself is acquired by his master — it's ownership over a person, not just money.
Gemara
Then it's like land — already taught! Why state it?
Rava
Because if he were truly purchased as a slave, it would have been public knowledge. You might think no oath applies without publicity — kmashma lan (teaches us) it still does.
Chiddush Even where the alleged transaction would have generated publicity and its absence weakens the claim, gilgul shevua still applies.

Mishna: Kinyan Chalifin (Barter Exchange)

What the Mishna Says

  • All items used as monetary value for another item…
  • Once one party acquires — obligated for the exchange item
  • Example: Ox for cow, donkey for ox

Rav Yehuda's Interpretation

  • 'All items' = items that can be appraised
  • Excludes coins (whose value is self-evident)
  • All items — not only vessels — can effect chalifin
  • Proof: Mishna's examples are animals, not coins
Rule Coins do NOT effect kinyan chalifin. The mishna refers to exchange of appraised goods — animals, produce, or vessels.

Money, Meshikha & the 'Wheat Burned' Decree

R' Yoḥanan
By Torah law, money effects acquisition of movable property.
R' Yoḥanan
The Sages decreed meshikha (pulling) is needed — lest the seller say 'Your wheat was burned in the loft' and not save the buyer's goods.
Reish Lakish
Meshikha is explicitly stated in the Torah — it's not merely a Rabbinic decree!
Gemara
If Reish Lakish holds like Rav Naḥman (no produce exchange) and no money acquisition by Torah law — how does he explain the mishna?
Gemara
Perforce — Reish Lakish must hold like Rav Sheshet that produce effects exchange.
Common vs. Uncommon R' Yoḥanan: The Rabbis only decreed meshikha for common transactions. For uncommon cases (like switching from money to barter mid-deal), Torah law of money acquisition still applies.
