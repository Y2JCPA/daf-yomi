Betrothal with Sacred Property: Offerings & Hekdesh

R' Yosei
One who betroths a woman with his portion of offerings — whether kodshei kodashim or kodashim kalim — she is not betrothed.
Rav
The matter is still disputed — the Sages never reached a final conclusion.
Abaye
R' Yochanan reports the halakha was decided like R' Yosei. Even R' Yehuda accepted this in the Sifra.
R' Meir
Betrothal with hekdesh: intentionally — betrothed; unwittingly — not betrothed.
R' Yehuda
The opposite: unwittingly — betrothed; intentionally — not betrothed.
Core Issue Sacred property — both priestly offerings and Temple treasury funds — creates complex problems when used for betrothal, because the items have inherent sanctity that conflicts with personal use.

Can Priests Betroth with Their Offering Portions?

R' Yosei
Whether kodshei kodashim (most sacred offerings) or kodashim kalim (lesser sanctity) — the woman is not betrothed.
R' Yochanan
They counted the opinions and concluded the halakha follows R' Yosei.
Rav
It is still a matter of dispute — no conclusion was reached.
Abaye
R' Yochanan's report is more reasonable. Even R' Yehuda in the Sifra rules that no law of exchange applies to priestly portions at all.
Resolution Abaye proves from the Sifra — attributed to R' Yehuda — that even R' Yehuda accepted R' Yosei's position, supporting R' Yochanan's claim of a concluded ruling.

Can Priests Exchange Offering Portions? (Baraita from Sifra)

Can a priest trade meal-offering portions for animal offering portions?
↓
❌ No — 'Shall all the sons of Aaron have' (Lev. 7:9–10)
↓
What about meal-offerings for bird-offerings?
(Birds substitute for meal-offerings in poverty)
↓
❌ No — 'All that is prepared in the deep pan…shall all the sons of Aaron have'
↓
What about bird-offerings for animal offerings?
(Both involve blood on the altar)
↓
❌ No — 'And on a griddle' teaches this too
Principle Each phrase in Leviticus 7:9–10 excludes another possible exchange, systematically closing every category: animal↔meal, bird↔meal, bird↔animal, even meal↔meal of different types.

Betrothal with Hekdesh: R' Meir vs. R' Yehuda

R' Meir

  • Intentionally uses hekdesh → She IS betrothed
  • Unwittingly uses hekdesh → She is NOT betrothed
  • Reason: Intentional use effects a real transfer despite the sin
  • Unwitting use = no genuine intent to betroth with hekdesh

R' Yehuda

  • Unwittingly uses hekdesh → She IS betrothed
  • Intentionally uses hekdesh → She is NOT betrothed
  • Reason: Intentional misuse of hekdesh = invalid act
  • Unwitting error = treated as ordinary money, betrothal works
Key Distinction The dispute turns on whether intentional misuse of hekdesh empowers or invalidates the transaction — does the person's deliberate act make it real, or does their knowing violation make it void?

Whose Satisfaction Matters? Tithe vs. Hekdesh

R' Ya'akov
I learned from R' Yochanan: unwitting betrothal with tithe (per R' Yehuda) and with hekdesh (per R' Meir) — both not betrothed. One reason is she doesn't want it; the other is neither wants it. But I don't remember which is which.
R' Yirmeya
Tithe: she doesn't want the trouble of transporting it to Jerusalem, but he is happy — he acquires a wife for free.
Hekdesh: neither wants sacred property desacralized.
R' Ya'akov
No — tithe: neither wants it. She dreads the journey; he also worries it may be lost en route, causing her distress.
Hekdesh: she doesn't want desacralization, but he gains a wife without spending his own money.
Unresolved The Gemara cannot determine by logic alone which reason applies to which case — the matter depends on the received tradition from R' Yochanan.
