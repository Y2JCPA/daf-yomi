Father's Consent & the Minor's Betrothal

A minor girl is betrothed without her father's consent
↓
Father desired it?
Betrothal may be valid — requires get + ḥalitza + refusal
Father did NOT desire it?
Betrothal is invalid — refusal alone suffices
↓
Uncertainty drives stringent requirements throughout the daf
KEY THEME When a father's intent is unknown, the Gemara layers multiple safeguards — get, ḥalitza, and refusal — to cover every possibility.

Selling a Widow-Daughter as Maidservant

Baraita
A father can sell his daughter to a Kohen Gadol even if she is a widow, or to a common priest even if she is a divorcée — the marriage is prohibited but takes effect.
Gemara
What widow? If the father betrothed her, he can't sell her after betrothal. So she must have betrothed herself — proving a minor's self-betrothal works! This contradicts Ulla!
Rav Amram / R' Yitzḥak
No — she is widowed from a betrothal of designation (yi'ud). Her master designated her as wife, then died. Per R' Yosei b. R' Yehuda, the original sale money was not for betrothal — so father can sell again.
KEY PRINCIPLE Yi'ud betrothal is unique: since the father received money for servitude, not marriage, he retains the right to sell her again after the master's death.

Minor Betrothed Without Father → Falls to Yibum

Minor betrothed without father's consent; husband dies → falls to yavam
↓
Yavam performs levirate betrothal
She needs three things:
① Get — maybe father wanted only the yavam
② Ḥalitza — maybe father wanted the first husband
③ Refusal (mi'un) — maybe father wanted neither
Yavam does NOT perform levirate betrothal
She needs only ḥalitza

No refusal needed — everyone knows a ḥalutza's sister is only rabbinically forbidden
RAV HUNA / RAV She performs refusal for levirate betrothal but NOT for the levirate bond itself. The triple requirement covers all possible states of the father's intent.

Two Incidents: Is the Betrothal Valid?

🍷 Poplar Trees

  • Two men drinking wine in Babylonia
  • One gives a cup: 'Betroth your daughter to my son'
  • Ravina: Even if father desired it, we don't assume the SON desired it
  • A son would not be so brazen as to appoint his father as agent

🥬 Vegetables in the Market

  • Man betroths minor girl with a bundle of vegetables in the marketplace
  • Ravina: Even if we worry about father's consent — only for DIGNIFIED betrothal
  • What counts as degrading? Both vegetables AND marketplace
  • Either one alone is sufficient to disqualify
RAVINA'S VIEW Ravina consistently rules leniently: he does not accept the concern that the father desired the betrothal, and adds that degrading circumstances eliminate any remaining worry.

The Roof Betrothal: Father's Word vs. Relative's Action

Scenario
Father agrees to marry daughter to wife's relative. During the festive meal, his own relative sneaks to the roof and betroths her.
Abaye
'The remnant of Israel shall not speak lies' (Zephaniah 3:13) — the father gave his word to wife's relative. He would not go back on it.
Rava
A person does not take the trouble of preparing a meal and then cause it to be lost. His investment in the feast proves he didn't want this betrothal.
Difference
If no meal was prepared: Abaye still says father keeps his word; Rava would be concerned the betrothal is valid.
NAFKA MINA When there is no meal — Abaye relies on moral integrity (keeping one's word), while Rava relies on practical investment (effort already expended).

Teruma Rights: Father's Presence & Consent

ScenarioRav / Rav HunaRav Asi / R' Yirmeya bar Abba
Betrothed with consent → married w/o consent → father overseas✅ May eat teruma until father protests❌ May not — perhaps father will come and protest retroactively
Betrothed with consent → married w/o consent → father present & silent❌ Silence = anger, not consent✅ Silence = comfort with the marriage
Betrothed without consent → married w/o consent → father present✅ May eat teruma (Ulla finds this 'irritating')❌ May not eat teruma
PRINCIPLE The interpretation of a father's silence is the crux: does silence indicate suppressed anger or tacit approval? Rav Huna and Rav Yirmeya bar Abba read the same silence in opposite ways depending on context.
