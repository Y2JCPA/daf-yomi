The Ear, the Gift & the Redemption: Parsing Every Word

First Tanna
The word 'his ear' teaches that one who sells himself may not be pierced
R' Elazar
'His ear' teaches piercing applies to his ear but not her ear — only male slaves, not maidservants
First Tanna
I derive that from 'the slave shall say' — a slave, not a maidservant
R' Elazar
I need that verse for a different rule: he must declare his wish while still a slave
KEY THEME This daf demonstrates how Tannaim derive entirely different halakhot from the same words, each building a coherent system from subtle textual cues.

Deriving Right Ear for Piercing

Torah says 'his ear' regarding piercing a Hebrew slave (Exodus 21:6)
↓
Which ear? The Torah doesn't specify right or left
↓
Gezera shava (verbal analogy): 'Ear' here ↔ 'Right ear' of leper purification (Leviticus 14:14)
↓
Just as the leper's right ear is specified, so too the slave's right ear is pierced
R' ELIEZER This gezera shava is the primary purpose of the word 'ear' in the piercing verse, linking it to the leper's right ear.

What Does 'His Ear' Exclude?

First Tanna
If 'ear' alone suffices for the gezera shava, why say 'his ear'? The extra word excludes a self-sold slave from piercing
R' Elazar
'His ear' — his, not hers. Only a male slave can be pierced, not a maidservant
First Tanna
I exclude maidservants from 'the slave shall say' (Exodus 21:5) — slave, not maidservant
R' Elazar
I need that verse to teach: he must say it while still a slave, not after emancipation
First Tanna
I derive both from 'slave' vs. 'the slave' — the definite article teaches the additional halakha
PATTERN Each Sage accounts for every word — what one derives from 'his ear,' the other derives from 'the slave,' and vice versa.

Slave Sold to a Gentile: Redemption Paths

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi

  • Redeemed only through relatives
  • NOT released after six years of labor
  • 'By any of these' (Lev. 25:54) is limiting
  • Rejects the gezera shava of 'hired worker'

Dissenting Tannaim

  • Accept the verbal analogy: 'hired worker' ↔ 'hired worker'
  • One sold to a gentile IS released after six years
  • Derives additional halakhot from the analogy
  • R' Yosei HaGelili and R' Akiva disagree on details
A FORTIORI Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi argues: if a court-sold slave (who can't be redeemed by relatives) goes free after six years, certainly a gentile-sold slave (who CAN be redeemed by relatives) should too — unless the verse specifically excludes it.

Redemption from a Gentile: To Freedom or Slavery?

R' Yosei HaGelili
Redeemed by relatives → to freedom
Redeemed by others → to slavery (must work off the redemption cost)
R' Akiva
Redeemed by relatives → to slavery (indentured to relatives)
Redeemed by others → to freedom
R' Yosei HaGelili
I compare relatives' redemption to self-redemption (juxtaposed before it) — both lead to freedom
R' Akiva
I compare others' redemption to self-redemption (juxtaposed after it) — both lead to freedom
HERMENEUTIC DISPUTE R' Yosei HaGelili interprets based on juxtaposition to the PRECEDING phrase; R' Akiva interprets based on juxtaposition to the FOLLOWING phrase.
