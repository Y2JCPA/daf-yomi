Kiddushin 52: Limits of Betrothal — What Property Can Effect Marriage?

DAF OVERVIEW The Gemara concludes the Abaye-Rava dispute on kiddushin she'eino masurin l'biah (betrothal not given to consummation), ruling for Abaye in one of six famous exceptions. It then analyzes the mishna of five women and a basket of figs, deriving laws about Sabbatical produce, stolen property, and agency in betrothal. Multiple cases illustrate that betrothal with stolen property fails. The new mishna addresses betrothal with sacred offerings, ma'aser sheni, and hekdesh.

Refutation of Rava: 5 Sons & 5 Daughters

Baraita (Tavyumei)
If a father says 'One of your daughters is betrothed to one of my sons' — each daughter needs 5 gittin (bills of divorce). If one son dies, she needs 4 gittin + ḥalitza.
Rava
This betrothal is not given to consummation — no specific couple can marry! It should be invalid.
Gemara
The baraita says 'one of your daughters to one of my sons' — they were never identified. Yet the betrothal is valid! Conclusive refutation of Rava.
Abaye
Betrothal not given to consummation is still valid. The halakha follows me.
HALAKHA The refutation of Rava is teyuvta — conclusive. Kiddushin that cannot lead to consummation is nonetheless valid betrothal.

Rav's Four Halakhot from the Five Women Mishna

🧺 A man gathered figs from the women's field during Shevi'it and betrothed all five women
↓
🌾 Halakha 1: Betrothal with Sabbatical Year produce is valid — not limited to eating only
↓
🚫 Halakha 2: Betrothal with stolen property is invalid — even if stolen from her
↓
👩‍👩‍👧 Halakha 3: A woman can serve as agent to accept betrothal for another — even becoming her rival wife
↓
❓ Halakha 4: Betrothal not given to consummation — Rav was uncertain whether to follow Abaye or Rava
KEY PROOF The mishna specifies the figs were Shevi'it produce from their field — implying only ownerless Shevi'it produce works, but ordinary stolen property would not effect betrothal.

Betrothal with Stolen Property: Three Cases

A man takes property not his own and uses it to betroth a woman
↓
Woman Washing Feet
Man grabs dinars from another person, throws them to her.
Rava: Owner has not despaired.
❌ Not betrothed.
Sharecropper's Onions
Takes a handful (moza) without permission.
Rava: Who gave you these?
❌ Not betrothed.
But a full bundle — permitted, as they split the crop.
Brewer's Sediment
Uses beer sediment for betrothal. Owner says 'give her the better stuff.'
Rava: Owner spoke from embarrassment, not consent.
❌ Not betrothed.
PRINCIPLE In all three cases, Rava rules the woman is not betrothed. Stolen property cannot effect kiddushin — even apparent owner consent may be mere embarrassment.

The Teruma Analogy: When Does Owner Silence = Consent?

Teruma (Tithing)

  • Someone separates teruma from another's produce without permission
  • Owner says: 'Take the better-quality produce instead'
  • If better produce actually exists → teruma is valid (sincere approval)
  • If it doesn't exist → teruma invalid (sarcastic)
  • Owner was obligated to separate teruma anyway — so consent is assumed

Betrothal

  • Brewer takes sediment to betroth a woman
  • Owner says: 'Why not give her the better sediment?'
  • Owner was NOT obligated to help the brewer betroth anyone
  • Comment stems from embarrassment, not genuine consent
  • Therefore: Not valid betrothal
DISTINCTION Owner silence or apparent approval counts as consent for teruma (a mitzva the owner must anyway perform), but not for betrothal, where the owner gains nothing and may simply feel too embarrassed to protest.
