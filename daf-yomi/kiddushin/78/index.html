Forbidden Priestly Unions, Converts' Daughters & Parental Credibility

Rav Yehuda
A High Priest with a widow receives two sets of lashes: one for 'shall not take' and one for 'shall not profane.'
Rava
But a mishna in Makkot says only two labels for a widow-divorcée — implying one set per label, not two!
Abaye
Betrothal alone triggers lashes for 'shall not take'; intercourse adds lashes for 'shall not profane.'
Rava
No — only intercourse triggers lashes. 'Shall not take' means: don't take so that you not profane.
R' Yehuda
A convert's daughter is like a ḥalal's daughter — unfit for the priesthood.
R' Yosei
Even a male convert who married a female convert — his daughter is fit for the priesthood.
📌 Core Tension This daf weaves together three themes: the mechanics of priestly prohibitions and lashes, the lineage status of converts' daughters, and the limits of parental credibility in declaring a child's status.

Abaye vs. Rava: When Does the Prohibition Apply?

Abaye
A priest who betrothed a forbidden woman is flogged for 'shall not take.'
Abaye
If he then engaged in intercourse, he receives additional lashes for 'shall not profane.'
Rava
If he engaged in intercourse, he is flogged. If he only betrothed — no lashes at all.
Rava
'Shall not take' means: why shall he not take? So that he not profane. The prohibition targets only the act that causes profanation.
Gemara
Both agree: remarrying one's divorcée — intercourse alone without betrothal does not incur lashes, as the Torah forbids only 'taking.'
Gemara
Both agree: High Priest with widow — intercourse without betrothal still incurs lashes, because 'he shall not profane' stands independently.
⚖️ Common Ground Despite their disagreement, Abaye and Rava agree on edge cases: remarrying a divorcée requires betrothal for liability, while a High Priest's intercourse with a widow incurs lashes even without betrothal.

Ḥalutza: Torah Law or Rabbinic?

SourceArgument
BaraitaThe verse says 'And a woman divorced' (Lev. 21:7) — the extra 'and' includes a ḥalutza
Gemara's ResponseThis is only an asmakhta (support) — the true prohibition is rabbinic
Practical ImpactA priest with a divorcée-ḥalutza receives lashes only for the divorcée status, not the ḥalutza
💡 Distinction Even when a verse appears to support a law, the Gemara distinguishes between a true Torah derivation and a mere 'asmakhta' — a verse cited as a mnemonic support for a rabbinic enactment.

Daughters of Converts: Who May Marry a Priest?

R' Yehuda

  • Daughter of a male convert = daughter of a male ḥalal
  • Unfit to marry into the priesthood
  • Logic: a ḥalal can marry an Israelite but daughter is unfit — same for a convert
  • Widow of a convert also forbidden to a priest

R' Yosei

  • Even a male convert who married a female convert — daughter is fit
  • Halakha follows R' Yosei
  • But after Temple destruction, priests acted stringently per R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov
  • Would only marry convert's daughter if one parent was born Jewish

Rav Naḥman / Rav Huna

  • If a priest consults us — we advise against marrying daughter of two converts
  • But if he already married her — we do not remove her
  • Follows R' Yosei's ruling as binding law
📌 Practical Ruling The halakha follows R' Yosei that a convert's daughter is fit for the priesthood, but the priestly custom was to be stringent and require at least one born-Jewish parent.

Interpreting Ezekiel's Verse

וְנֵר אֱלֹהִים טֶרֶם יִכְבֶּה וּשְׁמוּאֵל שֹׁכֵב בְּהֵיכַל ה׳
🔍 Hermeneutics Rav Naḥman challenged Rava: Can one verse address two different subjects — the High Priest (who may not marry a widow) and the common priest (who may)? Rava answered yes, citing a parallel: 'And the lamp of God was not yet gone out, and Samuel was laid down to sleep in the Temple' (I Samuel 3:3). Samuel could not sleep in the Temple — rather, the lamp was in the Temple and Samuel was in his own place. One verse, two subjects.
