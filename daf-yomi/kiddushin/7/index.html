Three Modes of Third-Party Betrothal

Case 1: Woman says: "Give 100 dinars to that person and I will be betrothed to you"
↓
Derived from Arev (Guarantor) — she commits without receiving benefit
↓
Case 2: Man gives 100 dinars to the woman and says: "Become betrothed to so-and-so"
↓
Derived from Eved Kena'ani — third party acquires without losing his own money
↓
Case 3: Woman says: "Give 100 dinars to so-and-so and I will be betrothed to him"
↓
Derived from both proofs combined (arev + eved)
Principle The Gemara proves each case step by step, using the guarantor and slave paradigms to show benefit need not flow directly to the party being acquired.

Guarantor vs. Canaanite Slave — The Two Proof Sources

Arev (Guarantor)

  • Commits to repay the debt
  • Receives no direct benefit
  • The loan money goes to the debtor
  • Proves: One can commit without receiving

Eved Kena'ani (Slave)

  • Acquires his own freedom
  • Loses nothing of his own
  • A third party pays the master
  • Proves: One can acquire through another's payment
Why Both? Neither proof alone suffices. The guarantor proves commitment without benefit, but there the payer acquires. The slave proves acquisition without loss, but there the acquirer transfers himself. Only together do they cover Case 3.

Can a Woman Pay for Her Own Betrothal?

Rava
Dilemma: A woman says "Here are 100 dinars and I will be betrothed to you" — what is the halakha?
Mar Zutra (in name of Rav Pappa)
She is betrothed.
Rav Ashi
Objection: This would mean nichsei she-yesh lahen aḥarayut (people, like land) are acquired through nichsei she-ein lahen aḥarayut (money) — but the Mishnah says the reverse!
Mar Zutra
We are dealing with an adam ḥashuv (important man) — the benefit of his acceptance of her gift is itself sufficient for her to transfer herself.
Adam Ḥashuv The satisfaction a woman derives from an important man accepting her gift counts as valid 'benefit' — transforming the transaction from a monetary acquisition into a consent-based one.

Combined Betrothal Dilemmas — Daughters, Cows & Land

Rava: "Your two daughters to my two sons with one peruta"
Follow Giver/Receiver
One peruta given and received → Valid
Follow Individuals
Less than one peruta per couple → Invalid
↓
Rav Pappa: "Your daughter and your cow with one peruta"
Split Payment
½ peruta each → neither works
Full Peruta + Meshikha
Daughter with peruta, cow by pulling → Valid
↓
Rav Ashi: "Your daughter and your land with one peruta" — same structure
↓
All three dilemmas: Teiku
Note These dilemmas test whether different acquisition methods (money, pulling, possession) can operate simultaneously within a single transaction.
