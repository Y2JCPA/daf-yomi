QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the prohibition against non-sacred slaughter in the Temple courtyard extend to birds, even though the Torah doesn't mention slaughter for birds?
A. From three extra instances of 'it' (oto) in slaughter verses
B. From a kal va-ḥomer from domesticated animals
C. From a gezeirah shavah with sacrificial birds
D. Birds are included by rabbinic decree only
Correct: A — The Gemara derives the inclusion of birds from three verses that each state 'and he shall slaughter it' — the repeated word 'it' (oto) serves as a textual basis to extend the prohibition beyond domesticated animals.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does Rabba resolve the contradiction between R' Shimon permitting betrothal with courtyard-slaughtered ḥulin and R' Shimon requiring such animals to be burned?
A. The first baraita is dealing with a tereifah — R' Shimon holds slaughter that doesn't permit eating isn't called slaughter
B. R' Shimon changed his opinion between the two baraitot
C. The first baraita is about rabbinic prohibitions, the second about Torah prohibitions
D. The burning requirement is only mi-derabbanan according to R' Shimon
Correct: A — Rabba explains that the first baraita (permitting betrothal) involves a tereifah slaughtered in the courtyard. Since R' Shimon holds that sheḥitah she'einah re'uyah (slaughter that doesn't render the animal fit to eat) is not considered slaughter, the courtyard-slaughter prohibition never applies.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why can't we derive from idol worship alone that money from all forbidden-benefit items retains the prohibited status?
A. Because idol worship and Shevi'it are two verses that come as one, and such pairs don't teach a general rule
B. Because idol worship is uniquely severe and cannot serve as a paradigm
C. Because the Torah explicitly states money from other prohibitions is permitted
D. Because the principle only applies to items that can be redeemed
Correct: A — Both idol worship and Sabbatical Year produce independently teach that their prohibition extends to proceeds. Since they are 'two verses that come as one' (shnei ketuvim ha-ba'im k'eḥad), the principle is that they cannot establish a general rule for other cases.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Shmuel, what is the minimum amount of terumah that satisfies the Torah requirement?
A. One-sixtieth of the produce
B. One-fiftieth of the produce
C. Even a single grain
D. One-fortieth of the produce
Correct: C — Shmuel rules that by Torah law, even one grain given as terumah exempts the entire heap, since the Torah does not specify a minimum amount for terumah. This has implications for how much a thief must repay for stolen untithed produce.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Abaye resolve the contradiction between the mishna (betrothal with purification water is valid) and the baraita (one who accepts payment for sprinkling — his water is invalid)?
A. The mishna refers to a kohen and the baraita to a Yisrael
B. The mishna allows payment for bringing/filling the vessel; the baraita prohibits payment for the actual sprinkling and sanctifying
C. The mishna is R' Shimon's view and the baraita is the Rabbis' view
D. The mishna deals with ashes and the baraita with water only
Correct: B — Abaye distinguishes between preparatory labor (bringing and filling the vessel), for which one may accept payment, and the mitzvah act itself (sprinkling and sanctifying), for which payment invalidates the water. The mishna's betrothal works because it represents the value of the permitted preparatory labor.
