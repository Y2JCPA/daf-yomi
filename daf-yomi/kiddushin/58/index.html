Key Principle Money received from selling forbidden-benefit items is permitted — derived from the fact that idol worship and Shevi'it are two verses that come as one and thus cannot teach a general rule extending their stringency to other prohibitions.

Expanding the Courtyard Slaughter Prohibition

🐄 Fit sacrificial animals — Base prohibition on ḥulin slaughtered in the Azara
↓
🦕 Blemished animals — Included because they are of the type fit for sacrifice
↓
🦌 Undomesticated animals (ḥayah) — Included because they require sheḥitah like domesticated animals
↓
🐦 Birds — Derived from three extra "it" (oto) in the verses about slaughter
↓
🚫 No benefit at all — Unlike tereifah which may be cast to dogs, courtyard-slaughtered ḥulin is fully prohibited
Source "You shall cast it to the dogs" (Exodus 22:30) — it (tereifah) to the dogs, but not ḥulin shenishtaḥatu ba-Azara.

Betrothal with Ḥulin Slaughtered in the Courtyard

Mar Yehuda
I found a contradiction in R' Shimon's rulings!
R' Shimon (Baraita 1)
One who betroths with ḥulin slaughtered in the Azara — she is betrothed. The prohibition is only rabbinic.
R' Shimon (Baraita 2)
Non-sacred animals slaughtered in the Azara must be burned! This implies a Torah prohibition.
Rav Yosef & Rav Shmuel
They were silent and had no answer...
Rabba
A quarrelsome person put you up to this! The first baraita deals with a tereifah slaughtered in the courtyard.
Rabba
R' Shimon holds: sheḥitah she'einah re'uyah — slaughter that doesn't permit eating is not considered slaughter at all.
Resolution Since the animal was already a tereifah, R' Shimon says the slaughter has no halakhic effect — so the courtyard-slaughter prohibition never applies, and benefit (including kiddushin) is permitted.

Money from Forbidden-Benefit Items

Can one betroth with money
from selling issurei hana'ah?
↓
Torah says about idol worship:
"You shall be banned like it" (Deut. 7:26)
— proceeds share its status
↓
Torah says about Shevi'it:
"It shall be holy" (Lev. 25:12)
— sanctity transfers to money
↓
Two verses that come as one
(shnei ketuvim ha-ba'im k'eḥad)
do NOT teach a general rule
↓
For all other prohibitions:
money from their sale is permitted
Principle Since both idol worship and Shevi'it independently teach that proceeds retain forbidden status, we cannot derive this rule for other prohibitions — so the money is permitted.

Stealing Untithed Produce: What Must the Thief Pay?

OpinionPayment RequiredReasoning
R' Yehuda HaNasiFull value of untithed produceIncludes value of terumot and tithes
R' Yosei b. R' YehudaValue of non-sacred portion onlyTovas hana'ah (benefit of discretion) has no monetary value
Four Explanations The Gemara offers four ways to explain the dispute: (1) whether tovas hana'ah has monetary value; (2) whether unseparated gifts are considered already separated; (3) whether the halakha follows Shmuel that one grain suffices as terumah; (4) penalty on thief vs. penalty on negligent owner.

Shmuel's Ruling & Its Impact on Theft Repayment

Follows Shmuel

  • By Torah law, even one grain of terumah exempts the entire heap
  • Owner can claim: "I would have separated just one grain"
  • Thief must pay for nearly all the produce as ḥullin

Does Not Follow Shmuel

  • Terumah requires a meaningful amount (at least 1/60th)
  • Owner must subtract terumah portion from his claim
  • Thief pays less — reduced by the terumah share
Note This is one of four possible explanations for the R' Yehuda HaNasi / R' Yosei dispute — each explanation shifts the fundamental point of disagreement.
