Can One Borrow or Partially Redeem Walled City Houses?

Baraita #1
One can borrow money to redeem a house in a walled city, and can partially redeem it.
Baraita #2
One cannot borrow money to redeem it, nor can one partially redeem it.
Gemara
No contradiction — Baraita #1 follows the Rabbis, Baraita #2 follows Rabbi Shimon.
Mnemonic
חרש, חבש, זמן — a mnemonic for the scholars who raise the following dilemmas: Rav Aḥa/Rav Ashi, Rav Aḥa Sava/Rav Ashi, Mar Zutra/Ravina.
RESOLUTION Contradictory baraitot are resolved by attributing them to different Tannaitic opinions — a classic Talmudic technique.

Common Denominator Derivation (Binyan Av)

Goal: Prove that one who consecrates a field cannot borrow to redeem or partially redeem
↓
Source 1: Ancestral Field
Power enhanced (redeem forever) — yet cannot borrow/partially redeem
Source 2: Walled City House
Power diminished (only 1 year) — yet cannot borrow/partially redeem
↓
Common denominator: Both can be redeemed, yet neither allows borrowing or partial redemption
↓
∴ Consecrated field also cannot be redeemed via borrowing or partial redemption
REFUTATION Mar Zutra objects: Both sources share the weakness that redemption is blocked in the second year — but a consecrated field can be redeemed in the second year. Ravina answers: A Hebrew slave sold to a gentile proves otherwise.

Can Relatives Redeem Houses in Walled Cities?

Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana
Can we derive from the verbal analogy of "ge'ullato" (Lev. 25:29 / 25:26) that relatives may redeem walled city houses, just as they redeem ancestral fields?
Rav Sheshet
No. The house cannot be redeemed by relatives.
Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana
But the verse says "in all your ancestral land you shall grant redemption" (Lev. 25:24) — this includes houses!
Gemara
That refers to houses in open areas, not walled cities. And the verse establishes that redemption of a Hebrew slave is an obligation.
KEY DISTINCTION The word ge'ula in the context of ancestral fields refers to partial redemption, not redemption by relatives — so the verbal analogy does not extend to relative-redemption of walled city houses.

The Pierced Hebrew Slave (Nirtza)

Acquisition

  • Master pierces the slave's ear with an awl (מרצע)
  • Based on Exodus 21:6 — "his master shall pierce his ear"
  • Slave had chosen to remain rather than go free after 6 years

Release

  • Released at the Jubilee Year (Yovel)
  • Released upon the master's death
  • "Forever" = until Jubilee, not literally eternal
  • Does NOT serve master's son or daughter
VERSE "He shall serve him forever" (Exodus 21:6) — le'olam means until the Jubilee cycle ends, not for eternity.

"Take" = general; "awl" = detail (metal); "through his ear" = general again"Take" = amplify (any tool); "awl" = restrict; "through his ear" = amplify again
Include only items similar to the detail (metal)Include everything except the most dissimilar item

CORE DISPUTE The debate hinges on two hermeneutical methods: Klal u'Frat narrows to metal tools; Ribbui u'Mi'ut broadly includes everything except chemical means.
