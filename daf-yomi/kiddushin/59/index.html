Why 'Agent' vs. 'Another'?

Mishna Here: 'Another'

  • Uses the term "another" (not agent)
  • Teaches: even a non-agent who acts for himself is a scoundrel
  • You might think only a trusted agent is deceitful — kol she'kein an outsider too

Mishna Ch. 2 (50a): 'Agent'

  • Uses the term "agent" (shaliach)
  • Teaches: even an agent who deviates in location — betrothal is invalid
  • You might think an agent would be given latitude — teaches otherwise
KEY INSIGHT Each Mishnah chooses its term to teach the less obvious case — a hallmark of Talmudic precision.

Ravin the Pious & Rav — Agents Who Acted for Themselves

Gemara
Ravin the Pious was sent to betroth a woman for his son — but betrothed her to himself!
Questioner
But the baraita says such a person is a scoundrel! How could a pious man do this?
Gemara
The family refused to give her to the son — they would only accept the father.
Questioner
He should have informed his son first!
Gemara
He feared: In the meantime, someone else will come and betroth her.
Gemara
Similarly, Rav bought land for himself instead of for Rabba bar bar Ḥana — the violent locals would only sell to Rav.
PRINCIPLE Acting as agent for oneself is normally deceitful, but justified when the principal's deal would fail and delay risks losing the opportunity entirely.

The Pauper's Loaf — Rav Giddel vs. Rabbi Abba

📋 Rav Giddel is negotiating to buy a plot of land
↓
💰 Rabbi Abba comes and purchases it first
↓
😤 Rav Giddel complains to R' Zeira → who tells R' Yitzchak Nappacha
↓
🍞 R' Yitzchak asks: If a pauper is reaching for a loaf and another snatches it — what is the law?
↓
⚖️ R' Abba answers: The one who took it is called wicked (rasha)
↓
😳 R' Yitzchak: Then why did you do exactly that? R' Abba: I didn't know Rav Giddel was negotiating!
↓
🎁 R' Abba offers it as a gift — Rav Giddel refuses, citing "He who hates gifts shall live" (Proverbs 15:27)
↓
🏛️ Neither takes the land — it becomes known as "The Land of the Sages"
ANI MEHAPECH B'CHARARA One who takes what another was actively pursuing is called rasha (wicked). This became a foundational principle in Jewish business ethics.

Can a Statement Nullify a Statement?

R' Yochanan
A woman who appointed an agent for betrothal can retract verbally — a statement nullifies a prior statement.
Reish Lakish
She cannot retract — a statement does not nullify a prior statement.
R' Yochanan
Proof: One who appoints an agent to separate terumah and cancels before he acts — the terumah is invalid!
Rava
That case is different — the owner separated terumah himself, which is an action, not mere words.
Reish Lakish
Counter-proof: For tumah, thought to improve a vessel doesn't negate prior thought — showing statements don't nullify statements!
R' Yochanan
Tumah is different — thought there has the force of action (per Rav Pappa's derivation from ki yuttan).
R' Yochanan
Decisive proof: A husband who sends a get through an agent can cancel it verbally — this is a statement nullifying a statement!
Gemara
Reish Lakish's position is conclusively refuted.
HALAKHA The law follows R' Yochanan: a verbal retraction can cancel a prior verbal appointment, even in the version regarding betrothal agency.
