Menachot 63 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
Which is deeper — the Machavat or the Marcheshet?
A. Machavat — it is deep and covered
B. Marcheshet — it is deep and covered
C. They are both the same depth
D. Neither has a fixed depth — it depends on the vow
Correct: B — Marcheshet is deep and covered. R' Chanina ben Gamliel and Beit Hillel agree: the Marcheshet is deep (like a pot with a cover), producing a soft product. The Machavat is flat and open, producing a firm, harder product. The prepositions confirm it: things go "in" (b') the Marcheshet but "on" (al) the Machavat.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
If you vowed "a meal offering in a Marcheshet," what does Beit Shammai say you must bring?
A. A Marcheshet offering only
B. A Machavat offering only
C. Both a Marcheshet and a Machavat offering
D. Either one — your choice
Correct: C — Both. Beit Shammai is uncertain whether the name "Marcheshet" refers to the vessel or the product. To cover all possibilities, they require bringing one of each type. Beit Hillel disagrees, saying it clearly refers to a specific Temple vessel, so one offering suffices.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
If someone vows "a meal offering baked in an oven," can they fulfill it by baking on roofing tiles?
A. Yes — any heated surface is an "oven"
B. No — only a proper oven (Tanur) qualifies
C. Yes — if there is no oven available
D. Only if the tiles are from Jerusalem
Correct: B — Only a proper oven (Tanur). The Mishna rules that baking on roofing tiles, in a Kupach (small portable oven), or in Arab desert pits does not fulfill a vow for an oven-baked Mincha. The word "oven" is restrictive and excludes these alternatives.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
If one vows a "baked meal offering" without specifying loaves or wafers, what does R' Yehuda rule?
A. He may bring half loaves and half wafers
B. He must bring all loaves OR all wafers — not a mixture
C. He may bring any combination he likes
D. He must consult a sage who will decide for him
Correct: B — All one type, no mixing. R' Yehuda derives this from the word "Korban" — a single unified offering. If you choose loaves, all 10 must be loaves. R' Shimon disagrees and permits mixing, since both types fall under the single category of "Minchat Ma'afeh Tanur."
QUESTION 5 OF 5
The two types of oven-baked Mincha are Challot and Rekikin. What is the main difference between them?
A. Challot use wheat flour; Rekikin use barley flour
B. Challot are thick loaves mixed with oil; Rekikin are thin wafers smeared with oil
C. Challot are for Kohanim; Rekikin are for Yisraelim
D. Challot are baked; Rekikin are fried
Correct: B. Challot are thick loaves that have oil mixed into the dough. Rekikin are thin wafers that have oil smeared on the outside after baking. Both use the same wheat flour and are baked in an oven — the difference is thickness and how the oil is applied.
