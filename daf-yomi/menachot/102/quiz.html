QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to R' Shimon, does an Olah (burnt offering) have the law of Piggul?
A. Yes — any offering with blood sprinkling can have Piggul
B. No — since no person eats it, only the altar "consumes" it, Piggul does not apply
C. Only if the Kohen explicitly stated Piggul intent
D. Yes, but only for the skin which goes to the Kohen
Correct: B. R' Shimon holds that Piggul only applies when there is both a Matir (permitting element) and a Nittar that is eaten by a person. Since the Olah is entirely consumed on the altar and no person eats it, R' Shimon says Piggul does not apply. The Chachamim disagree — they equate the altar's "consumption" to human eating.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the "Matir" (permitting element) of a standard meal offering (Mincha)?
A. The blood sprinkling on the altar
B. The Kemitzah — taking a handful of flour
C. The baking of the flour into bread
D. The Kohen's blessing over the offering
Correct: B. The Kemitzah (removal of a handful by the Kohen) is the Matir for a meal offering. Once the Kometz is burned on the altar, the remainder (Shirayim) becomes permitted for the Kohanim to eat. This is the meal-offering equivalent of blood sprinkling for animal offerings.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
If a meal offering becomes impure BEFORE the Kemitzah, what happens?
A. The Tzitz atones and it can still be offered
B. Only the impure portions are discarded
C. The entire offering is invalidated — Kemitzah cannot be performed on impure flour
D. It can still be offered but only on the outer altar
Correct: C. If the offering is impure before the Kemitzah, the permitting act has not yet occurred. Since the Kemitzah cannot be properly performed on impure flour, the entire offering is invalidated. The Tzitz can only atone for impurity that occurs AFTER the permitting act.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the Tzitz, and what does it do for impure offerings?
A. A golden headplate worn by the Kohen Gadol that atones for impurity of offerings (after the Matir was performed)
B. A copper altar where impure offerings are burned separately
C. A ritual bath for purifying offerings
D. A prayer recited by the Kohen Gadol over spoiled offerings
Correct: A. The Tzitz is the golden headplate inscribed "Holy to the Lord," worn on the Kohen Gadol's forehead. It atones for the impurity of offerings — meaning impure offerings that already had their permitting act performed can still be accepted. R' Yehuda says it only works while worn; R' Shimon says it works always.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to the Chachamim (Sages), why does Piggul apply to an Olah even though no person eats it?
A. Because the Kohen receives the skin, which counts as eating
B. Because the altar's "consumption" (burning) is treated as equivalent to human eating
C. Because all offerings automatically have Piggul by Torah law
D. Because the smoke ascending is considered a form of consumption
Correct: B. The Chachamim hold that the altar's burning of the flesh is halachically equivalent to a person eating it. Therefore, intent to burn the flesh beyond the designated time is Piggul just as intent to eat beyond the time would be for a Shelamim or Chatat.
