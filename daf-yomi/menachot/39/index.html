Linen Cloak: The Kilayim Problem

Beit Shammai

  • Linen cloak is exempt from tzitzit
  • Why? Sky-blue (wool) + linen = kilayim (forbidden mixture)
  • The positive mitzva of tzitzit does NOT override the prohibition of kilayim

Beit Hillel

  • Linen cloak is obligated in tzitzit
  • The positive mitzva DOES override kilayim
  • The Torah itself commanded wool strings on linen garments!
3 / 7

R. Eliezer b. R. Tzadok's Observation

"Anyone who affixes sky-blue strings to a linen cloak in Jerusalem is nothing other than one of those who cause astonishment."

Even though Beit Hillel permits it, in practice people in Jerusalem were cautious and didn't do it — perhaps because of the concern about counterfeit tekhelet (which would mean kilayim without the mitzva justification).
4 / 7

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's Question

Rabbi: "If Beit Hillel permits it, why did the Torah forbid kilayim at all?"

Answer: Kilayim is forbidden in general. The specific case of tzitzit is an exception — the Torah explicitly permits mixing wool tekhelet with a linen garment for this one mitzva.
5 / 7

Spinning for the Sake of the Mitzva

Rava / R. Zeira: "The Torah states: 'You shall prepare yourself twisted cords' — prepare it, and not from what is already made."

The spinning of the tekhelet strings must be done for the sake of the mitzva (lishmah). Pre-spun threads not designated for tzitzit are invalid.
6 / 7

Letters from Eretz Yisrael

Interesting precedent: For chametz on Pesach and eating on Yom Kippur — prohibitions punishable by karet — communities relied on letters sent from the rabbinic authorities in Eretz Yisrael to determine the calendar dates.

This shows the level of trust in the Sanhedrin's calendar authority, even across great distances.
7 / 7

Key Takeaways — Menachot 39

1. Beit Shammai: linen cloak exempt (kilayim concern). Beit Hillel: obligated (mitzva overrides).

2. In practice, even in Jerusalem people were cautious about wool tzitzit on linen.

3. Kilayim is generally forbidden; tzitzit is a specific Torah exception.

4. Spinning must be lishmah — "prepare it, not from what is already made."

5. Communities trusted letters from Eretz Yisrael even for karet-level prohibitions.
