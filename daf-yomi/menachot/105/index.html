📖 "Mincha" vs. "Minchot" — Singular vs. Plural

Q
If someone says "I am obligated in a Mincha" (singular), how many must they bring?
A
One. "Mincha" is singular — they bring one Mincha offering.
Q
What if they say "Minchot" (plural)?
A
Two. The minimum plural is two. Even though "Minchot" could imply many, the minimum is sufficient.
Q
"I said 'Minchot' but I don't remember if I meant two, three, or ten!"
A
You must bring the maximum you might have intended. The principle: when in doubt about a vow, be strict — bring enough to cover all possibilities.

📜 "From" a Type — Partial Language

Someone says: "I vow from a Machabat offering"
What does "from" (מן) mean here?
↓
Interpretation 1
"From" = I want that specific TYPE
→ Bring a Machabat offering
Interpretation 2
"From" = part of, not the whole
→ Bring ONE Issaron of Machabat
(minimum unit)
↓
Halacha: "From a Machabat" = bring one Issaron of Machabat offering. The word "from" implies a partial amount — the minimum.

🌾 "I Am Obligated" — Active vs. Passive Language

"Harei Alai" (Upon Me)

  • 🗣️ "Upon me is a Mincha offering"
  • 📖 Creates a personal OBLIGATION
  • 💰 If the offering is lost → must bring a replacement
  • ⚖️ The vow attaches to the PERSON
  • ✅ This is the standard vow formula

"Harei Zu" (This Is)

  • 🗣️ "This flour IS a Mincha offering"
  • 📖 Creates consecration of the OBJECT
  • 💰 If lost → NOT required to replace
  • ⚖️ The sanctity attaches to the FLOUR
  • 📌 Less common — but has practical differences
Practical difference: If a fire destroys the consecrated flour — with "Harei Alai" (personal obligation), you owe a new one. With "Harei Zu" (this specific flour), the obligation was on that flour, not you — so you're free.

🔢 Multiple Vows — Combining or Separating?

Q
"I vow one Mincha, and I vow another Mincha." Can I combine them in one vessel?
A
NO. Each vow is a separate obligation. Each requires its own vessel, its own Kemitzah, its own offering procedure. They cannot be merged.
Q
What if I say "I vow TWO Minchot" in one statement?
A
Still separate. Even though said together, "two Minchot" = two distinct offerings, each with its own procedure. The plural creates two independent obligations.

💡 The "Poor Person's Gift" — God Values Intent

נֶאֱמַר בְּעוֹלַת בְּהֵמָה ״אִשֵּׁה רֵיחַ נִיחוֹחַ״, וּבְעוֹלַת הָעוֹף ״אִשֵּׁה רֵיחַ נִיחוֹחַ״, וּבַמִּנְחָה ״אִשֵּׁה רֵיחַ נִיחוֹחַ״
The famous teaching:
For the animal Olah — "a fire-offering, a pleasing aroma to God."
For the bird Olah — "a fire-offering, a pleasing aroma."
For the humble Mincha — "a fire-offering, a pleasing aroma."

All use the same phrase! Whether you bring an expensive bull or a handful of flour — God says the same thing: "a pleasing aroma." This teaches that whether one gives much or little, what matters is that their heart is directed toward Heaven.
