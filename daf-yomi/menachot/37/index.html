Lo Ta'aseh Overriding Aseh, Transition to Tzitzit & White vs. Sky-Blue

"Lo Tasur" — You Shall Not Deviate

Context: Discussing when a positive commandment (aseh) can override a prohibition (lo ta'aseh).

The prohibition "you shall not deviate to the left or right" (Deut. 17:11) — a rabbinic prohibition — is overridden by a Torah-level positive commandment in certain cases.
3 / 7

Transition: White & Sky-Blue Strings

New Mishna begins the tzitzit section:

The absence of white strings (lavan) does not prevent fulfillment of the mitzva with sky-blue strings (tekhelet), and vice versa.

One can fulfill the mitzva with either type alone!
4 / 7

How Can White Work Without Blue?

Rav Yehuda / Rav: The Mishna means one who omitted one type still fulfilled a mitzva — but didn't fulfill the complete mitzva.

Think of it like: wearing tefillin of the arm without the head. You performed a mitzva, but not the full mitzva.
5 / 7

R. Shimon's View: Two Separate Mitzvot

The Rabbis

  • White and sky-blue are ONE mitzva
  • Ideally both together
  • But each can fulfill independently

R. Shimon

  • They are truly TWO separate mitzvot
  • White strings = one mitzva
  • Sky-blue strings = separate mitzva
  • Neither prevents the other

The Torn Garment Corner

Mar bar Rav Ashi's garment tore at the corner where tzitzit were attached.

Discussion: Is tearing the garment at the corner and then re-attaching tzitzit an issue of "ta'aseh v'lo min ha'asui" — the strings must be made as tzitzit, not pre-existing strings that happen to be on a garment?
7 / 7

Key Takeaways — Menachot 37

1. An aseh can override a lo ta'aseh — even "lo tasur" (rabbinic prohibition).

2. White strings and sky-blue strings don't prevent each other — can fulfill with either alone.

3. Rav: omitting one type means you performed a mitzva but not the complete mitzva.

4. R. Shimon: white and blue are two entirely separate mitzvot.

5. Torn corner issue introduces "ta'aseh v'lo min ha'asui" — must actively make the tzitzit.
