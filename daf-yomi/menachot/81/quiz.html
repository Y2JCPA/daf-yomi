QUESTION 1 OF 5
Rav Yitzchak bar Shmuel proposes a conditional solution using a substitute animal. Why does Rav Nachman reject this?
A. Because a substitute doesn't require loaves under any circumstances
B. Because deliberately separating a substitute incurs 40 lashes — you can't build a permitted solution on a prohibited act
C. Because a conditional vow about substitutes has no halachic effect
D. Because two animals cannot be brought for a single thanks offering obligation
Correct: B. Rav Nachman points out that one who deliberately separates a substitute is liable to receive 40 lashes. A halachic remedy cannot be built on a deliberately prohibited act.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Abaye and the Sages propose placing loaves outside the Temple courtyard wall as a conditional remedy. What blocks this solution?
A. Loaves cannot be prepared outside Jerusalem
B. The four teruma loaves must be waved "before the Lord," which requires being inside the courtyard — yet bringing non-sacred items inside is also forbidden
C. The Kohen performing the service cannot leave the courtyard during the ritual
D. Conditional vows are not valid for sanctified items
Correct: B. There's an impossible bind: waving must be done "before the Lord" (inside the courtyard), but if the loaves aren't yet consecrated, bringing them inside would mean bringing non-sacred food into the Temple. You can't wave outside (invalid) and can't wave inside (forbidden). No way out.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Can a thanks offering be brought from second-tithe money?
A. No — second tithe money may only be used for food purchased in Jerusalem
B. Yes — and the loaves may also come from second-tithe money
C. Yes, but only if the loaves come from non-sacred money
D. No — all parts of a thanks offering must come from non-sacred money
Correct: B. The Mishna permits bringing a thanks offering from second-tithe money, and its loaves may also come from second-tithe money. What is forbidden is bringing the loaves from second-tithe wheat directly (the grain itself) — it must be purchased with second-tithe money, not taken in kind.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why can't the loaves of a thanks offering be made from second-tithe wheat directly (the grain itself)?
A. Second-tithe produce must be eaten in Jerusalem, not used for offerings
B. A Gezerah Shavah links peace offerings to second tithe — just as peace offerings are not the same species as tithe, thanks offering loaves cannot be the same species (i.e., actual tithe grain)
C. Second-tithe wheat is too valuable to grind into flour for offerings
D. Only money, never produce, may be used for Temple obligations
Correct: B. Via Gezerah Shavah: peace offerings may come from second-tithe money but not from actual tithe produce (different species). The same rule extends to thanks offering loaves — second-tithe money may purchase them, but the actual tithe wheat (same species as second tithe) may not be used.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
If someone vows "I obligate myself to bring loaves of a thanks offering," does he also need to bring the animal? What is Rav Huna's ruling?
A. No — the vow only obligates him to bring the loaves; the animal is optional
B. Yes — since loaves cannot come without a thanks offering, the vow implicitly obligates both
C. He must bring only the animal; "loaves" is just a way of referring to the full offering
D. The vow is invalid because one cannot separate the loaves from the animal in a vow
Correct: B. Rav Huna rules: he must bring both. This person knows loaves don't come without a thanks offering — so the vow implicitly includes the animal. He said "loaves" because he was citing the final step of the process, but his full intent was to bring the complete thanks offering.
