QUESTION 1 OF 5
Which grade of olive oil is required specifically for the Menorah (Candelabrum), and may it also be used for meal offerings?
A. Grade 1 (free-flowing first drops) — required for Menorah, also valid for meal offerings
B. Grade 1 — required for Menorah only; meal offerings must use Grade 2
C. Any grade is acceptable for the Menorah
D. Grade 3 — required for the Menorah because it burns longer
Correct: A. Grade 1 oil (the free-flowing first drops, with no pressing) is required for the Menorah. It is also valid for meal offerings — it's not excluded, just not obligatory. Grades 2 and 3 are valid for meal offerings but not for the Menorah.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the dispute between R' Chiyya and R' Shimon b"R (son of Rabbi) about immature olive oil?
A. R' Chiyya says it may be used for the Menorah; R' Shimon says it may not
B. R' Chiyya treats it as mere sap and tosses it away; R' Shimon treats it as actual oil and would dip food in it
C. They disagree on whether immature olives may be picked on Shabbat
D. R' Chiyya says it is valid for meal offerings; R' Shimon says it is not
Correct: B. R' Chiyya would discard oil from olives that hadn't reached ⅓ of their growth — he considers it mere plant sap. R' Shimon b"R treated it as real oil and would dip food in it. The mnemonic: "the wealthy are parsimonious" — R' Shimon was wealthy and kept it.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does the Torah specify "refined pounded oil" for the Menorah specifically (Vayikra 24:2), if pounded oil is also valid for meal offerings?
A. Because only the Menorah burns continuously and requires the purest oil
B. To conserve expense — the Menorah requires only the highest quality, but meal offerings may use cheaper grades
C. Because the verse excludes pounded oil from meal offerings
D. Because the Menorah was only lit by the High Priest
Correct: B. The Gemara explains: "due to the sparing (haḥisakhon)" — the Torah requires Grade 1 for the Menorah as a matter of financial conservation. Grade 1 is expensive; if it were also required for all meal offerings, the cost would be enormous. So meal offerings may use the cheaper Grade 2 or 3.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rav Yirmeya bar Abba, what is the "oil of myrrh" used on the women in the Book of Esther?
A. A rare myrrh-scented oil imported from Arabia
B. The fragrant cosmetic oil known as setakhta
C. Oil from olives that haven't yet reached ⅓ of their growth — an acidic oil that removes hair and beautifies skin
D. Beeswax oil mixed with fragrant spices
Correct: C. Rav Yirmeya bar Abba identifies "oil of myrrh" as anpikanon — oil from immature olives (not yet ⅓ grown). A Baraita confirms: Rabbi Yehuda says this oil is used because it removes hair and pampers the skin — it was an ancient depilatory and skin treatment.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Which types of oil are explicitly disqualified for use in meal offerings?
A. Only oil that has been heated above room temperature
B. Oil from olives soaked in water, from pickled olives, and from residue after full pressing
C. All oil except Grade 1 free-flowing oil
D. Only imported oils — only locally grown olive oil is valid
Correct: B. The Mishna disqualifies oil from olives soaked in water, from pickled olives, and oil that is residue/sap from later pressings. These sources produce degraded or impure oil that doesn't qualify for sacred use in Temple offerings.
