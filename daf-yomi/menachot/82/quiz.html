QUESTION 1 OF 5
What Gezerah Shavah (verbal analogy) teaches that peace offerings may be purchased with second-tithe money?
A. "Holy" and "holy" — comparing peace offerings to first fruits
B. "There" and "there" — linking the peace offering verse (Devarim 27:7) to the second-tithe verse (Devarim 14:23)
C. "Eat" and "eat" — comparing peace offerings to the Paschal offering
D. "Offering" and "offering" — a general link between all voluntary offerings and tithes
Correct: B. The word "there" (sham/שם) appears in both: the peace offering verse ("you shall eat there") and the second tithe verse ("eat before the Lord there"). This verbal analogy teaches that peace offerings may be purchased with second-tithe money.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why may thanks offering loaves NOT be made from actual second-tithe wheat (the grain itself)?
A. Tithe grain is too coarse to grind into the fine flour required for offerings
B. Since peace offerings are not the same species as second tithe, thanks offering loaves also cannot be the same species — and tithe wheat IS the same species
C. All tithe produce must be consumed by the owner in Jerusalem, not used for Temple offerings
D. There is no prohibition — tithe wheat may actually be used for loaves
Correct: B. The logic follows from the Gezerah Shavah: peace offerings are not the same species as second tithe (animals vs. grain). Therefore, thanks offering loaves cannot be of the same species as second tithe either. Tithe wheat = same species as second tithe → excluded. Wheat bought with tithe money = not tithe itself → permitted.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Rabbi Ami rules that designating second-tithe money for a peace offering does not transform it into peace-offering money. Why?
A. Because second-tithe money loses its sanctity as soon as it leaves Jerusalem
B. Because the sanctity of a peace offering is not strong enough to override the existing sanctity of second tithe
C. Because one may not mentally designate money — only physical objects can be consecrated
D. Because second-tithe money is considered non-sacred and has no sanctity to begin with
Correct: B. R' Ami holds that when two sanctities compete, the weaker one cannot override the stronger. Peace-offering sanctity is weaker than second-tithe sanctity, so the designation doesn't transform the money. The two sanctities coexist — the flesh gets peace-offering status, the hide retains tithe status.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
R' Eliezer uses the verse "you shall perform this service in this month" (Shemot 13:5) to prove what?
A. That the Paschal offering may only be brought in the month of Nisan
B. That all services of Pesach for future generations should follow the same model as Egypt — including that the Paschal offering comes from non-sacred money
C. That the Paschal offering and peace offering are halachically equivalent
D. That a leftover Paschal offering must be burned, not eaten
Correct: B. R' Eliezer reads "this service" as a link: all future Pesach services shall be like the Egyptian one. In Egypt, the Paschal offering was brought from non-sacred money (there were no tithes). Therefore, a leftover Paschal offering of later generations is also brought from non-sacred money.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the "possible from the impossible" principle, and why does the wilderness Paschal offering resolve the debate?
A. The principle says you can derive laws from hypothetical cases; the wilderness Pesach proves it because it never actually happened
B. The principle is about deriving a law for a case that is permitted from a model that was only permissible in a unique context; the wilderness Pesach shows that even an offering requiring altar blood could be from non-sacred money — breaking R' Akiva's distinction
C. The principle allows deriving impossible laws from possible ones; the wilderness Pesach is the impossible case
D. The principle is unique to Pesach law and doesn't apply elsewhere in halacha
Correct: B. R' Akiva argued that Egypt Pesach (no altar blood) cannot teach generations Pesach (requires altar blood) — structurally different. But the wilderness Pesach did require altar blood AND was brought from non-sacred money (no tithes existed in the desert). This refutes R' Akiva's distinction and supports R' Eliezer's position.
