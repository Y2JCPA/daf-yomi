📖 "Impure Animal" — A Hidden Reference

Lev 27:11: וְאִם כׇּל בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה אֲשֶׁר לֹא יַקְרִיבוּ מִמֶּנָּה קׇרְבָּן לַה׳
"And if it be any impure animal of which they may not bring an offering to God..."
Q
Does "impure animal" mean a literally non-kosher animal (like a pig), or something else?
A
It means a blemished sacrificial animal — which is called "impure" because it cannot be brought as an offering. The verse tells us how to redeem blemished animals that were consecrated!
Q
But the next verse (27:27) explicitly mentions "impure animals" for redemption — isn't that the same thing?
A
No! Verse 27 refers to actually non-kosher animals. Verse 11 must therefore refer to blemished kosher animals that are consecrated but cannot be offered.

⏳ Temporary vs. Permanent Blemish

"Of which they may not bring an offering to God" (Lev 27:11)
↓
Temporary Blemish 🔄
Cannot be offered today
But CAN be offered tomorrow
❌ EXCLUDED from redemption
"Cannot be offered AT ALL" — not just temporarily
Permanent Blemish 🔒
Cannot be offered ever
Permanently unfit for the altar
✅ INCLUDED — can be redeemed
Must add a fifth (chomesh) to the valuation
Why exclude temporary blemishes? If an animal has a blemish that will heal (e.g., a cut), it's not truly "unfit to be offered" — it's just temporarily unfit. The verse's phrase "of which they may not bring" implies permanent unfitness. A temporarily blemished consecrated animal must simply wait until it heals, then be offered.

🍽️ Piggul and Food Impurity — The Connection

Can an offering rendered Piggul (invalid via disqualifying intent) also become impure with food-impurity?

If Piggul during Shechitah

  • ❌ The offering's blood never stood to be sprinkled
  • ❌ The meat was never "permitted" for consumption
  • 📖 R' Shimon: NOT susceptible to food-impurity
  • 💡 No "time of fitness" ever existed

If Piggul during Sprinkling

  • ✅ The blood WAS sprinkled (then Piggul was declared)
  • ✅ There WAS a moment the meat stood to be permitted
  • 📖 The offering WAS susceptible to impurity at that point
  • 💡 "If he had wanted, he WOULD HAVE sprinkled" = it stood to be valid

🥩 Blood of a Neveilah — Impure?

A neveilah (animal carcass) imparts impurity through its flesh. But what about its blood?

Beit Shammai — LENIENT

  • 🩸 Blood of a neveilah is pure
  • 📖 "The verse says 'its carcass' — not its blood"
  • 💡 Only the flesh of the animal imparts impurity
  • ✅ Blood is excluded from the impurity of neveilah

Beit Hillel — STRICT

  • 🩸 Blood of a neveilah is impure
  • 📖 Blood is part of the animal's essence
  • 💡 When the animal falls — all of it, including blood, is impure
  • ⚠️ R' Yosei adds: only if the blood is a quarter-log (enough to congeal to an olive-bulk)
Part of the "Six Leniencies": This is one of six cases where Beit Shammai is lenient and Beit Hillel is strict — the opposite of the usual pattern! R' Yehuda said to R' Yosei: "May your mind be at ease — you've put MY mind at ease!" (He was embarrassed to have a Babylonian lineage discussed critically.)

💡 Rav Ashi on Piggul — Redemption vs. Sprinkling

Rav Ashi's key distinction: R' Shimon says "if he had wanted, he would have redeemed" = items standing to be redeemed are treated as redeemed. But R' Shimon does NOT say "if he had wanted, he would have sprinkled" — items standing to be sprinkled are NOT treated as already sprinkled.
Redemption: Simple verbal act
Anyone can do it at any time
→ "Standing to be redeemed" = treated as redeemed ✅
↓↑
Sprinkling: Requires specific action by a Kohen
Requires fit blood, fit altar, correct intent
→ "Standing to be sprinkled" ≠ treated as sprinkled ❌
This asymmetry is the heart of the Piggul discussion. Redemption is so simple that "standing to happen" = "has happened." Sprinkling is complex enough that this logic doesn't apply.
