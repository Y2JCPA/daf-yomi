QUESTION 1 OF 5
What is the standard oil ratio for a Mincha offering?
A. Half a log per Issaron
B. 1 log per Issaron
C. 2 log per Issaron
D. 3 log for the entire offering regardless of size
Correct: B — 1 log per Issaron. The standard ratio is one log of oil per Issaron of flour. Notably, using less OR more oil does not disqualify the offering — the measures are ideal but not absolute minimums.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the key difference between a Machabat (griddle) and Marcheshet (deep-pan) Mincha?
A. Machabat is baked in an oven; Marcheshet is boiled
B. Machabat is flat with less oil pooling (drier); Marcheshet is deep with oil pooling (moist/soaked)
C. Machabat uses barley; Marcheshet uses wheat
D. Machabat is for Kohanim; Marcheshet is for Israelites
Correct: B. The Machabat is a flat griddle — the oil doesn't pool, producing a drier result. The Marcheshet is a deep pan — oil pools and soaks the dough, producing a moist result. The Talmud's mnemonic: if it "crackles" (rotsehevet, from the same root as Marcheshet), it's a Marcheshet.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Which Mincha types do NOT include frankincense?
A. The griddle and deep-pan types only
B. None — all Minchot include frankincense
C. The sinner's Mincha and the Sotah offering — because they represent sin, not joy
D. Oven-baked wafers only
Correct: C. The sinner's Mincha (Minchat Choteh) and the Sotah jealousy offering intentionally have no oil or frankincense. For the sinner: "it is a sin offering" — no pleasant aroma of festivity. For the Sotah: "it is a jealousy offering" — these are offerings of affliction, not celebration.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Where does leftover/surplus oil from a Mincha go?
A. Given to the Kohen's household
B. Burned in the Beit HaMokhed (Chamber of the Hearth) / on the altar fire
C. Poured out on the Temple floor
D. Used to light the Menorah
Correct: B. Surplus oil has sanctity and cannot be wasted. It is burned on the altar fire. This applies to oil that is leftover after the proper amounts were used — it goes to the altar rather than being discarded or given to non-Temple use.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why must baked Mincha loaves be broken into pieces before Kemitzah?
A. To make them fit in the service vessel
B. So the oil poured on top soaks through the pieces evenly before the Kometz is taken
C. To separate the burned portion from the eaten portion
D. It is only required for wafers, not loaves
Correct: B. The baked loaves must be broken (Pitat) before Kemitzah so that when oil is poured on them, it penetrates all the pieces evenly. This ensures the Kometz that the Kohen takes has the proper distribution of oil throughout — not just on the surface of a whole loaf.
