String Length: No Maximum, But a Minimum

The baraita says tzitzit have no measure. But this means:

No maximum — strings can be as long as you want.
But they DO have a minimum — enough to tie a small knot (about 4 thumb-widths of hanging string).

Compare to lulav: "3 handbreadths sufficient to wave" = minimum, not maximum.

What Does "Tzitzit" Mean?

"Strings" (tzitzit) means nothing other than hanging threads — from the word "and he took me by a lock (tzitzit) of my head" (Ezekiel 8:3).

The word implies something that hangs and dangles — hence the requirement for loose strings hanging from the garment.

Who May Write Torah Scrolls, Tefillin & Mezuzot?

A Torah scroll, tefillin, or mezuzot written by:
❌ A heretic (min)
❌ A Samaritan (Kuti)
❌ A gentile
❌ A Canaanite slave
❌ A woman
❌ A minor
❌ A Jewish apostate (meshummad)

All are invalid — they must be written by an obligated Jewish adult male.

Does a Jew Recite a Blessing When Writing?

Rav Chiyya b. Rav Huna: A Jew writing tefillin does not recite a blessing over the writing.

Why? Because the performance is not the completion of the mitzva. Writing is preparation; the mitzva is completed by wearing them. (Compare: circumcision — the act itself IS the mitzva, so one blesses during the act.)

The Angel Who Rebuked Rav Katina

Famous story: Rav Katina wore a cloak without tzitzit (because it had no corners). An angel appeared and said: "Katina, Katina! A linen cloak in summer and a wool cloak in winter — what will become of the mitzva of tzitzit?"

Rav Katina: "Do you punish for failure to perform a positive mitzva?"

Angel: "In a time of God's anger — yes!"

Lesson: Even when technically exempt, one should seek opportunities to fulfill mitzvot.

Key Takeaways — Menachot 41

1. Tzitzit strings: no maximum length, but minimum of 4 thumb-widths.

2. "Tzitzit" etymologically means hanging threads (from Ezekiel 8:3).

3. STaM must be written by an obligated Jewish adult male — everyone else is invalid.

4. No blessing on writing tefillin — the writing is preparation, not the mitzva itself.

5. Rav Katina's angel: seek out mitzva opportunities; in times of anger, even exempt people are held accountable.
