QUESTION 1 OF 5
Compared to Tenufah (waving), Semikha (placing hands) has a narrower scope — it only applies to living animals. Yet the Gemara rules that all co-owners must do Semikha, even though only one partner waves. Why can't all partners wave together?
A. Waving requires strength and only one person can provide that
B. Multiple partners waving creates a chatzitzah (interposition) between hands and offering, and sequential waving would mean multiple wavings instead of one
C. Only Kohanim are permitted to wave offerings
D. The Torah explicitly says only one waving per offering
Correct: B — Chatzitzah + multiple wavings. If all partners wave simultaneously, the outer hands create an interposition (chatzitzah) between the offering and the inner hands. And if they wave sequentially, each wave is separate — but the Torah requires one waving (singular), not multiple wavings. So Tenufah is inherently limited to one person.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why did the Kohen Gadol place his hands on the limbs of the Tamid (daily offering) as they were handed to him, even though Semikha doesn't apply to slaughtered animals?
A. The Tamid is an exception where Semikha applies after slaughter
B. This is a special Rabbinic enactment for the Tamid only
C. This was not actual Semikha — it was a ceremonial gesture to honor the Kohen Gadol's service
D. The Kohen Gadol owned the Tamid personally
Correct: C — Ceremonial honor, not halachic Semikha. Abaye explains: the Kohen Gadol placing his hands on the limbs was not Semikha in the halachic sense at all. It was done as a mark of dignity and prestige (kevod Kohen Gadol) — to make his offering of the Tamid more distinguished than when ordinary priests performed it.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to the Mishna, the Shewbread (Lechem HaPanim) is kneaded one by one but baked two by two. What is the purpose of the Defus (mold) used in its preparation?
A. To ensure the bread rises evenly in the oven
B. To measure out exactly 2/10 ephah of flour per loaf
C. To shape the dough before baking and to preserve the shape after baking so it doesn't get misshapen
D. To separate the loaves from each other in the oven
Correct: C — Shape formation and preservation. The Defus (mold) was used twice: (1) to give the dough its distinctive shape before going into the oven, and (2) after baking, to hold the loaves in shape while they cooled so they wouldn't warp or get misshapen during handling.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
R' Chanina says the Lechem HaPanim was shaped like an "open box." R' Yochanan says it was like a "rocking boat." Which opinion does R' Yehuda's statement support — that "the bread supports the panels and the panels support the bread"?
A. R' Chanina — the open box
B. R' Yochanan — the rocking boat
C. Neither — R' Yehuda holds a completely different shape
D. Both equally — the statement fits either shape
Correct: B — R' Yochanan's rocking boat. R' Yehuda says the loaves and panels leaned against each other for mutual support. This only works with the boat shape, where the narrow-based loaves needed lateral support from the panels. According to R' Chanina (open box), the panels were on the ground — they couldn't lean against the bread.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
The Two Loaves of Shavuot (Shtei HaLechem) are unique among Temple offerings. What distinguishes them from the Shewbread (Lechem HaPanim)?
A. The Two Loaves are offered on Pesach, not Shavuot
B. The Two Loaves are Chametz (leavened), while the Shewbread is Matzah
C. The Two Loaves require a mold; the Shewbread does not
D. The Two Loaves are baked two by two; the Shewbread one by one
Correct: B — The Two Loaves are Chametz! The Two Loaves (Shtei HaLechem) brought on Shavuot are one of the very few offerings in the Temple that are leavened (Chametz). The Shewbread (Lechem HaPanim) is Matzah (unleavened). Also note the baking: Two Loaves are baked one by one; Shewbread is baked two by two.
