🏛️ The Temple of Onias — Historical Background

One of the most dramatic chapters in Second Temple history — a rival Jewish Temple built in Egypt!
Onias III — legitimate Kohen Gadol candidate, son of Shimon HaTzaddik
The Mishna describes his father Shimon as one of the last surviving members of the Great Assembly
↓
Conflict: His brother was appointed Kohen Gadol instead
Onias fled to Egypt in humiliation and bitterness
Second Maccabees gives a different, complex account
↓
The Temple of Onias — built in Heliopolis, Egypt (~160 BCE)
Modeled on the Jerusalem Temple
Operated for ~230 years until Romans destroyed it (~73 CE)

📜 What Onias Said — Citing Isaiah

בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא יִהְיֶה מִזְבֵּחַ לַה׳ בְּתוֹךְ אֶרֶץ מִצְרָיִם
Isaiah 19:19: "On that day there will be an altar to the Lord in the midst of the land of Egypt"
ON
I have a prophecy supporting me! Isaiah said there would be an altar to God in Egypt. I am fulfilling this prophecy by building the Temple of Onias!
RB
R' Yehuda: Onias had good intentions — he thought he was being righteous. That's why the verse says "an altar... in the midst of Egypt" — honoring his motivation even if his action was wrong.
RY
R' Meir: Onias was a sectarian (Min) — he deliberately built a forbidden Temple outside Jerusalem in willful defiance of Torah law. His motives were not pure.

⚖️ Vows Made for "The Temple of Onias"

Someone vows: "I will bring this offering at the Temple of Onias"
What happens when they later come to Jerusalem?
↓
If vow said "Onias Temple"
❌ The offering is brought ANYWAY in Jerusalem
But it does NOT fulfill the original vow!
The vow remains unfulfilled.
If vow said "Heaven"
✅ Bringing in Jerusalem DOES fulfill it
"Heaven" clearly refers to the God of Israel.
Jerusalem is the right place.
The halachic principle: A vow explicitly naming the Temple of Onias creates an obligation that can only be fulfilled there — but since it's an illegal place of worship, the vow is essentially impossible to fulfill properly. The Talmud's message: never make vows tied to improper locations.

🌿 The Kohanim of Onias — What Happens to Them?

Cannot Serve in Jerusalem

  • 🚫 Kohanim who served at Onias Temple are disqualified from Jerusalem service
  • 📖 They are like Kohanim who served idols — permanently disqualified
  • ⚠️ But unlike actual idol-worshipping Kohanim, they don't receive the death penalty
  • 💡 Their disqualification is from the moment they served at Onias

Some Rights Retained

  • ✅ May eat Terumah (priestly tithes)
  • ✅ Retain their Kohen status for purposes of marriage laws
  • ✅ Their children born afterward retain full Kohen status
  • 📖 The disqualification is for Temple service only
  • 💡 R' Yehuda: they are like the Kohanim in Kings 23:9 — "did not ascend to the altar, but ate unleavened bread"

🗺️ Isaiah's Prophecy — Fulfilled or Cautionary?

Q
Isaiah 19:19 says "an altar to the Lord in Egypt." Was this about Onias? Was Onias's Temple a fulfillment of prophecy?
A1
R' Yehuda's reading: Isaiah foresaw that a sincere but misguided person would build this altar. The prophecy was descriptive — "it WILL happen" — not prescriptive (it should happen). Onias's sincerity is acknowledged even if his action was wrong.
A2
The messianic reading (others): The prophecy refers to the end of days — when there will be true worship throughout the world, including Egypt. Onias's Temple was a premature, improper attempt to fulfill a future reality.
Historical note: The Temple of Onias operated from ~160 BCE to 73 CE — nearly 230 years! It was destroyed by the Romans just after they destroyed Jerusalem. It outlasted the Second Temple by almost nothing.
