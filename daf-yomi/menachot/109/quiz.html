5 questions · Self-graded
QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why did Onias build his rival Temple in Egypt?
A. He was sent by the Sanhedrin to establish a Jewish community there
B. He fled to Egypt after his brother was appointed Kohen Gadol instead of him, and built a Temple in bitterness/grief
C. He was a Hellenist who rejected Jerusalem's authority
D. He was expelled from Israel for violating Temple law
Correct: B. According to the Talmud, Onias was the son of Shimon HaTzaddik and expected to become Kohen Gadol. When his brother received the position instead, Onias fled in humiliation to Egypt and built a rival Temple there. R' Yehuda views his motivations as mixed (grief + still religious), while R' Meir views him as a deliberate sectarian.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What verse from Isaiah did Onias cite to justify his Egyptian Temple?
A. "For my house shall be called a house of prayer for all peoples" (Isa 56:7)
B. "On that day there will be an altar to the Lord in the midst of the land of Egypt" (Isa 19:19)
C. "From the rising of the sun to its setting, my name is great among the nations" (Mal 1:11)
D. "A city of righteousness, a faithful city" (Isa 1:26)
Correct: B — Isaiah 19:19. Onias cited the prophecy "on that day there will be an altar to the Lord in the midst of Egypt." He claimed his Temple was the fulfillment of this prophecy. The Talmud debates whether this prophecy refers to him, to the messianic era, or is simply descriptive of what would happen.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
If someone made a vow "to bring an offering at the Temple of Onias," can they fulfill this vow by bringing the offering in Jerusalem instead?
A. Yes — Jerusalem is the true Temple, so it always counts
B. No — the vow explicitly named Onias's Temple; bringing to Jerusalem does not fulfill that specific vow
C. Yes, but only if they intended the true God
D. The vow is void since the Temple of Onias was forbidden
Correct: B. A vow naming "the Temple of Onias" creates a specific obligation that cannot be transferred to Jerusalem. The vow is essentially un-fulfillable (since the Onias Temple was forbidden) — it remains outstanding. This was the halachic trap of making vows tied to improper places.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What happened to Kohanim who served in the Temple of Onias? Could they serve in Jerusalem afterward?
A. Yes — they were fully reinstated after teshuvah
B. No — they were permanently disqualified from Temple service in Jerusalem, similar to Kohanim who served idols
C. They were executed for their service at an unauthorized altar
D. They could serve but had to bring extra atonement offerings first
Correct: B. Kohanim who served at the Temple of Onias were permanently disqualified from serving in Jerusalem's Temple — similar in status to Kohanim who served idols. However, unlike those who worshipped actual idols, they were not subject to the death penalty. They could still eat Terumah and retained Kohen status for other purposes.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
The Temple of Onias operated for approximately how long before being destroyed by Rome?
A. About 10 years
B. About 50 years
C. About 230 years (~160 BCE to ~73 CE)
D. About 400 years
Correct: C — About 230 years. The Temple of Onias was built around 160 BCE and destroyed by the Romans around 73 CE — just three years after the destruction of the Jerusalem Temple. It outlasted the Second Temple by only a few years, and both were destroyed by Rome in the same campaign.
