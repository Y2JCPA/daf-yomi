📖 "Yado" (His Hand) — Three Exclusions

"His hand" appears three times in Vayikra Chapter 3. Each exclusion builds on the previous:
"Yado" #1 (3:2)
→ Not the hand of his slave
A slave isn't commanded in mitzvot → clearly excluded
↓
"Yado" #2 (3:8)
→ Not the hand of his agent
An agent IS commanded + "shlucho k'moso" — so we need a verse!
↓
"Yado" #3 (3:13)
→ Not the hand of his wife
A wife is "like his own flesh" — strongest case, still excluded!
Why all three? Each verse excludes a progressively "closer" relationship. If we only had one verse, we'd think the exclusion applies only to the weakest case (slave). The Torah needed to build up to the wife — who is "his own flesh" — to make it clear: Semikha is strictly personal.

👨‍🦯 The Blind Person — How Do We Derive It?

Approach 1: From the Elders

  • 📖 The elders of the Sanhedrin do Semikha on the communal bull
  • 🔗 Derive individual Semikha from communal Semikha
  • 👨‍🦳 Elders must be fit judges → blind people can't serve as judges
  • 💡 Therefore: blind people can't do Semikha
  • ⚠️ This works because communal Semikha explicitly writes "placing hands"

Approach 2: From the Olat Re'iyah

  • 📖 The burnt offering of "appearance" (re'iyah) requires Semikha
  • 🔗 Derive: whoever is obligated in re'iyah can do Semikha
  • 👨‍🦯 Blind people are exempt from re'iyah (the mitzva of "appearing")
  • 💡 Therefore: blind people can't do Semikha
  • ⚠️ This derives individual from individual (preferred by some)
The methodological debate: Do we derive individual cases from individual cases, or from communal cases? Each approach has a different logic for why the source is authoritative.

⚖️ Is Semikha Essential or Non-Essential?

Q
The verse says: "He shall place his hand... and it shall be accepted to effect atonement." That sounds like Semikha IS needed for atonement! 🤔
A
But atonement comes through the BLOOD, not through hands! "For it is the blood that effects atonement" (Vayikra 17:11). 🩸
Q
So what does the verse about Semikha mean?
A
If someone treats Semikha as unimportant and skips it, the verse ascribes blame as if he didn't atone. But in reality — the korban still works. ✅

Semikha

  • 🤲 Non-essential (Eino Me'akev)
  • 📖 "He shall place his hand... and it shall be accepted"
  • ⚠️ Blamed if skipped, but atonement still works

Tenufah (Waving)

  • 🫸 Also non-essential (Eino Me'akev)
  • 📖 "To be waved to effect atonement" (Vayikra 14:21)
  • ⚠️ Same rule: blamed if skipped, but works regardless

👥 Partners — Everyone Places Hands

If multiple people co-own an offering, do all of them do Semikha?
The attempted Kal va'Chomer:
Tenufah applies to slaughtered animals (broader scope)
yet only ONE partner waves.
Semikha only applies to live animals (narrower scope) —
so surely only ONE partner should need to do Semikha?
↓
Rejected! "Korbano" teaches: ALL co-owners must do Semikha.
↓
Why not the reverse for Tenufah?
If all partners must do Semikha → shouldn't all wave?
↓
Impossible! Multiple people waving creates a chatzitzah (interposition).
And sequential waving = multiple wavings, but Torah says ONE waving.
So Tenufah is inherently limited to one person.
