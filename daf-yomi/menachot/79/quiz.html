QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to R' Yehuda's clarification, what scenario do R' Eliezer and R' Yehoshua actually dispute?
A. Whether a blemished animal's loaves were consecrated
B. Whether a tereifa animal's loaves were consecrated
C. Whether loaves are consecrated when the animal was slaughtered with intent to eat it outside its designated area
D. Whether loaves are consecrated when all 40 aren't yet ready
Correct: C. R' Yehuda clarifies: both R' Eliezer and R' Yehoshua agree that a blemished or tereifa animal does not consecrate loaves. Their actual dispute is about an animal slaughtered with intent to eat it outside its area (chutz limkomo) — R' Eliezer says the loaves are consecrated; R' Yehoshua says they are not.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does R' Eliezer say that slaughtering with intent "outside its area" consecrates the loaves?
A. Because any slaughter — even with wrong intent — consecrates whatever is nearby
B. Because "outside its area" is a disqualifying intent like "beyond its time," and intent beyond its time consecrates the loaves — so both should
C. Because the loaves are physically present and untouched by the wrong intent
D. Because a Kohen's mistake in thought has no halachic effect on the offering
Correct: B. R' Eliezer reasons by analogy: intent to eat "beyond its time" (piggul intent) is also a disqualifying thought — yet it consecrates the loaves. Since both "beyond time" and "outside area" are forbidden intentions that disqualify the offering, both should consecrate the loaves.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is R' Shimon's principle regarding blood that was collected in a cup but then spilled before sprinkling?
A. The offering is entirely void — spilled blood cannot be replaced
B. The blood must be collected again for the offering to be valid
C. Blood that stands ready to be sprinkled is considered as if it were already sprinkled — so the loaves are consecrated
D. Spilled blood disqualifies the offering but the loaves may still be eaten
Correct: C. R' Shimon teaches: "any blood that stands fit to be sprinkled is considered as if it were already sprinkled." Once the blood was collected in the cup and was halachically ready for sprinkling, it is treated as having been sprinkled — even if it spilled before actually reaching the altar. Therefore the loaves are consecrated.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why can libations separated for one offering be transferred to another, while oil separated for one meal offering cannot?
A. Oil is more expensive and the Torah protects its sanctity more strictly
B. Oil is an integral part of the meal offering itself; libations are separate accompaniments — so the court's tacit stipulation applies to libations but not oil
C. The Torah explicitly permits transferring libations but not oil
D. Libations belong to the Kohanim; oil belongs to the altar — different ownership rules apply
Correct: B. Rabbi Yannai explains: the court makes a tacit stipulation for libations — they go to whichever offering needs them. This works because libations merely accompany offerings and aren't integral to them. But oil is an inherent component of the meal offering — it cannot be separated from its intended offering and redirected to another.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why can't libations that were separated for a disqualified offering simply revert to non-sacred status?
A. Because the Kohen who separated them already received his portion
B. A rabbinic decree prohibits consecrated items from becoming non-sacred — they must be used for holy purposes
C. The Torah says libations may only go to the altar, never to any other use
D. Because the High Priest would need to perform a special ceremony to deconsecrate them
Correct: B. A rabbinic decree prevents dedicated items from simply reverting to non-sacred status. Even through a tacit stipulation, the court can only redirect libations to another sacred use — not free them from sanctity altogether. Consecrated items flow in one direction: toward holy purposes.
