🌾 Rubbing 300×, Striking 500×

The Mishna: All meal offerings require rubbing 300 times and striking 500 times to remove husks from the wheat kernels before grinding.
How to count: rub 1× → strike 2×. Rub 2× → strike 3×. Repeat 100 times = 300 rubs + 500 strikes.
↓
❓ R' Yirmeya's dilemma: Does moving the hand back and forth count as one rub or two?
↓
🤷 Teiku — unresolved

Chachamim

  • Rubbing and striking performed on wheat kernels
  • Done before grinding to flour
  • Removes husks from the grain

R' Yosei

  • Rubbing and striking performed on the dough
  • After kneading, to smooth the product
  • Disputed: does he replace or add to the wheat step?

🔢 How Many Loaves? The Dispute

R' Yehuda — Default: 10

  • Most offerings: 10 loaves/wafers
  • Exceptions = 12: Shewbread, High Priest's griddle cake
  • These 12-loaf offerings have explicit verses

R' Meir — Default: 12

  • Most offerings: 12 loaves
  • Exceptions = 10: Thanks offering loaves (4 kinds × 10) and Nazirite offerings
  • The "tens" are explicitly written
The Gemara's question: Where do we derive 12 for the High Priest's griddle-cake? → Verbal analogy (Gezerah Shavah) from the word "obligation" linking it to the shewbread.

📐 Shewbread = 12 — How Derived?

📖 Shewbread: "You shall take fine flour and bake 12 loaves" (Vayikra 24:5) — explicitly 12
↓
High Priest's griddle-cake: no explicit number
↓
Word "obligation" (חוק) appears both in griddle-cake passage and shewbread passage → Gezerah Shavah
↓
Just as shewbread = 12, so too High Priest's griddle-cake = 12

🍞 Rav Huna: One Loaf Suffices

Rav Huna rules: An oven-baked meal offering that was baked as only one loaf (instead of 10) — the person has fulfilled their obligation.
Verse: "It shall be unleavened loaves of fine flour" — the word מַצּוֹת (matzot) is written without a vav
↓
Can be read as singular מַצַּת (matzat) — even one loaf satisfies the requirement
↓
❓ Rav Pappa challenges: The thanks offering verse has matzot with a full vav — yet Shmuel says 4 loaves (one per variety) is enough. Doesn't that contradict Rav Huna?
↓
Answer: Shmuel's ruling disagrees with Rav Huna — they are two competing opinions
