🌾 Reaping the Omer on Shabbat

When the 16th of Nisan falls on Shabbat, the Omer must still be reaped — but how much?

Tanna Kamma

  • 🌾 Reap 5 Se'ah of barley on Shabbat
  • 📏 Even though only 1 Se'ah is needed for the Omer
  • 💡 More barley = more to choose from = better quality offering
  • ⚡ The extra reaping is permitted for the mitzva

R' Yishmael b"R Yochanan b"B

  • 🌾 Reap only 3 Se'ah on Shabbat
  • 📏 Minimize the Shabbat desecration
  • 💡 3 Se'ah is enough to produce the required amount
  • ⚡ Don't do MORE melacha than absolutely necessary
The principle at stake: When Shabbat is overridden for a mitzva, should we minimize the override to the bare minimum? Or is the entire mitzva (including enhancements) permitted?
3 / 9

🔗 Parallel: R' Chanina the Deputy Kohen Gadol

The same principle — minimize Shabbat work when possible
Mishna: R' Chanina says: On Shabbat, the barley was reaped by one person with one sickle into one basket
↓
On a weekday: three people with three sickles into three baskets
↓
Rabba connects: R' Yishmael and R' Chanina "said the same thing" — both hold that when you CAN accomplish the mitzva with less work, don't do extra on Shabbat
↓
Gemara pushes back: Maybe not! R' Chanina limits the NUMBER of people (doesn't change the result), but R' Yishmael limits the QUANTITY (changes the quality). These might be different principles!

🐑🐑 Oops — Two Sin Offerings on Shabbat!

Someone mistakenly slaughtered TWO communal sin offerings on Shabbat when only one was needed
🐑🐑 Two animals designated as communal sin offerings for a Festival that falls on Shabbat
↓
🔪 First animal slaughtered — this is the valid korban
↓
🔪 Second animal slaughtered by mistake — unnecessary Shabbat desecration!
↓
If first was choice (Shamein)
Second: 🔥 Burn it (Ibur Tzurah)
Can't be offered — it was unnecessary melacha
If first was gaunt (Kazush)
Second might be offered!
Since the first was deficient, the second serves a purpose
Rabba's principle: The person is exempt from a sin offering for the unnecessary slaughter, because at the time of the act, it was unclear whether it would be needed.

🎯 Rabba vs. Rava — The Fishing Net

Classic Shabbat sugya: A child drowning at sea — you spread a net to save him, and catch fish too

Rabba — Intent Matters

  • 🎣 He heard a child was drowning → his intent was to save
  • ✅ Exempt even though he also caught fish
  • 💡 Since his primary intent was the permitted act (Pikuach Nefesh), the byproduct doesn't make him liable
  • 🔑 "Mitasek" — occupied with a permitted activity

Rava — Result Matters

  • 🎣 Doesn't matter what he intended
  • ❌ Liable if we judge by the actual result (he caught fish on Shabbat)
  • 💡 The melacha was accomplished regardless of intent
  • 🔑 Once a prohibited act is done, intent doesn't exempt
Application to the sin offerings: The person who slaughtered the second animal — was his intent to serve Hashem (like the fisherman saving a child), or did the unnecessary result make him liable?

🍂 The Two Figs — A Shabbat Brain Teaser

Rava's famous dilemma about a dangerously ill person on Shabbat
😷 A dangerously ill person needs 2 figs to recover
↓
Option A: One stem holds 2 figs
Option B: Another stem holds 3 figs
Which stem should you cut?
↓
Cut the 2-fig stem
Exact amount needed
✅ No extra Shabbat desecration
But might be less food volume
Cut the 3-fig stem
One extra fig — unnecessary!
⚠️ More food detached than needed
But only ONE cut instead of possibly needing another
The deeper question: In Pikuach Nefesh, do we calculate the minimum melacha by the action (number of cuts) or by the result (amount of food detached)? This connects directly to the Omer debate!

🏘️ Where Does the Omer Barley Come From?

A new Mishna: the Omer should ideally come from fields near Jerusalem
Ideal: Fields proximate to Jerusalem
"Bring from nearby" — freshest, most accessible
↓
If not ripe nearby: Bring from anywhere in Eretz Yisrael
↓
Historical incident: The Omer once came from Gaggot Tzerifin (distant location)
And the Shtei HaLechem from the Valley of Ein Socher
Why nearby? Two reasons given:
1. The verse says "first fruits" — the first to ripen, which is naturally closest
2. אֵין מַעֲבִירִין עַל הַמִּצְווֹת — "Don't pass over a mitzva" — use what's closest and available first
