QUESTION 1 OF 5
R' Yishmael b"R Yochanan ben Beroka says the Omer should be reaped with how many Se'ah of barley on Shabbat?
A. 1 Se'ah — the absolute minimum
B. 3 Se'ah — enough but minimizing melacha
C. 5 Se'ah — same as a weekday
D. 10 Se'ah — extra for quality
Correct: B — 3 Se'ah. R' Yishmael holds you should minimize Shabbat melacha. The Tanna Kamma says 5 Se'ah (same as weekday) to maximize quality, but R' Yishmael says 3 is sufficient and avoids unnecessary Shabbat desecration.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In Rabba's famous case: a man spreads a net to save a drowning child and catches fish. According to Rabba, is he liable for Shabbat desecration?
A. Yes — the fish were caught regardless of intent
B. No — his intent was to save the child, so he's exempt
C. Only if he keeps the fish
D. It depends on whether the child was actually saved
Correct: B — Exempt. Rabba holds that intent matters. Since the man heard about the drowning child and acted to save him (Pikuach Nefesh), the incidental fish-catching doesn't create liability. Rava disagrees and holds the result matters.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
A sick person needs 2 figs on Shabbat. One stem has 2 figs, another has 3. What is the dilemma?
A. Which figs are riper
B. Whether to minimize by action (number of cuts) or by result (amount detached)
C. Whether figs are permissible for the sick person
D. Whether one stem is closer to reach
Correct: B. Both stems require one cut, but the 2-fig stem produces exactly what's needed (minimizing result), while the 3-fig stem detaches an extra unnecessary fig. The question is how we measure "minimum" Shabbat desecration — by the action or by the outcome.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
If someone mistakenly slaughtered a SECOND communal sin offering on Shabbat (only one was needed), what happens?
A. Both are offered on the altar
B. The person must bring a personal sin offering for the Shabbat violation
C. The second is burned (Ibur Tzurah); the person is exempt from a sin offering
D. Both must be burned since the confusion invalidated everything
Correct: C. According to Rabba, the person is exempt from a personal sin offering because at the time of slaughter, there was uncertainty about whether the second would be needed (e.g., if the first turned out to be gaunt). The unnecessary second animal is burned.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
The Mishna says the Omer should ideally come from fields near Jerusalem. Why?
A. Only Jerusalem barley is kosher for the Omer
B. "Don't pass over a mitzva" — use what's closest and first available
C. The Sanhedrin must supervise the reaping
D. Fields outside Jerusalem are impure
Correct: B. The principle of "Ein Ma'avirin al HaMitzvot" — don't pass over a mitzva that's readily available. If ripe barley is near Jerusalem, use it first. The Gemara also connects this to the concept of "first fruits" — use what ripens first, which is naturally the closest.
