🦅 The Raven-Eliminator (Tzinorah)

A fascinating detail about Temple maintenance — protecting the sacred precinct from birds!
Q
What is the "Tzinorah" (raven-eliminator) mentioned in the Mishna?
A
It was a device on the roof of the Temple — a series of iron spikes — to prevent ravens and other birds from landing and leaving droppings on the sacred building. Birds defiling the Temple was a serious concern.
Q
What does this have to do with donations and Minchot?
A
The Mishna mentions that someone who vows a "Tzinorah" (raven-eliminator) is obligated to provide the gold/material for it. This connects to the general discussion of voluntary Temple donations and their minimum amounts.

📜 "I Vow the Weight of Myself"

A Mishna elsewhere (Arakhin) but referenced here: "I vow my weight (Mishkal) to the Temple" — how is this evaluated?

In Gold

  • ⚖️ If they specify "my weight in gold" → their body weight in gold
  • 💡 An extraordinary, life-changing donation
  • 📖 Queen Helen of Adiabene donated a golden menorah weighing herself
  • ⚠️ The Temple never refused such donations

In Silver

  • ⚖️ "My weight in silver" → body weight in silver
  • 💡 Still enormous but more attainable
  • 📖 Biblical precedent: weighing silver for land purchases
  • ✅ The vow is binding — they must bring whatever they specified

🧮 Donation Amounts — The Logic Behind Minimums

Why set minimum donation amounts at all?
Shouldn't any amount be valid?
↓
The Problem:
If there were no minimums, someone could vow "one grain of flour" or "one drop of oil" — creating a real obligation for something essentially worthless and causing embarrassment for the Temple.
↓
The Solution — Meaningful Measures:
Minimums ensure vows are dignified and meaningful. Each minimum represents the smallest "useful" quantity: 1 Issaron is the smallest Mincha; 1 log is the smallest oil unit; 2 logs is the smallest wood bundle.
