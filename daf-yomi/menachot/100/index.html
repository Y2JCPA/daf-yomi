💰 Can Consecrated Items Be Redeemed?

When a consecrated offering becomes impure or otherwise unusable, can it be "redeemed" (sold and money used for a replacement)?
General rule: a blemished animal consecrated for the altar CAN be redeemed
↓
Redeemable ✅
Animals with blemishes
"Impure" = unfit for altar = blemished animal
Lev 27:11-13
NOT Redeemable ❌
🐦 Birds
🪵 Wood for the altar
🏺 Frankincense
🫙 Service vessels
Once ritually impure — no redemption
Why no redemption for these four? The verse "if it is an impure animal" (Lev 27:11) applies only where something has "inherent sanctity" like an animal. Wood and frankincense have "sanctity that rests upon them" (kedushot damim), not inherent sanctity — yet they still can't be redeemed because they are "not available" (not in common circulation) for easy replacement.

🐦 Why Can't Birds Be Redeemed?

Q
The verse about redeeming impure animals seems general — why exclude birds specifically?
A
Birds have inherent sanctity (kedushah she-b'gufo) — they're brought directly as offerings, not sold for their monetary value. The Torah only allows redemption of animals with inherent sanctity when they develop blemishes. Birds can't be blemished — so no redemption path exists.
The logic chain:
• Animals with inherent sanctity → CAN be redeemed if blemished
• Birds with inherent sanctity → CANNOT be redeemed (no blemish mechanism)
• Wood/frankincense (monetary sanctity) → CANNOT be redeemed (not available/common)
• Service vessels → CANNOT be redeemed (can be purified in a mikveh instead)

🍞 Piggul, Notar, Tumah and the Shewbread

Three ways to be liable for eating holy food improperly — but only when the food is permitted for eating first.
ViolationMeaningRequires
🚫 PiggulDisqualifying intent during the offering's serviceThe offering was valid BEFORE intent disqualified it
⏰ NotarEating after the time limitThe food was permitted for consumption at some point
☣️ TumahEating in a state of impurityThe food was valid and permitted
When do these NOT apply to Shewbread?
Case 1: Bread set on Shabbat, bowls set after Shabbat — bowls were never on the Table for a full week. When burned, it's not a valid act. Therefore bread was NEVER permitted → no Piggul/Notar/Tumah liability.

Case 2: Bread and bowls set on Shabbat, bowls burned AFTER the following Shabbat — same result. The burning must happen on Shabbat to be valid.

🔄 Shmuel's Disputed Rule on Redemption

Shmuel's Position

  • 📖 Pure meal offerings and libations CAN be redeemed
  • 💡 They haven't been consecrated in a service vessel yet
  • ✅ Once redeemed, the money goes to buy new offerings
  • ⚠️ Only before vessel consecration; after → no redemption

Counter-argument

  • 📖 The Mishna says birds, wood, frankincense, vessels → no redemption when impure
  • 💡 If pure items could be redeemed, why state impure ones cannot?
  • 📖 Wood and frankincense don't have inherent sanctity → yet cannot be redeemed
  • ⚠️ Suggests even pure monetary-sanctity items cannot be redeemed

🪵 Wood, Frankincense, Vessels — A Unique Category

These three items occupy a fascinating gray zone in halacha:
🪵 Wood: Not susceptible to Tumah as food
...UNLESS trimmed into logs. Then "regard for sanctity" makes it like food.
↓
🏺 Frankincense: Not susceptible to Tumah
...UNLESS consecrated in a service vessel. Then it becomes susceptible.
↓
🫙 Service vessels: Can be purified in a mikveh
Their impurity is revocable — so they're treated "as pure" for redemption purposes.
↓
Conclusion: All three are treated as if pure — even when impure — precisely because their impurity is partial or revocable. Pure sanctified items are generally not redeemed.
