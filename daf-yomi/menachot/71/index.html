🌱 How Do We Know "Taking Root" Is Enough?

Four Amoraim attempt to prove that the Omer permits grain from the moment of taking root
ScholarVerse / ProofObjection
R' Yoshiya"Grain in the ear" → implies grain NOT yet "in the ear" also exists (and is permitted)Maybe it means grain at 1/3 growth, not merely "taking root"
Shmuel"From the time the sickle is first put" → implies grain too early for a sickle existsMaybe it means fodder (with stalks but unripe)
R' Yitzchak"The standing grain" → implies grain too soft to stand existsMaybe it means soft marsh-grain
Rava ✅"Which you sow in the field" → from the time of sowing = taking rootRav Pappa: "sowing" ≠ root? Rava: "in the field" = became part of the field = took root
Winner: Rava — "Which you sow in the field" (Shemos 23:16) teaches that the Omer permits grain from when it takes root and becomes part of the field. All other proofs were challenged.

🏙️ The Six Actions of Jericho's Residents

The Gemara mentions the residents of Jericho and their controversial practices
#ActionSages' Response
1Grafted palm trees all day on Erev Pesach🟡 Did not protest
2Wrapped the Shema (said it without pauses)🟡 Did not protest
3Reaped and piled grain before the Omer🟡 Did not protest
4Permitted consecrated carob/sycamore branches🔴 Protested
5Broke garden walls to feed poor on Shabbat/Yom Tov🔴 Protested

Counting problem: The Baraita says "six" actions but lists seven (reaping + piling = two). Resolution: remove the reaping action from R' Yehuda's version — meaning R' Yehuda never objected to reaping before the Omer, consistent with the Mishna.

🐄 Reaping for Fodder — Is It "Real" Reaping?

Mishna (Pe'ah 2:1): Certain things divide a field for purposes of Pe'ah (the corner left for the poor). Among them: one who reaps for fodder.

R' Meir

  • Reaping for fodder divides a field
  • Based on R' Shimon: reaping for fodder is "not considered reaping" for Omer purposes
  • Since it's not "real reaping," it creates a separation in the field

Chachamim

  • Reaping for fodder does NOT divide unless you also plow the reaped area
  • Reaping alone isn't enough — you need plowing to show the sections are truly separate

⚖️ How Grown Can Fodder Be?

❓ Before the Omer, may you reap new grain to feed animals?
↓
Chachamim
Only if grain has NOT reached 1/3 growth
Once at 1/3, it's considered "real grain" and can't be reaped for fodder before the Omer
R' Shimon
Even grain that reached 1/3 growth can be reaped for fodder
"Any reaping for fodder is not considered reaping"
Key principle: The Torah says קְצִירְכֶם — "your harvest." The Omer must precede your personal harvest, but reaping for animal fodder or for a mitzvah is not considered "your harvest."

🦗 The Grasshopper/Ant Case

A section of a field was consumed by grasshoppers, or ants nibbled at it, or wind broke it down
↓
Does this damage divide the field for Pe'ah?
↓
All agree (including R' Meir):
If subsequently plowed → divides the field ✅
If not plowed → does NOT divide ❌
Why does R' Meir concede here? Natural destruction is different from intentional reaping. When someone deliberately reaps for fodder, the act itself creates a boundary. Natural damage (grasshoppers, wind) doesn't create a deliberate boundary — only plowing afterward does.

Important restriction: Even when reaping is permitted before the Omer, you may NOT fashion the grain into sheaves — leave it loose. We minimize involvement with the grain as much as possible.
