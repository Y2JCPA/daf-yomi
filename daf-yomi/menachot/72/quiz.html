QUESTION 1 OF 5
How many separate halachot does the Gemara derive from the repeated phrase "you shall bring" regarding the Omer?
A. Three
B. Five
C. Seven
D. Ten
Correct: C — Seven. From sheaves, from dry grain, reaped by day, overrides Shabbat, from anywhere in Israel, even in impurity, and brought in any manner.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rabbi (Yehuda HaNasi), does the reaping of the Omer override Shabbat?
A. Yes — the Omer overrides Shabbat like all other Temple offerings
B. No — it can be reaped on Friday instead, following R' Akiva's principle
C. Only in years when 16 Nisan falls on Shabbat
D. Only if the court explicitly authorizes it
Correct: B. Rabbi holds that based on R' Akiva's principle — any labor that can be done before Shabbat does not override Shabbat — the Omer can be reaped on Friday. Multiple mishnayot that mention Shabbat reaping are "not according to Rabbi."
QUESTION 3 OF 5
R' Elazar b"R Shimon holds that if the Omer grain was reaped during the day instead of at night, it is invalid. What is his reasoning?
A. A verse explicitly states "at night only"
B. Since reaping overrides Shabbat, it must be because the timing is essential — proving daytime reaping is invalid
C. The Kohanim were only permitted to reap during nighttime shifts
D. R' Shimon his father had a tradition from Sinai that it must be at night
Correct: B. The logic: if nighttime reaping were merely preferred (not essential), the reaping could be done before Shabbat, and there'd be no need to override Shabbat. The fact that it overrides Shabbat proves the nighttime timing is essential — and therefore daytime reaping invalidates.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the ideal (l'chatchila) source for the Omer offering?
A. Standing, moist grain reaped at night
B. Dry grain from sheaves reaped during the day
C. Any grain from anywhere, at any time
D. Only wheat from fields near Jerusalem
Correct: A. The ideal is standing grain (still attached to the ground), moist/fresh, and reaped at night. All other options — from sheaves, dry grain, reaped by day — are valid b'dieved (after the fact) but not ideal.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why is the case of burning fats/limbs on Shabbat different from reaping the Omer on Shabbat?
A. Burning fats is a rabbinic obligation; reaping the Omer is Torah law
B. Burning fats follows the slaughter which already overrode Shabbat — a natural continuation; reaping the Omer has no prior Shabbat labor
C. Burning fats happens inside the Temple; reaping the Omer is outside
D. There is no difference — both override Shabbat for the same reason
Correct: B. The slaughter of the offering already overrode Shabbat, making it natural to complete the process by burning its fats that same night. But reaping the Omer has no prior Shabbat labor — so if it overrides Shabbat, it must be because nighttime reaping is essential, not just a continuation of earlier work.
