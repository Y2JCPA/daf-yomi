🍷 Can You Voluntarily Vow Libations?

"I vow to bring wine as a libation on the altar."
Is this a valid vow?
↓
✅ YES — Wine libations CAN be brought as a voluntary offering!
Mishna: "Nedavah" (voluntary) libations exist independently.
↓
But how much? The minimum is 3 log of wine.
This is the smallest libation quantity in the Torah — accompanying a lamb offering.
↓
Valid amounts: 3, 4, 5, 6, or 9 log
Not 7, 8, 10, etc. — only amounts that correspond to actual offering requirements.

🫒 Oil as a Standalone Offering

Can Vow Oil Independently

  • 🫒 "I vow to bring oil on the altar" — valid!
  • 📏 Minimum: 1 log of oil
  • 📖 It is poured on the fire of the altar
  • 💡 Oil has sanctity as a Temple offering
  • ⚠️ No Kemitzah — oil is poured entirely

How It Differs from Mincha Oil

  • 🍞 Mincha oil is mixed INTO flour → they're one offering
  • 🫒 Standalone oil is its OWN offering → no flour at all
  • 📏 Mincha oil: 1 log per Issaron of flour
  • 📏 Standalone oil: minimum 1 log, up to 3 log
  • 🔥 Both end up on the altar fire

📖 Konam Vows — "This Shall Be Like a Korban"

Q
What is a "Konam" vow? How does it work?
A
"Konam" is a substitute word for "Korban." If someone says "Konam — this bread to me," they're declaring the bread forbidden to themselves AS IF it were a consecrated offering. It's a way of creating prohibitions through vow-language.
Q
But the bread doesn't actually become an offering, right?
A
Correct. The bread doesn't go to the Temple. But the person has created a binding prohibition on THEMSELVES — they may not eat that bread. Violating a Konam vow is like violating the prohibition of Me'ilah (misusing sanctified property).
Connection to our chapter: The Gemara discusses how Konam-style vow language interacts with actual Temple offerings. Can someone say "Konam — I will bring a Mincha"? The answer: the Konam language creates the obligation, and the offering rules then govern what they must bring.

🩸 Blood of Carcasses — Continuation

Continuing from Daf 101: The Gemara further analyzes the dispute about neveilah blood and its halachic status.
Is blood that flows from a dead animal at the moment of death impure?
↓
R' Yehuda
Blood that flows at death: impure
Because it's part of the dying process
R' Yosei
Only impure if it's a quarter-log or more
Minimum measure needed for impurity
↓
Quarter-log rationale:
A quarter-log of blood can congeal into an olive-bulk of solid material. An olive-bulk is the minimum for impurity of neveilah flesh. So the blood measure parallels the flesh measure.
