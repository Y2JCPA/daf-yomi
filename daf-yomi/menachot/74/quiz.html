QUESTION 1 OF 5
When a Kohen brings a sin-offering meal offering, how is it treated differently from an Israelite's sin-offering meal offering?
A. No handful is removed — it is entirely eaten by other Kohanim
B. A handful is removed and burned; the remainder is also burned — not eaten
C. It is returned to the Kohen and his family to eat
D. It is treated identically to an Israelite's — handful removed, remainder eaten
Correct: B. The verse states "every meal offering of a Kohen shall be offered entirely; it shall not be eaten." Both the handful AND the remainder are burned on the altar. The rite (removing the handful) is performed like an Israelite's offering — but unlike the Israelite's, the Kohen's remainder is not eaten by the priests.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
The Mishna says "the power of the altar is greater than the power of the priests" regarding three offerings. Which of the following is NOT one of them?
A. The Kohen's personal meal offering
B. The Anointed Kohen's (High Priest) daily meal offering
C. The Two Loaves of Shavuot
D. The meal offering accompanying libations (Nesachim)
Correct: C — The Two Loaves. The Two Loaves are in the opposite category — "the power of the Kohanim is greater," as the Kohanim eat them entirely with nothing going to the altar. The three offerings where the altar wins are: the Kohen's personal minchah, the High Priest's daily minchah, and the libation minchah.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why isn't the burnt offering (Olah) listed among offerings where "the power of the altar is greater"?
A. The Olah isn't considered a complete offering
B. The hide of the animal goes to the Kohanim — so it's not entirely for the altar
C. The Olah is a rabbinic institution not listed in the Mishna
D. The Olah is burned by the Levi'im, not the Kohanim
Correct: B. While an Olah (burnt offering) is indeed entirely burned on the altar (no eating), the animal's hide is given to the Kohanim. Since a part goes to the Kohanim, it doesn't qualify as a case where "the power of the altar is greater than the power of the Kohanim" in a pure sense.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Shmuel rules that one who donates wine should pour it over the altar fires. How does the Mishna relate to this ruling?
A. The Mishna supports it — wine is listed as entirely for the altar
B. The Mishna contradicts it — wine goes to the drainpipes, not burned on the altar
C. The Mishna is silent on wine donations
D. The Mishna says wine donations go to the Kohanim to drink
Correct: B. The Mishna's wording ("the power of the altar is greater" only in those three cases) implicitly excludes wine, contradicting Shmuel's view that donated wine is poured over the altar fires. The Gemara notes this and explains that wine donations go to the drainpipes (shitin), not treated as an altar offering.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to R' Eliezer, the prohibition "it shall not be eaten" applies to more than just a Kohen's meal offering. What does he extend it to?
A. Only to food offerings brought on Yom Kippur
B. To any sacrificial item categorized as "shall be offered entirely" — including the fats and limbs (emurim) of offerings
C. Only to bread offerings, not meat
D. Only to Kohanim — Israelites may eat any part of their own offerings
Correct: B. R' Eliezer extends the prohibition to every sacrificial item that belongs to the "shall be offered entirely" category. This includes the emurim (fats and limbs) of animal offerings — any part designated entirely for the altar carries a Torah-level prohibition against being eaten.
