🍞 What Is Chalah?

Mitzvas Chalah: When baking a minimum amount of dough (at least 43⅕ eggs of flour), one must separate a portion — Chalah — and give it to a Kohen.

רֵאשִׁית עֲרִסֹתֵכֶם חַלָּה תָּרִימוּ תְרוּמָה
"From the first of your doughs, you shall set aside a portion" (Bamidbar 15:20)
Key rule: The obligation of Chalah arises at the moment of kneading (Gilugl). If the dough is exempt at that moment — Chalah never applies, even if later redeemed or acquired.

🏛️ Hekdesh Kneading — Three Scenarios

When Was It Consecrated? Who Kneaded? Chalah Required?
Before kneading → redeemed before kneading Owner (after redemption) ✓ Yes
After kneading → redeemed Owner (before consecration) ✓ Yes
Before kneading → Gizbar kneaded → then redeemed Temple treasurer (while still Hekdesh) ✗ Exempt!
Why exempt in scenario 3? At the moment the obligation would have arisen (kneading), the dough was Temple property — which is exempt from Chalah. The rule: "At the time of its obligation, it was exempt."

Rava derives from this: Kneading by Hekdesh (Temple) exempts — even after redemption.

👤 Does Kneading by a Gentile Exempt?

❓ Rava's Question:
If a gentile kneads dough that will later be owned by a Jew — is it exempt from Chalah?
↓
Proof from Mishnah (Challah 3:6):
A convert who converted — dough kneaded before conversion: ✗ Exempt
Dough kneaded after conversion: ✓ Obligated
In doubt: ✓ Obligated
↓
🤔 But who taught that Mishnah?
Does everyone agree? Or only those who say gentile Miru'ach (smoothing grain) exempts?
↓
Rava's conclusion: The same machlokes applies!
Whoever obligates Miru'ach of a gentile → obligates kneading by a gentile
Whoever exempts Miru'ach → exempts kneading

⚖️ Miru'ach of a Gentile — The Core Machlokes

Miru'ach = smoothing/completing the grain pile — the act that triggers Ma'aser obligation

R' Meir & R' Yehuda

  • 📌 Miru'ach by a gentile DOES obligate Ma'aser
  • 📖 "Degancha" (your grain) written twice → two exclusions → "Mi'ut achar mi'ut" → comes to include even gentile's smoothing
  • 🔄 Therefore: kneading by gentile also obligates Chalah

R' Yosi & R' Shimon

  • 📌 Miru'ach by a gentile does NOT obligate Ma'aser
  • 📖 "Degancha" excludes gentile's grain and gentile's actions
  • 🔄 Therefore: kneading by gentile also exempts from Chalah
Key verse: דְּגָנְךָ — "your grain" (Devarim 18:4)
Written twice in the Torah. Each instance excludes something. Two exclusions (mi'ut achar mi'ut) → include even the seemingly excluded case (gentile's smoothing).

🔄 Terumah vs. Chalah — Different Rules, Different Reasons

Issue Terumah / Ma'aser Chalah
Gentile involvement exempts? Machlokes (R' Meir obligates) Follows same machlokes
Rabbinic decree to prevent abuse? ✓ Yes — sell to gentile trick blocked ✗ No decree needed
Why no decree for Chalah? — Easy workaround: bake less than 5/4 kav of flour
Why decree for Terumah? Alternative workarounds (entering via roof, in chaff) are embarrassing publicly —
Rav Oshaya's insight: A person can avoid Ma'aser by bringing grain through the roof or in its chaff for animal feed — but doing so publicly is embarrassing. So people would prefer to "sell" to a gentile, which the Rabbis blocked with a decree. For Chalah, the workaround (baking a small amount) is private and easy — no decree needed.

🌾 Before the Omer: Chadash Restrictions

The Gemara transitions to discussing the new-crop (Chadash) restrictions in the Omer chapter
🚫 Before the Omer is brought:
New grain from after 16 Nisan of previous year may not be eaten, ground, kneaded, or offered
↓
R' Yehuda
Chadash is prohibited by Torah law until 17 Nisan (after Omer)
"Until this very day" (Vayikra 23:14) = 16 Nisan itself is forbidden
Chachamim
Permitted from nightfall of 16 Nisan (once Omer is offered)
Waiting until morning is only to perform the mitzvah optimally
Practical implication: Before the Omer is offered, you may harvest the grain (in an atypical way — picking by hand, grinding by hand, sifting on top of the sifter) but not eat it. The unusual manner serves as a reminder of the prohibition.
