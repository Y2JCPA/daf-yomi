💰 Four Explanations — What Were They Really For?

The Gemara brings four Amoraic opinions on the purpose of these six horns:
AmoraTheir Explanation
R' AmiTo prevent quarrels — separate boxes for separate purposes, so donors know exactly where their money goes
R' Yitzchak b. PinchasTo prevent decay — coins left loose in one pile would be contaminated together; separate horns keep them clean
RavaTo separate between obligatory (Shekalim) and voluntary (wood, LB, bird) donations — different legal status
AbayeTo solve the specific problem of surplus — when one category had excess, it wouldn't mix with another category's funds
Why six? The Mishna elsewhere adds that there were also 13 horns — the 6 here plus 7 more for other specific purposes. The organization of Temple finances was remarkably sophisticated!

The debate: Can an animal offering be fulfilled by bringing a female?
Olot and most public offerings require males. But a private voluntary offering CAN be female — it becomes a Shlamim (peace offering) where females are acceptable.

📯 Why Horn-Shaped? — Symbolism & Practicality

The Practical Reason

  • 📯 Wide opening at top, narrow at bottom
  • 💰 Coins could be inserted from the top easily
  • 🔒 Hard to remove — the narrow bottom prevented theft
  • 🏦 Like a one-way deposit box
  • ✅ Regular donors could drop coins while walking by

The Symbolic Reason

  • 📯 A Shofar calls the nation together
  • 💡 These "Shofar boxes" call for donations — an ongoing summons to support the Temple
  • 🌍 Donors from all over Israel used them on pilgrimage
  • 📖 The act of giving itself was a form of worship
  • 🔥 Temple maintenance was everyone's responsibility

💡 The Spirit Behind the Collection System

The six Shofar collection horns represent something deeper than Temple administration:
T
The Tamid (daily offering) requires wood, frankincense, birds. Every morning and evening — these offerings need fresh supplies. The collection horns ensured a steady, organized supply from the community.
KL
The half-shekel was obligatory — even the poor and rich paid equally. The bird and wood horns were voluntary — anyone could contribute more. This created both universal obligation AND individual expression in Temple support.
A message for all time: The Temple wasn't just the Kohanim's institution — it was everyone's. The Shofar boxes stood as a silent, permanent invitation: "The fire on the altar is always burning. Will you add to it?"
