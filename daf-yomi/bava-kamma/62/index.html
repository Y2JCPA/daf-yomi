Liability for Fire Damage: What Must One Pay?

One kindles a fire that destroys another's property
↓
Where was the fire kindled?
On one's own premises
Dispute: R' Yehuda says liable even for concealed items; Rabbis say only visible items
On another's premises
All agree: Pays for everything inside the stack
KEY PRINCIPLE The extent of fire liability depends on where the fire originated and whether concealed items were reasonably foreseeable.

Concealed Items in a Burning Stack

Rabbi Yehuda
One who sets fire is liable even for concealed items (כלים טמונים) hidden inside a stack of grain.
Rabbis
The one who set the fire pays only for the stack itself — he could not have known about concealed items.
R' Yehuda (concession)
I agree: if one was given space to stack grain and hid items without permission, the field owner pays only for the stack.
R' Yehuda (concession)
And if one stacked wheat covered with barley, or barley covered with wheat — one pays only for the lesser value (barley).
AGREEMENT R' Yehuda concedes where the victim changed the terms — stacking the wrong grain or hiding items without permission limits the fire-setter's liability.

Rava's Gold Dinar Case

A man gives a gold dinar to a woman for safekeeping, telling her it is silver
↓
What happened to the dinar?
She actively damaged it
She pays the full gold value — "What business did you have damaging it?"
She was negligent / it was lost
She pays only the silver value — "I only undertook to guard a silver dinar"
PARALLEL Rav Mordekhai notes this is obvious from the baraita: one who stacks wheat covered with barley — pays only for barley. The same logic applies to misrepresented deposits.

Takkanat Nignav — The Robbery Victim's Oath

📜 Shmuel explains: R' Yehuda holds one is liable for concealed items in a fire
↓
⚖️ The Sages applied the ordinance of the robbery victim (תקנת נגנב) to fire cases
↓
🤚 Just as a robbery victim swears what was stolen and collects — so too the fire victim swears what was in the stack
↓
❓ Ameimar asks: Does this ordinance extend to an informer (מלשין) who causes confiscation?
↓
🔄 Only relevant according to the view that we judge indirect damage (גרמי) cases
↓
⏸️ The question remains unresolved (תיקו)
TEYKU Whether the robbery victim's oath extends to informer cases is left unresolved — a significant open question in damage law.

Hanukkah Lamp & Fire Liability

Camel Owner Liable

  • Storekeeper's lamp was inside the store
  • Camel's flax entered and caught fire
  • Camel set fire to the building
  • Owner of the camel pays for all damage

Storekeeper Liable

  • Storekeeper placed lamp outside
  • Flax on passing camel caught fire
  • Building burned as a result
  • Storekeeper pays — unless it's a Hanukkah lamp (R' Yehuda)
R' YEHUDA ON CHANUKAH A Hanukkah lamp is a mitzva to place outside — the storekeeper is exempt. Rava infers from this that the mitzva height is within 10 tefachim; above 20 amot is invalid (like sukka and mavoi).
