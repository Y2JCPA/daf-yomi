QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to R' Yehuda, when does he concede that the fire-setter pays only for the stack?
A. When the fire was set accidentally
B. When the victim hid items without permission in borrowed space
C. When the fire was set on Shabbat
D. When the stack was in a public domain
Correct: B — R' Yehuda concedes that when one lends space for stacking and the borrower conceals items without permission, the field owner pays only for the stack — not the hidden items.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In Rava's gold dinar case, what does the woman pay if she was negligent and the dinar was lost?
A. The full gold value
B. Nothing — she was misled
C. Only the silver value
D. Half the gold value
Correct: C — Since she was told it was silver, she only undertook to safeguard a silver dinar. Her obligation as a bailee was calibrated to the stated — not actual — value.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why is a Hanukkah lamp placed above 20 cubits invalid for the mitzva?
A. It violates a rabbinic decree about fire safety
B. People do not generally look up that high, defeating pirsumei nisa
C. The flame cannot be seen from inside the house
D. It is above the height of the Temple menorah
Correct: B — The purpose of the Hanukkah lamp is publicizing the miracle (pirsumei nisa). Above 20 cubits, people don't naturally look up, so it fails this purpose — the same rule as sukka and mavoi.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rav Ashi determine whether a fire victim's claim about a silver goblet is credible?
A. By the number of witnesses he brings
B. By whether the item was commonly found in that region
C. By assessing if he is wealthy enough or trustworthy enough to have such items
D. By requiring the claimant to produce a receipt
Correct: C — Rav Ashi says we assess the claimant: if he is wealthy (likely to own such items) or known as trustworthy (people deposit valuables with him), he can swear and collect. Otherwise, his claim is not believed.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to R' Ḥiyya bar Abba in the name of R' Yoḥanan, what happens to a bailee who falsely claims a deposit was stolen, when he himself stole it and then slaughtered the animal?
A. He pays only the principal value
B. He pays double payment only
C. He pays fourfold or fivefold payment
D. He is exempt because he was the original bailee
Correct: C — R' Ḥiyya bar Abba holds that a false-claiming bailee is treated as a thief in all respects — paying double for theft, and fourfold/fivefold if he slaughtered or sold the ox or sheep.
