Daf 10: Stringencies Between Damage Categories & Multiple Causes

The Baraita compares Ox, Fire, and Pit — each has unique stringencies over the others
↓
❓ Why doesn't the Baraita mention vessel liability as a stringency of Ox over Pit?
↓
Rabbis' View
Pit exempts vessels — the tanna omitted this case along with tamun (concealed items)
R' Yehuda's View
Pit does create liability for vessels — so no omission needed
↓
The Gemara then examines multiple causes of damage: bench case, beating case
Key Theme Each primary category of damage (avot nezikin) has unique stringencies — and when multiple actors contribute to damage, liability follows the proximate cause.

Stringencies: Ox vs Fire

🐂 Ox Stricter Than Fire

  • Owner pays kofer (ransom) if ox kills a Jew
  • Pays 30 sela if ox kills a slave
  • Once verdict is complete, ox is assur b'hana'ah (no benefit allowed)
  • Liable if ox given to cheresh, shoteh, or katan (deaf-mute, imbecile, minor)

🔥 Fire Stricter Than Ox

  • Fire is mu'ad mi-techilato — forewarned from its inception
  • No need for three prior incidents to establish forewarned status
  • Ox starts as tam (innocuous) and must become mu'ad
Note Fire's automatic forewarned status means full damages from the start, while an ox owner initially pays only half damages.
4 / 9

Stringencies: Fire vs Pit

🕳️ Pit Stricter Than Fire

  • Pit's initial formation (digging) is itself the act of damage
  • Liable if pit transferred to cheresh, shoteh, or katan

🔥 Fire Stricter Than Pit

  • Fire's typical manner is to proceed and cause damage at a distance
  • Forewarned for ra'ui lo (fitting — wood) AND she'eino ra'ui lo (not fitting — vessels)
  • Can scorch plowed fields or singe stones — Pit cannot
Dispute R' Yehuda holds one is liable for vessel damage from a Pit too, which affects how we read this baraita.

Whose Opinion Does the Baraita Follow?

Gemara
Why doesn't the baraita list vessel liability as a stringency of Ox over Pit?
Answer 1
It follows R' Yehuda, who holds Pit also creates vessel liability — so there's no distinction to list.
Gemara
But the baraita says Fire covers 'not fitting' items (vessels) and Pit does not — that contradicts R' Yehuda!
Answer 2
Actually it follows the Rabbis. The tanna omitted vessel liability — and also omitted tamun (concealed items exempt from Fire).
Gemara
That works — a tanna who omits one case may omit others.
Alt. Answer
Or it is R' Yehuda, and 'not fitting' means scorched fields and singed stones, not vessels.
Rav Ashi's Objection If it's R' Yehuda, why not mention liability for disqualified consecrated animals (pesulei ha-mukdashin) as a stringency of Ox over Pit?

Tamun — Concealed Items

טמון באש
Key Concept If a fire damages tamun (concealed/hidden items), the one who lit the fire is exempt. This exemption applies only to Fire, not to other categories of damage. The Gemara uses this as proof that the baraita omitted cases — since tamun was omitted, vessel liability could also have been omitted.

Five People on a Bench (Rav Pappa)

Five people sit on a bench (safsal) — it holds their weight
↓
A sixth person sits down and the bench breaks
↓
❓ Who is liable for the damage?
↓
Without him it wouldn't break
Obviously he alone is liable — his weight caused it
It would break anyway
Then what did he do? He seemingly caused nothing
↓
Resolution: Without him it would break in 2 hours; with him it broke in 1 hour. He hastened the damage.
↓
But he can counter: 'Without you still sitting, my weight alone wouldn't break it!'
↓
Final answer: He leaned on them instantly — his koach (force) broke it, and they couldn't move
Rav Pappa This applies where the last person was as heavy as Pappa bar Abba — capable of breaking it alone.
