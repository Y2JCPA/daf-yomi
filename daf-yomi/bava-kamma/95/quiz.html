QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the Gemara say the ordinance for penitents applies to the stolen beam built into a building, even though the beam still exists?
A. Because the beam was physically altered and no longer recognizable
B. Because destroying the building would cause excessive loss, so the beam is treated as if it doesn't exist
C. Because the robbery victim waived his rights to the beam
D. Because beams are categorized as real estate, not movable property
Correct: B — The Sages treat the beam as though it no longer exists because removing it would require destroying the entire building — an excessive loss that would discourage repentance.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the practical difference between the two possible explanations of Rabbi Meir's reasoning?
A. Whether the robber must return the offspring in addition to the animal
B. Whether the rule applies to slaves or only to animals
C. What happens when the stolen item decreased in value — return as is, or pay original value
D. Whether the ordinance for penitents overrides the obligation to return
Correct: C — If R' Meir holds shinui eino koneh (change doesn't acquire), a devalued item is returned as is. If his rule is a penalty, the robber must pay the higher original value since he technically acquired the item through change.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does the Gemara prove that Rabbi Meir's penalty applies only to intentional wrongdoing?
A. From the case of the stolen beam built into a building
B. From the ordinance for penitents regarding the belt
C. From the case of a dyer who dyed wool the wrong color — he pays only wool's value, not the enhancement
D. From the case of slaves who aged in the robber's possession
Correct: C — The dyer who mistakenly dyed wool the wrong color pays only the wool's value, not its enhancement. Since the dyer acted unintentionally (following instructions, just incorrectly), R' Meir does not impose the penalty — proving it applies only to intentional acts.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rav Zevid, what is the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon?
A. Whether change acquires a stolen item or not
B. Whether enhancement still on the stolen item belongs to the victim or the robber
C. Whether the robber keeps all or only a partial share of the enhancement
D. Whether the stolen item should be appraised at the time of robbery or at the time of return
Correct: B — Rav Zevid explains that R' Yehuda holds enhancement still on the item belongs to the robbery victim, while R' Shimon holds it belongs to the robber.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Meir distinguish between a robbed slave who aged and a robbed animal who aged?
A. Because animals depreciate faster than slaves
B. Because slaves can testify about their own value at the time of robbery
C. Because a slave has the legal status of real estate, which cannot be stolen
D. Because the ordinance for penitents applies to slaves but not animals
Correct: C — R' Meir holds that a slave's legal status is like real estate (mekarkei), and real estate cannot be stolen. Therefore the robber can say 'yours is before you' and return the slave as is, even if aged. The Rabbis disagree, treating slaves as movable property for robbery purposes.
