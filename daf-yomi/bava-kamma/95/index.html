Bava Kamma 95: Stolen Items, Change & Enhancement

Core Question: When a robber changes a stolen item, who owns the change?
↓
R' Meir
Returns animal and its wool/offspring
R' Yehuda
Stolen item returned as is
R' Shimon
Pay appraised value at time of robbery
↓
Gemara investigates: Is R' Meir's rule a legal principle or a penalty (kenas)?
↓
Conclusion: R' Meir penalizes intentional robbers but not unintentional ones
Key Principle The Gemara explores whether physical change (shinui) transfers ownership and how enhancement (shevach) on stolen property is allocated between robber and victim.

Takkanat HaShavim: Ordinance for Penitents

A robber wants to repent and return stolen goods
↓
Does the stolen item still exist?
Yes — Item Exists
Victim may accept it from the robber
No — Item Gone
Do not accept even payment — to encourage repentance
↓
Exception: Stolen beam built into a building (Gittin 55a)
↓
Though the beam still exists, Sages treat it as if destroyed — robber pays monetary value, need not demolish building
The Belt Story A robber's wife told him he'd have to return 'even the belt' — the Gemara clarifies this means the value of the belt, as the actual belt was gone.

Three Opinions: What Does the Robber Return?

R' Meir
The robber returns the animal and its sheared wool, or the animal and its offspring — the robbery victim keeps everything.
R' Yehuda
A stolen item is returned as is (gazul b'eino hu chozer) — whatever state it's currently in.
R' Shimon
The stolen item is viewed as if monetarily appraised at the time of the robbery — the robber pays only that fixed amount.
Scenario One robbed a pregnant cow that gave birth, or a wool-laden ewe that was shorn — what must the robber return?

Rabbi Meir's Reasoning: Two Possibilities

Option A: Legal Principle

  • Change does NOT transfer ownership (shinui eino koneh)
  • The animal always belonged to the victim
  • Wool/offspring naturally belong to victim
  • If item lost value → return as is (lower value)

Option B: Penalty (Kenas)

  • Change DOES transfer ownership
  • Robber technically owns the wool/offspring
  • Sages penalize robber to deter theft
  • If item lost value → pay original higher value
Test Case If the stolen animal aged and lost value, the two explanations yield different outcomes — this is how the Gemara tries to determine R' Meir's reasoning.

Gemara's Investigation of Rabbi Meir

📜 Mishna (96b): Robbed animal that aged — robber pays value at time of robbery
↓
🔍 R' Meir concedes on aged animals: pay original value, not return as is
↓
💡 This suggests R' Meir holds shinui IS koneh — his rule is a penalty!
↓
❌ Rebuttal: R' Meir may be arguing 'according to YOUR view' (the Rabbis')
↓
📜 Proof from dyer mishna (100b): R' Meir requires only wool value, NOT enhancement
↓
✅ Conclusion: R' Meir penalizes intentional robbers, NOT unintentional actors
Dyer Case A dyer told to dye wool red but dyed it black — R' Meir requires only wool's value, not the enhancement. This proves the penalty applies only to intentional wrongdoing.
