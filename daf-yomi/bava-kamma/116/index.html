Salvage, Stipulation & Shared Risk

Core Question: When someone sacrifices their property to save another's, what are the rules of compensation?
↓
With Stipulation: Owner agrees to pay — binding obligation
↓
Without Stipulation: Rescuer receives only standard wages
↓
Extension: Caravans
Shared risk among travelers — ransom by wealth, scout by headcount
Extension: Boats
Lightening cargo — calculate by weight, not monetary value
Key Principle Agreements made under genuine duress with real loss are binding — but shared risk requires fair division among all parties.

Pouring Out Wine to Save Honey: Binding or Bluffing?

Gemara
The owner of wine poured it out to save another's honey after stipulating compensation. Why is this binding?
Objection
Let the honey owner say: 'I was merely fooling' (meshateina bavakh) — I never truly agreed!
Baraita
A prisoner escaping tells a ferryman: 'Take this full dinar to cross me.' The ferryman collects only his usual wage.
Rami bar Ḥama
The second clause differs: if the ferryman is a fisherman (kavrei), he lost fish worth a dinar. Real loss = real obligation.
Gemara
Likewise, the wine owner sustained a genuine financial loss — so the honey owner's stipulation is fully binding.
Distinction When someone suffers actual loss (not just inflated pricing), the other party's agreement under pressure is binding — it's not 'fooling.'

Why the Mishna Needs Both Cases

Wine & Honey

  • Wine is lost through direct action of its owner
  • With stipulation → full compensation owed
  • Without stipulation → might think full value owed anyway (because active sacrifice)

Donkeys in the River

  • Donkey is lost by itself in the river
  • With stipulation → full compensation owed
  • Without stipulation → only wages for labor (no active sacrifice)
Conclusion Both cases are necessary: the wine case teaches that without stipulation, active sacrifice ≠ automatic full compensation; the donkey case teaches that stipulation binds even for passive loss.

Rav Kahana's Dilemma: Donkey Saved Itself!

Reuven stipulates: 'I'll save your donkey if you compensate me for mine'
↓
Reuven rescues Shimon's donkey from the river
↓
Meanwhile, Reuven's own donkey emerges by itself!
↓
Rav's Ruling
Shimon still pays. It was from Heaven that mercy was bestowed — Reuven's good fortune doesn't cancel the stipulation.
Proof: Rav Safra
Rav Safra sent his donkey to a lion as his turn required. The lion didn't eat it. He still fulfilled his obligation.
Principle A person's unexpected good fortune (min hashamayim) does not retroactively void a valid agreement or fulfilled obligation.

Caravan & Boat: Dividing Shared Expenses

ScenarioHow to CalculateReasoning
Paying ransom to banditsAccording to money carriedGreater wealth = greater target
Hiring a scoutAccording to number of peopleScout protects lives equally
Lightening a boat in a stormAccording to weight of cargoWeight is what sinks the boat
Replacing a lost donkeyCaravan provides a new oneOnly if lost without negligence
Replacing a lost boatFleet provides a new oneOnly if lost without negligence and on the usual route
Custom Prevails In all cases, the established custom (minhag) of donkey drivers or sailors takes precedence over these default rules.

When Is Sailing Off-Course Considered Negligence?

⛵ In Nisan, boats sail one rope-length from shore
↓
🌊 In Tishrei, boats sail two rope-lengths from shore
↓
📅 A sailor in Nisan sails to the Tishrei distance
↓
⚠️ Even though he follows his usual habits, this is negligence
↓
❌ The other sailors do NOT replace his boat
Takeaway Negligence is measured by objective seasonal standards, not personal habit — sailing too far from shore in Nisan counts as negligence even if the sailor routinely does so.
