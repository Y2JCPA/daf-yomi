Judging an Ox That Killed — Owner Fled

Sages of Rav's School
Once a death verdict is given, we don't delay for monetary evaluation — so monetary damages are not judged.
Rava
Even per Rabbi Akiva (no court assessment needed), the case here is where the owner fled — court can't judge monetary liability in his absence.
Gemara
If the owner fled, how can monetary law be judged at all? Testimony was accepted when he was present — he fled afterward.
Gemara
Damages are paid from plowing — the ox is rented to plow fields, and the fees cover the damages.
Rav Mari b. Rav Kahana Plowing fees are like superior-quality property (idit) of the owner — not part of the ox's value. So an innocuous ox (tam) cannot pay damages this way, since tam pays only from the ox itself.

Is Evaluation Required for Damage (Not Just Killing)?

Dilemma: Must the court evaluate whether an act sufficed to cause the injury?
↓
Only for Killing
Evaluation determines if a soul departs from this blow — but for damage, liable for whatever caused it
For Damage Too
No difference — court evaluates whether the act sufficed to cause that specific injury
↓
Proof attempt from Pit: Less than 10 tefachim — exempt from death, liable for damage
↓
Counting Up
Even 1 tefach = damage liability → no evaluation needed
Rejected: Counting Down
Slightly less than 10 = damage; evaluation may still apply
Unresolved The Gemara attempts multiple proofs but does not conclusively resolve whether evaluation (umdenah) applies to damage cases.

May a Person Injure Himself?

Permitted

  • Baraita on oaths: one who swears to 'do evil to himself' is liable for breaking the oath — implying self-harm is optional, not prohibited
  • Doing evil to oneself parallels doing good — both are voluntary acts
  • Some Tanna'im hold self-injury is within one's rights

Prohibited

  • R' Akiva (mishna): 'One who injures himself — although not permitted — is exempt from penalty'
  • R' Elazar: 'Your blood of your souls will I require' (Gen. 9:5) — liable even for taking one's own life
  • R' Elazar HaKappar: A nazirite is called a sinner for merely abstaining from wine
Conclusion The Gemara concludes this is a tannaitic dispute (machloket tanna'im) — there is no single ruling, but the prohibition view is strongly supported.

Shmuel's Reinterpretation of the Oath Baraita

📜 Baraita: One who swears to 'do evil to himself' and doesn't — is liable for the broken oath
↓
❓ Challenge: This implies self-harm is permitted (optional activity)
↓
💡 Shmuel reinterprets: 'Do evil to himself' means swearing to fast, not to injure
↓
🔄 Parallel: 'Do evil to others' = confining them so they can't eat (forced fasting)
↓
❌ Rejection: Another baraita defines 'evil to others' as 'I will strike and injure his brain'
↓
⚖️ Result: Shmuel's reinterpretation fails — must be a dispute between Tanna'im
Shmuel Shmuel tried to harmonize the sources by limiting 'self-harm' to fasting, but the explicit baraita about striking and injuring the brain undermines this reading.
