From Whom May One Purchase?

Core Question: When is it permitted to buy items from those who may possess stolen goods?
↓
Watchmen & Sharecroppers
Depends on their share in the land and manner of sale
Known Robbers
Depends on proportion of legally owned property
Craftsmen
Depends on whether materials underwent shinui (physical change)
↓
Principle: Robbery is so severe that stealing even a peruta is like taking a person's soul
Key Theme This daf systematically examines the boundaries of permitted commerce with those suspected or known to possess stolen goods — from produce watchmen to professional craftsmen.

Buying from a Sharecropper

Rava
Purchased grapevine branches from a sharecropper — treating it as a legitimate sale.
Abaye
But the Mishna says one may not purchase wood and produce from produce watchmen! The same should apply to a sharecropper!
Rava
A watchman (shomer) has no share in the land. But a sharecropper (aris) has a share — he may be selling from his own portion.
Distinction The key difference: a watchman is merely a guardian with no ownership stake, while a sharecropper has a legitimate share in the produce.

When May One Buy from Watchmen?

A produce watchman (shomer peirot) is selling items
↓
✅ Permitted
Sitting openly with baskets and scales before them
❌ Prohibited
They say: 'Conceal your purchase'
↓
✅ Permitted
Buying from the entrance of the garden
❌ Prohibited
Buying from the back of the garden (inconspicuous)
Baraita Principle Openness signals legitimacy; secrecy signals theft. The location and manner of the sale determine its permissibility.

Buying from a Known Robber

Rav

  • Prohibited to buy from a robber
  • Until the majority of his possessions are legally obtained
  • Stricter standard — assumes most items are stolen

Shmuel

  • Permitted to buy from a robber
  • Even if only a minority of his possessions are legally obtained
  • Rav Yehuda instructed his attendant Adda to follow this lenient ruling
Halakha Rav Yehuda followed Shmuel's lenient opinion in practice, permitting purchases even when only a minority of the robber's possessions were legally acquired.

The Informer's Property & Whose Soul Is Taken

Opinion A
An informer's (moseir) property may be destroyed — his property shouldn't be more protected than his body, which may be harmed.
Opinion B
It is prohibited to destroy it — perhaps he will have righteous children who deserve to inherit. As it says: 'The wicked may prepare it, but the just shall put it on' (Job 27:17).
Rav Ḥisda
I dismissed my dishonest sharecropper, reading: 'The wealth of the sinner is laid up for the righteous' (Proverbs 13:22).
R' Yoḥanan Anyone who robs another of even one peruta is considered as though he takes the victim's soul — 'It takes away the life of the owner thereof' (Proverbs 1:19).

Buying from Craftsmen: What's Permitted?

Craftsman❌ May NOT Buy✅ May Buy
CarderLoose strands of thread (unless local custom permits)Cushion or mattress stuffed with strands (shinui — change of form)
WeaverWoolen wads (irin), heddles (nirin), bobbin threads, spool remnantsSpotted garments, warp/weft threads, spun or twisted wool
DyerTest pieces of wool, color samples (dugmut), detached woolColored garments, spun threads, felt garments
TannerTrimmings and detached hairs (belong to customer)Substance from washing in water (belongs to tanner)
Underlying Principle Items that have undergone shinui (physical transformation) are considered acquired by the craftsman — even if originally stolen, the change of form creates legal ownership.
