How Far Does Pit Liability Extend?

Torah states: "An ox or donkey fall" into a pit — owner pays
↓
Is this a klal u'prat (generalization & detail)?
Only Ox & Donkey?
Klal u'prat: only what's in the detail
All Animals?
Klal u'prat u'klal: items similar to the detail
↓
Final source: "He shall recompense money to its owners" — any owned animal is included
↓
Excludes: People ("ox" — not a person) and Vessels ("donkey" — not vessels, per Rabbis)
Key Principle The Torah's mention of specific animals in pit liability is not limiting — through exegesis, liability extends to all animals, but excludes people and (according to the Rabbis) vessels.

Why Does the Torah Say Both 'Ox' AND 'Donkey'?

Rabbis
The word "or" separates the cases — liability applies if either one falls in individually.
R' Yehuda
I derive that from the singular verb "venafal" — even one animal triggers liability.
Gemara
If only ox were written, I'd think only animals sacrificed on the altar are included.
Gemara
If only donkey were written, I'd think only animals whose firstborn is sanctified are included.
Gemara
Therefore both are needed — to teach that all animals are included, not just those with special sanctity.
Takeaway Each named animal prevents a mistaken limitation — together they show pit liability covers all animals.

Klal u'Prat u'Klal: Deriving Pit Liability

Generalization: "And fall" — any item that falls
↓
Detail: "An ox or a donkey" — specific animals
↓
Generalization: "The owner of the pit shall pay" — broadens again
↓
Result: Items similar to the detail — what qualifies?
Included
All animals — similar to ox and donkey
Excluded?
Birds — carcasses don't cause impurity by contact/carrying
Problem The klal-u'prat-u'klal approach would exclude birds, so the Gemara ultimately seeks a different derivation.

What Does Pit Liability Include?

Rabbis' View

  • ✅ All animals (including birds)
  • ✅ Deaf, imbecile, or young oxen
  • ❌ People — derived from "ox"
  • ❌ Vessels — derived from "donkey"
  • ❌ Disqualified consecrated animals (Rava)

R' Yehuda's View

  • ✅ All animals (including birds)
  • ✅ Deaf, imbecile, or young oxen
  • ✅ Vessels — included via amplification
  • ❌ People — derived from "ox"
  • ❓ "Donkey" has no clear exclusion purpose
Rava's Difficulty According to R' Yehuda, the term "donkey" regarding Pit and "sheep" regarding lost items have no clear exegetical purpose — they remain unresolved.

Is a Standard-Intelligence Ox Covered by Pit Liability?

R' Yirmeya
The mishna uses lo tzricha style: Obviously a normal ox is covered. It teaches that even a deaf, imbecile, or young ox — where you might say its own deficiency caused the fall — is also covered.
Rav Aḥa
But a baraita says: If a mentally competent animal falls in, the owner is exempt!
Rava
R' Yirmeya must be rejected. A standard-intelligence ox is exempt — it should have watched where it was walking.
Baraita
Deaf, imbecile, young, blind, or walking at night — liable. Standard intelligence walking by day — exempt.
Principle A normal ox is expected to watch where it walks. Pit liability only applies when the animal cannot reasonably avoid the hazard.
