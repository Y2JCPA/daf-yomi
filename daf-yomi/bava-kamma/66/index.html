How Does a Thief Acquire a Stolen Item?

Item is stolen
The thief takes another's property
↓
Has the item undergone a physical change (שינוי)?
Yes — Shinui occurred
Thief acquires the item and pays monetary value
(Beit Hillel & Rabba — Torah law)
No — Item unchanged
Thief must return the item itself
("he shall restore the item that he robbed")
Core Principle Bava Kamma 66 establishes that shinui koneh — physical change causes acquisition — is rooted in Torah law, then debates whether ye'ush (owner's despair) has the same effect.

Beit Shammai vs. Beit Hillel: Does Shinui Acquire?

Beit Hillel
The word "these" teaches two things: the etnan is disqualified, but not in its changed form, and not its offspring.
Beit Shammai
"These" excludes only offspring. The word "even" teaches that a changed form is still disqualified — shinui does NOT acquire.
Gemara
The word "even" is indeed difficult for Beit Hillel's position.
Gemara
But both agree: the thief pays the double/fourfold/fivefold payment based on value at the time of theft.
Agreement Despite disagreeing on shinui, both schools agree that payment is calculated at the time of the theft.

Rav vs. the Baraita: When Is Payment Calculated?

Baraita

  • Principal — at time of theft
  • Double payment — at time of theft
  • Fourfold/fivefold — at time of theft
  • All payments based on original value

Rav

  • Principal — at time of theft
  • Double payment — at time of trial
  • Fourfold/fivefold — at time of trial
  • Penalty payments reflect current value
Rava's Resolution Physical changes (lambs→rams) — pay based on original state. Market fluctuations — pay based on trial value. This reconciles Rav with the baraita.

Rabba's Proofs That Shinui Acquires

📖 Torah: "He shall restore the item that he robbed" (Lev. 5:23) — only if it's the same as when stolen; otherwise, he pays money
↓
🪵 Mishna (93b): One who steals wood and makes vessels, or wool and makes garments — pays the original value and keeps the changed item
↓
🐑 Mishna (Ḥullin 135a): If one dyed the first of the sheared wool before giving it to a kohen — he is exempt, because the change made it a new item
↓
✅ Conclusion: Shinui koneh — physical change causes acquisition — is established from Torah law
Key Term שינוי קונה (Shinui Koneh) — A physical transformation of a stolen item transfers ownership to the thief, who must then pay monetary compensation.

Ye'ush (Despair): Does It Transfer Ownership?

Rabba
Ye'ush causes acquisition. It might be Torah law — just like a finder acquires a lost item after the owner despairs.
Rabba
Or perhaps it's only rabbinic — since theft is prohibited, unlike finding. The Sages enacted it as a תקנת השבים (ordinance for penitents).
Rav Yosef
Ye'ush does NOT cause acquisition at all — not even by rabbinic law!
Rav Yosef
Proof: Stolen chametz after Pesach — the robber can say "yours is before you." If ye'ush acquired, he'd owe money, not return the bread!
Rava
Ye'ush works only when the thief wants to acquire. Here the thief doesn't want worthless chametz!
Debate Status Rabba holds ye'ush acquires (uncertain if Torah or rabbinic). Rav Yosef holds it never acquires. The Gemara tests both views.

Abaye's Challenge: Stolen Offerings

Baraita: "His offering" — but not a stolen animal
↓
What case does this discuss?
Before ye'ush?
Obvious — he can't consecrate what isn't his.
No verse needed!
After ye'ush?
Still can't consecrate it → ye'ush doesn't acquire!
(Abaye's challenge to Rabba)
↓
Rava's answer: The baraita refers to a pre-consecrated animal that was stolen. Consecrated property belongs to the Temple wherever it is — it simply cannot be stolen.
Key Distinction Rava deflects the proof: consecrated animals are a special category — they can't be stolen because they belong to hekdesh (the Temple treasury) regardless of physical location.
