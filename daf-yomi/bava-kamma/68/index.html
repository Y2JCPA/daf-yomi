Does Owner's Despair (Ye'ush) Give the Thief Ownership?

Rav
Ye'ush does cause the thief to acquire the stolen item — after despair, the item belongs to the thief
Gemara
If ye'ush grants ownership, why pay fourfold/fivefold? He'd be slaughtering or selling his own animal!
R' Yochanan
The thief is liable for selling whether before or after the owner's despair
Reish Lakish
After ye'ush the thief is exempt — he acquired the animal, so it's his own that he sells
Core Issue The Gemara brings multiple baraitot to resolve whether ye'ush (owner's despair) transfers ownership to a thief, impacting fourfold/fivefold liability

Before Ye'ush

The 'Entrenched in Sin' Argument

Gemara
The Torah imposed 4x/5x because the thief became entrenched in sin (נתחבב בעבירה) through sale. When did this sale occur?
Gemara
If before ye'ush — the sale is invalid, so there's no real deepening of sin. It must be after ye'ush!
Gemara
But if ye'ush causes acquisition, why pay 4x/5x? He's selling his own animal!
Rava
The thief is liable because he repeated his sin — going through the motions of sale counts, even if the sale isn't legally binding
Rava's Resolution Even a sale before ye'ush (legally invalid) triggers liability because the thief deepened his wrongdoing by repeating his sin

Slaughter vs. Sale — Irreversibility Requirement

Slaughter (שחיטה)

  • Inherently irreversible
  • Cannot undo the act
  • Effective immediately
  • Liability is clear

Sale (מכירה)

  • Must also be irreversible (like slaughter)
  • Before ye'ush: sale is undoable → no parallel
  • After ye'ush: sale is permanent → parallels slaughter
  • Rav Nachman: excludes temporary transfers (e.g. 30 days)
Hekesh The Torah juxtaposes slaughter and sale — 'and slaughter it or sell it' — teaching that sale must share slaughter's quality of being a completed, irreversible act

ScenarioFirst Thief PaysSecond Thief Pays

Stole & sold → another stole from buyer4x/5x payment to ownerDouble payment to purchaser
Stole & slaughtered → another stole meat4x/5x payment to ownerPrincipal only (stole from thief)
Analysis The middle case proves the sale occurred after ye'ush — otherwise the buyer has no ownership, and the second thief would be stealing from a thief (no double payment). Yet the first thief still pays 4x/5x, challenging the view that ye'ush grants ownership.

R' Yochanan vs. Reish Lakish — Sale After Ye'ush

R' Yochanan
If the thief sells after ye'ush, he is liable for 4x/5x — liability applies whether before or after despair
Reish Lakish
After ye'ush he is exempt — the thief acquired the animal through ye'ush, so he slaughters/sells his own property
R' Yochanan
Proof from kidnapping: No one despairs of their own freedom, yet the Torah imposes death for selling a kidnapped person — showing sale liability exists without ye'ush!
R' Elazar
Ordinary theft results in ye'ush by default (סתם גניבה יאוש בעלים הוי) — the Torah's fourfold payment assumes despair has already occurred
Kidnapping Proof Exodus 21:16 — The kidnapper is liable for selling even though the victim never despairs, proving sale-liability can exist independent of ye'ush
