From Shabbat Fire Liability to Burden of Proof

Does lighting fire on Shabbat require needing the ashes to be liable?
↓
R' Yochanan
Yes — only liable if he needs the ashes (melakha she-tzerikha le-gufah)
Rava's Solution
Even unintentional Shabbat violation exempts from payment (kim lei bidrabba minei)
↓
MISHNA: Ox pursuing another ox — who bears the burden of proof?
↓
Hamotzi mechavero alav ha-ra'aya — the claimant must bring proof
Key Theme This daf transitions from capital-vs-monetary liability to the fundamental evidentiary principle: the burden of proof rests on the claimant.

The Intelligent Ox That Needed Ashes

Gemara
If R' Yochanan requires needing the ashes for fire liability, how can an ox need ashes?
Rav Avya
An intelligent ox bitten on its back burns the haystack to roll in the ashes (iggandar) and heal the bite!
Gemara
How do we know it intended this?
Rav Avya
Because after burning the haystack, it was seen rolling in the ashes.
Gemara
Can an ox really be that smart?
Rav Pappa
Yes! An ox at my house had a toothache — it broke open a barrel lid and drank the liquor inside, and was cured!
Insight The Gemara accepts that animals can act with surprising purposefulness — a legal fiction that preserves R' Yochanan's requirement of melakha she-tzerikha le-gufah.

Rava: Unintentional Shabbat Violation Still Exempts

One lights a fire on Shabbat unintentionally and destroys property
↓
Is he liable for monetary damages?
↓
Expected Ruling
No death penalty for unintentional act → should pay damages
Rava's Ruling
Still exempt — the category of act carries death penalty, so kim lei bidrabba minei applies
↓
Source: School of Chizkiyya — Leviticus 24:21 compares killing an animal to killing a man: just as animal-killer always pays, man-killer never pays monetary damages
Legal Principle Kim lei bidrabba minei — even unintentional capital offenses exempt from monetary liability, because the exemption follows the category of act, not the actual punishment.

MISHNA: Ox Pursuing Another Ox — Scenarios

One Pursuer, One Victim

  • Injured ox found hurt after being pursued
  • Owner claims: "Your ox injured mine!"
  • Pursuer's owner: "A rock caused it!"
  • Ruling: Burden of proof on the claimant

Two Pursuers, Different Owners

  • Two oxen from different owners chase one ox
  • Each owner blames the other's ox
  • Neither can prove which caused injury
  • Ruling: Both are exempt

Two Pursuers, Same Owner

  • Two oxen from same owner chase one ox
  • Owner cannot deflect blame to another party
  • One of his oxen definitely caused it
  • Ruling: Both are liable
Hamotzi Mechavero The fundamental principle: one who seeks to extract payment from another bears the burden of proof — המוציא מחבירו עליו הראיה.

Disputes: Large vs. Small, Innocuous vs. Forewarned

Injured Party ClaimsLiable Party ClaimsRuling
The large ox caused damage (higher restitution)No, the small ox caused it (lower value)Burden on claimant
The forewarned ox caused damage (full damages)No, the innocuous ox caused it (half damages)Burden on claimant
Large injured large, small injured smallNo — small injured large, large injured smallBurden on claimant
Forewarned injured large, innocuous injured smallNo — innocuous injured large, forewarned injured smallBurden on claimant
Pattern In every permutation, the owner liable for damage benefits from uncertainty — he pays according to his own admission unless the claimant proves otherwise.

Sumakhos vs. the Rabbanan on Uncertain Ownership

R' Chiyya bar Abba
Our Mishna shows the Rabbanan disagree with Sumakhos, who says uncertain property is divided equally.
R' Abba bar Memel
Does Sumakhos really say this even when both parties claim certainty (bari u-bari)?
R' Chiyya bar Abba
Yes — Sumakhos applies his rule even in a case of certain claim vs. certain claim.
Rav Pappa
But the latter clause must involve certain vs. uncertain claims — otherwise it refutes Rabba bar Natan!
The Dilemma If the defendant makes a certain admission that doesn't match the claim (wheat claimed, barley admitted), Rabba bar Natan says he's exempt. The Gemara must reconcile the Mishna with this view.
