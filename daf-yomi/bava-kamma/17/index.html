Bava Kamma 17 — From Hezekiah's Honor to the Laws of Trampling

Aggadic Opening: What unique honor was given to King Hezekiah at his burial?
↓
R' Yehuda
36,000 men with bared shoulders (gematria of לו)
R' Nehemiah
Torah scroll laid on his bier: 'This one fulfilled what is written in this'
↓
R' Yochanan: The unique honor was saying 'He taught what is written in this' — teaching Torah to others surpasses personal fulfillment
↓
MISHNA — Regel (Trampling): Animal is mu'ad (forewarned) for breaking objects while walking normally
↓
Full Damage
Trampling / breaking in normal walking
Half Damage
Kicking while walking, or pebbles propelled from under feet
Key Theme This daf transitions from aggadic teachings about honoring Torah scholars to the Mishna's detailed laws of trampling damage (Regel), including the important half-damage for pebbles rule.

What Unique Honor Was Given to Hezekiah?

R' Yehuda
36,000 men with bared shoulders went before his bier — hinted by the gematria of לו (= 36)
R' Nehemiah
They did the same for the wicked Ahab! That's standard honor for kings, not unique to Hezekiah.
R' Nehemiah
Rather, they laid a Torah scroll on his bier and proclaimed: 'This one fulfilled what is written in this.'
Gemara
But we do that nowadays too for great Torah scholars! What was unique?
R' Yochanan
Today we say 'fulfilled' — but for Hezekiah they said: 'He taught what is written in this' — teaching others is the greater praise.
Principle Teaching Torah to others (לימוד לאחרים) is greater than personal fulfillment of mitzvot.

Torah Study Leads to Action — But Teaching Surpasses Both

The Master said: Torah study is great because it leads to performance of mitzvot
↓
Question: If performance is the goal, why praise Hezekiah for teaching after already praising him for fulfilling?
↓
Studying for Oneself
Leads to performing mitzvot — this is what the Master meant
Teaching Others
The unique praise of Hezekiah — he enabled others to learn and fulfill Torah
Resolution Personal study → personal performance. But teaching others multiplies Torah observance — that was Hezekiah's unique greatness.

Torah + Kindness = Reward of Two Tribes

🐂 Joseph (Ox)

  • Ox = symbol of Joseph (Deut. 33:17)
  • Reward: a canopy of honor
  • Alt: enemies fall before him — 'his horns shall gore the nations'
  • Linked to: acts of kindness (sowing = charity)

🫏 Issachar (Donkey)

  • Donkey = symbol of Issachar (Gen. 49:14)
  • Reward: portion of Issachar
  • Alt: understanding of the times (I Chron. 12:33)
  • Linked to: Torah study (water = Torah)
Source 'Happy are you that sow beside all waters, that send forth the feet of the ox and the donkey' (Isaiah 32:20) — R' Shimon ben Yoḥai via R' Yoḥanan

Mishna: Categories of Trampling Damage (Regel)

ScenarioDamage CategoryPayment
Animal walks normally and tramples objectsPrimary Regel (Trampling)Full damage (נזק שלם)
Animal walks and breaks objects with its bodySubcategory of RegelFull damage
Animal kicks while walkingToldah — not standard walkingHalf damage
Pebbles propelled from under feet (צרורות)Toldah — indirectHalf damage
Tramples vessel → shard breaks 2nd vessel1st = Regel; 2nd = PebblesFull + Half damage
Key Rule Tzrorot (pebbles): Indirect damage caused by pebbles propelled during normal walking — owner pays half damage. This is a halakha l'Moshe mi'Sinai.

Why Does the Mishna Repeat 'Forewarned' for Trampling?

Ravina
Trampling with the foot and breaking with the body — it's the same category of Regel! Why state it twice?
Rava
First the Tanna teaches the primary category (foot), then the subcategories (body, hair, saddle, etc.).
Ravina
Then why does the Mishna about Eating (Shen) also repeat 'forewarned' — what subcategories are there?
Rava
😅 I solved one Mishna — now you solve the other!
Rav Ashi
The Mishna on Eating teaches first undomesticated animals (derived), then domesticated (explicit) — the derived halakha is 'dear to him.'
Structural Principle The Tanna sometimes teaches the derived halakha first because what is learned through interpretation (דרשא) is dearer — except when it would mean teaching a subcategory before its primary category.
