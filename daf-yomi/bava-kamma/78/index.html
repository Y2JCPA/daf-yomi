Does 'Bull or Sheep' Include or Exclude Diverse Kinds?

Gemara
The verse says 'bull or sheep' (Lev. 22:27) — two species that cannot produce kilayim. Should we include kilayim as fit for sacrifice?
Gemara
The latter clause 'or a goat' excludes — so the first clause must also exclude. Both phrases serve to exclude types from the altar.
Gemara
But why not say both include?
Gemara
If both included, the second inclusion is redundant — once kilayim is included, an animal merely resembling another species certainly should be. Two exclusions are needed: one for kilayim, one for animals resembling another species.
Logical Move The Gemara uses the principle that two exclusions make more sense than two inclusions, since the second inclusion would be logically superfluous.

Where Does Rava's Seh Principle Apply?

Rava: Wherever seh (שה) appears in Torah, it excludes kilayim
↓
Is it needed for sacrificial animals?
No
Already explicit from 'bull or sheep' (Lev. 22:27)
↓
Is it needed for animal tithe?
No
Derived via gezeira shava of 'under' (תחת) from sacrifices
↓
Is it needed for firstborn animals?
No
Derived via gezeira shava of 'passing' (העברה) from tithe
Yes!
Applies to pidyon peter chamor — redemption of firstborn donkey!
Key Conclusion Rava's principle finds its home: a firstborn donkey may NOT be redeemed with a kilayim animal (sheep-goat hybrid). Rabbi Elazar disagrees and permits it.

Stolen Bull Designated as Burnt-Offering

Rava
Dilemma: One vows a burnt-offering, sets aside a bull, and it's stolen. Can the thief repay with a sheep instead?
Rabbis
A burnt-offering vow can be fulfilled with either bull or sheep. So a sheep suffices as repayment.
R' Elazar b. Azarya
One may even fulfill a burnt-offering vow with a turtledove or pigeon! So even a bird would suffice.
Rava
But perhaps the owner can say: 'I want the mitzva done optimally' — with a bull!
Rava
Resolution: The thief can exempt himself with a sheep (Rabbis) or bird (R' Elazar b. Azarya). The vow-status, not the specific animal, defines the obligation.
Hiddush Rav Aḥa son of Rav Ika teaches this same ruling without the question-and-answer format — showing Rava reached certainty on this point.

Mishna: Cases Exempt from Fourfold/Fivefold Payment

Sold all except 1/100th✅ Pays❌ Exempt

Ripped open the animal instead of shechita✅ Pays❌ Exempt

Mishna Principle The fourfold/fivefold payment requires COMPLETE sale or VALID halakhic slaughter. Anything partial or defective triggers only the standard double payment.

What Must the Thief Retain to Be Exempt?

Rav

  • Must retain a part permitted through shechita
  • Horns and fleece do NOT count — they don't require slaughter
  • Follows R' Shimon ben Elazar (second version)

Levi

  • Even retaining the fleece alone is sufficient
  • Any retained portion makes the sale incomplete
  • Follows the first Tanna of the baraita

R' Yehuda HaNasi

  • Must retain an organ whose absence invalidates shechita
  • Examples: liver, intestines, gullet, windpipe
  • Strictest view — only vital organs count
Dispute The three-way dispute centers on how 'incomplete' a sale must be. Rav requires retaining something meaningful to slaughter; Levi accepts even fleece; Rabbi requires a vital organ.
