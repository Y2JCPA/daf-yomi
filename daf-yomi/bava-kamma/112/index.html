↓
Fed to Children
Children exempt — they received nothing tangible
Left as Inheritance
Must examine: what was left and who are the heirs
Still Extant
Must return the actual stolen item — adults or minors
Key Dispute Is the domain of an heir like the domain of a purchaser? Rami bar Ḥama says yes; Rava says no.

Is an Heir's Domain Like a Purchaser's?

Rami bar Ḥama
The domain of an heir is comparable to the domain of a purchaser — the money changes domains and the heirs acquire it.
Rava
The domain of an heir is not like a purchaser's. Interest money need not be returned because the Torah's command to return interest applies only to the lender himself, not his son.
Rava
The verse says 'that your brother may live with you' (Lev. 25:36) — the Merciful One cautioned him, not his son.
Practical Difference According to Rava, heirs keep interest money not because they 'acquired' it, but because the Torah's return obligation doesn't extend to them.

Borrowed Cow Left to Heirs — Rava's Ruling

Father borrows a cow and dies, leaving it to his children
↓
Heirs may use it for the remaining loan period
↓
What happens next?
Cow dies accidentally
Heirs are exempt — liability for unavoidable accident applies only to the actual borrower
Heirs slaughter & eat it (thinking it was theirs)
Must pay the value of inexpensive meat (בשר בזול)
If Father Left Land Dispute: Does land enable collection for accidental death of the cow, or only for slaughtering it? Two versions of Rava's statement.

Stolen Cow vs. Borrowed Cow — Slaughtered on Shabbat

Stolen Cow

  • Thief was already liable for theft before Shabbat violation
  • Slaughtering on Shabbat adds a new sin
  • Theft liability precedes the prohibition
  • Therefore: Liable to pay

Borrowed Cow

  • Borrower had lawful possession until slaughter
  • Liability and Shabbat violation occur simultaneously
  • Kim leh bid'rabbah mineh — greater punishment absorbs the lesser
  • Therefore: Exempt from payment
Rav Pappa's Principle קים ליה בדרבה מיניה — When a capital punishment and monetary liability arise at the same moment, the more severe penalty absorbs the monetary one.

Accepting Testimony in the Absence of a Litigant

Rav Ashi / R' Shabbtai
The court accepts witnesses even in the absence of a litigant.
R' Yoḥanan
The court does not accept testimony without the litigant present.
R' Yosei bar Ḥanina
Exception: If the plaintiff or witnesses are ill, or witnesses are about to travel overseas, and the defendant was summoned but did not appear.
Shmuel (via Mar Ukva)
Testimony is accepted in absentia only when the court already opened the case and sent for the defendant, but he didn't come.
Right to Decline If the case hasn't been opened yet, the defendant may say: 'I'm going to the High Court (Beit Din HaGadol)' — but not once proceedings have begun under license.
