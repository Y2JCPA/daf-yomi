Daf 4: Defining & Distinguishing the Categories of Damage

Can we derive one category from another?
E.g., Goring from Eating via kal va-chomer?
↓
Attempted Derivation
If Eating (no intent to damage) → liable, then Goring (intent to damage) → surely liable!
Rejection
Slave has intent to damage yet master is exempt — so intent alone doesn't prove liability
↓
Each category has unique features
Torah must state each one explicitly
Core Principle No single category of damage can be derived from another — each has distinguishing characteristics that require explicit Torah sources.

Why Can't We Derive Goring from Eating?

Gemara
If Eating (no intent to damage) makes the owner liable, then Goring (intent to damage) should certainly make him liable — a kal va-chomer!
Gemara
No — a slave has intent to damage, yet the master is exempt. So intent alone doesn't guarantee liability.
Rav Ashi
The slave exemption has a special reason: if the master were liable, a vengeful slave could bankrupt him by setting fires — costing 100 maneh daily!
Rav Ashi
Fundamentally, one should be liable for slave damage. The exemption is a policy safeguard, not a general principle. So we can't compare ox to slave.
Key Insight The slave exemption is pragmatic (preventing exploitation), not principled — so it cannot serve as a model for ox liability.

Eating vs. Goring: Why Neither Can Be Derived from the Other

Eating (Shen)

  • No intent to cause damage
  • Animal derives pleasure from the act
  • Forewarned from the outset (mu'ad)
  • Full damages from the first incident

Goring (Keren)

  • Intent to cause damage
  • No pleasure for the animal
  • Initially innocuous (tam)
  • Half damages until 3rd incident
Result Each category has a stringency the other lacks — neither can be derived from the other through logical inference alone.

Rava's Reading of Shmuel: Ox = Trampling, Maveh = Eating

Rav Yehuda (for Shmuel)
Ox = Goring, Maveh = Eating. But this leaves out Trampling — the tanna must include it via the general clause at the end.
Gemara
If the tanna meant to include Trampling, why not state it explicitly? This interpretation is problematic.
Rava
Rather: Ox refers to Trampling (foot damage), and Maveh refers to Eating (tooth damage). Then Goring is covered by the general clause.
Gemara
Why omit Goring? Because the mishna discusses only categories that are mu'ad from the outset — Goring starts as tam (innocuous).
Mu'ad vs. Tam The mishna explicitly lists only categories where the animal is forewarned from the start (Eating, Trampling). Goring, which begins as innocuous (tam), is covered implicitly.

Rejecting Alternative Meanings of 'Maveh'

Could Maveh = Water?
Rav Mari: 'fire boils (tiveh) water' (Isaiah 64:1)
↓
❌ Rejected: The verb tiveh is transitive — fire is the subject, not water. No proof Maveh means water.
↓
Could Maveh = Fire?
Rav Zevid: tiveh is written about fire!
↓
❌ Rejected: Mishna lists Maveh and Fire separately. Also says Maveh has a 'living spirit' — fire does not.
Conclusion Maveh cannot mean water or fire. It must refer either to Man (Rav) or Eating/Tooth (Shmuel).
