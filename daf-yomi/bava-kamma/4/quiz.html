QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does Rav Ashi say the master's exemption for slave damage cannot prove that an ox owner should also be exempt?
A. Because a slave has no intent to cause damage
B. Because the slave exemption is a pragmatic safeguard against exploitation, not a principled rule
C. Because slaves are considered property, not persons
D. Because the Torah never discusses slave damage
Correct: B — Rav Ashi explains that fundamentally a master should be liable for slave damage. The exemption exists only to prevent a vengeful slave from deliberately bankrupting his master. Since the exemption is pragmatic, it cannot serve as a model for ox liability.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rava's reading of Shmuel, why does the mishna omit Goring (Keren) from its explicit list?
A. Because Goring is not a real category of damage
B. Because Goring is included under Eating
C. Because the mishna lists only categories that are forewarned (mu'ad) from the outset, and Goring begins as innocuous (tam)
D. Because Goring only applies to humans, not animals
Correct: C — The mishna explicitly discusses only damage categories where the animal is mu'ad (forewarned) from the start. Goring begins as tam (innocuous) — the owner pays only half damages until the third incident — so it is covered by the mishna's general clause rather than listed explicitly.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does the Gemara reject Rav Zevid's suggestion that Maveh means Fire?
A. The verse he cites actually refers to water, not fire
B. The mishna lists Maveh and Fire as separate categories, and says Maveh has a 'living spirit' while Fire does not
C. Fire cannot cause damage to property
D. Maveh is an Aramaic word that only means 'man'
Correct: B — The mishna separately lists 'Maveh' and 'Fire,' implying they are distinct. Furthermore, the mishna describes Maveh as having a 'living spirit' (ruach chayyim), which fire lacks. Both points refute the identification of Maveh with Fire.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Oshaya enumerate 13 primary categories instead of the mishna's 4?
A. He disagrees with the mishna entirely
B. He includes damage caused by one's body (bailees and personal injury indemnities) in addition to damage caused by one's property
C. He counts each sub-type of ox damage separately
D. He includes categories from a different tractate
Correct: B — Rabbi Oshaya adds 4 types of bailees and 5 indemnity payments for personal injury to the mishna's 4 categories of property damage, totaling 13. The mishna (according to Shmuel) only addresses damage caused by one's property.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does the Gemara say there is no reason to split ox-caused damage into separate categories based on the victim, unlike human-caused damage?
A. Because an ox cannot intend to harm a specific type of victim
B. Because whether an ox damages property or injures a person, the owner pays only for the damage — the liability is the same
C. Because ox damage is always considered tam (innocuous)
D. Because the Torah does not distinguish between victims of ox damage
Correct: B — When a person injures another person, he pays five types of indemnity (damage, pain, medical costs, lost livelihood, humiliation). But when an ox causes damage — whether to property or a person — the owner pays only for the damage itself. Since the liability is identical regardless of victim type, there is no reason to create separate categories.
