Bava Kamma 100: Indirect Damage & The Dyer's Liability

Core Question: Is one liable for damage caused by indirect action (garmi)?
↓
Reish Lakish presents a dinar to R' Elazar for assessment — then says "I am relying on you"
↓
R' Elazar challenges: You yourself cited R' Meir as the only one who holds liability for garmi!
↓
R' Elazar's Read
R' Meir's view is rejected — no liability for indirect damage
Reish Lakish Clarifies
R' Meir's view is accepted as halakha — liability for garmi
Key Theme The Gemara searches for the precise statement of R' Meir that establishes liability for purely indirect damage — rejecting cases of direct action before finding the broken vineyard partition.

Beyond the Letter of the Law

ואת המעשה — זו שורת הדין; אשר יעשון — זו לפנים משורת הדין
Lifnim Mishurat HaDin The verse "the work that they must do" is expounded: "the work" = conducting oneself in accordance with the law; "that they must do" = conducting oneself beyond the letter of the law (lifnim mishurat hadin).

This indicates the Torah itself mandates going beyond strict legal obligation.

The Dinar Assessment Dispute

Reish Lakish
Presents a dinar to R' Elazar: "Assess this coin for me."
R' Elazar
"It is a proper coin."
Reish Lakish
"Realize that I am relying on you" — meaning: if it's bad, you must replace it.
R' Elazar
But you said R' Meir holds liability for garmi — implying we do not hold like him!
Reish Lakish
No — I meant R' Meir says this, and we DO hold in accordance with his opinion.
Practical Stakes If garmi creates liability, then an expert who negligently approves a bad coin must compensate the one who relied on that assessment.

Four Proofs for R' Meir on Garmi (דלמ"פ Mnemonic)

LetterCaseGarmi?Gemara's Verdict
ד (Dan)Judge who erred in judgment and caused monetary lossSeemingly indirectRejected — judge physically handed over the money (direct action)
ל (Litzboa)Dyer who dyed wool the wrong colorSeemingly indirectRejected — dyer directly acted on the wool
מ (Mesakekh)One who drapes a vine over another's grain (kilayim)Seemingly indirectRejected — direct physical action of draping
פ (Pirtza)Vineyard partition broke; owner abandoned repairPurely indirect — no action at allAccepted! Proves garmi liability
Key Distinction Only the broken partition case involves pure inaction/omission, making it the true proof that R' Meir holds one liable for indirect causation of damage.

The Broken Partition — True Garmi

A vineyard's partition breaks open next to a wheat field
↓
Owner of wheat tells vineyard owner: "Repair the breach!"
↓
Partition breaks again — wheat owner warns again
↓
Does the vineyard owner repair it?
Repairs
No liability — grain is saved
Abandons Repair
Grain becomes forbidden (kilayim) — vineyard owner must pay restitution
Proof The vineyard owner performed NO action — his mere failure to repair caused the damage. This is R' Meir's garmi at its purest.

Mishna: Dyer's Liability — Three Scenarios

Wool Burned in Cauldron

  • Wool completely ruined during dyeing
  • No enhancement whatsoever
  • Dyer pays the value of the wool

Dyed Unattractively (Ka'ur)

  • Dye not properly absorbed
  • If enhancement > expenses → owner pays expenses
  • If expenses > enhancement → owner pays enhancement value

Dyed Wrong Color

  • Asked for red, dyed black (or vice versa)
  • R' Meir: dyer pays value of wool
  • R' Yehuda: same rule as unattractive dye
Principle The worse the deviation from instructions, the greater the dyer's liability — from paying the lesser benefit (ka'ur) to full replacement value (wrong color per R' Meir).
