QUESTION 1 OF 5
What does the Gemara derive from the phrase 'that they must do' regarding conduct?
A. One must follow the strict letter of the law
B. One must conduct oneself beyond the letter of the law (lifnim mishurat hadin)
C. One must perform acts of kindness only when convenient
D. One must visit the sick before studying Torah
Correct: B — The Gemara expounds 'the work' as referring to the letter of the law, while 'that they must do' adds the obligation to go beyond it — lifnim mishurat hadin.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the Gemara reject the case of the erring judge as proof of R' Meir's garmi principle?
A. Because the judge is exempt from liability entirely
B. Because the judge physically took money and handed it over — a direct action
C. Because it is a rabbinic rather than biblical case
D. Because the judge acted unintentionally
Correct: B — Rav Ile'a citing Rav explains that the judge actively took money from one party and gave it to the other by hand, making it direct damage rather than indirect causation.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Which case finally proves that R' Meir holds one liable for purely indirect damage (garmi)?
A. A dyer who dyed wool the wrong color
B. A judge who erred in judgment
C. A vineyard owner who abandoned repair of a broken partition
D. One who draped a vine over another's grain
Correct: C — The broken partition is the only case where the tortfeasor performed no action at all — his mere failure to repair caused the grain to become forbidden, establishing pure garmi liability.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to R' Yehuda, if a dyer was told to dye wool red but dyed it black, what does the wool owner pay?
A. Nothing — the dyer pays the full value of the wool
B. The full cost of the dyer's expenses regardless of enhancement
C. The lesser of the enhancement value or the dyer's expenses
D. Half the value of the wool
Correct: C — R' Yehuda applies the same formula as unattractive dyeing: the owner pays whichever is less — the enhancement in the wool's value or the dyer's expenses.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the practical significance of Reish Lakish telling R' Elazar 'I am relying on you' about the dinar?
A. It was a polite expression with no legal significance
B. It established that R' Elazar must replace the dinar if it turns out to be bad
C. It meant Reish Lakish was gifting the dinar to R' Elazar
D. It created an obligation for R' Elazar to reassess the coin later
Correct: B — By declaring reliance, Reish Lakish established that if the coin assessment was wrong, R' Elazar would be liable to exchange it — an application of garmi liability for negligent expert assessment.
