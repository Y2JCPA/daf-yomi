Liability for Ox-Caused Death: Intent & Status

An ox kills a person
↓
Was the killing intentional?
Yes — Intentional
Ox is stoned; if forewarned, owner pays ransom
No — Unintentional
Shmuel: Forewarned owner pays ransom
Rav: Exempt from both
Core Issue This daf examines how intent, victim status (minor vs. adult), and ox classification (innocuous vs. forewarned) interact to determine liability for ox-caused death.

"Whether It Gored a Son or a Daughter"

אֶת־בֵּן יִגָּח אוֹ בַת יִגָּח
Biblical Source The Torah explicitly states "whether it has gored a son or has gored a daughter" (Exodus 21:31) to teach that an ox is liable for killing minors just as for adults. The repetition of "has gored" teaches this applies to both innocuous and forewarned oxen, and to both fatal and non-fatal gorings.

Why Logic Alone Can't Derive Minor-Liability

Attempted DerivationRefutation
From person killing person: liable for minors → ox should be tooA human killer pays 4 additional indemnities (pain, humiliation, medical, livelihood) — ox owner does not
From man/woman liability: no distinction innocuous/forewarned → same for boy/girlOne cannot derive a stringency for a lenient matter (innocuous) from a stringent one (forewarned)
Innocuous ox kills adults → should also apply to minorsAdults are obligated in mitzvot; minors are exempt — they have different legal standing
Conclusion Since logical derivation fails at every step, the verse "whether gored a son or daughter" is necessary — not redundant.

The Mishna's Exempt Cases

🧱 Ox rubs against wall → wall falls on person → Exempt
↓
🐑 Ox intended to kill animal → killed person instead → Exempt
↓
🎯 Ox intended to kill gentile → killed Jew → Exempt
↓
👶 Ox intended to kill non-viable baby → killed viable person → Exempt
Common Thread In all cases the ox lacked intent to perform the specific act for which it would be held liable — the death penalty requires directed intent.

Ransom for Unintentional Killing by a Forewarned Ox

Shmuel
The ox is exempt from death (no intent), but the owner is liable to pay ransom (kofer).
Rav
They are exempt from both — no death penalty for the ox and no ransom for the owner.
Gemara
But how can Shmuel impose ransom? The ox is innocuous regarding this behavior!
Gemara
Shmuel's case: the ox was forewarned for rubbing against walls. It rubbed for pleasure, not to kill — proven because it kept rubbing after the wall fell.
Baraita
A baraita supports Shmuel: forewarned ox that kills unintentionally — owner pays ransom, ox exempt from death. This is a conclusive refutation of Rav.
Key Proof The ox continued rubbing after the wall collapsed — proving its intent was self-pleasure, not killing.

R' Yehuda vs. R' Shimon: Unintentional Injury by Ox

R' Yehuda — Liable

  • Derives injury law from ransom law
  • Ransom applies even for unintentional killing → injury payment applies for unintentional goring
  • Rationale: both are monetary payments — proper to derive one from the other

R' Shimon — Exempt

  • Derives injury law from death penalty law
  • Ox exempt from death for unintentional killing → owner exempt from injury payment for unintentional goring
  • Rationale: both concern the ox's liability — ransom is the owner's liability, so it's not comparable
Methodological Dispute R' Yehuda: derive payment from payment. R' Shimon: derive ox-liability from ox-liability (excluding ransom, which is owner-liability).
