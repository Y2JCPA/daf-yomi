QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the Torah need to explicitly state that an ox is liable for killing a minor?
A. Because minors have less legal standing than adults
B. Because logical derivations from human-kills-human fail due to the extra indemnity payments a human assailant owes
C. Because minors are not considered full persons under Torah law
D. Because innocuous oxen are never liable for killing
Correct: B — The Gemara shows that you cannot derive ox liability for killing minors from the case of a human killer, because a human who kills pays four additional types of indemnity that an ox owner does not, making the logical comparison invalid.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Shmuel, when a forewarned ox rubs against a wall that collapses and kills someone, what is the liability?
A. The ox is stoned and the owner pays ransom
B. Both the ox and the owner are exempt
C. The ox is exempt from death but the owner pays ransom
D. The ox is stoned but the owner is exempt from ransom
Correct: C — Shmuel holds that since the ox did not intend to kill, it is exempt from death, but since the ox was forewarned for this behavior, the owner must still pay ransom. A baraita is cited as a conclusive refutation of Rav's opposing view.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does the Gemara prove the ox was rubbing against the wall for pleasure, not to kill?
A. Witnesses testified about the ox's state of mind
B. The ox had no prior history of aggression
C. The ox continued rubbing against the wall even after it collapsed
D. The owner testified that the ox was docile
Correct: C — The Gemara states that the proof of the ox's non-lethal intent is that it kept rubbing even after the wall fell, demonstrating that its purpose was self-pleasure, not causing harm.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the core methodological disagreement between R' Yehuda and R' Shimon regarding unintentional injury by an ox?
A. Whether injuries by oxen are ever compensable
B. R' Yehuda derives injury law from ransom (payment from payment); R' Shimon derives it from the death penalty (ox-liability from ox-liability)
C. Whether forewarned status affects injury payments
D. Whether the owner or the ox bears primary responsibility
Correct: B — R' Yehuda derives the halakha of injury payment from ransom — both are monetary obligations, so if ransom applies even for unintentional acts, so does injury payment. R' Shimon derives from the death penalty — both concern the ox's liability, and since the ox is exempt from death for unintentional killing, the owner is exempt from injury payment too.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does R' Shimon exempt an ox that intended to kill one person but killed a different person?
A. Because the ox must be forewarned specifically against the victim
B. Because the verse equates the ox's death with the owner's death — just as a person is only executed for killing the intended victim, so too the ox
C. Because unintentional killing never incurs the death penalty for an ox
D. Because the Rabbanan's view was overruled by a later baraita
Correct: B — R' Shimon derives from the juxtaposition in Exodus 21:29 ('the ox shall be stoned and its owner also shall be put to death') that just as a person is liable for capital punishment only when killing the intended victim, the same applies to the ox.
