Animal Falls Into a Garden — Liability Framework

🐄 Animal falls into another's garden
↓
Did it fall or enter on its own?
Fell (not usual manner)
Owner pays only for benefit derived (הנאה)
Descended normally
Owner pays for full damage (מה שהזיקה)
↓
Damage appraised relative to beit se'a within the larger field (1-in-60 ratio)
Key Principle When an animal accidentally enters a garden, the owner pays reduced compensation (benefit only); when it enters normally, the owner pays full damages — appraised relative to the surrounding field.

Why Isn't the Garden Owner Like One Who Repels a Lion?

Gemara
The garden produce softened the animal's fall. Shouldn't this be like matzil ari (repelling a lion) — where the benefactor gets no payment?
Gemara Answers
Distinction 1: One who repels a lion acts with intent, knowing he won't be paid. The garden owner didn't choose this — he'd prefer it never happened.
Gemara Answers
Distinction 2: The lion-repeller suffers no loss. The garden owner loses produce. He deserves compensation.
מציל ארי The 'repelling a lion' principle exempts a voluntary rescuer from payment — but only when the rescuer acts knowingly and suffers no loss.

How Did the Animal Fall Into the Garden?

Rav Kahana

  • Animal slipped on its own urine
  • Purely accidental — beyond owner's control
  • If another animal pushed it → owner was negligent
  • Owner should have led animals one by one
  • Stricter view of owner's duty of care

Rava

  • Another animal of the same owner pushed it
  • Owner pays only for benefit derived
  • If it slipped on urine → all the more so exempt from full damages
  • Kal va'chomer: urine-slip is even less the owner's fault
  • More lenient view of owner's responsibility
Practical Difference According to Rav Kahana, if another animal pushed it in, the owner pays full damages for negligence. According to Rava, even that case warrants only reduced payment.

Moving From Garden Bed to Garden Bed

Rav Kahana
If the animal moves from the bed where it fell to another bed, the owner pays full damages (מה שהזיקה) for eating in the second bed.
R' Yochanan
Even if the animal goes from bed to bed all day, the owner pays only for benefit derived — unless the animal leaves the garden entirely and returns with the owner's knowledge.
Rav Pappa
Clarification: Once the animal leaves with the owner's knowledge, even if it returns without his knowledge, he pays full damages — because the animal has now learned the path to that garden.
R' Yochanan's Logic As long as the animal never left and returned, the entire incident is treated as one accidental fall — regardless of how many beds it damages.

R' Yirmeya's Question: Animal Gives Birth in the Garden

🐄 Animal descends into garden normally and gives birth there
↓
Amniotic fluid damages the produce — is the owner liable?
If liable for negligence → accident
Clearly liable (entry was negligent, birth was accidental)
If exempt for negligence → accident
Still liable? Owner saw it was close to birth — perhaps entirely negligent?
↓
❓ Teiku — The dilemma stands unresolved
תיקו The question turns on whether seeing a pregnant animal near birth creates a heightened duty of care, making the entire incident negligence rather than accident.

How to Appraise the Damage — The 1-in-60 Ratio

AuthorityUnit of DamageAppraised Against
R' Yosei bar ChaninaBeit se'a (area for 1 se'a of seed)Area of 60 se'a
R' YannaiTarkav (½ beit se'a)Area of 60 tarkav
ChizkiyyaIndividual stalk eaten60 stalks
BaraitaSmall garden bedEvaluated as part of a larger area
בשדה אחר All opinions derive from Exodus 22:4 — 'bisde acher' — teaching that damage is appraised relative to the surrounding field, not the damaged patch alone. This reduces the per-unit cost of the damage.
