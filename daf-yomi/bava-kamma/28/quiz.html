Bava Kamma 28 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Naḥman, when may a person take justice into his own hands?
A. Only when authorized by a court
B. Whenever he is clearly in the right
C. Only to prevent physical harm to himself
D. Never — it is always prohibited
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman holds that a person may take justice into his own hands when he is clearly in the right, though the Gemara tests whether this applies even without potential loss.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In the ox-on-ox case, why is the bottom ox's owner liable if he pushed the top ox off rather than removing his own?
A. Because pushing another's ox is always prohibited
B. Because he could have protected his property in a harmless way by removing his own ox
C. Because the top ox was innocuous
D. Because the court did not authorize his action
Correct: B — The Gemara establishes that even when self-help is permitted, one must choose the least harmful method. Since he could have simply removed his own ox, pushing the other ox and causing its death makes him liable.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rav explain the difference between being liable and exempt for vessel damage caused by one's stone in the public domain?
A. It depends on whether the victim was negligent
B. It depends on whether ownership was renounced — renounced follows Pit rules (exempt), retained means liable
C. It depends on whether the stone was placed intentionally or accidentally
D. It depends on the value of the vessels
Correct: B — Rav distinguishes: if one renounced ownership of the obstacle, it is like ownerless Pit (exempt for vessels). If ownership was retained, it is his property causing damage, and he is liable for vessels too.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does Shmuel hold the owner of an obstacle is liable for personal injury but exempt for vessel damage?
A. Because personal injury is more severe
B. Because he derives stone/knife/load from Pit, and Pit law exempts vessel damage based on 'ox but not person, donkey but not vessels'
C. Because vessels can be replaced but injuries cannot
D. Because the Torah explicitly states this rule for public domain hazards
Correct: B — Shmuel applies the Pit derivation from Exodus 21:33 to all public domain obstacles: 'ox but not a person' (exempt for death of a person), 'donkey but not vessels' (exempt for vessel damage). For injury (not death) to a person, he is liable.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rabbi Elazar, in which scenario is the owner of a stone in the public domain exempt from liability for a broken flask?
A. When the pedestrian stumbled on the stone and the flask scraped against it
B. When the pedestrian stumbled on the ground and the flask fell onto the stone
C. When the flask was already cracked before the incident
D. When the stone was placed there by someone else
Correct: B — Rabbi Elazar rules that if the pedestrian stumbled on the ground (not on the stone) and the flask subsequently fell onto the stone, the stone's owner is exempt — because the stone was not the direct cause of the stumble.
