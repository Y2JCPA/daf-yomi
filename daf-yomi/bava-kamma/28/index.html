Taking Justice Into One's Own Hands & Public Domain Hazards

Rav Yehuda / Rav Naḥman
The Gemara brings multiple proofs to determine: May a person take justice into his own hands (avid inish dina l'nafshei)?
Key Question
Is self-help permitted only when one would suffer a loss, or even when there is no loss involved?
Amud B Shift
The daf transitions to obstacles in the public domain — stone, knife, or load — and whether they follow the rules of Pit (Bor).
Rav vs. Shmuel
Rav: Liable for vessel damage if ownership not renounced. Shmuel: Liable for personal injury but exempt for vessels — like Pit.
CORE TENSION Amud A asks when self-help is justified; Amud B asks who pays when hazards in public space cause harm — and whether the Pit model governs all such cases.

Can a Person Take Justice Into His Own Hands?

Rav Yehuda (in name of Rav)
A person may not take justice into his own hands — even if he is clearly in the right.
Rav Naḥman
A person may take justice into his own hands when he is clearly in the right.
Ben Bag Bag
Do not enter another's property secretly to take what is yours — lest you appear as a thief. Rather, break his teeth and say: I am taking what is mine!
Rabbi Yannai
'Break his teeth' means sue him in court — not literal force. But the Gemara notes the baraita's language doesn't fit this reading.
Gemara
Ben Bag Bag is an individual opinion; the Rabbis disagree. Rabbi Yannai's reading has textual difficulties.
KEY DISPUTE This mahloket between Rav Yehuda and Rav Naḥman drives the entire Amud A — each proof is tested against both positions.

The Ox-on-Ox Case: Removing Your Animal from Danger

🐂 An ox climbs on top of another ox, threatening to kill it
↓
The owner of the bottom ox pulls his ox away
↓
The top ox falls and dies — is the bottom ox's owner liable?
↓
Forewarned Attacker (Mu'ad)
Owner would receive full damages anyway — no loss if he waits → proof for Rav Naḥman?
Innocuous Attacker (Tam)
Owner would receive only half damages — real loss if he doesn't act → no proof
CRITICAL PRINCIPLE If one could protect his property harmlessly but instead damages another's property, he is liable — even when self-help is permitted. The bottom ox owner should have removed his ox, not pushed the attacker.

Proofs Tested for Self-Help Justice

Jugs in Courtyard

  • Someone fills another's courtyard with jugs of wine and oil
  • Owner may break through jugs to exit or enter
  • Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak: He breaks them to go to court or to bring proof documents
  • Ruling: Only permitted because refraining causes loss

Hebrew Slave at Jubilee

  • Pierced-ear slave's servitude ends at Jubilee; he refuses to leave
  • Master may use force to evict — exempt from injury liability
  • Gemara: Slave might steal now that he lacks awe of master
  • Alt: Slave wants to stay with Canaanite maidservant wife — master acts as emissary of court to prevent sin
PATTERN Each proof is reinterpreted to show the person acting in self-help faced a potential loss — thus no conclusive proof for Rav Naḥman that self-help is permitted even without loss.

The Pit (Bor) Derivation

שורו ולא אדם, חמורו ולא כלים
FOUNDATIONAL CONCEPT The Torah's laws of Pit (Bor) serve as the source for liability when one leaves obstacles in the public domain — a stone, knife, or load. The critical question: does one who leaves a hazard in public space follow all the rules of Pit (including exemption for vessel damage), or is it treated as ordinary property damage?

Rav's key distinction: Renounced ownership = like Pit (exempt for vessels). Retained ownership = his property caused damage (liable for vessels).
