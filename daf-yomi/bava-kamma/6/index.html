What Does the Common Denominator Add?

Mishna states a common denominator (צד השווה) for all damage categories
↓
What new case does this generalization come to include?
↓
Abaye
Stone/knife/load blown off roof by wind — not purely Fire or Pit
Ravina
Wall/tree that fell after court deadline — not purely Pit or Ox
↓
Both cases derived from the tzad hashaveh — the shared characteristics of two categories together
Key Concept When a damage case doesn't fit neatly into one category, the Gemara uses circular derivation (tzad hashaveh) — showing that each category's unique feature is disproven by another, until liability is established from their common ground.

Fire Beyond Consumption

לרבות שליחכה את הנירו ושליחכה את אבניו
Opening Teaching The verse about Fire includes cases where fire scorched a plowed field (שדה חרושה) or singed stones. Even though the damaged objects remain intact and are not consumed, one is still liable to pay for the damage caused.

What Does the Common Denominator Add?

Gemara
The mishna states a common denominator for all damage categories. What halakha does this come to add?
Abaye
It adds the case of one's stone, knife, or load placed on a roof that fell off by common wind and caused damage.
Gemara
But if damage occurs while in the air, that's a subcategory of Fire — another force (wind) is involved!
Gemara
And if the objects came to rest and someone stumbled — and he declared them ownerless — that's a subcategory of Pit!
Abaye
Even declared ownerless, these are not like Pit — because another force (the wind) was involved in placing them there. Pit has no such external force.
Key Distinction The case requires the tzad hashaveh because wind-blown objects share features of both Fire and Pit but fit neatly into neither.

The Circular Derivation: Pit ↔ Fire

Wind-blown objects from roof land in public domain & declared ownerless — who pays?
↓
Try to derive from Pit: damage is commonplace, your property, your responsibility
↓
❌ Challenge: Pit has no other force involved. Here, wind was involved!
↓
Try Fire instead: Fire also involves another force (wind spreads fire) — yet one is liable!
↓
❌ Challenge: Fire's nature is to proceed and spread. These objects don't naturally travel!
↓
Try Pit again: Pit doesn't travel either — yet one is liable!
↓
🔄 Derivation has reverted to its starting point — liability established from tzad hashaveh of Pit and Fire together
Method This revolving-door argument (חוזר הדין) is a classic Talmudic technique: each category's weakness is covered by the other's strength.

Why Each Category Alone Fails

Pit (בור)

  • ✅ Damage is commonplace
  • ✅ Your property & responsibility
  • ✅ Does NOT travel to cause damage
  • ❌ No other force involved in creation
  • Can't cover wind-blown objects alone

Fire (אש)

  • ✅ Another force (wind) is involved
  • ✅ Your property & responsibility
  • ✅ Damage is commonplace
  • ❌ Typical manner is to spread/travel
  • Can't cover stationary obstacles alone

Ox (שור)

  • ✅ Not initially formed to cause damage
  • ✅ Your property & responsibility
  • ❌ Typical manner is to walk and damage
  • ❌ Enters public domain with permission
  • Used by Ravina's derivation for wall/tree
Tzad HaShaveh The common denominator combines features shared by two categories to create liability even when neither alone suffices.

Ravina: The Fallen Wall and Tree

Ravina
The common denominator adds the case of a wall or tree that fell into the public domain. If the court gave a deadline to remove it and the owner didn't comply, he is liable.
Gemara
If declared ownerless, isn't this just Pit?
Ravina
No — Pit's initial formation (digging) is done in a way that causes damage. A wall and tree are initially built/planted properly!
Gemara
Then derive from Ox — an ox is also not born to cause damage!
Gemara
But Ox walks and causes damage — walls and trees don't travel!
Ravina
So Pit proves that non-traveling things cause liability. The derivation cycles between Pit and Ox → tzad hashaveh!
Ravina's Case A wall/tree fallen after court deadline: derived from Pit + Ox together, since neither category alone covers all features.
