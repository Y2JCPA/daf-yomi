Entering an Area of Danger

R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov
If a stone left one's hand and another person stuck out his head and was struck — the thrower is exempt from exile. The victim introduced himself into danger.
R' Yosei bar Ḥanina
He is exempt from exile — but if the victim was only injured, the thrower must pay four types of indemnity.
Rav Zevid / Rava
This teaching of R' Yosei bar Ḥanina refers to the case of one who stuck his head out — not the carpenter's workshop case.
Gemara
One who teaches it regarding this case would certainly apply it to the workshop case. But one who teaches it regarding the workshop would not apply it here — here the thrower is completely blameless.
Key Distinction Exemption from exile does not automatically mean exemption from monetary liability for injuries.

Laborers Injured in Employer's Courtyard

Laborers come to employer's courtyard to claim wages
Ox gored them or dog bit them
↓
Can the employer be found in town?
Always in town
Laborers should have met him there — all agree employer exempt
Only at home
Laborers entitled to enter — all agree employer liable
↓
Middle case: Sometimes in town, sometimes not
Laborers called at the gate; he said "Hen" (Yes)
↓
First Tanna
"Hen" = Stand in your place, I'll come out to you → Exempt
Others
"Hen" = Come in → He gave permission → Liable
Ruling A baraita supports the first tanna: even one who entered 'with permission' is exempt — because 'hen' means 'wait there.'

Can the Owner Sell the Belligerent Ox?

Rava
If the ox is liened to the injured party, can the owner sell it?
Rav Naḥman
It is not sold — the ox is liened to the injured party.
Rava
But a baraita teaches: if he sold it, it is sold!
Rav Naḥman
The injured party then collects it from the purchaser.
Gemara
If the injured party collects it back, in what sense is it sold? — It is sold for ridya (plowing) until collection.
Publicity Principle A goring ox generates publicity (like a slave rendered as designated payment), so the purchaser is presumed to know about the lien — and the injured party can collect from him.

R' Akiva vs. R' Yishmael: Who Owns the Belligerent Ox?

R' Akiva

  • The injured party has a share of ownership in the ox itself
  • If owner sold it → not sold (already partially belongs to injured party)
  • If injured party consecrated it → consecrated (he has ownership stake)
  • Ox is like shared property

R' Yishmael

  • The ox is liened to the debt but still belongs to the owner
  • Injured party has no ownership until court appraisal
  • If owner sold it → technically sold, but injured party collects from buyer
  • Ox is like collateral for a debt
Resolution Both agree: if the owner sold it, the injured party can still collect. If he consecrated it, he must redeem it — per R' Abbahu's decree, lest people say consecrated property leaves the Temple without redemption.
