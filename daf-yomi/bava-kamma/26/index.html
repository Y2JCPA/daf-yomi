Bava Kamma 26: Liability Boundaries Across Damage Categories

Core Question: Can we use kal va-chomer to extend or reduce liability across damage categories?
↓
Extend Liability?
Eating/Trampling in public domain? → Rejected by verse limitations
Reduce Liability?
Half-damage for Eating/Trampling on private property? → Rejected by "best of his field"
↓
Conclusion: Each category has its own Torah-defined scope — kal va-chomer cannot override explicit verses
↓
Rabba's Stone Cases: Graduated awareness → graduated liability across damages, exile, Shabbat, and slave law

Kal Va-Chomer #1: Eating/Trampling in Public Domain?

Argument: If Goring (half-damage) applies in public domain, shouldn't Eating/Trampling (full damage — stricter) also apply there?
↓
Full Damage?
Rejected: "The field of another" limits Eating/Trampling to private property
Half Damage?
Rejected: "And divide its monetary value" — only this ox (Goring), not others
Key Verse "The field of another" (Exodus 22:4) — Eating and Trampling liability is limited to the injured party's property

Kal Va-Chomer #2: Only Half-Damage for Eating/Trampling?

Questioner
If Goring — liable even in public domain — still only pays half damage on private property, then Eating/Trampling (exempt in public domain, i.e. more lenient) should also pay only half!
Gemara
The verse states: "The best of his field and the best of his vineyard he shall pay" (Exodus 22:4) — this means full, complete payment, not half.
Questioner
Then reverse it: If Eating/Trampling (full damage on private property) is exempt in public domain, Goring (only half damage) should also be exempt in public domain!
R' Yochanan
"And the carcass they shall also divide" (Exodus 21:35) — half-damage for Goring applies whether in public domain or private property.
Principle Each a fortiori inference is blocked by a specific verse — the Torah deliberately defines each category's scope

Ransom vs. Four Indemnities: Person vs. Ox Owner

Ox Owner

  • Liable for ransom (kofer) if ox kills
  • NOT liable for four types of indemnity if ox injures
  • Source: "A man maims another" — a man, not an ox

Person Who Kills/Injures

  • NOT liable for ransom if he kills
  • LIABLE for four types of indemnity if he injures
  • Source: "Upon him" — upon ox owner, not upon a person who kills
Why Not Kal Va-Chomer? Each attempt to extend one's liability via a fortiori is blocked by a limiting verse — the Torah keeps these categories distinct

Chizkiyya's Derivation: Unintentional = Intentional for Damages

פצע תחת פצע
Source "Wound for wound" (petza taḥat patza, Exodus 21:25) — The word taḥat is superfluous. It teaches two things:

1️⃣ One pays for pain even when also paying for damage
2️⃣ One is liable for unintentional damage just as for intentional, and for accidental just as for willful
