Returning Stolen Property: Key Decision Points

Item stolen or borrowed
↓
Was the theft known to the owner?
Yes — Owner Knew
Rav: Must return with owner's knowledge
Shmuel: Counting the flock suffices
No — Owner Unaware
Rav Ḥisda: Still needs owner's knowledge (lamb learned to wander)
R' Yoḥanan: Return to place suffices
Key Theme This daf explores when returning stolen property is effective — focusing on the owner's awareness, the location of return, and whether uncertainty in claims creates liability.

Mishna: Returning Items — Settled vs. Unsettled Area

Robbery / Loan / Deposit in Settled Area

  • Must return in a settled area
  • Owner needs to safeguard the item
  • Cannot force return in unsettled area

With Prior Stipulation

  • If agreed in advance, may return in unsettled area
  • E.g., depositor said 'I'm going to the wilderness' and bailee said 'So am I'
  • Explicit condition overrides the default
Abaye's Clarification A loan may be claimed anywhere, but the borrower cannot force the creditor to accept repayment in an unsettled area. A deposit and lost item may be claimed only in the type of place where they were received.

Certain Claim vs. Uncertain Claim: Who Pays?

Plaintiff
"You owe me 100 dinars!" — a certain claim (טענת ברי)
Defendant
"I don't know if that's true" — an uncertain claim (טענת שמא)
Rav Huna & Rav Yehuda
Liable to pay — the certain claim is superior (ברי עדיף)
Rav Naḥman & R' Yoḥanan
Exempt — העמד ממון בחזקת בעליו: establish money in possession of its current holder. No payment without clear proof.
Resolution R' Ḥiyya bar Abba in the name of R' Yoḥanan: the defendant is liable only if he comes to fulfill his obligation to Heaven (לצאת ידי שמים), not by force of law.

Mishna: 'I Don't Know' — Two Cases of Uncertainty

ScenarioAdmissionRuling
Case 1"I robbed you / you lent me / you deposited with me, and I don't know if I returned it"Liable — admits the obligation existed
Case 2"I don't know if I robbed you / you lent me / you deposited with me"Exempt — never admitted the obligation
Distinction In Case 1, the obligation is certain and the return is uncertain. In Case 2, the entire obligation is uncertain — so he cannot be forced to pay.

Stolen Lamb Returned to Flock: Is the Thief Exempt?

🐑 Thief steals lamb and returns it to the flock without telling the owner
↓
Did the owner know about the theft?
Owner Knew
Rav: Must inform owner of return
Shmuel: Counting the flock suffices
Owner Didn't Know
Counting the flock = exempt
(Rav & Shmuel agree)
↓
Rav Ḥisda disagrees: Even if owner didn't know, must still inform — because the thief taught the lamb to wander outside, so it needs extra supervision
Rav's Concession Rav admits that returning a lamb to a flock in an unsettled area is valid — but only for a spotted lamb (בירכואתא), since distinctive markings make the owner immediately aware.

R' Yishmael vs. R' Akiva: Returning Stolen Items

R' Yishmael
One who stole a lamb from a flock or a sela from a purse must return it to the place from where he stole it — that suffices.
R' Akiva
The thief must return it with the knowledge of the owners (לדעת הבעלים).
Rav Zevid / Rava
A bailee who stole from the owner's domain: everyone agrees he must return with owner's knowledge.
Rav Zevid / Rava
Their dispute is about a bailee who stole from his own domain: R' Akiva says his safeguarding was terminated; R' Yishmael says it was not terminated.
Key Distinction R' Akiva: Once the bailee stole, his status as guardian ended — he must actively re-establish the owner's awareness. R' Yishmael: The item never left his domain, so his guardianship continues.
