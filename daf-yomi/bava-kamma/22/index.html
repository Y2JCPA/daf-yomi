The Great Fire Debate: Arrows or Property?

Gemara
What is the legal basis for liability when fire spreads and causes damage at a distance?
R' Yochanan
Fire liability is due to its similarity to his arrows (isho mishum ḥitzav) — it is an extension of his direct force.
Reish Lakish
Fire liability is due to its similarity to his property (isho mishum mamono) — like an ox that wanders and damages.
Reish Lakish
Fire isn't like an arrow — an arrow proceeds by direct force, but fire requires wind to carry it.
R' Yochanan
Fire isn't like property — property is a tangible substance, but fire is not tangible.
Rava
A verse supports R' Yochanan: "If fire breaks out... the one who ignited shall pay" (Exodus 22:5) — implying both autonomous spread and human agency.
Core Issue This debate has practical consequences for cases of indirect fire damage — when a dog carries a coal, when fire is given to a minor, and when fire kills a slave.

The Dog, the Cake, and the Coal

🐕 Dog takes a cake baked on coals from someone's house
↓
Dog carries cake (with attached coal) to a grain stack
↓
Cake Damage
Dog eats the cake — direct damage by animal
→ Owner pays full damages
Fire Damage
Coal ignites the grain stack — indirect fire damage
→ Owner pays half damages (pebbles)
Key Distinction The cake damage is direct (Tooth). The fire damage is indirect — like pebbles (tzerorot), an atypical consequence of the dog's action.

How Does Each View Explain the Dog-Cake Mishna?

R' Yochanan
The dog placed the cake with coal on the stack. Full payment for the cake + spot. Half payment for the rest of the stack — fire = arrows of the dog, indirect force = pebbles.
Reish Lakish
The dog threw the coal onto the stack. Full payment for cake. Half for the spot only (pebbles). Exempt for the rest — the fire (property) wasn't his property.
Gemara
R' Yochanan's reading is more straightforward — it doesn't require the unusual detail of the dog throwing the coal.
Practical Difference Under R' Yochanan: liable for the entire stack (half damages). Under Reish Lakish: liable only for the spot the coal landed (half damages), exempt for the spread.

Camel Laden with Flax — Fire in the Public Domain

🐪 Camel loaded with flax passes through the public domain
↓
🕯️ Flax extends into a store and catches fire from the storekeeper's lamp
↓
🔥 Burning flax ignites the entire building
↓
📋 If lamp was inside the store → Camel owner is liable (shouldn't have overloaded)
↓
📋 If lamp was outside in public domain → Storekeeper is liable (shouldn't have placed lamp there)
↓
🕎 Exception: R' Yehuda says if it was a Hanukkah lamp, storekeeper is exempt — mitzva to place it outside
Note Both views (arrows and property) can explain this mishna — the Gemara finds it inconclusive for resolving the debate.

Slave Tied to a Stack — Death Penalty vs. Payment

Case 1: Goat Tied, Slave Free

  • One ignites a grain stack
  • Goat is tied to the stack → burned
  • Slave is nearby but free → should have fled
  • Liable for stack + goat damage
  • Exempt for slave (slave could escape)

Case 2: Slave Tied, Goat Free

  • One ignites a grain stack
  • Slave is tied → burned = murder
  • Subject to death penalty
  • Completely exempt from all monetary payment
  • Even to the goat's owner (different person)
Principle Kim leih bid'rabbah mineih — when one act triggers both death penalty and monetary liability, only the greater punishment (death) applies, even if the monetary claim belongs to a different person.
