Can a Stolen Item Be Consecrated?

An item is stolen and the owner has not yet despaired (ye'ush)
↓
Can either party consecrate (hekdesh) the item?
The Thief?
❌ Cannot consecrate — it doesn't belong to him
The Owner?
❌ Cannot consecrate — it's not in his possession
↓
Supported by verse: "When a man shall sanctify his house" — only items in one's possession can be consecrated (Lev. 27:14)
R' Yochanan's Principle Before ye'ush, a stolen item exists in legal limbo — neither thief nor owner has sufficient jurisdiction to consecrate it.

Court Ruling: Thief or Robber?

Rava
If the court issues a definitive ruling ordering the thief to pay, and he then slaughters or sells the animal — he does not pay fourfold or fivefold.
Rava
Why? Because after a court ruling, he is reclassified as a gazlan (robber), not a ganav (thief). The fourfold/fivefold payment applies only to a ganav.
Rava
But if the court merely says "You are obligated to return it" — without a definitive ruling — he still pays fourfold or fivefold.
Rava
Since the court hasn't issued a final ruling, he retains his status as a ganav (thief).
Key Distinction A definitive court order transforms a thief (ganav) into a robber (gazlan), changing the applicable penalties.

R' Yochanan: Neither Can Consecrate a Stolen Item

The Thief (Ganav)

  • Possesses the item physically
  • ❌ Cannot consecrate it
  • Reason: It does NOT belong to him
  • Lacks legal ownership (kinyan)

The Owner (Ba'al)

  • Retains legal ownership
  • ❌ Cannot consecrate it
  • Reason: It is NOT in his possession
  • Verse: 'his house' = in his domain
R' Yochanan Before ye'ush (despair), a stolen item is in legal limbo — ownership without possession cannot effect consecration.

The Practice of the Pious Ones (Chasidim)

🍇 Pious vineyard owners had fourth-year grapes that passersby might steal
↓
💰 They set aside coins and made a declaration
↓
📜 Declaration: 'Anything picked from this vine shall be desacralized onto these coins'
↓
✅ This protected even thieves from eating unredeemed sacred produce
↓
⚡ Implication: An owner CAN affect produce no longer in his possession
↓
❓ This seems to contradict R' Yochanan's ruling!
Motivation The pious ones acted beyond the letter of the law — they didn't want even sinners to stumble by eating unredeemed fourth-year produce.

Does R' Yochanan Accept Retroactive Designation (Bereirah)?

Gemara suggests R' Yochanan reformulated the pious ones' declaration: "Anything that WILL BE picked..." (future tense)
↓
This would mean desacralization occurs before the grapes leave the owner's possession
↓
But this relies on bereirah (retroactive designation) — does R' Yochanan accept it?
Evidence Against
R' Yochanan says brothers dividing inheritance are like purchasers — not retroactively designated. He rejects bereirah!
Conclusion
The Gemara retracts the reformulation. The pious ones said "was picked" (past tense) after all.
Resolution R' Yochanan found a different unattributed mishna (about double payment for stolen-from-a-thief) that supports his view and chose it over the pious ones' practice.
