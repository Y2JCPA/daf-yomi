Testifying Against a Jew in Gentile Court

A Jew testifies against another Jew in a gentile court
↓
How many witnesses?
One Witness
We excommunicate him — gentile courts expropriate money on one witness's word (unlike halakha)
Two Witnesses
Not excommunicated — sufficient evidence even by Jewish law
↓
For a single witness: Was it in a bei davar (official courthouse)?
Bei Davar
Not excommunicated — they only impose an oath, don't expropriate money
Village Court (Demagista)
Excommunicated — they expropriate money based on one witness
Unresolved What about an important person (adam chashuv) whose word carries the weight of two witnesses? The Gemara leaves this teiku — unresolved.

The Lion on My Border

ארי אתה מרביע על גבולי
Rav Ashi One who sells land to a gentile bordering a fellow Jew's property is excommunicated. Not because of bar metzra (right of first refusal) — that doesn't apply to gentile transactions. Rather: "You have placed a lion on my border" — the gentile neighbor may cause harm. He remains excommunicated until he accepts responsibility for all damages the neighbor suffers.

The Mishna: Replaced Items from Thieves

Customs Collector

  • Took your donkey, gave you another
  • Considered a robber (gazlan) — takes openly
  • Owner presumed to have despaired
  • Replacement item is yours to keep

Bandits (Listim)

  • Took your garment, gave you another
  • Considered a thief (ganav) — takes covertly
  • Owner also presumed to have despaired
  • Replacement item is yours to keep

River / Troop Salvage

  • Items salvaged from flood, army, or bandits
  • If owners despaired — finder keeps them
  • Same principle of ye'ush applies
  • Includes swarms of bees
Key Rule Ownership transfers when the original owner despairs (מתייאש) of recovering the item.

Does Ye'ush Alone Effect Acquisition?

Baraita
If he took a donkey from the customs collector, he must return it to the original owners.
First Interpretation
This proves ye'ush alone does NOT effect acquisition — the customs collector never acquired it, so the recipient holds it illegally.
Second Interpretation
No — the baraita means only if he voluntarily wants to go beyond the letter of the law (lifnim mishurat hadin). Legally it's his.
Rav Ashi
The Mishna's rule of presumed ye'ush applies only to a gentile bandit. With a Jewish bandit, the owner thinks: 'I'll take him to court!'
Rav Yosef
The opposite! Gentile courts enforce with power — no despair. Jewish courts only pronounce verdicts — more reason to despair!
Tension The strength of the court system determines whether an owner despairs: strong enforcement = less despair.

Swarm of Bees — Rights & Limits

ScenarioHalakha
Bees relocated to another's fieldOwner may enter the field to retrieve them
Caused damage entering the fieldMust pay for damages
Bees settled on a tree branchMay not cut the branch (Tanna Kamma)
R' Yishmael b. R' Yochanan b. BerokaMay cut the branch and pay compensation
Who can testify where bees came from?Even a woman or minor is believed (R' Yochanan b. Beroka)
Dispute Can you damage another's property to recover your own, if you pay? Tanna Kamma: No. R' Yishmael b"r Yochanan: Yes.
