Does Change (Shinui) Affect Legal Status?

A robber steals an item and it undergoes a physical change
↓
Does shinui (change) cause the robber to acquire the item?
Multiple Tanna'im: No
Changed item remains in its place — still belongs to victim
Mishna's Rule
Robber pays monetary value at time of theft — Takanat HaShavim
Core Tension By Torah law, changed stolen items should be returned as-is. The Sages instituted that the robber acquires the changed item and pays money — to encourage repentance.

Harlot's Hire After Change (Etnan Zonah)

Beit Shammai
The word "even" (gam) in Deut. 23:19 is an amplification — it includes the changed form of the harlot's hire in the prohibition.
Beit Hillel
The word "these" (eileh) is an exclusion — the prohibition applies only to the original form, not after a change.
Gemara
But Beit Shammai — the word "these" is also written! What do you do with it?
Beit Shammai
"These" excludes offspring of the animal — the hire itself is forbidden, but not its progeny.
Beit Hillel
We derive two halakhot from "these": not the changed form, and not the offspring.
Gemara
And the word "even"? Indeed, it is difficult for Beit Hillel.
Key Verse "You shall not bring the hire of a harlot or the price of a dog into the House of the Lord" (Deut. 23:19)

R' Shimon ben Elazar: Robber Enhanced the Stolen Animal

Robber steals an animal and it changes in value
↓
Was the animal enhanced or weakened?
Enhanced
Robber takes his enhancement — victim pays the difference between theft-value and current value
Weakened
Robber says: "That which is yours is before you" — no further compensation required
↓
Why does the robber get enhancement compensation if he never acquired the item?
↓
Takanat HaShavim — Ordinance for the penitent, so robbers won't refrain from returning stolen items
Rav Sheshet Rav Sheshet clarified: the robber's advantage only applies to enhancement, not weakening. The underlying principle is that change doesn't transfer ownership.

R' Yoḥanan on Returning Changed Stolen Items

R' Ḥiyya bar Abba / R' Yoḥanan
By Torah law, a changed stolen item is returned as-is: "He shall restore that which he took by robbery" (Lev. 5:23) — in any case.
Gemara
But our Mishna says the robber pays monetary compensation for changed items!
R' Yoḥanan
That was a rabbinic enactment — Takanat HaShavim — to encourage repentance.
Gemara
But R' Yoḥanan follows unattributed mishnayot, and one says dyeing wool exempts from first-shearing obligations — change does acquire!
R' Ya'akov
R' Yoḥanan explained to me directly: he refers to sanded wood → vessels, a reversible change. That's why Torah law doesn't grant acquisition.
Resolution R' Yoḥanan's ruling applies to reversible changes (shinui ha-chozer). Irreversible changes may indeed transfer ownership even by Torah law.

The Story of the Thief Who Wanted to Repent

🦹 A man who had been a thief for many years desired to repent
↓
👩 His wife said: "Reika! (Empty one!) If you repent, you must return everything — even your belt isn't yours!"
↓
😔 He refrained and did not repent
↓
📜 The Sages enacted: If robbers or usurers return stolen items or interest, do not accept from them
↓
🚫 One who accepts from them — the Sages are displeased with him, as it discourages repentance
Takanat HaShavim This enactment from the days of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi removed barriers to repentance — if victims must accept returns, thieves will never repent.
