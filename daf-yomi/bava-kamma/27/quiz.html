5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabba, if a man falls from a roof due to an atypical wind and causes damage, what must he pay?
A. All five types of indemnity
B. Damage only
C. Four indemnities (excluding humiliation)
D. Nothing — it was a complete accident
Correct: B — An atypical wind is completely unforeseeable, so he is liable only for the direct damage (nezek) but exempt from the four additional indemnities (pain, medical, livelihood, humiliation), since he had no negligence in falling.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does R' Ulla say a person who stumbles on a barrel in the public road is exempt?
A. Because the road was dark
B. Because it was at a corner
C. Because people do not normally examine the roads as they walk
D. Because the barrel owner gave implicit permission to break it
Correct: C — R' Ulla's reasoning is that the typical manner of people is not to examine roads while walking — they assume the path is clear. Therefore, one who stumbles is not considered negligent.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
In the dispute about self-help justice, when do Rav Yehuda and Rav Nachman agree that a person may take the law into his own hands?
A. When the other party refuses to come to court
B. When there is imminent, irreversible loss
C. When the person has witnesses
D. They never agree — it is a complete dispute
Correct: B — Even Rav Yehuda, who generally forbids self-help, agrees that when there is imminent loss (hefsed) that cannot be recovered if one waits for court, a person may act on his own — as in the case of someone stealing water from a shared cistern.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why is the one who places a coal on another's garment liable, but one who places it on their heart exempt?
A. A garment cannot remove itself but a person can remove the coal from their chest
B. Garment damage is more severe than bodily harm
C. The garment owner was not present to consent
D. Placing coal on a heart is not considered a direct act of damage
Correct: A — The victim should have removed the coal from his own chest — his failure to do so breaks the chain of liability. But a garment owner might deliberately leave the coal, planning to sue for damages, so the placer remains liable for the garment.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is Rav Pappa's objection to Rav's explanation that the mishna refers to filling the entire road with barrels?
A. It contradicts a verse in the Torah
B. If the whole road is blocked, even intentional breaking should be exempt — so why does the mishna specify 'stumbled'?
C. Rav's explanation only works in a narrow alleyway
D. The mishna explicitly says it was a single barrel
Correct: B — Rav Pappa argues that if the entire road was blocked, breaking the barrels intentionally should also be exempt — the owner had no right to obstruct the public domain. So the mishna's specific mention of 'stumbling' is imprecise under Rav's reading. Rav Zevid resolves this by explaining the mishna chose 'stumbled' for the second clause about injury liability.
