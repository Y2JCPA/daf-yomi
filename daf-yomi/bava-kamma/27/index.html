Bava Kamma 27: Unintended Harm & Public Domain Liability

Core Question: When does unintended or indirect harm create liability?
↓
Falling & Damage
Liability depends on foreseeability of the fall and intent to land on victim
Vessels in Road
One who stumbles on objects in public domain is generally exempt
Self-Help
Taking justice into own hands when imminent loss is at stake
↓
Principle: Liability tracks intent and negligence — not merely causation
Key Theme This daf explores the boundaries between accident, negligence, and intent across damages, humiliation, and self-defense of property.

A person falls from a roof and causes damage while falling
↓
Atypical Wind
Liable for damage only
Exempt from 4 indemnities
(Completely unforeseeable)
Typical Wind
Liable for 4 indemnities
(damage, pain, medical, livelihood)
Exempt from humiliation
Tumbled Intentionally onto Person
Liable for all 5 payments
including boshet (humiliation)
(Intended to land on them)
Rabba's Principle Humiliation (boshet) requires intent to strike — even if one didn't intend to shame, intending to land on someone creates liability for boshet.

Vessels in the Public Domain: Why Is the Stumbler Exempt?

Rav
The mishna refers to one who filled the entire road with barrels. Since the public domain belongs to everyone, the pedestrian may traverse even if it means breaking vessels.
Shmuel
The mishna refers to darkness — the pedestrian could not see the barrel and therefore could not avoid it.
R' Yochanan
The barrel was placed at a corner — the pedestrian rounded the bend and could not see it before stumbling.
R' Ulla
Even if the barrel is clearly visible, the stumbler is exempt — people do not normally examine roads as they walk, assuming the path is clear.
Practical Difference According to R' Ulla, any obstacle left in a public road exempts the stumbler — the one who placed it bears responsibility.

Coal on Heart vs. Coal on Garment

Coal on Heart

  • Victim is burned and dies
  • Placer is EXEMPT from liability
  • Reason: victim should have removed the coal himself
  • Similar to pushing someone near fire where they can escape (Sanhedrin 76b)

Coal on Garment

  • Garment is burned and destroyed
  • Placer is LIABLE for the damage
  • Reason: victim may have refrained from removing it, planning to sue for damages
  • Similar to: 'Break my jug' — permission doesn't equal exemption (92a)
Rava's Insight Both rulings are hinted at in existing mishnayot — the coal cases map onto established principles about a victim's duty to mitigate harm.

Self-Help Justice (Avid Inish Dina L'Nafshei)

Rav Yehuda
A person may not take justice into his own hands. He must go to beit din (court) to resolve disputes.
Rav Nachman
A person may take justice into his own hands when he is in the right.
Rav Chisda
A man struck someone stealing his cistern water with a hoe handle. What does he owe for humiliation?
Rav Nachman
He was right to do so! He could have hit him a hundred times. When there is imminent loss (פסידא), even Rav Yehuda agrees one may act.
Agreed Rule Where there is imminent, irreversible loss — like water being stolen in real time — ALL agree a person may take the law into his own hands to prevent it.
