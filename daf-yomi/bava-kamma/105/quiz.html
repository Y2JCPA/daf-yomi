Bava Kamma 105 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava, why is the heir exempt from paying the one-fifth on his father's stolen item?
A. Because the heir swore truthfully — he didn't know his father had stolen the item
B. Because heirs never pay the one-fifth on inherited debts
C. Because the one-fifth only applies to land, not movable property
D. Because the robbery victim forgave the additional payment
Correct: A — Rava explains that the father's sack (disakaya) was deposited with others, so when the heir swore the item wasn't in his possession, he swore truthfully since he didn't know about the theft.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In Rava's case of three bundles that depreciated from 3 perutot to 2, why must the robber return the third bundle?
A. Because the victim never forgave the debt
B. Because at the time of robbery, each bundle was worth a peruta and retains its obligation
C. Because returning only two bundles is considered an insult
D. Because the bundles might appreciate in value again
Correct: B — Rava's principle is that the original value at the time of robbery governs the obligation. Since each bundle was originally worth a peruta, the duty to return persists even after depreciation.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
If a robber stole two bundles worth one peruta total and returned one, what is Rava's ruling?
A. He must return the second bundle to fulfill the mitzva
B. He must pay the monetary difference in cash
C. He is exempt from returning the second, but gets no mitzva credit for returning either
D. He must pursue the victim to return the second bundle
Correct: C — Rava resolves that since the remaining bundle is worth less than a peruta, there is no 'stolen item' — but since the returned bundle was also less than a peruta, there is no fulfillment of the mitzva of returning stolen property either.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rabba, when a robber falsely swears he is an unpaid bailee rather than admitting to theft, why is he liable for a guilt-offering?
A. Because he lied about the identity of the owner
B. Because claiming bailee status exempts him from liability for theft and loss, denying a monetary claim
C. Because unpaid bailees have no right to possess the item
D. Because the oath itself is a form of theft
Correct: B — By claiming to be an unpaid bailee instead of a robber, the defendant exempts himself from liability in cases of theft and loss. This constitutes denial of a monetary matter, triggering the guilt-offering obligation.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Rava reconcile his ruling about bailee oaths with the baraita that exempts a robber who claims bailee status?
A. He says the baraita is from an earlier, superseded tradition
B. He says the baraita refers to cases where the robber immediately presents the item ('Here you are'), while his ruling is when the item is elsewhere
C. He says the baraita only applies to paid bailees
D. He says the baraita is discussing a different type of oath
Correct: B — Rava explains that the baraita's exemption applies only when the robber says 'Here you are' and returns the ox immediately. His own ruling addresses cases where the ox is in a different location (like a swamp) and not yet returned.
