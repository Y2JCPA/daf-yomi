Returning Robbery Proceeds & the Role of Agents

Robber wants to return stolen item
Must it reach the victim directly?
Took False Oath
Money must reach victim's hands for atonement (kaparah)
No False Oath
Money treated as deposit (pikadon) with robber until victim collects
↓
Can the robber use an agent?
Rav Ḥisda vs. Rabba debate on agent appointed before witnesses
Rav Ḥisda
Agent is legally recognized — payment to agent = payment to creditor
Rabba
Agent is not legally recognized — debtor responsible until money reaches creditor
KEY THEME This daf explores when payment through intermediaries satisfies legal obligations — especially where atonement or legal certainty is at stake.

Rava's Resolution: False Oath vs. No Oath

Rava
The mishna works for both R' Tarfon and R' Akiva. Since the robber knows whom he robbed and admitted it, the victim effectively says: 'Keep my money as a deposit.'
Rava
But when the robber took a false oath (shevu'at sheker), he needs kaparah (atonement) — so the money must physically reach the victim's hands.
Rava
Without a false oath, the stolen money simply sits as a pikadon (deposit) with the robber until the owner comes to collect it.
PRINCIPLE A false oath transforms a civil debt into a matter requiring spiritual atonement, raising the standard for proper return.

Agent Appointed Before Witnesses

Rav Ḥisda

  • Agent appointed before witnesses IS a legal agent (shali'aḥ)
  • Payment to agent = payment to creditor
  • Debtor is released from responsibility
  • Reasoning: the creditor took the trouble to appoint him publicly — placing the matter under the agent's domain

Rabba

  • Agent appointed before witnesses is NOT a legal agent
  • Debtor responsible until money reaches creditor
  • Reasoning: creditor is merely saying 'this person is trustworthy — IF you choose to rely on him, that's your risk'
  • The agent is a recommendation, not a legal appointment
PRACTICAL IMPACT If money is lost after being given to the agent, Rav Ḥisda exempts the debtor; Rabba holds the debtor still liable.

Challenge to Rav Ḥisda from Bava Metzia

Mishna (BM 98b): One borrows a cow, owner sends it via borrower's agent — cow dies en route
↓
Borrower is exempt!
But if the agent was appointed with witnesses...
↓
Per Rav Ḥisda, agent = legal agent → borrower should be liable!
This contradicts him!
↓
Rav Ḥisda's answer:
That mishna discusses a hired hand (sachir) or regular harvester (leket) — no formal witness-based appointment
RESOLUTION Rav Ḥisda limits the contradicting mishnayot to cases of informal agents (hired workers), preserving his rule about formally appointed agents.

Sending Payment via a Sign (Deyokani)

Shmuel
A debtor may not send money with someone carrying a deyokani (identifying sign/figure) of the creditor — even if witnesses verify the sign.
R' Yoḥanan
If witnesses are signed on the deyokani verifying it belongs to the creditor, one may send the money with the bearer.
Gemara
According to Shmuel, what recourse does a creditor have? The method of R' Abba: transfer the debt rights via land acquisition to a third party who can collect directly.
DEYOKANI A deyokani was an identifying mark or seal used in place of a signature — an early form of credentialing for financial transactions.

R' Abba's Debt Collection Method

📋 R' Abba is owed money by Rav Yosef bar Ḥama in a distant locale
↓
🗣️ R' Abba tells Rav Safra: 'When you return from there, bring my money'
↓
⚠️ Rava (Rav Yosef's son) objects: 'Where is the receipt? What if R' Abba dies and heirs inherit the debt?'
↓
💡 Rava's solution: R' Abba should transfer the debt rights to Rav Safra via land acquisition (kinyan)
↓
📝 Then Rav Safra writes a receipt, collects payment, and delivers it
↓
✅ Rav Pappa used this same method to collect 12,000 dinars from Bei Ḥozai via Rav Shmuel bar Abba
TAKEAWAY Transferring debt rights via kinyan (land acquisition) creates a clean legal chain that protects both debtor and creditor — even if the original creditor dies.
