QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Ashi, why is someone who fans a fire with wind assistance exempt from damages but liable for Shabbat violation?
A. Shabbat prohibits planned labor where the objective is met; damages requires direct causation, and this is only indirect (grama)
B. Shabbat has stricter penalties so the standard of proof is lower
C. Fire damage is always considered an act of God, unlike Shabbat violations
D. The exemption only applies when the wind is stronger than the fanning
Correct: A — Rav Ashi distinguishes between Shabbat (where melekhet maḥshevet — planned constructive labor — makes one liable even with wind assistance) and damages (where indirect causation, grama be-nizakin, results in exemption).
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the Torah need to mention both 'thorns' and 'stack of grain' in the fire verse (Exodus 22:5)?
A. Thorns are flammable and grain is not, showing liability applies to both
B. Thorns teach liability for minimal-value items; grain stack teaches liability even when fire is not commonly near the item
C. Thorns represent wild growth and grain represents cultivated crops
D. The Torah is listing items in order of increasing value
Correct: B — Without 'thorns,' we'd think liability only applies to substantial losses (like grain). Without 'grain stack,' we'd think liability only applies where fire is commonly nearby (like thorns). Each term prevents an incorrect limitation.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the Talmud's advice when there is plague in the city versus famine?
A. Plague: leave the city; Famine: stay indoors and ration food
B. Both: go to the synagogue for communal prayer
C. Plague: stay indoors; Famine: leave the city
D. Plague: walk in the middle of the road; Famine: walk on the sides
Correct: C — The Gemara teaches opposite responses: during plague, gather your feet and stay inside (the danger is outside); during famine, leave the city (staying guarantees starvation, leaving offers hope).
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to the Gemara, when is it dangerous to enter a synagogue during a plague?
A. At night only
B. When there are no children learning and no minyan of ten praying there
C. When the doors are open
D. When there are more than ten people inside creating crowding
Correct: B — The Angel of Death leaves his 'utensils' in the synagogue, making it dangerous — but only when it is empty of communal spiritual activity. Children learning or ten men praying provide protection.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What point did R' Yitzḥak Nappaḥa make with the parable of the man with two wives?
A. A teacher should never compromise on his curriculum
B. Halakha and aggada are fundamentally incompatible
C. When pulled in opposite directions by competing demands, you end up with nothing — so he'd teach something combining both
D. Students should defer to their teacher's choice of subject matter
Correct: C — One student wanted halakha and blocked aggada; the other wanted aggada and blocked halakha — like two wives making a man bald. R' Yitzḥak's solution was to teach a passage (the fire verse) that contained both legal and aggadic content.
