The Dayyo Principle: Limits of Logical Inference

R' Tarfon
If Goring requires half-damages even in the public domain (lenient zone), then on private property (strict zone) it should require full damages!
Rabbis
Dayyo — it is sufficient for the conclusion of a kal va'chomer to equal its source. Half-damages only!
Gemara
But dayyo is Torah law! Miriam's case proves it. How can R' Tarfon reject it?
Gemara
R' Tarfon rejects dayyo only when applying it would completely nullify the kal va'chomer, leaving no derived law at all.
Core Question When a logical inference (kal va'chomer) yields a novel ruling, can the dayyo principle reduce it back to nothing — or must the inference produce some new result?

R' Tarfon's Kal Va'Chomer for Goring

🏙️ Public Domain (lenient for Eating/Trampling): Owner exempt from Eating/Trampling damages
↓
Yet Goring still requires half-damages in the public domain
↓
🏠 Private Property (strict for Eating/Trampling): Owner pays full damages for Eating/Trampling
↓
R' Tarfon
Goring on private property → Full damages
Rabbis
Dayyo → Only half-damages (same as the source)
Key Logic R' Tarfon argues: if the lenient domain already has half-damages for Goring, the strict domain must have more. The Rabbis cap the inference at its source.

Miriam's Banishment: The Torah Source for Dayyo

Torah
"If her father had spit in her face, she would hide in shame 7 days" (Numbers 12:14)
Gemara
Kal va'chomer: If a father's rebuke = 7 days, then the Divine Presence's rebuke should = 14 days!
Gemara
Yet Miriam was banished only 7 days. Why? Because dayyo — the conclusion cannot exceed the source.
R' Tarfon
There, the kal va'chomer still produced something new — the 7 days themselves weren't written explicitly. But with Goring, half-damages is already written!
Numbers 12:14-15 The Torah's own application of dayyo becomes the battleground: does the verse "Let her be shut up seven days" state the law independently, or is it the kal va'chomer's output after dayyo?

When Does Dayyo Apply? R' Tarfon vs. Rabbis

R' Tarfon

  • Accepts dayyo when it still leaves the kal va'chomer with some novel result
  • Miriam case: dayyo reduced 14→7, but the 7 days were new (derived by the inference)
  • Goring case: dayyo would reduce full→half, but half was already written — inference adds nothing!
  • Rejects dayyo when it completely refutes the entire inference

Rabbis (Chakhamim)

  • Apply dayyo in all cases, even when it completely nullifies the inference
  • "Let her be shut up 7 days" — these 7 days are stated independently in the Torah
  • The kal va'chomer would only have added the extra 7 days, which dayyo removed
  • This proves dayyo applies even when it completely refutes the inference
The Crux Both agree dayyo is Torah law. They disagree on scope: does it apply even when it renders the entire inference meaningless?

Amud B: Mat Impurity — A Challenge to Dayyo

❓ Rav Aḥa of Difti challenges: A baraita derives that a thick mat contracts corpse impurity via kal va'chomer
↓
📐 The inference: If small clay vessels (pure from zav) become impure from a corpse, then a mat (impure from zav) should also become impure from a corpse
↓
⚠️ Problem: The baraita derives 7-day impurity — but dayyo should limit it to evening-level impurity (like zav contact)!
↓
💡 Abaye's resolution: The mat's corpse impurity is actually derived in two stages, not one inference
↓
✅ Stage 1: Kal va'chomer from clay vessels → mat is impure from creeping animal contact
↓
🔗 Stage 2: Gezera shava of "garment/leather" links creeping animal impurity → corpse impurity (with 7-day duration)
Resolution The 7-day impurity comes from the gezera shava (verbal analogy), not from the kal va'chomer — so dayyo is never violated.
