QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does R' Tarfon reject the dayyo principle in the case of Goring on private property?
A. He doesn't believe dayyo is a Torah principle at all
B. Applying dayyo would completely nullify the kal va'chomer, leaving no new law derived
C. He holds that Goring is more severe than Eating and Trampling
D. The verse about Miriam doesn't apply to monetary cases
Correct: B — R' Tarfon accepts dayyo in general but rejects it here because half-damages for Goring is already written explicitly. Applying dayyo would reduce the inference to half-damages — the same as what's already known — making the entire kal va'chomer pointless.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In the Miriam case, what does R' Tarfon say the verse 'Let her be shut up seven days' teaches?
A. That Miriam was actually banished for 14 days
B. That the dayyo principle exists as a general rule of Torah interpretation
C. That the seven days were due to Moses' honor, not the kal va'chomer
D. That Divine rebuke is equivalent to a father's rebuke
Correct: B — R' Tarfon reads this verse as establishing the dayyo principle itself — not as an independent legal statement. The second verse ('And Miriam was shut up...') teaches that dayyo is a general principle, not limited to this case.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does the Gemara resolve the apparent challenge from the baraita about a mat contracting corpse impurity for seven days?
A. The tanna of the baraita simply doesn't accept dayyo
B. The seven-day impurity comes from a gezera shava, not from the kal va'chomer
C. Corpse impurity is always seven days regardless of the source
D. The mat is treated like a garment, which has different rules
Correct: B — Abaye explains that the derivation happens in two stages: a kal va'chomer derives that a mat can contract creeping-animal impurity, and then a gezera shava ('garment/leather') extends this to corpse impurity with its seven-day duration. Since the seven days come from the verbal analogy, dayyo is not violated.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the key difference between R' Tarfon and the Rabbis regarding the dayyo principle?
A. R' Tarfon rejects dayyo entirely; the Rabbis accept it
B. R' Tarfon applies dayyo only to purity laws; the Rabbis apply it everywhere
C. Both accept dayyo, but disagree on whether it applies when it would completely refute the inference
D. The Rabbis apply dayyo only when the kal va'chomer involves monetary law
Correct: C — Both accept dayyo as Torah law. Their dispute is about scope: R' Tarfon says dayyo doesn't apply when it would erase the entire kal va'chomer (leaving no new derivation), while the Rabbis apply it even then.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why must the terms 'garment' and 'leather' be extraneous (mufneh) in their contexts for the gezera shava to work?
A. Because extraneous terms carry extra holiness
B. Because a gezera shava based on non-extraneous terms can be logically refuted
C. Because the Torah only uses verbal analogies with repeated words
D. Because garments and leather are the only items susceptible to impurity
Correct: B — A gezera shava based on extraneous (mufneh) terms cannot be refuted. If the terms weren't extraneous, one could challenge the analogy: creeping animal impurity is stricter (applies to a lentil-bulk) while corpse impurity requires an olive-bulk — undermining the comparison.
