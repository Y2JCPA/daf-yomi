Bava Kamma 50: The Laws of Pit

Who dug the pit and where?
Private Property
R' Akiva (per Rav Yosef): Liable if he renounced ownership of surrounding land
R' Yishmael: Also liable
Public Domain
R' Yishmael (per Rav Yosef): Liable — derived from 'open' and 'dig'
R' Akiva: Exempt — 'owner of the pit' implies private property
↓
What causes the damage?
Rav
Liable only for lethal fumes (hevel), not impact
Shmuel
Liable for both fumes and impact (chabtah)
Key Verse "The owner of the pit shall pay" (Exodus 21:34) — the Torah assigns liability to the one who created the hazard.

Private vs. Public: Rabba's Explanation

Rabba
R' Akiva and R' Yishmael disagree about a pit dug on private property where the owner renounced ownership of the surrounding area.
R' Akiva
"The owner of the pit shall pay" — the Torah speaks of a pit that has an owner. One who digs on private property is liable.
R' Yishmael
"Owner of the pit" means the one responsible for the hazard — even in the public domain. But on private property? Exempt.
Gemara
What does R' Akiva mean by 'This is the case of Pit in the Torah'? It is the pit with which the verse initially introduces the subject of compensation.
Core Dispute Does 'owner of the pit' refer to ownership of the property, or responsibility for the hazard?

Rav Yosef's Alternative Explanation

R' Yishmael

  • Liable for pit in public domain
  • Derives from: 'open a pit' AND 'dig a pit'
  • If liable for opening, digging is obvious — so verse teaches liability for public hazards
  • Also liable on private property (all agree)

R' Akiva

  • Liable only on private property
  • 'Open' and 'dig' are both necessary — not extraneous
  • 'Open' teaches: a cover suffices for pre-existing pit
  • 'Dig' teaches: a cover also suffices for newly dug pit
  • 'Owner of the pit' = literal owner on private land
Reversal In Rav Yosef's reading, R' Akiva and R' Yishmael swap positions compared to Rabba's explanation!

Why Does the Torah Say Both 'Open' and 'Dig'?

Torah states: "If a man shall open a pit" AND "If a man shall dig a pit" (Exodus 21:33)
↓
If liable for opening (less effort), isn't digging (more effort) obvious?
R' Yishmael
Verses are extraneous → teach liability even in public domain where one doesn't own the land
R' Akiva
Both are needed:
• 'Open' alone → maybe only covering works for pre-existing pit
• 'Dig' alone → maybe covering only works when you created it
→ Both teach: covering suffices in either case
Method Classic Talmudic technique: when the Torah seems redundant, the 'extra' words must teach something new.

The Parable of the Stones

מפני מה אתה מסלק אבנים מרשות שאינה שלך לרשות שלך
Aggadah A man was throwing stones from his property into the public domain. A pious man (chasid) said to him: "Reika! (Lowlife!) Why do you throw stones from property that is not yours into your property?"

The man laughed. But days later, he was forced to sell his field. Walking through the public domain, he stumbled on those same stones.

He said: "That pious man spoke well — I threw stones from land that was never truly mine into the public domain, which is the only place that remains mine to use."
