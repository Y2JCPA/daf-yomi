QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Yosef, what do R' Akiva and R' Yishmael agree upon?
A. One who digs on private property and renounces surrounding ownership is liable
B. One who digs in the public domain is always liable
C. A pit less than 10 tefachim can cause death liability
D. Fumes are the only source of pit damage
Correct: A — Per Rav Yosef, both agree that 'owner of the pit' indicates liability when there's an actual owner — i.e., when one digs on private property and renounces surrounding land. Their dispute is only about the public domain.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the practical difference (nafka mina) between Rav and Shmuel regarding pit liability?
A. A pit full of water vs. an empty pit
B. A mound 10 tefachim high in the public domain
C. A pit exactly 10 tefachim deep vs. 9 tefachim
D. A covered pit vs. an uncovered pit
Correct: B — A mound has no fumes — only impact when the animal falls off. Rav exempts (no fumes = no liability); Shmuel holds liable (impact alone suffices).
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does the Torah mention BOTH 'opening' and 'digging' a pit, according to R' Akiva (per Rav Yosef)?
A. To teach liability in both public and private domains
B. To teach that each case requires a covering, and neither is obvious from the other
C. To include liability for trenches and water channels
D. To distinguish between fumes and impact
Correct: B — R' Akiva holds both terms are necessary: 'open' alone might suggest only a pre-existing pit needs covering; 'dig' alone might suggest only a newly created pit needs covering. Both are needed to teach that a covering suffices in either case.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In the parable of the stones, what was the pious man's message?
A. Don't litter in public spaces
B. Private property is temporary, but the public domain is always yours to use
C. Throwing stones is a form of pit liability
D. One must build a fence before throwing stones
Correct: B — The pious man called the private field 'not yours' (since ownership is temporary) and the public domain 'yours' (since you always use it). The man later lost his field and stumbled on his own stones in the public road.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to the baraita, one who digs a pit with its hollow in the public domain but its opening in private property is:
A. Liable, because the hollow is in the public domain
B. Liable, because digging under public domain is forbidden
C. Exempt, though it is forbidden to dig under public domain
D. Exempt, and it is permitted since the opening is on private land
Correct: C — The baraita teaches that even though one may not dig under the public domain, if the opening is on private property (where people don't walk), the digger is exempt from pit liability. Liability follows the location of the opening, not the hollow.
