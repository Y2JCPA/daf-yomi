Ownership & Liability in Sacred and Secular Damage

Core Question: When does an owner have property rights over consecrated animals — and when is one liable for damage?
↓
Amud A: Sacred Property
Three-way dispute: Which kodshim kalim (lesser sanctity offerings) are considered the owner's property?
Amud B: Damage Liability
Mishna principles: Liability requires covenant membership, assigned ownership, and ownership at time of trial
↓
Unifying Principle: Ownership status — whether of sacred or secular property — determines both the right to collect damages and the obligation to pay them
Key Concept The daf bridges sacrificial law and tort law through a single question: What does it mean to 'own' something?

Are Kodshim Kalim the Owner's Property?

R' Yosei HaGelili
All kodshim kalim (offerings of lesser sanctity) are the property of their owners. One who steals them and swears falsely violates 'and deals falsely with his neighbor' (Lev. 5:21).
Ben Azzai
The verse includes only shelamim (peace-offerings) — not all kodshim kalim.
Abba Yosei ben Dostai
Ben Azzai actually said this only about bekhor (firstborn offerings) — even more limited.
R' Yochanan
Both Ben Azzai and Abba Yosei ben Dostai exclude ma'aser beheimah (animal tithe) — they hold it is not the owner's property.
Core Dispute The scope of ownership over consecrated animals determines liability for theft and false oaths.

Gifts of the Priesthood Are Different

משולחן גבוה קא זכו
Rava's Distinction Why can't Ravina resolve the contradiction by attributing one view to R' Yosei HaGelili and the other to the Rabbis? Because priestly gifts (like the bekhor) are fundamentally different: the priest receives them from the table of the Most High (משולחן גבוה). The bekhor is sanctified from birth — the priest may never have truly 'owned' it. Rather, the Torah grants only the right and obligation to eat it after sacrifice.

Rava: Damage by a Consecrated Thanks-Offering

An innocuous ox consecrated as a todah (thanks-offering) causes damage
↓
Can the injured party collect damages?
↓
Meat Portions
✅ Collects from the owner's edible meat portions
Sacrificial Portions
❌ Cannot collect — these belong to God
Bread (40 Loaves)
❌ Cannot collect — bread didn't cause damage
↓
Rava's ruling: The injured party eats the meat according to the damage value; the owner still brings the bread — it is the owner's obligation, not the eater's
Novel Point The owner cannot say: 'Why should you eat my meat while I bring the bread?' The bread obligation belongs to whoever brings the offering.

Mishna's Liability Requirements

Members of the Covenant

  • Excludes damage to gentile-owned property
  • A Jew's ox that gores a gentile's ox → exempt
  • Stated as a principle here; detailed in Mishna 37b

Assigned Property

  • Excludes cases of uncertain ownership
  • Two oxen chasing one — unclear who caused damage → both exempt
  • Excludes ownerless (hefker) property
  • Ravina: Excludes ox consecrated or declared ownerless after goring
Principle The mishna establishes two fundamental prerequisites for tort liability: the parties must be bound by the covenant, and the damaging property must have a clearly identifiable owner.
