QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabba, why does the Torah impose a partial admission oath specifically for loans but not deposits?
A. Because loans involve written documents but deposits do not
B. Because a borrower feels shame before his creditor who did him a favor, preventing full denial
C. Because deposits are always witnessed by third parties
D. Because the Torah considers loans more serious than deposits
Correct: B — Rabba explains that a borrower won't fully deny the debt out of shame (azut panim) toward his creditor who did him a favor. This psychological dynamic doesn't exist with deposits, where the depositor did the bailee no favor.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does Rava derive that a borrower (sho'el) is included in the oath requirement for partial admission?
A. From an explicit verse about borrowers
B. From a verbal analogy (gezeira shava) with unpaid bailees
C. From the conjunction 'vav' in 'And if (vekhi) a man borrow' connecting it to the previous passage
D. From a kal va'chomer argument — if paid bailees are included, certainly borrowers are
Correct: C — Rava explains that the 'vav' (and) in 'vekhi yish'al' connects the borrower's laws to the preceding topic of bailees, thereby extending the partial admission oath requirement to borrowers.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
R' Ḥiyya bar Yosef and Rav Sheshet disagree about an unpaid bailee who misappropriates a deposit before falsely swearing it was stolen. What is the dispute?
A. Whether the bailee must return the item itself or its monetary value
B. Whether misappropriation before the oath makes the bailee liable or exempt from double payment
C. Whether the bailee's oath is administered by the court or taken voluntarily
D. Whether the case applies to all four types of bailees or only to unpaid bailees
Correct: B — R' Ḥiyya bar Yosef holds misappropriation before the oath makes the bailee LIABLE for double payment, while Rav Sheshet holds it makes him EXEMPT — both deriving their positions from Exodus 22:7.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to R' Yoḥanan (via R' Avin), why is a bailee exempt from double payment when he first falsely swears the deposit was lost, then falsely swears it was stolen?
A. Because the court only administers one oath per claim
B. Because the item already 'left the owner's possession' with the first oath
C. Because a claim of loss is more severe than a claim of theft
D. Because witnesses can only testify about the most recent claim
Correct: B — R' Yoḥanan holds that the first false oath (claiming loss) effectively severed the ownership connection. Therefore, the second false oath (claiming theft) cannot generate a new double-payment obligation — the legal relationship was already altered by the first oath.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the significance of Rav Naḥman's point about the three oaths administered to an unpaid bailee?
A. It proves that bailees must always take exactly three oaths
B. It challenges Rav Sheshet by noting that one standard oath already addresses misappropriation, undermining the idea that misappropriation changes the legal framework
C. It shows that the Torah requires more oaths for bailees than for borrowers
D. It demonstrates that courts have unlimited authority to impose additional oaths
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman challenges Rav Sheshet: if the court routinely makes the bailee swear 'I did not misappropriate it,' this suggests misappropriation is a standard consideration within the oath framework — not something that removes the bailee from the framework entirely, as Rav Sheshet claims.
