Why Does Partial Admission Oath Apply Only to Loans?

Gemara
The verse "zeh hu" ("This is it") from which partial admission is derived — it is written regarding a loan, not a deposit. Why the distinction?
Rabba
A borrower won't show total azut panim (insolence) to his creditor who did him a favor by lending money.
Rabba
He wants to deny everything to be exempt, but shame prevents full denial. So he admits part.
Rabba
He also wants to admit all, but reasons: "I'll stall until I have money, then pay in full." So the Torah imposes an oath.
Gemara
But for a deposit, the depositor did the bailee no favor — so he will exhibit insolence. The oath framework differs entirely.
Rabba's Insight The partial admission oath is grounded in human psychology — the tension between shame and self-interest that exists only in creditor-debtor relationships.

When Does False Claim of Theft Trigger Double Payment?

R' Ḥiyya bar Yosef

  • Unpaid bailee falsely claims theft of deposit
  • Liable for double payment only if he misappropriated the item before taking the oath
  • Source: "If the thief shall not be found... if he misappropriated" (Ex. 22:7)
  • Misappropriation → then double payment liability

Rav Sheshet

  • Unpaid bailee falsely claims theft of deposit
  • Exempt from double payment if he misappropriated it before the oath
  • Reads same verse differently: "if he has not misappropriated"
  • Verse applies only when bailee did NOT misappropriate
Same Verse, Opposite Readings R' Ḥiyya bar Yosef and Rav Sheshet derive opposite conclusions from Exodus 22:7 based on how they parse the conditional clause about misappropriation.

R' Yoḥanan: Sequential False Oaths

Unpaid bailee has a deposit
↓
First false oath:
"The item was lost" (אבד)
↓
Retracts, second false oath:
"A thief stole it" (נגנב)
↓
Witnesses testify he lied about the theft claim
↓
Exempt from double payment on the theft claim
Because the item already "left the owner's possession" with the first oath
R' Yoḥanan via R' Avin Once the bailee took the first false oath (claiming loss), the deposit effectively left the owner's domain. A subsequent false claim of theft cannot generate a new double-payment obligation.

R' Zeira Challenges: What Did R' Yoḥanan Really Mean?

R' Ḥiyya bar Abba
R' Yoḥanan taught this about an animal still standing over its feeding trough — the bailee still guards it for the owner.
R' Zeira
Does R' Yoḥanan mean specifically when the animal is still in the bailee's care? So if he misappropriated it, the oath is ineffective entirely?
R' Zeira
Or does he mean even when standing over its trough — in addition to cases of misappropriation?
R' Ḥiyya bar Abba
I did not hear R' Yoḥanan's precise intent. But I heard something similar from R' Asi in R' Yoḥanan's name...
R' Ḥiyya bar Abba
The case of sequential false oaths: first claiming loss, then theft — exempt from double payment because the first oath already severed ownership.
Unresolved R' Zeira's precise question — whether misappropriation blocks the oath mechanism or simply adds another scenario — remains without a definitive answer from R' Yoḥanan.
