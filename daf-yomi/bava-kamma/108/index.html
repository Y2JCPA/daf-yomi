Bava Kamma 108 — One-Fifth Payment, Double Payment & Bailee Standing

Core Question: What is the relationship between the chomesh (one-fifth) and kefel (double payment)?
↓
Monetary Obligation Exempts
Being liable for double payment as a monetary matter exempts from one-fifth
Oath Exempts
The oath itself creating double payment liability exempts from one-fifth
↓
Rava resolves: It is the monetary obligation that exempts from one-fifth, not the oath
↓
Further dilemmas: split obligations, repeated oaths, and bailee standing remain teyku
DAF THEME This daf explores the boundaries of penalty payments—when one-fifth and double payment interact, how bailee status affects collection rights, and multiple unresolved dilemmas (teyku).

What Exempts from the One-Fifth Payment?

Rami bar Ḥama
A bailee swore the deposit was stolen (witnesses prove it false), then swore it was lost (he admits this was false). Does he pay the chomesh (one-fifth)?
Rami bar Ḥama
Option 1: The monetary obligation of double payment exempts from one-fifth — so here, since witnesses created double payment liability, he's exempt from one-fifth on the second oath too.
Rami bar Ḥama
Option 2: The oath creating double payment liability exempts from one-fifth — but the second oath (claiming 'lost') doesn't create double payment, so he would owe one-fifth.
Rava
Proof from Shevuot 49b: A non-bailee who steals — witnesses create double payment, but if he admits, he pays one-fifth. If oath exempted, he should still owe one-fifth since he's not a bailee!
Rava
Conclusion: It is the monetary obligation that exempts from one-fifth, not the oath. Dilemma resolved.
KEY PRINCIPLE The Torah does not impose both double payment (kefel) and one-fifth (chomesh) on the same monetary obligation — this exemption is based on the type of monetary liability, not on the oath itself.

Rava's Proof from Shevuot 49b

A non-bailee is accused of stealing an ox and takes an oath denying theft
↓
Witnesses Testify
He pays double payment (kefel)
He Admits
He pays principal + one-fifth + guilt-offering
↓
Critical inference: If he admits after witnesses testified → no one-fifth
↓
Since this non-bailee's oath can't create double payment, it must be the monetary obligation (not oath) that exempts from one-fifth
LOGIC If the oath were the exempting factor, then a non-bailee (whose oath doesn't trigger double payment) should still owe one-fifth even after witnesses testify. But the Mishnah says he doesn't — proving it's the monetary obligation that matters.

Abaye vs. Rava: To Whom Does the Thief Pay Double?

Abaye

  • If bailee paid the owner → thief pays double to the bailee (who now has rights)
  • If bailee took oath and paid → thief still pays double to the bailee
  • Key: once the bailee pays, he acquires the right to double payment regardless of oath

Rava

  • If bailee paid without oath → thief pays double to the bailee
  • If bailee took oath then paid → thief pays double to the owner
  • Key: taking an oath (even if later nullified by payment) shows bad faith and forfeits bailee's claim to double payment
DISPUTE The core disagreement: Does taking an oath — even one later nullified by payment — permanently affect the bailee's standing to collect the penalty from the thief?

Bailee's Standing After Oath: True vs. False

📋 Owner demands deposit from bailee; bailee claims it was stolen and takes oath
↓
🔍 The thief is later identified
↓
🗣️ Bailee demands payment from thief; thief admits to bailee but denies to owner
↓
✅ If bailee's oath was true (deposit really was stolen) → bailee retains standing as agent; thief's admission to bailee exempts from double payment
↓
❌ If bailee's oath was false (e.g., claimed it died) → owner wouldn't rely on him; bailee loses standing; thief's admission to bailee does not exempt
↓
❓ If bailee attempted a false oath but owner stopped him → Teyku
RAVA'S RULE A bailee who swore truthfully retains the owner's trust and remains their agent for collection. A false oath severs that relationship — the bailee can no longer act on the owner's behalf.
