When Is Damage Assessed? Dividing Oxen After Injury

Baraita
An innocuous ox worth 200 dinars gored another ox worth 200, causing 50 dinars of damage. If the injured ox later appreciated, pay based on time of injury.
Gemara
If it depreciated, pay based on time of trial. But what about the belligerent ox — same rules?
R' Akiva
The injured party is a partner (shutaf) in the belligerent ox. If the owner fattened it, the appreciation belongs to the owner alone.
Mishna
R' Meir and R' Yehuda dispute how to read 'They shall sell the live ox and divide it' (Exodus 21:35).
Key Daf Theme This daf explores how damage valuation changes over time and whether the injured party is a creditor or partner in the belligerent ox.

↓

Principle The injured party's claim is tied to the specific ox that caused damage, giving it priority over a general monetary debt.

R' Yishmael vs. R' Akiva: Status of the Injured Party

R' Yishmael
The injured party is a creditor (ba'al chov). He has a monetary claim against the owner — no ownership share in the belligerent ox.
R' Akiva
The injured party is a partner (shutaf). He has actual ownership in the belligerent ox itself.
Gemara
The baraita's rule — if the belligerent ox appreciated, pay at time of injury; if depreciated, pay at time of trial — fits R' Akiva.
Rav Pappa
Appreciation from fattening belongs to the owner alone. But for the injured ox, even natural appreciation is valued at time of injury.
Resolution The entire baraita follows R' Akiva. When the belligerent ox appreciated because its owner fattened it, the injured partner has no share in that increase.

R' Meir vs. R' Yehuda: Dividing the Oxen (Mishna)

R' Meir

  • Ox worth 200 gores ox worth 200, carcass worth nothing
  • Reads verse: 'Sell the live ox and divide its value'
  • Each party gets 100 dinars from the live ox
  • Injured party's loss = 100 dinars (half damage)

R' Yehuda

  • You upheld 'sell the live ox and divide' — but ignored 'the dead they shall also divide'
  • Both live AND dead ox must be divided
  • Carcass appreciation belongs half to each party
  • The liable party must never gain from the damage
R' Yochanan Their dispute concerns appreciation of the carcass after death: R' Meir gives it all to the injured party; R' Yehuda splits it.

The One Liable Cannot Profit

יְשַׁלֵּם — משלם ואינו נוטל
Key Principle R' Yehuda derives from 'He shall pay' (Exodus 21:36): the liable party pays — he does not take. Even where both parties split the live and dead oxen, the liable party's share can never exceed the original value of his own ox. One who causes damage cannot profit from it.
