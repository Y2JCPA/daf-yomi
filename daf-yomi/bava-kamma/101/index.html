Daf 101: Changes, Dyes & Substantive Enhancement

R' Meir
A carpenter who deviates from the customer's order acquires the item through shinui (change) and pays the value of the wood.
R' Yehuda
The carpenter does not acquire the item. He receives the lesser of his expenses or the wood's enhancement.
R' Meir
But I concede: if he made the same item but ugly instead of beautiful, there is no significant change — he does not acquire it.
Rava
The core dilemma: Is dye absorbed in wool still a substantive, independent entity — or has it become one with the wool?
CORE QUESTION When materials undergo transformation — whether wood into furniture or dye into colored wool — who owns the result, and does the added substance retain independent identity?

Carpenter Deviates: R' Meir vs. R' Yehuda

R' Meir

  • Carpenter acquires the item through shinui (change of form)
  • Pays the owner the value of the wood
  • Keeps the chair or bench he fashioned
  • Exception: ugly version of the same item — no acquisition

R' Yehuda

  • Carpenter does not acquire the item
  • Owner keeps the finished product
  • Carpenter receives the lesser of expenses or enhancement
  • Deviation penalizes the craftsman, not the customer
KEY PRINCIPLE Both agree: when the craftsman made the correct item but poorly, the lesser-of-two rule applies — no acquisition through minor deviation.

The Dye Dilemma: Is Enhancement Substantive?

Gemara
A dilemma was raised: Is the enhancement that dye provides to wool substantive (mamash) — meaning the dye remains an independent entity?
Position A
Dye is substantive: It remains a separate entity. The robber can say: 'Your dyes lie before you in the wool — take them!'
Position B
Dye is not substantive: It merges with the wool entirely. The robber must pay monetary compensation for lost dye.
Gemara
'Take them' — with what? With soap? The dye cannot be recovered even if removed!
PRACTICAL IMPACT Whether dye is 'substantive' determines if a robber has returned stolen property or still owes compensation.

Rava's Contradiction: Orla Dye vs. Corpse Blood

דם תמצית דרבנן
RAVA'S CHALLENGE Orla mishna: A garment dyed with orla peels must be burned — the dye retains its orla identity (substantive!).

Oholot mishna: Blood absorbed in a garment — only what can be washed out counts as blood. What remains absorbed is insignificant (not substantive!).

Rav Kahana's resolution: The blood case involves dam tamtzit (blood of submission) which is impure only rabbinically. The Rabbis applied a leniency to quarter-log measurements. In general, absorbed substances are substantive.
