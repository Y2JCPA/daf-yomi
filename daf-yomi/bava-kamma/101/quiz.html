QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to R' Meir, when a carpenter is told to make a chair but makes a bench instead, what happens?
A. The carpenter must make a new chair at his own expense
B. The carpenter acquires the bench through shinui and pays the value of the wood
C. The owner keeps the bench and pays the carpenter his expenses
D. The transaction is void and the wood is returned
Correct: B — R' Meir holds that significant deviation constitutes shinui (change) — the carpenter acquires the finished item but must compensate the owner for the raw materials.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
When does R' Meir concede that the carpenter does NOT acquire the item through change?
A. When the bench is more valuable than the chair
B. When the carpenter made the correct item but made it ugly instead of beautiful
C. When the owner specifically requested the item in writing
D. When the wood belonged to a third party
Correct: B — R' Meir concedes that making the correct item (e.g., a chair) but in an ugly manner is not a sufficient deviation to constitute shinui — the carpenter made what was requested, just poorly.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does the Gemara reject the scenario of a robber who stole solid pigments, ground them, and dyed wool?
A. Because pigments have no monetary value
B. Because grinding the pigments already constitutes shinui, making the dye dilemma irrelevant
C. Because the wool belongs to the robber
D. Because the Torah does not consider pigments as property
Correct: B — The act of grinding solid pigments into dye constitutes a change of form (shinui). The robber already acquired them at that point, so the question of whether dye is substantive in the wool is moot.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rav Kahana resolve Rava's contradiction between orla dye (substantive) and corpse blood absorption (not substantive)?
A. Dye and blood have fundamentally different chemical properties
B. The blood case involves dam tamtzit, which is only rabbinically impure — a leniency was applied to quarter-log measurements
C. Orla is a Torah prohibition while tumah is rabbinic
D. Blood can always be fully washed out but dye cannot
Correct: B — Rav Kahana explains that the blood of submission (dam tamtzit) is impure only rabbinically, and the Rabbis applied a leniency. In general, absorbed substances are considered substantive.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is Rava's principle for determining which items have Sabbatical Year (shevi'it) sanctity?
A. Only edible produce has shevi'it sanctity
B. Any plant that grows from the ground has shevi'it sanctity
C. Items whose benefit and consumption coincide have shevi'it sanctity
D. Only items sold in the marketplace have shevi'it sanctity
Correct: C — Rava derives from 'the Sabbath produce of the land shall be for food' that shevi'it sanctity applies when benefit and consumption coincide — like dye plants (benefit during use) but unlike firewood (benefit comes after burning, from the charcoal).
