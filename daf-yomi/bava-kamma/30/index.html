Pouring Water: Clothes vs. Injury

Mishna
One who pours water in the public domain and another is damaged — liable.
Rav
Liable only for soiled clothes. If the person was injured by the fall, exempt — the ground caused the injury, not the water.
Rav Huna
Why exempt? The muddy ground is his filth — his water mixed with the dirt. He should be liable!
Rav
We're speaking where the water was absorbed into the ground. No identifiable mud remains that belongs to him.
Key Distinction Rav distinguishes between damage caused by the water itself (soiled garments) and damage caused by impact with the ground (bodily injury).

Summer vs. Rainy Season: Pouring Water

☀️ Summer (Dry Season)

  • No permission to drain gutters into the public domain
  • No permission to flush cave water into the street
  • If one pours water and causes damage — liable
  • This is the ruling of one mishna

🌧️ Rainy Season

  • Permitted to drain gutters into the public domain
  • Permitted to flush cave water — streets are wet anyway
  • Even with permission — still liable if damage occurs
  • This is the ruling of the second mishna
Why Two Mishnayot? One teaches liability in summer (no permission), the other in the rainy season (with permission) — to show liability applies even when the activity is permitted.

Concealing Thorns & Glass: When Is One Liable?

One conceals thorns or glass in a wall or builds a thorn fence
↓
Do the thorns project into the public domain?
Yes — Projects Out
Liable for any damage caused
(R' Yoḥanan)
No — Within Property
Exempt — people don't typically rub against walls
(Rav Aḥa b. Rav Ika)
R' Yoḥanan Liability for concealed hazards depends on whether they extend into shared space. Within one's own property, pedestrians are expected to keep their distance from walls.

Pit covered with another's bucket; owner takes the bucketThe pit owner (Ravina)Should have anticipated the bucket would be reclaimed
Why isn't the bucket owner liable for not warning?Still the pit ownerBucket owner has no obligation to inform — it's his property
Ravina's Novelty Even though the bucket owner knew who dug the pit and could have warned him, he has no obligation to do so. The pit owner bears full responsibility.

The Early Pious Ones (Ḥasidim Rishonim)

חסידים הראשונים היו מצניעין קוצותיהן וזכוכיותיהן בתוך שדותיהן ומעמיקין להן שלשה טפחים
Ethical Ideal The early pious ones would conceal their thorns and glass in their own fields, burying them at least 3 tefaḥim deep so that even a plow could not unearth them. They went beyond the letter of the law to prevent any possible harm to others.
