Stolen Items Sold to Innocent Buyers: Three Explanations

Rav (R' Ḥiyya)
The owner's claim can be pursued only with the thief — not the purchaser
R' Yoḥanan (R' Yannai)
The owner's claim can also be pursued with the purchaser
Rav Yosef
They don't disagree! Rav speaks of after ye'ush, R' Yoḥanan of before ye'ush
Abaye
They do disagree — the case of matanot kehunah (priestly gifts) proves it!
Rav Zevid
They disagree about ye'ush after shinui reshut — does the order matter?
Rav Pappa
All agree the item returns. They disagree about takkanat hashuk — marketplace protection
Core Issue When a thief sells stolen property, how do ye'ush (despair) and shinui reshut (change of possession) affect ownership rights?

Rav vs. R' Yoḥanan: Who Does the Owner Claim From?

Rav (R' Ḥiyya)
The owner pursues only the thief (the first one). The purchaser keeps the item.
R' Yoḥanan (R' Yannai)
The owner can pursue even the purchaser (the second one) to recover the stolen item.
Rav Yosef
No dispute! Rav = after ye'ush (despair), so purchaser acquires it. R' Yoḥanan = before ye'ush, so purchaser must return it.
Abaye
They do disagree! Matanot kehunah is like before ye'ush, yet Rav still rules against the priest claiming from the buyer!
Rav Ḥisda's Principle (111b) Before ye'ush: the owner can press his claim against either the thief or the purchaser. Both Rav Yosef and Rav Zevid accept this as agreed-upon law.

Three Explanations of the Rav / R' Yoḥanan Dispute

Rav Yosef

  • No real dispute — different cases
  • Rav: after ye'ush → purchaser acquires
  • R' Yoḥanan: before ye'ush → owner reclaims
  • Both accept Rav Ḥisda's ruling

Rav Zevid

  • Dispute: does ORDER of ye'ush & shinui reshut matter?
  • R' Yoḥanan: Ye'ush THEN shinui reshut = purchaser acquires; reverse = no acquisition
  • Rav: Order doesn't matter — ye'ush alone suffices
  • Both agree: before ye'ush, owner claims from either

Rav Pappa

  • All agree the stolen item itself must be returned
  • Dispute: does takkanat hashuk apply?
  • Rav: Yes — purchaser is reimbursed by thief (marketplace protection)
  • R' Yoḥanan: No — owner deals directly with purchaser
Key Concepts Ye'ush = owner's despair of recovery. Shinui reshut = change of possession. Takkanat hashuk = rabbinic provision protecting marketplace buyers.

Matanot Kehunah: Abaye's Challenge to Rav Yosef

ScenarioWho Pays the Priest?
Buyer purchases innards unweighed (lump sum)Buyer gives gifts to priest; no deduction from price
Buyer purchases innards by weightBuyer gives gifts to priest; deducts gift value from price
Butcher weighed innards for the buyer (Rav's ruling)Priest pursues claim only with the butcher
Abaye's Argument Priestly gifts are like stolen goods before ye'ush (the priest hasn't despaired). Yet Rav still says the priest claims only from the butcher — proving Rav and R' Yoḥanan truly disagree!

Salvaging Honey at the Cost of Wine (Amud B)

🍯 A barrel of honey is about to break and leak out
↓
🍷 A bystander has wine — he can pour it out to save the honey in his vessel
↓
🤝 Without prior agreement: rescuer receives only his wage for effort
↓
📜 With prior agreement ('save my honey, I'll pay for your wine'): full wine value owed
↓
🫏 Same rule applies: abandoning one's donkey to save another's more valuable donkey
Why Not Ownerless? The Gemara asks: why can't the rescuer claim the honey as hefker (ownerless)? Answer: the basket of the olive press was wrapped around it — contents aren't fully lost, so not ownerless.
