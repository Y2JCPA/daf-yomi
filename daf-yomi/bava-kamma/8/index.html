How Do We Appraise Land Quality?

First Baraita
If debtor has only intermediate + inferior land: creditor collects from intermediate.
Second Baraita
If debtor has only intermediate + inferior land: creditor collects from inferior.
Gemara
These two baraitot contradict each other! How do we resolve them?
Rav Ḥisda
The first baraita is where the debtor once had superior land and sold it. His intermediate was already liened as intermediate at the time of the loan.
Gemara
The second baraita is where he never had superior land — so his intermediate is reclassified as superior, and the creditor can only take inferior.
Resolution The contradiction is resolved by distinguishing the debtor's land ownership history — what he held at the time the lien took effect.

Ulla's Principle: Torah Law vs. Rabbinic Ordinance

שלא תנעול דלת בפני לווין
Torah Source Ulla says: By Torah law, a creditor collects from inferior land.

Source: "The man you have a claim against will bring his collateral out to you" (Devarim 24:11) — a person brings out his most inferior utensils as collateral.

The Sages enacted that creditors collect from intermediate land so as not to lock the door before borrowers (shelo tin'ol delet bifnei lovim) — lenders would refuse to lend if limited to inferior land.

When the Debtor Sells Land & Dies

📋 Debtor owes damages, a loan, and a ketubah
↓
🏠 Debtor sells all three plots (superior, intermediate, inferior) to one buyer
↓
⚰️ Debtor dies — his orphans have no obligation to repay
↓
🔗 Liens remain on the sold land — creditors collect from the buyer
↓
📊 Each creditor collects from the plot originally liened to them
↓
🙅 Buyer cannot threaten to return the bill of sale to orphans (it wouldn't help creditors)
Why Orphans? The Gemara specifies the debtor died because otherwise the buyer could threaten to return the land, forcing creditors to collect from the debtor's remaining property.
