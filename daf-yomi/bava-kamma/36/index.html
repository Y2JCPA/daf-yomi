Serial Goring: Who Collects First?

🐂 An innocuous ox (tam) gores multiple oxen in succession
↓
Half-damages paid from the body of the goring ox only
↓
❓ How is the ox's value divided among multiple victims?
R' Meir
Last victim collects first — earlier victims get the surplus
R' Shimon
All victims share proportionally as partners in the ox
Core Issue When an innocuous ox causes serial damage, the limited fund (the ox itself) must be divided — but the method depends on whether victims are creditors (R' Yishmael) or partners (R' Akiva).

Seized Ox: Can the Court Compel Payment?

Gemara
The mishna says the injured party receives compensation — does he actually collect?
Answer
It means it is fitting for him to take compensation, but in practice he does not receive any.
Challenge
But a baraita says he receives payment for his small ox from the forewarned ox and for his large ox from the innocuous ox!
Answer
The baraita refers to a case where the injured party seized (תפס) the defendant's ox — the court lets him keep it, but cannot compel payment ab initio.
Principle Seizure (תפיסה) creates a de facto right: the court won't remove the ox from the injured party, but won't force the liable party to hand it over either.

Two Oxen Pursuing: Innocuous vs. Forewarned

Forewarned (מועד)

  • Owner pays full damages from any property
  • Doesn't matter which ox caused the injury
  • Both oxen liable because owner pays regardless
  • R' Ashi's initial interpretation of the mishna

Innocuous (תם)

  • Half-damages paid only from the goring ox itself
  • Critical to identify which ox caused injury
  • Burden of proof on the claimant
  • R' Akiva: injured party is a partner in the ox
Rava of Parzika's Challenge If the mishna meant forewarned oxen, why does the latter clause care which ox (large or small) caused the damage? For forewarned oxen, full damages are paid regardless!

R' Akiva's Partnership Model Applied

Rav Ashi
The first clause refers to forewarned oxen, the latter clause to innocuous oxen.
Rav Aḥa the Elder
If forewarned, why say 'they are liable' in plural? It should say 'the man is liable!' And what does 'both' mean?
Rav Aḥa the Elder
Rather, the first clause is innocuous oxen, following R' Akiva — the injured party is a partner in the ox.
Rav Aḥa the Elder
Both oxen are liable because both are available — the owner can't dismiss the claim. But if one died, the injured party must prove which one gored.
Key Insight The word 'both' (שניהם) proves the mishna discusses innocuous oxen — under R' Akiva's view, the injured party has a partnership claim on each specific ox.

Rava's Explanation: R' Meir Follows R' Yishmael

R' Meir rules: last victim gains priority
↓
❓ How does this fit R' Yishmael's creditor model?
(Creditors normally: first lien = first collection)
↓
🔑 The first injured party seized the ox and became a paid bailee (שומר שכר)
↓
As bailee, he is responsible for subsequent gorings the ox commits
Result
Each successive victim seizes from the previous one — last seizure takes priority
Surplus
Any remaining value after last victim collects goes back up the chain
Ravin / R' Yoḥanan The mishna touches on the topic of bailee negligence — by seizing the ox, the injured party assumes custodial responsibility for its future behavior.

📋 Bava Kamma 36 — Summary Map

🐂 Innocuous ox gores multiple oxen in succession — half-damages paid from its body only
↓
R' Yishmael / R' Meir:
Victims are creditors.
Last victim collects first (seizure = bailment).
Surplus goes to earlier victims.
R' Akiva / R' Shimon:
Victims are partners.
All share proportionally.
Each gets half of remaining value.
↓
🔑 Practical difference: Can a victim consecrate (הקדש) the ox? Partner — yes. Creditor — no.
