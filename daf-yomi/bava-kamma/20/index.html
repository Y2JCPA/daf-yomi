Classifying Animal Damage: Eating, Goring & Location

An animal causes damage by eating or breaking something
↓
Is the behavior typical (Shen/Eating) or atypical (Keren/Goring)?
Typical (Shen)
Private property: Full damage
Public domain: Exempt
Atypical (Keren)
Private property: Half damage
Public domain: Half damage
↓
Key question: When does an atypical result flow from a typical action?
Core Theme Daf 20 explores the boundaries of Shen (Eating) — when damage chains from typical behavior, when location matters, and whether one must pay for benefit received without the other's loss.

The Goat, the Turnip & the Barrel

🐐 A goat sees a turnip on top of a clay barrel (dedanna)
↓
Goat climbs up, eats the turnip, and breaks the barrel
↓
Is breaking the barrel typical goat behavior?
Rava's Ruling
Full damage for both turnip AND barrel — since climbing to eat is typical, the breakage is classified as Shen (Eating)
Rejected View
Breaking barrels is atypical → should be Keren (Goring) → only half damage for barrel
Principle When atypical damage is a natural consequence of typical eating behavior, the entire chain is classified as Shen.

Eating from Another Animal's Load in Public

Ilfa
If an animal stretched its neck and ate from a sack on another animal's back in the public domain — the owner pays full damage.
Gemara
Why full damage in public? Because the back of the other animal is like the courtyard of the injured party (reshut ha-nizak).
Baraita Support?
A man's basket was slung behind him, and an animal ate from it — owner is liable. Does this support Ilfa?
Rava
No proof. That baraita may deal with a jumping animal — atypical behavior classified as Keren, not Shen. Liability in public for Keren is only half damage.
Key Concept An animal's back or a person's body can serve as the reshut ha-nizak (injured party's domain) even while in the public domain.

Walking vs. Standing vs. Jumping — R' Oshaya & Rava

Walking & Eating

  • Animal eats while walking in public domain
  • This is TYPICAL animal behavior
  • Classified as Shen (Eating)
  • Owner is EXEMPT — Shen in public domain

Standing & Eating

  • R' Oshaya: Standing and eating → LIABLE
  • Gemara objects: standing is also typical!
  • Rava clarifies: R' Oshaya refers to a JUMPING animal
  • Jumping is atypical → classified as Keren

Jumping & Eating

  • Animal jumps to reach food
  • ATYPICAL behavior → Keren (Goring)
  • Owner liable even in public domain
  • But pays only HALF damage
Rava's Clarification R' Oshaya's distinction isn't really walking vs. standing — it's typical vs. atypical (jumping) eating behavior.

Garments & Vessels in Public: Rav vs. Shmuel

Rav
If an animal ate garments or vessels in the public domain — owner is exempt from ALL cases. Even though eating cloth is atypical (Keren), the injured party deviated first by leaving items in public.
Rav's Principle
When one deviates from the norm, and another then deviates regarding that person's property — the second party is exempt.
Shmuel
The mishna's exemption in public applies only to typical eating (produce). But eating garments is atypical → Keren → half damage even in public domain.
Gemara
The dispute: Does the injured party's negligence in leaving items in public cancel the damager's atypical behavior?
Rav's Principle שינוי בשינוי — When both parties deviate from normal conduct, the damage-causer is exempt. The injured party's own abnormal behavior negates their claim.
