Unauthorized Use of Another's Property

Someone uses another's property without permission
↓
Did the owner suffer a monetary loss?
Yes — Loss occurred
Must pay for rental value or depreciation
No — No loss
Generally exempt from payment (e.g., slave used off-hours, empty courtyard)
Exception If the user is a creditor of the owner, even free use creates an appearance of interest (ribbit) and is prohibited.

Seizing Another's Slave for Labor

Rav Daniel b. Rav Ketina
Rav says: One who seizes another's Canaanite slave and works him is exempt from paying the master.
Gemara
But Rav holds a slave is like real estate — always in the owner's possession! The seizer used the slave, so he should pay!
Gemara
Resolution: He worked the slave outside regular hours — no loss to the master.
Sages
It is satisfactory for the master that his slave's work habits not be undone — even during off-hours.
Principle When no monetary loss occurs and the owner even benefits indirectly, the unauthorized user is exempt.

Courtyard vs. Slave — Benefiting Without Permission

Empty Courtyard

  • Owner incurs no loss
  • An inhabited house stays habitable
  • A desolate house falls to ruin (Isaiah 24:12)
  • Resident does not need to pay rent

Another's Slave

  • Slave may be weakened by extra labor
  • But: work habits are preserved
  • Satisfactory for owner — slave stays productive
  • User is exempt from payment
Key Difference The courtyard analogy is straightforward (no loss), but the slave case requires the additional reasoning that maintaining work habits benefits the owner.

Rav Yosef bar Ḥama's Slave-Seizure Practice

Rav Yosef bar Ḥama seizes slaves of his debtors and works them
↓
His son Rabba challenges him: Why?
↓
Rav Yosef cites Rav Naḥman: A slave isn't worth even his bread
↓
Rabba objects: That applies only to lazy slaves like Daru who dances among wine barrels!
↓
Rav Yosef cites Rav Daniel: Seizing a slave and working him is exempt
↓
Rabba: Since they are your debtors, this looks like ribbit (interest)!
↓
Rav Yosef retracts — ceases the practice
Halacha Even permitted use of another's property becomes prohibited when a creditor-debtor relationship exists, due to the appearance of interest.

Seizing Another's Ship — What Must Be Paid?

Rav
The owner may choose: rental cost or depreciation — whichever is greater.
Shmuel
He can take only the depreciation amount.
Rav Pappa
They don't disagree! Rav discusses a ship commonly available for rental. Shmuel discusses one that is not.
Rav Pappa
Or: Both discuss rental ships. Rav = took it intending to rent. Shmuel = took it as a robber.
Resolution The distinction turns on intent (renter vs. robber) or ship type (rental-available or not).

Repaying Loans with Invalidated Coins

AuthorityRulingReasoning
Rav HunaDebtor pays with currently circulating coinInvalidated coin has no local value
ShmuelDebtor can say: Go spend it in MeishanThe coin still has value elsewhere
Rav NaḥmanShmuel's ruling applies only if creditor intends to travel to MeishanOtherwise the coin is useless to the creditor
Practical Rule A coin invalidated locally but valid elsewhere can repay a debt only if the creditor can actually use it there.
