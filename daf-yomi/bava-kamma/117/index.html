Bava Kamma 117: Fines, Informers & Self-Preservation

Core Question: Can we learn from a knas (fine) and apply it elsewhere?
↓
Halakha
We do learn from it and apply to other cases
Fine (Knas)
We do not learn from it — may be case-specific
↓
Proof from hezek she'eino nikar: Sages had to add each category separately
↓
Applications: Informers who show another's property to gentiles & saving oneself with another's property
Key Theme This daf weaves together the theoretical question of fines vs. halakha with dramatic narratives about informers, self-preservation, and the limits of liability.

Halakha or Fine? Learning from Precedent

Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya
If it is halakha, we learn from it and apply to other cases. If it is a fine (knas), we do not learn from it.
Gemara
Proof: The Sages had to add each category of hezek she'eino nikar separately — rendering food impure, pouring wine as libation, intermingling teruma.
Gemara (Rejection)
Perhaps the additions weren't because it's a fine — but because initially they were only concerned with large financial losses, and later extended to small losses too.
Father of R' Avin
But my version of the baraita has libation (a large loss!) added last — so the 'large vs. small' reasoning doesn't work!
Gemara
That version is explained by the shift from R' Avin's position to R' Yirmeya's — about when monetary and capital liability arise.
Principle A fine (knas) imposed in one case cannot automatically serve as precedent for other cases, unlike a ruling of halakha.

R' Avin vs. R' Yirmeya: Libation & the Death Penalty

One lifts another's wine and pours it as libation for idol worship
↓
R' Avin (Initially)
Lifting + pouring = one act. He incurs the death penalty, so he is exempt from payment (kim lei bidrabba minei)
R' Yirmeya (Ultimately)
At lifting → liable for money (theft). At pouring → liable for death. Since liabilities arise at different times, he pays both.
Kim Lei Bidrabba Minei When one act triggers both a death penalty and monetary liability simultaneously, only the greater punishment (death) applies. R' Yirmeya's innovation: if they arise at different moments, both apply.

Rav Kahana, R' Yochanan & the Serpent

Narrator
Rav Kahana fled to Eretz Yisrael. R' Yochanan found the cave where he was buried, encircled by a serpent with its tail in its mouth.
R' Yochanan
Serpent! Let the teacher enter to be near the disciple. (No response.) Let a colleague enter near his colleague. (No response.)
R' Yochanan
Let the disciple enter near his teacher! (The serpent opened.) R' Yochanan requested mercy and raised Rav Kahana from the dead.
Rav Kahana
If you can ensure I won't die again, I'll come to the study hall. If not, I won't go.
R' Yochanan
What I said was yours (Eretz Yisrael's scholars) is in fact theirs (Babylonia's scholars) — as Rav Kahana resolved all my uncertainties.
Context R' Yochanan had to call himself the disciple and Rav Kahana the teacher before the serpent would open — acknowledging Babylonian Torah greatness.

Saving Oneself with Another's Property: Three Cases

Silver Cup (Deposit)

  • Thieves came upon a bailee
  • He handed them the deposited silver cup
  • Rabba: Exempt
  • Rav Ashi: If wealthy → liable (thieves came for his money). If poor → exempt (they came for the cup)

Captive Redemption Purse

  • Thieves came upon holder of pidyon shvuyim funds
  • He gave the purse to the thieves
  • Rabba: Exempt — 'No greater captive redemption than this!'
  • Using funds to save oneself = fulfilling their purpose

Donkey on the Ferry

  • Man loaded donkey before others boarded
  • Donkey endangered the boat
  • Another man pushed the donkey into the river
  • Rabba: Exempt — the donkey owner was a rodef (pursuer)
Abaye's Challenge In all three cases, Abaye objected: 'He is saving himself with another's property!' Rabba found a distinct justification for each exemption.
