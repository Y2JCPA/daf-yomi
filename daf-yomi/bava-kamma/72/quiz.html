QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does slaughtering a non-sacred animal in the Temple courtyard pose a problem for the fourfold/fivefold payment?
A. Because the slaughter is invalid and the animal is considered alive
B. Because the animal becomes forbidden during slaughter, potentially removing ownership before the act is complete
C. Because Temple slaughter requires specific intent that a thief lacks
D. Because the animal must be returned to the owner before slaughter
Correct: B — If slaughter takes effect throughout the process, the first cut renders the animal forbidden as a non-sacred animal in the azarah. This removes its value/ownership, so when slaughter is completed, it is arguably not the owner's animal being slaughtered — undermining the 4x/5x liability.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is Rav Gamda's solution (in the name of Rava) to the Temple slaughter problem?
A. The thief slaughtered the animal at night when Temple restrictions don't apply
B. The thief partially cut the simanim outside the Temple and finished inside
C. The slaughter was performed by a non-Jewish agent of the thief
D. The animal was consecrated before slaughter, changing its status
Correct: B — Rav Gamda explains the mishna discusses a case where the thief began cutting the trachea and esophagus outside the Temple and completed them inside. This way, the prohibition and the completion of slaughter (triggering 4x/5x liability) occur simultaneously at the end.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
If both sets of witnesses (theft and slaughter) are found to be conspiring, how is the penalty divided?
A. Both sets split the total 4x/5x payment equally
B. The first set pays double payment; the second set pays the remainder (2x for sheep, 3x for ox)
C. Only the second set pays, since they added the more severe testimony
D. The first set pays the full 4x/5x and the second set is exempt
Correct: B — Each set pays what they conspired to impose: the first set conspired to make the defendant pay the double payment (for theft), so they pay that. The second set conspired to add the slaughter/sale liability (2x for sheep, 3x for ox above the double), so they pay that additional amount.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does Rava hold that a conspiring witness is disqualified only from the point he is discovered, not retroactively?
A. Because witnesses have a presumption of innocence until proven guilty in court
B. Because the law of edim zomemim is a Torah novelty (chiddush) — logically it's 2 vs. 2, so we limit the anomaly's scope
C. Because retroactive disqualification would violate the witness's rights
D. Because the Sanhedrin lacked jurisdiction over past cases
Correct: B — Rava argues that the entire concept of edim zomemim is anomalous — why believe the second pair over the first when it's 2 vs. 2? Since the Torah introduced this as a novelty, Rava limits its reach: disqualification applies only going forward, not retroactively.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does one conspiring witness in the FIRST set nullify the entire case, while one in the SECOND set only nullifies the slaughter testimony?
A. Because the first set's testimony is considered more important by Torah law
B. Because without valid theft testimony, there can be no liability for slaughter or sale
C. Because the first set always consists of more reliable witnesses
D. Because the second set's testimony was given later and is therefore weaker
Correct: B — The mishna establishes a dependency principle: liability for slaughter/sale depends entirely on the validity of the theft testimony. If one witness in the first set is invalid, the theft is unestablished — and without theft, there is no basis for slaughter/sale liability. The second set's testimony is structurally dependent on the first.
