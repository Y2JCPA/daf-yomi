Does a Change in Name Count as a Real Change?

Rav Yosef
A beam (korah) built into a building is still called a beam. This is not a genuine shinui hashem — so the thief hasn't acquired it through name change.
Proof
Ezekiel 41:26 calls structural elements 'tzalot' (casings) and 'ubbim' (beams) — beams retain their name even in a house.
R' Zeira
A reversible change in name doesn't count. Since the joist can be removed and called a 'beam' again, this isn't a true shinui hashem.
Implication
R' Zeira implies that an irreversible name change would effect acquisition — unlike Rav Yosef who questions despair altogether.
Key Distinction Rav Yosef denies this is a name change at all; R' Zeira accepts name changes matter but requires irreversibility.

The Mikveh & Hollowed Log Problem

A log is used as a water duct to channel water into a mikveh
↓
When was the log hollowed out?
Hollowed THEN Attached
❌ Invalidates the mikveh — it was a vessel while detached, so water is 'drawn water'
Attached THEN Hollowed
✅ Does not invalidate — it never had vessel status while detached
↓
But if shinui hashem matters, hollowing should create a 'new vessel' even when attached!
↓
Answer: Drawn water for mikveh is rabbinic, so the Sages were lenient for the attached case
Challenge This case tests whether a change in name (log → duct) should create vessel status. The Gemara resolves it by distinguishing rabbinic vs. Torah-level law.

Teruma, Tithes & Consecration — Shinui HaShem in Action

Before Separation

  • Produce is called tevel (untithed)
  • Property is called chullin (non-sacred)
  • Thief possesses stolen goods

After Separation

  • Portion now called teruma or ma'aser
  • Item now called hekdesh (consecrated)
  • Thief's teruma/tithes/consecration are valid
Objection to Rav Yosef A baraita teaches that a thief's consecration and teruma are valid — proving despair effects acquisition! The Sages answer: these involve a shinui hashem (tevel→teruma, chullin→hekdesh), not mere despair.

Rav Chisda's Biblical Source for Shinui

וְהֵשִׁיב אֶת הַגְּזֵלָה אֲשֶׁר גָּזָל
Torah Derivation Rav Chisda says R' Yonatan says: 'And he shall return the stolen item that he took by robbery' (Leviticus 5:23). The phrase 'that he took by robbery' is seemingly superfluous — it teaches: only if the item is in the same state as when stolen must it be returned. If it has changed, the thief pays money and keeps the item.

But isn't that phrase needed to exclude an heir from paying the one-fifth penalty?

Answer: 'His stolen item' alone would teach about heirs. The extra phrase 'that he took by robbery' teaches both lessons: heirs don't add one-fifth, and shinui effects acquisition.

Stolen Animals as Offerings — Does Despair Work?

Rava
'If his offering is a burnt-offering' (Lev 1:3) — his, not one stolen from another. When does this verse apply?
Rava
Before despair, it's obvious one can't consecrate another's property. So the verse must refer to after despair — yet the animal still can't be offered!
Conclusion
This proves despair alone does not effect acquisition for the thief — otherwise he could consecrate the animal as his own.
Challenge
But Rava elsewhere refuted this proof, saying the baraita deals with stealing an already-consecrated animal! Rava contradicts himself.
Resolution
Either Rava retracted one statement, or one of the two statements was actually said by Rav Pappa, not Rava.
Takeaway The Gemara concludes that despair alone does not transfer ownership of a stolen item to the thief.
