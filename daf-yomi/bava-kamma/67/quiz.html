QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to R' Zeira, why doesn't a beam built into a building constitute a shinui hashem (change in name)?
A. Because beams are never renamed when placed in buildings
B. Because the change is reversible — the beam can be removed and called a beam again
C. Because only physical changes count, never name changes
D. Because the Sages enacted a special leniency for building materials
Correct: B — R' Zeira holds that a reversible change in name is not considered a sufficient change. Since the joist can be removed and revert to being called a beam, it doesn't constitute a true shinui hashem.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does a log hollowed out AFTER being attached to the ground not invalidate a mikveh, while one hollowed BEFORE attachment does?
A. The Torah explicitly distinguishes between the two cases
B. A log attached first never had vessel status while detached, and the Sages were lenient since drawn water is rabbinic
C. Hollowing a log after attachment is physically impossible to do properly
D. The name change from 'log' to 'duct' only occurs when hollowed while detached
Correct: B — The distinction is that in the first case, the log already had vessel status while detached. In the second case, it was already part of the ground when hollowed, so it never became a vessel while independent. Since drawn water rules are rabbinic, the Sages were lenient.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How do the Sages explain why a thief's teruma and consecration are valid, despite Rav Yosef's view that despair alone doesn't effect acquisition?
A. The thief acquires through despair in these specific cases as a rabbinic enactment
B. These involve a shinui hashem: tevel becomes teruma, chullin becomes hekdesh
C. The owner implicitly consents to the separation of teruma and tithes
D. Teruma and consecration are valid even without ownership
Correct: B — The Sages reconcile this by noting that these acts involve a change in name (shinui hashem): the produce changes from tevel to teruma, and from chullin to hekdesh. It is the name change — not mere despair — that effects acquisition.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the Gemara's conclusion regarding whether the owner's despair (ye'ush) alone — without any physical or name change — transfers ownership to the thief?
A. Ye'ush always transfers ownership immediately
B. Ye'ush transfers ownership only for movable property
C. Ye'ush alone does NOT effect acquisition for the thief
D. Ye'ush transfers ownership only when combined with the passage of time
Correct: C — The Gemara concludes from Rava's derivation of 'his offering' (Lev 1:3) that despair alone does not transfer ownership. A stolen animal cannot be offered even after the owner despairs, proving that ye'ush without shinui is insufficient.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does Rava say the fourfold/fivefold payment cannot be extended to all animals via a gezera shava (verbal analogy) from the Shabbat verse?
A. The Shabbat verse discusses rest, not theft, so no analogy is possible
B. The Torah repeats 'ox' and 'sheep' in the theft verse, limiting the law specifically to those two animals
C. A gezera shava requires a tradition from Sinai, which doesn't exist here
D. Undomesticated animals are excluded from all monetary penalties by a separate verse
Correct: B — Rava explains that the Torah deliberately repeats 'ox' and 'sheep' twice in the theft verse (Exodus 21:37). This repetition serves to limit the fourfold/fivefold payment exclusively to ox and sheep, blocking any attempt to extend it via verbal analogy.
