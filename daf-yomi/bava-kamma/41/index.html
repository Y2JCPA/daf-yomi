Bava Kamma 41 — The Forewarned Killer Ox

MISHNA: Forewarned ox that kills → owner pays kofer (ransom)
Innocuous ox → exempt from ransom
Both are put to death
↓
PROBLEM: If we kill the ox after its first killing, how does it ever become forewarned?
↓
7 ANSWERS proposed by the Amoraim to resolve this paradox

Key Theme This daf wrestles with the logical paradox of a forewarned killer ox, producing a cascade of creative legal scenarios, then examines prohibitions on the condemned animal.

Abaye vs. Rava: Ransom Without Intent to Injure

Abaye
An ox that trampled a child in the injured party's courtyard — the owner pays ransom, even though the ox did not intend to injure.
Rava
The owner does not pay ransom. Ransom requires that the animal intended to cause injury — like goring with its horn.
Rava
Similarly, an ox that killed through bestiality — its intention was pleasure, not injury. No ransom is owed.
Baraita
A stadium ox (trained to fight) that kills is not put to death and may be offered on the altar — it acted under compulsion.
Principle The debate hinges on whether kofer (ransom) requires the animal's intent to harm, or merely a causal connection to the death.

7 Answers: How Can a Forewarned Killer Ox Exist?

🔵 Rabba: Three near-kills — court assessed that victims would have died had they not escaped
↓
🔴 Rav Ashi: Three gorings — all victims died only after the third goring
↓
🟢 Rav Zevid: It killed three animals (not people) — forewarned but not executed
↓
🟡 Rav Shimi: It killed three gentiles — not executed under Jewish law
↓
🟣 Reish Lakish: It killed three tereifot (terminally ill people) — no death penalty for the ox
↓
🟠 Rav Pappa: It killed and fled to the marsh three times — court couldn't catch it
↓
⚪ Rav Aḥa / Ravina: Procedural complications with conspiring witnesses or witnesses who recognized the owner but not the ox
The Paradox Since an ox is executed after its first killing, the Gemara must find creative scenarios where it kills multiple times without being put to death — allowing it to become forewarned.

Prohibition of Benefit from a Condemned Ox

"Its flesh shall not be eaten"
(Exodus 21:28)
↓
Does this prohibit only eating, or also deriving benefit?
↓
R' Abbahu
Derives both prohibitions — eating and benefit — from 'its flesh shall not be eaten'
Other Tanna'im
Derive benefit prohibition from 'the owner shall be clear' — includes the hide too
↓
The word 'et' (את) in the verse extends the prohibition to what is secondary to the flesh — i.e., the hide
Key Detail Slaughter with flint vs. knife makes no difference — the mishna in Ḥullin establishes that slaughter with a flint is valid, so the distinction is rejected.

Shimon HaAmasoni and the Word 'Et'

את ה׳ אלהיך תירא — לרבות תלמידי חכמים
Famous Aggadah Shimon HaAmasoni (some say Neḥemya HaAmasoni) interpreted every את (et) in the Torah as including an additional halakha.

When he reached "את ה' אלהיך תירא" — "You shall fear the Lord your God" — he withdrew: Whose fear could be added to the fear of God?

His students asked: What about all your previous interpretations?
He replied: "Just as I received reward for expounding, so I receive reward for withdrawing."

Rabbi Akiva later resolved it: את includes Torah scholars — one must fear them as one fears God.
