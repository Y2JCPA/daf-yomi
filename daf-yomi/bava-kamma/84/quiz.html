QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why do the Sages reject R' Shimon ben Yochai's proof that a blind person who blinds another shows 'eye for an eye' must be monetary?
A. Because blind people are exempt from all commandments
B. Because where literal punishment is possible it applies; where not, the person is exempt
C. Because the verse about 'one manner of law' only applies to murder
D. Because monetary payment is always preferred over physical punishment
Correct: B — The Sages argue that 'one manner of law' doesn't require identical treatment in every case — where the literal punishment can be applied, it is; where it cannot (as with a tereifa who kills), the person is simply exempt.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does the School of R' Yishmael derive that bodily injury requires monetary payment?
A. From the word 'eye' which also means a gold coin
B. From the superfluous repetition of 'so shall it be given unto him' when the prior verse already stated the rule
C. From a tradition passed down from Moses at Sinai
D. From comparing injury law to the law of property damage
Correct: B — Leviticus 24:19 already states 'as he has done, so shall it be done.' The repetition in v. 20 ('so shall it be given') is superfluous — the term 'given' (netinah) teaches that the compensation is monetary.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What THREE conditions must be met for Babylonian courts to adjudicate a case as agents of Eretz Yisrael?
A. The case must involve Jews, property, and witnesses
B. The case must be common, involve monetary loss (not a penalty), and not require special appraisal
C. The case must involve Torah law, two witnesses, and a confession
D. The case must be civil, involve less than 200 zuz, and have clear precedent
Correct: B — The Gemara progressively narrows Babylonian jurisdiction: they can only act as agents in cases that are (1) common, (2) involve actual monetary loss rather than a penalty, and (3) don't require specialized appraisal beyond market valuation.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Abaye citing the School of Ḥizkiyya, what logical problem arises if 'eye for an eye' is taken literally?
A. The injurer might die during the punishment, meaning the court took both an eye AND a life for just an eye
B. The injured party might not want the attacker's eye removed
C. Two eyes are never exactly the same size
D. The Torah never specifies which eye should be taken
Correct: A — If taken literally, removing someone's eye could kill them — meaning the court took 'an eye AND a life for an eye,' which the Torah's juxtaposition of 'eye for eye' and 'life for life' implicitly forbids.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does Rava say 'there are no forewarned oxen in Babylonia'?
A. Because oxen in Babylonia were better trained
B. Because Babylonian courts lacked ordained judges (mumḥin) needed to declare an ox forewarned
C. Because the climate in Babylonia made oxen more docile
D. Because Babylonian law did not recognize the category of forewarned animals
Correct: B — Declaring an ox forewarned (mu'ad) requires ordained judges (elohim/mumḥin), and Babylonia had no ordained judges. The only forewarned ox in Babylonia would be one declared forewarned in Eretz Yisrael and then transported — an extremely rare scenario.
