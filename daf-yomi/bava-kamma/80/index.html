Raising Small Animals in Eretz Yisrael

Mishna: One may not raise small domesticated animals in Eretz Yisrael
↓
Butcher's Exception
May buy and keep animals for slaughter — but no longer than 30 days for the last one
Medical Need
A pious man kept a goat for health — yet even he considered it a sin
Repentant Shepherd
May sell animals gradually — not required to liquidate immediately
Key Tension The Sages strictly prohibited raising small animals because they graze on others' crops — yet practical exceptions were carved out for commerce, health, and transition periods.

The Butcher's Exception & Rabban Gamliel's Ruling

General Rule: No raising small domesticated animals in Eretz Yisrael
↓
A butcher may buy and slaughter small animals repeatedly
↓
Last animal purchased must not be kept beyond 30 days
↓
Rabban Gamliel: Keeping briefly is permitted — but tie it to the bed legs, don't let it graze with the flock
Emendation The Gemara corrects the baraita: Rabban Gamliel's students didn't ask about raising animals (the mishna already prohibits that) — they asked about keeping them temporarily.

The Pious Man and His Goat

לסטים מזויין בביתו של זה
Story A ḥasid (pious man) suffered from heart pain. Physicians prescribed warm goat's milk each morning. A she-goat was tied to his bed leg.

When his friends visited, they turned back in horror: "There is an armed bandit (לסטים מזויין) in this man's house!" — comparing the goat to a thief because small animals steal others' crops.

After investigation, his friends found no other sin in his entire life. On his deathbed, the man himself confirmed: "My only sin was keeping this goat, transgressing the words of my colleagues."

Gradual Transitions for Those Leaving Prohibited Practices

Repentant Shepherd

  • Not required to sell all animals at once
  • May sell them gradually over time
  • Court supports the transition process

Convert with Dogs/Pigs

  • Inherited animals as part of estate
  • Not forced to sell immediately
  • May sell gradually at fair prices

Vow to Buy House / Marry in Israel

  • Not forced to take the first option
  • May wait for an appropriate match
  • Intent of the vow is what counts
Principle The Sages interpret obligations charitably — a person is not forced into unreasonable compliance but may fulfill commitments in a measured, dignified way.

Rav's Ruling on Cats After the Baby's Injury

Narrator
At a circumcision celebration, while Rav, Shmuel, and Rav Asi debated who enters first — a cat severed the baby's hand
Rav
A cat: (1) permitted to kill it, (2) prohibited to keep it, (3) no theft prohibition applies, (4) no obligation to return if lost
Gemara
If permitted to kill, why state one can't keep it? — Lest you think killing is optional but keeping is fine
Gemara
If no theft prohibition, why mention returning? — Ravina: The no-return rule refers to its hide after death
R' Shimon b. Elazar
But we learned: One may raise cats — they clean the house of mice!
Resolution Rav's ruling applies to white cats (dangerous). R' Shimon ben Elazar permits black cats (harmless). A black cat offspring of a white cat is also dangerous.

📋 Bava Kamma 80 — Summary Map

Core Prohibition
No raising small domesticated animals in Eretz Yisrael — they graze on others' crops
↓
Permitted Exceptions
Butchers (30-day limit), medical need (tied to bed), village cats/dogs for pest control
Strict Consequences
Even a pious man's only sin was keeping a goat; R' Yishmael's family destroyed for grazing flocks
↓
Extension to Babylonia
Rav applied the Eretz Yisrael prohibition to Babylonia — and further ruled that dangerous (white) cats may not be kept at all
