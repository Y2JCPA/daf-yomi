Bava Kamma 48 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the Gemara distinguish between bringing produce into a courtyard with vs. without permission?
A. To determine if the produce owner can sue
B. Permission changes whether the courtyard is classified as the injured party's domain or public domain
C. Permission determines who owns the produce
D. It affects whether the damage is categorized as Goring or Trampling
Correct: B — With permission, the courtyard is treated as the injured party's domain (making Shen liable). Without permission, it's treated as public domain (Shen exempt).
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why did Rava rule the woman who entered to bake was liable for the goat that ate her dough and died?
A. She entered without permission
B. Baking requires privacy, so the owners left and she assumed safeguarding responsibility
C. The dough was inherently dangerous
D. She should have known the goat was there
Correct: B — Since baking involves exposing one's elbows and requires privacy, the courtyard owners absented themselves, transferring safeguarding responsibility to the woman.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
If an ox without permission digs a pit in someone's courtyard and a person falls in, who is liable for the Pit damage?
A. Only the ox's owner
B. No one — the Torah exempts pits dug by animals
C. The courtyard owner, because he should have filled the pit
D. Both are equally liable
Correct: C — Although the Torah limits Pit liability to 'a man who opens a pit,' the courtyard owner's failure to fill the pit makes him legally equivalent to having dug it himself.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rava, when is an ox's owner liable for contaminating water in a pit the ox fell into?
A. Always, regardless of timing
B. Only if the ox was forewarned
C. Only if the water was contaminated at the moment of the fall
D. Only if the ox survived the fall
Correct: C — Rava distinguishes: contamination at the time of falling creates liability, but later contamination (decomposition or stench) is either a Pit-utensils exemption or indirect causation (gerama), both of which are exempt.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Ulla explain the mishna's ruling that the ox's owner pays ransom when the ox falls on someone in a pit?
A. The ox is forewarned and pays full ransom per standard rules
B. R' Yosei HaGelili holds an innocuous ox pays half ransom
C. R' Yosei HaGelili (ransom for tam) combined with R' Tarfon (full damages in injured party's domain) = full ransom
D. The courtyard owner pays ransom, not the ox's owner
Correct: C — Ulla combines two opinions: R' Yosei HaGelili (ransom applies even for innocuous oxen) and R' Tarfon (Goring in the injured party's courtyard requires full payment), yielding a full ransom despite the ox being innocuous.
