Permission & Liability in Another's Courtyard

Someone brings property into
another's courtyard
↓
Was permission granted?
With Permission
Courtyard = domain of injured party
Owner accepts safeguarding responsibility
→ Liable for damage
Without Permission
Courtyard treated as public domain
No safeguarding accepted
→ Exempt from damage
Core Principle Permission transforms the legal status of the courtyard and determines who bears responsibility for safeguarding.

Permission & Damage Categories

Questioner
Why should it matter if produce was brought in with or without permission? The stranger's ox did the damage either way!
Sages
With permission → damage is Shen (Eating) in the injured party's domain → liable
Sages
Without permission → damage is Shen (Eating) in the public domain → exempt
Questioner
A baraita says: ox brought in with permission and gored — liable. Without permission — exempt. Doesn't this show the courtyard owner accepted all damage responsibility?
Sages
No — the liable/exempt one is the goring ox's owner. Per R' Tarfon: Keren in the injured party's domain → full damages; in public domain → half damages.
Key Distinction Permission changes the domain classification, which determines whether Shen or Keren liability applies at full or reduced rates.

The Woman Who Baked — Rava's Ruling

A woman enters a house with permission to bake
↓
The homeowner's goat eats her dough,
becomes overheated, and dies
↓
Is the woman liable for the goat?
Grinding Wheat
No privacy needed →
owners remain present →
Their responsibility to safeguard
Baking (Our Case)
Privacy needed (exposed elbows) →
owners leave →
Her responsibility to safeguard
Rava's Ruling The woman is liable. Since baking requires privacy and the owners absented themselves, she accepted responsibility for safeguarding everything in the courtyard.

Ox Digs Pits in the Courtyard

Ox Owner's Liability

  • Ox entered without permission
  • Ox dug pits, ditches, or caves
  • Owner liable for damage to courtyard
  • Also liable if ox injures the homeowner

Courtyard Owner's Liability

  • Torah says: 'If a man opens a pit' — not an ox
  • But courtyard owner should have filled the pit
  • Failure to fill = as if he dug it himself
  • Liable for Pit damages to others who fall in
Rava's Principle Even though the ox dug the pit, the courtyard owner's failure to fill it makes him the legal creator of the hazard.

Contaminated Water: Direct vs Indirect Damage

ScenarioLiable?Reasoning
Ox contaminates water at time of fall✅ LiableDirect damage caused by the ox's entry
Ox dies and carcass decomposes in water❌ ExemptOx = Pit; water = utensils; no Pit liability for utensils
Ox contaminates water with its stench❌ ExemptDamage by indirect action (gerama) — not directly caused
Rava's Distinction Only contamination at the moment of falling creates liability. Post-fall contamination is either a Pit-utensils exemption (Shmuel) or indirect causation (Rav).

Ransom for an Ox Falling into a Pit

Mishnah
If the homeowner's father or son were in the pit when the ox fell in and died — the ox's owner pays ransom.
Rav
This is an ox forewarned (mu'ad) for falling on people in pits.
Rav Yosef
But then the ox should be put to death! No — it saw grass on the edge and fell accidentally. No death penalty, but ransom still applies.
Shmuel
The ox is innocuous (tam). Per R' Yosei HaGelili, an innocuous ox's owner pays half ransom.
Ulla
R' Yosei HaGelili + R' Tarfon: Keren in the injured party's domain → full damages, so full ransom even for a tam.
Three Views Rav: forewarned ox, full ransom. Shmuel: innocuous ox, half ransom. Ulla: innocuous ox but full ransom via R' Tarfon's rule about the injured party's domain.
