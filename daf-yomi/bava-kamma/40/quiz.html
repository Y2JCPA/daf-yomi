QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov, when is an ox owner liable for damages?
A. Only when no safeguarding was provided at all
B. When reduced safeguarding was provided but not superior
C. When superior safeguarding was provided but the ox escaped
D. When the ox was forewarned and any level of safeguarding failed
Correct: A — R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov holds that for both innocuous and forewarned oxen, reduced safeguarding exempts the owner. Liability exists only when no safeguarding was provided at all.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why are stewards exempt from paying ransom (kofer) for orphans' oxen that kill?
A. Stewards don't own the ox
B. Orphans have no obligation of atonement since they lack moral responsibility
C. Ransom only applies to forewarned oxen
D. The Torah exempts court-appointed guardians from all payments
Correct: B — Since ransom serves as atonement (kaparah), and orphans are not morally responsible, their stewards cannot fulfill an atonement obligation on their behalf.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the core disagreement between the Rabbanan and R' Yishmael son of R' Yoḥanan ben Beroka regarding ransom?
A. Whether ransom applies to innocuous oxen
B. Whether the amount is based on the victim's value or the owner's value
C. Whether ransom can be paid in installments
D. Whether stewards can pay ransom on behalf of orphans
Correct: B — The Rabbanan hold the ransom amount equals the victim's value (derived from a verbal analogy to miscarriage damages), while R' Yishmael holds it equals the owner's value (from 'redemption of his life').
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does the Gemara resolve the borrower's claim that he could have smuggled the ox to the marsh?
A. The borrower has a legal duty not to smuggle property
B. The court seized the ox before the borrower had the chance
C. The marsh is too far to practically smuggle an ox
D. The injured party would have found it anyway
Correct: B — The Gemara establishes that the court seized the ox first, before the borrower could admit liability or hide it, eliminating the borrower's hypothetical defenses.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rav Pappa, why does an ox remain forewarned when lent to a borrower despite his view that change of custody normally resets status?
A. The borrower accepted liability when borrowing
B. Wherever the ox goes, the name of its owner is upon it — so no true custody change occurred
C. Borrowing is not considered a legal change of custody
D. The ox's behavior doesn't change just because it moved locations
Correct: B — Rav Pappa holds that since the ox remains identified with its owner even while borrowed, it hasn't truly undergone a change of custody, so its forewarned status persists.
