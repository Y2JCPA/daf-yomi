The Ox's Legal Journey: Before & After Verdict

Ox gores and kills a person
↓
Has the court issued its verdict (din)?
Before Verdict
Owner can sell, consecrate, or slaughter the ox — all valid. Bailee can return it.
After Verdict
Sale invalid, consecration invalid, meat forbidden. Bailee's return is disputed.
Key Principle The verdict transforms the ox's legal status entirely — it is considered already dead.
3 / 9

Before vs. After the Verdict

Before Verdict

  • ✅ Sale is valid — ox belongs to purchaser
  • ✅ Consecration takes effect
  • ✅ Slaughter permitted — meat may be eaten
  • ✅ Bailee returning ox = valid return

After Verdict

  • ❌ Sale is not valid — ox no longer 'his'
  • ❌ Consecration does not take effect
  • ❌ Meat is forbidden even if slaughtered
  • ❌ Bailee's return is disputed (Rabbis vs. R' Ya'akov)
Insight Once sentenced, the ox is legally considered dead — the owner has no ownership rights to transfer.

Can a Bailee Return a Condemned Ox?

Rabbis
After the verdict, returning the ox is not valid. The bailee deprived the owner of the chance to hide it.
R' Ya'akov
Even after the verdict, returning it is valid — 'that which is yours is before you.'
Rabba
Everyone agrees on hinei shelkha lefanekha for prohibited items. The real dispute is: Can an ox be sentenced in absentia?
Rabbis (per Rabba)
No — verdict requires the ox's presence. Just as a person is sentenced in their presence, so too an ox. Had the bailee returned it, the owner could have hidden it!
R' Ya'akov (per Rabba)
An ox can be sentenced in absentia — unlike a person, it cannot present claims in its own defense. The verdict would happen regardless.
Source Exodus 21:29 — 'The ox shall be stoned, and its owner also shall be put to death' — comparing the ox's trial to a human trial.

Why Is the Unpaid Bailee Exempt?

Ox in bailee's care gores and kills
↓
Did the bailee provide reduced safeguarding (שמירה פחותה)?
Yes — Reduced Guard
Unpaid bailee = fulfilled duty → exempt from reimbursing owner
Paid bailee / borrower / renter = NOT fulfilled duty → must reimburse owner
No — No Guard at All
ALL bailees are liable, including unpaid
Talmudic Logic The case specifically involves reduced but not superior safeguarding — the middle ground that distinguishes unpaid from paid bailees.
7 / 9

Whose Opinion Does the Baraita Follow?

Sages
If it's R' Meir (renter = unpaid bailee), it should exempt the renter too! If it's R' Yehuda (renter = paid bailee), forewarned oxen should need only reduced guarding — all exempt from ransom!
Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana
It follows R' Eliezer: no safeguarding suffices except slaughter. And regarding the renter, it follows R' Yehuda (renter = paid bailee).
Abaye
Actually it follows R' Meir — but per Rabba bar Avuh's reversal: R' Meir says a renter is like a paid bailee, and R' Yehuda says like an unpaid bailee.
Note Rabba bar Avuh reverses the traditional attribution — R' Meir and R' Yehuda swap positions on the renter's status.
