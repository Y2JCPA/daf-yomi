What Does 'The Owner Shall Be Clear' Exclude?

R' Yosei HaGelili
The verse 'the owner of the ox shall be clear' teaches exemption from paying compensation for miscarried offspring (ולדות) caused by an innocuous ox.
R' Akiva
That's unnecessary! Exodus 21:22 already implies: men who cause miscarriage are liable, but ox owners are not — by inference from the word 'men.'
Abaye & Rava
R' Yosei HaGelili needs the verse for a different reason: when the woman dies, men receive capital punishment and are exempt from payment — but ox owners would still pay. The verse excludes this.
R' Akiva
I interpret 'the owner shall be clear' differently: it exempts the innocuous ox owner from paying the thirty-shekel fine for a Canaanite slave.
Core Dispute Multiple Tannaim derive different exemptions from the same verse — each reflecting a different understanding of ox-owner liability.

The Fisherman Analogy: How Did Rabbi Eliezer Present His Answers?

Rav Kahana
Rabbi Eliezer gave the better explanation first, then added the weaker one — like a fisherman who catches big fish first and also keeps small ones.
Rav Tavyumei / Rava
No — he gave the weaker explanation first, then replaced it with the better one — like a fisherman who catches small fish, then discards them when he finds big ones.
Methodology This dispute reflects how Talmudic scholars understood the process of refining arguments — do you keep all answers or replace weaker ones?

R' Yosei HaGelili: Exemption from Miscarried Offspring

An innocuous ox causes a pregnant woman to miscarry
↓
Is the owner liable for compensation for miscarried offspring (דמי ולדות)?
↓
R' Yosei HaGelili
Exempt — derived from 'the owner shall be clear' (Exodus 21:28)
R' Akiva's Challenge
Verse is unnecessary — Exodus 21:22 already limits liability to men, not ox owners
Key Verse Exodus 21:22 — 'If men struggle and hurt a pregnant woman...' implies only men, not oxen, trigger this liability.

Three Attempts to Justify R' Yosei HaGelili's Reading

Rav Ulla bar Rav Idi

  • Exodus 21:22 excludes only forewarned oxen (parallel to men)
  • Innocuous ox still needs a verse for exemption
  • REJECTED by Rava: makes innocuous MORE liable than forewarned!

Rava's Attempt

  • Men liable; forewarned oxen exempt by inference
  • Innocuous oxen exempt by a fortiori
  • Verse teaches: only innocuous exempt, forewarned LIABLE
  • REJECTED by Abaye: should also apply to humiliation!

Abaye & Rava (Final)

  • When woman dies: men get capital punishment, exempt from payment
  • But ox owners would still owe payment even if she dies
  • Verse teaches: ox owner is ALSO exempt from offspring payment
  • ACCEPTED as the correct reading
Principle 'The native is on the ground and the stranger is in the heavens' — Rava's idiom for an argument that inverts the expected stringency hierarchy.

R' Akiva's Interpretation: Exemption for Canaanite Slave

📜 A forewarned ox that kills a Canaanite slave → owner pays fixed thirty shekel fine
↓
🐂 What if an innocuous ox kills a slave? Torah is stricter with slave (fixed payment even if worth less)
↓
🤔 Maybe innocuous ox owner should also pay from superior-quality property (not just ox's body)?
↓
📖 Verse teaches: 'The owner shall be clear' — exempt from paying for a Canaanite slave killed by innocuous ox
↓
✅ Baraita confirms: R' Akiva says 'he shall be clear from payment for a Canaanite slave'
Rava's Logic Since Torah imposes a fixed 30 shekel for a slave (even if worth only 1 sela) — more stringent than freeman — we might think innocuous ox liability extends too. The verse prevents this.
