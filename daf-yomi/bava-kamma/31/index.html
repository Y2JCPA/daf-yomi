Key Mishna The first potter who stumbles is liable for the damage to the second — the Gemara debates why.
3 / 9

Why Is the First Potter Liable?

R' Yoḥanan
The mishna holds him liable because he had the opportunity to stand up after falling, and he did not. Even the Rabbis who say stumbling is ones (beyond one's control) agree here.
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
Even if he couldn't stand up, he had the opportunity to warn the person behind him, and he didn't warn him.
R' Yoḥanan
If he couldn't stand up, he was too busy trying to rise to warn anyone. The mishna must mean he could have stood up.
Core Dispute R' Yoḥanan requires the ability to stand; Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says the duty to warn alone suffices for liability.

Rava's Distinction: Body Damage vs. Property Damage

First Person (Original Stumbler)

  • Liable for body damage to the second ✓
  • Liable for property damage to the second ✓
  • He is clearly negligent — full liability

Second Person (Fell Over First)

  • Liable for body damage to the third ✓
  • Exempt from property damage to the third ✗
  • His body on the ground = a pit — and pit law exempts vessel damage
Principle The second person's body becomes like a bor (pit) in the public domain. Under pit liability, one does not pay for damage to vessels (kelim).
7 / 9

Is the Fallen Body a 'Pit'? Rav vs. Shmuel

Shmuel
Any obstacle in the public domain is a bor (pit). The second person's fallen body qualifies — so he's exempt from vessel damage.
Rav
An obstacle is only a bor if the owner renounces ownership (hefker). A person doesn't renounce ownership of his own body!
Gemara
For Rav's view, we revert to Rava's original explanation: the second is exempt from property damage because he didn't create the obstacle — the first person's fall caused his items to be on the ground.
Resolution Rav Adda bar Minyumi explains the baraita as referring to vessels damaged by vessels — broken items on the ground have pit status, exempting their owner from vessel-to-vessel damage.
