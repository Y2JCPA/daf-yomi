Passing Judgment to Heaven

R' Ḥanan
One who passes judgment of another to Heaven is punished first. Sarah said: "The Lord judge between me and you" (Genesis 16:5) — and Sarah died before Abraham.
Gemara
This applies only where he has someone to do judgment for him on earth and has no need to appeal to the heavenly court.
R' Yitzḥak
Woe to he who cries out to Heaven more than the one about whom he is crying out.
Baraita
Both the one who cries out and the one cried about are included in "If you afflict them… I will surely hear their cry" (Exodus 22:22–23) — but they are quicker to punish the one who cries out.
Lesson Seek earthly justice before appealing to Heaven — divine judgment may rebound on the petitioner first.

The Curse of an Ordinary Person

בראשית כ:טז, כז:א
R' Yitzḥak The curse of an ordinary person should never be regarded as light. Abimelech cursed Sarah: "Behold, it is to you a covering of the eyes" (Genesis 20:16) — and it was fulfilled in her descendant Isaac, whose "eyes were dim, so that he could not see" (Genesis 27:1).

Consent to Injury: Limb Loss vs. Pain

Loss of Limb (Blind eye, cut hand)

  • Says: 'On condition you are exempt'
  • Assailant is still LIABLE
  • Rav Sheshet: Causes מום משפחה (family flaw)
  • Cannot forgive harm to family's reputation
  • R' Yoḥanan: 'Yes' that is really a 'No'

Pain Without Limb Loss (Strike me)

  • Says: 'On condition you are exempt'
  • Assailant is EXEMPT
  • No lasting family flaw results
  • Personal pain can be forgiven
  • Baraita: Exemption stands
Key Distinction Rav Sheshet explains: A person can forgive his own pain, but he cannot forgive damage that creates a family flaw (מום משפחה) — the stigma affects others, not just himself.

Why Can't One Consent to Limb Loss?

R' Oshaya
Because loss of a limb causes a family flaw (מום משפחה) — he cannot forgo what harms his family.
Rava
A person simply does not forgo compensation for damage to his extremities — he was not sincere when he said 'exempt.'
R' Yoḥanan
There is a 'yes' that is like a 'no' — when he said 'on condition to be exempt,' it was rhetorical, not true consent.
Rav Asi bar Ḥama
Rava, if a person never forgives bodily harm, why does the baraita say 'strike me' on condition of exemption — and he's exempt?
Rav Sheshet
The distinction is family flaw. Limb loss = family flaw (can't forgive). Pain alone = personal (can forgive).
Three Opinions R' Oshaya and Rav Sheshet focus on mum mishpacha; Rava on insincere forgiveness; R' Yoḥanan on the 'yes that is no' principle.
