Appraising Crop Damage: From Field to Courtroom

Animal eats produce from another's field
↓
What stage of growth was the produce?
Young branches/shoots
Appraise vineyard before vs. after damage (relative to 60× area)
Budding/unripe grapes
View as if ready to be picked and appraise accordingly
Ripe produce (R' Shimon)
Pay full value of what was eaten
Core Principle The Torah's appraisal method depends on how close the produce was to being harvested — the closer to ripe, the more directly we value what was lost.

How to Appraise Damage to Unripe Grapes

R' Yehoshua
View the damaged grapes as if they were about to be picked and appraise them at that higher value.
Rabbis
Compare how much the vineyard was worth before the animal ate and how much it is worth now.
R' Shimon ben Yehuda (in name of R' Shimon)
It depends on the stage of growth: young branches → before/after method; budding grapes or unripe figs → view as ready to be picked.
Ravina
Combine both clauses of R' Shimon ben Yehuda: young branches use before/after; budding, unripe, and half-ripe are all viewed as ready to pick.
Key Distinction The difference between R' Shimon ben Yehuda and R' Yehoshua concerns weakening of the vine (כחש דגופנא) — whether to deduct the benefit of the vine no longer being drained by the grapes.

Principle The more developed the produce, the more directly we measure the actual loss rather than appraising the diminished field value.

"Best of His Field" — R' Yishmael vs. R' Akiva

R' Yishmael

  • "Best of his field" = best-quality field of the injured party
  • Appraisal based on produce remaining in the field once it ripens
  • Rejects Rav Idi bar Avin's interpretation
  • Does NOT apply burden of proof (המוציא מחבירו) to pay less
  • Agrees with R' Yosei HaGelili: assess based on what remains

R' Akiva

  • "Best of his field" = collect from superior-quality land of the damager
  • Verse allows collecting from the best assets of the liable party
  • Derives by kal va-chomer: applies to consecrated property too
  • Compensation for damaging hekdesh is from one's best assets
  • Focus is on collection method, not appraisal method
Abaye's Insight R' Yosei HaGelili and R' Yishmael both hold: appraisal is based on the value of produce remaining in the field once it ripens — linking damage to agricultural potential.

The Story of Eliezer Ze'eira in Neharde'a

👟 Eliezer Ze'eira wears black shoes in the market — unusual for Jews of that time
↓
👮 Officials of the Exilarch confront him: Who are you to publicly mourn Jerusalem?
↓
🏛️ They imprison him for acting against custom; he claims to be a great scholar
↓
❓ They test him: One who cuts a cluster of date flowers — what does he pay?
↓
📐 He answers: Appraise relative to an area sixty times the size of the damage
↓
📜 They verify with Shmuel, who confirms: the halakha is as Eliezer Ze'eira says
↓
🔓 Recognizing his greatness, the officials release him
Halakhic Proof Eliezer Ze'eira demonstrated mastery of the sixty-times appraisal (שישים) — the same method discussed throughout this sugya for valuing crop damage in context of the surrounding field.

Stacking Produce in Another's Field

Person stacks produce in another's field
↓
Was permission granted?
Without Permission
Field owner's animal eats it → Exempt
Animal injured by produce → Stack owner liable
With Permission
Field owner is liable for damage to the produce
↓
But doesn't R' Yehuda HaNasi require explicit acceptance of responsibility?
↓
Rav Pappa: This refers to a supervisor of threshing floors — saying "bring it in" implies "I will watch it"
Rule Permission to enter creates a duty of care. The threshing-floor supervisor's invitation inherently includes responsibility.
