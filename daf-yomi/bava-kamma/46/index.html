Bava Kamma 46: Uncertainty, Burden of Proof & Dangerous Oxen

KEY THEME This daf addresses how courts handle uncertainty in damage cases: from a forewarned ox that R' Eliezer says must be slaughtered, to the foundational principle that the burden of proof rests upon the claimant (ha-motzi me-chaveiro alav ha-re'ayah). The Gemara applies this to cases of a goring ox with an uncertain fetus, disputed sales of dangerous animals, and courtroom procedure.
3 / 9

Why Must a Forewarned Ox Be Slaughtered?

R' Eliezer
An ox has no sufficient safeguarding other than slaughtering it with a knife.
Rabba
The verse says "the owner has not secured it" (Exodus 21:36) — once forewarned, no safeguarding suffices.
Abaye
But a pit uses similar language and can be covered! If someone covers a pit properly, he's exempt!
Abaye
Rather, the reason is R' Natan's principle: "You shall not bring blood into your house" (Deut. 22:8) — one may not keep a dangerous animal, just as one may not keep a vicious dog or unstable ladder.
PRINCIPLE R' Eliezer's ruling is about removing danger from one's property, not about the impossibility of physical restraint.

The Case: Ox Gores a Pregnant Cow

🐂 Innocuous ox gores a cow
Cow dies; fetus found dead at its side
↓
❓ Did the cow give birth before or after the goring?
Before goring
Fetus death unrelated to ox — no payment for fetus
After goring
Fetus died from goring — ox owner pays half-damages for fetus
↓
Since it's uncertain: pays ½ damages for cow + ¼ damages for fetus
SUMAKHOS Uncertain property is divided between the parties — hence half of the half-damages (= ¼) for the fetus.

Sumakhos vs. The Rabbis on Uncertainty

Sumakhos

  • Property of uncertain ownership is divided
  • Pays ½ damages for cow + ¼ for fetus
  • Uncertainty = split the difference
  • This is the ruling of our Mishna (per Shmuel)

The Rabbis

  • Ha-motzi me-chaveiro alav ha-re'ayah
  • Burden of proof rests on the claimant
  • No payment for fetus without proof
  • Even if claimant says 'I'm certain' and defendant says only 'perhaps' — still no payment
KEY DISPUTE This is called a significant principle of monetary law — even a definite claim vs. a speculative defense requires proof.

Selling an Ox That Turns Out to Gore

Rav
This is a mistaken transaction (mekach ta'ut) — the buyer can void the sale. Most people buy oxen for plowing, and a goring ox is unfit.
Shmuel
The seller can say: "I sold it to you for slaughter" — the goring is irrelevant. We don't follow the majority in monetary matters.
Gemara
We limit the dispute to a buyer who buys for both purposes, where the price was ambiguous, and the seller has funds to refund.
Shmuel
We follow the majority only for issur v'heter (permitted/prohibited status), not for money. In money — burden of proof on the claimant.
CORE ISSUE Can we apply the principle of following the majority (rov) to take money from someone? Shmuel says no.

Source for 'Burden of Proof on the Claimant'

📖 R' Shmuel bar Nachmani: "Whoever has a cause, let him come near" (Exodus 24:14) — the claimant must bring proof
↓
🤔 Rav Ashi objects: This is just common sense! One who suffers pain goes to the doctor — the one with the claim bears the burden
↓
📜 Rather, the verse teaches: the court hears the claimant first, then the defendant (Rav Nachman / Rabba bar Avuh)
↓
⚠️ Exception (Sages of Neharde'a): If the defendant's assets are depreciating due to the pending claim, the court hears him first
PROCEDURE The verse establishes courtroom order: claimant speaks first — unless delay causes the defendant financial loss.
