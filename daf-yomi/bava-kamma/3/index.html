Why Must the Torah Prove 'And He Sends Forth' = Trampling?

Gemara
"And he sends forth" must mean Trampling (Regel). Goring has its own verse, Eating has its own verse — what else could it be?
Gemara Answers
Without the proof-verse, you might think both "sends forth" and "consumed" refer to Eating — one for complete destruction, one for partial.
Gemara
So Isaiah 32:20 — "send forth the feet of the ox" — proves it means Trampling, not a second type of Eating.
Follow-up
But now how do we know Eating applies even when damage is not complete destruction? The verse says "consumed" — implying total consumption!
Gemara
Through hekkesh (juxtaposition): Eating is compared to Trampling. Just as Trampling applies whether fully or partially destroyed, so too Eating.
Hekkesh Juxtaposition of Shen and Regel in the same verse allows each category to learn from the other's broader scope.

What Each Category Learns from the Other via Hekkesh

Eating (Shen) Learns from Trampling

  • Verse "and it consumed" implies complete destruction
  • Hekkesh teaches: liability even if damage is NOT complete
  • Just as Trampling applies to partial damage, so does Eating

Trampling (Regel) Learns from Eating

  • Verse "and he sends forth" implies the owner sent the animal
  • Hekkesh teaches: liability even if animal went on its OWN
  • Just as Eating applies when animal acts alone, so does Trampling
Mutual Teaching The Torah placed both categories in one verse so each could expand the other's scope of liability.

Why Are Both Phrases Needed? Can't 'Sends Forth' Cover Both?

"And he sends forth" could mean both:
• Trampling — "send forth the feet" (Isaiah 32:20)
• Eating — "teeth of animals I will send forth" (Deut. 32:24)
↓
Without "and it consumed," we'd only know one category applies — not both
↓
Only Trampling?
Damage is commonplace
(hezeko matzui)
Only Eating?
Animal derives pleasure
(hana'ah l'hezeko)
↓
✅ Both phrases needed to establish two separate categories with distinct characteristics
Answer Each category has a unique feature — Trampling's commonplace damage and Eating's pleasure — so one phrase cannot subsume the other.

Tzrorot: The Unique Subcategory

צרורות — חצי נזק — הלכתא גמירי לה
Halakha L'Moshe Mi'Sinai Tzrorot (צרורות) — pebbles inadvertently propelled by a walking animal's foot — are a subcategory of Trampling (Regel), yet the owner pays only half the cost of the damage. This rule is a halakha l'Moshe mi'Sinai (a law transmitted by tradition from Sinai), not derived from any verse. This is what Rav Pappa meant: among the primary categories, Trampling alone has a subcategory whose law differs from the primary category.
