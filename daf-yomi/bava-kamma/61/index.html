David's Dilemma: 'He Would Not Drink It'

Questioner
According to Rava/Rav Naḥman, who says David asked about concealed articles in fire — since they sent him an answer of halakha, what does 'he would not drink it' mean?
Gemara
He did not say the halakha in their names. Tradition from the court of Shmuel of Rama: one who risks his life for Torah — we don't cite halakha in his name, lest others endanger themselves.
Questioner
And 'he poured it out to the Lord' — what does that mean if it was a halakhic ruling, not a personal leniency?
Gemara
Because they said the halakha as a tradition (kabbalah) — anonymously, without attributing it to any individual.
Principle One who endangers his life to transmit Torah — we do not cite halakha in his name, to prevent others from following dangerously.

Rav Pappa's Resolution The Mishna counts down from above (6→5→4 = exempt). The Baraita counts up from below (2→3→4 = liable). Both agree: at exactly 4 cubits, the status changes.

Rav vs. Shmuel: High Flame vs. Low Flame

Rav

  • Mishna's exemption for crossing a road applies to a high-blazing flame (kolaḥat)
  • A low-creeping flame (nikhpefet) stays close to the ground
  • Low flame: liable even up to 100 cubits
  • Supported by a Baraita

Shmuel

  • Mishna's exemption for crossing a road applies to a low-blazing flame (nikhpefet)
  • A high-blazing flame is more easily stopped by gaps
  • High flame: exempt even with any minimal gap
  • Opposite logic of Rav
Key Dispute They disagree which type of flame the Mishna's public-road exemption addresses — the practical difference is whether a low-creeping fire makes one liable across great distances.

How Far Does Fire Liability Extend? (Mishna 61b)

🔥 One kindles a fire on his own premises
↓
How far can the fire travel
before the kindler is exempt?
R' Elazar b. Azaria
Half a beit kor
(treats fire location as center)
R' Eliezer
16 cubits
(width of public domain)
R' Akiva
50 cubits
R' Shimon
No fixed limit — depends on the size of the fire
↓
✅ Halakha follows R' Shimon
(Shmuel via Rav Yehuda; Rav Naḥman)
R' Shimon Explained Rav Naḥman/Rabba bar Avuh: 'Everything according to the fire' means — a small fire has limited liability distance; a large fire extends liability further. The verse shallem yeshallem teaches proportionality.

Concealed Vessels in a Burning Stack (Mishna 61b)

R' Yehuda
One who kindles a fire that burns another's stack must pay for everything inside, including concealed vessels (tamun ba'esh).
Rabbis
He pays only for the stack of grain itself. He is not liable for concealed items — he couldn't have known they were there.
Rabbis
But we concede: if one sets fire to a building, he pays for everything inside — it is normal (derekh) to keep items in houses.
Rav Kahana
The dispute is only when fire started on one's own property and spread. If kindled on another's property, all agree: liable for everything.
Rava
No — if the Rabbis conceded for another's property, the Mishna would distinguish within the same case. The dispute must cover both scenarios.
Rava's View R' Yehuda and the Rabbis disagree about concealed items in two situations — fire on one's own property that spreads AND fire on another's property.
