Bava Kamma 47 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava, why can the injured party collect from a cow's offspring but NOT from a hen's egg?
A. The offspring is more valuable than the egg
B. A fetus is an integral part of the body; an egg is merely a secretion
C. Cows cause more damage than hens
D. Eggs belong to the buyer, not the hen's owner
Correct: B — Rava distinguishes between a fetus (ever — integral limb/part of the body) and an egg (pirsha — a mere secretion), making only the fetus available for debt collection.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Rava rule that damage to a pregnant cow must be assessed as ONE unit rather than cow + calf separately?
A. It is too difficult to assess two items separately
B. The Torah explicitly requires joint assessment
C. Separate assessment unfairly increases the liable party's payment beyond actual loss
D. The calf has no independent market value
Correct: C — A newborn calf's standalone market value exceeds the price difference between a pregnant and non-pregnant cow. Separate assessment would 'weaken' (overcharge) the damager beyond the real depreciation.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
In the Mishna, if a potter brings pots into a courtyard WITHOUT permission and the owner's animal breaks them, who is liable?
A. The homeowner is liable for the broken pots
B. They split the cost equally
C. No one is liable — the homeowner is exempt
D. The potter can collect half-damages
Correct: C — Without permission, the potter entered at his own risk. The homeowner bears no responsibility for damage to the pots, but the potter IS liable if the animal is injured by his pots.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rava resolve the apparent contradiction within the Mishna between the Rabbis' and R' Yehuda HaNasi's views?
A. The entire mishna follows R' Yehuda HaNasi
B. The beginning follows the Rabbis entirely — permission creates a one-directional duty on the courtyard owner
C. Each clause follows a different Tanna with no resolution
D. The mishna is rejected as non-authoritative
Correct: B — Rava reads the beginning as entirely following the Rabbis: with permission, the courtyard owner safeguards items (even from wind), but the potter accepts no reciprocal duty to safeguard the owner's property.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rav, if someone places produce in another's courtyard without permission and the animal EATS it and becomes sick, what is the ruling?
A. The produce owner is fully liable
B. The produce owner is exempt — the animal should not have eaten it
C. The produce owner pays half-damages
D. The courtyard owner is liable for not controlling his animal
Correct: B — Rav distinguishes slipping (liable) from eating (exempt): an animal that voluntarily eats something bears its own responsibility. The produce owner is only liable if the animal slipped and fell on the produce.
