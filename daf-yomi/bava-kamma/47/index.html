Bava Kamma 47: Permission, Property & Liability

Core Question: When items or animals enter another's property, who bears liability for resulting damage?
↓
Without Permission
The one who entered bears liability for damage caused by their property
With Permission
The courtyard owner bears liability — but scope is debated
Key Debate Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi requires explicit acceptance of responsibility; the Rabbis hold that granting permission implicitly creates a duty to safeguard.

Fetus vs. Egg: Collecting from Offspring

🐄 Cow's Fetus

  • Fetus is an integral part of the cow's body
  • Injured party can collect damages from the offspring
  • If cow is missing, collect from offspring

🐔 Hen's Egg

  • Egg is merely a secretion (pirsha)
  • Injured party cannot collect from the egg
  • Payment only from the hen's body itself
Rava's Principle The biological relationship determines collectibility: an integral limb (ever) vs. a waste product (pirsha).

Appraising Damage to a Pregnant Cow

Rava
We appraise the offspring together with the cow as one unit — not separately. Otherwise, the liable party is unfairly weakened (paying more than the actual loss).
Rav Aḥa b. Rava
But if that is the correct halakha for assessment, why shouldn't the liable party simply pay it?
Rav Ashi
Because the damager can say: 'I damaged a pregnant cow' — so I pay for a pregnant cow's depreciation, not for a cow + calf separately.
Applied Principle This same rule applies to severing a slave's hand (value with/without hand) and damaging part of a field (value as part of larger area).

Mishna: Potter's Pots in the Courtyard

A potter brings pots into a homeowner's courtyard
↓
Was permission granted?
No Permission
Animal breaks pots → Owner exempt
Animal injured by pots → Potter liable
With Permission
Animal breaks pots → Owner liable
Potter did not accept duty to safeguard owner's animal
Same Rule Identical framework applies to produce and oxen brought into another's courtyard.

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi vs. the Rabbis on Implicit Responsibility

Rabbis
Granting permission implicitly means the homeowner accepts responsibility to safeguard the items brought in.
R' Yehuda HaNasi
The homeowner is never liable unless he explicitly accepts responsibility to safeguard the items.
R' Zeira
The mishna is disjointed — different clauses follow different opinions.
Rava
No — the beginning follows the Rabbis entirely. With permission, the owner safeguards pots even from wind damage. The potter accepts no responsibility.
Halakha Rava reads the mishna as a coherent statement of the Rabbis' view: permission creates a one-directional duty on the courtyard owner.
