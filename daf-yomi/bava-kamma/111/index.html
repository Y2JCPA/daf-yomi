Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's Rulings on R' Yehuda's View

R' Yehuda
The money should return to Joiarib to be with the guilt-offering.
R' Yehuda HaNasi
But if Joiarib exits their watch without demanding the money, they have waived their rights.
R' Yehuda HaNasi
In that case, Joiarib must give the offering to Jedaiah to sacrifice.
Gemara
What if both watches exit without demanding from each other?
R' Yehuda HaNasi
Since Jedaiah didn't demand the offering, we revert to the default rule — money returns to Joiarib.
PRINCIPLE Silence can constitute waiver of rights (mechilah), but mutual silence restores the default rule.

Rav Ḥisda: Stealing from a Thief

Rav Ḥisda
If someone steals from a robber before the owner despaired (ye'ush), the owner may collect from either one.
Gemara
But our Mishna says children who ate stolen food are exempt — a refutation of Rav Ḥisda!
Rav Ḥisda
The Mishna deals with after ye'ush — after the owner despaired. I'm discussing before ye'ush.
Gemara
The same answer applies to Rabbi Oshaya's baraita — it too speaks of after ye'ush.
KEY DISTINCTION Before ye'ush: The item still belongs to the original owner, so anyone who takes it is stealing from the owner. After ye'ush: The owner's despair affects the chain of obligation.

Heir's Domain: Purchaser or Not?

Rami bar Ḥama

  • Heir's domain = purchaser's domain
  • Inheritance = complete change of ownership
  • After ye'ush + change of domain → heir exempt
  • This explains why Mishna exempts heirs

Rava

  • Heir's domain ≠ purchaser's domain
  • Inheritance is NOT a full change of ownership
  • If item is extant, heir must return it
  • Mishna's exemption = items already consumed
RAVA'S CONCESSION Rava ultimately aligns with Rabbi Oshaya's baraita: extant items must be returned, consumed items are exempt, and land creates payment obligations.
