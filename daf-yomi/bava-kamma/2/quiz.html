Bava Kamma 2 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the Mishna list each of the four damage categories separately rather than deriving one from another?
A. Each has a unique defining characteristic that cannot be derived from the others
B. The Torah mentions only one category and the Rabbis added the rest
C. They are all subcategories of a single primary category
D. The categories are listed separately only for pedagogical convenience
Correct: A — The Mishna systematically demonstrates that each category has a unique characteristic (living spirit vs. none, stationary vs. moving) that prevents deriving its laws from any other category.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rav Pappa, what is the relationship between primary categories and subcategories in the laws of damages?
A. All subcategories are identical to their primary categories
B. All subcategories are dissimilar to their primary categories
C. Some subcategories are similar and some are dissimilar to their primary categories
D. The relationship depends on the monetary amount of damage
Correct: C — Rav Pappa teaches that damages has a mixed system — unlike Shabbat (all similar) or Tumah (all dissimilar), each subcategory of damage must be assessed individually.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What distinguishes Pit (Bor) from the other three primary categories of damage?
A. Pit involves a living spirit while the others do not
B. Pit is stationary — the victim encounters the hazard rather than the hazard moving to the victim
C. Pit only applies to damage caused on public property
D. Pit requires intent to cause damage
Correct: B — The Mishna explains that Ox, Maveh, and Fire all typically 'proceed from one place to another and cause damage,' while Pit remains in place and the injured party encounters it.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does the Gemara cite the verse 'His horns are the horns of the wild ox; with them he shall gore the nations' (Deut. 33:17)?
A. To prove that only wild oxen can cause legally recognized damage
B. To show that goring with an attached horn also distinguishes between an innocuous and forewarned ox
C. To establish that goring applies only to damage between nations
D. To prove that horns must be made of iron to be classified as goring
Correct: B — The Gemara brings this verse to refute the idea that the tam/mu'ad distinction applies only to a detached horn (like Zedekiah's). This verse shows goring with an attached horn, proving the distinction applies in all cases.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Which of the following is classified as a subcategory (toladah) of Keren (Goring) rather than a separate primary category?
A. An ox eating another's produce
B. An ox trampling belongings while walking
C. An ox biting with intent to cause damage
D. A fire spreading to a neighbor's field
Correct: C — Biting, along with pushing, crouching, and kicking, are listed as subcategories of Keren because they involve the ox using its body with intent to damage — the defining characteristic of Goring. Eating (Shen) and Trampling (Regel) are their own primary categories.
