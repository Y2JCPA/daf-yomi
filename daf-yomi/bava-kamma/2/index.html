The Architecture of Tort Law in the Mishna

Mishna: Four primary categories of damage (ארבעה אבות נזיקין)
↓
🐂 Ox (Shor)
Living spirit, moves to cause damage
🕳️ Pit (Bor)
No living spirit, stationary — victim encounters it
🐄 Maveh
Tooth of animal or person causing damage
🔥 Fire (Esh)
No living spirit, moves to cause damage
↓
Common denominator: Their manner is to cause damage & the obligation to safeguard is on the owner
↓
Damage caused → owner pays restitution with best-quality land (מיטב)
Key Principle The Mishna establishes that despite their differences, all four categories share a common thread: the owner's duty to prevent damage and liability when that duty is breached.

The Four Primary Categories of Damage

🐂 Ox (Shor)

  • Living spirit (רוח חיים)
  • Moves and causes damage
  • Includes goring, eating, trampling

🕳️ Pit (Bor)

  • No living spirit
  • Stationary — does NOT move
  • Victim encounters the hazard

🐄 Maveh

  • Living spirit
  • Moves and causes damage
  • Tooth of animal or person

🔥 Fire (Esh)

  • No living spirit
  • Moves and causes damage
  • Spreads from place to place
Mishna Logic The Mishna systematically distinguishes each category from the others — no single category's laws can be derived from another because each has a unique defining characteristic.

Why Can't We Derive One Category from Another?

Ox vs Maveh — characteristics are dissimilar to each other
↓
Ox + Maveh (living spirit) vs Fire (no living spirit) — dissimilar
↓
Ox + Maveh + Fire (move to cause damage) vs Pit (stationary) — dissimilar
↓
Therefore: each category must be stated independently in the Torah
↓
Common denominator: All cause damage & owner must safeguard → pays with best-quality land
Talmudic Method This layered comparison (צד השווה) is a classic technique: if categories share enough features, one could derive the other. The Mishna shows why that fails for each pair.

Avot and Toladot: Are Subcategories Like Their Primary Categories?

Gemara
The Mishna says primary categories — implying there are subcategories (toladot). Are they similar or dissimilar to their avot?
Re: Shabbat
Similar. Unwitting violation of an av or toladah — both require a sin-offering. Intentional — both carry stoning.
Re: Tumah
Dissimilar. An av of impurity contaminates people and vessels. A toladah contaminates only food and drink.
Rav Pappa
For damages: some subcategories are similar to their primary categories, and some are dissimilar.
Rav Pappa's Principle Unlike Shabbat (all similar) or Tumah (all dissimilar), damage law has a mixed system — each subcategory must be analyzed individually.

Avot vs. Toladot Across Halakha

DomainToladot Similar to Avot?Practical Difference
Shabbat✅ Yes — same penaltiesTwo avot in one lapse = two offerings; av + its toladah = one offering
Tumah❌ No — reduced effectAv contaminates people & vessels; toladah only food/drink
Nezikin (Damages)⚖️ Mixed — depends on caseSome toladot carry same rules as their av; others differ (Rav Pappa)
R' Eliezer on Shabbat✅ Same liabilityEven av + toladah = two offerings; classification based on significance in the Tabernacle
R' Eliezer's View According to R' Eliezer, one is liable for two sin-offerings even when performing an av and its toladah together. The classification of av vs. toladah is based on the labor's significance in building the Mishkan.

Three Primary Categories of Ox Damage

🐂 The Torah describes three distinct ways an ox causes damage
↓
🦬 Keren (Horn/Goring) — ox gores a person or animal with intent to harm
↓
🦷 Shen (Tooth/Eating) — ox consumes another's produce for its pleasure
↓
🦶 Regel (Foot/Trampling) — ox tramples belongings in the normal course of walking
↓
📖 Each is a distinct primary category because each is stated explicitly in the Torah
Baraita The Sages taught: Three primary categories of damage were stated in the Torah with regard to an ox — Keren, Shen, and Regel — each representing a fundamentally different mode of causing harm.
