Bava Kamma 86 — Humiliation & Temporary Injury

DAF OVERVIEW This daf explores two major themes: (1) How to compensate for temporary injuries — where the limb will heal — debated by Abaye and Rava; and (2) the nature of humiliation (boshet) — how it is assessed, whether a naked, blind, or sleeping person can be humiliated, and whether compensation is for the victim's embarrassment or for their disgrace in the eyes of others.

Permanent — not relevant to dilemma

Hair returns — fits the dilemma!
↓
Objection: Perhaps he used a depilatory agent (נשא) — hair won't return → permanent injury, dilemma unresolved
KEY POINT The Gemara systematically tests scenarios to resolve whether temporary injury requires full sheves (loss of livelihood) compensation, but each attempt is deflected.

Temporary Injury: How to Compensate?

Abaye
He pays major sheves (decrease in slave-market value due to temporary paralysis) plus minor sheves (daily lost wages during recovery)
Rava
He pays only daily lost wages — not the full decrease in the hand's value on the slave market
Abaye
The temporary disability has real market-value impact — the victim deserves compensation for that diminished worth
Rava
Since the hand will heal, we only compensate for the actual period of lost work, day by day
PRACTICAL DIFFERENCE Abaye's approach yields a larger lump-sum payment reflecting diminished value; Rava's approach yields smaller daily payments only for the recovery period.

Severing Hand of a Hebrew Slave: Who Gets Paid?

Abaye

  • Major sheves (decrease in value) → goes to the slave
  • Minor sheves (daily lost wages) → goes to the master
  • Split based on whose interest each payment protects

Rava

  • All compensation goes to the slave
  • Land is purchased with the money
  • Master collects profits from the land during slavery term
  • Slave gets land itself upon release
SPECIAL CASE If the injury only reduces the slave's value to himself (e.g., splitting tip of ear/nostril) but not his work capacity, all compensation goes directly to the slave — no dispute.

Three Views on Assessing Humiliation (Boshet)

TannaHow Boshet Is Assessed
R' MeirAll Jews assessed equally — as freemen who lost their property, since all are children of Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov
R' YehudaEach person assessed according to their individual stature — great by greatness, small by smallness
R' ShimonWealthy assessed as freemen who lost property; poor assessed as the least among the poor — lessens the payment
WHOSE VIEW IS THE MISHNA? The Mishna says humiliation depends on the stature of both parties. This fits R' Shimon — R' Meir treats all alike, and R' Yehuda exempts the blind (contradicting a later Mishna).

Mishna: Rules of Humiliation Liability

😳 Humiliating a naked person — LIABLE (but less than if clothed)
↓
🦯 Humiliating a blind person — LIABLE
↓
😴 Humiliating a sleeping person — LIABLE
↓
💤 A sleeping person who humiliates another — EXEMPT
↓
🏠 Falling from roof onto someone — LIABLE for damage, EXEMPT from humiliation
↓
⚡ Principle: Intent is required for humiliation liability
KEY RULE A person is always forewarned (liable for damage regardless of intent), but humiliation requires deliberate intent to humiliate.
