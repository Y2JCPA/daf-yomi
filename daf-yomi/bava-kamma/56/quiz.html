Bava Kamma 56 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why did Rabbi Yehoshua list specifically four cases of heavenly liability, when there are clearly more?
A. He was unaware of the other cases
B. He listed cases where you might mistakenly think there's no liability at all — even by Heaven
C. The other cases are only rabbinic, not biblical
D. He listed only cases involving property damage, not personal injury
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that Rabbi Yehoshua's four cases are ones where a person might reason that since the damage was so indirect, there should be no liability whatsoever. He teaches that even in these cases, dinei Shamayim applies.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rav Ashi, what does 'bending grain before a fire' refer to?
A. Bending the grain directly into the flames
B. Bending grain over a concealed item, making it unrecoverable after fire damage
C. Placing grain in a path where atypical wind would carry the fire
D. Hiding grain underground before a fire reaches it
Correct: B — Rav Ashi explains the person bent grain over another's item, effectively making it a 'concealed item' (tzanua). Since one is not liable to pay for concealed items damaged by fire, the item's owner suffers an irrecoverable loss.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why is placing poison before another's animal exempt by human law?
A. Because poison is not considered direct damage
B. Because the animal caused its own death by eating it
C. Because the animal's owner should have supervised it
D. Because poison is a natural substance
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that since the animal itself chose to eat the poison, the person who placed it is considered an indirect cause. Human courts cannot impose liability for indirect causation, but he is liable by the laws of Heaven.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rav Yosef, why is a finder safeguarding a lost item considered a paid bailee?
A. Because the Torah promises him a reward in the World to Come
B. Because the owner of the lost item must compensate him
C. Because while performing the mitzva of guarding the item, he is exempt from other mitzvot like giving to the poor
D. Because the community must pay him for his time
Correct: C — Rav Yosef reasons that since one who is engaged in a mitzva is exempt from performing another mitzva (osek b'mitzva patur min hamitzva), the finder derives a tangible benefit — exemption from charity obligations — making him equivalent to a paid bailee.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does the Gemara resolve the apparent refutation of Rava's rule (that a bailee who transfers remains liable) from the mishna about a shepherd?
A. Rava concedes his rule is wrong
B. The mishna refers to a shepherd transferring to his own assistant, which is standard practice and expected by the owner
C. The mishna is discussing a different type of bailment entirely
D. The shepherd is considered the same legal entity as the original bailee
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that the mishna's 'shepherd' who transfers refers specifically to a shepherd giving the animal to his assistant — a normal, expected practice. This doesn't contradict Rava's rule about unauthorized transfers to unrelated third parties.
