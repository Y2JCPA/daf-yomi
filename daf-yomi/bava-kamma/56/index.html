Why Did Rabbi Yehoshua List Only Four Cases?

Gemara
There are clearly more than four cases of heavenly liability. Why did Rabbi Yehoshua limit his list?
Resolution
Rabbi Yehoshua chose cases where you might think there is no liability at all — not even by dinei Shamayim.
Gemara
For example: the wall was about to fall anyway — so what did the breacher really do? You'd think he's totally exempt.
Resolution
Rabbi Yehoshua teaches: even in these edge cases, dinei Shamayim still applies — he is still morally accountable.
Principle The four cases aren't the only cases of heavenly liability — they're the ones most likely to be mistakenly excluded entirely.

Bending Grain Before a Fire — Two Explanations

A person bends another's grain toward a fire
↓
What are the circumstances?
First Explanation
Fire reaches grain only in an atypical wind (רוח שאינה מצויה)
→ Exempt by human law, liable by Heaven
Rav Ashi's Explanation
He bent grain over a concealed item (צנוע)
→ Made the item into a 'concealed item in a fire' — owner can't collect
Rav Ashi By covering another's item with grain, he caused indirect damage: the item became a 'concealed item' for which fire liability doesn't apply.

Shepherd Receives Animal: In Place of Whom?

In Place of the Owner?

  • Already taught in Mishna 44b
  • All four bailees replace the owner
  • This would be redundant — rejected

In Place of the Bailee

  • Owner gave animal to Bailee #1
  • Bailee #1 conveyed it to a shepherd
  • Shepherd now bears responsibility
  • First bailee is released
Key Question Does this refute Rava's rule that a bailee who transfers to another bailee remains liable? No — the mishna refers to a shepherd giving to his assistant, which is standard practice.

Safeguarding a Lost Item: What Level of Responsibility?

Rabba
The finder is like an unpaid bailee (שומר חינם). What benefit does he get from guarding it? He gets nothing.
Rav Yosef
He is like a paid bailee (שומר שכר). While guarding the item he is exempt from giving bread to a poor person — that's a benefit! (עוסק במצוה פטור מן המצוה)
Rav Yosef (alt.)
Alternative reason: The Torah compels him to guard lost items — so it imposes a higher standard of care on him.
Practical Difference An unpaid bailee is liable only for negligence. A paid bailee is also liable for theft and loss — a significantly higher standard.
