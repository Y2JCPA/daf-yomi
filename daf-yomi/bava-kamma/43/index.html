Liability When an Ox Kills Unintentionally

An ox kills a person unintentionally
↓
Killed a Freeman
Rabba: Owner exempt from kofer (ransom)
But may pay monetary value of victim
Killed a Slave
Reish Lakish: Owner exempt from 30 shekel fine
Fine depends on ox being stoned
Core Principle Both Reish Lakish and Rabba derive from Torah verses that liability to pay compensation is dependent on the ox being liable to stoning — no stoning means no fine or ransom.

Who Inherits a Woman's Injury Compensation?

Baraita
If an emancipated slave woman or a convert died without heirs, the assailant gains by not paying — proving heirs (not the husband) inherit her compensation.
Rabba
This baraita refers to a divorcée — since they divorced, the husband doesn't inherit from her.
Rav Naḥman
I agree — the baraita speaks of a divorcée.
Sages
If she's divorced, why does the ex-husband get full compensation for the miscarried offspring?
Rav Pappa
The Torah awards miscarriage compensation to the father — even after divorce. 'As the ba'al of the woman shall impose' (Exodus 21:22) — ba'al means the man who impregnated her.
Key Verse Exodus 21:22 — ba'al is read not as 'husband' but as 'the one who engaged in intercourse with her,' granting the father exclusive rights to miscarriage damages.

Why Not Explain via 'Property Due' (Ra'uy)?

Rabba's View on Firstborn

  • Debt collected as land → firstborn gets double portion
  • Debt collected as money → no double portion
  • Money = property 'due' (ra'uy), not 'possessed' (muchzak)

Rav Naḥman's View on Firstborn

  • Debt collected as money → firstborn gets double portion
  • Debt collected as land → no double portion
  • Opposite categorization of ra'uy vs. muchzak
Resolution They follow Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, who holds a firstborn receives a double portion even in property appreciation after death — so everything 'due' is considered 'possessed.' Therefore they must explain the baraita as referring to a divorcée.

Reish Lakish & Rabba: Unintentional Ox Killing

An ox kills someone unintentionally
↓
Is the ox liable to stoning (sekilah)?
↓
Yes — Intentional
Freeman: Owner pays kofer (ransom)
Slave: Owner pays 30 shekels
No — Unintentional
Freeman: Exempt from ransom (Rabba)
Slave: Exempt from 30 shekels (Reish Lakish)
Textual Basis Reish Lakish derives from Exodus 21:32 — 'the ox shall be stoned' is linked to the 30-shekel payment. Rabba derives from Exodus 21:29-30 — stoning is linked to ransom.

Self-Admission: Freeman vs. Slave

ScenarioFreeman VictimSlave Victim
Witnesses + owner admits forewarnedPays ransom (kofer)Does NOT pay 30 shekels (it's a fine)
No witnesses, owner admits ox killedPays monetary value of victimDoes NOT pay value of victim
Witnesses confirm unintentionalPays monetary valuePays monetary value
Witnesses confirm intentionalPays ransomPays 30 shekel fine
Principle A person who admits liability for a fine (kenas) is exempt. Since the 30-shekel slave payment is a fine, self-admission creates no liability at all — not even for the slave's monetary value.

Rava Challenges Rabba on Fire Damage

Rava
If one pays the value of the victim when an ox kills unintentionally, then one who burns another to death unintentionally with fire should also pay the victim's value!
Gemara
Perhaps the mishna about a slave burned in a fire proves he doesn't pay? No — Reish Lakish says that case is where he ignited the slave directly, and capital punishment exempts him from paying damages.
Gemara
Perhaps the baraita comparing Fire to Pit proves it? No — the baraita may have omitted that distinction; it only listed one stringency of Fire.
Gemara
Conclusion: Rava didn't object — he genuinely had a dilemma: Does one pay the value of a person killed unintentionally by fire?
Unresolved The Gemara leaves Rava's dilemma as a teiku-style open question — the distinction between ox damage (where owner pays value) and fire damage remains unresolved.
