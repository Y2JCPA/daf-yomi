Overview

כִּי יִתֵּן אִישׁ אֶל רֵעֵהוּ... לְכֹל דְּבַר פֶּשַׁע (שמות כב:ח)
Core Question How broadly does the Torah's double payment (תשלומי כפל) apply? The Gemara uses klal u'prat u'klal hermeneutics to determine which items are covered — debating whether birds, non-kosher animals, land, slaves, and documents qualify. It then shifts to the bailee who falsely claims theft, deriving that double payment applies only when the bailee swore an oath — not for a mere false claim of loss.

↓
Ox alone?

Donkey alone?

Both ox + donkey?
Still excludes animals not matching either criterion
↓

Principle Each listed animal teaches a unique lesson; the extra detail of 'sheep' expands the scope of double payment

Are Non-Kosher Birds Included?

Gemara asks
Sheep teaches kosher birds are included — their neveilah (carcass) transmits impurity through the esophagus, like sheep. But what about non-kosher birds, whose carcasses don't transmit impurity at all?
Gemara answers
The word "כֹּל" (kol) in 'for any matter of trespass' is an amplification (ריבוי) — it includes even non-kosher birds in double payment.
Gemara challenges
But regarding second tithe, the Torah also writes "בְּכֹל" (bekhol) — and we expound that as a generalization, not an amplification!
Gemara resolves
The term "בְּכֹל" (bekhol) is a generalization, but standalone "כֹּל" (kol) is an amplification. Alternatively: since the passage already has a klal u'prat u'klal, this later kol must be an amplification.
Key Distinction בְּכֹל = generalization (klal) | כֹּל = amplification (ribbui)

What Do the Three Animals Exclude?

Animal ListedWhat It ExcludesReasoning
🐂 Ox (שׁוֹר)Land (קרקע)An ox is movable property — unlike land
🫏 Donkey (חֲמוֹר)Canaanite slaves (עבדים)A donkey is property but not a person — slaves are treated like land
🐑 Sheep (שֶׂה)Financial documents (שְׁטָרוֹת)A sheep has intrinsic value — documents are merely proof of debt
Result Double payment does NOT apply to land, Canaanite slaves, or financial documents — only movable property with intrinsic value

Bailee's False Claim: Stolen vs. Lost

Claims 'It Was Stolen'

  • Bailee falsely claims a thief stole the deposit
  • Takes an oath to support the claim
  • Witnesses later prove the bailee stole it
  • → Pays DOUBLE PAYMENT (תשלומי כפל)

Claims 'It Was Lost'

  • Bailee falsely claims the deposit was lost
  • Takes an oath to support the claim
  • Witnesses later prove the bailee took it
  • → Pays PRINCIPAL ONLY (no double payment)

Admits Before Witnesses

  • Bailee confesses on his own (either theft or loss)
  • Before any witnesses testify
  • → Pays principal + ONE-FIFTH (חוֹמֶשׁ)
  • → Brings a GUILT-OFFERING (אָשָׁם)
Source Mishna on 108b — the distinction between stolen and lost claims is derived from Exodus 22:6–8

Deriving the Oath Requirement

📖 Exodus 22:7 — 'The homeowner shall approach the judges to determine if he laid his hand (שָׁלַח יָדוֹ) on his neighbor's goods'
↓
❓ Does 'approach the judges' mean for judgment or for an oath?
↓
🔗 Gezera shava (verbal analogy): 'שְׁלִיחוּת יָד' appears in Exodus 22:10 — explicitly about an oath
↓
⚖️ Just as 'laying the hand' later means oath, so too here it means oath
↓
✅ Double payment for false theft claim applies only when the bailee swore an oath
Method The gezera shava of שְׁלִיחוּת יָד links the two verses, proving the oath requirement
