Does Admitting to a Fine Create Exemption Even After Witnesses Testify?

Rav
One who admits liability for a fine (מודה בקנס) is exempt even if witnesses later testify.
Shmuel
He is liable — the subsequent testimony overrides the admission.
Rav
The Torah writes 'himmatze timmatze' — doubling the verb to require witnesses finding the theft, excluding self-incrimination.
Shmuel
That double expression teaches that a thief himself pays double — not just a bailee who swore falsely.
Rav
A baraita proves my point: a thief who admits before witnesses arrive pays only principal.
Shmuel
I follow R' Elazar b. R' Shimon, who says: Let the witnesses come and testify — he is liable!
Core Issue The fundamental dispute: Is an admission of liability for a fine a permanent exemption, or merely a temporary status that witnesses can override?

Rabban Gamliel & Rabbi Yehoshua: Two Baraita Versions

Baraita 1
R' Yehoshua told Rabban Gamliel: 'You have no witnesses' — implying if witnesses came, the fine would apply.
Baraita 2
R' Yehoshua said: 'You have already admitted' — implying even if witnesses come, the admission exempts.
Gemara
Are these two baraitot reflecting a tannaitic dispute about modeh b'knas?
Gemara
No — both agree modeh b'knas is exempt. They disagree whether R' Yehoshua was inside or outside the court.
Resolution If R' Yehoshua was outside the court, Rabban Gamliel's statement was not a valid judicial admission — so witnesses could still impose the fine.

Rav's Derivation: Himmatze Timmatze (הִמָּצֵא תִמָּצֵא)

Exodus 22:3 — 'himmatze timmatze'
Double expression of 'shall be found'
↓
Himmatze
The theft must be found through witness testimony
Timmatze
The theft must be determined through judges
↓
Exodus 22:8 already teaches: 'The one whom judges convict shall pay double' — excluding self-incrimination
↓
Two verses for the same principle → Additional novelty:
Exempt even if witnesses later come and testify
Rav's Logic If one verse suffices to exclude self-incrimination, the second verse must teach that admission exempts even retroactively when witnesses subsequently testify.

Rav vs. Shmuel: Modeh B'Knas

Rav — Exempt

  • Derives from doubled 'himmatze timmatze'
  • Admission permanently exempts from fine
  • Even if witnesses later testify — still exempt
  • Follows the first tanna of the baraita
  • Claims even R' Elazar b. R' Shimon may agree in most cases

Shmuel — Liable

  • 'Himmatze timmatze' teaches thief pays double (not just bailee)
  • Admission is only temporary — witnesses override it
  • Follows R' Elazar b. R' Shimon
  • Admits his view is a tannaitic dispute
  • Concedes the first tanna disagrees with him
Key Difference Shmuel admits his position follows only R' Elazar b. R' Shimon, while Rav claims his view works according to all tanna'im.

The Thief Who Sees Witnesses Approaching

👀 Thief sees witnesses approaching to testify against him
↓
🗣️ Thief declares: 'I admit I stole, but I did not slaughter or sell'
↓
💰 He pays only the principal — no double, fourfold, or fivefold payment
↓
⚡ R' Elazar b. R' Shimon disagrees: Let the witnesses come and testify!
↓
🔑 Rav: His admission was sincere despite fear → still exempt
↓
🔑 Shmuel: Even fear-based admission? → R' Elazar says liable
Rav's Distinction R' Elazar b. R' Shimon holds the thief liable only because the admission was motivated by fear of the approaching witnesses — it was not a genuine, voluntary admission.

Rav Hamnuna: When Does Admission Exempt from Fines?

ScenarioTheft Established BySlaughter AdmittedResult
Theft by 2 witnesses, thief admits slaughterWitnessesSelf-admissionLiable — admission didn't obligate principal
Theft by 2 witnesses, 1 witness to slaughterWitnessesOne witnessLiable when second witness joins
Thief admits theft AND slaughterSelf-admissionSelf-admissionExempt — admission obligated principal
Thief admits theft, denies slaughter, witnesses confirmSelf-admissionWitnessesExempt — admission already covered principal
Rav Hamnuna's Principle An admission only exempts from a fine when it simultaneously obligates the person to pay the principal. If the theft was already proven by witnesses, admitting to slaughter doesn't obligate any new principal — so it doesn't count as a real 'admission.'
