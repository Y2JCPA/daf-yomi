Bava Kamma 90 — Liens, Humiliation & Self-Harm

Rava's Principle: Consecration, chametz prohibition, and emancipation abrogate liens
↓
Does emancipation of wife's slave abrogate husband's lien on usufruct property?
Approach 1
Sages strengthened the husband's lien — it is not abrogated
Approach 2
Dispute: Is ownership of usage rights like ownership of the item itself?
↓

Does Emancipation Abrogate the Husband's Lien?

Rava
Consecration, the chametz prohibition, and emancipation all abrogate any existing lien on property.
Gemara
If so, when a wife's slave is emancipated by injury, the husband's lien should be abrogated too — contradicting the baraita!
Answer 1
Everyone agrees with Rava, but the Sages strengthened the husband's lien on his wife's property as a special enactment.
Answer 2
Or: the dispute is about whether ownership of usage rights (kinyan peirot) equals ownership of the item itself (kinyan ha-guf).
Principle Rava's rule that emancipation abrogates liens is universally accepted — the dispute is only about the special strength of a husband's lien.

Kinyan Peirot vs. Kinyan Ha-Guf — Practical Impact

A man sells his slave but retains usage for 30 days
↓
During those 30 days, the seller uses the slave and the buyer owns the slave
↓
If one of them strikes the slave and he dies after a day or two — who is the 'master'?
Usage = Ownership
Seller is the master (R' Meir)
Usage ≠ Ownership
Buyer is the master (R' Yehuda)
Core Dispute Is kinyan peirot (ownership of usage rights and produce) equivalent to kinyan ha-guf (ownership of the item itself)? This question echoes across many areas of halakha.

R' Akiva on the Dignity of Every Jew

אפילו עניים שבישראל רואין אותם כאילו הם בני חורין שירדו מנכסיהם
Mishna Assessing humiliation: It is all evaluated according to the honor of the one humiliated.

R' Akiva says: Even the poorest Jews are viewed as though they were freemen who lost their property — for they are all children of Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov, and are of prominent lineage.

The Uncovered Woman — R' Akiva's Famous Incident

👨 A man uncovers a woman's head in the marketplace
↓
⚖️ R' Akiva rules: pay her 400 dinars for humiliation
↓
⏳ Man asks for time to pay — R' Akiva grants it
↓
🏺 Man breaks a jug of oil near her courtyard; she uncovers her own head to collect the oil
↓
👁️ Man sets up witnesses: 'She shamed herself for an issar of oil!'
↓
⚖️ R' Akiva: 'You said nothing.' One who harms oneself is exempt, but others who harm are always liable.
Broader Principle Just as one who cuts down his own saplings violates bal tashchit but owes no penalty, while others who cut them must pay — self-harm and other-harm follow different rules.
