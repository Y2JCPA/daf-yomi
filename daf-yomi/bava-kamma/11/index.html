Bava Kamma 11: Appraisal, Bailees & Liability

Core Question: When an animal dies or an item is damaged, how does the court assess liability?
↓
Damages (Nezikin)
Court does appraise carcass value — injured party owns the carcass
Theft/Robbery
Court does not appraise — thief/robber takes carcass, pays full prior value
Borrower
Court does appraise — per Rav Kahana & Rav Asi
KEY PRINCIPLE Shmuel's framework: The type of wrongdoer determines whether the court appraises the damaged item's current value or demands full replacement.

Who Bears the Loss of the Diminishing Carcass?

First Tanna
The bailee must bring witnesses (ed) that the animal was mauled beyond his control.
Abba Shaul
Ed means carcass (aduda) — bring the carcass to court immediately for appraisal.
Gemara's Initial Reading
They must disagree: Abba Shaul holds the injured party bears the carcass's declining value; the first tanna says the liable party bears it.
Gemara's Rejection
No — both agree the injured party bears the loss. They disagree only on who must retrieve the carcass to court.
PRINCIPLE The injured party owns the carcass (ha-neveilah shel nizak), so any decline in its value is the injured party's loss.

Retrieving the Carcass from the Pit

An ox falls into a pit and dies. Must the pit owner retrieve the carcass?
↓
Abaye's challenge: If carcass is worth 1 dinar in the pit but 4 dinars at the edge…
↓
The liable party retrieves it for his own benefit — it reduces his liability! No Torah command needed.
↓
Rava's answer: The Torah's command is needed when the carcass is worth 1 dinar in the pit and 1 dinar out.
↓
Proof: A beam of wood sells for a dinar in the city and a dinar in the field — accessibility doesn't always change price.
EXODUS 21:34 "The owner of the pit shall pay; he shall restore money to its owner, and the carcass shall be his" — 'restore' applies to both money and the carcass.

The Broken Ax: Does a Borrower Get Appraisal?

Rav
A man borrowed an ax and broke it. Rav ruled: Pay him a full ax — no appraisal of the broken pieces.
Rav Kahana & Rav Asi
Is this the halakha? A borrower should get appraisal — pay the difference, not the full value!
Rav
(Rav remained silent) — implying he conceded to their challenge.
Gemara
Conclude: The court does appraise for a borrower. The broken ax remains with the owner, and the borrower pays only the difference.
LEGAL PRINCIPLE Rav's silence in the face of challenge is treated as concession — a standard Talmudic interpretive move.

Bailee Who Transfers a Deposit to Another Bailee

Ulla / R' Elazar: Exempt

  • First bailee is exempt for any mishap he'd have been exempt for
  • Even a paid bailee → unpaid (lower protection) is exempt
  • Rationale: He conveyed it to a mentally competent person
  • He fulfilled his duty of safeguarding

Rava: Liable

  • First bailee is liable for any loss — even mishaps he'd normally be exempt from
  • Even an unpaid bailee → paid (better protection) is liable
  • Owner can say: "You are credible to me for an oath, but he is not"
  • First bailee can't swear about what happened since he wasn't there
KEY CONCEPT Rava's reasoning: Trust in a bailee is personal. The owner chose this bailee — the oath exemption depends on the specific relationship of trust.
