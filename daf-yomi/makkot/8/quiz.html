QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Pappa, when is one who demolishes a wall into a scrap heap exiled for accidentally killing a passerby?
A. Only at night, when people use the heap for defecating
B. Only during the day, when people are not expected to be there
C. Any time, since the public domain is always dangerous
D. Never — demolishing into a scrap heap is never grounds for exile
Correct: B — Rav Pappa explains that a scrap heap is used at night for relieving oneself but not during the day. Demolishing during the day, when no one is expected, is genuinely unintentional — and exile applies.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What halakha does Abba Shaul derive from the verse 'as when one goes with his neighbor into the forest'?
A. Exile applies only if the killing occurs in a forest or rural setting
B. Exile applies only if both parties were engaged in the same activity
C. Exile applies only when the unintentional killing occurs during an optional, not obligatory, activity
D. The verse excludes foreigners from the exile laws entirely
Correct: C — Abba Shaul derives that just as chopping wood is a purely optional act, exile applies only to unintentional killings that occur during optional activities — not when fulfilling an obligation such as a father teaching his son or a court agent carrying out a ruling.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Rava resolves the contradiction about a son who kills his father by explaining that the baraita's exclusion ('one who strikes his father is not exiled') refers to which case?
A. A son who unintentionally kills his father is never exiled under any circumstance
B. A father who unintentionally kills his son while teaching a craft
C. One who intentionally strikes his father — that triggers execution, not exile
D. Only adopted sons are excluded from exile when killing their fathers
Correct: C — Rava clarifies: the baraita excludes intentional striking of one's father (which carries a capital penalty, not exile). A son who unintentionally kills his father is certainly subject to exile under the standard rules.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
When does a father who accidentally kills his son while teaching him a trade become liable to exile?
A. Never — teaching one's son is always a mitzva and always exempt
B. When the son has already learned another trade and can earn a livelihood without this additional teaching
C. Only if the son was an adult at the time of the accident
D. Only if the teaching involved a dangerous tool
Correct: B — The Gemara explains: if the son already knows another craft and can support himself, further teaching is no longer strictly obligatory. Without the obligatory character, it becomes an optional act — and the forest analogy applies, making the father liable to exile.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to the baraita cited at the end of the daf, which groups are both exiled and flogged in connection with a Jew?
A. Converts and freed slaves
B. Kohanim and Levites who violate their special duties
C. Slaves and Samaritans
D. Non-Jewish residents who dwell outside any city of refuge
Correct: C — The baraita specifies slaves and Samaritans: they are exiled for unintentionally killing a Jew, and flogged in a case where they serve as conspiring (zomem) witnesses against a Jew — a Torah prohibition that carries lashes.
