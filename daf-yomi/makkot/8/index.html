🗑️ The Scrap Heap — Rav Pappa's Time-Based Ruling

Premise: One demolishes his wall into a public scrap heap and a person is killed
↓
Problem: Isn't a public scrap heap a public domain where people are expected? Why is he exiled?
↓
Rav Pappa: The scrap heap is used at night for relieving oneself, but not during the day
↓
He demolishes during the day → people not expected → unintentional → Exiled
↓
He demolishes at night → people regularly present → more foreseeable → Not Exiled / Liable
↓
Key Principle: Exile depends on the reasonable expectation of finding people at that specific time and place

🔤 Does Ve'asher Always Signal an Optional Act?

Rava
The term ve'asher ("and as when") in the verse implies optionality — the act is done by free choice. Therefore exile applies only to voluntary, non-obligatory actions.
Rav Adda bar Ahava
Is asher always optional? The Torah says "a man who [asher] becomes impure from a corpse" — is contracting impurity optional? It can happen entirely against one's will!
Rava
There it is different — the verse adds "he shall be impure," indicating the laws apply regardless of how he became impure. That is an exception, not a counterexample.
Gemara
But that verse is needed for a different derivation — to include a tevul yom who immersed and awaits nightfall. Rava's proof from asher collapses!
Rava
I retract my proof. The real derivation: harvesting is juxtaposed to plowing in the verse — just as plowing is always optional, so too harvesting must be optional.
Conclusion Rava abandons the asher-proof and anchors the optional-act requirement in the verse's juxtaposition of plowing and harvesting — both are quintessentially optional agricultural activities.

👨‍👦 Father & Son — The Exile Paradox Resolved

Father Who Strikes Son

  • Previous mishna: father who strikes son is not exiled
  • Teaching a son a trade = obligatory mitzva act
  • BUT: if son already learned another craft, teaching him is no longer obligatory
  • If father kills son while teaching a second trade → son could earn a living already → father IS exiled
  • Reason: once the teaching is optional, the forest analogy applies

Son Who Kills Father

  • Baraita: 'one who strikes a person' — to exclude one who strikes his father
  • Apparent contradiction: mishna says son IS exiled for killing father!
  • Rava: the exclusion refers to intentional striking of father (liable to execution, not exile)
  • Unintentional killing of father: son IS exiled — normal rules apply
  • Rabbi Shimon vs. Rabbis debate strangulation/sword affects which case applies
