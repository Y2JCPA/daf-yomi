Makkot 18: Prohibitions, Lashes & First Fruits

Deuteronomy 12:17 is a superfluous verse — items already listed in 12:6–7
↓
Repetition = separate prohibition for each item enumerated
↓
Rava: Non-priest eats burnt-offering before sprinkling — 5 sets of lashes (R' Shimon)
↓
Gemara challenges: Why not more lashes for additional prohibitions?
↓
Each rejected because
Burnt-offering flesh is never fit for consumption — prohibitions only apply when item was once fit
Exception
R' Eliezer's rule on kalil (entirely burned): adds a 5th prohibition
KEY PRINCIPLE A prohibition applies only when the item was previously fit for the permitted use — if it was never fit, the specific prohibition doesn't attach.

Why Is Deuteronomy 12:17 Superfluous?

Deut. 12:6–7 already lists all items eaten in Jerusalem: tithes, vows, firstborn, etc.
↓
Deut. 12:17 repeats: "You may not eat within your gates the tithe of your grain…"
↓
Why repeat? Could have said "You may not eat them" generally
↓
Each specific enumeration = a separate prohibition carrying its own set of lashes
R' SHIMON R' Shimon derives additional prohibitions via a fortiori (kal va-chomer) inferences rather than from the superfluous verse.

Rava's Five Lashes & the Gemara's Challenges

Rava
A non-priest (zar) who ate burnt-offering flesh before blood sprinkling, outside the walls — per R' Shimon, he receives 5 sets of lashes.
Gemara
Why not also for "A non-priest may not eat, as they are sacred" (Ex. 29:33)?
Answer
That prohibition applies only where food is fit for priests. Burnt-offering is not fit for anyone.
Gemara
Why not for "Flesh torn in the field you shall not eat" — meat taken outside its partition?
Answer
That applies only when meat is fit for consumption inside the courtyard. Burnt-offering is never fit for eating.
PATTERN Each challenge is rejected with the same logic: the prohibition requires prior fitness for the permitted use.

Priest vs. Non-Priest: Eating Before Blood Sprinkling

Kohen (Priest)

  • Eats sin/guilt offering before sprinkling
  • Rav (original version): Flogged
  • Reason: "They shall eat them, with which they were atoned" — after atonement yes, before — no
  • Rava objects: Inference from positive mitzva = positive mitzva, not a prohibition
  • Conclusion: Original statement retracted

Zar (Non-Priest)

  • Eats sin/guilt offering before sprinkling
  • Rav Giddel/Rav (revised): Exempt from lashes
  • Reason: "A non-priest may not eat" only applies where priests may eat
  • Before sprinkling, even priests may not eat
  • Therefore the non-priest prohibition doesn't attach
MNEMONIC The Gemara provides a mnemonic: כ-ו-ז-א (Kaf, Vav, Zayin, Alef) for Rav Giddel's statements.

Does a Prohibition Inferred from a Positive Mitzva Carry Force?

Rav
A prohibition derived from a positive mitzva has the status of a prohibition — one is flogged for its violation.
Rava
"That, you may eat" (Deut. 14:6) implies: other animals you may not eat. If this inference = a prohibition, why do we need "This you may not eat" (14:7)?
Rava
The explicit verse proves the inference alone is insufficient — it has the status of a positive mitzva only.
Conclusion
Rav's original statement about the kohen is retracted. The revised teaching applies to the zar instead.
HALACHA Lav ha-ba mi-klal aseh (לאו הבא מכלל עשה) — a prohibition inferred from a positive commandment has the status of a positive commandment, not a negative one.
