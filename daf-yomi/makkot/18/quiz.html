QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the Gemara say Deuteronomy 12:17 is a superfluous verse?
A. Because the items were already enumerated in Deuteronomy 12:6–7
B. Because it contradicts an earlier verse in Leviticus
C. Because it repeats the same language as Deuteronomy 14:6
D. Because it was added by a later scribal emendation
Correct: A — The items listed in Deut. 12:17 (tithes, firstborn, etc.) were already enumerated in Deut. 12:6–7. The repetition is 'superfluous' and therefore teaches that each item carries its own separate prohibition.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why doesn't the prohibition against non-priests eating sacred food (Ex. 29:33) apply to a non-priest eating burnt-offering flesh?
A. Because the burnt-offering was already sprinkled
B. Because the non-priest was inside the Temple courtyard
C. Because burnt-offering flesh is never fit for consumption by anyone, including priests
D. Because the prohibition only applies to sin-offerings and guilt-offerings
Correct: C — The prohibition 'a non-priest may not eat' only applies where the food is fit for priests. Since burnt-offering flesh is not permitted for anyone to eat, the non-priest-specific prohibition doesn't attach.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What does Rava prove from the verse 'That, you may eat' (Deut. 14:6)?
A. That only animals with split hooves are kosher
B. That a prohibition inferred from a positive mitzva has the status of a positive mitzva, not a negative one
C. That one may eat sacrificial meat after blood sprinkling
D. That the Torah sometimes uses redundant language for emphasis
Correct: B — Rava argues: If the inference 'that you may eat' (implying others you may NOT) had the force of a full prohibition, the Torah wouldn't need the explicit 'this you may not eat.' This proves an inference from a positive mitzva carries only positive-mitzva status.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rabbi Elazar citing Rabbi Hoshaya, which element invalidates first fruits (bikkurim)?
A. Lack of recitation of Torah verses
B. Lack of placement alongside the altar
C. Bringing them after Sukkot
D. Both placement and recitation are required to invalidate
Correct: B — R' Elazar says R' Hoshaya holds that lack of placement alongside the altar invalidates bikkurim, but lack of recitation does not — provided recitation is still possible.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Rabbi Zeira's principle about meal-offerings resolve the apparent contradiction regarding first fruits after Sukkot?
A. Meal-offerings and first fruits follow completely different rules
B. When a ritual act is possible but omitted, it doesn't invalidate; when it becomes impossible, it does invalidate
C. The mixing of oil always invalidates regardless of circumstances
D. After Sukkot, first fruits become a different category of offering entirely
Correct: B — R' Zeira's principle: when mixing (or recitation) is possible, omitting it doesn't invalidate. But when it becomes impossible — like recitation after Sukkot — the omission does invalidate. This resolves why bikkurim set aside before Sukkot must decay if the Festival passes.
