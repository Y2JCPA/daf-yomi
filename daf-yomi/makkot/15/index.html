🗣️ Did Rabbi Yoḥanan Say It? The Opening Dispute

Students
Did you really say this halakha — that one is flogged for violating a prohibition preceded by a positive mitzva?
R. Yoḥanan
No, I did not say that.
Rabba bar bar Ḥana
By God! He DID say it — and this halakha is written in the Torah and learned in the Mishna!
Gemara
Why did R. Yoḥanan retract? Because the rapist (אוֹנֵס) case was difficult — his positive mitzva 'she shall be his wife' appears to PRECEDE the prohibition 'he may not send her away,' which would mean he is never flogged.
Core Tension Does the mitzva precede the prohibition (exempting from lashes) or does it follow and merely rectify it? This question governs all the cases on this daf.

🔄 Ulla's Analysis: The Rapist's Mitzva Rectifies, It Does Not Precede

Apparent problem: 'And she shall be his wife' (Deut. 22:29) appears before 'He may not send her away' — suggesting the mitzva precedes the prohibition
↓
Ulla's answer: The positive mitzva does NOT precede — it cannot apply before marriage because she is not yet his wife
↓
The principle: 'If it is not relevant to the matter before marriage, apply it to the matter after marriage' (אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן... תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן)
↓
What the mitzva teaches: If he married her and then divorced her — he is obligated to remarry her
↓
Conclusion: The mitzva FOLLOWS and rectifies the prohibition; therefore the rapist IS subject to lashes if he violates the prohibition without remedy
↓
Similarly the defamer: 'She shall be his wife' is redundant (she is already his wife), so it must teach the same remarriage rule after divorce

💍 Rapist (אוֹנֵס) vs. Defamer (מוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע)

אוֹנֵס — Rapist

  • Must marry his victim (Deut. 22:29)
  • Cannot divorce her: 'he may not send her away'
  • 'She shall be his wife' — redundant before marriage → teaches remarriage duty
  • Mitzva FOLLOWS & rectifies the prohibition
  • If divorced, must remarry her all his days
  • Rava: source is the superfluous phrase 'כָּל יָמָיו — all his days'

מוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע — Defamer

  • Cannot divorce his wife whom he slandered (Deut. 22:19)
  • She was already his wife before the prohibition arose
  • 'She shall be his wife' is entirely redundant here
  • Redundancy teaches: if he divorces, he must remarry
  • The defamer is the source from which the rapist's law is derived
  • Both carry the lifelong obligation to remarry if divorced

❓ The Great Dispute: Uncertain Forewarning (הַתְרָאַת סָפֵק)

Baraita
For a lav ha-nitak l'aseh: if he fulfilled the positive mitzva — exempt; if he did not fulfill it — liable to lashes. But in both cases he is exempt!
R. Yoḥanan
This baraita is self-contradictory! The first clause says 'if not fulfilled — liable,' yet the end says 'in both cases exempt.' How can both be true?
Gemara
They disagree about הַתְרָאַת סָפֵק — forewarning where it is uncertain whether he will eventually fulfill the mitzva or not.
R. Yoḥanan
Uncertain forewarning IS valid forewarning. He can be warned and then flogged — even if he later fulfills the mitzva, the lashes were properly administered.
Reish Lakish
Uncertain forewarning is NOT valid forewarning. If he eventually fulfills the mitzva, it proves the warning was never truly valid — hence he is always exempt.
Parallel Case — Their Standard Reasoning 'I swear I will eat this loaf today' — and the day passed without eating. R. Yoḥanan: flogged (valid forewarning). Reish Lakish: not flogged (the warning was uncertain — he might have eaten).
