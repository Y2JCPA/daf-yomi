⚖️ Ger Toshav Who Kills Another Ger Toshav — Exiled or Executed?

Rav Ḥisda
The contradiction between the mishna and baraita depends on direction of the blow: a downward motion → exile; an upward motion → execution.
Rava
Objection (a fortiori): If downward — where a Jew would be exiled — the ger toshav suffices with exile, then for upward he should be exiled a fortiori! Your distinction collapses.
Rava
Rather: The baraita refers to a ger toshav who said it was permitted to kill the victim. If he acted unintentionally without such a claim, he is exiled in accordance with the mishna.
Abaye
Objection to Rava: Abimelech said "Will You slay a righteous nation?" and God did not call him wicked — implying an unknowing gentile is not liable!
Underlying Dispute Rav Ḥisda and Rava disagree about a ger toshav who thought he was killing an animal but killed a person: Rav Ḥisda says exiled (like a Jew); Rava says executed (like a gentile who lacked license).

👁️ Is a Blind Person Exiled for Unintentional Murder?

Rabbi Yehuda
Not exiled. "Without seeing" (Num. 35:23) excludes a blind person who never sees. The forest analogy (Deut. 19:5) likewise implies the capacity to see — which the blind person lacks.
Rabbi Meir
Is exiled. "Without seeing" AND "without knowledge" are two consecutive restrictions — ribui achar ribui (restriction after restriction) — which actually expands the law to include the blind.
Rabbi Yehuda
Rebuttal: "Without knowledge" excludes one with intent (who intended to kill an animal but killed a person). It does not expand to include the blind.
Key Verses "Without seeing" (Num. 35:23) · "Without knowledge" (Deut. 19:4) · "As one who goes into the forest with his neighbor" (Deut. 19:5) — each side reads these differently to determine whether sight is required for exile.

😠 The Enemy of the Victim — Three Rabbinic Views

Mishna (Tanna Kamma)

  • An enemy is not exiled
  • Presumed to have acted with at least partial intent
  • Cannot be classified as purely unintentional
  • Lacks the benefit of the doubt given to strangers

Rabbi Yosei

  • An enemy is executed
  • His status equals one who is forewarned
  • Even without explicit warning, the enmity itself is prior notice
  • Courts treat him as an intentional killer

Rabbi Shimon

  • Some enemies are exiled, some are not
  • Rope snapped upward → hit friend: exiled
  • Rope snapped upward → hit enemy: not exiled
  • Object displaced downward onto enemy: exiled (per Sages)

🏙️ Cities of Refuge — Infrastructure and Procedure

Moses designated 3 cities in Transjordan (east bank of the Jordan)
↓
Joshua designated 3 cities in the land of Canaan — cities were aligned like two rows of vines in a vineyard
↓
Roads were paved and straightened from every city to the cities of refuge: "Prepare for you the road" (Deut. 19:3)
↓
Signposts at every crossroads read "Miklat! Miklat!" (מִקְלָט — refuge!) to guide the fleeing unintentional killer
↓
Two Torah scholars escorted the exile — to speak to the blood redeemer (go'el hadam) and persuade him not to kill the exile on the road
↓
Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda: Originally both intentional and unintentional killers fled to the city; the court later retrieved the intentional killer for trial and execution
