Makkot 22 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does Rava say to replace 'kindling fire' in the list of prohibitions?
A. Because kindling is not a biblical prohibition
B. Because kindling is subsumed under cooking on a Festival (no division of labors)
C. Because kindling fire is permitted on a Festival
D. Because the sciatic nerve cannot be cooked with fire
Correct: B — Since there is no division of labors on a Festival, kindling is not a separate prohibition — it is already included in the prohibition of cooking the sciatic nerve.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does Rava's statement about people standing for a Torah scroll teach?
A. One must stand for the Torah scroll at all times
B. The Sages deserve great honor because they have authority to interpret the Torah — reducing 40 to 39
C. Torah scholars are more important than the Torah itself
D. Standing for a Torah scroll is not required by law
Correct: B — Rava argues: if the Sages can reinterpret the Torah's 'forty' as thirty-nine, their authority is immense, and people should stand before them just as they stand for a Torah scroll.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does R' Mani reject R' Zeira's suggestion to add an oath-violation to the list of prohibitions?
A. Oaths cannot apply to mitzvot at all
B. The Tanna excludes matters subject to dissolution by a Torah scholar
C. R' Zeira's oath scenario is impossible
D. Oaths are only rabbinic in nature
Correct: B — R' Mani explains that the mishna's Tanna does not enumerate prohibitions that fall into the category of matters that can be dissolved by a Sage — and oaths can be dissolved.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rav Sheshet reconcile the mishna and baraita regarding changed assessments?
A. The mishna is the later, authoritative source
B. The mishna deals with a mistaken same-day assessment; the baraita deals with an actual change in condition
C. The baraita applies only to women
D. The mishna discusses capital cases while the baraita discusses lashes
Correct: B — Rav Sheshet distinguishes: the mishna's case involves a mistaken assessment (so he must actually be flogged first to be exempt), while the baraita's case involves a genuine change in condition (exempt even without flogging).
QUESTION 5 OF 5
When can a single assessment cover two sets of lashes for two different prohibitions?
A. Whenever the offender agrees to receive them together
B. Only when the total assessed capacity allows division into two groups each divisible by three
C. Always — the court prefers efficiency
D. Only when the two prohibitions are from the same Torah verse
Correct: B — Rav Sheshet explains: if the assessed total (e.g., 42) can be split into groups each divisible by three (39 + 3), one assessment suffices. If not (e.g., 41 yields only 39 usable), two separate assessments are needed.
