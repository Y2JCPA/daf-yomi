💬 What Does 'Pays His Share' Mean? (Rav's Ruling)

Rav Yehuda / Rav
A conspiring witness pays according to his share — i.e., half the stated sum.
Gemara
But can a single witness impose financial liability? One witness alone cannot obligate anyone!
Rava
Rav's ruling applies when a witness declares: 'We testified and were convicted as conspiring witnesses in court X and found liable for a specific sum.' He is admitting an established judgment.
Gemara
You might think: since his admission cannot obligate his co-witness, he too should go free. Rav teaches: he still pays his own half!
Key Principle A witness's personal admission about his own liability binds him — even when it cannot obligate his fellow witness.

💍 Calculating Payment: The Marriage Contract Case

Scenario: Witnesses falsely testify that a man divorced his wife without paying her ketubbah
↓
Question: What do the conspiring witnesses owe?
↓
Step 1 — Mishna: Assess how much a buyer would pay to acquire rights to this woman's ketubbah, knowing she may or may not collect (she might die before her husband; he might not divorce her)
↓
Rav Natan bar Oshaya: Frame the calculation from the wife's side — the probability she will outlive her husband or be divorced and thus collect
↓
Rav Pappa adds: Include only the ketubbah amount in the calculation — not usufruct property she brought into the marriage
↓
Result: Witnesses pay the market price of the probabilistic right to collect — not the full face value of the ketubbah

🌾 Two Versions: Shmuel on Shemita and a 10-Year Loan

Version 1 — Shemita DOES Abrogate

  • Shmuel: A 10-year loan is cancelled by the Sabbatical Year
  • Even if repayment was not yet due when shemita arrived, the debt is wiped out
  • Rav Kahana objects: The mishna calculates full loan value — implying the term holds!
  • Rava answers: The mishna discusses collateral-backed or court-transferred loans — those are never abrogated by shemita

Version 2 — Shemita Does NOT Abrogate

  • Shmuel: A 10-year loan is not cancelled by the Sabbatical Year
  • Even when shemita arrives mid-term, the debt survives intact
  • Rav Kahana supports: The mishna calculates full loan value — proving the shemita has no effect
  • Rava deflects: Same answer — mishna deals only with non-abrogable loans; no proof either way

📋 Can You Stipulate Away Shemita Abrogation?

Lender says: 'I lend you money on condition that shemita will not cancel this debt'
↓
Formulation A — Invalid
'On condition that you have no claim of abrogation against me'

Framed as nullifying a Torah rule → Shemita still cancels the debt
Formulation B — Valid
'On condition that you will not invoke abrogation during shemita'

Borrower waives his own monetary right → Debt survives shemita
↓
Rav Anan citing Shmuel: Wording is everything — waiving a personal right is valid; purporting to nullify Torah law is not

📋 Makkot 3 — Summary Map

Core Rule: Edim Zomemim (עדים זוממים) pay the market value of what they tried to impose — calculated probabilistically
↓
Ketubbah case:
Actuarial value of wife's right to collect, calculated from her perspective (Rav Natan, Rav Pappa)
Loan case:
Benefit-of-delay value — difference between 30-day and full-term holding of 1,000 dinars
↓
Shemita complication: Does Sabbatical Year cancel long-term loans? Two versions of Shmuel disagree — but collateral-backed and court-transferred loans are exempt in all opinions (Rava)
↓
Stipulation rule: A borrower may waive his own right to shemita abrogation with proper wording — but no stipulation can nullify the Torah's cancellation rule itself
