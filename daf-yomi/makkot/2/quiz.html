Makkot 2 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the tractate Makkot open by asking 'How ARE witnesses rendered conspiring?' rather than focusing on the exceptions?
A. To establish the basic definition before discussing exceptions
B. Because Makkot continues directly from Sanhedrin, which discussed those not liable until the act is done — Makkot parallels that theme
C. Because all witnesses are presumed to be conspiring until proven otherwise
D. To introduce the requirement of lashes for false testimony
Correct: B — The Gemara explains: Makkot follows Sanhedrin (which ended: 'not liable until the act is done'). Makkot continues the parallel — just as some criminals escape punishment, so do some conspiring witnesses.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is Bar Padda's a fortiori argument for why conspiring witnesses in priesthood cases are not disqualified?
A. If the victim of false testimony suffers no lasting harm, the witnesses should not either
B. If a priest who actually disqualifies his own son through marriage does not himself lose priestly status, witnesses who only spoke certainly should not
C. If non-priests cannot receive priestly punishments, witnesses are exempt regardless
D. Witnesses are always exempt from laws that apply only to priests
Correct: B — Bar Padda: a priest who actually commits the disqualifying act (marrying a divorcée) does not lose his own priestly status — so witnesses who merely testified should certainly not lose theirs.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Ulla, what specific scenario does 'do to him as he conspired' (Deuteronomy 19:19) address?
A. Any and all cases of false testimony before a court
B. Only monetary disputes involving false witnesses
C. Witnesses who condemned an innocent person, but other witnesses came and vindicated the accused before execution
D. Only cases where the accused was actually executed based on the false testimony
Correct: C — Ulla says the verse addresses the case where witnesses condemned the righteous but the accused was vindicated by other witnesses — and crucially, punishment only applies if this happened BEFORE execution.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Who holds that 'ransom is atonement,' and what is the practical consequence for conspiring witnesses?
A. The Rabbis hold this; therefore conspiring witnesses pay double ransom
B. Rabbi Yishmael son of R. Yoḥanan ben Beroka holds this; therefore conspiring witnesses cannot pay ransom since they have nothing to atone for
C. Rav Ḥisda holds this; therefore the court assesses ransom based on the victim's value
D. Rava holds this; conspiring witnesses pay ransom only if they have no other means
Correct: B — Rabbi Yishmael holds that ransom serves as atonement for the person whose ox killed another. Since the conspiring witnesses did not kill anyone, they have no atonement to make — so no ransom applies to them.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is Rabbi Akiva's unique addition to the laws of conspiring witnesses regarding monetary payment?
A. Conspiring witnesses must pay double the standard amount as a deterrent
B. Conspiring witnesses do not pay based on their own admission, since their liability resembles a kenas (fine)
C. Conspiring witnesses are exempt from all payments if they are priests
D. Conspiring witnesses pay only after a second court confirms the conspiracy
Correct: B — Rabbi Akiva holds that conspiring witnesses' payment has the character of a kenas (punitive fine). By talmudic law, a kenas cannot be collected based solely on the defendant's own confession — only court-proven liability triggers payment.
