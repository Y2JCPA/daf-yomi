☠️ The Destructive Tribunal

Tanna Kamma
A Sanhedrin that executes once in seven years is called a destructive tribunal.
R. Elazar b. Azarya
Even once in seventy years earns the label destructive.
R. Tarfon & R. Akiva
Had we sat on the Sanhedrin, no one would ever have been executed — we would have found technical grounds to spare every defendant.
Rabban Shimon b. Gamliel
They too would have multiplied the shedders of blood in Israel — a court that never convicts removes deterrence and emboldens murderers.
The Tension Mercy for the individual vs. safety of society — the eternal judicial dilemma at the heart of this daf.

🏃 Fleeing Defendant: Which Court Retries?

Defendant is sentenced to death — then flees before execution
↓
Returns to same court
Verdict stands — not overturned or retried
Returns to different court
Verdict overturned — case retried from scratch
↓
Abaye's resolution: If the original verdict was rendered in Eretz Yisrael, a different Israeli court overturns and retries it. If the original verdict was outside Eretz Yisrael, even a different court does not overturn — as taught by R. Yehuda ben Dostai in the name of R. Shimon ben Shataḥ.

🔪 How Would R. Tarfon & R. Akiva Avoid Execution?

In a murder case
They would challenge the witnesses: Did you warn the defendant immediately before he struck? Without precise prior warning, no conviction.
Rav Ashi
Even examined postmortem and found intact — perhaps there was a pre-existing perforation in an organ rendering the victim a treifa (already dying), meaning the blow did not legally cause death.
Abaye & Rava
In a forbidden relations case: they would ask the witnesses, Did you observe the intercourse like a brush inserted in a tube? — an impossibly precise evidentiary standard.
Rabban Shimon b. Gamliel
This strategy multiplies murderers — a court known never to convict destroys the deterrent function of capital law entirely.
Principle Capital punishment demands near-impossible certainty. Technical judicial challenges are legitimate tools — but they carry a societal cost.

🏛️ Sanhedrin Authority Outside Eretz Yisrael

סַנְהֶדְרִין נוֹהֶגֶת בֵּין בָּאָרֶץ בֵּין בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ
Halakhic Ruling The mitzva to establish a Sanhedrin with authority to administer capital punishment applies both in Eretz Yisrael and outside Eretz Yisrael. This is derived from: "These are the statutes and laws you shall observe to do" (Deut. 12:1) — these laws apply everywhere. The verse "Judges and officers you shall appoint in all your gates" (Deut. 16:18) limits local lower courts to within Eretz Yisrael ('your gates'), but the Great Sanhedrin's authority transcends geographical borders.

📉 Downward Motion — The Exile Principle

Exiled ✅ — Downward Motion

  • Rolling a roller on a flat roof — it rolls off the edge and kills
  • Climbing a ladder — falls downward onto a person below
  • Butcher swings cleaver downward — blade flies off and kills
  • Ax head displaced from handle while chopping downward (Rabbis)

Not Exiled ❌ — Upward Motion

  • Pulling a rope or beam upward — it slips and falls back
  • Climbing ladder — rung snaps and projects victim upward first
  • Throwing a stone upward — it arcs back and kills
  • Intentional killer — faces death penalty, not exile

Ladder rung displaced underfoot; rung falls and kills person belowDilemma raised by R. Abbahu to R. Yoḥanan: is this upward or downward motion? Unresolved directlyResolution: worm-infested rung = liable (should have checked); sturdy but loose = exempt; tight & checked = exempt
Butcher's backswing — cleaver raised behind him for momentum, kills bystanderDownward swing in front: exiled. Upward backswing: not exiledBaraita: both liable if cleaver swings down both directions; both exempt if motion is purely upward both ways
