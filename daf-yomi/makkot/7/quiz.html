Makkot 7 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Abaye, when does a second (different) court overturn the verdict of the first court for a defendant who fled?
A. Always — any different court may retry the case
B. Only when the original verdict was issued in Eretz Yisrael and the defendant appears before another Israeli court
C. Only when the original verdict was issued outside Eretz Yisrael
D. Never — a delivered verdict is always final regardless of court
Correct: B — Abaye resolves the contradiction: if the original verdict was in Eretz Yisrael, a second Israeli court overturns and retries it. If it was issued outside Eretz Yisrael, even a different court does not overturn — as taught by R. Yehuda ben Dostai.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, how often must a Sanhedrin execute to be labeled 'destructive'?
A. Once in seven years
B. Once in seventeen years
C. Once in seventy years
D. Once in a hundred years
Correct: C — Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya says even a Sanhedrin that executes once in seventy years is called destructive — a far stricter threshold than the Tanna Kamma's 'once in seven years.'
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How would Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel respond to R. Tarfon and R. Akiva's position that they would never execute anyone?
A. He agreed — sparing life is always the greater mitzva
B. He said their questioning methods were halakhically invalid
C. He said they would have multiplied the shedders of blood in Israel
D. He said they lacked authority to challenge witnesses in that manner
Correct: C — Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel countered that a court known never to convict removes the deterrent effect of capital law, effectively enabling more murders — multiplying bloodshed rather than reducing it.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the fundamental principle determining whether an unintentional murderer is exiled to a City of Refuge?
A. Whether the victim received a prior warning
B. Whether the killing occurred within Eretz Yisrael
C. Whether the killing occurred through a downward motion
D. Whether the killer had any negligence whatsoever
Correct: C — The mishna establishes: one who kills unintentionally through his downward motion is exiled; one who kills through an upward motion (e.g., pulling a beam upward that falls back) is not exiled — derived from the verse about the ax head flying while chopping.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In the dispute about the ax blade that flies from its handle and kills, what is Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's reason for exempting the killer from exile?
A. The killer had intent, so he faces death rather than exile
B. The verse states the blade displaces 'from the wood' — meaning the tree being chopped, not a wooden handle
C. Ax-related deaths are always classified as intentional in rabbinic law
D. The displacement occurred through upward rather than downward motion
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi reads Deut. 19:5 literally: 'the blade displaces from the wood' refers to the tree being chopped down — not from a wooden ax handle. Since the handle-displacement scenario isn't covered by the verse, no exile applies.
