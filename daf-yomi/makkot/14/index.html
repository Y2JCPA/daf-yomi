⚖️ The Great Karet-Lashes Debate

Rabbi Yitzchak
One liable to karet is NOT flogged — karet exempts from lashes. I derive this from the fact that karet for intercourse with one's sister emerged from a generalization, indicating these two punishments are mutually exclusive.
The Rabbis (R. Yishmael & R. Akiva)
One liable to karet IS flogged! The karet verse for one's sister serves to divide the prohibitions into discrete offenses — paternal sister and maternal sister are separate, each requiring its own sin-offering.
The Stakes This dispute determines whether transgressors punishable by karet also receive court-administered lashes, or whether heavenly punishment stands alone.

🔀 Why Is Karet Written for Intercourse with One's Sister?

Question: Since sister's karet came from a general verse (all abominations), why does the Torah repeat karet specifically for one's sister?
↓
Rabbis' Answer
To divide the prohibitions — paternal sister and maternal sister are counted as separate offenses; one brings separate sin-offerings for each
Rabbi Yitzchak's Answer
The repetition signals that karet for sister emerged from a generalization, teaching it does not coexist with lashes — two punishments cannot apply simultaneously
↓
⚠️ Each opinion must find an alternative source for what the other derives from the sister-karet verse

📊 Rabbi Akiva's Question: Multiple Sin-Offerings for One Lapse

Rabbi Akiva's Question

  • A man had relations with his mother, his father's wife, and his daughter-in-law in a single lapse of awareness
  • Are these one sin-offering or three separate sin-offerings?
  • Three different women, three different prohibition names — does each trigger separate liability?

Rabban Gamliel & R. Yehoshua

  • "We did not hear the halakha in that case"
  • But for five menstruating women in one lapse — he brings five separate sin-offerings
  • Separate bodies + separate prohibition names = separate liability

The A Fortiori Attempt & Its Refutation

  • If five women with the same prohibition name each require a separate offering...
  • Surely three women with different prohibition names require separate offerings!
  • ⚠️ Refuted: Menstruating women have a unique feature — what is notable about them may not apply to the other case

🕍 Source for Prohibition: Impure Person Eating Sacrificial Food

Reish Lakish
The prohibition derives from "No consecrated item shall she touch" (Leviticus 12:4), written for a woman after childbirth. "Touch" is read broadly as eating. The verse applies before and after the blood-sprinkling with no distinction.
Rabbi Yochanan (via Bardela)
The prohibition derives via גזרה שוה — a verbal analogy on the term "טומאתו / his impurity" which appears in both the verse about eating sacrificial food and the verse about the impure person, linking the two contexts.
Why Reish Lakish Rejects the Gezerah Shavah A gezerah shavah may only be applied when received as a tradition from one's teachers. Reish Lakish did not receive this verbal analogy as a tradition and therefore cannot independently invoke it.

✨ Rabba bar bar Chana's Rule: Positive Mitzvot That Precede Prohibitions

כָּל לָאו שֶׁקָּדְמוֹ עֲשֵׂה — לְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל לוֹקִין עָלָיו
Rabba bar bar Chana citing Rabbi Yochanan Any prohibition that has a positive mitzva that preceded it — everyone agrees that one is flogged for its violation. A lav hanitak l'aseh (prohibition remediable by a positive mitzva) exempts from lashes only when the positive mitzva follows as a corrective remedy. When the positive mitzva came before the prohibition, it cannot serve as a remedy, and full lash liability applies without any dispute.
