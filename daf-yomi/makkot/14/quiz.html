Makkot 14 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yitzchak, from where is it derived that one liable to karet is NOT flogged?
A. From a verbal analogy (gezerah shavah) between two verses about punishment
B. From the fact that karet for intercourse with one's sister emerged from a general verse
C. From an explicit Talmudic ruling that karet replaces lashes
D. From the principle that only rabbinically-enacted prohibitions incur lashes
Correct: B — Rabbi Yitzchak derives that karet exempts from lashes because the karet for intercourse with one's sister was already included in the general verse about karet for all abominations. Its specific restatement indicates it stands as karet alone, without accompanying lashes.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
When Rabbi Akiva asked Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua about a man who had relations with his mother, his father's wife, and his daughter-in-law in one lapse of awareness, what was their response?
A. He brings one sin-offering since it was a single lapse of awareness
B. He brings three sin-offerings — one for each woman
C. They had not heard that case, but knew that five menstruating women in one lapse require five separate sin-offerings
D. He is exempt because the violations share overlapping prohibitions
Correct: C — Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua said they had not heard the halakha for that specific case, but they had a tradition that one who engages in intercourse with five menstruating women in a single lapse of awareness brings five separate sin-offerings — one per woman.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Reish Lakish's source for the prohibition against a ritually impure person eating sacrificial food?
A. A verbal analogy (gezerah shavah) linking 'his impurity' in two different verses
B. The verse 'No consecrated item shall she touch' (Leviticus 12:4), concerning a woman after childbirth
C. An explicit prohibition in the laws of the Temple service
D. A kal v'chomer inference from the laws governing teruma
Correct: B — Reish Lakish derives the prohibition from 'No consecrated item shall she touch' (Leviticus 12:4). He reads 'touch' broadly to include eating, and applies the verse to any impure person — not only the woman after childbirth for whom it was originally written.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does Reish Lakish NOT employ the verbal analogy (gezerah shavah) that Rabbi Yochanan uses to derive the prohibition for impure eating of sacrificial food?
A. Because a gezerah shavah cannot be used for prohibitions involving Temple matters
B. Because the two verses use slightly different forms of the word 'impurity'
C. Because he did not receive this gezerah shavah as a tradition from his teachers
D. Because he holds that a gezerah shavah only applies to positive commandments
Correct: C — A gezerah shavah (verbal analogy) may only be applied when received as an authoritative tradition. Reish Lakish did not receive this particular gezerah shavah from his teachers, and therefore he cannot independently invoke it — he must find an alternative source.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rabba bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, what is the rule when a positive mitzva PRECEDED a prohibition?
A. The positive mitzva can remedy the violation, so one is NOT flogged
B. Everyone agrees one IS flogged — the prior positive mitzva does not serve as a post-violation remedy
C. The Rabbis disagree whether lashes apply in such a case
D. One brings a guilt-offering instead of receiving lashes
Correct: B — Rabba bar bar Chana rules that if a positive mitzva preceded (rather than followed) the prohibition, everyone agrees one is flogged. A 'lav hanitak l'aseh' (prohibition remediable by a positive mitzva) exempts from lashes only when the positive mitzva follows as a corrective; a preceding positive mitzva cannot serve that role.
