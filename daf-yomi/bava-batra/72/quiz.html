Bava Batra 72 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Akiva, how does the generosity of consecration compare to that of selling?
A. Consecration is less generous — it follows a strict interpretation
B. Consecration and selling are equally generous
C. Consecration is even more generous than selling (kal v'chomer)
D. Consecration follows the buyer's intent, not the consecrator's
Correct: C — Rabbi Akiva holds מוכר בעין יפה מוכר — a seller sells generously. By kal v'chomer (a fortiori), one who consecrates does so even more generously, since consecration is a more complete act of religious giving.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does the Gemara resolve the apparent contradiction between Rabbi Shimon's mishna ruling and his known agreement with Rabbi Akiva?
A. Rabbi Shimon retracted his agreement with Rabbi Akiva later in his career
B. In the mishna, Rabbi Shimon was speaking in the Rabbis' voice, not expressing his own view
C. Rabbi Akiva's rule applies only to sales of movable property, not fields
D. The mishna passage was ruled a later editorial addition by the Gemara
Correct: B — The Gemara concludes that Rabbi Shimon in the mishna articulated the Rabbis' position — 'speaking to the Rabbis in accordance with their statement' — to show a difficulty within their own view, not to express his personal halakhic opinion.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What dual halakhic status does Rav Huna assign to a grafted carob tree?
A. Status of a tree and status of a fruit
B. Status of land and status of a vegetable garden
C. Status of a tree and status of land
D. Status of a sheaf and status of a heap
Correct: C — Rav Huna rules that a grafted carob has both the status of a tree (counting toward the two-tree minimum for acquiring surrounding land) and the status of land (so it does not interrupt the field's consecration or purchase).
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rabbi Meir, if someone buys a field from his father, the father dies, and only then he consecrates the field — how is it treated?
A. As an ancestral inherited field with higher redemption value
B. As a purchased field with lower redemption value
C. The consecration is invalid because ownership is disputed
D. As a donated field — a separate halakhic category
Correct: B — Rabbi Meir holds that consecration status is determined at the time of consecration. Since the field was purchased (not yet inherited) when consecrated, it is treated as a purchased field — the subsequent death does not retroactively elevate its status.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
A large sheaf containing two se'a is forgotten in the field. According to Rav Huna and Mishna Pe'a, what is the correct halakhic outcome?
A. It must always be left for the poor, since it is a sheaf
B. It is always exempt as a heap, regardless of other sheaves nearby
C. Alone it is exempt (status of heap), but it counts as a second sheaf when paired with another forgotten sheaf
D. Its status is disputed and no clear ruling emerges
Correct: C — Rav Huna assigns it dual status: as a heap, it is exempt when forgotten alone (Mishna Pe'a 6:6); but as a sheaf, it counts toward the two-sheaf minimum. So if a small sheaf is also forgotten nearby, the large sheaf serves as the second sheaf, obligating the small one to be left for the poor.
