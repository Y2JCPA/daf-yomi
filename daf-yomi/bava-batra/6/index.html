⚖️ Can Migu Override a Chazakah?

Questioner
When a borrower says 'I already paid,' why not believe him via a migu? He could have said 'I never borrowed' — so we should believe him when he claims repayment!
Counter
But a chazakah exists: people do not repay early! This presumption against his claim undermines any migu argument.
Rav Aḥa b. Rava
Proof from Shevuot (38b): If A says 'You owe me 100 dinars' and B admits but claims 'I gave it back,' B must still swear — showing migu does not automatically exempt!
Rav Ashi
That case differs — the admission itself creates a new obligation. The migu question here depends on specific timing circumstances.
Core Tension Throughout Daf 6, the Gemara probes whether a migu (I could have claimed worse) can override an established chazakah (legal presumption). The answer turns on whether the presumption is strong enough to neutralize the migu's force.

🪵 Escalating Beam Rights — Rav Naḥman

Unchallenged use of neighbor's wall establishes an acquired right (chazakah)
↓
Version 1 (Ascending)
Right to place thin beams → grants right to thick beams;
thick beams → grants right to a full roof covering
Version 2 (Descending)
Right to place thick beams → grants right to thin beams;
right to roof covering → grants right to thick beams
↓
Principle: A lesser acquired right does not automatically confer a greater one — each level must be independently established

💧 Water Drainage Rights — Two Versions of Rav Naḥman

Version 1 — Lesser to Greater

  • Right to let water drip from roof → right to pour via a drainpipe
  • Right to use a drainpipe → right to channel via a gutter
  • Neighbor's tolerance of lesser intrusion grants the greater
  • Logical: if you allowed the smaller burden, you accepted the larger

Version 2 — Greater to Lesser

  • Right to use a drainpipe → right to let water drip from roof
  • Right to channel via a gutter → right to use a drainpipe
  • Neighbor's tolerance of greater intrusion implies lesser
  • Logical: one who accepted more certainly accepted less

🏘️ Rights of a Renter in a Large Building

בבא בתרא ו' ע"ב — השוכר בית בחצר גדולה
Rabba bar Avuh (via Rav Naḥman) A renter may use the building's external projections (ledges, overhangs) and wall cavities within four cubits of his rented room. He may also use the thickness of the wall wherever a window opens into it. These are considered part of the normal entitlements of any tenant — the rental implicitly includes these conveniences.

Ravina adds: a beam resting on a neighbor's wall for up to thirty days does not create an acquired right — the neighbor may claim he permitted it temporarily.

🔒 Rooftop Fences — Abaye vs. Rav Naḥman

Abaye
Two houses across a public domain: each owner builds a fence on half his own roof, but positioned so the fences are not directly opposite — eliminating the line of sight between rooftops.
Questioner
Why specify a public domain? The same rule applies across a private domain!
Abaye
Needed for a case where one built first. The other cannot say 'you build it all and I'll pay you.' Each must build his own section — otherwise deadlock results in no fence at all.
Rav Naḥman (Shmuel)
If one's roof is adjacent to a neighbor's courtyard, he must build a fence four cubits high on the roof to prevent viewing in — but need not build a fence between roof and roof.
Privacy Principle — הֶזֵּק רְאִיָּה Damage through sight (hezeq re'iyah) is legally recognized harm. The public's view by day does not exempt a neighbor who can see by both day and night.
