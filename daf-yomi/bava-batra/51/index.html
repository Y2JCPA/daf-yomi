💰 Does a Wife Acquire a Field Sold by Her Husband?

Rav Naḥman
If a husband sells a field to his wife, she has acquired it. We do not assume the sale is an artifice to expose her concealed money.
Rav Huna bar Avin
Agreed — she acquires the field, but the husband retains the right to enjoy its profits as with any usufruct property.
Rabbi Abba & Rabbi Abbahu
The husband did not intend to give her full ownership — only usufruct rights. She does not acquire title.
Rava (Final Ruling)
Sale to wife → she does not acquire (likely an artifice). Gift to wife → she does acquire, and husband does not retain usufruct.
Core Tension Is a husband's transaction with his wife a genuine legal act, or a stratagem to reveal her hidden money (מָמוֹן צָנוּעַ) and reclaim both it and the field?

🏠 Wife Cannot Establish Chazakah Over Husband's Property

אין האשה מחזקת בנכסי בעלה
Mishna Principle A wife cannot establish presumption of ownership (chazakah) over her husband's property through use. Since she has a right to sustenance from his property, her use is attributed to that right — not to independent ownership. The same logic prevents a husband from establishing chazakah over his wife's property.

Gemara adds: by inference, a wife can bring documentary proof of ownership — so a sale from husband to wife is at least conceptually possible.

📜 Bill of Sale vs. Deed of Gift — Shmuel's Distinction

Husband transfers field to wife — which document was used?
↓
📜 Deed of Gift
Document alone suffices — she acquires immediately upon receiving the deed, even before money changes hands
💰 Bill of Sale
She does not acquire until money is actually paid — document alone is insufficient (Shmuel)
↓
Why does a gift deed work alone? A seller leaving town is appeased by the document even before payment. But a husband selling to his wife lacks this typical commercial motive — making the transaction suspect.

🎭 The Concealed Money Artifice — Objections and Responses

Gemara's Concern
When a husband sells a field to his wife, perhaps he intends to expose her concealed savings — she pays him, he pockets the money and then reclaims the field, gaining both.
Rav Naḥman
We do not suspect this. The sale is valid and she acquires. Only a genuine artifice with additional evidence warrants suspicion.
Objection (Baraita)
If one borrows money from his own slave then frees him, or from his wife then divorces her — they have no legal claim on him! Proves borrowing from wife is always deemed an artifice.
Rav Naḥman's Resolution
That case is different — a man is deeply ashamed to appear as a borrower-servant to his own wife. He would never genuinely intend such a debt relationship, so it is presumed an artifice.
Verse Applied "The rich rules over the poor, and the borrower is servant to the lender" (Proverbs 22:7) — a husband as servant to his wife is intolerable, hence no genuine loan

⚖️ Sale vs. Gift to Wife — Full Ruling Spectrum

💸 Sale of Field to Wife

  • Rav Naḥman: She acquires; husband keeps usufruct profits
  • Rav Huna bar Avin: She acquires; husband keeps usufruct profits
  • Rabbi Abba & Abbahu: She does not acquire full ownership
  • Rava (final): She does NOT acquire — artifice presumed
  • Husband continues enjoying all profits regardless

🎁 Gift of Field to Wife

  • Rav: She acquires; husband enjoys profits (usufruct)
  • Rabbi Elazar: She acquires; husband does NOT get profits
  • Rav Ḥisda: Ruled like Rabbi Elazar in practice
  • Rava (final): She DOES acquire the field
  • Husband does not retain any usufruct rights
