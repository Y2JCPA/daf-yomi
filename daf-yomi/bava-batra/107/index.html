⚖️ Brothers Who Divided: Heirs or Purchasers?

Rav
Brothers who divided inherited property remain heirs with respect to each other. If a creditor seizes one brother's portion, the original division is void — they re-divide the remainder equally.
Shmuel
Brothers who divided are like purchasers from each other. Each implicitly guaranteed the other's portion. If a share is seized, the remaining brothers compensate proportionally.
Rav Asi
Uncertain whether brothers are heirs or purchasers — the question is unresolved. He raises practical cases testing both possibilities.
Rav Pappa
Halakha follows Shmuel in all these disputes. A brother whose share is seized receives proportional compensation from each of the others' portions.
Core Question Does dividing an inheritance transform brothers into 'buyers' of each other's portions — with purchase guarantees — or do they remain co-heirs who absorb losses together?

⚖️ Rav vs. Shmuel: Two Models of Inheritance Division

Rav — Brothers Remain Heirs

  • Division of inheritance is provisional
  • Creditor seizes one portion → entire division is void
  • Brothers re-divide the remaining property equally
  • No one 'sold' anything — all remain heirs together
  • Loss falls on all brothers equally

Shmuel — Brothers Become Purchasers

  • Division of inheritance is permanent and binding
  • Each brother 'purchased' his share from the others
  • Implicit guarantee (אחריות) accompanies each portion
  • If portion seized: others compensate proportionally
  • Division stands — only guarantors bear the loss

📐 Aḥerim's Formula Step by Step

Three appraisers submit valuations:
80 dinars / 100 dinars / 120 dinars
↓
Calculate total spread between extremes:
120 − 80 = 40 dinars
↓
Divide spread evenly by three appraisers:
40 ÷ 3 = 13⅓ dinars
↓
Add result to the lowest assessment:
80 + 13⅓ = 93⅓ dinars
Why this formula?
Distributes total appraiser error equally across all three — no single assessor's error dominates
Rav Huna's Ruling
Halakha follows Aḥerim — this weighted minimum method is the accepted legal standard

🌾 Selling Half a Field: Which Half Does the Buyer Receive?

R. Yoḥanan
When one sells 'half my field' without specifying — the buyer receives the leaner (lower quality) half. The seller retains the better portion for himself.
R. Ḥiyya bar Abba
But the latter clause of the Mishna says: if the seller specifies 'the southern half,' an assessment is made and the buyer takes exactly that half — even if it is inferior in quality!
R. Yoḥanan
No contradiction! Unspecified sale → buyer gets leaner half (seller keeps the better). Specified side → buyer gets exactly what was named, regardless of quality.
Mishna Rule (107b) The buyer accepts upon himself to provide — out of his own property — the space for the boundary fence, the larger ditch, and the smaller ditch.

🪨 Dividing a Sold Field: Procedure and Obligations

Step 1 — Assessment: Experts evaluate the field's total value and the relative quality of each section
↓
Step 2 — Unspecified Sale ('Half a Field'): Field is divided; buyer receives the leaner, lower-quality portion; seller keeps the better half
↓
Step 3 — Specified Sale ('Southern Half'): Both sides are assessed; buyer receives exactly the named portion whatever its quality
↓
Step 4 — Boundary Obligations: The buyer bears the cost and provides the land for the separating fence from his own portion
↓
Step 5 — Ditch Obligations: The larger irrigation ditch and smaller drainage ditch are dug on the buyer's side of the boundary to protect both fields
