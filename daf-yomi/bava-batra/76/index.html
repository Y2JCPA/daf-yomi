⚖️ First Tanna vs. Rabbi Natan

First Tanna
A ship is acquired by pulling. A promissory note is acquired by handing it over to the buyer.
Gemara
Why does Rabbi Natan suddenly mention promissory notes? They were not discussed by the First Tanna! The baraita must be incomplete — something is missing.
Rabbi Natan
On ships, I fully concede to you — they are acquired by pulling. But with regard to promissory notes, I disagree: the buyer must receive explicit written transfer of all liens within.
Gemara
So their dispute is not about ships at all — it is entirely about whether letters (the debt contents of a shtar) are automatically conveyed or require special language.
Key Insight Rabbi Natan concedes on ships entirely. The whole dispute concerns promissory notes and whether handing over the document alone transfers the shiabud (lien) on the debtor's property.

🔍 Rav Ashi's Resolution: When Do Rabbis Allow 'Passing'?

Rabbis (Default Rule)

  • Seller is particular about acquisition method
  • Only pulling into private domain effects transfer
  • Merely passing by is not sufficient
  • Requires a formal act of physical control

Rav Ashi's Exception

  • Seller says: "Go — take possession of it"
  • Even Rabbis agree passing now suffices
  • Seller has waived particularity about method
  • Explains Abaye & Rava's practice — within this exception

R. Yehuda HaNasi

  • Seller is indifferent to acquisition method
  • Any valid kinyan act suffices
  • Passing always works in public domain
  • No explicit permission from seller required

💰 Rav Pappa: Properly Selling a Promissory Note

Rav Pappa's Ruling: When a seller transfers a שטר (promissory note) to a buyer, the document alone is insufficient
↓
Required language: "Acquire this document and all liens on property contained within it" (קני שטרא וכל שיעבודא דאית ביה)
↓
Why? Without this language, the buyer acquires only the physical parchment — not the right to collect from encumbered property
↓
Rav Ashi's challenge: Why would anyone buy a promissory note just to use it as a flask stopper? Obviously the buyer wants to collect the debt!
↓
Rav Pappa's answer: Buyer's intent is irrelevant — we still require explicit written language to formally convey the shiabud nekhasim (lien on assets)
↓
Practical consequence: A purchased shtar without proper transfer language may be unenforceable against the debtor's encumbered property
