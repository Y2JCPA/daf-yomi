🔍 Three Rulings About Tumtum Found Male

Ameimar

  • Tumtum found to be male
  • Does NOT reduce firstborn's double portion
  • His share is ignored in the calculation

Rav Sheizevi

  • Tumtum found to be male
  • NOT circumcised on 8th day if it falls on Shabbat
  • Verse: 'gives birth to a male' — tumtum excluded

Rav Sherevya

  • Tumtum found to be male
  • Mother is NOT ritually impure for 7 days
  • Verse requires a definite male — tumtum excluded

Two wives give birth to two males in hiding — unknown which was born first
↓
Father subsequently has other sons with known birth order
↓
Rava's initial ruling
Each uncertain firstborn takes half a double portion from the other brothers — but not from each other
Correction via Ravin / Rabbi Yannai
Only if the sons were once recognized then lost. If never recognized as firstborn — neither receives a double portion

📜 Who Can Identify the Firstborn? Rabbi Yehuda vs. Rabbis

Rabbi Yehuda
A father is deemed credible to say 'This is my firstborn son' — even against an existing court presumption. Derived from: 'He shall acknowledge' (Deut. 21:17). Similarly, a priest is credible to name his firstborn animal.
Rabbis
A father is credible only when there is NO prior presumption about the firstborn. Once a son has a court-established presumption, the father cannot override it.
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
According to the Rabbis, for what situation is the verse needed? It teaches credibility regarding property that came into the father's possession after he testified — he could not have gifted it in advance.
Gemara (final answer)
Even according to Rabbi Meir (who allows transferring future property), the verse applies to property acquired while the father was moribund — when he cannot otherwise transfer ownership.
Shmuel's Response to Akra De'Agma Shmuel was uncertain whether the halakha follows Rabbi Yehuda or the Rabbis, so he sent a ruling incorporating both: the father is credible to name the firstborn, but cannot override an established court presumption.

🏛️ Contradictory Statements: Son vs. Slave

At home — Stage 1: Person declares about another, 'He is my son'
↓
At home — Stage 2: Same person then says, 'He is my slave'
↓
❌ Latter statement is NOT credible — cannot disqualify him from Jewish marriage or remove his inheritance rights
↓
At the tax house — Stage 1: Person says 'He is my son' to the tax collector
↓
At the tax house — Stage 2: Later says 'He is my slave'
↓
✅ Latter statement IS credible — the first was presumed a tax-evasion ploy, not a genuine declaration
↓
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak's nuance: Rabbi Yoḥanan's case involves someone already widely presumed to be a slave — reversal to 'slave' is therefore credible even at home

✉️ New Case: Stolen Slave or Purchased?

כִּי יִתֵּן אִישׁ אֶל-רֵעֵהוּ — When one person gives to another (Exodus 22:6)
✉️ Rabbi Abba's Letter from Eretz Yisrael One person claims: 'You stole my slave.' The other denies theft. When asked, 'Then where did he come from?', he replies: 'I bought him from you.'

This new case — where the defendant's story shifts from denial to an affirmative claim of purchase — opens discussion about the credibility of evolving counter-claims regarding ownership of persons.
