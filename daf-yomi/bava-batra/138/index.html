🗣️ Rabbi Yoḥanan vs. Shmuel — Gift Protest Rules

R. Yoḥanan
If the recipient shouts in protest immediately upon being given the deed, he does not acquire the property.
Shmuel
If ownership was transferred via a third party and the recipient was initially silent, he has acquired the gift.
Rava
Both are right in their cases: shouting at the outset = no acquisition (all agree); silent throughout = acquired (all agree). They only dispute: silent then shouted later.
R. Shimon b. Gamliel
His ultimate shouting proves his initial intent — he reasoned: why protest until they actually enter my domain? He never truly wanted the slaves.
Key Principle Timing of protest determines acquisition: initial silence creates it, immediate objection prevents it

💬 'As Is Appropriate For Him/Her' — Deathbed Gift Language

🧑 Firstborn Son

  • Father: 'Give 200 dinars to my firstborn, as is appropriate for him'
  • ✅ Receives the 200 dinars as a gift
  • ✅ Also takes his extra firstborn portion of the estate
  • The phrase adds his legal firstborn entitlement

👩 Wife

  • Husband: 'Give 200 dinars to my wife, as is appropriate for her'
  • ✅ Receives the 200 dinars as a gift
  • ✅ Also collects her full ketubbah
  • If amounts differ, she chooses whichever is greater

💰 Creditor

  • Debtor: 'Give 200 dinars to my creditor, as is appropriate for him'
  • ✅ Receives the 200 dinars as a gift
  • ✅ Also collects payment of the underlying debt
  • Follows Rabbi Akiva's superfluous-language rule

📋 Deathbed Bequests — Does Order Matter?

Dying person dictates gifts to multiple recipients
↓
No ordering phrase
All listed recipients stand equally; no priority order; creditor cannot prefer one over another
With 'after him' language
Priority order established; creditor may seize from later-named recipients first
↓
If creditor seizes from a later recipient, that recipient may recover from the person named immediately before him in the sequence

🔍 Rabbi Akiva — Superfluous Language Adds Meaning

רבי עקיבא דריש לישנא יתירא — Rabbi Akiva expounds superfluous language
Rabbi Akiva's Principle When a speaker uses words that were not necessary, those words must be coming to add something new. A statement that could have been omitted without changing the basic meaning must convey an additional legal entitlement.

Example from the mishna (64a): The seller who says 'apart from the pit and cistern' was not required to say this — so by saying it, he was adding to his rights, namely that he need not buy a path through the buyer's domain to access them.
