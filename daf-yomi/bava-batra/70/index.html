🌳 Bent-Back Trees & Exception Clauses in Field Sales

Shmuel & Karna
A tree bent back by the ox yoke so it does not impede plowing is NOT retained by the seller. It is not a prominent 'sign' and therefore not significant enough to be excluded from the sale automatically.
Rav Aḥa bar Huna
Dilemma: if a seller says 'I sell you this field except for such-and-such carob tree' — does the buyer acquire the other carob trees in the field, or not?
Rav Sheshet
The buyer DOES acquire the other carob trees. By naming only one exception, the seller implicitly includes everything else. Proof: 'except for the adjacent field X' means only that field is withheld — the rest transfers.
Rav Aḥa bar Huna
Objection from a baraita: 'except for such carob — buyer does not acquire it.' Does that mean he loses the other carobs too?
Rav Sheshet
No — 'does not acquire' refers only to that one named carob. An exception isolates exactly what is named and nothing more.
Principle A named exception in a sale transaction isolates only what is explicitly mentioned — it does not expand to undermine the buyer's acquisition of everything else.

📋 Two Versions of the Carob Exception Dispute

First Version: Whole Tree Excepted

  • Seller: 'I sell you the field except for this carob tree'
  • Question: Do the other carob trees transfer to buyer?
  • Rav Sheshet: Yes — named exception implies all else is included
  • Baraita proof: 'except for field X adjacent' → only that field excluded
  • Rule: Exception proves inclusion of everything else

Second Version: Half-Tree Excepted

  • Seller: 'I sell you the field except for half of this carob'
  • Question: Does buyer acquire the other half of that same carob?
  • Rav Sheshet: No — buyer does not even acquire the remaining half
  • Baraita proof: 'except for half of field X' → other half also excluded
  • Rule: Partial exception over one item excludes that entire item

📜 Deposit Document: Can the Bailee Claim He Returned the Items?

Depositor receives signed document
Witnesses attest: 'These items were deposited with [bailee]'
↓
Bailee later claims: 'I returned the items to you already'
Document is still in depositor's possession
↓
Rav Amram's Argument
If items were returned, why does the depositor still hold the document? Its presence disproves the return claim.
Rav Ḥisda's Response
Even a bailee claiming loss by oness must take an oath — so a return claim is no weaker. The document alone does not disprove return.
↓
Rav Huna bar Avin (sent ruling):
Bailee is NOT deemed credible — document in depositor's hand is decisive proof of non-return

⚖️ Rava's Ruling: Collecting on a Purse Document from Orphans

Situation: Claimant presents a purse document (כִּיס) against orphans, alleging their father owed him money from a joint business venture
↓
Sages of Neharde'a establish: Joint venture money is split — half treated as a loan, half as a pikadon (investment deposit)
↓
Loan half: The claimant takes a formal oath that this money was never returned to him by the father
↓
After the oath: Claimant collects the loan half from the orphans, just as he could have demanded it from the father himself
↓
Investment half: Cannot be collected from orphans — it is uncertain whether the venture produced profit, loss, or return of principal
↓
Rava's ruling: Claimant swears and collects half the sum recorded in the purse document — the debt stands for the loan portion despite the father's alleged return-claim
