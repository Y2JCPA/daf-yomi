⚖️ What Does Meḥitza Mean?

View A
Meḥitza means division — a share of the courtyard allocated to each party. Therefore hezek re'iya (damage by sight) must be the compelling reason to build a wall.
View B
Meḥitza means partition — a physical dividing wall. The mishna simply describes the case where both parties mutually wish to erect one.
Objection to B
If meḥitza means partition, the tanna should have written 'they build it' — the wall and the partition are one and the same thing!
Objection to A
If meḥitza means division and hezek re'iya is real damage, why must both parties wish to build? Even one party should be able to compel the other!
Resolution Even granting that hezek re'iya is real damage, a verbal agreement to divide without an act of acquisition (kinyan) cannot compel either party to build — both must genuinely consent.

🔁 The Meḥitza Logic Chain

Mishna: If both wish, they build a wall between their courtyards
↓
Meḥitza = Division
Hezek re'iya is real damage → one party should compel building even alone
Meḥitza = Partition
Mishna describes a mutual, voluntary case — no implication about compulsion
↓
Both views face fatal objections. Final ruling: verbal agreement is insufficient — a formal kinyan is required to bind the parties

🧱 Four Wall Materials — Thickness by Custom

Gevil

  • Uncut, non-planed stones
  • Total thickness: 6 handbreadths
  • Each party contributes 3 handbreadths
  • Rough protruding edges add 1 extra handbreadth vs. gazit
  • Definition known by tradition

Gazit

  • Chiseled, smoothed stones
  • Total thickness: 5 handbreadths
  • Each party contributes 2½ handbreadths
  • Smooth surface reduces bulk
  • Used in fine construction (e.g., Temple)

Kefisin

  • Small bricks (half the size of leveinim)
  • Total thickness: 4 handbreadths
  • Each party: 1½ bricks + ½ plaster
  • Two rows with 1-handbreadth fill between
  • Definition known by tradition

Leveinim

  • Large bricks (full-size)
  • Total thickness: 3 handbreadths
  • Each party contributes 1½ handbreadths
  • Twice the width of kefisin
  • Abaye: gap between rows always 1 handbreadth

🏯 Why No Stone Wall in the Second Temple?

Background
The First Temple had an amah teraksin — a 1-cubit-thick stone wall separating the Kodesh HaKodashim from the Heikhal. The Second Temple substituted two curtains with a gap between them.
Question
Why didn't the Second Temple builders construct a proper amah teraksin stone wall as in the First Temple?
Answer
The Second Temple was taller than the First — as Haggai 2:9 says: 'The glory of this latter house shall be greater than that of the former.' A stone wall would need to reach the ceiling.
Abaye
Tradition teaches: the dividing partition must span floor to ceiling. Given the Second Temple's greater height, a full stone wall was structurally impractical — hence two curtains with a handbreadth of space between.
Rav vs. Shmuel on Haggai 2:9 One holds 'greater' means greater height; the other holds it means greater longevity (years of standing). The Gemara concludes: both are true.

🕍 Rav Ḥisda's Rule: Demolishing a Synagogue

Core Rule (Rav Ḥisda): One may not demolish a synagogue until a replacement synagogue is already built
↓
Reason A — Negligence: Once demolished, the builder may procrastinate and never complete the new structure
↓
Reason B — Sanctity: A synagogue must never be left in a ruined state, even temporarily
↓
Practical difference: If another synagogue exists in the city — Reason A permits demolition (alternative prayer available); Reason B still forbids it (this building's own sanctity)
↓
Ravina asks: What if funds are collected and ready? — Rav Ashi: still forbidden
↓
Ravina asks: What if bricks, beams, and boards are all prepared on-site? — Rav Ashi: still forbidden until the new building is actually standing
↓
Exception: If cracks are visible in the old synagogue, one may demolish first — danger to life overrides the rule

👑 Herod, Bava ben Buta, and the Temple Rebuilt

הֵרוֹדֶס עַבְדָּא דְּבֵית חַשְׁמוֹנָאֵי הֲוָה — נָתַן עֵינָיו בְּחַד אַמְּתָא
Aggadic Narrative Herod was a slave in the Hasmonean household who coveted power. Hearing a Divine Voice proclaim that 'any slave who rebels now will succeed,' he rose up and massacred the Hasmoneans and all the Sages of Israel — save one: Bava ben Buta, whom he blinded.

Herod later disguised himself and approached Bava ben Buta to test him. Bava ben Buta gently rebuked 'Herod' for destroying the Sages of Israel. When Herod revealed himself and asked how to atone, Bava ben Buta replied:

'You extinguished the light of the world [the Sages, who are called a light]. Go and attend to the light of the world [the Temple, which is called a light].'

Halakhic tension: But doesn't Rav Ḥisda forbid demolishing before rebuilding? The Temple had visible structural deterioration — or Herod's immediate intent to rebuild distinguished this case.
