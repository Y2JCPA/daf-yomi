🫙 Storekeeper & the Jug: Liability Explained

Rabba
The storekeeper is liable because he took the jug in his hands. This is like a finder who causes animals to stray further — he worsened the situation and is therefore responsible.
Rava
Rabba's rule fits animals — the finder makes them stray farther. But a jug is not worsened by being taken. That reasoning cannot apply here. I and the 'lion of the group' — Rabbi Zeira — explained it differently.
R. Zeira (via Rava)
The storekeeper took the jug for his own purposes, not for the child's benefit. This makes him a borrower without the owner's knowledge — and such a borrower bears full liability from the moment he takes the item.
Rabbi Yehuda
Once the storekeeper returns the jug to the child, his liability ends — he is treated like a regular borrower who has completed use and returned the item. The Rabbis disagree: liability continues until the child safely arrives home.
Core Dispute Rabbis vs. R. Yehuda: Does handing back the jug end the storekeeper's liability, or does it persist until the child (the original owner's agent) reaches home safely?

מידות שקר — ויקרא י״ט:ל״ה–ל״ו
⚠️ Rabbi Levi — The Gravity of False Measures The punishment for using false measures is more severe than for forbidden sexual relations.

• Forbidden relations: Torah says 'ha-elleh' (these) — a shortened form
• False measures: Torah says 'kol ha-osseh elleh' (anyone who does these things) — the full severe language

Moreover: one who transgresses forbidden relations can repent and seek forgiveness. But the false measurer cannot — he does not know whom he cheated, and cannot make restitution to the unknown victims.

🏛️ Rabbi Levi's Three Aggadic Teachings

False Measures vs. Forbidden Relations

  • False measures: 'kol ha-osseh' — fullest condemnation
  • Forbidden relations: shorter 'ha-elleh'
  • False measurer cannot identify victims to repent
  • Violator of relations can seek atonement and be forgiven

Robbing Ordinary Person vs. Most High

  • Robbing a person: verse places 'sin' before 'me'ila'
  • Robbing consecrated property: 'me'ila' appears first
  • Word order in Torah signals severity
  • Robbing an ordinary person is considered graver than robbing the Most High

God's Attribute vs. Human Attribute

  • God blessed Israel: 22 letters (aleph of 'And I will walk' → mem of 'Shalom')
  • God cursed Israel: only 8 letters (vav of 've'im' → mem of 'nafsham')
  • Moses (flesh & blood): blessed with 8 letters, cursed with 22
  • Lesson: God's way magnifies blessing; humans tend toward curse

🛒 Kinyan Hagbaha — Acquisition by Lifting: Cases

Shmuel's Principle: One who takes a vessel from a craftsman to examine it and it breaks is liable. Taking an item to examine = accepting responsibility.
↓
Case 1 — Thigh of Meat (Rav Yeimar): Buyer lifted a thigh at the butcher's shop. A horseman seized it. Ruling: Buyer is liable to pay — lifting constituted acquisition before the seizure.
↓
Case 2 — Pumpkins at Pum Nahara: Townspeople lifted pumpkins to buy; seller grew angry and declared: 'May they be consecrated to Heaven!' Rav Kahana: Declaration has no effect — the buyers had already acquired by lifting; one cannot consecrate what is no longer his.
↓
Case 3 — Vegetables from Am Ha'aretz: Merely inspecting vegetables does NOT yet obligate in tithes. Only when the buyer determines to purchase with an act of acquisition does the tithe obligation attach.
↓
Rav Hoshaya's Clarification: The tanna speaks of a buyer who paid first, then lifted. Both acts converge — determination alone, without an act, is insufficient. Payment + lifting together seal the acquisition.
