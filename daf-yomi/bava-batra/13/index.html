💰 The 'You Price or I Price' Dispute

Rav Yehuda
When co-owners cannot divide an asset, one may demand: Either you appraise and buy me out, or I will appraise and buy you out. This forced buyout applies to slaves, animals, and other indivisible inherited property.
Rav Naḥman
I disagree — there is no such halakha of 'you price or I price.' Then what do we do when a firstborn and ordinary brother inherit a slave and a non-kosher animal that cannot be physically split?
Ameimar
The halakha follows Rav Yehuda. Rav Naḥman's objection is distinguishable: the slave/animal case involved both brothers each wanting both items — that was a mutual trade, not a forced buyout.
Final Ruling Ameimar: the halakha follows Rav Yehuda — the forced 'you price or I price' buyout mechanism is valid for indivisible co-owned property.

🌊 Dividing a Field with Water on Both Sides

A field with a water channel on one side and a river on the other must be split between two brothers
↓
A straight split leaves one brother with only river access and the other with only channel access — unfair
↓
Divide diagonally (bekarna zol) — each brother receives a triangular portion touching both the river and the water channel

⛓️ The Half-Slave Half-Freeman

Mishna (Gittin 41a)
Original ruling: a slave whose one master emancipated his half serves his master one day and works for himself one day. Both halves are honored.
Beit Hillel
This arrangement wrongs him — he cannot marry a maidservant (he is half-free) nor a free woman (he is half-slave). He is permanently trapped.
Revised Ruling
The court forces the master to fully emancipate him. The slave writes a bond to repay half his value. He can say 'I will pay,' but cannot say 'you buy me' — his Jewish side prohibits self-sale into slavery.
Gemara's Key Distinction The slave can initiate the 'I will price' side (pay for freedom), but not the 'you price' side (offer himself for sale), because Jewish law forbids a Jew from selling himself as a Canaanite slave.

🏛️ Inherited Property: Rich vs. Poor Brother

Poor Brother's Claim

  • Cannot personally benefit from the bathhouse or olive press
  • Wants: 'Buy my share — give me cash instead'
  • Claims the 'you price or I price' rule should apply
  • But: he is the one seeking a sale; the rich brother has no obligation to buy

Rich Brother's Defense

  • Father built the facilities for profit, not personal use
  • Can say: 'Go hire servants — they will bathe in the bathhouse'
  • Can say: 'Bring your olives and press them here for sale'
  • Poor brother has full commercial access — the 'you price' rule does not apply here

📖 May Torah, Prophets & Writings Be Combined?

תּוֹרָה, נְבִיאִים, וּכְתוּבִים — Torah, Prophets, and Writings
Tannaitic Dispute Rabbi Meir: A person may attach the Torah, Prophets, and Writings together as one scroll — a full Tanakh in a single volume.

Rabbi Yehuda: Torah should be its own scroll; Prophets their own; Writings their own. Each section deserves distinct honor.

Precedent: Baitos ben Zunin combined eight Prophets books with Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya's approval — but this was within one section only.

Three-line rule for Minor Prophets: If each of the Twelve were fully separate, a short book could be lost between scrolls — so the three-line gap keeps them together while marking boundaries.
