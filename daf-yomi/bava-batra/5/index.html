⚖️ When Is a Borrower Believed?

Reish Lakish
If a borrower claims 'I repaid the loan before the due date,' he is not believed. People do not repay debts early — they wait until the deadline so as not to trouble themselves.
Abaye & Rava
A lender does not leave his money uncollected. If the lender had not been paid, he would have demanded it. Therefore, in the appropriate circumstances a borrower's claim of repayment is credible.
✅ Final Ruling The halakha follows Abaye and Rava. Rav Pappa and Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua ruled accordingly; Mar bar Rav Ashi ruled like Reish Lakish.

🌾 Bar Metzra — The Neighbor's Preemption Right

וְעָשִׂיתָ הַיָּשָׁר וְהַטּוֹב — Do what is right and good (Devarim 6:18)
דינא דבר מצרא When Ronya purchased land adjacent to Ravina's field, Ravina considered invoking dina de-bar metzra — the Torah-based right granting a neighboring landowner priority to purchase adjoining land before an outsider acquires it. Ravina ultimately relented and did not remove Ronya, but the episode illustrates this important halakhic principle.

💰 The Credibility Dispute: Two Hazakot (Presumptions)

Reish Lakish

  • Borrower claims: 'I repaid before the due date'
  • NOT believed
  • Hazaka: People do not pay debts early
  • Borrowers wait until the last moment — early repayment is implausible
  • Reason: 'So he won't trouble me later by demanding payment'
  • Mar bar Rav Ashi ruled this way in practice

Abaye & Rava ✓

  • Counter-hazaka: 'A person does not leave money in another's possession'
  • A lender would have collected already if not paid
  • In the appropriate case, borrower's claim IS believed
  • The mishna's evidence cuts both ways — context determines credibility
  • Halakha follows this view
  • Rav Pappa and Rav Huna b. Rav Yehoshua ruled this way in practice
