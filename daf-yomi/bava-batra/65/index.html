⚖️ The Great Dispute: Does a Seller Sell Generously?

Shmuel
A seller sells generously — he does not withhold a path for himself. Brothers who divided an estate have no rights of way against each other.
Rav
A seller retains all privileges. Brothers who divided their father's estate do retain rights of way and other easements over each other's land.
Ruling Rav Naḥman (disciple of Shmuel) asked Rav Huna for the definitive ruling — Rav Huna deferred: 'The halakha is in accordance with your opinion' (i.e., Shmuel's).

🏛️ Rav Yirmeya bar Abba Reports: What Did Rav Actually Rule?

Rav Yirmeya bar Abba
Many times I stated before Rav that the halakha follows Rabbi Akiva (seller sells generously) — and Rav never protested! So Rav agrees with Shmuel.
Rav Huna
The halakha follows the Rabbis, not Rabbi Akiva. The seller does not sell generously and retains rights for himself. Rav sides with the Rabbis.
Note Shmuel explicitly says halakha follows Rabbi Akiva. Ravina observes that Rav and Shmuel each follow their own consistent lines of reasoning — the same dispute reappears in both the brothers' case and the house-sale case.

🏘️ Two Residences: Outer & Inner — Who Gets a Right of Way?

Owner transfers two residences
Outer to one person, inner to another
↓
Both by Sale
Inner resident receives right of way through outer — seller sold generously
Both by Gift
Inner resident receives right of way — giver gives generously; mishna (71a) supports this
Outer by Sale, Inner by Gift
Initial view: no right of way.
Rejected: mishna proves gift-giver always gives generously — inner gets right of way

🔑 House Sale: Included vs. Excluded by Default

✅ Sold with the House

  • Door (fixed to building)
  • Door bolt and lock (attached fixtures)
  • Mortar hollowed out of the ground
  • Immovable lower millstone (itzterobil)
  • Built-in oven and stove

❌ NOT Sold with the House

  • Key (portable personal tool)
  • Portable (free-standing) mortar
  • Upper (portable) millstone
  • Pit and cistern (always excluded)
  • Gallery/portico (unless explicitly stated)

🛁 Ritual Bath (Mikveh) & Attached Ducts

אמבטי שחקקה ולבסוף קבעה — פוסלת את המקוה
Halakhic Ruling A duct hollowed out first, then attached to the ground or building — channels water that invalidates a ritual bath.

Water drawn via an attached vessel is considered drawn water (מים שאובים), disqualifying a mikveh when added in sufficient quantity. The critical issue: attachment happened after the duct was made, so it retains vessel status.

Rabbi Eliezer on beehives: A beehive attached to the ground with clay is treated as land — documents may be written on it and it does not contract ritual impurity as a vessel.
