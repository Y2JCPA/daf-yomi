💰 Two Categories of Property in Inheritance Law

מוחזק — Muchzak

  • Property the father actually possessed during his lifetime
  • Bechor (firstborn) receives a double portion
  • Husband fully inherits from his wife
  • Example: land the father owned and farmed
  • Example: money already in the father's possession

ראוי — Ra'ui

  • Property expected but never held by the father
  • Bechor receives only a single (equal) portion
  • Husband does NOT inherit from his wife
  • Example: debt collected by orphans after father's death
  • Example: inheritance wife was due but never received

👵 The Great-Grandmother Case — Unfolding Step by Step

Dying man declares: 'All my property is given to my grandmother; after she dies, it goes to my heirs — not to her heirs.'
↓
The man dies. He has a married daughter and grandchildren.
↓
The daughter predeceases the grandmother, survived by her husband.
↓
Eventually the grandmother dies. The property is now ready to pass to 'the man's heirs.'
↓
Key Question: Does 'my heirs' mean only direct heirs (grandchildren), or also heirs of heirs — i.e., the son-in-law?
↓
Dispute erupts: Rav Huna says the son-in-law qualifies; Rav Anan says only the man's direct heirs (grandchildren) inherit.

⚖️ Rav Huna vs. Rav Anan — Defining 'My Heirs'

Rav Huna
'My heirs' means even the heirs of my heirs. The daughter's husband is heir to the daughter, who was heir to the man — therefore the husband inherits.
Rav Anan
'My heirs' means only my direct heirs. The husband does not inherit this property because his wife never possessed it — it is Ra'ui, not Muchzak.
Rav Huna
A husband inherits his wife's estate fully — including property she was due to receive. He stands in her place as her heir.
Rav Anan
A husband only inherits property his wife actually possessed (Muchzak). He does not inherit Ra'ui — property that was never in her hands.
📜 Ruling from Eretz Yisrael The halakha follows Rav Anan's conclusion (husband does not inherit) — but NOT his reasoning about the wording of 'my heirs.' The true reason is the Ra'ui principle: this was never the daughter's Muchzak property.

📜 Rabbi Elazar Explains the Eretz Yisrael Ruling

נכסי ראוי לא כנכסי מוחזק דמי — Property due is not treated like property possessed
The Proof from the Power to Sell If someone says: 'My property is given to you, and after you it goes to so-and-so' — if the first recipient sold it before dying, the sale is valid. This means the grandmother truly owned the property and could have sold it. When it then passed to 'the man's heirs,' it arrived as Ra'ui — property the daughter was merely due to receive, never in her actual possession. A husband does not inherit Ra'ui as he does Muchzak. Therefore the son-in-law has no claim.
