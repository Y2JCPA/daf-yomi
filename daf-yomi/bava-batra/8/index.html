⚖️ May Sages Be Taxed? — Three-Source Rebuttal

Rav Naḥman bar Rav Ḥisda
I have imposed the poll tax (karga) on the Sages — they must pay like all other residents.
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
Torah (Deut. 33:3): 'All His holy ones are in Your hand' — Moses told God: even kings' tax collectors have no dominion over Torah scholars!
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
Prophets (Hosea 8:10): 'They will be diminished by the burden of kings and princes' — this warns against burdening scholars with royal taxation.
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
Writings (Ezra 7:24): 'It shall not be lawful to impose tribute, impost, or toll upon them' — the Sages, like the Temple priests, are shielded by all three sections of Tanakh.
Principle Torah scholars are the city's true walls — taxing them strikes at the spiritual defense of Israel itself.

📦 Two Charity Systems: Kuppah vs. Tamḥui

Kuppah — Charity Fund

  • 💰 Money collected
  • Collected by 2 people (quasi-judicial authority)
  • Distributed by 3 people (like a court)
  • Distributed weekly on Erev Shabbat
  • For poor of the city only
  • Collectors may seize collateral, even on Shabbat

Tamḥui — Charity Platter

  • 🍞 Food collected
  • Collected by 3 people
  • Distributed by 3 people
  • Collected & distributed daily
  • For poor of the world — any visitor qualifies
  • Three needed because collection & distribution are same day

🌾 Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi Opens His Storehouses

רבי יהודה הנשיא פתח את אוצרותיו בשני בצורת — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi opened his storehouses in drought years
Aggadic Story During years of drought, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi announced: 'Masters of Bible, Mishna, Talmud, halakha, and aggada may enter — but not ignoramuses!' A man (Yonatan ben Amram) entered saying: 'Feed me as you feed a dog or a crow!' Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi fed him. Afterward, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi sat distressed: 'Woe — I gave bread to an ignoramus!' His son Rabbi Shimon revealed: 'That was your student Yonatan ben Amram, who has always refused to benefit from the honor of Torah.' Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi was comforted. Lesson: Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi held that suffering in the world comes only because of ignoramuses — yet his own student subverted this assumption through humility.
