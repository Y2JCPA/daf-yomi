Bava Batra 38 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
What is Rava's objection to the ruling that buying a tree acquires the land beneath it?
A. The seller could claim he sold only a saffron crocus — a plant uprooted and taken away — so no land occupation ever occurred
B. A buyer of trees can never acquire any land rights whatsoever
C. The sale of trees always requires a formal written deed
D. Rava fully agrees with the ruling and raises no objection
Correct: A — Rava argues the seller could say 'I sold you only a karkum (saffron crocus),' a plant the buyer uproots and removes — implying no lasting land occupation, and therefore no acquisition of the land beneath it.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to the Mishna, why does the possessor fail to establish chazakah when the owner lives in a different 'land' (e.g., owner in Judea, field in Galilee)?
A. Each of the three lands has an entirely separate legal system
B. The physical and practical difficulty of travel makes cross-regional protest impossible — treated like a period of crisis
C. Chazakah only applies to fields within the Jerusalem metropolitan area
D. The possessor must reside in the same region as the field itself
Correct: B — Rabbi Abba bar Memel explains: travel between Judea and Galilee was so difficult it was treated like a sh'at ha-shmad (crisis period), making it impossible for the owner to effectively lodge or transmit a protest.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Anan citing Shmuel, what makes a protest valid when NOT made in the possessor's direct presence?
A. The protest must be recorded in a formal legal document before a court
B. The owner must repeat the protest publicly three separate times
C. The protest must be told to two people who are able to personally relay it to the possessor
D. The protest is only valid if made within the same city as the possessor
Correct: C — Rav Anan clarifies Shmuel's position: protest-not-in-presence is valid when told to two people capable of personally conveying it to the possessor — giving him constructive notice of the owner's objection.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is Rava's final combined ruling regarding the fleeing owner (bariach) and protest-not-in-presence?
A. Can establish chazakah against a bariach; protest-not-in-presence is valid
B. Cannot establish chazakah against a bariach; protest-not-in-presence is also invalid
C. Cannot establish chazakah against a bariach; but protest-not-in-presence IS valid
D. Can establish chazakah against a bariach; protest-not-in-presence is invalid
Correct: C — Rava holds both: (1) no chazakah against a bariach — because he literally has no opportunity to protest; and (2) protest-not-in-presence IS valid — because the Gemara shows these two positions are entirely consistent.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rav Zevid, which statement constitutes a valid protest against a possessor?
A. "Ploni is a gazlan (robber)" — a general accusation of wrongdoing
B. "Ploni is a robber who stole my field" — specifically identifying the contested property
C. Any verbal expression of anger or displeasure directed at the possessor
D. A written complaint submitted to the local beit din within 30 days
Correct: B — Rav Zevid rules that a vague claim of 'robber' is insufficient because it does not identify any specific property. The owner must explicitly state 'he stole my field' — naming the contested land — for the protest to be legally effective.
