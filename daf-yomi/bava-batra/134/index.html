Beit Ḥoron Incident: Son transfers property so father can attend wedding feast despite a vow
↓
Recipient consecrates gift to Temple — blocks everyone. Friend protests.
↓
True absolute gift
Consecration takes effect — recipient had full ownership
Conditional / incomplete gift
Consecration does NOT take effect — not a valid gift
↓
Rule: A gift not absolute enough for consecration to take effect is not a valid gift at all

כָּל מַתָּנָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ שֶׁאִם הִקְדִּישָׁהּ תְּהֵא מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת — אֵינָהּ מַתָּנָה
🎁 Shammai Silenced by Yonatan ben Uzziel Setup: Yonatan ben Uzziel returned property to a deceased man's children. Shammai protested, citing the Beit Ḥoron precedent — where a 'gift' was not truly absolute.

Yonatan's counter: If the property is truly mine (a real gift), I may do with it as I wish — including returning it to the children. Either way, you cannot repossess it.

Shammai's concession: "Ben Uzziel reprimanded me; ben Uzziel reprimanded me — and I have no response."

Beit Ḥoron Rule (Mishna, Nedarim 48a): Any gift that is not so absolute that if the recipient consecrated it the consecration would take effect — is not a valid gift.

💔 Is a Husband's Claim of Divorce Credible?

Shmuel (via Rav Yehuda)
'This is my son' is credible because a husband who says 'I divorced my wife' is deemed credible — since he has the power to divorce her, his claim is trusted.
Rav Yosef
This makes the Mishna dependent on a teaching not found in the Mishna! Rather: father is credible about his son because his acknowledging a son also governs his wife's exemption from levirate marriage — both flow from one source of authority.
Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef (citing Rabbi Yoḥanan)
A husband who says 'I divorced my wife' is NOT deemed credible.
Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin (citing Rabbi Yoḥanan)
Rabbi Yoḥanan says the husband IS deemed credible — contradiction!
Resolution Retroactive claim (e.g., 'I divorced her on a specific past date') — not credible; creates retroactive complications.
Future / undated claim (e.g., 'I divorced her today' or no date) — credible, since he has the power to divorce her at any time.

✂️ Can We Divide a Statement? One Body vs. Two Bodies

Husband claims retroactively: 'I divorced my wife on [past date]'
Can we accept it for the future while rejecting it retroactively?
↓
Does the claim concern one body or two bodies?
↓
Two Separate Bodies
We divide the statement
e.g., Husband + witness against adulterer: accept re: man's guilt; reject re: wife's status
(Rava's ruling)
One Body
We do NOT divide
A single person's status cannot simultaneously be divorced-from-date-X and not divorced until now
(unresolved dilemma)
