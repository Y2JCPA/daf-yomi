QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why did Shammai initially protest Yonatan ben Uzziel's return of property to the deceased man's children?
A. He believed the property had been consecrated to the Temple and was forbidden
B. He held that because the original transfer was not a full gift, it could be legally reclaimed
C. He argued that only a court could authorize redistribution of estates
D. He thought Yonatan ben Uzziel had no standing to make rulings in Jerusalem
Correct: B — Shammai's concern was rooted in the Beit Ḥoron precedent: if incomplete gifts can be reclaimed, perhaps the original transfer to Yonatan was also recallable — and so Yonatan had no right to redistribute it. Yonatan refuted this by showing the logic cuts both ways.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to the Beit Ḥoron rule cited in the Gemara, when is a gift legally invalid?
A. When the gift was given under duress or emotional pressure
B. When the gift was not witnessed by two valid witnesses
C. When the recipient could not effectively consecrate it to the Temple — meaning full ownership was not transferred
D. When the gift was given to a relative rather than a stranger
Correct: C — The Sages derived from the Beit Ḥoron incident that a valid gift requires complete transfer of ownership. If the recipient's attempt to consecrate it would not take legal effect (because the giver retained control), the gift itself was never valid.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Among Hillel's 80 students, who was considered the 'least' — yet still mastered the full breadth of Torah, astronomy, parables, and more?
A. Yonatan ben Uzziel
B. Rabbi Akiva
C. Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai
D. Shammai
Correct: C — The Gemara identifies Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai as the least of Hillel's students, yet proceeds to list an astonishing array of knowledge he possessed. The point: 'if the least was this great, the greatest was all the more so.'
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does the Gemara resolve the apparent contradiction between Rabbi Yoḥanan's two rulings on whether a husband's divorce claim is credible?
A. One ruling was retracted and the other stands as the final law
B. Retroactive claims (specifying a past date) are not credible; future or undated claims are credible
C. The husband is credible only if two witnesses corroborate the divorce
D. Rabbi Yoḥanan only trusted a husband's claim when made in front of a rabbinic court
Correct: B — The Gemara distinguishes: when the husband says he divorced her on a specific past date, this is retroactive testimony — not credible. When he says he divorced her today or gives no date, he is deemed credible because it is within his power to divorce her at any time.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
The Gemara discusses 'dividing a statement' — accepting one part while rejecting another. When is this permitted?
A. Always, as long as both parts of the statement are independently verifiable
B. Only when the statement involves monetary claims, not personal status
C. When the statement concerns two separate persons (two bodies), but not when it concerns one person's single legal status
D. Only when the speaker is a woman testifying about her own status
Correct: C — Rava's principle: for two separate legal subjects (two bodies), we can divide — accepting one part against one person and rejecting another part regarding someone else. But for a single person's unified legal status (one body), we cannot split the claim.
