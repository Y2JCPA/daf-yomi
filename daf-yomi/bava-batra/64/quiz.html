Bava Batra 64 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Mishna, if a seller writes in the bill of sale that he is selling 'the depth and the height of the house,' what happens to the pit and cistern?
A. They are included — the 'depth' language explicitly covers them
B. They are included only if the buyer paid extra
C. They are still NOT included — they are not part of the house structure
D. They are included only if the seller fails to object within 30 days
Correct: C — The Mishna rules explicitly that even the 'depth and height' clause does not include the pit and cistern, because anything not structurally part of the house requires explicit mention to be sold.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
When the seller keeps the house but sells the pit to another, what does Rabbi Akiva rule about the buyer's need for a path?
A. The buyer must purchase a path through the seller's domain
B. The buyer need not purchase a path — access is implied by the sale
C. The buyer and seller share a path by default
D. The buyer must petition the court for an easement
Correct: B — Rabbi Akiva rules that the buyer of the pit need not purchase a path. Since Rabbi Akiva holds one sells generously, the sale of the pit implies the seller gave whatever access was necessary to use it.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the Rabbis' view on how a seller sells?
A. A seller sells generously — exclusions must be stated explicitly
B. A seller sells at fair market value regardless of what is listed
C. A seller sells sparingly — inclusions must be stated explicitly
D. A seller sells whatever the local custom dictates
Correct: C — The Rabbis hold 'one who sells, sells sparingly (מוכר בעין רעה)' — whatever is not explicitly included in the sale remains with the seller, including access rights.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why did the Gemara ultimately need the latter clause of the field mishna (71a) to prove the dispute is a general principle?
A. Because the house mishna dispute could be explained by buyer intent, and the first field clause alone seemed redundant with the house case
B. Because Rabbi Akiva only ruled on agricultural disputes, not residential ones
C. Because the field mishna was authored by different Tannaim
D. Because the house mishna was later retracted by Rabbi Akiva
Correct: A — The Gemara rejected earlier proofs: the house-case dispute could be explained as following buyer intent (not general principle), and citing both house and field mishnayot together seemed redundant. Only the latter field clause — seller keeps field, sells cistern/winepress — combined with the house case establishes a truly universal rule.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What distinction did Rava Tosfa'a offer between a pit (בּוֹר) and a cistern (דּוּת) to answer Ravina's question?
A. A pit is indoors; a cistern is always outdoors
B. A pit collects only rainwater and is unlined; a cistern is plastered and holds collected water from multiple sources
C. A pit belongs to the buyer by default; a cistern to the seller
D. A pit is used for immersion (mikveh); a cistern is only for irrigation
Correct: B — Rava Tosfa'a explained that a bor (pit) is unlined and holds only rainwater, while a dut (cistern) is plastered and retains water from various sources — making it more valuable and permanent. Both terms are needed since one might assume the more substantial cistern would be included in a house sale.
