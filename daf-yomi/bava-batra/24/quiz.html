Bava Batra 24 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava, when is following the majority completely undisputed by all opinions?
A. When the item is found in the public domain
B. When there is proximity alone
C. When there is both majority (rov) and frequency together
D. When two witnesses testify to the majority
Correct: C — Rava teaches that when both rov (majority) and frequency are present simultaneously, no one disputes following the majority. The debate only applies when majority exists without frequency.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Shmuel forbid the barrel of wine found floating in the river, even near a Jewish majority town?
A. Most inhabitants of the nearest city are gentiles
B. The river currents flow from Dekira, a gentile settlement upstream
C. The barrel had no identifying Jewish markings
D. A lost barrel is always presumed to belong to a gentile
Correct: B — Shmuel forbids the wine because the river currents in that area flow from Dekira, where gentiles live. The actual upstream source overrides the majority status of the nearest downstream Jewish town.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How far must a standard (non-carob, non-sycamore) tree be distanced from a city?
A. 10 cubits
B. 25 cubits
C. 50 cubits
D. 100 cubits
Correct: B — A standard tree must be distanced 25 cubits from a city. Carob and sycamore trees require 50 cubits due to their especially large branches. Ulla gives the reason as preserving the beauty of the city.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
When it is uncertain whether the city or the tree was planted first, and the tree is cut down — does the city pay compensation to the tree owner?
A. Yes, the city always pays when ordering a tree cut
B. Yes, the city pays half the market value
C. No, the city does not pay
D. The Beit Din arbitrates a payment based on damage
Correct: C — When priority is uncertain, the tree must be cut but the owner receives no payment. The burden of proof lies with the claimant — without proof that the tree preceded the city, the city retains its money.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Rabbi Yosei son of Rabbi Ḥanina define a 'non-permanent' threshing floor (which has no fixed distance requirement)?
A. One built of wood rather than stone
B. One used only during summer months
C. One where grain is so little that no winnowing fork is used
D. One jointly owned by multiple partners
Correct: C — Rabbi Yosei son of Rabbi Ḥanina defines a non-permanent threshing floor as one where the quantity of grain is so small that a winnowing fork is not used. Without winnowing, chaff does not disperse far, so no fixed 50-cubit rule applies.
