👶 Does a Fetus Acquire Property?

Rav Naḥman
One who transfers ownership to a fetus — the fetus does not acquire it. A fetus lacks legal personhood for acquisition.
Rav Huna
But if the transfer is worded 'when she gives birth', the fetus acquires it retroactively — the condition saves the gift.
Rav Sheshet
Proof from a baraita: when a convert died and people took his property, if it turns out his wife was pregnant, they must take possession again — meaning the fetus had temporarily acquired it!
Abaye
Inheritance is different — it passes automatically without a formal act. A fetus can inherit by operation of law without having general acquisition capacity.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
One who transfers to a fetus — does not acquire. The mishna about gifts to an unborn child must be read as referring to a different mechanism entirely.
Core Rule (Rav Naḥman & Rabbi Yoḥanan) Transferring property to a fetus is ineffective — a fetus has no legal standing for voluntary acquisition (קניין). Inheritance operates differently as it comes automatically.

⚖️ Rav Sheshet's Proof & Refutations

Rav Sheshet
Baraita proof: Convert died; Jews took his property. His wife was pregnant — they had to take possession again after the miscarriage. Why re-acquire if the fetus never owned it?
Abaye
Refutation: Inheritance is different (ירושה שאני). The fetus inherited the convert's estate automatically — this proves nothing about voluntary acquisition.
Rava
Different refutation: The case is where they heard the fetus had died, then took possession, then heard it had been alive. Their first taking was premature.
Gemara
Practical difference: Abaye and Rava disagree on the case where they heard the fetus died, took possession, then discovered the fetus had been alive — valid taking or not?
Distinction Abaye: inheritance ≠ acquisition; Rava: first taking was invalid due to erroneous information about fetus's death.

👑 Firstborn Portion — Posthumous Birth Disputes

Sura Tradition

  • A son born after father's death does not reduce the firstborn's double portion
  • Reason: 'And they shall divide' — division must occur while all sons are alive
  • The posthumous son was not 'present' at the moment of inheritance division
  • Firstborn's double portion calculated without counting him

Pumbedita Tradition

  • A firstborn born after father's death does not receive double portion
  • Reason: 'The firstborn he shall recognize' — father must recognize the firstborn
  • Father cannot recognize a son born posthumously
  • Such a firstborn receives only a single portion

Both Agree

  • A one-day-old child inherits and bequeaths (Nidda 44a)
  • One-day-old does reduce the firstborn's double portion
  • Fetus: presumed to have died before mother (ולד מת תחלה)
  • Mar son of Rav Yosef citing Rava — both traditions

📜 'Sons I Will Have From You' — The Conditional Gift Case

Facts: A man told his wife, 'My property is given to the sons I will have from you' — before she conceived
↓
Older son's claim: The man's existing son (from a prior wife) came and asked: what about me? The father responded, 'You too — take as much as one of them'
↓
Legal question: Does the older son now receive an equal extra share alongside whatever share comes from the original conditional gift, or is his portion absorbed into it?
↓
Dispute: Rabbi Abbahu and Rabbi Yirmeya disagree on whether the older son receives an additional grant on top of his original inheritance or merely a portion equal to one of the new sons
↓
Resolution: Rabbi Yirmeya's opinion prevails — 'we are older [scholars]' — the older son receives a share equal to one of the sons born from the second wife, not an additional windfall
