QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Gemara's resolution of Rav Yirmeya bar Abba's position, why does a gutter pipe give its owner the right to protest construction beneath it?
A. Because all gutter pipes automatically confer acquired privilege regardless of material
B. Because the pipe is made of stone and built into the walls, allowing the owner to say 'it is not amenable to me that you build beneath it'
C. Because the field owner must always leave 4 cubits opposite the pipe opening
D. Because Rav Yirmeya holds that the field owner cannot build beneath any pipe at all
Correct: B — The Gemara resolves the difficulty by specifying that the mishna deals with a stone pipe built into the walls. In that case the pipe owner has a real stake in the construction below and can legitimately object — giving the mishna's acquired privilege ruling its meaning.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What defines an 'Egyptian ladder' according to the school of Rabbi Yannai?
A. A ladder imported from Egypt, which is considered foreign property
B. A ladder made of papyrus rather than wood
C. A small ladder with fewer than 4 rungs that is portable and easily moved
D. A ladder that leans against a wall rather than standing freestanding
Correct: C — The school of Rabbi Yannai explains that an Egyptian ladder has fewer than 4 rungs and is portable. Its impermanence means no fixed use is established, so no acquired privilege can be gained through its use.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rabbi Zeira, which window configuration allows for acquired privilege to be established?
A. Any window, regardless of size or height
B. A large window built at a height lower than 4 cubits from the ground
C. A large window built at a height higher than 4 cubits from the ground
D. Only a window explicitly designated for light, not for viewing
Correct: B — Rabbi Zeira rules that a large window below 4 cubits can establish acquired privilege. The neighbor is able to protest its construction from the outset, and if they fail to do so for 3 years, the privilege is established.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the dispute between Rav Huna and Rav Yehuda regarding a projection smaller than 1 handbreadth?
A. Rav Huna says chazakah applies; Rav Yehuda says it does not
B. Rav Huna says the courtyard owner can protest because the roof owner might look down when frightened; Rav Yehuda says the projection is too small to stand on so there is no reason to look into the courtyard
C. Rav Huna says the projection must be removed; Rav Yehuda says it may remain
D. Rav Huna says privacy is not violated; Rav Yehuda says it is always violated
Correct: B — Rav Huna holds that even a tiny projection allows the courtyard owner to protest — the roof owner might become frightened and look into the courtyard. Rav Yehuda counters that a projection too small to stand on gives no occasion to look down, so there is no real privacy threat.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does the mishna specifically state the prohibition on opening windows into a shared courtyard (חצר השותפין), when opening them into a stranger's private courtyard is obviously also forbidden?
A. Because a shared courtyard has a different legal category and the prohibition does not automatically extend to it
B. Because partners have implicitly waived their privacy rights by sharing the space
C. The mishna uses the 'lo tzricha' (it is not necessary) style — stating the obvious prohibition against a stranger's courtyard, then teaching the novel case that even one's own shared courtyard is forbidden due to partners' privacy
D. Because shared courtyards are smaller and the privacy violation is therefore more severe
Correct: C — The Gemara explains the mishna employs the literary device of 'it is not necessary to say' (לא צריכא). The obvious case is a stranger's private courtyard. The novel ruling is that even in a courtyard the person co-owns, opening windows violates the partners' privacy and is forbidden.
