💧 Three Views on the Gutter Pipe Mishna

Shmuel
The field owner may build beneath the gutter pipe — but cannot raise the building so high as to block the pipe's drainage.
Rabbi Ḥanina
He may build, but must leave 4 cubits open opposite the mouth of the gutter pipe so it can continue to function.
Rav Yirmeya bar Abba
He may build freely beneath the pipe — so why does the mishna say chazakah applies at all?
Gemara (Resolution)
The case is a stone pipe built into the walls. The pipe owner can say: 'It is not amenable to me that you build beneath it' — this is the basis for established privilege.
Key Principle A pipe permanently integrated into the structure creates a legitimate claim; portable or minor structures do not.

🪜 Egyptian vs. Regular: Acquired Privilege Test

Egyptian Ladder (סולם מצרי)

  • Fewer than 4 rungs (school of Rabbi Yannai)
  • Small and portable — easily removed
  • ❌ No acquired privilege possible
  • No need for neighbor to formally protest
  • Impermanence defeats any chazakah claim

Regular Ladder

  • 4 or more rungs — fixed, substantial
  • Permanent usage pattern established
  • ✅ Acquired privilege can be gained
  • Neighbor must protest to prevent chazakah
  • 3 years of unchallenged use = established right

Egyptian Window (חלון מצרית)

  • Too small for normal use or viewing
  • Cannot transmit meaningful light or sight
  • ❌ No acquired privilege
  • Mishna explicitly defines it (unlike ladder)
  • Gemara: mishna explains window to clarify why no chazakah

📐 Wall Projection (Ziz): The Handbreadth Rule

Owner extends a projection (זיז) from house wall over the shared or adjacent courtyard
↓
Protrudes ≥ 1 handbreadth (טפח)
✅ Chazakah possible
Courtyard owner may protest initially
If silent for 3 years → right fully established
Protrudes < 1 handbreadth
❌ No chazakah
Courtyard owner need not protest
(too small to matter — Rav Yehuda)
↓
Rav Huna disagrees: Even a tiny projection — courtyard owner can protest! The roof owner might get frightened on the projection and look down into the courtyard.
↓
Rav Yehuda responds: Too small to stand on → no reason to look down → no real privacy threat → no grounds for protest

🏘️ Windows into a Shared Courtyard (חצר השותפין)

Mishna ruling: One may not open windows into a courtyard belonging to partners — even if he co-owns it.
↓
Gemara challenge: Obviously it is forbidden into a stranger's private courtyard. Why does the mishna specify a shared courtyard?
↓
Answer (לא צריכא style): Needless to say one cannot open into another's private courtyard. The novel ruling is that even in your own shared courtyard, opening windows violates your partners' privacy rights.
↓
Baraita incident: A man opened windows into a shared courtyard and came before Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yosei — who acknowledged he had established chazakah through years of unchallenged use.
↓
Rav Naḥman's ruling: Even established chazakah has limits — foundational privacy rights in a shared courtyard are not fully extinguished by years of tolerance.
