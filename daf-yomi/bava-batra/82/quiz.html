Bava Batra 82 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why can't the buyer of two trees in another's field recite the bikkurim declaration, according to Rav Ashi?
A. He does not legally own the trees until the field owner agrees
B. It has the appearance of falsehood — he declares before God the land is his, but may not own the ground
C. The bikkurim declaration must be recited by the field's original owner
D. The trees must be within city limits to qualify for bikkurim
Correct: B — Rav Ashi says the problem is 'מחזי כשקרא' — the appearance of falsehood. The declaration implies land ownership before God, but the buyer of two trees does not own the ground beneath them.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, what determines whether tree growth belongs to the tree buyer or the field owner?
A. Whether the growth originates above the height of purchase
B. Whether the growth is thicker than the original trunk
C. Whether it 'sees the face of the sun' — is visible and aboveground
D. Whether the field owner consented to the original planting
Correct: C — Rabbi Yoḥanan rules: anything that 'sees the face of the sun' (aboveground, trunk-derived) belongs to the tree owner; anything concealed underground (root-derived) belongs to the field owner.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What does Rav Naḥman's tradition teach about a purchased palm tree, and how does Rav Pappa respond?
A. A palm's roots belong entirely to the field owner; Rav Pappa agrees this applies to all trees
B. A purchased palm has no trunk-growth rights; Rav Pappa argues this rule is palm-specific, not universal
C. A palm must be uprooted within three years; Rav Pappa says this applies only to date palms
D. A palm's date clusters belong to the field owner; Rav Pappa limits this to palms over ten cubits
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman teaches that a purchased palm 'has no trunk' — meaning no trunk-growth rights. Rav Pappa proves from the two-tree mishna that trunk rights exist for ordinary trees, so Rav Naḥman's rule must be uniquely palm-specific.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
When one buys three trees in another's field, what surrounding land area does he acquire beyond the trees themselves?
A. Four cubits measured from the outermost branches of each tree
B. The entire strip of land between the outermost trees on each side
C. Enough space for a fig gatherer and his basket to stand near each tree
D. Half the distance to the nearest fence or boundary of the field
Correct: C — Rabbi Yoḥanan rules — confirmed by a baraita — that the buyer acquires the ground under the trees, the area between them, and outside the trees enough space for a fig gatherer and his basket to stand and work near each tree.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the maximum distance between the three trees for them to count as one unit and trigger land acquisition?
A. 4 cubits — the standard boundary measurement
B. 8 cubits — double the standard boundary
C. 16 cubits — derived from vineyard spacing in Kilayim
D. 32 cubits — two vineyard-row widths combined
Correct: C — Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel derives from Mishna Kilayim 4:9 that 16 cubits is the relevant measure. A vineyard of 16×16 cubits creates a distinct, workable space — so trees more than 16 cubits apart are not considered one unit.
