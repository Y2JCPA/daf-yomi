🌳 Trees in Another's Field — Rights and Limits

Core Question How many trees must one buy in another's field to acquire land rights? What growth belongs to the buyer vs. the field owner? This daf establishes that two trees grant no land rights and limited growth rights, while three trees grant actual land ownership — with debates about exactly how much land, the palm-tree exception, and how far apart trees can be while still counting as one unit.
3 / 9

📜 Can the Buyer of Two Trees Recite the Bikkurim Declaration?

Rav Aḥa b. Rav Avya
The bikkurim passage consists of Torah verses — let the buyer simply read them without making a formal declaration of land ownership!
Rav Ashi
The problem is מחזי כשקרא — the appearance of falsehood. He stands before God and implies the land is his, but he may not own the ground at all.
Rav Mesharshiyya b. Rav Ḥiyya
This is why the bikkurim ritual requires that both the taking and the bringing of first fruits be performed by the same person — and that person must be the ground's owner.
Ruling One who buys only two trees in another's field cannot recite the bikkurim declaration — he lacks ownership of the ground, making the declaration false.

🌴 What Exactly Does Rav Naḥman's Palm Ruling Mean?

Rav Zevid's Reading

  • Rav Naḥman's rule applies to all trees
  • No tree buyer gets trunk-growth rights
  • Reason: all bought trees stand ready to be uprooted when they die
  • Since buyer cannot replant in their place, he has no ongoing trunk rights
  • Mishna must mean: seller kept the stump for himself explicitly

Rav Pappa's Objection

  • Two trees also stand ready to be uprooted!
  • Yet the mishna grants them trunk rights
  • Therefore Rav Naḥman's rule must be palm-specific
  • Palm trees are unique: they regrow from the crown, not the trunk
  • Palm trunk-growth is atypical and so excluded from buyer's rights

🏡 Three Trees: Acquiring Land and Its Scope

Buy 2 trees → No land acquired; field owner may NOT cut branches during growth; roots belong to field owner; trunk belongs to tree buyer
↓
Buy 3 trees → Land is acquired along with the trees — a landmark threshold (Rabbi Yoḥanan)
↓
How much land? The ground under the trees + area between them + outside space sufficient for a fig gatherer and his basket to stand near each tree
↓
Rabbi Elazar objects: The buyer has no path to his trees and must purchase access — so why does the outside area matter? It implies he has no path!
↓
Rabbi Zeira derives: Three trees → buyer acquires a separate parcel, so no path is included. Two trees → path IS included (no separate land parcel was granted)
↓
Abaye: Who sows the fig-gatherer area the rest of the year? The tree owner — because the field owner sowing it would cause the tree owner a loss, unlike the inner/outer garden case

📏 Maximum Distance Between Three Trees — The 16-Cubit Rule

Source / AuthorityRulingRationale
Shmuel (via Rav Yehuda, via Rav Yosef)Max distance: 16 cubits between treesDerived by analogy from vineyard spacing in Kilayim 4:9
Mishna Kilayim 4:9Vineyard planted 16×16 cubits allows other species in the intervening space16-cubit gap = a legally distinct, independently workable area
Rabbi Yehuda — incident at TzalmonOne bent branches of two rows toward each other, creating a 6-cubit clearing; sowed other species there lawfullyConfirms 16 cubits as the measure for a viable separate space
Practical implicationTrees more than 16 cubits apart are not one unit — no land acquisition is triggeredEach tree would be treated as a separate purchase without ground rights
Rav Yosef's caveatHe did not recall this precise tradition — the Tzalmon incident served as the practical precedentShows the weight of local incidents in halakhic determination
