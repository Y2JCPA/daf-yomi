⚖️ Two Great Disputes on Daf 170

Rabbi Meir
The signatory witnesses on the document effect the transaction. Witnesses at delivery are unnecessary.
Rabbi Elazar
The delivery witnesses effect the transaction. Signatory witnesses are not strictly required.
Rabbi Yehuda
For partial repayment: exchange the note for a new one stating the balance; tear up the first.
Rabbi Yosei
The creditor may keep the original note and give the debtor a receipt for the partial payment.
Rav's Ruling Neither Rabbi Yehuda nor Rabbi Yosei! The court itself tears up the original note and writes a new one for the remaining balance.

📜 Abaye Challenges Rav Dimi

Rav Dimi
Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds that letters are not acquired by transferring possession of a bill of sale.
Abaye
That contradicts Rabba's explanation of the same baraita! The baraita (168b) can only be read Rabba's way — so your interpretation must be wrong.
Abaye
Rather, the baraita deals with a case where one witness was found to be a relative, invalidating his testimony entirely — that is the dispute.
Rabbi Avina
A modification: if the court formally investigated and certified the witnesses' signatures, all agree the document is valid despite one bad witness.
Underlying Machlokes Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi follows Rabbi Elazar (delivery witnesses effect transaction); Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel follows Rabbi Meir (signatory witnesses effect transaction).

⚖️ Rabbi Meir vs. Rabbi Elazar: What Effects a Document?

Rabbi Meir

  • Signatory witnesses effect the transaction
  • Witnesses must sign the document for it to be valid
  • If no valid signatories → document is invalid
  • Related witness disqualifies the entire document
  • Supported by: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel

Rabbi Elazar

  • Delivery witnesses effect the transaction
  • Signatures serve only as evidence, not legal act
  • Document can still be valid without signatories
  • Concedes: document 'inherently falsified' is invalid
  • Supported by: Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi

🏛️ Partial Debt Repayment — Which Procedure?

Debtor repays part of his debt; balance remains
↓
Rabbi Yehuda
Creditor tears old note and writes a new one for the remaining balance only
Rabbi Yosei
Creditor keeps original note and gives debtor a receipt (shover) for the partial payment
Rav (Halakha)
Court tears old note and writes a new one — neither party alone handles the document
↓
Why not R. Yehuda? Creditor might forge a replacement note for more. Why not R. Yosei? Debtor may lose receipt and be double-charged.

🔍 The Case of Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef vs. Rabbi Abba

אמר לו רב יצחק נפחא: אנא נמי הכי קאמינא — לאסהודי בעלמא קאמינא
Practical Halakha in Action Facts: Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef was owed money by Rabbi Abba. Rabbi Abba claimed: "I already repaid you in the presence of so-and-so and so-and-so."

Problem: Those witnesses denied it. Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa ruled citing Rabbi Abba's own teaching — if a debtor names witnesses and they deny it, he loses.

Clarification: Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa softened: this does not mean automatic loss — only that Rabbi Abba is required to verify his claim if he is able to do so. He is not penalized for inability to produce proof.
