⚖️ Who Can Establish Presumptive Ownership?

Baraita
A craftsman is credible when claiming he was not paid — because he could have said 'it is purchased,' yet did not.
Gemara Rejects
No — the baraita speaks where the owner never saw his item in the craftsman's possession. The 'migu' argument doesn't prove general credibility.
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
Shmuel's paraphrase of the mishna: 'A craftsman cannot establish chazaka on items in his care.' This proves Rabba's ruling is wrong — craftsmen are never trusted for ownership.
Rav Naḥman
A sharecropper who installs OTHER sharecroppers can establish chazaka — since an owner would not silently allow strangers to work his field.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
But a sharecropper who merely divides the field among sub-sharecroppers cannot — the owner may have simply appointed him as an administrator!
Core Principle Chazaka (presumptive ownership) requires occupying land in a way inconsistent with a tenant relationship. Craftsmen and sharecroppers face elevated scrutiny.

🪡 Switched Utensils at the Craftsman's Shop

Customer receives wrong utensils from craftsman's shop by mistake
↓
May the customer use these utensils?
Craftsman himself gave them
Permitted — we say: the real owner likely asked the craftsman to sell them. The craftsman may have done so lawfully.
Craftsman's wife/children gave them
Forbidden — we cannot apply the same reasoning. The 'sell for me' logic only works when the craftsman directly handled the transaction.
↓
Rav Ḥiyya (Rav's uncle) gave Rav this ruling personally. Rav Ḥiyya son of Rav Naḥman added the wife/children distinction.

🎭 The Pumbedita Swindlers — Abaye vs. Rava

Abaye
Come, I'll show you what swindlers in Pumbedita do! An owner says to a craftsman: 'Give back my sarbelai (cloak).' The craftsman replies: 'I have no cloak of yours.' But the owner sees his cloak there and says: 'That's mine!'
Craftsman
This cloak was given to me by a different customer — it is not yours.
Rava
The craftsman's claim is valid! Per the baraita: an owner can only reclaim an item when he and witnesses previously saw it in the craftsman's possession. Mere visual similarity is insufficient.
Rav Ashi
If the owner is clever, he can force the issue: 'Why are you holding it? Because I owe you money!' — this admission converts it into a case of acknowledged possession, and the owner can reclaim it.
Lesson A cloak can only be reclaimed through prior witnessed identification — but a craftsman's own admission of a lien transforms the legal picture entirely.

🌾 Sharecropper Chazaka — Two Rulings Compared

Rav Naḥman

  • Sharecropper who installs other sharecroppers → CAN establish chazaka
  • Reason: An owner would not silently watch strangers occupy his field without objecting
  • Three years of sub-sharecroppers = strong proof of ownership claim
  • Silence of the owner is significant evidence

Rabbi Yoḥanan

  • Sharecropper who divides land among sub-sharecroppers → CANNOT establish chazaka
  • Reason: Owner may have merely appointed him as an administrator (apotropos)
  • Administrative division ≠ ownership behavior
  • Ambiguity destroys the chazaka claim

Testimony Bias Question

  • Can a sharecropper testify about a field he works?
  • Rav Yosef: Depends on whether produce is currently on the land
  • If produce present → biased (fears losing his share)
  • If no produce → may testify without conflict

מִגּוֹ דְּאִי בָּעֵי אָמַר לְקוּחָה הִיא בְּיָדִי — Since if he wished he could have said: It is purchased in my possession (Bava Batra 46a)
⚖️ Craftsman's Migu — The Core Legal Argument Rabba argued: since the craftsman could have falsely claimed 'I purchased it' (a more audacious lie), we believe his lesser claim that he was not paid — this is the legal principle of migu (since he could have said). The Gemara challenges whether this migu applies when the owner never witnessed the craftsman holding the item, since without such prior identification the stronger claim was never truly available.
