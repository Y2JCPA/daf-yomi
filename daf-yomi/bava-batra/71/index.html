🌾 What Transfers with a Field?

Sale

  • ❌ Cistern not included
  • ❌ Winepress not included
  • ❌ Dovecote not included
  • Even if seller says 'and everything in it'
  • Seller must buy path to retained items (R. Akiva)

  • ✅ Cistern included
  • ✅ Winepress included
  • ✅ Dovecote included

📜 Mishna: What's Excluded from a Field Sale?

Excluded — Even If Said 'Everything In It'

  • 🚰 Cistern (בור)
  • 🍷 Winepress (גת)
  • 🕊️ Dovecote (שובך)
  • These are valuable fixed installations
  • Seller presumed to retain them unless explicit

Included in a Gift of the Same Field

  • 🚰 Cistern transfers
  • 🍷 Winepress transfers
  • 🕊️ Dovecote transfers
  • Giver presumed generous — no reservations
  • Rule: one who gives a gift gives all of it

🛤️ Rabbi Akiva: The Path Problem

ורבי עקיבא מודה כשאמר לו חוץ מאלה שאינו צריך לקנות לו דרך
Rabbi Akiva's Ruling When a seller retains a cistern or winepress within a field he sold, he must purchase a path from the buyer to access his retained items — because he failed to reserve access rights in the sale document.

Exception: If the bill of sale explicitly states the field is sold 'apart from these things' (חוץ מאלה), the seller does not need to buy a path — the explicit reservation implies that access rights were preserved as well.

🎁 Why Does a Gift Differ from a Sale?

Gemara Q
In what way is a sale different from a gift? In NEITHER case did the seller or giver explicitly specify what they were retaining — so why do different rules apply?
Yehuda ben Nekosa
In a sale the seller specified the retained items; in a gift the giver did not specify — implying he gave everything.
Challenge
But this one did not specify and that one did not specify! The seller never explicitly wrote 'I am keeping the cistern' in the sale document either. Your answer doesn't hold.
Rav Ashi
The mishna itself teaches the answer: one who sells acts with a narrow eye — he wants value for each item. One who gives a gift gives with a generous eye (בעין יפה) — he is presumed to give everything without reservation.
Key Principle הנותן בעין יפה נותן — One who gives a gift gives with a generous eye

🪣 The 120 Barrels Case

Facts: A man instructed others: 'Give so-and-so my house containing 100 barrels as a gift.' His actual house contained 120 barrels.
↓
Mar Zutra: The owner specified '100 barrels' — implying he is giving only what he described. The extra 20 barrels are retained by the giver's estate.
↓
Rav Ashi objects: The mishna rules that one who gives a gift gives all of it — a giver gives with a generous eye. The full 120 barrels should transfer to the recipient.
↓
Conclusion: Rav Ashi's derivation from the mishna prevails — the generous presumption in gift-giving means all contents transfer, consistent with the broader rule distinguishing gifts from sales.

🌳 Rav Huna: Do Retained Trees Include the Land?

Baseline Rule: If one buys two trees in another's field, he does not acquire any land — he acquires only the trees themselves
↓
Rav Huna's Extension: When a seller retains trees within a field he sold, he retains the land beneath and around those trees as well
↓
Why Necessary?
Without retained land, buyer could say: 'Uproot your trees and go!' — trees damage buyer's soil by drawing water and nutrients. Seller must retain land so trees are nurtured from his own soil.
Rabbi Shimon's Limit
One who consecrates a field does NOT retain land under excluded trees. Rav Huna's rule follows only the Rabbinic opinion — Rabbi Shimon disagrees.
