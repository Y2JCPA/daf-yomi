QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava's resolution of his own dilemma, must a person on his deathbed formally say 'You are my witnesses' for his monetary admission to be legally binding?
A. Yes — formal witness designation is always required for monetary admissions
B. No — a person does not jest at the time of death, so his statements carry full legal weight without formal declaration
C. Only if the amount exceeds 200 dinars
D. Only if there are already two witnesses physically present in the room
Correct: B — Rava resolved his own dilemma: since אין אדם מהתל בשעת מיתתו (a person does not jest at the moment of death), deathbed statements are treated as if written in a document and transmitted — no formal witness designation is needed.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why did Ben Nannas argue that a voluntary marketplace guarantor cannot have his previously sold property seized by the creditor?
A. Because guarantors can never be held liable for the full debt amount
B. Because the original loan predated the guarantee, voiding any lien
C. Because he acted from compassion to save the debtor, not to formally mortgage his property — he relied on the debtor to repay him
D. Because the creditor had already received partial payment from the debtor
Correct: C — Ben Nannas compared the guarantor to someone who saves a person being strangled in the marketplace — his intervention was spontaneous and compassionate, not a formal lien-creating commitment. He expected the debtor to repay him, so only unsold property is subject to collection.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Ulla's position on the Torah law basis for oral loan collection from sold property?
A. Torah law always prohibits collection from sold property for all loan types
B. By Torah law both promissory note and oral loan creditors could collect from sold property; oral loans were restricted by the Rabbis to protect buyers
C. Torah law only permits collection from sold property for promissory notes, never oral loans
D. Torah law gives oral loan creditors stronger collection rights than promissory note creditors
Correct: B — Ulla holds that by Torah law both types of creditors could collect from sold property. The Rabbis specifically restricted oral loans to unsold property in order to protect purchasers who had no way of knowing about the unrecorded debt.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to the Mishna, which type of loan allows collection from property the debtor subsequently sold to a third party?
A. Only loans guaranteed by a third party
B. Oral loans witnessed by two people only
C. Only loans recorded in a formal promissory note (שטר)
D. All loan types equally allow collection from sold property
Correct: C — The Mishna explicitly states that one who lends by promissory note can collect from liened/sold property, while one who lends with witnesses but without a written note can collect only from unsold property — the written record creates a public lien that purchasers should have investigated.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What do Rav and Shmuel rule regarding a creditor's ability to collect an oral loan from the debtor's heirs after his death?
A. Heirs are fully liable, as the debt passes with the estate
B. Heirs are liable only if they inherited real estate (land)
C. The creditor cannot collect the oral loan from the heirs of the debtor
D. Heirs are liable only if the debt was acknowledged before two witnesses at the time of death
Correct: C — Rav and Shmuel rule that an oral loan binds only the borrower personally — the creditor cannot collect from heirs after his death, nor from purchasers of his property. Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish disagree, ruling that heirs and purchasers are also liable for oral loans.
