📜 Deathbed Admissions: Formal Witnesses Required?

Rava's Dilemma
If a person on his deathbed admits a monetary claim against him — must he formally say 'You are my witnesses,' as is usually required for a legal admission?
Rava's Resolution
A person does not jest at the time of death! Deathbed statements are treated as if written in a document and transmitted — no formal witness declaration is required.
Rabba
If the father said 'So-and-so has 100 dinars in my possession' and the orphans claim 'We repaid it after his death' — they ARE credible.
Rabba
But if the father said 'Give 100 dinars to so-and-so' and orphans say 'We already gave it' — they are NOT credible. The father's explicit directive is not easily dismissed.
Core Principle אין אדם מהתל בשעת מיתתו — A person does not jest at the moment of death

🏠 Three Types of Credit: Collection Rights

Promissory Note (שטר)

  • ✅ Collect from liened sold property
  • ✅ Collect from debtor's heirs
  • Purchasers had constructive notice
  • Written record creates public lien
  • Purchasers' negligence — no protection

Oral Loan (מלווה על פה)

  • ❌ Cannot collect from sold property
  • ⚠️ Heirs: disputed (see slide 5)
  • Purchasers had no way to know
  • Rabbis: only unsold property
  • Encourages lending, balances buyer protection

Guarantor After Signing (ערב לאחר חיתום)

  • ❌ Cannot collect from guarantor's sold property
  • Ben Nannas: spontaneous compassion, not a lien
  • No advance notice to purchasers
  • Rabbi Yishmael accepts Ben Nannas's rule
  • Only guarantor's unsold property

📖 Ulla vs. Rabba: What Does Torah Law Actually Say?

Ulla
By Torah law, BOTH promissory note and oral loan creditors can collect from sold/liened property. The Rabbis restricted oral loans to protect purchasers who had no notice of the debt.
Ulla's Logic
For promissory notes, purchasers were negligent — they could have checked. So we don't protect them. For oral loans, purchasers genuinely couldn't know — Rabbis protect them.
Rabba
By Torah law, NEITHER type of creditor can collect from sold property! A debtor's assets are not automatically mortgaged to a creditor under Torah law.
Rabba's Logic
The Rabbis expanded creditor rights to encourage lending. Promissory note creditors got collection from sold property; oral loan creditors got only unsold property.
Gemara's Resolution Rabba was explaining the view of the people of Eretz Yisrael (125a) — his own stated position was attributed incorrectly; he actually holds with Ulla's framework
