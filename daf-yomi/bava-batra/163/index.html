📜 The Invalidating Gap: Measurement & Forgery

Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
The two lines that invalidate a document are measured with their interlinear spaces — a line alone has no independent meaning.
Ulla (via Rav Ḥiyya bar Ami)
The space must fit a lamed on the upper line and a final khaf (ך) on the lower — the tallest ascending and descending Hebrew letters.
Rabbi Abbahu
Enough space to write בָּרוּךְ בֶּן לֵוִי on one line — it contains both a final khaf and a lamed, a more generous standard.
Rav
A gap between text and witnesses invalidates; a gap between witnesses and court ratification is valid — the court removes the forgery concern.
Core Rule A two-line gap between document body and witnesses' signatures invalidates — measured with interlinear spaces, by the witnesses' own handwriting.

📏 Lines With Spaces or Without?

Opening Question
When the Sages say a two-line gap invalidates a document — do they mean lines including interlinear spaces, or bare lines without spaces?
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
It must mean lines with their spaces. A bare line without space cannot be written upon — what purpose could such a measurement serve?
Rabbi Shabbtai citing Ḥizkiyya
The gap is measured by the handwriting of the witnesses, not the handwriting of the document's scribe — the witnesses set the standard.
Established Rule Lines are measured with their interlinear spaces, scaled to the witnesses' own handwriting.

🔡 How Large Must the Interlinear Space Be?

Rav Yitzḥak ben Elazar

  • Space enough to write a small letter dalet (ד)
  • A minimal height standard
  • Dalet has the smallest ascent above the baseline

Ulla

  • Space for a lamed (ל) ascending from the upper line
  • And a final khaf (ך) descending from the lower line
  • Both letters reach their maximum vertical extent
  • A moderate standard

Rabbi Abbahu

  • Enough to write בָּרוּךְ בֶּן לֵוִי on one line
  • Barukh ends with final khaf (ך)
  • Levi begins with lamed (ל)
  • The most generous standard of the three

🖊️ Documents Written Over an Erasure

שְׁטָר שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ וְעֵדָיו מְחוּקִין — כָּשֵׁר
Rav's Ruling A document in which both its content and its witnesses' signatures are written over an erasure is valid.

The court ratifies such a document not merely by recognizing the witnesses' signatures, but also by recognizing the scribe's handwriting throughout — confirming the entire document is consistent and untampered.

This resolves the excision concern: even over an erasure, the court's comprehensive review guards against fraud.
