⚖️ The Great Dispute: Shtar Amana vs. Modaa

Rav Naḥman
Witnesses who say 'our signed document was a shtar amana (trust document)' are NOT credible. And witnesses who say 'we signed under a modaa (preemptive duress declaration)' are ALSO not credible. In both cases, oral testimony cannot come and weaken a written document.
Mar bar Rav Ashi
Witnesses claiming 'shtar amana' are NOT credible — agreed. But witnesses claiming 'we signed under a modaa' ARE credible! A modaa is a formal legal instrument declared before witnesses in advance of the signing — it is qualitatively different from mere oral recanting.
Key Distinction A modaa (מודעה) is a formal preemptive declaration made before witnesses stating an upcoming act is performed under duress. Mar bar Rav Ashi holds this recognized legal instrument can defeat a later document, unlike a bare oral claim.

📋 Two Types of Post-Signing Witness Claims

Shtar Amana (שטר אמנה)

  • Witnesses signed a document 'in trust' — not a genuine transaction
  • Claim: 'the document was never meant to be enforced'
  • Rav Naḥman: NOT credible
  • Mar bar Rav Ashi: NOT credible
  • All agree — oral testimony cannot undo a signed written document

Modaa (מודעה)

  • Witnesses declare in advance: 'we will be coerced into signing'
  • Claim: 'a formal modaa was lodged before we signed'
  • Rav Naḥman: NOT credible — same rule applies
  • Mar bar Rav Ashi: CREDIBLE — modaa is a formal instrument, not speech
  • A modaa was a recognized legal mechanism, distinct from oral testimony

🏠 Can a Spouse Establish Chazaka on the Other's Property?

Spouse uses or profits from partner's property for 3 years without protest
↓
Husband on Wife's Field
NO chazaka established
He profits by right of marriage (usufruct) — his use is legally accounted for. Silence does not imply he purchased it.
Wife on Husband's Property
NO chazaka established
She lives there with marital permission — his silence reflects tolerance, not tacit admission of sale.
↓
Exception: Husband brings witnesses proving he actually purchased the field from his wife → valid documented purchase stands

אין לי עסק בנכסייך — לא אמרו אלא כשכתב לה כשהיא ארוסה
📍 Rabbi Yannai's School: Timing Is Everything The Mishna allows a husband to waive rights to his wife's property by writing 'I have no dealings with your property' (אין לי עסק בנכסייך). But this only works if written during betrothal (erusin), before marriage — when he has NO rights yet. The declaration creates a new condition from the outset. After marriage, his rights are already vested; he cannot unilaterally waive what he already possesses through a mere declaration.

🤝 Rav's Ruling: A Woman's Autonomy Over Maintenance

Default arrangement: By Torah law, a husband must provide his wife with maintenance (mezonot). In return, he is entitled to her earnings (ma'aseh yadayim).
↓
Rav's ruling (via Rav Huna): A woman may declare to her husband — 'I will not be sustained by you, and I will not work for you' — opting out of the entire standard arrangement.
↓
Why is this valid? She is waiving a right that will come to her in the future. One can waive future rights proactively. This is unlike vested rights, which cannot be unilaterally surrendered after the fact.
↓
Rava's application: This principle — that future/inheritance rights can be conditioned from the outset — connects to the broader Daf theme of when declarations and conditions are legally effective.
↓
The 'pleasing her husband' problem (Gittin 55b): A woman who sold property merely to appease her husband may later claim the sale was not genuine — marital pressure can compromise free will, and courts must scrutinize such transactions.
