🤔 The Forged Document Dilemma

Possessor holds bill of sale
Claimant says: 'That bill is forged!'
↓
Possessor admits forgery…
Claims he had a valid deed that was lost, so he used this one as a placeholder
Rabba's reasoning
Why would he lie this way? He could have claimed validity and won — his honesty lends credibility
↓
Rav Yosef objects: You rely on a forged document?
He himself admitted it is forged — it cannot establish ownership
↓
Rav Idi bar Avin — Split ruling:
Rabba wins for land • Rav Yosef wins for money debt

⚖️ Priestly Status: Can One Witness Restore What Two Took Away?

Rabbi Elazar
Once we downgraded him from priestly status based on two witnesses, we do NOT restore him on the strength of one — even if that single witness contradicts the two entirely.
Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel
One witness CAN restore his priestly status. We are concerned about contempt of court — but after he is restored, two witnesses may again downgrade him.
Rav Ashi
If the dispute is only about contempt of court, why must the case specifically involve one witness first and then another — rather than both appearing at once?
Core Tension Does allowing one witness to reverse a two-witness ruling create unacceptable 'contempt of court' — or is judicial flexibility required to protect the innocent?

🧬 Priestly Lineage Challenge — The Full Sequence

Father presumed to be a kohen (priest)
Son inherits this presumptive status
↓
Rumor emerges: son is child of a gerushah (divorcée) or ḥalutza
→ lineage would disqualify him from priesthood
↓
Two witnesses testify: he is of disqualified lineage
→ Son is downgraded from the priesthood
↓
One witness arrives and says: he IS a valid priest!
↓
Rabbi Elazar
One witness cannot restore him — two already ruled against him; no going back
RSbG
One witness can restore him — then two witnesses may re-downgrade if needed

📊 Two Parallel Cases: Land vs. Money Debt

🏡 Land (Bill of Sale)

  • Possessor admits bill of sale is forged
  • Claims he had a valid deed — since lost
  • Rabba: miggo gives him credibility
  • Rav Yosef objects: you are relying on a forged document
  • Ruling: Rabba wins — possessor retains the land

💰 Money Debt (Promissory Note)

  • Claimant admits promissory note is forged
  • Claims he had a valid note — since lost
  • Rabba again invokes miggo
  • Rav Yosef: the note is admittedly forged — it is worthless
  • Ruling: Rav Yosef wins — debtor is not obligated

📋 Rabba vs. Rav Yosef: Is Miggo Enough?

Possessor
Yes, this bill is forged — but I had a REAL deed that was lost. I only used this as a placeholder so I would not be left empty-handed. The land is genuinely mine!
Rabba
Why would he lie in this particular way? If he wished to deceive, he would simply claim the bill is valid — and he would have won! His admission of forgery signals he is telling the truth about the underlying ownership.
Rav Yosef
In the final analysis, on what are you relying? A forged document! He himself declared it forged. Miggo cannot breathe life into an admittedly counterfeit deed.
Miggo Defined מִגּוֹ — 'Since he could have said X and won, we believe him when he says Y instead.' A form of logical credibility, not documentary proof.

🤝 The Guarantor Case: Miggo Meets Its Limit

Guarantor paid the creditor on the debtor's behalf and received a receipt document in exchange
↓
Guarantor demands repayment from debtor; debtor responds: 'You already returned that receipt document to me — the debt is settled!'
↓
Guarantor admits: 'Yes, I returned the document — but you later borrowed the money again from me directly'
↓
Rav Idi bar Avin asks Abaye: Does Rabba's miggo reasoning apply here and obligate the debtor?
↓
Abaye: This follows Rav Yosef — the guarantor admitted returning the document, fatally weakening his claim. Debtor is not obligated.
↓
Exception: If guarantor says 'I returned the money back to you, not the document' — debtor IS believed that the debt was discharged
