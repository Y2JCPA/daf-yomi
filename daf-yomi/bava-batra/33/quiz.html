QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why could the creditor NOT successfully claim 'I purchased the orphans' land' to retain possession of the collateral?
A. He had not held the land for three full years of chazakah
B. The land was publicly known as orphans' property, making a purchase claim implausible
C. A written deed was required for all land transactions in Babylonia
D. Orphans could legally void any purchase of their father's land within a year
Correct: B — Abaye explains that since there is public knowledge (פרסומא) that the land belongs to orphans, no court would believe a purchase claim. Additionally, orphans cannot effectively protest to establish chazakah, so three years of silence proves nothing about the validity of a purchase.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
When the other relative admitted he was not the closer kin to the deceased, what legal consequence followed regarding the date tree's produce?
A. He owed only half the produce value since the dispute was conducted in good faith
B. He was required to return the tree but owed nothing for past produce
C. He was fully liable to reimburse all produce consumed, retroactively from the day he seized the tree
D. He was exempt because consuming produce during a genuine inheritance dispute is permitted
Correct: C — By admitting he had no right to the tree, the relative retroactively admitted he never had any right — from the very beginning of his possession. Therefore, by his own admission, he is liable to Rav Idi bar Avin for all the produce he consumed. Rav Ḥisda is surprised Rav Idi did not know this basic ruling himself.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the miggo (מיגו) argument that Rav Ḥisda applies when two parties both claim ancestral ownership of land?
A. The possessor is believed because three years of uncontested possession proves ownership
B. The possessor is credible because he could have made the stronger claim 'I purchased it' and been believed — so his weaker claim is also believed
C. Two sets of witnesses cancel each other out, leaving the possessor with the default advantage
D. Ancestral testimony always overrides a purchase claim in Talmudic property law
Correct: B — Rav Ḥisda explains: the possessor could have said 'I purchased this land' — and he would have been believed, since he has chazakah. Instead, he made a weaker, more vulnerable claim (ancestral inheritance). Because he would have been believed with the stronger claim, he is believed with this weaker one too — that is the miggo principle.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rav Zevid, when a possessor initially claims 'produce purchase' but later claims 'full land purchase,' what is the specific outcome?
A. He is believed for the full land claim since it logically includes the produce claim
B. He loses all credibility and must vacate the land immediately with no compensation
C. He is believed for the produce he consumed via miggo, but must produce a bill of sale (שטר) for the land itself
D. The original produce claim is ignored since his chazakah supersedes it
Correct: C — Rav Zevid distinguishes carefully: for the produce consumed, the original miggo still applies (he could have claimed full purchase from the start, so his produce claim is believed). But for ownership of the land itself, the court says: 'Show your bill of sale (שטר).' Miggo alone cannot substitute for the documentation required to prove land ownership.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
The case at the end of Daf 33 involves a man who snatched cast metal, with only one witness testifying against him. What is the halachic significance of having only one witness in such a case?
A. One witness is fully sufficient in monetary disputes involving tangible stolen objects
B. One witness creates an obligation for the defendant to take an oath denying the claim, but cannot establish full monetary liability alone
C. One witness is entirely disqualified in theft cases and the victim has no legal recourse
D. One witness reverses the burden of proof, forcing the accused to prove his innocence
Correct: B — In Jewish monetary law, two witnesses (עדים) are required to establish full liability. A single witness (עד אחד) cannot independently convict but does obligate the defendant to swear an oath denying the charge. This rule underlies the cast metal case brought before Rabbi Ami, where the Gemara must determine how to proceed with only one witness available.
