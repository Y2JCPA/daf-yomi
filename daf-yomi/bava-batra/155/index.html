⚖️ From When Can a Minor Sell Inherited Property?

Rav Naḥman (citing Rav)
From age 18 — once he reaches eighteen years, his sale of inherited property is valid and cannot be contested by the family.
Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana
From age 20 — only at twenty years old does a minor gain legal capacity to sell his deceased father's inherited property.
Practical Stakes The age at which an heir can legally sell inherited land determines whether buyers have secure title or face family challenges after the seller's death — with real property and livelihoods at risk.

⚔️ The Bnei Brak Incident: Contesting a Dead Seller's Sale

Family of Deceased
Our relative sold his father's property and then died. We contest the sale — he may have been a minor when he sold it.
We ask Rabbi Akiva: may we examine the corpse for signs of maturity?
Rabbi Akiva
You have no authority to dishonor the dead to advance your monetary interests.
The question of examination only arises because the sale threshold is relevant — proving the age debate is real and practical.
Why This Matters If the age threshold is 20, then at age 18–19 the sale could be void. The family wanted to examine the body to prove minority — showing how high the stakes of this dispute truly were.

📊 Minor's Legal Capacity: Three Areas Compared

Selling Inherited Land

  • Requires age 18 (Rav Naḥman) or 20 (Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana)
  • Must show physical signs of adulthood
  • Family can contest if seller was underage
  • Year of majority itself: treated as majority (Rava, inferred)

Giving Testimony

  • Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua: valid testimony broadly
  • Mar Zutra: only valid for movable property
  • Rav Ashi: if sale of movables is valid, so is testimony
  • Land testimony: more restrictive rules apply

Giving a Gift

  • Ameimar: gift is valid even if too young to sell
  • Rav Ashi challenges: sale yields money, gift does not
  • Yet Ameimar's ruling stands — gift upheld
  • Inferred from an incident, not stated explicitly

📜 The Scroll of Esther Competency Test When buyers needed to prove that a young seller (under 20) was mentally competent to conduct a sale, they instructed him to say before Rava: "The Scroll of Esther — a large scroll — sells for a dinar; why then does a small scroll sell for more?"

This demonstrated his grasp of market economics — scarcity drives up price — and validated the transaction. Rava inferred from his response that the young man understood business negotiations, confirming capacity to sell.
