⚖️ Core Dispute: 'My Property to You, and After You, to So-and-So'

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
The first recipient cannot sell the property itself. The second designee is like an heir with a claim. Only the profits (פירות) belong freely to the first recipient.
Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel
The first recipient can sell the property. The second designee only receives what remains at the first recipient's death. The sale is fully valid.
Final Halakha Rabbi Yoḥanan rules: halakha follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel — the sale is valid. Yet Abaye condemns one who acts on this as an 'עריום רשע' (cunning wicked person), for the giver's moral intent is violated.
3 / 9

🧠 Abaye & Rabbi Yoḥanan: Morality vs. Law

Abaye
Who is a 'cunning wicked person' (עריום רשע)? One who advises the first recipient to sell the property under Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's ruling. The law permits it — but the spirit of the gift is violated. Permission is not endorsement.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Halakha follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. However, if the first recipient gave it as a matanat shechiv mera (deathbed gift), the second designee still acquires — because a deathbed gift takes effect only at death, the same moment 'after you' triggers.
Key Distinction Regular sale: first recipient defeats second designee (Rabban Shimon). Deathbed gift: second designee acquires — both 'after you' and the deathbed gift trigger simultaneously at death.

🔄 Does the Second Designee Acquire Anything?

Giver declares: 'My property to you, and after you, to so-and-so'
↓
First recipient sells (normal sale)
Sale is valid (Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel = halakha).
Second designee receives nothing.
First recipient gives as deathbed gift
Second designee still acquires. Deathbed gift takes effect at death — same moment as 'after you.'
First recipient holds until death
Second designee acquires whatever property remains at death.
↓
Special cases: Canaanite slaves and shrouds — sale remains valid even if use seems improper (Rabbi Yoḥanan / Rav Yosef).

🍋 Three Etrog Gift Cases: Fulfilling the Mitzvah

Gift 'After You'

  • Rav Naḥman bar Rav Ḥisda's case
  • 'This etrog is yours; after you, to X'
  • All agree: he fulfills his Day 1 obligation
  • Dispute (R. Yehuda vs. Rabban Shimon) only applies if he then sells or destroys it
  • Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak: fulfilling mitzvah is the entire purpose of the gift

Brothers' Joint Etrog

  • Brothers buy etrog from shared estate funds
  • One takes it on Sukkot to fulfill his obligation
  • Valid only if he has the right to eat it
  • Requires each brother to receive an etrog
  • Other brothers getting quince/pomegranate is insufficient — obligation not fulfilled

Conditional Gift (על מנת להחזיר)

  • Rava's ruling
  • 'This etrog is yours on condition you return it'
  • Recipient takes it on Sukkot, then returns it
  • If returned: obligation fulfilled
  • If not returned: never truly his — obligation not fulfilled

🐂 Conditional Gift of an Ox — Rava vs. Rav Naḥman

Case (Rav Naḥman): 'This ox is given to you on condition you return it.' Recipient consecrates it, then returns it.
↓
Rav Naḥman rules: It is consecrated AND returned. The consecration takes effect because he had valid ownership, and the return satisfies the condition.
↓
Rava objects: What exactly did he return? The ox is consecrated — it belongs to the Temple. He cannot truly return it to the giver.
↓
Rav Naḥman responds: What loss did he cause? He returned the ox as promised. The condition of return was fulfilled.
↓
Rav Ashi concludes: Neither is correct as stated. The halakha requires further analysis — the return of a consecrated item is legally ambiguous.
