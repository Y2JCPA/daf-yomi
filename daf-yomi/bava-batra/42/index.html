🤝 Chazakah, Partners, and the Loan-Sale Divide

חזקה שאין עמה טענה — אינה חזקה
Core Theme Daf 42 examines two major issues: (1) the distinction between loans and sales regarding collection from liened property — sales are public, loans are discreet, creating different legal rules; and (2) who can and cannot establish chazakah (presumption of ownership) through possession — craftsmen, partners, sharecroppers, and stewards are excluded because their possession is known to be by permission.

📜 Can Witnesses Alone Prove Collection from Liened Property?

Rav
One who buys property can collect from liened property through witnesses alone — no promissory note required, because a sale is publicly known.
Gemara (Contradiction)
But the Mishna (175a) teaches: one who lends with a promissory note collects from liened property — implying without a note one cannot collect. And Rav himself said an oral loan cannot collect liened property!
Rav + Shmuel
An oral loan does not collect from liened property at all — so even if Rav is a tanna who can dispute a mishna, his own ruling seems to contradict itself.
Gemara (Resolution)
No contradiction! Loan vs. sale are fundamentally different. A loan is made discreetly so buyers have no notice; a sale is publicly known so buyers should have investigated. Different rules apply to each.
Key Distinction Loan = private → subsequent buyers unaware → promissory note required for lien collection. Sale = public → buyers had notice → witnesses suffice, and buyers who failed to investigate caused their own loss.

⚖️ Loan vs. Sale: Why Different Lien Rules Apply

💸 Loan (הלוואה)

  • Made discreetly and privately
  • Borrower hides debt to protect property value
  • Subsequent buyers have no way to know of lien
  • Oral loan: cannot collect liened property
  • Written promissory note: can collect liened property
  • Buyers who purchased are innocent — protected

🏠 Sale (מכירה)

  • Transaction is public and widely known
  • Publicity gives constructive notice to all parties
  • Subsequent buyers could and should have investigated
  • Witnesses alone suffice per Rav — no document needed
  • Buyers who failed to investigate caused their own loss
  • Seller can collect from liened property sold to third parties

💰 Shmuel on Partners: Permission, Enhancement, and Testimony

Shmuel's Rule: A partner is like one who enters a field with permission (ברשות נכנס) — therefore cannot establish chazakah over his partner's share of joint property.
↓
Apparent Contradiction: Elsewhere Shmuel implies a partner can establish chazakah. The Gemara offers two resolutions: (a) entering half gives chazakah for that half only; or (b) the field must be large enough — if too small, one partner entering the whole field shows shared use, not exclusive possession.
↓
Rav Naḥman bar Avuh's Clarification: Shmuel's primary intent was about enhancement: a partner who proactively improves joint property collects his share of the enhancement when produce 'reaches shoulders' (תוחלין למפרע) — i.e., when the crop is harvestable.
↓
Partners May Testify for Each Other: A partner may join a second witness to testify that his co-partner owns a share of the field — countering a third party who claims full ownership. Partnership does not disqualify cross-testimony here.
↓
Mishna's Exception: The rule that chazakah requires three years applies to ordinary possessors. But one who holds property as a craftsman, partner, sharecropper, or steward never establishes chazakah — no matter how many years pass — because their possession is understood to be non-adversarial.
