Bava Batra 87 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Ela, whose measuring vessel does the mishna describe when it says the sale is finalized upon filling?
A. The buyer's vessel
B. The seller's vessel
C. A middleman's vessel
D. A communal market vessel
Correct: C — Rabbi Ela resolves the Gemara's paradox by explaining the mishna refers to a middleman's vessel — loaned to the seller while empty and transferred to the buyer the moment it is filled.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What must a seller do after pouring liquid into a buyer's vessel from the measuring cup?
A. Seal the buyer's vessel immediately
B. Drip three extra drops from the measure into the buyer's vessel
C. Weigh the vessel to verify the amount
D. Return any unused liquid to the barrel
Correct: B — The mishna requires the seller to drip three extra drops (שלש טיפין) into the buyer's vessel after completing the pour, to ensure the buyer receives full measure.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
After tipping a barrel on its side to drain it, the residual liquid at the bottom belongs to:
A. The buyer, as part of the purchase
B. The priest, since it is treated like teruma
C. The seller
D. Whoever drained it
Correct: C — Ze'eiri and Rabbi Elazar ruled that the last dregs belong to the seller. Rabbi Abbahu resolves the teruma objection by explaining the Sages reinforced the teruma status there as a safeguard for the priest, not as proof of buyer ownership.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In the mishna about a father's son sent to the storekeeper with a pundeyon, where exactly do the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda disagree?
A. Whether a minor can legally make a purchase
B. Who is liable when the jug the father sent breaks
C. Whether the storekeeper must give change to a child
D. Whether oil sales require a written contract
Correct: B — The Rabbis hold the storekeeper liable for the broken jug (having accepted agency), while Rabbi Yehuda holds the father liable since he sent the jug at his own risk and the storekeeper never formally accepted it.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Yehuda exempt a storekeeper from dripping three drops on Erev Shabbat at nightfall?
A. Sales made on Erev Shabbat are only rabbinically valid
B. The storekeeper is preoccupied with Shabbat preparations
C. Three drops would constitute forbidden labor on Shabbat
D. The buyer waives this right by shopping so late
Correct: B — The baraita resolves the Gemara's dilemma by stating explicitly: 'On Erev Shabbat at nightfall a storekeeper is exempt because the storekeeper is busy (טרוד)' — confirming the exemption is due to preoccupation, not a restriction of the general rule.
