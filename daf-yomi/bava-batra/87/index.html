💧 The Three-Drop Obligation After Measuring

Seller measures out wine or oil into the buyer's vessel
↓
After completing the measure, the seller tips the measuring vessel to drain remaining drops
↓
The seller must allow three extra drops (שלש טיפין) to drip into the buyer's vessel
↓
After the three drops, the seller rights the vessel — any remaining residue belongs to the seller
↓
Exceptions: A storekeeper is always exempt; Rabbi Yehuda also exempts any seller on Erev Shabbat at nightfall due to preoccupation

⚖️ Whose Measuring Vessel Does the Mishna Mean?

Questioner
If the vessel belongs to the buyer, why does filling it finalize the sale? The liquid is still in the seller's container — the buyer hasn't drawn it into his own domain yet!
Questioner
If the vessel belongs to the seller, why does filling it finalize the sale? It's the seller's own vessel — the buyer acquires nothing by its being filled!
Rabbi Ela
The mishna refers to a middleman's vessel — loaned to the seller while empty, then transferred as a loan to the buyer the moment it is filled. Filling marks the precise transfer of ownership.
Key Resolution The middleman's vessel acts as a neutral mechanism: its filling simultaneously ends the seller's claim and begins the buyer's acquisition.

🍷 The Drained Barrel — Last Drops Belong to Whom?

Ruling: Dregs Belong to Seller

  • Ze'eiri and Rabbi Elazar ruled in Eretz Yisrael: when a barrel is tipped on its side, the residual liquid is the seller's
  • The seller only committed to give what flows freely; he retains the last stubborn drops
  • This is consistent with the mishna: seller rights the vessel after three drops and keeps the rest

Objection from Teruma (Terumot 11:8)

  • Mishna Terumot: oil or wine of teruma drained from a vessel — the dregs remain teruma and go to the priest
  • This implies the dregs still belong to the owner (buyer), not the original owner (seller)!
  • Resolution (Rabbi Abbahu): The owner despairs of these tiny dregs, so the Sages reinforced their teruma status to protect the priest's portion — a rabbinic safeguard, not proof of ownership

📅 Rabbi Yehuda: Erev Shabbat Exemption for Storekeeper

בלילי שבת עם חשיכה — חנווני פטור, מפני שהוא טרוד (ברייתא)
Halakha Mishna: A storekeeper is never obligated to drip three drops. Rabbi Yehuda adds: even a private seller is exempt on Erev Shabbat at nightfall.

The Gemara raises a dilemma: is Rabbi Yehuda adding to the Sages' exemption (storekeeper is always exempt; and on Erev Shabbat even a private seller is exempt) — or restricting it (the storekeeper is exempt only on Erev Shabbat, but not otherwise)?

Resolution from baraita: 'On Erev Shabbat at nightfall, a storekeeper is exempt because the storekeeper is busy.' This confirms Rabbi Yehuda speaks of the storekeeper specifically — proving he adds to, rather than restricts, the exemption.
