📜 Five Special Gifts & the Rule of Full Transfer

Central Rule (Rava citing Rav Naḥman)
Five transactions only take full legal effect when the owner writes a deed granting all property — reserving nothing
↓
Dying Person
Reserves any amount → heirs inherit, gift void
To Slave
Reserves any amount → slave not emancipated
To Wife
Gives all → she is only steward, not owner
To Sons
Writes any land to wife → she forfeits ketuva claim
Sheltering Woman
Gives all to another → husband cannot acquire
↓
Key Principle: Reserving movable property counts as a significant reservation for all five — except for the wife's marriage contract (ketuva)

⚖️ Is a Canaanite Slave Legally Like Land or Movable Property?

Rav Aḥa b. Rav Avya
Come and hear from the mishna (68a): a landowner who gives a slave along with land to his daughter — the slave is treated alongside land, proving slaves have land-like status!
Rav Ashi
That proves nothing. If the slave were simply movable property, why does the mishna separately mention land? The mishna had a reason to use that language — it is not proof of legal equivalence.
Ravina
Then hear from Pe'a (3:8): One who gives all his property to his slave — the slave is freed. But if the master reserves 'any amount of land' the slave is not freed. Land triggers the rule — like land!
Rav Ashi
The reason is not that the slave is like land. Rather, the master retains the slave himself within the reservation — the slave cannot be freed by a gift that the slave himself is part of.
Status The Gemara leaves the dilemma unresolved — the slave's legal classification (land vs. movable property) remains תיקו

💰 What Is Included in the Term 'My Property' (נכסי)?

Canaanite Slave (Eved Kena'ani)

  • Explicitly called 'property' (nikhsai)
  • Source: Pe'a 3:8 — 'one who writes all his property to his slave'
  • Included when one says: 'My property shall go to X'

Money (זוזי / Zuzei)

  • Explicitly called 'property'
  • Source: same Pe'a mishna — 'acquired together with property that serves as a guarantee by means of money'
  • Coins and currency are included in a general property gift

Deed (שטר / Shtar)

  • Explicitly called 'property'
  • Source: Rabba bar Yitzḥak — three types of deeds for acquiring land
  • A document/deed is itself property and passes with a general gift of 'my property'

🔑 Movable Property as a Reservation — General Rule and Exception

General Rule: For all five special gifts, reserving movable property (not only land) constitutes a legally significant reservation that defeats the transaction
↓
Dying Person: Reserves movable property → heirs inherit. Movable property reservation is fully valid.
↓
Slave Emancipation: Reserves movable property → slave is not freed. Movable property reservation is fully valid.
↓
Woman Sheltering Property: Reserves movable property → husband can acquire. Movable property reservation is fully valid.
↓
Exception — Ketuva (Marriage Contract): Reserving movable property is NOT considered significant for the purpose of the ketuva obligation
↓
Ameimar's Caveat: If specific movable property was explicitly written into the ketuva as collateral and it is still extant, then reserving that property does count as a significant reservation — and the wife's claim is affected

👰 Woman Sheltering Property from Her Prospective Husband

Problem: Upon marriage, a husband automatically acquires his wife's property under rabbinic law — how can she protect assets she wants to keep?
↓
Solution: Before the marriage, she writes a deed granting all of her property to a third party
↓
Transfers ALL property
✓ Valid transfer — husband acquires nothing upon marriage
Reserves any amount
✗ Transfer fails entirely — husband acquires the whole estate
↓
Movable Property Rule Applies: Even reserving movable property (not just land) defeats the sheltering strategy — she must give everything
