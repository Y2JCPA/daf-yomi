🕊️ Did the Cherubs Occupy Physical Space?

Shmuel
The cherubs stood miraculously and did not occupy any physical space. Each wing was 5 cubits; together 20 cubits — yet the room was only 20 cubits. There was no room for their bodies. This proves a miracle!
Abaye
Objection! Perhaps their bodies extended beneath their wings like chickens — wings raised above, bodies tucked below. Then no extra lateral space is needed, with no miracle required.
Rav Huna b. Rav Yehoshua
Further objection! Perhaps the 20 cubits describes only ground level. The room may have widened at the top, easily accommodating the wings plus the bodies of both cherubs.
Rabbi Levi / Rabbi Yoḥanan
This is a tradition handed down from our ancestors: The space occupied by the Ark and cherubs is simply not included in the Temple's measurements — miraculous non-occupation of space.
Core Principle אין ארון מן המידה — The Ark is not [counted] from the measure. Sacred objects in the Holy of Holies occupy a dimension beyond physical space.

👥 Which Direction Did the Cherubs Face?

Faces Toward Each Other

  • Source: Exodus 25:20 — "with their faces one toward the other"
  • Consistent with the Tabernacle cherubs
  • Resolution: 'Toward the House' = slightly angled toward walls while still facing each other
  • 💕 Represents Israel in God's favor — fulfilling His will

Faces Toward the House

  • Source: II Chronicles 3:13 — "their faces were toward the House"
  • Consistent with the Temple's permanent cherubs
  • Resolution: 'One toward the other' = a middle point between them
  • 😢 Represents Israel not fulfilling God's will — estrangement

Aggadic Synthesis

  • Both verses are true — at different times
  • Cherubs mirrored Israel's spiritual state
  • Facing each other → love and intimacy between God and Israel
  • Facing away → divine displeasure; like the verse: 'He has gone away' (Song of Songs)

🔐 Cistern & Garden Access Rights

מי שהיה בורו בתוך שדה חבירו — One whose cistern lies within another's field (Mishna Bava Batra 99a)
Mishna + Gemara Cistern beyond neighbor's house (99a): The cistern owner may enter only at normal times (not night). Both the homeowner and the cistern owner construct a lock on the cistern opening — so neither can shut out the other, yet neither can enter freely.

Why two locks? (Gemara, 99b): Suspicion of יחוד (yichud) — seclusion. If the cistern owner could enter at will, he might be alone in the house with the homeowner's wife when the homeowner is absent.

Garden beyond neighbor's garden (99b): Same rules apply — enter and leave only at normal times; cannot store equipment in the outer garden; cannot bring workers who will linger.

Exception: If a court assigned him a side-path with both parties' agreement, he may enter at any time and even bring merchants — because the concern for intrusion is eliminated.

🛤️ Public Thoroughfare: The Exchange & Its Limits

Situation: A public thoroughfare passes through a field owner's property, which he finds inconvenient.
↓
The Exchange: He appropriates the original path and provides the public with an alternative thoroughfare on the side of his field.
↓
Mishna Rule: The alternative thoroughfare he gave them must be at least as good as the original — he cannot substitute an inferior or circuitous path.
↓
The Question: Can he later reclaim the original path? Logically: 'Let him take a stick and sit on it — it's his land now!'
↓
Rav Zevid (Rava): Rabbinic decree prohibits reclaiming it — lest owners be incentivized to give circuitous routes that lengthen the public's travel.
↓
Rav Mesharshiyya (Rava): The original path never fully transferred — the public retains a residual legal claim preventing reclamation.
↓
Standard widths (Mishna): Private path = 4 cubits | Public thoroughfare = 16 cubits | King's highway = unlimited | Funeral path = 4 cubits | Field path = 4 cubits
