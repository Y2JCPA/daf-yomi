📜 Who Pays the Scribe's Wages?

Get (Bill of Divorce)

  • Husband pays
  • He initiates the divorce
  • Verse: 'he writes and gives her'
  • Written even without wife present

Promissory Note (הלוואה)

  • Debtor pays
  • He benefits from receiving the loan
  • Written without creditor present
  • Creditor's interests are protected

Bill of Sale (שטר מכר)

  • Purchaser pays
  • Buyer benefits from the deed
  • Written without buyer present
  • Seller needs no document

Betrothal/Marriage

  • Groom pays
  • Requires consent of both parties
  • Cannot write without bride's consent
  • Both bear mutual obligations

🤝 Asmakhta: Is a Conditional Acquisition Binding?

Rabbi Yehuda
The asmakhta does not effect acquisition. When the debtor said 'if I don't repay, the note is yours,' he did not give this with full, unconditional intent — it was merely a penalty clause.
Rabbi Yosei
An asmakhta does effect acquisition! The debtor willingly undertook a penalty, and deposited the note with a trustee as a concrete sign of serious intent — this is binding.
Rav Naḥman (citing Rav)
The halakha follows Rabbi Yosei. When Rabbi Ami was asked, he also affirmed this — since Rav rules this way, so we rule.
Gemara Conclusion
Final ruling: The halakha does not follow Rabbi Yosei. An asmakhta is not binding — valid acquisition demands full, unconditional intent.
The Case Debtor repaid part of his debt and deposited the note with a trustee. He declared: 'If I don't repay the remainder by date X, the full note is valid against me.' Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Yehuda dispute whether this condition takes effect.

🏕️ Abaye and the Arab Robbers of Pumbedita

Crisis: A band of Arabs came to Pumbedita, robbing people's land and forcing victims to surrender their property deeds to prevent future legal claims.
↓
Request: The displaced landowners came to Abaye asking him to write duplicate ownership documents so they could reassert their claims.
↓
Abaye's refusal: Rav Safra rules one may not write two deeds for one field — the owner could repossess the same property twice, defrauding future purchasers.
↓
Clever solution: After persistent badgering, Abaye told his scribe: 'Write on paper, then erase it, then write the actual document over the erased surface' — technically a replacement, not a duplicate.
↓
Challenge from Rav Aḥa bar Minyumi: What if the erased writing's imprint is still recognizable? That would make it appear as a valid prior document, creating exactly the fraud we sought to prevent!
↓
Abaye's response: In a case of great loss, I rely on the scribe erasing thoroughly. A recognizable imprint would itself be invalid — but we ensure it is fully erased.

תלמיד חכם שהולך לקדש אשה יטול עם הארץ עמו
🌿 Practical Wisdom for Torah Scholars Abaye teaches: A Torah scholar who goes to betroth a woman should bring an am ha'aretz (ignoramus) with him. Why? The scholar is absorbed in learning and may not notice if a different woman is substituted — an unscrupulous family could exploit his naivety. The am ha'aretz is worldly and alert to such tricks, providing practical protection. Connected case: Rav Yirmeya bar Abba was signed on a receipt; a woman claimed it was not what he thought. As a Torah scholar, he was willing to retract — the Gemara notes this is exceptional behavior, underscoring that scholars can be manipulated in mundane matters. Torah wisdom and worldly shrewdness serve different functions — both are needed.
