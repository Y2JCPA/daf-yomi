QUESTION 1 OF 5
When one buys the produce of a dovecote, what must the buyer leave for the seller?
A. The entire first brood of the year
B. The first pair of the brood
C. Half of each subsequent brood
D. Nothing — the buyer takes all produced doves
Correct: B — The mishna states the buyer must leave the first pair (mafriaḥ) of the brood for the seller. The reason: the mother dove is attached to her young, so leaving the pair ensures she stays in the dovecote rather than fleeing.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is Rabbi Yoḥanan's interpretation of 'mesares' in the context of beehive sales?
A. The seller applies an herb to render the queen bee permanently infertile
B. The seller destroys the hive after the third swarm
C. The buyer takes alternate swarms — 1st, 3rd, and 5th — skipping the others
D. Both buyer and seller share all remaining swarms equally
Correct: C — Rabbi Yoḥanan derives mesares from 'seirus' (skipping). The buyer takes the 1st, 3rd, and 5th swarms; the seller retains the 2nd, 4th, and 6th. No biological intervention is needed — the alternating pattern itself defines the buyer's three-swarm portion.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rabbi Eliezer, what is the legal status of a beehive?
A. Movable property, susceptible to ritual impurity
B. Equivalent to land — not susceptible to impurity
C. Ownerless property once the bees swarm and leave
D. A vessel, always susceptible to impurity regardless of location
Correct: B — Rabbi Eliezer holds the beehive is like land: not susceptible to ritual impurity (when in place), a prosbol may be written based on it, and removing honey on Shabbat incurs liability. He derives this from 'ya'arat hadevash' — the word ya'ar (forest) implies land-like status.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
When purchasing a tree for felling, at what minimum height above the ground must the buyer cut it?
A. Even with the ground
B. Three handbreadths above the ground
C. One handbreadth above the ground
D. Ten handbreadths above the ground
Correct: C — The baraita teaches that one who buys a tree for felling must cut it one handbreadth above the ground. This preserves the stump so the tree can regrow for the seller's benefit. (The three-handbreadth rule applies specifically to the untrimmed sycamore.)
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Lulyani say the verse must compare the righteous to BOTH the palm tree and the cedar?
A. Because both trees are native to the Land of Israel
B. Each comparison alone would imply something negative: cedar = no fruit; palm = trunk doesn't regrow
C. To show that righteous people come in two distinct spiritual types
D. Because both trees are mentioned together in the building of the Temple
Correct: B — If the verse mentioned only cedar, one might conclude the righteous bear no reward (cedar has no fruit). If only palm, one might conclude the righteous leave no legacy after death (the palm's trunk doesn't regrow). Together, the verse teaches that the righteous both produce reward in this world (like the fruit-bearing palm) and leave an enduring legacy (like the cedar that regrows).
