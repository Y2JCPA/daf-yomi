Bava Batra 144 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
A widow delays collecting her ketubah and enhances her deceased husband's estate. Where do her profits go?
A. To herself alone — she is only a creditor, not an heir
B. To the middle, shared with the orphans
C. To the orphans alone, since it is their father's estate
D. To charity, as she has no legal share at all
Correct: B — The mishna rules that since she delays and remains in the household like an heir, her enhancements go to the middle — shared with the orphans. The Gemara adds that this must be stated explicitly, lest we think her toil was only for herself.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Rabbi Ḥanina rules: if a father designates a house for his son's wedding canopy and feast, what does the son acquire?
A. Nothing — intent alone creates no acquisition
B. The house, as a gift
C. The furniture in the house only
D. Half the house and the right to use it
Correct: B — Rabbi Ḥanina teaches that the son acquires the house as a gift when the father designates it for the ḥuppah and feast — a ruling Mar Zutra lists among three halakhot enacted without a fully articulated mechanism.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Huna, why may the brothers refuse to support a brother who left to study Torah?
A. Torah study is a personal obligation and not the estate's concern
B. The blessing of the house is in its abundance — the estate benefits from his physical presence
C. A Torah scholar has other sources of income
D. The mishna explicitly exempts absent brothers from support
Correct: B — Rav Huna teaches: 'the blessing of the house is in its abundance' — the estate benefits from having members physically present. An absent brother therefore cannot demand equal sustenance from the shared estate.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
If one of the brothers in a shared estate becomes ill and requires treatment, where does the cost come from?
A. The middle — shared equally by all brothers
B. His own personal resources
C. The eldest brother's share as guardian
D. The estate only if the illness was not his fault
Correct: B — The mishna rules that medical costs come from the sick brother's own resources. Ravin adds in the name of Rabbi Ela: this applies specifically when he became ill through negligence — pointing to a distinction based on cause.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Shmuel explain the mishna's ruling that groomsmen gifts return 'to the middle' rather than to the sender's heirs alone?
A. The father sent the gifts without specifying which son to credit
B. The brothers all contributed to the gifts jointly
C. The son who sent the gifts died childless and his brother entered levirate marriage with the widow
D. The gifts were sent from common estate funds, not personal funds
Correct: C — Shmuel explains: the deceased son died childless, his brother entered into yibum (levirate marriage) with the widow, and the groomsmen gifts that return now flow to the yavam — who stands in the deceased's place — and thus go to the middle.
