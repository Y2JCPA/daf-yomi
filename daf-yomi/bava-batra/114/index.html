⚖️ When Is a Transaction Final?

Rabba
As long as both parties remain seated in the same location, either may still renege on the transaction.
Rav Yosef
Once the parties arise from their seats, the transaction is irrevocable — neither may renege.
Proof Text Both opinions appeal to Rav Yehuda's ruling about three men who visited a sick person at night — the case where a will is written and witnessed but the witnesses cannot later adjudicate.

🤝 Can You Back Out of a Deal?

Rabba
A party may renege as long as they remain seated at the place where the deal was made. Rising ends the ability to withdraw.
Rav Yosef
Once the parties arise from their seats, the deal is final. That is the decisive act that seals the transaction.
Rav Ashi
I challenged this before Rav Kahana: Even by Rav Yosef's logic, shouldn't we fear the sick person might still change his mind? The concern for renege doesn't disappear just because someone stood up!
Resolution According to Rabba: the sick man arose and sat again, breaking his train of thought — that constitutes finality. According to Rav Yosef: the conversation shifted to a new topic, which similarly seals the decision.

🛏️ Three Men Visit the Sick: The Proof Case

Three men visit a sick person at night
Sick person wishes to distribute his property — wants a will written
↓
✅ Permitted
Write the will and sign as witnesses
❌ Forbidden
Act as judges over disputes the next day
↓
Why can't they judge?
They are a beit din she-ra'u — a court that already saw the matter. Prior knowledge of the sick person's wishes disqualifies them from impartial adjudication.
↓
This case supports Rav Yosef: once the sick person arose (or shifted topic), his will became final — renege was no longer possible

👩 Woman's Inheritance: Why the Mishna Repeats Itself

Woman Bequeaths to Husband

  • Husband inherits her property upon her death
  • Limitation: He does NOT inherit property that comes to her estate after she dies
  • Example: if her son dies after she does, the son's inheritance flows to her estate — but the husband cannot claim it
  • He only inherits what was hers at the moment of death

Woman Bequeaths to Son

  • Son inherits her property upon her death
  • Same limitation applies: Son does NOT inherit property arriving posthumously to her estate
  • Mishna states both cases to teach their structural parallel
  • One case alone would not reveal the shared rule — both were necessary

📜 Torah Law of Inheritance: Father Before Mother

מִן הַתּוֹרָה — אָב יוֹרֵשׁ אֶת בְּנוֹ, וְאִשָּׁה יוֹרֶשֶׁת אֶת בְּנָהּ כְּשֶׁאֵין שָׁם אָב
Rabbi Yoḥanan citing Rabbi Yehuda ben Rabbi Shimon By Torah law: A father inherits from his son. A mother (woman) inherits from her son only when the father is not alive — the father's claim to the son's estate takes absolute precedence. Only in the father's absence does the mother's inheritance right activate.
