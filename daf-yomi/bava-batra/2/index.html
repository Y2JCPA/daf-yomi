👁️ Is Hezek Re'iya (Damage by Sight) Halakhic Damage?

Tanna (Mishna)
Partners who both wish to build a partition in their shared courtyard build it in the middle at joint expense.
Gemara Inference
'Both wished' implies: if only one wishes, the court does not compel — therefore hezek re'iya is NOT considered damage.
Gemara Challenge
But see the garden case — one partner can compel the other to build a partition, implying hezek re'iya IS actionable!
Rav (via R. Abba, Rav Huna)
A garden is different: it is forbidden to stand in another's field at harvest — heightened privacy makes the garden a special case.
Rav Naḥman (Shmuel)
A roof adjacent to a neighbor's courtyard requires a 4-cubit parapet — this definitively proves hezek re'iya IS called damage.
Key Term הֶזֵּק רְאִיָּה (Hezek Re'iya) — halakhic damage caused by visual intrusion into another's private domain

🧱 Wall Materials and Thicknesses

Uncut Stone (גָּוִיל)

  • Total thickness: 6 tefachim
  • Each partner contributes: 3 tefachim
  • Roughest, most traditional construction

Cut Stone (גָּזִית)

  • Total thickness: 5 tefachim
  • Each partner contributes: 2½ tefachim
  • Smoother, precisely shaped blocks

Half-Bricks (כְּפִיסִין)

  • Total thickness: 4 tefachim
  • Each partner contributes: 2 tefachim
  • Intermediate fired-clay units

Full Bricks (לְבֵנִים)

  • Total thickness: 3 tefachim
  • Each partner contributes: 1½ tefachim
  • Thinnest standard wall material

📖 What Does מְחִיצָה Mean?

Suggestion A
Meḥitza = Partition (physical wall). Supported by a baraita: a meḥitzat vineyard that separates it from a grain field must be maintained — physical structure implied.
Suggestion B
Meḥitza = Division (splitting property in half). Supported by Numbers 31:43 — וּמֶחֱצַת הָעֵדָה, 'the half of the congregation.'
Refutation
If meḥitza meant division, the Mishna should say 'who wished to divide' (לַחֲלֹק) — not 'to make a meḥitza.' The unusual phrasing proves a physical structure is meant.
Resolution
Meḥitza = wall/partition. The Tanna's word choice is deliberate and precise. This conclusion launches the hezek re'iya discussion.
Key Verse וּמֶחֱצַת הָעֵדָה (Numbers 31:43) — 'the half of the congregation' — proposed as proof that meḥitza means division, but ultimately rejected

👁️ The Gemara's Search for Proof on Hezek Re'iya

Question: Is hezek re'iya (damage by sight) considered halakhic damage that courts enforce?
↓
Proof Attempt 1 — Mishna 5a
Wall in courtyard falls: court compels rebuilding. Rejected: they already agreed once to build — no new compulsion proven.
Proof Attempt 2 — Mishna 7b
Residents compel building a gatehouse and gate. Rejected: this concerns theft prevention, not visual privacy.
Proof Attempt 3 — Mishna 11a
Divide courtyard only if each gets 4 cubits. Rejected: concerns usability/utility, not privacy from sight.
↓
Mishna (22a): Must distance a new wall 4 cubits from a neighbor's window — but this concerns outdoor sight, homes are different from courtyards.
↓
Rav Naḥman (Shmuel): Roof adjacent to neighbor's courtyard requires a 4-cubit parapet — no other reason than preventing visual intrusion.
Proof accepted!

🏗️ Step-by-Step: How Partners Divide a Courtyard

Step 1: Both partners express a wish to create a division in their shared courtyard (חָצֵר הַשֻּׁתָּפִים).
↓
Step 2: Determine the local custom — what building material is standard in this region?
↓
Step 3: Each partner measures and yields land proportional to the wall's thickness from their respective sides (e.g., 3 tefachim each for gavil).
↓
Step 4: The wall is constructed on the property line, centered between both portions, at joint expense.
↓
Step 5: If only one partner builds (where permitted), he builds entirely within his own land and places a boundary mark (סִימָן) on the outer face.
↓
Step 6: The boundary mark proves ownership so that if the neighbor later wishes to use the wall, he must compensate the builder.
