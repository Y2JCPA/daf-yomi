💱 Why Only One-Sixth? Three Explanations for Shmuel's Rule

Reason 1
Prevent exploitation (ona'ah): An increase beyond one-sixth nullifies the transaction — ona'ah above one-sixth voids a sale entirely.
Objection
This works in both directions! If the point is to avoid transaction nullification, the reasoning doesn't fully explain why the limit is one-sixth specifically.
Reason 2
Protect merchants from loss: A merchant typically earns one-sixth profit. If measures increase beyond that without his knowledge, he loses his livelihood.
Objection
But a merchant must also earn a profit! The adage says: 'Buy and sell without profit — you are not a merchant.' The reasoning still doesn't hold.
Rav Ḥisda
Scriptural derivation: The prohibition is not based on logical reasoning at all — Shmuel found a verse in Ezekiel and derived the one-sixth limit homiletically from the Torah itself.
Bottom Line The one-sixth limit on changing measures is a Torah-sourced ruling derived by Shmuel from Ezekiel 45:12 — not from marketplace logic

📏 Standard Measures of the Mishnah

Dry Measures

  • Kav = basic unit of volume
  • Tarkav = 3 kav
  • Tomen = ⅛ kav
  • Half-tomen = 1/16 kav
  • Ukla = 1/20 kav (one-fifth of a quarter-kav)

Liquid Measures (Log-based)

  • Hin = 12 log
  • Half-hin = 6 log
  • Third-hin = 4 log
  • Quarter-hin = 3 log
  • Log and sub-log fractions below

🔍 Which Measures May Be Prepared? (The Confusion Problem)

May one prepare a measure of 2 kav?
↓
No — too similar to tarkav (3 kav); people err by one-third and will confuse the two
↓
But if we fear one-third error, should we also forbid half-tomen and ukla? They differ from each other by only one-quarter!
↓
Also: 4 log vs 3 log?
Third-hin (4 log) and quarter-hin (3 log) differ by one-quarter — yet both are permitted
Resolution
These measures are so well-known and distinct in usage that confusion is unlikely — familiarity overrides the theoretical concern

📜 Rav Ḥisda's Homiletical Derivation

עֶשְׂרִים שְׁקָלִים חֲמִשָּׁה וְעֶשְׂרִים שְׁקָלִים עֲשֶׂרֶת הַשְּׁקָלִים הַמָּנֶה יִהְיֶה לָכֶם (יחזקאל מה:יב)
Ezekiel 45:12 The verse states: "Twenty shekels, five and twenty shekels, ten and five shekels, shall be your maneh." — The three groups listed are 20 + 25 + 15 = 60 shekels per maneh. One-sixth of 60 = 10, the permitted increment of adjustment. Thus Scripture itself encodes the one-sixth limit for measures and coinage.

Objection raised: But a maneh is actually 25 shekels (each shekel = 4 dinars, maneh = 100 dinars = 25 shekels)! The Gemara works through how the verse's arithmetic resolves with actual currency values.

🚫 The Four Economic Offenders (Amud B)

1. Hoarders of produce — Buy and withhold goods to create artificial scarcity and inflate prices. Example: Shabbtai the hoarder.
↓
2. Usurers (ribis) — Charge interest on loans, exploiting those in financial distress in violation of Torah law.
↓
3. Those who falsely reduce measures — Cheat buyers by using inaccurate weights or volumes, violating the commandment of honest measures.
↓
4. Those who raise market prices — Sell above the accepted market rate, destabilizing the communal economy.
↓
The Sages declare: these four are like enemies attacking Israel from within — their harm to the community is as severe as an external enemy.
