⚖️ חוזר וטוען — Can a Litigant Modify Their Claim?

Ulla
A litigant can return and state a modified version of their claim. The court should hear the clarification.
Sages of Neharde'a
A litigant cannot return and modify their claim. Once stated in court, the original claim is binding.
Ameimar
Though I am from Neharde'a, I side with Ulla — a litigant can modify their claim. And the halakha follows this view.
Halakhic Conclusion The final ruling: a litigant CAN return and modify their claim (חוזר וטוען) — with narrow exceptions where the original claim explicitly contradicted the opponent.

👥 Contradicting Witnesses: Two Foundational Positions

Rav Huna
When two groups of witnesses contradict each other, each group testifies independently. Neither is disqualified — we simply have irresolvable uncertainty. Both testimonies stand.
Rav Ḥisda
When two groups contradict each other, both groups are disqualified entirely — since one group is certainly lying, neither can be trusted for any purpose.
Practical Impact on This Case Per Rav Ḥisda: even the unchallenged ownership testimony is tainted when delivered by witnesses whose companion testimony was contradicted. Per Rav Huna: each testimony is evaluated on its own.

⚖️ Where Ulla and Neharde'a Each Concede

Ulla Concedes — Cannot Change

  • If the original claim stated: "It belonged to my ancestors and NOT to your ancestors"
  • An explicit denial of the opponent's ancestral connection cannot be retracted
  • The direct contradiction locks the claim — no modification allowed

Neharde'a Concedes — Can Change

  • If the litigant clarifies: "When I said 'my ancestors,' I meant ancestors who purchased it from yours"
  • This is a clarification, not a reversal — consistent with the original
  • The modified claim harmonizes with the first statement — permitted

🔄 Rav Naḥman's Dramatic Reversal

Step 1 — Initial Ruling: Rav Naḥman rules in favor of one litigant, pairing and canceling the competing profit-testimonies
↓
Step 2 — First Objection: Rava raises a challenge from a baraita about contradicting witnesses in a case of a woman whose husband's fate was unknown
↓
Step 3 — Second Objection: Rav Hamnuna independently raises the same objection against Rav Naḥman while in Syria
↓
Step 4 — Declaration: Rav Naḥman says: "I will not perform an action in this matter" — apparently standing down from his ruling
↓
Step 5 — Reversal: Rav Naḥman goes out and reverses the ruling anyway, taking the land from the original winner
↓
Step 6 — Vindication: Observers thought it was error — but he acted deliberately, anchored in Rabbi Menaḥem ben Rabbi Yosei's principle on conflicting testimony
