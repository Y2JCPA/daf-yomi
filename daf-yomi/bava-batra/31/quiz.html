QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the final halakhic ruling on this daf, can a litigant return and modify their initial legal claim (חוזר וטוען)?
A. No — the original claim is always binding once stated in court
B. Only if both parties agree to allow the modification
C. Yes — with narrow exceptions where the original claim explicitly denied the opponent's ancestral connection
D. Only if the litigant brings new documentary evidence to support the modified claim
Correct: C — The Gemara concludes that a litigant can return and modify their claim. Ameimar, though from Neharde'a, agrees with Ulla. The key exception: if the original claim explicitly said 'it belonged to my ancestors and NOT to yours,' that denial cannot be retracted.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In the disputed land case, what is Rav Naḥman's method for reaching a verdict when both litigants have partial testimony?
A. Rule in favor of the current physical possessor of the land
B. Pair the profit-testimonies against each other so they cancel, leaving the unchallenged ownership testimony
C. Accept whichever testimony was presented first in court
D. Divide the land equally between both parties
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman says: establish the testimony about profiting alongside the other testimony about profiting — the two cancel each other out. This leaves the testimony regarding ancestral ownership standing alone and unchallenged, determining the outcome.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Rav Ḥisda's position on two groups of witnesses who directly contradict each other?
A. The larger group is believed
B. The court suspends judgment indefinitely (תיקו)
C. Both groups are disqualified, since one group is certainly lying
D. The first group to arrive in court is given precedence
Correct: C — Rav Ḥisda holds that contradicting witness groups are both entirely disqualified — we know with certainty that one group is lying, so neither can be trusted. This contrasts with Rav Huna, who holds each group testifies independently and both remain valid.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
After initially saying 'I will not perform an action in this matter,' what did Rav Naḥman actually do?
A. He deferred the case to the Exilarch for final decision
B. He maintained his original ruling and refused to reverse it
C. He went out and reversed the ruling, taking land from the original winner
D. He declared the case undecidable and returned the land to joint possession
Correct: C — Despite his hesitation after objections from Rava and Rav Hamnuna, Rav Naḥman ultimately went out and reversed his ruling. Observers thought it was a mistake, but he acted deliberately — supported by Rabbi Menaḥem ben Rabbi Yosei's principle about conflicting testimony.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rabbi Elazar, when is one witness sufficient to elevate someone to presumptive priestly status?
A. Never — two witnesses are always required for matters of personal status
B. Only when combined with documentary evidence
C. When there are no challengers to contest the testimony
D. Only when all three judges unanimously accept the single witness
Correct: C — Rabbi Elazar holds that one witness suffices for priestly status when there are no challengers — if no one contests the testimony, its reliability is presumed. Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, always requires two witnesses. The Gemara then questions how Rabbi Elazar differs from Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who seems to say the same thing.
