QUESTION 1 OF 5
One who throws turnip seeds into the crevices of a convert's ownerless land — has he acquired it?
A. Yes, planting seeds is always a valid act of land acquisition
B. No, because at the time of the act there was no enhancement to the land's value
C. Yes, but only if he verbally declared intent to acquire the field
D. No, because seeds can only acquire movable property, not real estate
Correct: B — The Gemara rules he has not acquired it: at the moment of throwing the seeds, the land received no improvement in value. The benefit only materializes later when the seeds grow — too late for the initial act to count as kinyan.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Shmuel, if one opens a water blockage allowing water into a field, intending only to catch the fish that enter — has he acquired the field?
A. Yes, any act that physically improves access to the land acquires it
B. Yes, but he must share ownership with whoever owns the fish rights
C. No, because his intent was to catch fish, not to irrigate and improve the field
D. No, because opening water channels is never a recognized act of kinyan
Correct: C — Shmuel's principle is that intent is determinative. Opening the blockage to catch fish is personal benefit, not land improvement. Only if his intent was to irrigate the field — improving it for agricultural use — would he acquire it.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
A woman cut branches from an ownerless palm tree for 13 years. Another person came and plowed beneath the tree briefly. Who acquired it?
A. The woman, because 13 years of continuous use establishes possession (chazakah)
B. The woman, because cutting branches is itself an act of land improvement
C. The one who plowed, because plowing is a land-improving act done with proper intent
D. Neither — the property must be acquired through a formal legal document
Correct: C — Levi (or Mar Ukva) ruled that 13 years of cutting branches only reflected personal benefit — not land improvement. The person who briefly plowed beneath the tree performed a genuine land-improving act and thereby acquired it.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Rav Huna rules that one strike of a hoe acquires an entire bounded field. What reasoning supports this?
A. A hoe strike is equivalent to plowing by rabbinic enactment in all circumstances
B. Since the field has clearly demarcated boundaries, one act demonstrates intent to acquire the entire defined area
C. The ruling applies only to fields smaller than four cubits where one strike covers all
D. Rav Huna's rule derives from the principle that money alone suffices for land acquisition
Correct: B — Rav Huna (citing Rav) reasons that defined boundaries signal the scope of the acquirer's intent — one act within a clearly bounded space implicitly covers the whole. Shmuel disagrees, holding that only what was physically worked is acquired.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Abaye resolves the apparent contradiction in Shmuel's rulings on dina d'malkhuta by distinguishing which type of fields?
A. Fields in Israel vs. fields in the diaspora
B. Fields owned by Jews vs. fields previously owned by gentiles
C. Concealed fields where owners evade the land tax vs. normal registered taxed fields
D. Fields with natural boundaries vs. fields with artificial boundaries
Correct: C — Abaye explains that the problematic incident involved fields in Dura whose owners concealed them to avoid the royal land tax (taska). Since those fields operate outside the king's legal system, dina d'malkhuta does not apply — Shmuel's rule that land stays ownerless until kinyan holds there. For regular tax-paying fields, dina d'malkhuta may validate money payment.
