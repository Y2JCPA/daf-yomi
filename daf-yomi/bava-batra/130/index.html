⚖️ Can a Father Override Halakhic Inheritance?

First Tanna
If a father says 'So-and-so will inherit from me' instead of his halakhic heir, he has said nothing — one cannot stipulate against Torah law of inheritance.
R' Yoḥanan ben Beroka
His bequeathal stands — when redistributing among those who are legally eligible heirs, the Torah grants the father authority to choose among them as he wishes.
Halakha Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel, Rava, and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi all rule: the halakha follows Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka.

🌿 When Is a Father's Bequeathal Valid?

Father designates his estate to a non-default heir
↓
Recipient is an eligible heir
e.g., one son chosen over another son, or one daughter over another
Recipient is not an eligible heir
e.g., daughter chosen when sons exist; or a stranger
↓
✓ Valid (R' Yoḥanan ben Beroka)
Torah grants father authority to choose — halakha follows him
✗ Void (all agree)
Stipulating against Torah inheritance law has no effect

🔎 Scope of R' Yoḥanan ben Beroka's Ruling — Two Readings

R' Yishmael (son of R' Yoḥanan ben Beroka)
They only disagree when bequeathing to one son among other sons, or one daughter among other daughters. But bequeathing to a daughter when there is a son? Everyone agrees it is void — the daughter is not an eligible heir when sons exist.
Gemara's Alternative Reading
The mishna is incomplete. Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka validates any bequeathal to an heir who could potentially inherit — even a daughter when sons exist, since daughters do inherit when there are no sons.
Core Question Does the father's authority extend only within the same class of heirs, or to any halakhically possible heir?

📖 Deuteronomy 21:16 — One Verse, Two Essential Lessons

Rava's Derivation

  • "Then it shall be, in the day that he causes his sons to inherit"
  • The Torah grants the father permission to bequeath to whichever son he chooses
  • This is the source for R' Yoḥanan ben Beroka's ruling
  • Abaye: Isn't this already derived from the prohibition on disinheriting the firstborn?
  • Answer: No — both halves of the verse are independently necessary

Abba Ḥanan's Baraita

  • First half → father's right to choose among sons (cannot be derived from second half via kal v'chomer)
  • Second half → prohibition against disinheriting the firstborn (cannot be derived from first half via kal v'chomer)
  • Each clause is indispensable — the a fortiori arguments fail in both directions
  • Result: the verse teaches two independent halakhot

📜 Rules for Deriving and Transmitting Halakha (Baraita)

Rule 1: Do not derive the practical halakha solely from a statement (shema'ta) you heard from a posek — it may have been theoretical
↓
Rule 2: Do not derive the practical halakha solely from seeing a ruling done in practice (ma'aseh) — a specific case may not generalize
↓
Exception: One may act when the Sage explicitly says: "this is the practical halakha" — halakha l'ma'aseh
↓
Tereifot Principle: Do not compare one type of fatal wound to another — "this wound is similar to that wound" is forbidden; each wound-category has independent halakhot
↓
Example: Severing the spine with spinal cord intact — fatal in one organ, not fatal in another. No analogy may be drawn across cases

👨‍⚖️ How Poskim Handle Their Own Rulings

וְהָיָה בְּיוֹם הַנְחִילוֹ אֶת-בָּנָיו — דברים כ"א:ט"ז
Two Instructions from the Amoraim Rabbi Yoḥanan → Rabbi Asi: "Do not act upon a ruling of mine unless I explicitly say: this is a practical halakha (halakha l'ma'aseh)."

Rava → Rav Pappa & Rav Huna b. Rav Yehoshua: "If a ruling of mine comes before you and you perceive a refutation, do not tear it up until you come discuss it with me. If I have a valid explanation I will tell you — and if not, I will retract."

Both instructions reflect the same principle: a posek's ruling deserves careful, collaborative engagement — neither blind deference nor unilateral rejection.
