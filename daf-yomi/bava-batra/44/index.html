🔎 Challenging Rav Sheshet's Reading of the Baraita

Gemara
According to Rav Sheshet, the baraita disqualifies the robbery victim from testifying for the buyer. But why discuss a buyer at all? The baraita could contrast testifying for the robber himself!
Tanna (Baraita)
The buyer case is needed for the latter clause: If the robber sold a cow or cloak, the robbery victim can testify — demonstrating the contrast between land and movable property.
Gemara
But even for movable property — the owner despaired of the item, but did he despair of being reimbursed its value? He still has a financial stake in the buyer winning!
Abaye
Rav Sheshet's entire explanation is difficult. The baraita's stated reason — that the victim cannot recover movables — does not hold if he can still sue the robber for the value from other assets.
Core Problem If the robbery victim can still sue for the monetary value of stolen movables, he has a financial interest in the buyer winning — so why is he not disqualified for movable property testimony too?

📜 Shmuel's Principle — Selling Without a Guarantee

המוכר שדה לחבירו שלא באחריות — אינו יכול להעיד לו עליה
Ravin bar Shmuel in the name of Shmuel One who sells a field to another even without a guarantee (אחריות) — even without promising to compensate if the field is repossessed — nonetheless cannot testify on behalf of that buyer.

Why? Because if the buyer loses the field, he will attempt to collect reimbursement from the seller's other properties via court order. The seller therefore has a financial stake in the buyer winning, creating disqualifying bias.

🏠 Land vs. Movable Property — Creditor Liens

🏠 Land / Fields

  • A creditor's lien attaches to land even after it is sold to a buyer
  • If the debtor sells the field, the creditor can repossess it from the buyer
  • Buyer may then seek reimbursement from the seller/debtor
  • Therefore: the seller is biased and disqualified from testifying for the buyer
  • The lien creates a lasting financial connection between seller and buyer's fate

🐄 Movable Property (Cow / Cloak)

  • Movable property is not liened to a creditor by default
  • A creditor cannot repossess movables from a subsequent buyer
  • The seller of movables has no financial stake in how the buyer fares
  • Therefore: the seller (robbery victim) can testify for the buyer
  • Even designated apoteiki on movables lacks the publicity needed to bind buyers

⚔️ Rav Pappa vs. Rav Zevid — Can the Buyer Reclaim from the Seller?

Rav Pappa
If a seller sold a field without a guarantee and a creditor repossessed it from the buyer, the buyer cannot return to the seller to demand reimbursement. He accepted the risk knowingly.
Rav Pappa (exception)
But if the debt was connected to this very buyer — and the buyer recognizes the obligation as his own — then he can seek reimbursement from the seller.
Rav Zevid
Even if it is found that the field was never the seller's to sell — the buyer still cannot return to the seller. Purchasing without a guarantee means accepting total risk, no exceptions.
Gemara
Rav Pappa allows recourse in a narrow case. Rav Zevid is stricter: shelo be-achrayut is an absolute waiver — the buyer assumed all risk regardless of circumstances.
Practical Implication A buyer purchasing שלא באחריות (without guarantee) knowingly waives protection. The dispute: does this waiver cover all scenarios, or only standard repossession cases?
