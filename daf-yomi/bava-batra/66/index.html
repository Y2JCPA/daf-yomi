🐝 Rabbi Eliezer vs. Rabbis: Is a Fixed Beehive Like Land?

Rabbi Eliezer
A beehive fixed in the ground is legally like land: one may write a prosbol based on it, it is NOT susceptible to ritual impurity, and one who removes honey on Shabbat is liable for a sin-offering — like harvesting produce.
Rabbis
A beehive is NOT like land: one may not write a prosbol based on it, it IS susceptible to ritual impurity even when fixed, and one who removes honey on Shabbat is exempt from a sin-offering.
Gemara
Rabbi Eliezer's beehive ruling is derived from a specific verse — 'ya'ar devash' (honey forest) — equating honey removal to picking from a tree. It is NOT a general principle that all affixed vessels equal land.
Critical Limit Because Rabbi Eliezer's beehive ruling is text-based (drash), not a general rule, it does not automatically extend to the baker's board or duct cases — those require independent analysis.

🍞 Baker's Board (Daf shel Naḥtomin): Who Is Right?

Rabbi Eliezer
A baker's kneading board (daf shel naḥtomin) that is affixed to the ground loses its vessel-status and becomes like land — therefore NOT susceptible to ritual impurity.
Rabbis
Even a baker's metal board fixed to a wall retains full vessel-status and IS susceptible to ritual impurity. Flat wooden vessels without a receptacle may differ, but metal boards do not.
Gemara's Resolution The baraita about the hollowed duct can be read as following the Rabbis — even though Rabbis hold metal boards remain susceptible to impurity, the drawn-water rules for mikveh operate on different logic and still depend on when the duct was hollowed.

🚰 Hollowed Duct: Does Water Through It Invalidate the Mikveh?

A duct channels water into a mikveh
Does the water count as sheuvim (drawn water) that invalidates the mikveh?
↓
First hollowed out → then affixed to ground
The duct was a vessel before being fixed — it could hold drawn water.

Result: water flowing through it = drawn water → mikveh INVALIDATED
First affixed to ground → then hollowed out
The duct was attached to the ground before being hollowed — it is like land from the start.

Result: water is NOT drawn water → mikveh remains VALID

🌧️ Rav Yosef's Dilemma: Rainwater and Fixed Millstones

בבא בתרא ס״ו ע״ב — רב יוסף מיבעיא ליה: גשמים שירדו ונתכוון לשפשף בהן רחיים התחתונות
Rav Yosef's Question (66b) Rainwater is falling and the owner consciously desires it to wash his immovable lower millstones (which are fixed to the ground). Does this conscious intent (machshavah) render the rainwater 'drawn' — making seeds in the millstones susceptible to impurity?

According to Rabbi Eliezer: No dilemma — millstones attached to the ground are like land itself; water over them can never be 'drawn.'

The real dilemma is for the Rabbis: Since Rabbis hold that affixed objects are NOT automatically like land, does the owner's machshavah to direct the water over them matter here and activate susceptibility to tum'ah?

⚖️ Two Frameworks: Affixed Objects in Halakha

Rabbi Eliezer

  • Anything attached to the ground = land
  • Beehive fixed in ground: not susceptible to tum'ah
  • Baker's board affixed: loses vessel-status entirely
  • Duct hollowed after affixing: water through it is not drawn
  • Millstones: definitively like land — Rav Yosef's dilemma does not apply
  • Result: very broad 'land' category

Rabbis

  • Affixing to ground does NOT confer land status
  • Beehive: susceptible to tum'ah even when fixed
  • Baker's metal board: susceptible even when fixed to wall
  • Duct: drawn water rules still may apply based on history
  • Millstones: Rav Yosef's dilemma is live — machshavah may activate tum'ah
  • Result: vessels remain vessels despite affixing
