📜 Deed Formula Dispute: When Can a Deathbed Gift Be Retracted?

Rav
A deed written 'in life and in death' is treated as a deathbed gift. The word 'death' is operative — the gift only fully takes effect at death. If the giver recovers, he may retract it.
Shmuel
A deed written 'in life and in death' is treated as a healthy person's gift. The word 'life' is operative — the gift takes effect immediately during his lifetime. Even if he recovers, he cannot retract it.
The Stakes Deathbed gifts (מַתְּנַת שְׁכִיב מְרַע) are revocable upon recovery. Gifts of a healthy person are irrevocable. The deed's exact wording determines which category applies — and the difference is enormous.

📋 Three Deed Formulas — Legal Effects Compared

"In Death" (בְּמִיתָה)

  • Both Rav and Shmuel agree
  • Treated as a deathbed gift
  • Gift activates at death only
  • Revocable upon recovery
  • Standard deathbed gift formula

"In Life and In Death" (בְּחַיִּים וּבְמִיתָה)

  • Rav: read 'death' → deathbed gift
  • Shmuel: read 'life' → healthy gift
  • Core dispute of this daf
  • Ameimar: halakha follows Rav
  • Revocable per final ruling

"During Life and In Death" (מֵחַיִּים וּבְמִיתָה)

  • Rava: recipient acquires immediately
  • 'During life' = effective now
  • Cannot be retracted upon recovery
  • Ameimar rejects Rava's ruling
  • Rava applied it in practice

⚖️ Ameimar vs. Rav Ashi — Clarifying the Final Halakha

Ameimar
The halakha is not in accordance with Rava's opinion. The phrase 'during life and in death' does not create a special irrevocable category distinct from Rav's framework.
Rav Ashi
Isn't that obvious? The Sages of Neharde'a already rule that the halakha follows Rav — so Rava's more lenient distinction for the recipient is already overridden.
Ameimar
I needed to clarify this explicitly — lest you say Rava's ruling holds within Rav's framework as an independent innovation. I am telling you: even that is rejected.
Final Ruling The halakha follows Rav: 'in life and in death' = deathbed gift, revocable upon recovery. Rava's special category for 'during life and in death' is rejected by Ameimar. Rava, however, applied his own ruling in practice.

⚰️ Rabba's Presumption — The Grave as Legal Proof

מֵת הוּא וְקִבְרוֹ מוֹכִיחַ עָלָיו — "He is dead and his grave proves it" (Rabba, Bava Batra 153b)
Rabba's Ruling (153b) A deed records that the giver was sick and lying in his bed but omits whether he recovered. The giver has since died. Rabba rules:

"He is dead — and his grave proves that he died from that very illness."

Rabba presumes the giver never recovered, validating the deathbed gift. Abaye challenges this: we don't normally presume a specific cause of death merely because someone eventually died.

Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua traces Rabba's view to Rabbi Natan: the current status creates a legal presumption reflecting the status at the time of the gift — whoever is in possession today is presumed to have been in that state back then.
