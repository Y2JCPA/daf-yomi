Bava Batra 153 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav, how is a deed that says the gift is given 'in life and in death' classified?
A. As a healthy person's irrevocable gift
B. As a deathbed gift — revocable upon recovery
C. As void due to contradictory language
D. As requiring a new act of acquisition to be valid
Correct: B — Rav reads 'death' as the operative word: the gift is intended to take effect at death, making it a deathbed gift (matnat shchiv mera). If the giver recovers, he may retract it.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is Shmuel's interpretation of a deed that says the gift is given 'in life and in death'?
A. It is a deathbed gift — revocable upon recovery
B. It is void because the terms conflict
C. It is like a healthy person's gift — effective immediately, irrevocable
D. The recipient only acquires after the giver dies
Correct: C — Shmuel reads 'life' as the operative word: the gift takes effect during the giver's lifetime, making it like a healthy person's irrevocable gift. Recovery does not entitle the giver to retract it.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Rava's ruling about a deed that says the gift is given 'during life and in death' (mei-ḥayyim u-ve-mitah)?
A. Same as 'in life and in death' — a revocable deathbed gift
B. The gift is void for excessive language
C. The recipient acquires immediately and it cannot be retracted
D. The gift requires witnesses to testify before it takes effect
Correct: C — Rava rules that 'during life' (mei-ḥayyim) signals the gift takes effect while the giver still lives — the recipient acquires immediately and it is irrevocable. Ameimar later rejects this ruling, but Rava applied it in practice.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In the Mishna (153a), when the giver claims he was ill and the recipients claim he was healthy, who bears the burden of proof according to the Rabbis?
A. The giver, since he seeks to retract the gift
B. The recipients, since they must prove they are entitled to the gift
C. Neither party — the gift is divided equally
D. The local court decides without requiring proof
Correct: B — The Rabbis hold that 'the burden of proof rests on the claimant.' The giver retains possession of the property, so the recipients — who claim the gift is valid — must bring proof that they are entitled to it.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is Rabba's legal reasoning when a deed states the giver was 'sick and lying in his bed' and the giver has since died?
A. The gift is void because we cannot know if he recovered
B. We follow the majority — most sick people recover, so the gift is retracted
C. His death and grave prove he died from that illness, validating the deathbed gift
D. New witnesses must confirm the cause of death before the gift takes effect
Correct: C — Rabba says 'He is dead and his grave proves it' — presuming the giver died from the illness mentioned in the deed. This validates the deathbed gift. Abaye challenges this logic, but Rav Huna traces Rabba's view to Rabbi Natan's principle that current status reflects the status at the time of the gift.
