⚖️ House Collapse: Who Died First?

Husband's Heirs
The wife died first and was inherited by her husband. Therefore we now inherit everything from him — including her dowry.
Wife's Family
The husband died first. The wife survived him briefly. Therefore her ketubah and guaranteed property (nichsei tzon barzel) return to us.
The Core Problem A house collapsed on both husband and wife. The order of death is unknown. Two sets of heirs make competing inheritance claims — and neither can prove their case.

⚖️ Beit Shammai vs Beit Hillel — and the Amoraim

Beit Shammai
Divide the disputed property between the two sets of heirs — since neither can prove their claim, both receive a share.
Beit Hillel
The guaranteed property (nichsei tzon barzel) retains its previous ownership status. The ketubah sum remains in the possession of the husband's heirs.
Rabbi Yochanan
According to Beit Hillel, the guaranteed property remains with the husband's heirs — since the husband was responsible for it, it stays in his estate.
Reish Lakish (bar Kappara)
They divide the property. Since these heirs and those heirs both come to inherit, and neither can prove the claim — the property is split equally between them.
Key Term: Nichsei Tzon Barzel Property the wife brought into the marriage whose assessed value the husband guaranteed — even if it depreciated, he owed the full value. Literally: 'iron flock property.'

👩‍👦 Second Mishna: Son & Mother Collapse

Mother's Paternal Family

  • The son died first
  • Mother then survived briefly
  • Mother inherited son's estate
  • We inherit from mother
  • Property stays in mother's tribe

Son's Heirs

  • The mother died first
  • Son survived briefly
  • Son inherited from mother
  • We now inherit from son
  • Property passes through son's line

Rabbi Akiva's Ruling

  • Property retains prior ownership
  • Returns to mother's paternal tribe
  • Abaye: tribal land cannot be removed without proof
  • Preserves ancestral tribal inheritance
  • Ben Azzai objects to this new dispute

💰 Son Borrowing on Father's Future Estate

During father's lifetime: Son takes a loan and pledges his father's property as collateral — the son does not yet own this property
↓
Father dies: Son inherits the estate, now becoming the actual owner of the pledged property
↓
Problem: Father may have sold the land to a third-party buyer before his death, or the son sold it after inheriting
↓
Ruling from Eretz Yisrael: The son can repossess the property from buyers — his future inheritance gave him standing to create a valid lien
↓
Underlying principle: A person's anticipated inheritance (yerusha) can serve as the basis for a financial obligation even before the inheritance is received
