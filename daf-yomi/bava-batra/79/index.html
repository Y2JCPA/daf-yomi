⚖️ Does Consecrating a Container Consecrate Its Contents?

Rabbi Yehuda
If one consecrated an empty cistern or dovecote, the water or doves that later fill it are not consecrated — you cannot transfer ownership of something not yet in the world.
Rabbi Yosei
Even contents that arrive after consecration are included — especially for a field or tree whose produce is naturally expected to come.
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
Rabbi Yehuda is correct for cistern and dovecote; Rabbi Yosei is correct for field and tree — because produce of a field is anticipated, while rainwater filling a cistern is not.
Rava
If one consecrated a full cistern without specification, just as a seller can say 'I sold you the cistern but not the water,' so too the water is not consecrated — only the structure.
Mishna Principle One who sold a cistern has sold its water — but consecration follows different rules than sale.

🔥 Aggadeta: Straying from Torah

כָּל הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה — אֵשׁ אוֹכַלְתּוֹ
Rav (citing Rav Yehuda) & Rabbi Yonatan Anyone who separates himself from Torah is consumed by fire — as it is written: 'Out of the fire they come forth, and the fire shall devour them' (Ezekiel 15:7). Rabbi Yonatan adds: such a person falls into Gehenna — 'The man who strays from the way of understanding shall rest in the congregation of the dead' (Proverbs 21:16).

🪣 Consecrating When Empty vs. When Full

Consecrated Empty → Later Filled

  • Cistern: later water fills it
  • Dunghill: later manure fills it
  • Dovecote: doves arrive later
  • Field/Tree: produce grows later
  • ❓ Are the contents consecrated?

Rabbi Yehuda's View

  • Contents NOT consecrated
  • Cannot consecrate the non-existent
  • Cistern ✓ correct (Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi)
  • Dovecote ✓ correct
  • Field/Tree — Rabbi Yosei disagrees

Rabbi Yosei's View

  • Field/Tree contents ARE consecrated
  • Produce is naturally anticipated
  • Liable for misuse of consecrated property
  • Cistern/dovecote — agrees with Rabbi Yehuda
  • Water/doves are not inevitable

💧 Can You Consecrate the Non-Existent? — The Core Principle

First Tanna (Rabbis)
Ein adam makneh davar she-lo ba l'olam — a person cannot transfer ownership of something not yet in existence. Therefore, consecrating an empty cistern does not consecrate future water.
Rabbi Elazar b. Rabbi Shimon (Rabbi Meir)
Rabbi Meir holds that one can transfer something not yet in the world. But even so — the Gemara questions whether this applies to rainwater, which unlike fruit, is not naturally destined to fill the cistern.
Gemara's Distinction
Rabbi Meir's leniency may apply to palm fruit (likely to come), but rainwater filling a cistern is unpredictable — even Rabbi Meir might not extend his view there.
Key Term דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בָּא לָעוֹלָם (davar she-lo ba l'olam) — 'a thing not yet come into the world.' Central to whether future contents can be legally transferred or consecrated.
