Bava Batra 79 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav (citing Rav Yehuda), what happens to one who separates himself from Torah?
A. He loses his portion in the World to Come
B. A fire consumes him
C. He falls into poverty
D. He is judged by Heavenly court immediately
Correct: B — Rav derives from Ezekiel 15:7 that one who abandons Torah is consumed by fire — 'out of the fire they come forth, and the fire shall devour them.'
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to the Mishna (as cited in this daf), one who sells a cistern has also sold:
A. The land beneath it
B. The pipes leading to it
C. The water within it
D. Nothing beyond the cistern structure itself
Correct: C — The Mishna states: one who sold a cistern has sold its water — the contents are included in the sale by default.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi ruled that Rabbi Yehuda's view is correct regarding which cases of consecration?
A. Field and tree
B. Dunghills and fields
C. Cistern and dovecote
D. All cases equally
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi held that Rabbi Yehuda's position (contents not consecrated) is correct for cisterns and dovecotes, while Rabbi Yosei's position holds for fields and trees.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does the Gemara question whether Rabbi Meir's rule ('one can consecrate the non-existent') applies to rainwater in a cistern?
A. Because Rabbi Meir only spoke about animals, not water
B. Because rainwater is ownerless (hefker) and cannot be consecrated
C. Because unlike fruit of a palm tree, rainwater is not naturally destined to fill the cistern
D. Because the Rabbis explicitly excluded water from Rabbi Meir's rule
Correct: C — The Gemara distinguishes: Rabbi Meir may permit consecrating future palm fruit (likely to arrive), but rainwater filling a cistern is contingent and unpredictable — making it a weaker case.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rava explains that consecrating a 'full cistern without specification' does NOT include the water because:
A. Water has no monetary value for hekdesh purposes
B. Just as a seller can say 'I sold the cistern not the water,' consecration follows the same default
C. The Rabbis decreed that water is always excluded from consecration
D. A full cistern's water belongs to whoever drew it, not the owner
Correct: B — Rava applies the logic of ordinary transactions to hekdesh: since in a sale 'cistern' does not automatically include water (the seller can disclaim it), the same default applies when consecrating — the water is not automatically included.
