QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava, which condition makes a coerced sale of a specified field valid even without counting the money?
A. The sale was witnessed by a Jewish court
B. The seller verbally agreed before the coercion began
C. The seller had an escape route available but chose not to use it
D. The coercion was applied by a gentile, not a Jew
Correct: C — Rava rules that if the seller had a way to escape the coercion but did not use it, this is treated as a choice to sell — making the sale valid even for a specified field without money being counted.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why did Rav Mesharshiyya say a get given under gentile compulsion is valid by Torah law?
A. Gentile courts have the same legal authority as Jewish courts in monetary matters
B. The Torah only requires that a get be given voluntarily, and gentile courts ensure this
C. By Torah law coercion does not void a get; the Rabbis only added invalidation as a protective measure
D. A husband is always presumed willing to give a get since it fulfills a mitzva
Correct: C — Rav Mesharshiyya clarifies that the Mishna's invalidation of gentile-compelled gets is a rabbinic ruling, not a Torah-level one. The Torah itself considers the husband's will sufficient even under duress — the Rabbis added the restriction to prevent abuse of gentile courts.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the basis for Mar bar Rav Ashi's ruling that coerced betrothal is categorically invalid?
A. A woman must give explicit verbal consent for betrothal to take effect
B. The Torah requires a woman to willingly accept the betrothal ring
C. The Sages declared the man's betrothal money ownerless, removing the monetary foundation of the betrothal
D. Coercion in personal-status matters always invalidates the transaction
Correct: C — Mar bar Rav Ashi's reasoning is institutional: because the man acted improperly, the Sages retroactively declared his money ownerless (hefker). Since betrothal requires a valid transfer of money, and the money is now ownerless, no betrothal ever took effect.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Under what specific condition does Rav say that the purchase from the original owner (after buying from a Sicarius) is void?
A. Whenever a Sicarius was previously involved in controlling the property
B. When the original owner used language expressing resignation rather than genuine willingness to sell
C. When the buyer did not pay full market value to the original owner
D. When the Sicarius coercion occurred within the past twelve months
Correct: B — Rav explains that the original owner's subsequent sale is void only when the owner expressed resignation — saying something like 'go acquire what you already stole from me.' This language shows he was not genuinely selling but merely accepting a fait accompli, so no real transfer of will occurred.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What logical problem did Rav Huna bar Ḥinena identify in Rabba bar bar Ḥana's dual signing for Pafi?
A. A single witness cannot sign two different documents in the same transaction
B. The preemptive declaration and the bill of sale rest on mutually exclusive legal premises — each valid only if the other is void
C. Rabba bar bar Ḥana lacked standing as a witness because he was related to one of the parties
D. Documents signed under coercion are invalid regardless of their content
Correct: B — The challenge is one of logical contradiction: a valid preemptive declaration means the sale never went through, making a bill of sale pointless. But if the bill of sale is valid, the sale succeeded, making the declaration pointless. The same witness signing both implies one document must be fraudulent.
