⚖️ Four Opinions: Slave Sold with 30-Day Service Condition

Rabbi Meir

  • First master only is included in 'a day or two days'
  • Usufruct ownership = full ownership
  • Service rights negate second master's liability
  • Second master is exempt from punishment

Rabbi Yehuda

  • Second master only is included
  • Slave is 'his money' — second master's property
  • First master retains only service rights, not ownership
  • First master is exempt from punishment

Rabbi Yosei

  • Both masters are included
  • First: slave is under his authority
  • Second: slave is 'his money'
  • Each qualifies on a different basis — both exempt

Rabbi Elazar

  • Neither master is included — both face court-imposed death
  • Second: slave not under his authority
  • First: slave is not 'his money'
  • Neither master satisfies both conditions of the verse

🔑 Rabbi Elazar's Principle and Ameimar's Ruling

Rabbi Elazar
One can sell property only if he possesses both the item itself (title) and the right to enjoy its profits (usufruct). Neither alone is sufficient.
Ameimar
Neither the husband nor the wife can unilaterally sell usufruct property. If the husband sold and then died, the wife may come and remove the property from the buyer.
Gemara
Ameimar's ruling aligns with Rabbi Elazar: the husband holds usufruct only; the wife holds title only — neither alone possesses both elements required for a valid sale.
Conclusion
Where both sold together the sale is valid. Similarly, if the wife sold to her husband, the sale is valid — she voluntarily transferred her own title.
Core Principle A valid sale requires the unity of title and usufruct rights in a single seller

💨 Rav's Ruling: A Married Woman Must Protest

Rav's Statement: A married woman must lodge a protest against unauthorized use of her property or she may lose her claim.
↓
Question: Against whom — her husband or a stranger?
↓
Rava's Answer: Against her husband — specifically when he digs pits, ditches, and caves, which constitutes damaging use beyond his usufruct rights.
↓
Challenge: Rav Naḥman cited Rabba bar Avuh: 'There is no presumptive ownership with regard to damage' — so why must she protest?
↓
Resolution 1: Damage cannot create chazakah, but silence still forfeits her right to object. The rules operate on separate tracks.
↓
Rav Yosef's Alternative: Rav refers to protest against a stranger who used the property for part of the three-year chazakah period — she must interrupt that count.
↓
'Damage' Defined: Rav Mari = smoke; Rav Zevid = privies. These ongoing nuisances are the paradigm case of damage that cannot build chazakah.

כִּי כַסְפּוֹ הוּא — שמות כ״א:כ״א
📜 Talmudic Source — The Slave Sale Baraita Case: Reuven sells his Canaanite slave to Shimon, but contracts that the slave shall serve Reuven for 30 days before transfer to Shimon.

During those 30 days:
• First master (Reuven) — has authority and service rights, but slave is no longer 'his money'
• Second master (Shimon) — slave is 'his money,' but not under his authority

The Dispute: Exodus 21:21 exempts a master from court-imposed death if the slave survives 'a day or two days' because כִּי כַסְפּוֹ הוּא — 'he is his money.' Which master qualifies?

Rabbi Elazar: The verse requires both conditions — the slave must be his property and under his authority. Since neither master fulfills both conditions, neither is exempt — both would receive court-imposed capital punishment.
