⚖️ Who Gets Examined — Rav Huna vs. Rav Yehuda

Rav Huna
Do not examine one who asks for food — he is hungry and suffering immediately.
Examine only those who ask for clothing.
Rav Yehuda
Do not examine one who asks for clothing — he stands before us in rags, exposed to shame.
Examine only those who ask for food.
Both derive support two ways Each sage backs his ruling with (1) logical reasoning about suffering vs. shame, and (2) the verse in Isaiah 58:7 — 'Deal your bread to the hungry… and when you see the naked, cover him.'

אין מדקדקין עם גבאי צדקה
📜 Charity Collectors Are Trusted — No Audit Needed The Sages teach: One does not calculate sums with charity collectors for the money they received, nor with Temple treasurers for offerings they handled. They are presumed fully trustworthy — just as Moses and Aaron were never audited for the communal donations in the wilderness.

Rabbi Elazar adds: Even so, a prudent person should still tie up and count his own money before handing it over, as the king's scribe and the High Priest did when managing Temple funds (II Kings 12:11).

👕🍞 Food vs. Clothing — Opposite Rulings, Equal Logic

Rav Huna
Give food immediately — hunger is active suffering. One cannot eat tomorrow's meal today.

But examine clothing requests — the person who is cold today could have managed differently.
Rav Yehuda
Give clothing immediately — the one in rags stands before us in public contempt and disgrace.

But examine food requests — perhaps he has hidden provisions or family who can help.
Rav Pappa's lesson When Rav Pappa did not attend to a poor man at his door, Rav Sama rebuked him: 'If the Master does not attend to him, who will?' — urgency of immediate response.

✨ How Many Blessings? — Rabbi Yitzḥak

Gives a Peruta 💰

  • Receives 6 blessings
  • Source: Isaiah 58:7
  • 'Deal bread to the hungry…'
  • Practical act of material giving
  • Relieves physical need

Consoles with Words 🗣️

  • Receives 11 blessings
  • Source: Isaiah 58:10
  • 'Draw out your soul to the hungry…'
  • Giving of self — time, empathy, presence
  • Addresses emotional and spiritual suffering

🌟 Ascending Virtues of Charity — A Hierarchy

Minimum obligation (Rav Asi): Never give less than one-third of a shekel per year — derived from Nehemiah 10:33 where the returnees pledged this yearly charge for Temple service
↓
Facilitator > Giver (Rabbi Elazar): One who causes others to perform charity is greater than one who gives himself — the organizer multiplies the mitzvah
↓
Charity as armor (Isaiah 59:17): Just as armor is made of many interlocking scales — each one small but together impenetrable — charity accumulates from many small acts into a mighty protection
↓
Rava to the people of Meḥoza: Give charity to each other — it keeps you at peace with the government. Otherwise you will end up paying fines and taxes that exceed what you would have given willingly
↓
Legacy of the charitable (Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi): Those who habitually give merit sons who are masters of wisdom, wealth, and aggada
