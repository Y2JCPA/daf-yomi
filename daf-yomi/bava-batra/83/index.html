📏 How Must Three Trees Be Arranged?

Rav
The three trees may be planted in a straight line — the buyer still acquires the ground between them.
Shmuel
No — the trees must form a tripod (triangle), enclosing space between them, so that one cannot sow between them.
Rav Hamnuna
Challenge to Shmuel: If the reason is that one cannot sow between them, then even two trees should suffice — why require three?
Practical Stakes This dispute determines whether a buyer of trees planted in a row acquires the ground between them — affecting rights to small saplings growing there and the ability to retain land if a tree dies.

🔍 Two Analyses: Source of the 4-Cubit Minimum

Rav Yosef
The 4 cubits derive from vineyard work — enough for an ox and plow to turn without entering the neighbor's field. This follows Rabbi Shimon, who requires 8-cubit spacing between vineyard rows.
Rav Naḥman
The 4 cubits derive from the Tzalmon incident where Rav Yehuda ruled: sufficient land for an ox and its vessels. This follows the Rabbis, who permit 16-cubit spacing between vines.
Gemara
Even so, the Tzalmon incident is cited as the primary proof — a practical ruling carries more weight than a theoretical derivation from a mishna.
Key Asymmetry Rav Yosef's analysis establishes BOTH the 4-cubit minimum AND the 16-cubit maximum. Rav Naḥman's analysis establishes only the 16-cubit maximum — the minimum spacing remains unclear according to his view.

🌳 Rava's Ruling: Does the Buyer Acquire the Land?

One buys 3 trees planted in another's field
↓
Less than 4 cubits apart
Trees treated as part of the field — buyer does not acquire the land
4 to 16 cubits apart
Buyer acquires the land + any small trees growing between them
(Rava's combined ruling, favorable to buyer)
More than 16 cubits apart
Trees are too scattered — buyer does not acquire the land
↓
If land was acquired: tree later dries up or is cut down → buyer retains the land (acquisition already established)

⚖️ Rav Yosef vs. Rav Naḥman: What Each Analysis Establishes

Rav Yosef (per Rabbi Shimon)

  • ✅ Maximum spacing: 16 cubits (Tzalmon incident)
  • ✅ Minimum spacing: 4 cubits (vineyard work — ox & plow)
  • Source: Kilayim mishna — 8×8 cubit vineyard planting
  • Both upper AND lower limits are fully established
  • Preferred: cites a practical ruling (Tzalmon) as primary proof

Rav Naḥman (per the Rabbis)

  • ✅ Maximum spacing: 16 cubits (Tzalmon incident)
  • ❌ Minimum spacing: NOT established by his analysis
  • The Rabbis permit 16-cubit vineyard spacing — no 4-cubit rule
  • Minimum remains an open question on his view
  • Limitation: only one limit (upper) is derived

🐄 New Mishna (83b): Sale of Animals & Seller–Buyer Cases

Sale of Large Animal Parts: Selling the head does NOT include the forelegs; selling the forelegs does not include the head. Each limb is important in its own right and sold independently.
↓
Case 1 — Seller said wheat was good, found to be bad: The buyer may renege. The seller may not — he misrepresented the goods.
↓
Case 2 — Seller sold 'bad' wheat, found to be good: The seller may renege. The buyer received a windfall beyond what he paid for.
↓
Case 3 — Both knew quality and it matched: Neither party may renege — the sale stands exactly as agreed.
↓
Case 4 — Wrong type entirely (reddish wheat sold as white; olive wood sold as sycamore): Either party may renege, since the item is fundamentally different from what was transacted.
