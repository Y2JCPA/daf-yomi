⚖️ Contradictory Expressions: Who Controls?

Ben Nanas
When a contract contains contradictory expressions, follow the final (latter) expression. The last words spoken represent the party's true and final intent.
The Sages
When expressions contradict, divide the disputed amount equally between the parties. Neither expression fully controls; ambiguity demands splitting.
Rav (via Rav Abba bar Memel)
The Sages' disagreement with Ben Nanas is universal — not limited to the intercalated month case. Their 'divide' principle applies wherever contradictory terms arise.
Core Mishnaic Case A deed states 'measured precisely with a rope' then adds 'more or less.' Ben Nanas: the approximation ('more or less') wins — it was stated last.

⚖️ Ben Nanas vs. the Sages: The Foundational Dispute

Ben Nanas
Principle: Always follow the latter expression. A deed reading 'precise rope measurement' then 'more or less' — the approximation wins. Final words nullify earlier ones.
The Sages
Principle: Contradictory expressions create mutual uncertainty. Neither party gains the advantage — they split the difference. The amount in dispute is divided equally.
Rav
Rav stresses: even if we only had the intercalated month ruling, we might think the Sages limit their 'divide' principle to that specific case. Rav clarifies it is a general rule for all contradictory expressions.
Why Rav's Teaching Matters Without Rav's clarification, we might think the Sages only divided the intercalated month because of its unique ambiguity — not as a universal principle. Rav teaches otherwise.

📅 The Intercalated Month Rental Case

Rental Agreement: Tenant pays landlord twelve gold dinars per year
↓
Problem: A leap year occurs — the Jewish calendar intercalates a 13th month (Adar II). Does 'twelve dinars a year' cover this extra month?
↓
Tenant's Claim
'A year' means the calendar year as-is — I owe nothing extra for the 13th month.
Landlord's Claim
'Twelve dinars' means 12 monthly payments — a 13th month requires an additional payment.
↓
Ruling of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel & Rabbi Yosei: The landlord and tenant divide the disputed month's payment — consistent with the Sages' principle

🌾 Two Grain Sale Formulas — Different Legal Outcomes

Formula A: Bulk Price Only

  • Seller: 'I sell you a kor for 30 sela'
  • No per-unit price is specified
  • Buyer acquires the entire kor at once
  • If prices shift before delivery, it affects the whole deal
  • One unified transaction — single moment of acquisition

Formula B: Per-Unit Price Added

  • Seller: 'A kor for 30 sela, each se'a for 1 sela'
  • Per-unit price is explicitly stated
  • Buyer acquires each se'a as it is measured out
  • Seller cannot renege on portions already measured
  • Incremental acquisition — multiple mini-transactions

🔍 Shmuel's True Position — Unraveled by the Gemara

Shmuel's initial statement: 'This is the position of Ben Nanas — follow the latter expression.' The Gemara asks: does Shmuel himself agree with Ben Nanas?
↓
Apparent proof he does agree: Both Rav and Shmuel rule that when a seller says 'kor for 30 sela / each se'a for 1 sela,' the buyer acquires incrementally — seeming to follow the latter specification, like Ben Nanas.
↓
Resolution: The grain case is not truly about 'latter expression' — the per-unit pricing shows seller's intent to sell incrementally. It is independent logic, not an application of Ben Nanas's principle.
↓
Further proof against Shmuel agreeing with Ben Nanas: Shmuel separately rules that the intercalated month is only divided when the landlord came to demand rent mid-year — a contextual ruling, not 'follow the latter expression.'
↓
Conclusion: Shmuel agrees with the Sages — divide in genuine contradiction cases. He quoted Ben Nanas only to introduce the dispute, not to endorse it.
