QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Shmuel, why doesn't useful grass growing in a window reduce its dimensions for impurity purposes?
A. Grass is too thin to physically block impurity
B. Since it has a use, it will be removed and cannot serve as a permanent barrier
C. Grass is susceptible to ritual impurity and therefore disqualified
D. Grass does not occupy a full tefach of the window
Correct: B — Shmuel holds that any item with a practical use will be removed, so it cannot serve as a permanent barrier. The Gemara resolves the grass case specifically: this grass damages the wall, so the owner will remove it — making it effectively unusable as a barrier.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the Gemara conclude that a bird resting in a window DOES reduce its dimensions despite seemingly being removable?
A. It is a non-kosher bird that cannot be eaten or sold
B. It is a kelanita — a scratching bird that cannot be given to a child to play with
C. Birds are not susceptible to ritual impurity and are therefore ignored
D. The bird is sleeping at night and physically cannot fly away
Correct: B — The Gemara works through multiple objections: the bird could fly away, be given to a child, or be sold to a gentile. It concludes it is a kelanita — it scratches, so children won't want it; and it is non-kosher and unsellable. Thus it has no practical use.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What does Abaye derive from the mishna stating the oven base requires 3 handbreadths from the wall while the oven rim requires 4?
A. The base of an oven is made of thicker material than the rim
B. The base extends one handbreadth beyond the rim on each side
C. The rim generates significantly more heat than the base
D. Both the base and rim measurements refer to the same structural element
Correct: B — Abaye deduces from the one handbreadth difference in required distance that the base must physically extend one handbreadth further than the rim on each side — otherwise the same measurement would apply to both parts.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Rav rules that one may build a Shabbat barrier with anything EXCEPT two things. What are they?
A. Water and fire
B. Salt and water
C. Salt and fire
D. Mud and ash
Correct: C — Rav specifically excludes salt and fire as valid barrier materials for Shabbat domains or blocking impurity. Rabba later refines this: while salt alone is invalid, salt that mutually supports a cross beam (each holding the other) may constitute a valid combined barrier.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
The mishna generally prohibits a bakery beneath a neighbor's storeroom. What is the stated exception to this rule?
A. If the storeroom contains wine, the bakery is permitted because heat and smoke improve wine
B. If the bakery was established before the storeroom was built
C. If the storeroom owner signs a written waiver of objection
D. If the bakery operates only during daylight hours
Correct: A — The Gemara explains that heat and smoke actually improve wine quality. Therefore, a bakery operating beneath a wine storeroom is permitted — the very damage that would harm other goods becomes a benefit to wine.
