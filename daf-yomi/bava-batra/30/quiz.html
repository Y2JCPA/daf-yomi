QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why was there no contradiction between Rava's two rulings on the bar Sisin land dispute?
A. Rava issued one ruling before and one after consulting Rav Naḥman
B. In the bar Sisin case the seller had established prior possession; in the other case he had not
C. The bar Sisin case involved a house while the other involved a field
D. The bar Sisin buyer produced a written deed, while the other buyer did not
Correct: B — The Gemara resolves the contradiction by noting a critical factual difference: in the bar Sisin case, the seller was already established as owning the land (it bore his name). That prior chazakah validated his claim. Without it, the ruling differs.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
When a possessor claims to have bought land from a third party (not the original owner), what does Rava rule?
A. The possessor must immediately vacate until he proves his title
B. The chazakah period must restart from the date of litigation
C. The original owner is not legally answerable to the possessor — the possessor should claim against the third party
D. The original owner must disprove that any sale ever occurred
Correct: C — Rava rules that the owner who says 'I never sold to YOU — I am not legally answerable to you' states the halakha correctly. The owner has no direct legal relationship with someone who bought from a third party.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does the owner explain his advice to the possessor to 'go purchase the land'?
A. He denies ever speaking to the possessor
B. He says he was coerced into giving the advice
C. He says he advised the purchase only to protect the possessor from financial loss — not as a concession that the seller had authority
D. He argues verbal statements are not admissible as legal evidence
Correct: C — The owner explains his counsel was financially protective advice, not a legal concession. Telling someone 'buy it' to avoid loss does not mean conceding that the person selling had valid title to convey.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does the Gemara distinguish Rava's ruling from the Admon principle in tractate Ketubot?
A. Rava explicitly adopts Admon's view and the cases are identical
B. In the Ketubot case the claimant signed a bill of sale — an action creating estoppel; here the owner only gave verbal advice
C. The Ketubot case involves movable property while this case involves land
D. Admon's view was rejected by all Sages and cannot be applied anywhere
Correct: B — The Gemara distinguishes: signing a document is a concrete legal action that creates estoppel (you cannot contest what you formally endorsed). Verbal advice to buy is not an action — so even the Rabbis would not apply estoppel in this case.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Under what condition does a possessor's chazakah prevail over a claimant's written document, according to the end of the daf?
A. When the possessor has two witnesses to the original purchase
B. When the possessor paid fair market value for the property
C. When the possessor profited from the land for seven years, so that his chazakah preceded the document
D. When the document lacks a proper signature from two valid witnesses
Correct: C — The Gemara states this applies only when the possessor profited for seven years — meaning his presumptive ownership (chazakah) began before the claimant's document was issued. When chazakah precedes the competing deed, possession stands.
