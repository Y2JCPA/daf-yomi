👑 King Munbaz: Four Defenses of Giving Away Treasures

מונבז המלך עמד ובזבז כל אוצרותיו ואוצרות אבותיו בשני שחורות
Aggadic Story — Amud A King Munbaz distributed all his ancestral treasures to the poor during a drought. His family protested: 'Your ancestors added to the treasury and you are dispersing it!' He replied with four distinctions: • My ancestors stored below (earthly vault) — I store above (heavenly merit) • My ancestors stored where hands can reach — I store where no hand can reach • My ancestors stored money that yields no profit — I store what generates profit (Isaiah: 'say of the righteous it shall be well with them') • My ancestors stored for this world — I stored for the World to Come

🏠 Residency: When Are You 'One of the People of the City'?

Baraita #1 — General Rule

  • Must reside in city for 12 months
  • Then obligated to pay city taxes
  • Applies to: wall, gates, water supply
  • Mere presence, not purchase

Rabban Shimon b. Gamliel

  • Bought land suitable for residence
  • Immediately = resident of the city
  • Purchase signals permanent intent
  • No waiting period required

Reconciliation

  • No real contradiction exists
  • Baraita #1: renting / temporary dwelling
  • R. Shimon b. G.: purchase = permanence
  • Both rulings can stand together

📐 Dividing a Courtyard: How Are the Cubits Counted?

Mishna
A courtyard is not divided unless each owner receives at least 4×4 cubits. If insufficient space exists for both, the court cannot compel division.
Rabbi Asi / Rabbi Yoḥanan
The 4 cubits in the mishna are in addition to the 4 cubits in front of each house entrance. Those entrance cubits belong to the house owner regardless of the division.
Rav Huna
A courtyard is divided according to its entrances — not simply split in half. More entrances = larger proportional share of the courtyard.
Rav Ḥisda
Each entrance is allotted 4 cubits. If one owner has 1 entrance and the other has 2, the one-entrance owner takes 4 cubits; the two-entrance owner takes 8; the remainder is split equally. A baraita supports this view.
Practical Formula (Rav Ḥisda) 4 cubits reserved per entrance → remaining courtyard divided equally between owners.

🛤️ Alleyway Rights: Can Residents Block Changes?

Setup: Multiple courtyards open onto a shared alleyway. Residents have collective usage rights throughout the alleyway.
↓
Scenario A — Moving an Entrance: A resident wants to redirect his entrance to a different alleyway. Rav Huna: current alleyway residents can prevent him — he diminishes their collective rights.
↓
Scenario B — Closing Off Space: A resident wants to enclose area adjacent to his entrance within the alleyway. Rav Huna: other residents can prevent him from narrowing the shared space.
↓
Key Exception — Restoring Prior Entrance: If an entrance existed there previously and was sealed, residents of the other alleyway cannot prevent him from reopening it. He is restoring an established right, not creating a new burden.
↓
Unresolved Question (Rav Huna): When a king billeting order applies to a courtyard — is the burden divided by number of persons per household or by number of houses? The Gemara leaves this unresolved.
