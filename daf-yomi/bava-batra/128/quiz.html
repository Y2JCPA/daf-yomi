QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the baraita on 128a, when is a witness disqualified from testifying?
A. Only if he was blind at the time of the event he witnessed
B. Only if he is blind at the time he actually testifies in court
C. If he was unfit (blind or disqualified) at either the time of the event OR at testimony
D. Only if he became blind as a direct result of witnessing the event
Correct: C — The baraita's principle covers both endpoints: anyone whose initial state (at the incident) or ultimate state (at testimony) is one of disqualification is disqualified. Both the moment of witnessing and the moment of testifying must occur while the person is fit.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does Rava (as opposed to Abaye) interpret Rabbi Abba's ruling that a father is 'deemed credible' regarding 'one child among his sons'?
A. A father may give all his property to one son, overriding normal inheritance rules
B. A father who declares which son is his firstborn (bechor) is believed
C. A father's character testimony about any son is always accepted in court
D. A father may exclude one troublesome son entirely from the inheritance
Correct: B — Rava explains the ruling as following Rabbi Yehuda (127b): a father is deemed credible to identify which son is his firstborn. Abaye's reading — about giving all property to one son — is rejected by Rava as linguistically imprecise, since the language 'deemed credible' fits an identity question, not a transfer of property.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Rav Sheshet's view on a blind person's capacity to testify, and how is it ultimately resolved?
A. He permits it only for land boundary disputes; this ruling is accepted
B. He permits it for all matters since a blind person can identify people by voice; this is refuted by the baraita
C. He forbids it entirely; his ruling is accepted as the halakha
D. He permits it only if the person was never fully sighted; this is debated but unresolved
Correct: B — Rav Sheshet held that a blind person can identify individuals by voice and is therefore fit to testify even about personal matters — a more permissive stance than Shmuel's. However, the baraita conclusively refutes both Shmuel and Rav Sheshet: a witness must be fit at both the time of witnessing and the time of testifying.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Under what condition does Rava qualify Rabbi Abba's ruling that a wife receives 'a portion like one of the sons'?
A. Only if the declaration was made before two valid witnesses
B. Only if the wife had no children of her own from a prior marriage
C. Only when there are sons (heirs) present — since her share is defined relative to theirs
D. Only if the husband wrote the declaration in a valid legal document (shtar)
Correct: C — Rava clarifies that the wife's entitlement to 'a portion like a son' only applies when there are actual sons in the estate, because the gift is defined proportionally to the sons' shares. Without sons, the language has no referent and the gift cannot be calculated.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In the promissory note case on 128b — creditor claims nothing was repaid, debtor claims half was repaid, and witnesses confirm half was repaid — what is the ruling?
A. The creditor collects nothing, since witnesses contradict his claim
B. The creditor collects the full amount, since a promissory note carries presumptive force
C. The debtor swears about the admitted portion and is thereby freed from the disputed remainder
D. The case is left unresolved (teiku) due to conflicting testimony
Correct: C — Since the debtor admits that half was repaid and the witnesses corroborate this, the debtor is in the position of modeh b'miktzat (admitting to part of a claim). He takes an oath on the admitted half, which frees him from the creditor's claim to the remaining disputed amount. This is Rabbi Abba's ruling upheld by Mar Zutra.
