👁️ Can a Blind Person Testify About What He Once Saw?

Shmuel
A blind person is fit to testify about land boundaries — he can still determine them by touch and memory. But he cannot identify a movable item like a cloak.
Rav Sheshet
He can even identify people by voice! Therefore he is fit to testify even about personal matters — his blindness does not disqualify him.
Baraita (Tosefta, Sanhedrin)
If he was fit at the time of the event but disqualified at the time of testimony — he is disqualified. The principle: both the initial state AND the final state must be fit!
Gemara — Final Ruling
The refutation of all amora'im who permitted blind witness testimony in any form is a conclusive refutation (תיובתא). The baraita's principle is binding.
⚖️ The Baraita's Principle Anyone whose initial state (at the incident) OR ultimate state (at testimony) is one of disqualification — is disqualified from bearing witness.

👨‍👦 Abaye vs. Rava: What Does Rabbi Abba's Ruling Mean?

Abaye's Reading

  • Rabbi Abba: Father who gives ALL property to one son is 'deemed credible'
  • Follows Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka: father may designate any legal heir
  • Rabbi Yoḥanan disagrees: father cannot override standard inheritance rules
  • ⚠️ Rava objects: Abaye's language is imprecise — the issue is inheritance, not credibility!

Rava's Reading (Accepted Halakha)

  • Rabbi Abba: Father who declares one son to be his FIRSTBORN is 'deemed credible'
  • Follows Rabbi Yehuda (Daf 127b): father's word establishes bechor status
  • Rabbi Yoḥanan: Father is NOT credible to identify the firstborn
  • ✅ The term 'deemed credible' is precise — it is about establishing legal identity, not transferring property
