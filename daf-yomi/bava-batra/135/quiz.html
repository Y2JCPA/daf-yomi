QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yoḥanan (cited by Rava), when a husband declares 'I have no brothers,' what is the legal consequence for his wife?
A. She must still perform ḥalitza before remarrying
B. She is exempt from yibum because a husband is deemed credible about his own family status
C. Her status is unresolved until two witnesses confirm there are no brothers
D. She is obligated in yibum unless a court investigates independently
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan rules that a husband is believed about his own marital and family status — extending the principle that a husband who says 'I divorced my wife' is credible. Rava applies this to exempt the wife from levirate marriage.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
When brothers claim 'we do not know' whether a man is their brother, what is their obligation regarding inheritance according to Rava?
A. They must share equally until the claim is disproved
B. They must set aside a portion pending investigation
C. They are exempt from sharing their portion — uncertainty creates no obligation
D. They must go to court and the court decides
Correct: C — Rava derives that 'I don't know' is a valid defense — just as in financial claims where a defendant who says 'I don't know' is exempt, brothers who genuinely don't know owe no share from their own portions.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why is a will found bound to a dead man's thigh ruled invalid by the Mishna?
A. The will was not signed by the deceased himself
B. No act of kinyan (formal transfer) was performed during the man's lifetime
C. The will lacked witnesses
D. A will found after death is always inadmissible
Correct: B — The will's authenticity is not the issue — even an undeniably genuine will is void without a kinyan. Property transfer requires a legally recognized act during the giver's lifetime, not merely a written expression of intent.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What specific phrase is required to make a deed of gift valid for a person on their deathbed?
A. 'This gift shall be honored after I pass from the world'
B. 'I give my property to so-and-so upon my death'
C. 'From today and after my death'
D. 'Let this deed stand as my final will'
Correct: C — The phrase מהיום ולאחר מיתה ('from today and after my death') establishes that the transfer takes legal effect immediately — the recipient has a present ownership right, with actual possession deferred until death.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak conclude it was Rabbi Elazar — not Rabbi Yoḥanan — who originated the ruling about writing deeds for the dying?
A. Rav Naḥman found a manuscript explicitly attributing it to Rabbi Elazar
B. It would be odd for Rabbi Elazar to affirm a ruling of his own teacher; a teacher affirming a student's ruling makes more sense
C. Rabbi Yoḥanan never discussed property transfer law
D. Ravin stated explicitly that Rabbi Yoḥanan disagreed with the ruling
Correct: B — The logic of authority supports Rabbi Elazar as originator: a junior scholar affirming a senior's ruling is unusual and redundant, but a teacher (Rabbi Yoḥanan) affirming a student's (Rabbi Elazar's) novel ruling is natural and meaningful.
