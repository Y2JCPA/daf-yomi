⚖️ Levirate Marriage: Is the Dying Man's Claim Reliable?

Rava
A husband who says 'I have no brothers' is believed — his wife is exempt from yibum. Rabbi Yoḥanan rules: a husband who says 'I divorced my wife' is deemed credible; the same applies here.
Abaye
Shall we arise and rely on mere theoretical answers to issue a halakhic ruling? The original presumption of yibum obligation remains; we cannot dismiss it based on an unverified resolution.
Rav Yosef
The witnesses who could contradict the man are not present before us. This parallels Rabbi Ḥanina's leniency regarding women released from captivity — when direct contrary evidence is absent, we rule leniently.
Abaye
A captive woman actively makes herself repulsive before her captor — that is why we assume she was not violated. There is no comparable reason here to extend leniency.
Core Question When a man presumed to have brothers dies claiming otherwise, is his wife automatically released from levirate marriage?

👥 'I Don't Know' — Inheritance vs. Financial Claims

Inheritance: Brothers Say 'Lo Yadaʿnu'

  • Unknown man claims to be a brother of the deceased
  • Other brothers respond: 'We don't know if he's our brother'
  • Result: They are not obligated to share their portion with him
  • But: if he later acquires property elsewhere and dies, they inherit
  • Rava: Uncertainty = no obligation, like a monetary 'I don't know'

Finance: Defendant Says 'I Don't Know'

  • Claimant: 'You have 100 dinars of mine in your possession'
  • Defendant: 'I don't know if that is true'
  • Rava: He is exempt — uncertainty does not create obligation
  • Abaye: The cases differ — brother case involves shared family estate
  • Abaye: In finance, defendant may actually be obligated despite uncertainty

📋 Dayetikei: What Makes a Deathbed Will Valid?

Definition (Tosefta 8:10): A dayetikei is collected after death only if it states: 'This will stand and exist after my death'
↓
Invalid (Mishna): A will found bound to a dead man's thigh — written without a kinyan (act of transfer) — is void even if undeniably authentic
↓
Valid Deed of Gift: Must include 'from today and after my death' — establishing the transfer takes effect immediately, with possession deferred
↓
Abaye clarifies: The baraita means 'from now' is sufficient — any language grounding the transfer in the present, before death, validates the deed
↓
Rav Dimi's Two Rules: (1) A later dayetikei cancels an earlier one; (2) A deed of gift can cancel a dayetikei — priority follows sequence

✍️ Who Said the Deed Ruling — Rabbi Elazar or Rabbi Yoḥanan?

Rabba bar Rav Huna
In the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan: A dying man who says 'Write a deed and give 100 dinars to so-and-so' — we write and give, because his precise wording must be examined carefully.
Rav Sheizevi
No — the attribution is reversed. Rabbi Elazar said this halakha; Rabbi Yoḥanan was the one who affirmed it. The tradition was transmitted incorrectly.
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
Rav Sheizevi's version is more logical: if Rabbi Elazar ruled, it makes sense for Rabbi Yoḥanan to affirm it. But why would Rabbi Elazar need to affirm a ruling of his own teacher?
Ravin (from Eretz Yisrael)
Rabbi Abbahu confirms it: Rabbi Elazar sent this ruling to Babylonia citing 'our teacher.' That phrasing proves Rabbi Elazar originated the halakha and attributed it upward to Rabbi Yoḥanan.
The Ruling Itself For a dying person: write the deed and give the money. For a healthy person who makes the same request and then dies: do not write or give — his conditional intent expired.
