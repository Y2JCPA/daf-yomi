📐 Ulla's 16-Cubit Rule: Square or Circle?

Ulla: Tree roots extend 16 cubits from the trunk
↓
Problem: 16×16 square = 1,024 sq. cubits, but beit se'a allows only 833⅓ per tree
↓
Rejected Resolution
Ulla was simply imprecise — but can we accept imprecision leading to leniency?
Accepted Resolution
Measure in a circle, not a square — circle = ¾ of square = 768 sq. cubits ✓ fits within 833⅓

📐 Square vs. Circle: How to Measure Root Spread

Square Measurement

  • 16 cubits in each of four directions
  • Area = 16 × 16 = 1,024 sq. cubits
  • Exceeds beit se'a share of 833⅓ sq. cubits
  • Creates an unsolvable contradiction with Ulla
  • Would require accepting imprecision toward leniency

Circle Measurement

  • Circle inscribed within 32×32 square
  • A circle = ¾ of its bounding square
  • ¾ × 1,024 = 768 sq. cubits
  • 768 < 833⅓ — fits within beit se'a
  • Resolves the contradiction precisely and elegantly

🌍 Conflicting Reports: Tree Near the Border of Eretz Yisrael

Rav Dimi
Reish Lakish asked Rabbi Yohanan: A tree within 16 cubits of the border — what is the law? Rabbi Yohanan answered: He does not bring bikkurim and does not recite the verses — the produce grew on 'borrowed' land.
Ravin
Rabbi Yohanan says: Both a tree near the border AND a tree that leans into a neighbor's yard — one brings bikkurim AND recites the verses, for it was on this very tree that the obligation fell.
Gemara
These two traditions of Rabbi Yohanan directly contradict each other. The question remains unresolved. A separate dispute: Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds the air above mixes nutrients; Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds 'this side stands on its own.'
Core Question Does drawing nutrients from a neighbor's land through roots disqualify the fruit from bikkurim, or does the tree's physical location alone determine the obligation?

🐂 Cutting Overhanging and Leaning Trees: The Rules

General Rule (Mishna): A tree leaning into a neighbor's field — branches cut to height of an ox goad raised above the plow (enough for plowing)
↓
Abba Shaul — Non-irrigated field: Refers to the first clause; barren trees specifically are cut entirely along the plumb line (fruit trees only to ox-goad height)
↓
Abba Shaul — Irrigated field: ALL trees, even fruit trees, are cut along the plumb line, because shade is harmful to an irrigated field
↓
Rav Ashi's linguistic proof: Mishna says 'all barren trees' — this precise language only makes sense if Abba Shaul is adding to the first clause about non-irrigated fields
↓
Public Domain (Mishna): Cut branches so a laden camel with rider can pass; Rabbi Yehuda: camel loaded with flax or bundles of branches
↓
Rabbi Shimon: Cut ALL branches over public domain along the plumb line — due to tum'at ohel (tent impurity from corpse bones that might land on branches)

🐪 Whose Opinion Does the Mishna Reflect on Future Damage?

Reish Lakish
The mishna cutting branches to camel height reflects Rabbi Eliezer, who holds we assess damage currently and do not penalize for future risk. Rabbi Eliezer similarly permits digging under the public domain while the cover is still strong.
Rabbi Yohanan
Even the Rabbis agree here! Underground tunnel covers will eventually rot — hence Rabbis prohibit it. But a tree only grows larger, so cutting to current camel height is sufficient — the hazard never worsens beyond the current trim.
Gemara
Proof the Rabbis' measure (camel + rider) is GREATER than Rabbi Yehuda's (camel + flax): If Yehuda's were greater, the Rabbis' standard would always cover it — both must serve distinct scenarios, with the Rabbis being more lenient overall.
Halakhic Principle We assess damage based on the current situation. Where future deterioration is certain (rotting wood), we penalize now. Where it is not (a living tree), current compliance suffices.
