📋 Mishna: Rules of the 'From Today' Gift Document

Scenario: Healthy person gifts property to sons but wishes to use it until death
↓
Required language (Tanna Kamma): 'I give this property from today and after my death'
↓
Resulting split: Father cannot sell (written to son); son cannot sell (father still possesses it)
↓
If father sold: Buyer receives usufruct only until father dies — corpus then passes to son
↓
If son sold during father's life: Buyer acquires nothing until father's death — validity disputed in Gemara

⚖️ Must One Write 'From Today'? Tanna Kamma vs. Rabbi Yosei

Tanna Kamma
A healthy person gifting property to his sons must write 'from today and after my death.' Without this phrase, the transfer cannot take effect at death — it looks like a deathbed gift.
Rabbi Yosei
He need not write 'from today and after my death.' A standard deed has its own legal force. The written document itself establishes that the gift takes effect immediately — no special clause is needed.
Rav (via Rabba bar Avuh)
The halakha follows Rabbi Yosei. His reasoning: writing conveys a present transfer — the 'from today' language is already implied in any deed of gift.
Halakhic Ruling Rav rules: Halakha follows Rabbi Yosei — no 'from today' required

🤝 Does a Kinyan (Act of Acquisition) Eliminate 'From Today'?

Rava asks Rav Naḥman: If the father performs a kinyan transferring property to his son after his death — must he still write 'from today and after my death'?
↓
Rav Naḥman: In a case of kinyan, he need not write 'from today' — the kinyan itself establishes present transfer
↓
Rav Pappi — Distinction A
'He transferred it AND we acquired from him'
→ Must still write 'from today'
kinyan supplemented the deed
Rav Pappi — Distinction B
'We acquired from him AND he transferred to him'
→ Need NOT write 'from today'
kinyan preceded the deed
↓
Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua: Either order — any kinyan always eliminates the need to write 'from today'

🏛️ Son Sells Father's Property During Father's Lifetime — Valid?

Rabbi Yoḥanan
The purchaser did NOT acquire the property. Ownership of usufruct (the right to use and collect produce) is considered equivalent to ownership of the item itself. Therefore, the father's retain of produce rights effectively blocks the son from selling.
Reish Lakish
The purchaser DID acquire the property. Usufruct rights are NOT equivalent to ownership of the item itself. The father only holds produce rights; the corpus belongs fully to the son, who may sell it freely.
Gemara
This mirrors their earlier dispute: does one who sells a field for its produce retain the field for first-fruits purposes? Yoḥanan says yes (usufruct = ownership); Reish Lakish says no. Both disputes needed to be stated for distinct new-case reasoning.
Core Question Is ownership of peiros (produce/usufruct) equivalent to ownership of the guf (corpus)?

🌾 Peiros = Ownership? The Two Fundamental Views

Rabbi Yoḥanan — Peiros IS Like Ownership

  • Father's usufruct = full legal control of the property
  • Son cannot transfer corpus while father holds produce rights
  • Son's sale during father's life: purchaser acquires nothing
  • One who sells a field retains first-fruits obligation
  • Sequential gift: heirs of first recipient inherit (not third)

Reish Lakish — Peiros is NOT Like Ownership

  • Father holds only a produce right — son owns the corpus
  • Son's ownership of corpus is complete and transferable
  • Son's sale during father's life: purchaser acquires it
  • One who sells produce of his field: no first-fruits obligation
  • Sequential gift: property returns to giver's heirs (not first recipient's)
