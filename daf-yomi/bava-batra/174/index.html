🔐 Standard Guarantor vs. Unconditional Guarantor

Rav Ḥisda
All expressions are unconditional guarantees (קבלן kablan) — EXCEPT: 'Lend money to him and I am a guarantor'
Rava
All expressions are standard guarantees (ערב arev) — EXCEPT: 'Give money to him and I will give the money back to you'
Rabbi Yitzḥak
Follow the primary language used: 'lend' language = standard guarantee; 'give' language = unconditional — regardless of surrounding wording
Mar bar Ameimar
'Give to him and I will repay' = creditor has no claim against debtor at all — his only recourse is the guarantor himself
Core Distinction Standard guarantor (ערב): creditor must first pursue debtor, then guarantor. Unconditional guarantor (קבלן): creditor may collect directly from guarantor without pursuing debtor first.

👶 Orphans & Guarantor: Two Reasons for the Same Ruling

Rav Pappa

  • Repaying a creditor is a mitzva
  • Minor orphans are not obligated in mitzvot
  • Therefore exempt from repaying father's debts until majority
  • If father admits on deathbed he didn't pay:
  • → Orphans still exempt — mitzva argument unchanged
  • Practical upshot: guarantor must always wait for majority

Rav Huna b. Rav Yehoshua

  • Orphans exempt because: maybe father already paid
  • Father may have given 'bundles of money' before death
  • Guarantor cannot prove father did not settle the debt
  • If father admits on deathbed he didn't pay:
  • → Orphans must repay — doubt is resolved
  • Gentile creditor: always assume paid; no deathbed repayment

⚖️ The Case of Rav Huna & His Father's Guarantee

Rav Huna's father guaranteed his son's marriage contract (כתובה)
↓
Rav Huna later divorced his wife — she sought to collect the ketubah from the father-guarantor (Moshe bar Atzari)
↓
Abaye's advice: Let Rav Huna divorce her conditionally — so she gets the ketubah but the marriage remains intact, sparing the guarantor
↓
Revealed: Rav Huna was a priest (כהן) — he cannot remarry a divorcée, even his own ex-wife!
↓
Abaye said: This confirms the adage — 'If you don't know, learn from your neck' (i.e., reality teaches what reasoning misses)
↓
Abaye's principle vs. his advice: Advising one's own son is not 'wickedness'; helping a Torah scholar obtain money is not cunning wickedness

💀 Deathbed Admission After Consecrating All Property

Rav Huna
A dying person who consecrated all his property, then declared: 'So-and-so has 100 dinars in my possession' — is believed; the creditor collects from the consecrated property
Rav Naḥman
Objection: Would a person collude against his own children? Rav and Shmuel rule: a dying man's admission is only trusted if he made it while still healthy enough to stand and walk
The Tension A dying man's word carries the legal weight of a written document (שכיב מרע). But when property is already consecrated (הקדש), does his admission divert funds from the Temple treasury to a private creditor — and can we trust him not to collude against his heirs?
