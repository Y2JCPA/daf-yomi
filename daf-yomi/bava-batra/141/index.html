⚖️ Can a Fetus Acquire a Gift?

Rav Huna
One who transfers property to a fetus — the fetus does not acquire it. The gift simply does not take effect.
Rav Naḥman
Challenge from the mishna! 'If my wife gives birth to a male, he shall receive 100 dinars' — and he does receive it. Does the fetus not acquire the gift at conception?
Rav Huna
The mishna follows Rabbi Meir, or Rabbi Yosei, or Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka. Or: the gift was made after birth but before the sex was known.
Gemara
Each authority is rejected in turn: Rabbi Meir speaks of transferring to existing persons; Rabbi Yosei's ruling covers inheritance only; Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka addresses existing entities — none extend to a fetus receiving a gift.
Core Distinction Inheritance comes to the heir by itself — no kinyan required — so a fetus can inherit. A gift requires a formal act of acquisition, which a fetus cannot perform.

👶 Rav Ḥisda's Sign — The First-Born Daughter

יָלְדָה נְקֵבָה תְּחִלָּה — סִימָן יָפֶה לְבָנִים
אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא Rav Ḥisda teaches: If a woman gives birth to a daughter first, it is a good sign that sons will follow.

This explains the mishna's surprising structure: the father offers 200 dinars for a daughter and only 100 for a son — he celebrates the daughter as a harbinger of future sons, expressing his joy with greater generosity.

Shmuel applies this specifically to a first-time mother.

⚖️ Inheritance vs. Gift — Fetal Acquisition

Inheritance (יְרוּשָׁה)

  • Passes to heir automatically by force of law
  • No formal act of acquisition (קִנְיָן) required
  • Fetus CAN inherit
  • Source: Rabbi Yosei (Yevamot 67a) — slave-owner's fetus inherits slaves
  • The heir need not 'do' anything — the estate flows to him

Gift (מַתָּנָה)

  • Requires a formal act of acquisition by the recipient
  • Fetus has no hands, no legal capacity to perform kinyan
  • Fetus CANNOT acquire a gift
  • Rav Huna's ruling — gift to fetus is void
  • Even Rabbi Yosei's leniency does NOT extend here

🔄 Why Does a Father Prefer the Daughter? Resolving the Contradiction

Question
The mishna implies the father prefers a daughter (200 dinars) over a son (100 dinars). But for inheritance a son is clearly preferred — he carries the father's name and tribal heritage. Contradiction!
Gemara
No contradiction. Inheritance: son preferred — he perpetuates the family name and holds the ancestral portion within the tribe. Monetary provision: daughter preferred — she needs a dowry and the father worries more about her future.
Shmuel
The mishna speaks of a first-time mother. Per Rav Ḥisda: a first-born daughter is a good sign for sons! The father joyfully gives more for the daughter who heralds sons to come.
Gemara
Or: the mishna follows Rabbi Yehuda, who rules a father is obligated to sustain children only until age six. Daughters require ongoing provision for marriage — justifying the larger conditional gift.
Rabbi Yehuda's Rule A father is not legally obligated to provide sustenance for his children beyond age six — yet daughters typically require greater support for marriage, explaining why the father's conditional gift is larger for a daughter.
