📖 Overview

קנאת סופרים תרבה חכמה — Kohelet 4:4 / Bava Batra 22a
📚 קנאת סופרים תרבה חכמה Daf 22 opens with the principle that jealousy among Torah scholars increases wisdom, then works through commercial competition law (can townspeople bar outside merchants?), the dramatic saga of Rav Dimi's rotting dried figs and Rav Adda bar Abba's mysterious death — with four sages claiming responsibility — before transitioning to the Mishna's construction laws: four cubits between walls, four cubits from windows, and four cubits from dovecotes.

🏪 Can Townspeople Bar Outside Merchants?

Rav Huna b. Rav Yehoshua
Townspeople can bar craftsmen from other cities — they have a right to protect their livelihood from outside competition entering their market.
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
Even Rav Huna concedes: traveling perfume sellers cannot be barred — women depend on them, so it is a public benefit that overrides local merchant rights.
Ravina
Basket sellers from outside Babylonia may not be barred — they came from the river region and their trade is essential to the local supply chain.
Rav Kahana
Wool sellers at Pum Nahara may sell freely. The halakha permits outside merchants when their goods serve the broader regional need.
⭐ Special Rule A Torah scholar who trades is granted exclusive market rights — the Exilarch ordered Rava: if he is a Torah scholar, reserve the entire market for him alone.

🐟 The Story of Rav Dimi's Dried Figs

Setup: Rav Dimi of Neharde'a sails dried figs to the market, hoping to sell them before they rot.
↓
Opportunity: The Exilarch instructs Rava: "If he is a Torah scholar, reserve the entire market for him exclusively."
↓
The Test: Rava sends Rav Adda bar Abba to evaluate Rav Dimi's scholarship. Rav Adda poses a difficult ritual impurity question: "If an elephant swallowed a wicker basket and excreted it intact — is it still susceptible to tumah?"
↓
Failure: Rav Dimi cannot answer. Rav Adda concludes he is not a genuine Torah scholar and refuses to reserve the market for him.
↓
Consequence: Without market exclusivity, Rav Dimi's figs rot unsold. He goes before Rav Yosef to complain that Rav Adda caused his financial ruin.
↓
Aftermath: Rav Adda bar Abba dies. Four sages each claim their own curse or grievance caused his death.
5 / 9

⚰️ Four Claimants — Who Caused Rav Adda bar Abba's Death?

Rav Yosef

  • "I punished him" — I cursed him
  • Rav Adda showed disrespect to Rav Dimi, a guest scholar
  • Rav Yosef's curse as yeshiva head carried severe weight

Rav Dimi

  • "I punished him" — he caused my loss
  • Rav Adda's refusal to reserve the market caused Rav Dimi's figs to rot
  • Causing another's financial ruin invites divine retribution

Abaye

  • "I punished him" — he shamed me
  • Rav Adda publicly embarrassed Abaye before students
  • Public humiliation (malbin panim) is equated with bloodshed

Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak

  • "I punished him" — he disrupted Torah study
  • As head of the kalla lectures, Rav Naḥman waited while Rav Adda delayed the students
  • Interrupting communal Torah study incurs severe punishment

🧱 Mishna: Building Walls Near a Neighbor's Property

❓ Can you build a new wall near your neighbor's existing wall?
↓
✅ Permitted
Build with 4 cubits distance from neighbor's wall — allows passage, working the soil, and structural safety
❌ Prohibited
Building closer than 4 cubits — blocks passage, damages foundations, deprives neighbor of working space
↓
❓ What about building a wall opposite a neighbor's window?
↓
Parallel to window
Must distance 4 cubits — prevents blocking light from entering the window (Mishna)
Perpendicular to window
Must leave space equal to full width of window — per Rav (via Yeiva, father-in-law of Ashyan bar Nidbakh)
