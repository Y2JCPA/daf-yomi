📏 Hierarchy of Path Widths

Private Path

  • 4 cubits — Mishna standard
  • OR: width for donkey with load (Aḥerim)
  • Halakha follows Aḥerim (Rav Huna)

City-to-City Road

  • 8 cubits wide
  • Connects between cities
  • Fixed baraita standard

Public Thoroughfare

  • 16 cubits wide
  • Reshut harabbim
  • Standard mishna measure

City of Refuge Road

  • 32 cubits wide
  • Double the public road
  • Derived from verse (Rav Huna)

🏛️ Can the Public Keep the Alternative Thoroughfare?

Mishna
If an owner gave the public an alternative thoroughfare, the public may use both — the original and the new one.
Questioner
Why can't the owner say: Take back your original path and return my alternative route to me? He gave it willingly!
Rabbi Eliezer
The public keeps whichever path they choose. The owner cannot dictate which thoroughfare they must use.
Rav Giddel (citing Rav)
Rabbi Eliezer speaks only where the original path was lost — its location was forgotten — so the public acquired the new one by necessity.
Rabba bar Rav Huna (citing Rav)
The halakha is not in accordance with Rabbi Eliezer. Yet once an owner designates a new path and the public uses it, he cannot retract it (Rav Yehuda).
Resolution Rav Yehuda: Once the owner demarcated a new path and the public used it, he is bound by his own designation — the public has acquired it through use.

👣 Walking as Acquisition — Rabbi Eliezer's Proof

Question: Can walking the length and breadth of a path constitute a legal act of kinyan (acquisition)?
↓
Baraita: One who walks the length or breadth of a field thereby acquires a path through it
↓
Rabbi Eliezer
Yes — walking acquires.
Source: Genesis 13:17 — God told Abraham: 'Arise, walk through the land, its length and breadth; for I will give it to you' — walking itself is the acquisition.
Rabbis (concession)
Only for vineyard paths.
Rabbi Yosei son of Rabbi Ḥanina: Rabbis concede to Rabbi Eliezer for paths through vineyards — since such a path exists solely for walking, walking is the acquisition.
↓
Practical ruling (Rav Yitzḥak bar Ami): For a vineyard path purchased by a buyer, he is given a width of four cubits

⚰️ Ma'amad U'Moshav — The Mourning Ceremony

After burial: Mourners and comforters begin the return journey together from the grave
↓
Stop and sit: After traveling a short distance, stop to bewail the deceased — then rise and continue
↓
Repeat 7 times (Judea): Standing and sitting alternately — Rabbi Yehuda: no fewer than seven stops on the return
↓
Three requirements: (1) In the cemetery only; (2) With a group — not for one individual alone; (3) Only where it is locally customary
↓
Rav Avya's three errors when performing for his wife: performed it alone (not a group), in the city (not cemetery), in a locale where it was not customary

👑 Paths Without Measure — King, Funeral & Comforting

דֶּרֶךְ הַמֶּלֶךְ אֵין לָהּ שִׁיעוּר
אֵין לָהּ שִׁיעוּר — No Maximum Measure King's Thoroughfare: A king may breach the fence of any private individual to create a road for himself — the Torah grants kings this authority and none may protest.

Funeral Path (Derech Kevurah): The path for accompanying the deceased to burial has no fixed maximum — it is widened as needed out of כְּבוֹד הַמֵּת (kavod ha-met), honor for the dead.

Comforting Area (Makom Ha'amidah): The judges of Tzippori ruled the standing area for comforting mourners must accommodate the sowing of four kav of seed — large enough for the full assembly of mourners and comforters.

End of Chapter / New Mishna: One who sells land to build an underground catacomb — a contractor receives a plot under commission to construct it.
