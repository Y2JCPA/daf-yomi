⚖️ House Collapse: Who Died First?

Beit Shammai
Divide the estate: father's heirs receive half, son's creditors (wife's ketubbah, lenders) receive the other half — uncertainty requires splitting.
Beit Hillel
Property retains its presumptive status (chazakah). Each estate stays with its prior holders — neither side can prove the other's claim, so the status quo prevails.
🏚️ The Scenario A house collapses on a father and son (or a person and those from whom he stands to inherit). Who died first is unknown. The son had obligations: wife's ketubbah and creditors. If son died first, his estate passed through the father; if father died first, son's creditors have a claim.

🏚️ The House Collapse: Stakes and Legal Sequence

The Mishna Case: A house collapses on a father and son simultaneously. The order of death is unknown — both parties had estates and obligations.
↓
If son died first → his estate transferred to the father → father's heirs ultimately inherit the combined estate, leaving son's creditors with nothing.
↓
If father died first → son inherited the father's estate first → son's creditors (wife's ketubbah, lenders) can now collect from that inherited wealth.
↓
Beit Shammai: The uncertainty itself creates a 50/50 split — father's heirs and son's creditors each receive half the disputed portion.
↓
Beit Hillel: Chazakah (presumptive status) governs — property stays with whoever held it last. Neither side can upset the status quo without proof.

🔗 Shmuel's Dilemma: Future Property as Collateral

Borrower writes in promissory note: 'All property I will acquire in the future shall be liened to this debt'
↓
Borrower later acquires new property — then sells it to a third party
↓
✅ Lien IS valid
Lender can seize from the buyer — future-acquired property was effectively collateralized at the time of the loan
❌ Lien is NOT valid
Property not liened — buyer keeps it safely. Lender has no claim against purchasers of future-acquired land
↓
Gemara's conclusion: Every proof cited is rejected as reflecting only Rabbi Meir's minority opinion — Shmuel's dilemma remains unresolved

🏦 Two Creditors, One Future-Acquired Property: Who Has Priority?

Rav Naḥman
The first lender acquires the future-acquired property outright. His lien preceded the second lender's in time — priority follows chronological order. (Ruling confirmed by Sages of Eretz Yisrael)
Rav Huna
The two lenders divide the property between them equally. Since both liens attached to property not yet acquired at loan time, neither has meaningful chronological priority over the other.
📨 Response from Eretz Yisrael Rav Naḥman reports: The Sages sent a ruling — the first lender acquires the property, since his lien was written first and therefore precedes in legal force.

📋 Proofs Attempted — All Rejected as Rabbi Meir's View

Proof Attempts

  • Rav Yosef: Mishna (Ketubot 175a) — promissory note vs. later bill of sale
  • Rav Ḥana: Mishna about orphans' mitzva obligation to repay father's debts
  • Rav Yaakov from Nehar Pekod: Mishna (Shevi'it 10:5) — antedated promissory notes are invalid
  • Rav Mesharshiyya: Baraita about collecting debt when purchased land appreciated in value

Rejection Pattern

  • Each proof is answered: 'That mishna/baraita follows Rabbi Meir alone'
  • Rabbi Meir holds a person CAN transfer something not yet in the world
  • The Rabbis (majority opinion) hold such transfers are invalid
  • Proofs based on Rabbi Meir's rulings cannot resolve the general halakha

Rabbi Meir's Principle

  • אָדָם מַקְנֶה דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בָּא לָעוֹלָם
  • A person can transfer ownership of an entity not yet in the world
  • Minority opinion — Rabbis disagree with this position
  • Underlies all the mishnayot cited; hence each proof is deflected

🌾 Shevach HaKarkas: The Closing Argument

שֶׁבַח הַקַּרְקַע — Enhancement accrued by the buyer's labor and investment
🌱 Baraita: Enhancement of Land — Final Proof and Resolution The baraita asks: How can it occur that a lender cannot collect from liened property that was sold? — If the borrower wrote that future property is liened, and he later acquired and sold land, the buyer should be liable to the lender.

The Gemara answers: This baraita follows Rabbi Meir. Shmuel's dilemma is therefore left unresolved.

Final twist: The Gemara clarifies the baraita's ruling that 'the buyer collects the enhancement' — this means the buyer collects only half the value of the land's improvement.
