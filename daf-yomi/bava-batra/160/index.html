📜 Two Types of Legal Documents

Get Pashut — Ordinary Document

  • Witnesses sign on the inside (written side)
  • Valid with 2 witnesses
  • Text is fully open and readable
  • If witnesses sign on back → invalid
  • Simpler to produce and verify

Get Mekushar — Tied Document

  • Witnesses sign on the back/outside
  • Valid with 3 witnesses
  • Text is folded and tied between each section
  • If witnesses sign inside → invalid
  • Instituted rabbinically to slow down impulsive divorces

✍️ Where Do Witnesses Sign on a Tied Document?

Rav Huna
Witnesses sign between each tied fold — on the outside of the document, between successive folds.
Rav Yirmeya bar Abba
Witnesses sign on the back of the written side, taking care that signatures are exactly opposite the writing on the outside.
Rami bar Ḥama
Objection to Rav Huna: A tied document was once brought before Rav — the witnesses had signed between the folds on the inside, and he declared it valid. But this seems problematic for the outside theory...
Rav Ḥisda
Rav Huna never meant the inside! He clearly meant between the folds on the outside. The document in the story must be understood differently.
Anti-Forgery Concern If signatures appear outside the folds, could a fraudster add text inside? Solution: the document must contain the formula כל מה שכתוב בו מקויים ועומד — 'Everything written therein is confirmed and established' — written only once, preventing additions.

📖 Talmudic Sources for Two Document Types

Question: What is the biblical basis for two types of documents?
↓
Rabbi Ḥanina — Jeremiah 32:11 (first reading)
"Subscribe the deeds" = ordinary doc (2 witnesses)
"Seal them" = tied doc (3 witnesses)
"Call witnesses" = extra 3rd witness
Rafram — Jeremiah 32:11 (second reading)
"That which was sealed" = tied doc
"That which was open" = ordinary doc
"The terms and conditions" = laws distinguishing the two
Rami bar Yeḥezkel — Deuteronomy 19:15
"Two witnesses...or three witnesses" — two suffice for ordinary, three needed for tied, where extra effort of tying is required
↓
Gemara's Conclusion: None of these verses are the true source — each verse was needed for its own teaching. The entire tied document institution is rabbinic in origin; these verses serve only as asmakhta (biblical support).

גט מקושר — תקנת חכמים משום כהנים בעלי חמה (בבא בתרא קס ע"ב)
⚡ Why Did the Rabbis Institute the Tied Document? There was a place where many kohanim (priests) lived, and they were notoriously quick-tempered (בעלי חמה). They would impulsively decide to divorce their wives in a moment of anger. The Rabbis instituted the complex tied document for bills of divorce (gittin) to introduce deliberate delay — by the time all the folding, tying, and multiple witness signatures were completed, the priest's anger would cool and he might reconsider. Once instituted for divorce documents, it was extended to all legal documents so as not to create an obvious distinction (which would undermine the purpose — a savvy kohen could insist on an ordinary document).

🔒 The Anti-Forgery Formula: How It Works

Step 1 — The Problem: In a tied document, witnesses sign on the outside between folds. A dishonest holder could potentially unfold and add fraudulent text inside.
↓
Step 2 — The Solution: Every tied document must end with the formula: כל מה שכתוב בו מקויים ועומד — 'Everything written therein is confirmed and established.'
↓
Step 3 — Why It Works: Any text written after this formula has no legal validity. If a forger adds content, it appears after the formula and is ignored by the court.
↓
Step 4 — Objection: But couldn't the forger erase the formula, add his fake text, and then rewrite the formula afterward?
↓
Step 5 — Response: Courts rule that only one such declaration may appear in a valid document. If the formula appears twice, the document is suspect and disqualified — so the forger cannot rewrite it.
↓
Step 6 — Further Objection: What if the forger erases the declaration, adds text, and rewrites it just once? The Gemara's discussion of this continues into the next daf — demonstrating the ongoing Talmudic concern for document integrity.
