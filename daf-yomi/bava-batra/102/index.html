⚔️ Rabbi Shimon vs. Rabbis: What Makes a Graveyard?

Rabbis
Three corpses 4–8 cubits apart = presumptive graveyard. But if they are fewer than four cubits apart, the middle corpse is disregarded — it is NOT a graveyard.
Rabbi Shimon
Even close-together corpses (under 4 cubits) CAN establish a graveyard. We do not disregard the middle corpse — all three count.
Rabbis
In a vineyard (Kilayim), middle vines ARE disregarded when spacing is too small — the outer rows define the vineyard boundary.
Rabbi Shimon
In a vineyard, yes — people plant vines irregularly, so close spacing is unreliable. But graves? Burying bodies so close is disgraceful — no one does it unintentionally. Therefore closeness proves deliberate burial, and all corpses count.
Resolution Each ruling is internally consistent: the Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon swap positions between graveyard and vineyard based on context-specific reasoning about human practice — not arbitrary reversal.

📐 The 20-Cubit Examination Rule — How It Works

Standard burial cave: Two chambers, each 8 × 6 cubits, separated by a 4-cubit courtyard
↓
Rule: Once a graveyard is found, examine 20 cubits outward in every direction
↓
Difficulty: Straight length = 8 (chamber) + 4 (courtyard) + 8 (chamber) = 20 — that only covers one side. Why 20 and not more?
↓
Rabbis' answer: Examine along the diagonal of the 8×6 chamber ≈ 10 cubits, then courtyard (4) + second chamber diagonal (~6) = 20
↓
Objection: If we use the diagonal for one chamber, use it for the other too — that gives ~22, not 20
↓
Final answer (Rav Sheisha): Non-viable newborn chambers are only 6 cubits long — smaller chambers make the arithmetic resolve correctly at exactly 20

🍇 Graveyard vs. Vineyard: The Contradictions Resolved

⚰️ Graveyard (Bava Batra 102)

  • Rabbis: <4 cubits → middle corpse disregarded → NOT a graveyard
  • Rabbi Shimon: All three count even when very close together
  • Reasoning: Burying too closely is disgraceful — it must be intentional
  • Close spacing = evidence of deliberate communal burial site
  • Therefore R. Shimon says: close corpses CAN establish a graveyard

🍇 Vineyard (Kilayim 5:2)

  • Rabbis: <4 cubits → middle vines ARE disregarded → outer rows define vineyard
  • Rabbi Shimon: <4 cubits → NOT a proper vineyard at all
  • Reasoning: People DO plant vines in irregular, varying patterns
  • Close spacing = unreliable indicator of vineyard intent
  • Therefore R. Shimon says: close vines do NOT establish a vineyard

🌾 New Mishna: Selling a Beit Kor Field

בֵּית כּוֹר — שָׂדֶה הַנִּזְרַעַת בְּכוֹר זֶרַע
📜 Mishna — Daf 102b Case: Reuven sells Shimon a "beit kor" — a field of the size required for sowing one kor of seed.

If the field contains:
• Crevices (נְקָעִים) — 10+ handbreadths deep, OR
• Rocks — 10+ handbreadths high

→ These are NOT measured as part of the beit kor. The buyer must receive fully usable land of the stated size.

But if crevices/rocks are less than 10 handbreadths, they ARE included in the measurement — the buyer accepted them as part of the field.

The Gemara then connects this to Arakhin 25a: one who consecrates an ancestral field redeems it at 50 silver shekalim per beit kor.
