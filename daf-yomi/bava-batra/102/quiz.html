QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Mishna, what spacing between the outer two of three parallel corpses establishes a presumptive graveyard?
A. Between two and four cubits
B. Between four and eight cubits
C. Between six and twelve cubits
D. Exactly ten cubits
Correct: B — The Mishna states that if the outer two of three parallel corpses are between four and eight cubits apart, this matches the standard spacing of a catacomb burial chamber and is presumed to be a graveyard requiring no disturbance.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does the Gemara (according to the Rabbis' opinion) explain that examining only 20 cubits is sufficient?
A. Because Rabbi Shimon counts only five graves at four cubits each
B. Because one examines along the diagonal of the 8×6 cubit chamber, approximately 10 cubits
C. Because the courtyard between chambers is counted twice
D. Because non-viable newborn chambers are only 4 cubits long
Correct: B — The Rabbis explain that examining along the diagonal of the standard 8×6 burial chamber yields approximately 10 cubits (since √(8²+6²) = 10). Adding the 4-cubit courtyard and approximately 6 cubits of the second chamber brings the total to 20.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Shimon hold that close corpses (under 4 cubits) CAN establish a graveyard, yet close vines (under 4 cubits) do NOT establish a vineyard?
A. Graveyard laws are always stricter than vineyard laws in halakha
B. The Torah explicitly defines graveyards but leaves vineyards to rabbinic ruling
C. Burying bodies so closely is disgraceful and therefore intentional; people plant vines irregularly, making close spacing unreliable
D. Rabbi Shimon later retracted his vineyard ruling but kept the graveyard ruling
Correct: C — Rabbi Shimon reasons that since burying bodies so closely together is a disgrace, it must be intentional — proving a deliberate burial ground. But people commonly plant vines in irregular patterns, so close vine spacing is unreliable as an indicator of an intentional vineyard.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is Rav Sheisha son of Rav Idi's solution to the problem with the 20-cubit examination rule?
A. The Mishna follows the Rabbis, measuring along the length of each chamber
B. The Mishna follows Rabbi Shimon, dealing with non-viable newborns buried in smaller 6-cubit chambers
C. The 20-cubit rule applies only to outdoor graves, not burial caves
D. Two witnesses must each independently examine 10 cubits
Correct: B — Rav Sheisha explains that the Mishna follows Rabbi Shimon and deals with non-viable newborns buried in smaller chambers (6 cubits long instead of 8). With these reduced dimensions, the arithmetic of the 20-cubit examination rule resolves correctly.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In the Mishna about selling a 'beit kor' field, what is the rule for crevices ten handbreadths deep?
A. They are measured as part of the beit kor and add to the total area
B. They are excluded from the beit kor measurement — the buyer must receive fully usable land
C. They reduce the sale price by five shekalim per crevice
D. They are only excluded if the seller explicitly disclosed them before the sale
Correct: B — The Mishna rules that crevices ten or more handbreadths deep (and rocks ten or more handbreadths high) are not counted toward the beit kor measurement. The buyer is entitled to fully usable, plantable land of the stated size, and unusable terrain features are excluded.
