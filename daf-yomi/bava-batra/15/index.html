📖 Who Wrote the Last 8 Verses of the Torah?

Rabbi Yehuda
Joshua wrote the final 8 verses of the Torah — the verses describing Moses' death. How could Moses write about his own death while still alive?
Rabbi Shimon
Can a Torah scroll be missing even a single letter? It says 'Take this Torah scroll' (Deut. 31:26) — it was complete! Moses wrote those verses with tears.
Rav Giddel (citing Rav)
The last 8 verses must be read by one person and not split up — this halacha supports Rabbi Yehuda, treating that section as a distinct unit.
The Verse "וַיִּכְתֹּב מֹשֶׁה" — And Moses wrote (Deut. 31:22) — Rabbi Shimon: Moses wrote in tears; Rabbi Yehuda: Joshua completed it.

🤔 Did Job Really Exist?

Anonymous Sage
Job never existed and was never created! His story is a mashal — a parable — to teach about suffering and Divine justice. There is no historical Job.
Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani
If Job is a parable, why is his name and hometown explicitly stated? "There was a man in the land of Utz, whose name was Job" — this proves he was a real historical person!
Anonymous Counter
But Natan's parable also says 'there were two men' — yet that is clearly a parable (II Samuel 12:1)! Specific detail alone doesn't prove historicity.
Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani
In Natan's parable, no names or places are given. Job's story specifies his name AND his location — that combination marks a real person.
Conclusion Job was a real person — the tanna'im dispute only when he lived and whether he was Jewish or a gentile.

🌟 Job's Righteousness — What Made Him Unique

"Perfect and upright" — Rabbi Yoḥanan: What is said about Job concerning his fear of God is greater than what is said about Abraham
↓
"Turns away from evil" (Job 1:8) — Rabbi Abba bar Shmuel: Job was forgiving with his money. He would waive the half-peruta owed to him, so others wouldn't sin for his sake
↓
"A hedge about him" (Job 1:10) — The Satan acknowledges God's protection encompassed Job, his household, and all his possessions
↓
"His livestock breached [פָּרְצוּ] the land" — Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina: Job's livestock broke the natural order — they entered fields and paid for the damage
↓
The Generation of Job — Rabbi Yoḥanan: That generation was awash in licentiousness (based on wordplay: ḥazitem in Job 27:12 parallels language of immorality)
