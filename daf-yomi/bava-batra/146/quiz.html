QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava, what is the minimum a groom must eat at his father-in-law's house for the sivlonot to be forfeited upon a failed marriage?
A. Any amount whatsoever
B. Half a dinar's worth
C. A dinar's worth
D. Two dinar's worth
Correct: C — Rava rules specifically that eating food worth a dinar (or more) at the father-in-law's house forfeits the return of presents. Eating less than a dinar's worth does not trigger this — the presents may still be reclaimed.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
The Sages at Usha ruled on consumable presents that were NOT consumed. What was their ruling?
A. They are returned in full since they were not used
B. They are returned only if worth more than a dinar
C. They are not collected — consumables are never returned
D. Only half their value may be claimed
Correct: C — Rabbi Aḥa Sar HaBira brought this before the Usha Sages, who ruled: presents typically consumed are not collected — even if they happened to remain unconsumed. The category, not the fact of consumption, determines the law.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What does Ben Sira add to the biblical verse 'all the days of the poor are terrible'?
A. Even Shabbat brings him no relief
B. The nights are terrible too
C. His children suffer alongside him
D. Even the wealthy fear poverty
Correct: B — Ben Sira extends the verse: not only the days but the nights of the poor are terrible — his roof is lowest so rain pours onto it; his vineyard is highest so his soil erodes away.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In the mishna about a death-bed gift, if a sick man gives away ALL his property (reserving nothing) and then recovers — what happens?
A. The gift stands — he gave it freely and in writing
B. The gift is void — we assess he only intended it if he died
C. The gift stands only for movable property, not land
D. The heirs may contest only half the gift
Correct: B — Since he reserved nothing for himself, we assess (uman da'ato) that he intended the gift only in the event of death. Upon recovery, the gift is void — he never meant to impoverish himself while living.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the key difference between Rav Naḥman's and Rav Sheshet's positions on which tanna the death-bed gift mishna follows?
A. Rav Naḥman requires two witnesses; Rav Sheshet requires one
B. Rav Naḥman says it is Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya (infer from silence); Rav Sheshet says Rabbi Shimon Shezuri (infer from ambiguous words)
C. Rav Naḥman limits the rule to land; Rav Sheshet extends it to movables
D. Rav Naḥman requires the sick man to have been bedridden; Rav Sheshet does not
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman attributes the mishna to Ben Menasya, who permits inferring intent from total silence. Rav Sheshet attributes it to Shezuri, who infers intent from ambiguous speech (like the man led to execution). Rav Naḥman rejects Shezuri because there the man actually said something.
