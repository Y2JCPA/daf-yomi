↓

↓
✅ Yes — married

❌ No — marriage failed

↓
Yes ≥ 1 dinar (Rava)
Presents not returned — he implicitly waived his claim
No / < 1 dinar

📜 Ben Sira on the Poverty of the Poor

כָּל יְמֵי עָנִי רָעִים — וְאַף הַלֵּילוֹת
Ben Sira — ספר בן סירא "All the days of the poor are terrible" (Proverbs 15:15). Ben Sira adds: the nights as well. His roof is the lowest of all roofs — rain from every neighbor's roof drains onto his. His vineyard sits on the mountaintop — the soil of all the surrounding vineyards erodes down onto his.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi objects: What of Shabbat and Festivals, when even the poor enjoy good meals and rest? The Gemara resolves: the verse is understood in light of Shmuel's teaching — the poor man is tormented by constant anxiety and dread, even when he eats well.

Marriage fails — what type of presents were sent?
↓
Durable goods (garments, vessels)
Typically returned to sender's heirs
Consumables (wine, oil, food)
Were they actually consumed?
↓
Yes — consumed
Gone — cannot be collected
No — still intact
Still not returned (Usha: consumables are never collected)
Small personal gifts
Never returned — sent for her use
↓
Proof from Rav Yehuda/Shmuel: 100 wagons of wine and oil — one cup of hot drink ended the claim. Rav Ashi: they may have also ground spices worth a dinar.

⚖️ Which Tanna Holds We Infer Unstated Intent?

Rav Naḥman
The mishna on death-bed gifts follows Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya: where a sick man gives away all his property but reserves no land, we assess he believed he would die — so if he recovers, the gift is void.
Rav Sheshet
No — it follows Rabbi Shimon Shezuri: even ordinary speech is judged by intended meaning. A man led to execution who says 'write a get for my wife' — we write it, because his intent is clear even without explicit direction.
Rav Naḥman (rebuttal)
Shezuri's case is different — the condemned man said something. The sick man in our mishna said nothing at all. Only Ben Menasya's principle permits us to infer intent from total silence.
Core Question Can a court infer what a person intended when they expressed nothing — or only when they spoke ambiguously?

🏛️ Shchiv Mera — Rules for Death-Bed Gifts

Scenario: A person who is ill and bedridden (שְׁכִיב מְרַע) writes a deed giving all his property to others
↓
He reserves any amount of land: The gift is valid even if he fully recovers — by keeping something he showed the gift was deliberate and unconditional
↓
He reserves nothing: If he recovers, the gift is void — we assess (אוֹמְדִין דַּעְתּוֹ) that he only intended to give upon death; keeping nothing for himself proves that assumption
↓
Tanna dispute: Rav Naḥman: this principle is Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya's. Rav Sheshet: it is Rabbi Shimon Shezuri's rule that intent governs even ambiguous speech
↓
Later in the daf: Gemara continues — a sick man asked 'to whom shall my property go?' and responded. This case probes the boundary of explicit vs. assessed intent.
