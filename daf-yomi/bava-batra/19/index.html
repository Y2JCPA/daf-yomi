⛏️ 'And Plasters' or 'Or Plasters'? The Pit Digging Dispute

Questioner
What is the exact mishna wording? Does it say 'and plasters with lime' — requiring both distancing AND plastering — or 'or plasters with lime' — meaning either distancing OR plastering suffices?
Gemara
It is obvious we learned 'and plasters.' If it said 'or plasters,' the tanna would have used the same language as the olive refuse clause. The different phrasing signals a stricter standard for pit digging.
Rejoinder
Perhaps the clauses are taught separately not because of different wording, but because moisture damage (pit) differs from weight/heat damage (olive refuse)?
Rabbi Yehuda (Baraita)
Even with crumbling rock — each digs from his own side and plasters with lime. This proves plastering is always required alongside distancing, not an alternative to it.
Conclusion The mishna reads 'and plasters' — both distancing AND plastering with lime are independently required when digging a pit near a neighbor's property.

🪨 Why Aren't Rocks Listed for Pot Insulation?

Problem: Mishna forbids insulating a pot with residue, manure, salt, lime — but omits rocks, even though rocks must be distanced 3 handbreadths from walls
↓
Rav Yosef
People don't customarily insulate food with rocks
Abaye objects
But wool fleece and grape clusters appear in the baraita — nobody insulates with those either! Rav Yosef's reasoning fails.
Rava (accepted)
Rocks scratch and damage the pot — so they are never used for insulation. The tanna rightly omits them from that list while including them in distancing rules.
↓
Abaye's alternative: The tanna follows the biblical style — 'its neighbor tells about him' (Job 36:33) — one case is mentioned and the adjacent case is implied. But Rava's practical reason is preferred.

💧 Urinating Near a Neighbor's Wall: Permitted or Forbidden?

Rabba bar bar Ḥana — Permits

  • Cites verse: 'I will cut off every male' (מַשְׁתִּין בְּקִיר — he who urinates against a wall)
  • Urinating alongside a wall is a normal act — the verse treats it matter-of-factly
  • Therefore permitted near a neighbor's wall

Baraita — Prohibits

  • One may not urinate alongside another's wall
  • Must distance 3 handbreadths from a brick wall
  • Verse reread: God will destroy even those who urinate near walls — i.e., a reproach, not permission

Resolution

  • Baraita applies specifically to brick walls — urine erodes mortar
  • Stone walls: urine runs off harmlessly — permitted
  • The verse describes the act neutrally; the legal ruling depends on wall material

🫓 Shmuel: Does a Wafer Reduce a Window's Ritual Impurity Dimensions?

Context: A window of sufficient size allows ritual impurity (טומאה) to pass between rooms. Does bread placed in the window reduce its effective dimensions?
↓
Shmuel rules: A wafer does not reduce the window's dimensions — the window retains its full size for impurity calculations.
↓
Why not thick bread? Thick bread is fit to eat and the owner will remove it — it is not nullified as a permanent fixture. A thin wafer, however, may be mentally set aside as part of the opening.
↓
Challenge: A wafer is susceptible to ritual impurity — and susceptible items cannot serve as barriers to impurity anyway! So why state the ruling?
↓
Answer: Shmuel speaks of bread that has already become ritually impure and is now unfit — theoretically it could serve as a barrier, but he rules it still does not reduce the window.
↓
Objection from Mishna Oholot: A basket of straw or barrel of dried figs in a window can reduce its size.
Resolution: Rotted straw (unfit for animals) and worm-infested figs (נִתְעַפְּשׁוּ) — the owner has mentally nullified them as permanent features of the window.

🌱 Seeds, Plow, Urine — Three Independent Rulings

בבא בתרא י״ט — זרעים, מחרישה, ומי רגלים
Why Each Rule Is Necessary Seeds: Must be distanced 3 handbreadths — roots grow downward and loosen the wall's foundation (not sideways, as one might assume from grapevine bending).

Plow: Cannot be derived from seeds — one might plow without ever planting. The plow itself causes direct soil disruption near the wall.

Urine: Cannot be derived from seeds or plow — urine damages brick walls through moisture rather than physical force or root pressure. Each ruling addresses a distinct mechanism of damage.
