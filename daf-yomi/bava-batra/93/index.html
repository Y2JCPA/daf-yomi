⚖️ Does Majority (Rov) Resolve Monetary Uncertainty?

Rav
We do NOT follow the majority (rov) in monetary matters. Factual uncertainty in financial cases cannot be resolved by statistics — each case stands on its own.
Shmuel
We DO follow the majority even in monetary matters. Just as rov determines halachic status elsewhere, it resolves monetary doubt as well.
Gemara
This dispute parallels a tannaitic debate — the first tanna vs. Rabbi Yosei and 'They said to him' — regarding whether the seller of non-sprouting flaxseeds bears liability, since the majority of flaxseeds are sold for planting.
Conclusion The Gemara identifies this as a classic tannaitic dispute: Rav aligns with the first tanna; Shmuel aligns with Rabbi Yosei's opponents who invoke majority to impose seller liability.

🐂 Challenge to Rav: The Ox-Fetus Case

Challenge
An innocuous ox gored a cow and the fetus was found dead at her side — we don't know if the cow calved before or after the goring. The ox's owner pays only ¼ of fetal damage. But why not follow the majority of cows, who calve successfully, and charge full payment?
Rav
The reason for partial payment is different: there is uncertainty about how the fetus died. The ox may have approached from the front (causing miscarriage) or from the side (not causing it). This is ambiguity about the manner of goring, not a case where majority statistics apply.
Key Distinction Rav deflects each challenge by showing the real uncertainty is factual/physical — not a question resolvable by majority. The case does not prove Shmuel's position.

🌱 Seeds That Didn't Sprout — Who Bears Loss?

Seller sells produce — sometimes purchased for eating, sometimes for planting
↓
Buyer eats — unfit for eating
Seller pays value of seeds
Buyer plants — doesn't sprout
Seller pays value of seeds only (basic rule)
↓
Special case: Flaxseeds — majority purchased for planting. Does majority determine seller's full liability?
↓
Rav / First Tanna
No — majority doesn't apply. Seller pays only seed value
Shmuel / Rabbi Yosei
Yes — it's a mistaken transaction. Seller bears full responsibility

📜 Three Tannaitic Opinions on Flaxseed Liability

First Tanna

  • Seller pays only the value of the seeds
  • Does not pay field prep expenses
  • Buyer assumed risk of non-sprouting
  • Parallels Rav: majority not followed in monetary law

Rabbi Yosei

  • Seller bears full financial responsibility
  • Majority of flaxseeds sold for planting → seller liable
  • This is a mistaken transaction
  • Parallels Shmuel: follow majority in monetary law

'They Said to Him' (Sages)

  • Many buyers purchase flaxseeds for eating too
  • Cannot assume majority purpose was planting
  • Limits Rabbi Yosei's ruling
  • Buyer's stated purpose governs, not aggregate majority

💰 Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: Extended Seller Liability

Basic Rule (First Tanna): Seller of non-sprouting seeds pays only the value of the seeds themselves — no more.
↓
'Some Say': The seller also pays for the buyer's field preparation expenses — plowing, fertilizing, and labor costs wasted.
↓
Rav Ḥisda identifies: 'Some say' = Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who systematically extends liability beyond direct damage.
↓
Proof — the Miller Case: A miller who ruined wheat into bran — Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds him liable for the owner's humiliation before guests and consequential loss, not just the grain's value.
↓
Garden Seeds Rule: Seeds grown only for planting (not eaten) — Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel makes the seller fully responsible, since the buyer has no fallback use.

🏛️ Jerusalem Feast Custom + New Mishna: Produce Impurities Jerusalem Feast Custom (Minhag Yerushalayim): A cloth (mappa) was spread at the banquet hall entrance. While spread → guests may still enter; once removed → the feast has ended. This protected latecomers from embarrassment of arriving to find the meal over.

New Mishna: When selling produce in bulk, the buyer accepts up to ¼ kav of impurities per se'a as tolerable variation.

Rav Ketina clarifies: This tolerance applies to legumes mixed in — but not to actual dirt or pebbles, which must be entirely absent from the sale.
