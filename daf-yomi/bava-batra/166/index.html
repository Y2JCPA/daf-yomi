🪙 Dinarei or Dinarin? The Currency Debate

Gemara
If a document says 'silver in dinars,' why must it mean two golden dinars' worth? Perhaps it means two silver dinars — a far smaller amount?
Rav Ashi
Emend the baraita: the first clause uses dinarei — which specifically denotes golden dinars. The second clause uses dinarin — which denotes silver dinars. The plural form itself signals the denomination.
Gemara
From where do we know these two plural forms carry different meanings?
Karetot Mishna
The price of bird-nests rose to golden dinarei, then collapsed to a quarter of a silver dinar. Clearly dinarei signals gold — a vastly higher value than dinarin.
Key Distinction 'Dinarei' (דִּינָרֵי) = golden dinars — higher denomination. 'Dinarin' (דִּינָרִין) = silver dinars — lower denomination. The plural ending determines which currency is meant in a legal document.

🪙 Two Plural Forms, Two Currencies

דִּינָרֵי / Dinarei

  • Plural ending: -ei
  • Refers to golden dinars
  • Higher denomination
  • Used in first clause of baraita
  • Proof: Jerusalem nests priced in golden dinarei (Karetot 8a)
  • When a document says 'silver in dinarei' → owes gold-dinar value of silver

דִּינָרִין / Dinarin

  • Plural ending: -in
  • Refers to silver dinars
  • Lower denomination
  • Used in second clause of baraita
  • Proof: same nests later priced at ¼ silver dinar
  • When a document says 'gold in dinarin' → owes silver-dinar value of gold

🕊️ The Jerusalem Bird Market — Proof from Karetot 8a

מסכת כריתות ח׳ א׳ — רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל
Historical Incident Women uncertain about five births or five irregular discharges (ziva) were each obligated to bring multiple bird-offerings. The resulting surge in demand drove the price of nests (pairs of birds) to golden dinarei in Jerusalem.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel ruled: a woman in doubt about all five obligations need bring only one pair of birds, and the rest are forgiven. That very day, the price of bird-nests fell to one-quarter of a silver dinar.

This mishna definitively establishes that dinarei (gold) represents a far greater sum than dinarin (silver) — proving Rav Ashi's linguistic distinction.

🔡 Rav Pappa's Sefel/Kefel Analysis & the 600-Dinar Question

Background: Sefel (סֶפֶל) = a cup; Kefel (כֶּפֶל) = double/fold. The letters samekh (ס) and kuf (כ) are visually similar in script, making scribal confusion plausible.
↓
Rav Pappa's obvious case: If sefel is written above and kefel below — we follow the bottom. The debtor owes kefel (the double amount). Bottom text always controls.
↓
Rav Pappa's dilemma: What if kefel (larger obligation) is above and sefel (smaller — a cup) is below? Does the bottom's lower obligation control as usual — or do we say the scribe erred and the lender deserves the higher kefel? This question is left unresolved (תֵּיקוּ).
↓
Rav Sherevya's question to Abaye: A document stated the debt was 'six hundred and a dinar' without specifying what denomination the 600 referred to. Is it 600 zuz? 600 dinars? The daf ends with this question pending resolution.
