📜 The Three Categories of Inheritance (Mishna)

Both Inherit & Bequeath

  • Father inherits from son / Son from father
  • Paternal brothers (from each other)
  • A man and his mother's male relatives
  • Reciprocal: death of either transfers to the other

Inherit but Do NOT Bequeath

  • A woman inherits from her sons
  • She does not bequeath to them (they inherit her via their father's line)
  • One-directional relationship

Bequeath but Do NOT Inherit

  • A husband bequeaths to his wife (she receives ketuva)
  • But the husband inherits from his wife
  • Torah law vs. rabbinic ketuva distinction

🔢 Why Does the Mishna List the Father First?

Questioner
Why does the Mishna say 'a father with regard to his sons' first? It should list 'sons with regard to their father' first — sons inheriting from fathers is the more common and natural case!
Tanna (Defense)
The Tanna teaches the father inheriting first because this halakha is learned through derivation (not explicit in the Torah) — and what is novel and derived is most dear to the Tanna, so he teaches it first.
Questioner
But the verse in Numbers 27 explicitly mentions the son inheriting from the father first! The Torah's own order should govern the Mishna's list.
Tanna (Defense)
Precisely — the Tanna highlights what is not explicit. A father inheriting from his son is derived via interpretation; its special status warrants pride of place in the list.
Principle A halakha derived through rabbinic interpretation (not explicit in scripture) is especially beloved to the Tanna — he teaches it prominently to ensure it is not forgotten.

📖 The Verse and the Derivation

וְנָתַתֶּם אֶת נַחֲלָתוֹ לִשְׁאֵרוֹ הַקָּרֹב אֵלָיו מִמִּשְׁפַּחְתּוֹ — במדבר כז:יא
Numbers 27:11 "And if his father has no brothers, then you shall give his inheritance to his kinsman who is next to him of his family, and he shall inherit it."

The baraita interprets: 'his kinsman' — this refers to the son as the nearest relative. But what about the father? From here we also derive that a father inherits from his son: the father is the one who stands closest after the son in the chain of inheritance.

The verse's phrase "next to him of his family" opens the door to this derivation — all who are related as 'family' are potential heirs.

🤝 Son vs. Brother — Who Is the Closer Heir?

Gemara (Claim)
Why include the son as the closer relative (over the father) and exclude the brother? The verse says 'nearest kinsman' — we must identify who is 'nearest.'
Answer
The son is nearer: he stands in his father's place to designate a Hebrew maidservant as a wife for himself (יעוד). A brother cannot do this for his brother's maidservant.
Counter-Claim
On the contrary — include the brother as the closer heir! A brother stands in the place of his deceased brother for yibum (levirate marriage). The son does not perform yibum for his father!
Refutation
Is there levirate marriage when there is a son? No! The entire obligation of yibum is voided when there is a living son. Therefore the son — who eliminates yibum — is clearly the closer relative.
Key Insight The son's power to cancel yibum demonstrates he is the primary heir. His presence supersedes the brother entirely — in inheritance and in levirate obligation.

🔗 Logic Chain: Why Son Outranks Brother as Heir

Step 1 — The Verse: Numbers 27:11 says the inheritance goes to 'the kinsman nearest to him' — requires us to rank relatives.
↓
Step 2 — First Candidate: Son. A son stands in his father's place for yiud (designation of a Hebrew maidservant). Brother cannot do this for his brother.
↓
Step 3 — Challenge: Brother. A brother stands in his brother's place for yibum (levirate marriage). Son cannot perform yibum for his father's wife.
↓
Step 4 — Refutation: Yibum only applies when there is no son. The son's very existence cancels yibum — proving the son is the superior relative.
↓
Conclusion: Son inherits before brother. Father's inheritance from son is derived from 'nearest kinsman' — and is dear to the Tanna for being a derived, not explicit, law.
