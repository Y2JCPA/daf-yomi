Bava Batra 37 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Gemara's inference from Rabbi Yishmael, if one harvests olives from some trees in a grove of 20, hazaka is established for:
A. Only the olive trees that were harvested
B. All 20 trees, including date palms of a different species
C. Only trees of the same species as the harvested fruit
D. No trees — one must harvest all species to establish hazaka
Correct: B — Rabbi Yishmael holds that harvesting one species establishes hazaka for all 20 trees, even different species — provided the other trees did not bear fruit that season.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the Gemara distinguish between a seller who retains a pit and one who retains trees?
A. Trees are more valuable than pits under Jewish law
B. A pit can be moved; trees cannot
C. A pit causes no harm to surrounding land; trees do cause harm
D. The dispute between Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbis applies only to trees
Correct: C — The Gemara states explicitly: a pit causes no harm to adjacent land, so no surrounding land is reserved. Trees cause harm to surrounding land, so a seller retaining trees implicitly keeps the adjacent land — otherwise the buyer could demand he uproot them.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the practical consequence that drives the ruling that a seller who retains trees also keeps the surrounding land?
A. The tree owner must irrigate the land around the trees
B. Without adjacent land rights, the buyer could demand the seller uproot his trees
C. Trees planted on another's land become the landowner's property
D. The seller would owe rent to the buyer for using the surrounding land
Correct: B — The Gemara reasons: since trees cause harm to the land, if the seller had no rights to the surrounding land, the buyer could say 'uproot your trees and go.' To prevent this, the law grants the seller the surrounding land automatically.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rabbi Zeira resolve Rava's challenge to the Sages of Neharde'a regarding overcrowded orchards and alfalfa fields?
A. Alfalfa is an exception because it regrows after harvesting
B. Rava's challenge is rejected — alfalfa also has no hazaka
C. This is a dispute between tanna'im: Rabbi Shimon rules a crowded vineyard is not legally a vineyard
D. The ruling of Neharde'a applies only in Babylonia, not in Israel
Correct: C — Rabbi Zeira shows this is a tannaitic dispute. Rabbi Shimon holds that a vineyard with less than 4 cubits of open space is not legally a vineyard — consistent with Neharde'a. The Rabbis disagree, which explains why hazaka might apply to such dense plantings.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to the Sages of Neharde'a's ruling about selling a date tree, what does the buyer acquire beyond the tree itself?
A. Nothing — only the tree is conveyed
B. The fruit of the tree for the next three years
C. The land surrounding the tree at ground level
D. The land from the base of the tree downward to the depths of the earth
Correct: D — The Sages of Neharde'a rule that a buyer of a date tree acquires the land from the tree's base to the depths — reflecting that a date tree's deep roots make the subterranean land integral to the tree's existence and thus included in the sale.
