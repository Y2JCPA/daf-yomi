📖 Overview

המוכר את החצר — מכר את הבתים, הבורות, השיחין והמערות
🏛️ Three Mishnas: What Does a Sale Include? Daf 67 continues the foundational question of sales law: when a seller transfers property without explicit specification, what exactly passes to the buyer? Three mishnayot tackle courtyards, olive presses, and bathhouses — each drawing the line between immovable fixtures (sold) and movable property (not sold). The Gemara probes edge cases, resolves contradictions between tannaitic sources, and applies the principle that a buyer acquires only what is conventionally understood to be part of the named item.

🏠 Courtyard Sale: What Is Included?

✅ Included (Sages)

  • Outer houses opening to courtyard
  • Inner houses accessed via outer houses
  • Pits, ditches, and caves
  • Stores opening into the courtyard
  • The area/space of the courtyard itself

❌ Not Included (Sages)

  • Movable property of any kind
  • Stores opening to public domain only
  • Items not fixed to the courtyard

⚖️ Rabbi Eliezer's View

  • Sale of 'courtyard' conveys only its airspace
  • No houses are included — not even outer ones
  • Buyer acquires the open space alone
  • Most restrictive interpretation of sale

⚖️ Stores Opening to Two Domains — Contradiction Resolved

Baraita (Tosefta)
Stores that open both into the courtyard and into the public domain are sold along with the courtyard — they are attached to it and are part of the sale.
Rabbi Ḥiyya's Baraita
Such stores are not sold with the courtyard — because they open to the public domain, they have independent access and are not truly part of the courtyard.
Rabba
No contradiction. If the seller said darta (the full estate complex), everyone agrees the houses are included. The dispute arises only when he says simply 'courtyard' — then Tosefta and R. Ḥiyya disagree.
Alternative Version
When the seller says darta, all agree houses are sold. The dispute is only over stores opening to both domains — Tosefta includes them, R. Ḥiyya excludes them.
📌 Key Term דרתא (Darta) — Aramaic for a courtyard estate complex, implying the full compound including buildings

🏖️ Sand Field & Pond — Acquiring Two Distinct Properties

Seller sells buyer a sand field (for glass-making) and a pond (for fishing) together
↓
Buyer takes possession of sand field only
Rav Naḥman (via Rabba): Has he acquired the pond?
Compare: Shmuel's case
If one sells ten fields and buyer takes possession of one — he acquires all ten
↓
Distinction: Ten fields share one use (farming) in one geographic block → possessing one acquires all. But sand field (glass) and pond (fish) have different uses → may require separate acts of acquisition
↓
Rabba's final ruling: Even so, taking possession of the sand field does acquire the pond — they were sold together as a unit

🛁 Bathhouse Sale — What Passes to Buyer

Mishna: One who sells a bathhouse has not sold the floor boards, the basins, or the curtains (habilaniyot) — these are movable furnishings
↓
But if the seller says 'I sell you it and all that is within it' — then even movable items are included
↓
Baraita (Tosefta): The sale does include the storeroom for boards, the storeroom for yekamin implements, and the storeroom for other bath equipment — these rooms are integral to the bathhouse
↓
Fixed structures (boilers, pools, tiled floors, permanent walls) — sold with the bathhouse as part of its identity
↓
Unifying principle: Anything that gives the property its name and function is included; furnishings that could be moved to any location are not
