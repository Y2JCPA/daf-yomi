⚖️ Husband's Status: Heir or Purchaser?

Rav Pappa
If the husband is an heir, then his father-in-law's widow can still collect sustenance from the estate — it remains liened.
Rav Pappa
If the husband is a purchaser, sustenance cannot be collected from him — a creditor cannot collect from a purchaser for an oral loan.
Abaye
We already know from the mishna in Bekhorot: property inherited from a wife does not return in Jubilee. This proves the husband is an heir, not a purchaser!
Rava
But the Usha ordinance — a wife who sold her מלוג property and died, the husband repossesses it from buyers — treats him as a purchaser when it benefits him!
Rav Ashi
The Sages equated the husband with both an heir and a purchaser — applying whichever status is more favorable to him in each specific context.
📌 Conclusion The husband is treated as an heir or purchaser situationally — whichever benefits him. This is not contradiction but intentional rabbinic flexibility.

👶 Adults vs. Minors: Separate Shares

Adults (בנים גדולים)

  • Inherit their portion of the estate
  • Cannot take from minors' share for sustenance
  • Wedding expenses paid from common pool
  • If one brother wears communal garments — what's done is done (Rava)

Minors (בנים קטנים)

  • Also inherit their portion separately
  • Cannot be sustained from adults' funds
  • Cannot demand same wedding budget as adults had
  • Father's actual spending sets the standard

🔑 Is the Husband an Heir or Purchaser? (Avuh bar Geneiva's Question)

Avuh bar Geneiva
A woman borrowed money, then married. Is her husband a purchaser (creditor cannot collect from him) or an heir (creditor can collect)?
Rava
The mishna hints: adult daughters married and transferred funds to husbands — this shows the husband receives property like a purchaser!
Gemara
Rejected! Wedding expenses are different — they generate publicity (פרסומי מילתא). The creditor could have collected before the wedding. No proof.
Rabbi Ḥiyya
When adults marry and transfer funds, minors can actually collect back from the older sisters' husbands — suggesting the husbands hold communal property!
⚠️ No Resolution from Mishna The wedding-expense context is too unique to prove the husband's general status regarding prior debts.

⚖️ Rav Ashi's Framework: Dual Status of Husband

Step 1 — Jubilee Year: Property inherited from wife does not return at Jubilee. Husband treated as heir → benefits him by keeping land permanently.
↓
Step 2 — Usha Ordinance: Wife sells מלוג property; husband reclaims it from buyers after her death. Husband treated as purchaser → benefits him by recovering assets.
↓
Step 3 — Purchasers' Own Risk: Buyers who purchased from a married woman knew her husband existed. They accepted the risk — their loss is self-caused.
↓
Step 4 — General Principle: The Sages intentionally gave husbands dual status. Apply heir-status or purchaser-status based on which is more favorable in context.
↓
Step 5 — Practical Result: Pre-marital oral debts of the wife — the husband is likely treated as a purchaser, shielding him from collection (though the mishna provides no direct proof).
