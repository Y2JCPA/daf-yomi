Bava Batra 139 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the mishna, what happens when minor children request the same wedding budget that the adult children received during their father's lifetime?
A. The court grants them the same amount as the adults received
B. The court does not listen to them; they receive only what the father originally allocated
C. The court splits the difference between the father's allocation and the adults' actual spending
D. The minors may collect the difference from the adults' shares
Correct: B — The mishna explicitly states that if minors demand the same as adults received during the father's lifetime, the court does not listen — they receive whatever the father had originally designated for them.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Rava ruled about a brother who wore communal garments from the estate. What was the halakhic outcome?
A. The brother must reimburse the estate for the full value of the garments
B. The other brothers may deduct the garment's value from his inheritance share
C. What he did is done — the other brothers cannot subtract it from his share
D. He loses his right to future distributions
Correct: C — Rava rules that for a brother who 'handles' the estate (not merely idle), wearing communal garments is done and cannot be deducted from his share — unlike an unemployed person who has no right to extra provisions.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the definition of a 'large estate' according to Rav (as cited by Rav Yehuda) and Rabbi Yoḥanan?
A. Any estate worth more than 200 zuz
B. An estate large enough to provide sustenance for all parties for twelve months
C. An estate that contains real property, not just movables
D. An estate sufficient to pay off all the deceased's debts with remainder
Correct: B — Both Rav and Rabbi Yoḥanan define a 'large' estate as one that can provide sustenance for both the sons and daughters for twelve months — only then do the sons inherit and daughters receive sustenance.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How did Rav Ashi resolve the apparent contradiction between the husband being treated as an heir (Jubilee) and as a purchaser (Usha ordinance)?
A. He ruled the husband is always an heir, and the Usha ordinance is an exception
B. He ruled the husband is always a purchaser, and the Jubilee case is a special leniency
C. The Sages equated the husband with both statuses and applied whichever is more favorable to him
D. He distinguished between melog property and iron-flock property
Correct: C — Rav Ashi's resolution is that the Sages deliberately gave the husband dual status — heir and purchaser — applying whichever benefits him in the specific circumstance. This is not contradiction but intentional rabbinic design.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
The Gemara explains why purchasers who bought from a wife have no claim against the husband who reclaims the property under the Usha ordinance. What is the reason?
A. The wife was legally prohibited from selling, so the sale was void ab initio
B. The purchasers knew a husband existed and should not have bought property under his authority — their loss is self-caused
C. The husband paid fair market value to the purchasers before reclaiming
D. The Usha ordinance only applies when the husband was unaware of the sale
Correct: B — The Gemara justifies the purchasers' loss by saying they knew the woman had a husband and still bought from her — removing property from under his authority. Having taken that known risk, they bear responsibility for any resulting loss.
