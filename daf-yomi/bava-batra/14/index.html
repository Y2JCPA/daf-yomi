📖 Overview

בבא בתרא י״ד
🏛️ Daf 14: Sacred Proportions & Canon This daf moves from the physical requirements of Torah scrolls (length must equal circumference) to a rich debate about the Ark of the Covenant — its dimensions and precisely what it contained. Rabbis Meir and Yehuda disagree on the length of the biblical cubit, yielding different conclusions about whether the Torah scroll fit inside the Ark or rested on the Philistine chest beside it. The daf concludes by establishing the canonical order of Nevi'im and Ketuvim and the tradition of who authored each biblical book.

⚖️ Was the Torah Scroll Inside the Ark?

Rabbi Meir
The cubit used for the Ark was 6 handbreadths. The Ark was therefore 15 handbreadths long and 9 handbreadths wide. The two tablets (6 hb wide each) occupied 12 hb, leaving 3 handbreadths for the Torah scroll — which sat inside the Ark. The broken first tablets were also inside. The Philistine chest was placed outside.
Rabbi Yehuda
The cubit was only 5 handbreadths. The Ark was 12.5 handbreadths long and 7.5 handbreadths wide. The tablets took up 12 hb, leaving only 0.5 handbreadths — no room for a Torah scroll inside! The scroll therefore rested on the Philistine chest placed alongside the Ark.
📖 The Verse at Stake "There was nothing in the Ark except the two tablets of stone" (I Kings 8:9). Rabbi Meir reads this as an amplification — something else was inside: the broken tablets. Rabbi Yehuda: it literally means only the tablets.

📐 Ark Dimensions: Two Cubits, Two Worlds

Rabbi Meir — 6-Handbreadth Cubit

  • Cubit = 6 handbreadths (טפחים)
  • Ark length: 2.5 × 6 = 15 handbreadths
  • Ark width: 1.5 × 6 = 9 handbreadths
  • Two tablets (6 hb each) = 12 hb in length
  • Remaining 3 hb → Torah scroll inside Ark
  • Broken tablets: also inside the Ark
  • Philistine chest: stored outside

Rabbi Yehuda — 5-Handbreadth Cubit

  • Cubit = 5 handbreadths (טפחים)
  • Ark length: 2.5 × 5 = 12.5 handbreadths
  • Ark width: 1.5 × 5 = 7.5 handbreadths
  • Two tablets (6 hb each) = 12 hb in length
  • Remaining 0.5 hb → too small for scroll
  • Torah scroll: rested on Philistine chest alongside Ark
  • Source: "put it in a chest by the side of it" (I Sam. 6:8)

📏 The Torah Scroll Proportions Rule

A Torah scroll is written and rolled up
↓
Is the scroll's height (length) equal to its circumference when fully rolled?
✅ Equal — Ideal
Valid, ideal scroll
Rav Huna wrote 70 scrolls and achieved equality only once
Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov achieved it by writing on calf hide
❌ Unequal — Suboptimal
Does not meet the standard
Neither length > circumference nor circumference > length is permitted by the Sages — the scroll should be neither too tall nor too wide

✍️ Who Wrote Each Biblical Book?

Moses wrote: the Torah (Five Books), the portion of Balaam (Numbers 22–24), and the book of Job
↓
Joshua wrote: his own book and the last eight verses of the Torah describing Moses' death (since Moses could not write his own death)
↓
Samuel wrote: his own book, the book of Judges, and the book of Ruth
↓
David wrote: the book of Psalms — incorporating compositions of ten elders: Adam, Melchizedek, Abraham, Moses, Heman, Yedutun, Asaph, and the three sons of Korach
↓
Jeremiah wrote: his own book, the book of Kings, and Lamentations
↓
Hezekiah and his colleagues edited/transmitted: Isaiah, Proverbs, Song of Songs, and Ecclesiastes
↓
The Men of the Great Assembly wrote/transmitted: Ezekiel, the Twelve Prophets, Daniel, and the Scroll of Esther
↓
Ezra wrote: his own book and the genealogies of Chronicles up to his era; Nehemiah ben Hachaliah completed Chronicles
