⚖️ Rav Sheshet vs. Rav Naḥman: Who Gets the Property?

Rav Naḥman
The mother gave property to Rami bar Ḥama (evening deed), then retracted and gave to Rav Ukva bar Ḥama (morning deed). I rule: Rav Ukva owns the property — a shechiv mera may retract and reassign.
Rav Sheshet
But she died! A deathbed gift not retracted before death takes effect. Rami bar Ḥama received the first deed — he should be the owner!
Rav Naḥman
Shmuel taught that a shechiv mera may retract. I hold this extends to redirecting the gift to another person — her power over her property lasted until death.
Rav Sheshet
Shmuel said he may retract to keep it for himself. Did Shmuel also say he may retract in order to give it to another? That is a separate question you cannot simply extend!
Ruling Rav Naḥman's ruling stood: Rav Ukva bar Ḥama was awarded the property. A shechiv mera retains full authority over property — including power to redirect gifts — until death.

🔄 Can a Shechiv Mera Retract to Give to Another Person?

Shmuel
A shechiv mera may retract his gift if he wishes to retain the property for himself. The gift is like a will — if he recovers, it becomes void.
Rav Sheshet
Shmuel said he may retract to keep it for himself. But did Shmuel also rule that he may retract in order to transfer it to a different person? You cannot simply infer this!
Rav Naḥman
The shechiv mera's power over his property is comprehensive. It includes the ability to redirect any gift to a new recipient — the right to retract is not limited to self-retention.
Rav Sheshet
You have left me with no answer. You must have a tradition from Shmuel that I lack. I accept your ruling, though I do not find the logic compelling.
Principle — מתנת שכיב מרע A shechiv mera retains dominion over property until death, including power to revoke and reassign any prior gift.

🏛️ Full Deathbed Gift vs. Partial Deathbed Gift

Full Gift — All Property

  • Donor gives away everything he owns
  • No kinyan (act of acquisition) required
  • Gift takes effect automatically at death
  • Rabbinic enactment — treated like a will
  • Exception: if he wrote a deed, kinyan may still be required (Shmuel)

Partial Gift — Some Property

  • Donor retains some land or property for himself
  • Kinyan IS required for validity
  • Without kinyan: gift fails even if donor dies
  • Heirs inherit the gifted portion
  • Final halakha follows this strict requirement

📖 Five Incidents of Deathbed Gifts on This Daf

Mother of Rav Zutra bar Toviyya — Gave property to avoid her future husband Rav Zevid acquiring it. Married him, then died. Gift was upheld as a valid shechiv mera transfer.
↓
Mother of Rami bar Ḥama — Wrote two conflicting deeds to two sons. Rav Naḥman awarded to the second son (Rav Ukva), ruling a shechiv mera may redirect any gift.
↓
Mother of Rav Amram the Pious — Verbally gave a bundle of promissory notes at death. Brothers argued: no meshikha (pulling) performed. Rav Naḥman: monetary documents are acquired by transfer of the document itself.
↓
Sister of Rav Tovi bar Rav Mattana — Gave property to Rav Tovi in the morning; cried at by Rav Aḥadvoi that evening, retracted. Validity of the retraction was examined.
↓
Sister of Rav Dimi bar Yosef — Repeatedly gave orchard when sick, retracted when recovering. Finally gave via messenger; witnesses testified she said 'Woe, that woman is dying!' Rav Naḥman ruled it a valid deathbed gift.
