🍷 When Is the Seller Liable for Soured Wine?

Seller sold wine — it subsequently turned sour
↓
Soured in buyer's jugs
Seller not liable — buyer's domain, buyer's risk
Soured in seller's jugs
Seller is liable — wine was already defective at sale
↓
Special rule: if seller's wine is known to always sour → sale is a mistaken transaction (מקח טעות) regardless of location

🍷 Why Does Wine Sour? A Fundamental Dispute

R. Yosei b. R. Ḥanina
Wine souring reflects a defect in the wine itself. If it soured while still in the seller's jugs, the buyer may void the sale — it was already flawed at the moment of purchase.
Rav Ḥiyya bar Yosef
Wine souring is caused by the owner's bad fortune (מזל). Once the buyer acquires it, it becomes his misfortune. As Habakkuk 2:5 states: "Wine is treacherous" — it deceives its new owner.
R. Yosei b. R. Ḥanina
Proof from the mishna: if a particular seller's wine always sours, it is a mistaken transaction — this shows souring is an objective defect, not mere personal misfortune.
Rava (explaining)
R. Yosei needed to explain the mishna's case as wine left for cooking (not drinking). This is why the souring location matters — the buyer specifically asked for wine that would keep.
Key Verse — Habakkuk 2:5 "The haughty man abides not at home" (יַהִיר וְלֹא יִנְוֶה) — interpreted as wine that betrays its owner

⚖️ Rava's Vintner Partnership: The Vol Shefat Case

Vintner sends wine with a middleman to sell at Vol Shefat; they split profits
↓
Middleman agrees: "I accept the risk if wine turns to vinegar after reaching Vol Shefat"
↓
Vinegar before Vol Shefat
Vintner bears the loss — outside scope of middleman's accepted risk
Vinegar after Vol Shefat
Middleman bears the loss — within his agreed acceptance
↓
Alternate opinion: Even before Vol Shefat, vintner bears loss — per R. Yosei b. R. Ḥanina: wine still in vintner's domain = vintner's misfortune

📚 Ben Sira on Living Arrangements "I weighed everything in the balance and found nothing inferior to bran; but inferior to bran is a son-in-law who lives in his father-in-law's house; and inferior to him is a guest who overstays his welcome."

This supports the Mishna's phrasing: a bridal house is built for the son — he brings his wife to his own home. A widowhood home is for the daughter — she returns to her father's house after her husband dies. Living in one's in-law's home is considered deeply undesirable.

🕍 The Height Formula: Sources and Opinions

First Tanna

  • Height = ½ length + ½ width
  • Mathematical proportional standard
  • Proof: modeled on the Sanctuary
  • Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel affirms this matches Tabernacle proportions
  • Tabernacle: 10 cubits high, 10 wide → height = width

Aḥerim (Others)

  • Height = length of the crossbeams
  • Crossbeams span the full width
  • Therefore: height ≈ width of structure
  • Geometrically equivalent to First Tanna for square buildings
  • Differs in framing, not necessarily outcome

R. Ḥanina's Contradiction

  • Two verses describe Sanctuary dimensions differently
  • Contradiction raised when teaching in the villages
  • Resolution: one verse counts only the area above the cherubs
  • The cherubs themselves occupied space below that line
  • Shows the height formula's scriptural grounding requires careful exegesis
