Rules for Scribes: Dates & Locations

📅 Rava bar Rav Sheila: When writing a deed of acquisition, write the actual date of the transaction
↓
❓ If you don't know the exact date, write today's date — the day you are writing
↓
🚫 Never write an approximate or estimated date — the document must not appear as a falsehood (שקר)
↓
📍 Rav & Rav Huna to their scribes: Always write the location where you are currently writing
↓
🏘️ Even if the transaction occurred in Hini, if you're writing in Shili, write Shili — and vice versa
PRINCIPLE Document integrity requires that the metadata (date and location) reflect the act of writing, not estimates about the underlying transaction.

Why Can't We Split a 100-Dinar Note Into Two 50s?

Creditor
Exchange my 100-dinar note for two 50-dinar notes — so when debtor pays half, I can return one note and keep the other.
Rava
❌ We do not prepare this. The Sages designed the system to benefit both parties.
Rava
📈 Benefit to creditor: A single large note gives more leverage to coerce repayment.
Rava
📉 Benefit to debtor: When he pays half, the note becomes vitiated (שטר מקויים) — creditor must take an oath to collect the rest.
KEY CONCEPT A vitiated note (שטר שנפרע מקצתו) requires the creditor to swear an oath before collecting the remainder — a powerful protection for the debtor.

Restructuring Promissory Notes: Three Refusals

Split 100 → Two 50s (Rava)

  • Creditor loses coercion power of large note
  • Debtor loses oath protection from vitiated note
  • ❌ Court refuses

Combine Two 50s → 100 (Rava)

  • Creditor loses clean collection on first 50
  • Debtor faces greater coercion from larger note
  • ❌ Court refuses

Reduce 100 → 50 (Rav Ashi)

  • Debtor may have already paid all 100
  • Creditor could use new 50 note + claim receipt was for different loan
  • ❌ Court refuses — fraud risk
RAVA & RAV ASHI Courts never restructure promissory notes — whether splitting, combining, or reducing — because the original form protects both parties or prevents fraud.

Mishna: Two People Named Yosef ben Shimon

Two people in one city both named Yosef ben Shimon
↓
Can they present promissory notes against each other or be sued by others?
Against Each Other
❌ Debtor claims: "You owed me — I returned this note to you when you repaid!"
Third Party Sues
❌ Each says: "It's the other Yosef ben Shimon who owes you!"
↓
Solution: Triple the name — Yosef ben Shimon ben [grandfather]
↓
If grandfathers match too → add a distinguishing marker (e.g., "the short one," "the tall one")
MISHNA If a receipt says 'Yosef ben Shimon's note is repaid' and both owe money, both notes are considered repaid — we cannot determine which was meant.
