Bava Batra 26 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Baraita, what is the primary reason the Sages required four cubits between a tree and a neighbor's vineyard?
A. To prevent the tree's shade from harming the grapevines
B. To allow the vineyard owner room to bring oxen and a plow
C. To prevent roots from reaching the neighbor's soil
D. To give space for birds to land without entering the vineyard
Correct: B — The Baraita explicitly states the four cubits are 'for the work of the vineyard' — the owner needs room to plow with oxen without trespassing on the neighbor's field.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why did Rava bar Rav Ḥanan ultimately refuse to cut his palm trees, even after Rav Yosef demanded it due to bird damage?
A. He proved the birds were Rav Yosef's responsibility under Talmudic law
B. He cited his acquired right (chazakah) to maintain the trees
C. He relied on Rav's ruling that a fruit tree producing even one kav may not be cut down
D. He showed the trees were beyond the four-cubit boundary
Correct: C — Rava invoked Rav's ruling based on bal tashchit (Deut. 20:19): a palm producing even one kav of fruit is prohibited to cut down, making it impossible to comply with Rav Yosef's demand.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Ravina resolve the apparent contradiction in the two clauses of the Me'ila mishna regarding tree roots?
A. In both cases the roots belong to the tree regardless of distance
B. Within 16 cubits roots follow the tree; beyond 16 cubits they follow the land
C. The two clauses discuss different species of trees, not distance
D. The contradiction is left unresolved — it is a teku
Correct: B — Ravina distinguishes by distance: roots within 16 cubits of the trunk are considered part of the tree; roots beyond 16 cubits are considered part of the land they grow in, resolving both clauses of the Me'ila mishna.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Ulla, what are the two legal consequences when a person maintains a tree within sixteen cubits of a property boundary?
A. The neighbor acquires the tree, and the owner must pay compensation
B. The tree must be cut and its fruit declared ownerless
C. The owner is considered a robber and may not bring first fruits (bikkurim) from that tree
D. The owner must give half the fruit to the neighbor as payment
Correct: C — Ulla rules such a person is a 'gazlan' (robber) since the tree steals nourishment from the neighbor's soil, and cannot bring bikkurim from its fruit — doing so would be a mitzva fulfilled through a transgression.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
When Rav Huna was digging deep and cutting Rav Pappa's palm roots, how did he justify going beyond three handbreadths?
A. He claimed roots beyond three handbreadths are automatically ownerless
B. He cited the Mishna: one digging a cistern, ditch, or cave may cut as deep as needed and owns the wood
C. He argued Rav Pappa's trees had no legal standing within the four-cubit zone
D. He invoked an acquired right (chazakah) to use the land beneath
Correct: B — Rav Huna cited the Mishna explicitly: if one is digging a cistern, ditch, or cave, he may cut roots to any depth required, and the wood from those cut roots belongs to the digger — overriding the usual three-handbreadth limit.
