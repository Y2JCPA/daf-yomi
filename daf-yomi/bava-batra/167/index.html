⚖️ Mishna: When One Party Suffices for the Scribe

One Party Sufficient

  • Get (bill of divorce) — husband requests alone
  • Ketubbah receipt — wife requests alone
  • Promissory note — debtor requests alone
  • Bill of sale — seller requests alone

Both Parties Required

  • Promissory note requested by creditor — needs debtor present
  • Bill of sale requested by buyer — needs seller present
  • Betrothal and marriage documents — groom and bride
  • Sharecropper/contractor agreements — both parties
  • Arbitration and court enactments — both litigants

✍️ Abaye's Document Safety Rules

בבא בתרא קסז.
Abaye's Rulings Rule 1 — Signature corroboration: Never show your signature by writing it at the end of blank parchment. An unscrupulous person may discover it and write text above the signature, forging an entire document.

Rule 2 — Numbers at line's end: Do not write any number from 3 through 10 at the end of a line. In Hebrew/Aramaic, these can be extended — e.g., ג (3) becomes ג׳ with an added mark — inflating the debt amount.

📜 Three Incidents of Document Forgery

Case 1 — The Orchard: A bill of sale read 'one-third of the orchard [befardeisa].' A forger erased letters of befardeisa (פרדיסא) to make it read pardes (פרדס) — claiming the entire orchard rather than one-third.
↓
Case 2 — The Brother Aḥai: A bill of sale listed 'the portions of Reuven and Shimon, brothers [aḥei].' They happened to have a brother named Aḥai. Without vowel marks, aḥei (brothers) reads as Aḥai (a name) — potentially implicating him as a third seller.
↓
Case 3 — Rava's Signature: A document bore Rava's signature alongside Rav Aḥa bar Adda's. Rava said: 'This is indeed my signature — but I never signed any document before Rav Aḥa bar Adda in my life,' suggesting his signature had been forged onto a later document.

🏷️ What Did Rav Mean? The Sages Clarify

Rav Yehuda citing Rav
The Mishna requires the scribe to 'recognize the parties.' This means: he must know the man's name when writing a get, and the woman's name when writing a receipt.
Rav Safra & Colleagues
If he only knows one name, there's a problem! A man could write a get and give it to a different man's wife who shares her name. And a woman could give a receipt to a different husband who shares his name!
Abaye
You are not understanding Rav correctly. Rav requires the scribe to know both the man's name and the woman's name before writing any document. Knowing only one is insufficient.
Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana
But what of a newcomer? Anyone whose name has been established in a city for thirty days — there is no concern his name is false. It is presumed genuine.
Abaye
For a newcomer under thirty days: it is sufficient that people call him by that name and he responds to it.
Practical Outcome A scribe may write a document only when he knows both parties' names — either from 30-day residency or from observed name-responses.
