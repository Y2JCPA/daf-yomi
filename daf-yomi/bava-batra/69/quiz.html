QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Ulla, what do 'stones for its use' — the stones sold with the field — refer to?
A. Stones placed on top of sheaves to secure grain during threshing
B. Stones arranged in preparation for building a fence around the field
C. Stones used as corner boundary markers for the field
D. Stones embedded in the soil as part of the field's foundation
Correct: B — Ulla interprets 'stones for its use' as stones arranged for the future building of a fence, in contrast to the Babylonian interpretation of stones placed on sheaves during threshing.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why is a plastered watch station NOT included in the field sale, even though it is attached to the ground?
A. Rabbinic decree always treats watch stations as personal property of the farmer
B. It is considered a distinct, significant entity — the plastering makes it permanent and valuable enough to stand apart
C. It is too large to be considered part of the field's agricultural function
D. Watch stations require a separate bill of sale regardless of construction
Correct: B — Even though the plastered watch station is physically attached to the ground, the Gemara rules it is a distinct significant entity. Its permanence (due to plastering) makes it significant enough not to transfer automatically with the field.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
If a seller says 'I sell you the land with palm trees' but there are no palm trees on the land, what is the legal result?
A. The sale is valid; the buyer receives a monetary credit for the absent trees
B. The sale stands but the seller must plant two palm trees within thirty days
C. The transaction is void as a mistaken transaction (מקח טעות)
D. The buyer may rescind only the portion of the price attributed to the trees
Correct: C — When a seller conditions a sale with 'with palm trees,' the palm trees become a prerequisite of the transaction. If they do not exist, the sale is a mistaken transaction (מקח טעות) and is entirely void.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
From which biblical verse does Rav derive that a grafted carob and sycamore trunk are not included in a field sale?
A. The laws of bikkurim (first fruits) which separately enumerate field and tree produce
B. The verse describing Ephron's field at Machpelah, which itemizes specific trees distinctly
C. The laws of shemita (sabbatical year) which treat trees and field crops as different categories
D. A verse in Devarim specifying that fruit trees survive a military conquest separately
Correct: B — Rav (citing Rav Yehuda) derives this from the Torah's unusually detailed description of Ephron's field in Machpelah (Bereishit 23:17), where specific trees are enumerated — implying that certain notable trees are distinct entities not automatically transferred with the field.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rav's rule when a seller says 'except for the trees,' which trees does the seller retain?
A. All fruit-bearing trees regardless of height; ornamental trees transfer with the land
B. Any tree so tall that one must climb it by rope to harvest; shorter trees are included in the sale
C. All trees over three handbreadths tall, measured from the base of the trunk
D. Only trees that were planted by the seller personally; inherited trees transfer with the land
Correct: B — Rav rules that when a seller excludes 'the trees,' the operative distinction is harvestability: any tree requiring a rope to climb for harvesting is retained by the seller, while any tree that can be harvested without climbing is included in the sale.
