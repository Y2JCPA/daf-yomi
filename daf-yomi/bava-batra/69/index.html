🪨 Two Interpretations: Stones 'For Its Use'

Babylonia
Stones 'for its use' refers to stones placed on top of sheaves during threshing — weighted down to secure the grain.
Ulla
Stones 'for its use' refers to stones arranged in preparation for building a fence around the field.
R. Meir (Babylonian view)
Stones ready for use on sheaves are included in the sale. Stones not yet prepared remain with the seller.
R. Meir (Ulla's view)
Stones prepared for a future fence are included in the sale. Stones not yet prepared for the fence are excluded.
Shared Principle Both opinions agree: stones that actively serve the field's function transfer with the sale. The dispute is solely about which function defines 'for its use.'

🌾 Field Sale: Included vs. Excluded Items

✅ Sold With the Field

  • Stones designated for field use
  • Reeds in vineyard used as vine supports
  • Produce still attached to ground (even past harvest time)
  • Watch station NOT plastered with clay
  • Palm trees (with proper bill of sale language)
  • Grapevines (even when trees excluded)

❌ NOT Sold With the Field

  • Stones not designated for the field
  • Unsmoothed reeds (per R. Meir)
  • Reed cluster occupying a beit rova area
  • Watch station plastered with clay
  • Grafted carob tree
  • Sycamore trunk
  • Trees too tall to harvest without a rope (per Rav)

🌲 Biblical Source: Grafted Carob & Sycamore Excluded

שְׂדֵה עֶפְרוֹן אֲשֶׁר בַּמַּכְפֵּלָה — "The field of Ephron which was in Machpelah" (Bereishit 23:17)
Rav citing Rav Yehuda — Derived from Ephron's Field The Torah describes the sale of Ephron's field in Machpelah with an unusually detailed enumeration of its trees (Bereishit 23:17). This teaches that specific trees are distinct entities not automatically transferred with the field. A grafted carob (a high-value, cultivated tree) and a sycamore trunk (valuable for its regenerating stump) are retained by the seller unless explicitly included in the sale deed.

📜 Bill of Sale: Palm Tree Scenarios

Standard bill of sale should include: "Acquire the palm trees, dates, branches, and seedlings." Even without this, buyer acquires them — but explicit language is best practice.
↓
"I sell you land and palm trees" → Seller must provide at least 2 palm trees (minimum to justify the plural) plus the land itself.
↓
"I sell you land with palm trees" → If palm trees exist on the land: valid sale. If none exist: מקח טעות — mistaken transaction; sale is void.
↓
"...except for the trees" → If field has multiple tree types (olive + fig), seller keeps all trees; buyer gets land. If only one type of tree: seller keeps that type only.
↓
Rav's Rope Rule: Any tree so tall it requires a rope to climb for harvesting → retained by seller. Any tree harvestable without climbing → included in the sale.
