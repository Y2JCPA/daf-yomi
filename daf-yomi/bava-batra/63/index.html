🏠 Property Transactions: Precision of Language

דבר שלא בא לעולם — one cannot transfer ownership of something not yet in existence
Core Theme Daf 63 probes the precise language of property sales and gifts. Three tensions emerge: (1) Does 'portion' differ from 'share' in a gift? (2) Can a Levite retain tithe rights to a field he already sold — or does דבר שלא בא לעולם block this? (3) When a seller reserves the upper story of a house, what rights does he retain? The daf also begins examining what bill-of-sale language includes pits and cisterns.

🤝 'Share' vs. 'Portion' — What Amount Is Conveyed?

חֵלֶק — Share

  • 'So-and-so should share in my property'
  • Unambiguously conveys half of the property
  • Equal partnership language
  • No legal dispute about quantity

מָנָה — Portion

  • 'Give so-and-so a portion of my property'
  • Amount is legally unclear — Gemara raises the question
  • Sumakhos: for a cistern, minimum = ¼ log of water
  • Context-dependent; minimum threshold applies

⚖️ Davar Shelo Ba L'olam — Challenge and Resolution

Questioner
Why should the Levite ever receive tithes after selling the field? A person cannot transfer ownership of something not yet in the world! The tithes don't exist at the time of the stipulation — this should be invalid.
Gemara
The Levite is not transferring the tithes to himself — he is withholding them from the sale. He never sold them in the first place. A seller may always exclude any part of a property from a sale.
Reish Lakish
From here we learn: one who sells a house saying 'the upper story [דְּיוֹטָא] is mine' — the upper story remains his. He sold only the lower story. Retention ≠ transfer of a future object.
Rav Zevid
Reish Lakish's novelty is not simply that the seller keeps the upper story — that is obvious. His teaching is that if the upper story collapses, the seller may rebuild it, and the buyer must allow him to maintain the supporting structures.
Distinction Retaining part of a sale is always valid. Transferring a future nonexistent object (davar shelo ba l'olam) is not. These are fundamentally different acts.

📋 Rav Dimi: How to Write a Complete House Sale

Basic sale: 'I sell you this house' — transfers only the main structure; pit, cistern, and upper story are all excluded by default
↓
Add 'depth and height' (עומקא ורומא): Rav Dimi of Neharde'a — this language is necessary to convey the full vertical scope of the property
↓
But 'depth and height' is still insufficient: the mishna (64a) explicitly rules that pit and cistern remain excluded even with this expanded language
↓
Seller must enumerate underground structures: pit and cistern require explicit listing in the bill of sale to transfer ownership
↓
General principle: The more distinct and valuable a feature, the more explicit the language required — a sale is interpreted narrowly in favor of the seller's retained rights
