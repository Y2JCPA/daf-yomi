⚖️ Unspecified Cellar, Buyer Silent: Must He Accept Souring Wine?

Rav Aḥa
Even when the seller says 'a wine cellar' without specifying which one, and the buyer states no purpose, the buyer still accepts up to 10 souring barrels per 100. I derive this from a precise reading of Rav Zevid's baraita, which requires good quality only when the buyer specifies 'for cooking.'
Ravina
No — when the seller says 'a wine cellar' unspecified, the buyer need not accept even a single souring barrel. I derive this from the first baraita: 'he must give wine that is all of good quality' — no exception is stated for the silent buyer.
Resolution The Gemara leaves this as an unresolved dispute. Both baraitot can be read in either direction, and neither definitively settles the question of the silent buyer with an unspecified cellar.

📜 Onaah (Price Exploitation) Threshold — Rav Huna's Ruling

What is the price discrepancy between agreed price and true value?
↓
Less than 1/6
Deal stands; no return required
Seller keeps the difference
Exactly 1/6
Entire exploitation sum returned
Not merely reduced to below 1/6
More than 1/6
Buyer may void the sale entirely
↓
Why return the full 1/6 rather than trim to just under? Because the seller represented a fair price — any exploitation is inherently unacceptable, unlike a sale with pre-stated variance.

🌳 Why Onaah ≠ Land Sales: The Gemara's Rejection

Exploitation / Onaah (אונאה)

  • Seller implied: 'I am selling at fair value'
  • Any price gap is inherently objectionable
  • Exactly 1/6 → full exploitation sum returned
  • Threshold is binary: past it, full remedy applies
  • Buyer never agreed to absorb any discrepancy

Land Sale Discrepancies

  • Seller said: 'slightly more or less' — variance accepted
  • Minor discrepancy: buyer keeps extra land
  • Excess ≥ area to sow 9 kav: difference returned
  • Threshold marks qualitative change in land unit
  • Buyer pre-accepted variance — not comparable to onaah

🌳 Tree-Planting Contract: Deficient Tree Allowance

Setup: A field owner contracts another to plant trees throughout his field
↓
Baraita ruling: The field owner accepts upon himself up to 10 deficient (non-bearing) trees per beit se'a of land
↓
Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua: When deficient trees exceed 10 per beit se'a, the affected area constitutes an entire 'field' — making the contractor fully liable
↓
Attempted proof for Rav Huna's onaah ruling: The threshold is meaningful because crossing it changes the legal category entirely, not merely the degree of deficiency
↓
Gemara rejects proof: The tree contract has its own unique rationale (whole-field equivalence). It cannot be generalized to the onaah threshold in commercial sales
