Bava Batra 94 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the Gemara say a buyer of lentils accepts a quarter-kav of dirt per se'a, while a buyer of wheat does not?
A. Lentils are more expensive so buyers are more lenient
B. Lentils are dug from the ground, making dirt admixture inevitable
C. The Torah explicitly exempts lentils from purity requirements
D. Wheat buyers are more sophisticated and demand higher standards
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that lentils grow underground and are dug up at harvest, so dirt easily gets mixed in. Wheat and barley are harvested above ground, so their buyers reasonably expect a stricter standard for dirt content.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What exactly does Rav Huna rule when a buyer sifts produce and discovers excess impurities?
A. The buyer may return only the amount of impurities exceeding the quarter-kav threshold
B. The sale is void and the full purchase price must be refunded
C. The buyer sifts all the produce and returns all impurities to the seller
D. The buyer receives a price reduction proportional to the excess impurities
Correct: C — Rav Huna rules that the buyer sifts the entire batch and returns ALL the impurities to the seller — not merely the amount in excess of the acceptable quarter-kav threshold. The seller absorbs even the normally-tolerated portion.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to the 'penalty' interpretation of Rav Huna's ruling, what is the normal presumption in a produce sale?
A. The buyer assumes the produce is completely free of impurities
B. A buyer presumes he accepts a quarter-kav of impurities per se'a as part of the transaction
C. The seller warrants all produce to be of the highest grade
D. Impurity levels are assessed only after the sale is finalized
Correct: B — The penalty view holds that a quarter-kav per se'a is the presumed norm in any produce transaction — the buyer accepts it. Returning ALL impurities is therefore a rabbinic penalty imposed only when the seller transgresses by delivering excessive impurities.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does the Gemara resolve the challenge from the Kilayim mishna's quarter-kav threshold for diverse seeds?
A. The Gemara accepts the challenge and limits Rav Huna's ruling
B. The Kilayim rule is a rabbinic decree due to appearance, not proof of a general tolerance standard
C. R. Yosei's strict view in Kilayim is adopted as the controlling halakha
D. The Gemara distinguishes seeds from impurities based on their monetary value
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that the Kilayim requirement to reduce diverse seeds is a rabbinic decree due to mar'it ayin — it looks like intentional forbidden sowing. It does not reflect an underlying acceptance of quarter-kav admixture and is therefore irrelevant to Rav Huna's produce ruling.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does the Gemara reject the proof from the deposits mishna (Bava Metzia 37a) as support for Rav Huna's penalty ruling?
A. The deposits case involves land, not movable goods, so it is categorically different
B. There, a swindler certainly exists; in produce sales the seller may be entirely innocent
C. The deposits mishna predates Rav Huna and cannot be used to support later rulings
D. Rabbi Yosei's view in the deposits case is not accepted as normative halakha
Correct: B — The Gemara distinguishes the cases: in the disputed deposits, it is certain that one of the parties is lying and a swindler definitely exists — penalizing both parties is designed to flush out the deceiver. In Rav Huna's case, a seller who delivers excess impurities may have done so innocently, making the reasoning for a penalty different.
