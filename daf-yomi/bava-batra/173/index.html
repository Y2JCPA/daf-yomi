⚖️ Guarantor (Arev): When Can the Creditor Collect?

Creditor lent money with a guarantor (arev)
↓
No special condition stated
Must first attempt to collect from the debtor; guarantor is secondary
Creditor said: 'I will collect from whomever I wish'
May go directly to the guarantor without first pursuing the debtor
↓
Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: Even with that explicit condition — if the debtor has property, the creditor must collect from the debtor first

📜 Must the New Holder of a Transferred Note Bring Proof?

Abaye
When a creditor transfers a promissory note, the new holder must bring proof that he legitimately acquired the right to collect. Source: the baraita about brothers who are heirs — they must prove they acquired the note from the estate!
Rava
No proof is required. The brothers case is exceptional — co-heirs commonly seize estate property from one another, so suspicion is heightened there. For ordinary note transfers, the new holder collects without proof.
Abaye
There is no difference between brothers and anyone else — all must prove acquisition. The baraita mentions brothers specifically because you might have thought heirs are more suspect, and even they must bring proof.
Shared premise Both agree: letters (שטרות) are acquired by transferring the document. The dispute concerns only the burden of proof on the new holder.

📋 Two Baraitot: Can Namesakes Issue Promissory Notes?

Baraita A — Cannot produce notes against each other

  • Just as the two Yosef ben Shimons cannot produce notes against each other…
  • …they also cannot produce notes against third parties
  • Those third parties could claim the note was fraudulently obtained from the other namesake
  • Underlying view: a scribe may NOT write a note for a debtor unless the creditor is present and consents

Baraita B — Can produce notes against each other

  • Just as the two Yosef ben Shimons can produce notes against outsiders…
  • …they can also produce notes against each other
  • Each namesake can verify which document belongs to whom
  • Underlying view: a scribe may write a note for a debtor even without the creditor present

🔢 Tripling Names & The Unknown Repaid Note

Problem: Two men in the same city share the name Yosef ben Shimon — documents cause dangerous confusion
↓
Solution — triple the name: Include grandfather's name: Yosef ben Shimon ben Ploni
↓
If even their grandfathers share a name, they must add a distinguishing physical mark or description
↓
New mishna case: A dying man says: 'One of my promissory notes in this bundle has been repaid — but I do not know which one'
↓
Ruling: All the promissory notes are considered repaid — the doubt benefits the debtors
↓
Rava: If a creditor says 'one of the two notes I hold against you is repaid' without specifying — the larger note is deemed repaid; ambiguity goes against the creditor (the note-holder)

🤝 Rav Naḥman's Full Ruling on the Guarantor

Creditor seeks to collect from the guarantor
↓
First: was there a special condition? 'I will collect from whomever I wish'
↓
No special condition
Must pursue debtor first; only after debtor has no assets or has fled may creditor turn to guarantor
Special condition stated
May collect directly from guarantor — Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagrees if debtor has property
↓
Rav Naḥman: A ruling that simply exempts the guarantor with no reason is 'Persian law' — arbitrary. The proper ruling requires exhausting the debtor first.

📖 Sources & Debate on the Guarantor's Obligation

AmoraSource / ArgumentPoint
Rav HunaJudah to Jacob: 'I will be his guarantor; of my hand shall you request him' (Gen. 43:9)Proof that a guarantor becomes obligated by his word
Rav Ḥisda (objects)Reuben: 'Deliver him into my hand, and I will bring him back' (Gen. 42:37)Judah/Reuben gave unconditional guarantee (kablanut), not ordinary arevut — different category
Rabbi Yitzḥak'Take his garment as surety for a stranger' (Proverbs 20:16)A creditor may seize the guarantor's garment — proving the obligation is real and enforceable
AmeimarDispute: Rabbi Yehuda vs. Rabbi Yosei on asmakhtaIf guarantor obligation is asmakhta (conditional resolve), it may not be binding according to Rabbi Yosei
Rav AshiThe guarantor's niyaḥ da'atei — satisfaction when the creditor trusts himThis genuine satisfaction causes the guarantor to resolve fully — it is NOT asmakhta; the obligation is real
