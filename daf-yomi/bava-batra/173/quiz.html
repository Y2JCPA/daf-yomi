QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Naḥman, when may a creditor collect from a guarantor without first pursuing the debtor?
A. At any time the creditor chooses, since the guarantor accepted responsibility
B. Only after establishing that the debtor has no assets or has fled
C. Only if the guarantor agreed in writing before a court
D. Only in cases where the debtor has died
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman rules that the creditor must first attempt to collect from the debtor; only when the debtor is unavailable or without assets may the creditor turn to the guarantor.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the actual dispute between Abaye and Rava regarding transferred promissory notes?
A. Whether promissory notes can be transferred at all
B. Whether the new holder must bring proof that he legitimately acquired the note
C. Whether a scribe must witness the transfer
D. Whether the debtor must consent to the note being transferred
Correct: B — Both Abaye and Rava agree that letters (documents) are acquired by transfer. They disagree only about whether the new holder must prove he legitimately obtained the right to collect.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
If a creditor declares 'one of the two notes I hold against you has been repaid' without specifying which, what is Rava's ruling?
A. The smaller note is considered repaid
B. The larger note is considered repaid
C. Both notes are considered repaid due to the doubt
D. Neither note is affected until the creditor specifies
Correct: B — Rava rules that the ambiguity goes against the creditor (the note-holder), so the larger — most burdensome — note is considered repaid. The holder of the document bears the disadvantage of his own ambiguity.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why did Rav Ḥisda reject Rav Huna's proof from Judah's guarantee of Benjamin?
A. Judah's promise was oral and not legally binding
B. Judah offered unconditional personal responsibility (kablanut), not ordinary guarantorship (arevut)
C. The verse is aggadic, not halakhic
D. Benjamin was a minor, so different laws apply
Correct: B — Rav Ḥisda pointed out that Judah (and similarly Reuben) offered kablanut — unconditional personal responsibility — which is a stronger form of obligation than ordinary arevut, and therefore cannot serve as a proof-text for standard guarantors.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Rav Ashi explain why a guarantor's obligation is binding and not mere asmakhta?
A. The guarantor signs a formal document witnessed by a court
B. The guarantor's satisfaction (niyaḥ da'atei) when trusted by the creditor causes him to fully resolve to be obligated
C. The Torah explicitly commands that guarantors must pay
D. It is a rabbinic decree to protect commerce
Correct: B — Rav Ashi argues that when the creditor trusts the guarantor and lends money based on his word, the guarantor feels genuine satisfaction — this niyaḥ da'atei causes a complete, unconditional resolve to pay, distinguishing it from asmakhta (conditional commitment that does not effect acquisition).
