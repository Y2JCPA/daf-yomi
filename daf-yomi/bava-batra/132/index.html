⚖️ A Creditor Emerges After Husband's Deed of Gift to Wife

Rabbi Eliezer
She should sell from the gifted property to pay the creditor. Her deed of gift does not override the prior promissory note taken during the marriage.
Rabbis
She need not sell anything. The creditor cannot collect from property that was already transferred to her as a gift.
Rabbi Yehuda the Baker
This actually happened with my son and his wife (my sister's daughter). The Sages ruled she must sell from the gift to satisfy the creditor — she cannot retain property at his expense.
Rav Naḥman's Ruling Halakha follows the Rabbis. She is not forced to sell. But she must tear up her ketubbah and keep only the deed of gift — she cannot collect both.

✂️ 'Bald From Here and From There'

מוצאת קרחת מכאן ומכאן
Rav Naḥman's Warning The wife who keeps the deed of gift must tear up her ketubbah. She has accepted the property as full settlement. But if a creditor subsequently seizes the gifted property, she is left with neither the gift nor the ketubbah. She is 'bald from here and from there' — stripped from both sides.

🏠 Husband Writes Property to Sons — Does Wife Lose Her Ketubbah?

Husband distributes property to sons and gives wife any amount of land (Mishna, Pe'ah 3:7)
↓
Wife loses her ketubbah — her receipt of a portion signals acceptance of the arrangement in lieu of it
↓
Rav
Wife participated in the act of acquisition (kinyan) that transferred property to the sons
R. Yosei b. R. Ḥanina
Either the writing or her acceptance of the distribution alone suffices to forfeit the ketubbah
Rav Naḥman
Once he rendered her a partner among the sons, she irrevocably lost her ketubbah

🔍 Deathbed vs. Healthy Person: Does the Rule Differ?

Person on His Deathbed

  • Writing all property to wife = she is a steward (not full owner)
  • Established rule: she acquires only if he dies without revoking
  • Creditor can seize if she chose gift over ketubbah
  • Wife accepting partition among sons forfeits her ketubbah
  • Multiple cases settle the halakha firmly

Healthy Person — Rava's Dilemma

  • Does the 'steward' presumption still apply?
  • A healthy person's gift may carry stronger intent to convey ownership
  • No deathbed anxiety — perhaps she truly acquires?
  • Or do we say: same rule applies regardless of health?
  • Gemara leaves this dilemma unresolved
