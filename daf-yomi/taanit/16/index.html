🏙️ Why Go Out to the Square?

Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba
Going to the square is a symbolic act of public mourning — as if to say: We cried out publicly and are shamed before all.
Reish Lakish
The square symbolizes exile — as if to say: We have been exiled from our place; may our exile atone for us.
Gemara
Practical difference: If a community fasted outdoors or in a field — Rabbi Ḥiyya's view: still valid (it was public). Reish Lakish's view: invalid unless they moved from their usual location to represent exile.
Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi
On why the Ark is brought to the square: We had a modest vessel always kept hidden — but our sins have shamed it and exposed it publicly before all.
📦 Har Moriah Dispute Rabbi Levi bar Ḥama and Rabbi Ḥanina also dispute the name: Moriah = from hora'ah (Torah instruction issued from there) OR from yir'ah (the awe/fear of God centered there).

🔣 Ritual Symbols and Their Meanings

Sackcloth

  • Worn directly over the body
  • Rough, coarse animal hide
  • "We are before You like animals"
  • Symbol of total self-abasement

Ashes on the Ark

  • Placed on the Torah ark itself
  • "I am with him in distress" (Ps. 91:15)
  • God suffers alongside Israel
  • Communal, not just individual grief

Ashes on Each Person

  • Rabbi Levi bar Ḥama vs. Rabbi Ḥanina
  • View 1: We are like ashes before You
  • View 2: Evokes ashes of Isaac (Akeidah)
  • Either way: complete nullification of self

Going to Cemetery

  • Same two sages dispute this
  • View 1: We are like the dead before You
  • View 2: To invoke merit of the ancestors
  • Both: desperate appeal to divine mercy

🐂 How Nineveh Repented (Jonah 3:7–8)

Separated animals from their nursing mothers — the animals' cries of hunger were themselves a form of prayer that moved heaven
↓
"They cried out mightily to God" — They said: Master of the Universe! Look at these innocent animals who suffer for our sins!
↓
"Let them turn from their evil way" — genuine behavioral change, not merely outward mourning rituals
↓
"Violence in their hands" — Shmuel: The king decreed that all stolen goods must be returned, even if already built into a palace — tear down the building!
↓
Lesson for Israel (baraita): It is not sackcloth and fasting that atone — but repentance and good deeds. As stated: "And God saw their deeds" (Jonah 3:10) — not their sackcloth.

🦎 Confession Without Repentance

"מוֹדֶה וְעֹזֵב יְרֻחָם" — משלי כח:יג
Rav Adda bar Ahava One who confesses a sin but does not repent — what is he like? Like one who holds a dead creeping animal (שֶׁרֶץ) in his hand. Even if he immerses in all the waters of the world, he remains ritually impure. But the moment he throws it away and then immerses — he is immediately purified. As it says: "He who covers his transgressions shall not prosper, but whoever confesses and forsakes them shall obtain mercy" (Proverbs 28:13). Confession alone is futile without accompanying abandonment of the sin.
