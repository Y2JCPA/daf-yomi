🤲 Three Who Petitioned God Unreasonably

Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani citing Rabbi Yonatan: Three people entreated God in an unreasonable manner — yet God responded mercifully
↓
Eliezer
Prayed that the maiden offering water to his camels would be Isaac's bride — could have been unworthy, yet Rebecca appeared
Saul
Promised riches and his daughter to Goliath's slayer — could have been a slave or mamzer, yet David stepped forward
Jephthah
Vowed 'whatever exits my house first shall be to the Lord' — his daughter emerged; pride prevented annulment → irreversible tragedy

⚖️ Three Petitioners: Request, Risk, and Outcome

Eliezer

  • Prayed beside the well for a specific sign
  • Asked that the maiden who offers to water his camels be Isaac's match
  • Risk: could have been an unworthy or crippled woman
  • God arranged it: Rebecca — the ideal match — appeared first

Saul

  • Announced: the king will give his daughter to Goliath's slayer
  • Risk: a slave, a mamzer, or someone unfit could have won
  • God arranged it: David, a man of valor and righteousness, stepped forward
  • Outcome: David became the king's son-in-law

Jephthah

  • Vowed: 'Whatever exits my house first… it shall be to the Lord'
  • His daughter came out to greet him after his victory
  • Phineas could have annulled the vow — but both were too proud to approach the other
  • Phineas lost the divine spirit; Jephthah died limb by limb

📜 Jephthah's Vow — The Prophet's Lament The vow (Judges 11:31): "Whatever comes forth from the doors of my house to meet me when I return in peace… it shall be to the Lord, or I will offer it up as a burnt offering." The tragic deadlock: • Phineas said: He needs annulment — let him come to me. • Jephthah said: I am king — he should come to me. • Neither went to the other. The vow stood. The Gemara's commentary: "And they have built the high places of Ba'al, to burn their sons… which I did not command, and I did not speak, nor did it come into My heart" (Jeremiah 19:5) — God never wanted this sacrifice.

🌧️ Israel Asks Unreasonably — God Responds Generously

Israel
"Let us know the Lord… He shall come to us as the rain" (Hosea 6:3) — we compare Your revealed presence to rain
God
My daughter — rain is sometimes desired (summer drought) and sometimes undesired (harvest time). I am always desired. Compare Me to something universally beneficial!
Israel
"Set me as a seal upon Your heart, as a seal upon Your arm" (Song of Songs 8:6) — a permanent, ever-present bond
God
A heart-seal is sometimes concealed; an arm-seal sometimes exposed. But I will be to you as the sun (Isaiah 30:26) — always shining, always giving light and warmth to all
Rabbi Berekhya's Principle Even when Israel's requests are imperfect, God's response transcends the request — elevating the relationship beyond what Israel imagined.

🔥 Torah as Fire — The Scholar's Anger Explained

Rava teaches: A Torah scholar who grows angry — it is his Torah study that angers him. Give him the benefit of the doubt.
↓
Proof from Jeremiah 23:29: "Is not My word like fire?" — just as fire cannot be suppressed once kindled, so too the divine word burns within the scholar and erupts as intensity.
↓
Rabbi Abba to Rav Ashi: We derive the same idea from Deuteronomy 8:9 — "A land whose stones (avaneha) are iron." Read instead: its builders (boneha) are iron — Torah scholars are hard and unyielding as iron.
↓
Drizzle (Urpila): Even gentle rain penetrates to a seed buried beneath a clod of earth — even subtle Torah study reaches and nourishes the hidden depths of a student.
↓
Connection to the daf: The Gemara opens with this aggadic material as context for the broader discussion of rain — physical rain and Torah are both life-giving forces that penetrate what seems impenetrable.
