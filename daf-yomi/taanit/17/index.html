🍷 vs. ✂️ — Why Wine Today But Not the Hair Restriction?

Rami bar Abba
A priest with long hair can sleep briefly and then enter the Temple — sleep eliminates the disqualification just as it does for wine. So the long-hair restriction should also apply today!
Rav Ashi
Not so. Wine actively desecrates the Temple service and carries a death penalty — that is why the Sages issued a decree for today. Long hair merely invalidates the service with no death penalty, so no decree was issued.
Gemara
Ezekiel 44:20 juxtaposes wine and long hair — but only wine carries the 'desecration' language from the Torah. Hence the asymmetric treatment: modern decree for wine; none for hair.
📌 Bottom Line Wine = active desecration + death penalty → modern decree stands. Long hair = passive invalidation, no death penalty → no modern decree issued.

✂️ Three Levels of Priestly Haircuts

King

  • Every day
  • His royal honor demands it
  • Source: 'His appearance is like Lebanon'
    Song of Songs 5:15 — Rabbi Abba bar Zavda

High Priest

  • Every Friday
  • Must not neglect grooming between weekly service
  • Prevents growth of disqualifying long hair
    More frequent than common priest

Common Priest

  • Once every 30 days
  • Derived via gezera shava on word פֶּרַע
  • Matches the minimum nazirite term
    Leviticus 10:6 ↔ Numbers 6:5

🔗 Gezera Shava: Proving the 30-Day Hair Interval

Step 1 — Torah text on priests: 'They shall not let their hair grow long [פֶּרַע]' — Leviticus 10:6
↓
Step 2 — Torah text on nazirite: 'He shall let the hair of his head grow long [פֶּרַע]' — Numbers 6:5
↓
Step 3 — Verbal analogy applied: Same word פֶּרַע in both contexts → the priest's permitted hair length mirrors the nazirite's
↓
Step 4 — Nazirite's term: Rav Mattana: an unspecified naziriteship is a received tradition of 30 days
↓
Conclusion: A common priest must cut his hair every 30 days — the nazirite's minimum term defines the priest's maximum interval

🏛️ Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi vs. The Rabbis — All Priests & Wine

The Rabbis
A priest who does not know his watch is prohibited to drink wine all year. A priest who does know his watch is only restricted during that week. The rule is proportional to uncertainty.
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
All priests should be forbidden wine at all times! When the Temple is rebuilt, the watches may be reorganized — or all priests may serve simultaneously. No priest can be certain he won't be called.
Gemara
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's ruling is so extreme it was never adopted — 'his misfortune is his advantage.' His own stringency exempts him from any practical decree. The Rabbis permit wine for priests who know their watch.
💡 Ironic Principle Misfortune is his advantage — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's extreme position (all priests, always) is too stringent to adopt, so in practice he remains permitted where the Rabbis would restrict.
