🌧️ The Greatness of Rain — and the Torah It Mirrors

יום גשמים גדול מתחיית המתים — שזו לצדיקים וזו לצדיקים ולרשעים
Core Theme Daf 7 opens with a crescendo of praise: rain surpasses resurrection (Rabbi Abbahu), equals Sinai (Rav Yehuda), and even rivals Creation (Rabbi Ḥama). The daf pivots to rich Torah metaphors — fire, water, wine, milk — each teaching how Torah is transmitted and lost. It closes with five sages identifying specific sins that close the heavens and withhold rain.

🌧️ How Great Is the Day of Rain? Three Comparisons

Rabbi Abbahu
The day of rain is greater than the resurrection of the dead. Resurrection benefits only the righteous — rain benefits both the righteous and the wicked.
Rav Yehuda
The day of rain equals the day the Torah was given at Sinai: "My doctrine [lekaḥ] shall drop as the rain" (Deut. 32:2) — and lekaḥ always refers to Torah.
Rabbi Ḥama b. Ḥanina
The day of rain equals the day heaven and earth were created: "Drop down, heavens, from above; let the skies pour down righteousness" (Isa. 45:8) — evoking the primordial ordering of creation.
Escalating Praise Three different sages each compare rain to a different cosmic event — resurrection, Sinai, and Creation — building a cumulative case for rain's unique theological significance.

🔥💧🍷🥛 Torah Is Compared to Four Liquids (and Fire)

🔥 Fire

  • Fire needs a pile of wood — not a lone stick
  • → Torah thrives in group study; dims in isolation
  • Rabba bar bar Ḥana — Jer. 23:29

💧 Water

  • Water flows downward, not upward
  • → Torah rests only on the humble
  • Rabbi Ḥanina bar Idi — Isa. 55:1

🍷 Wine

  • Wine improves and is stored in old vessels
  • → Torah is best preserved by elders and seasoned scholars
  • Rabbi Oshaya — Isa. 55:1

🥛 Milk

  • Milk is kept in humble clay vessels, not gold
  • → Torah endures with those who make themselves modest
  • Rabbi Oshaya — proven by emperor's daughter story

📜 Torah for Its Own Sake — Life or Death?

Rabbi Bena'a: One who learns Torah לשמה (for its own sake) — Torah becomes an elixir of life: "It is a tree of life to them who lay hold upon it" (Prov. 3:18)
↓
One who learns Torah שלא לשמה (not for its own sake) — Torah becomes a potion of death: same verse in Deuteronomy uses ya'arof, evoking a violent storm that destroys
↓
Rava's resolution: Deut. 32:2 moves from ya'arof (harsh storm) to revivim (gentle showers) — the same Torah can be deadly or life-giving depending entirely on the learner's intent
↓
Rabbi Yirmeya to Rabbi Zeira: Even the tree that may be cut down in war teaches Torah — context determines whether destruction or life is the outcome ("You may eat of them but not cut them down" — Deut. 20:19–20)
↓
Remedy for withheld rain: Rabbi Ami teaches — increase prayer, for "He has commanded it due to imploring" (Job 36:32), and imploring means nothing other than prayer
