⚖️ Is Fasting a Sin, a Sanctity, or Piety?

Shmuel
One who fasts is called a sinner. Just as the nazirite must bring a sin-offering for abstaining from wine, one who needlessly afflicts himself sins against his own body.
Rabbi Elazar
One who accepts a fast is called sacred. Just as the nazirite is called kadosh for his self-restraint (Numbers 6:5), so too the faster elevates himself through abstinence.
Reish Lakish
One who fasts is called pious. As it says: 'The pious man does good [gomel] to his own soul' (Proverbs 11:17) — by weaning from pleasure he acts with loving-kindness toward himself.
Internal Contradiction Rabbi Elazar himself also taught: A person should consider himself as having a sacred object within, and not damage it — derived from 'The sacred is in your midst' (Hosea 11:9). The Gemara leaves the tension unresolved, each principle applying in its own context.

🌾 Required Behaviors During Famine Years

Rav Yehuda citing Rav

  • One who has food but restrains himself during famine
  • Will be saved from an unusual death
  • Source: "In famine He will redeem you from death" (Job 5:20)
  • Extra word implies: treat famine as real famine → God protects you

Reish Lakish

  • Prohibited to have conjugal relations during famine
  • So children not be born into these difficult years
  • Source: Joseph's sons born before famine began (Gen. 41:50)
  • Exception: one who has no children may still be intimate

Rav Pappa

  • Ate one loaf per parasang of travel
  • Held: fasting prohibition on travelers is due to digestion
  • Since he was healthy, travel did not upset his bowels
  • Ate proactively to avoid inadvertent fasting, not from hunger

✡️ Do Not Separate From Communal Suffering

כָּל הַמֵּצֵר עִם הַצִּבּוּר זוֹכֶה וְרוֹאֶה בְּנֶחָמַת צִבּוּר
Baraita — Two Ministering Angels When the Jewish people are in distress and one separates himself from the community, the two ministering angels who accompany every person place their hands on his head and declare: "This one who separated from the community shall not witness the community's consolation."

And if he goes home, eats, drinks, and says "peace be upon you, my soul" — Scripture applies to him: "Behold joy and gladness… but regard not the work of the Lord" (Isaiah 22:13).

Rather, be distressed together with the community — as Moses did during the war with Amalek: his hands grew heavy, yet Aaron and Hur supported them, one on each side (Exodus 17:12).

⏱️ Individual Fast: Ate the Night Before — Still Valid?

Individual accepts a fast upon himself for the following day
↓
That evening he eats and drinks before the fast was to begin
↓
Rav Huna's Ruling
He may still observe the fast and it is valid — he even recites the Aneinu prayer of a fast day
Rav Yosef's Question
Does Rav Huna hold: no fast for hours (night hours irrelevant)? Or yes fast for hours (but those night hours were never accepted)?
Abaye's Resolution
Rav Huna holds one does fast for hours — but the night hours were never included in his original acceptance, so eating then cannot retroactively invalidate the daytime fast

🏙️ Mar Ukva at Ginzak — Three Questions Brought to the Study Hall

Mar Ukva arrives at Ginzak. The inhabitants ask him three unresolved halakhic questions he could not answer on the spot.
↓
❓ Question 1: Does one fast for hours (partial day fast), or does a valid fast require the full day from dawn to nightfall?
↓
❓ Question 2: Are jars belonging to gentiles that contained wine permitted once they have not been used for twelve months?
↓
Mar Ukva brings both questions to the study hall — he did not presume to rule without consultation, modeling halakhic humility.
↓
✅ Answer 1: One does fast for hours — and he even recites the Aneinu prayer of a full fast day.
↓
✅ Answer 2: Gentile jars are permitted after twelve months of disuse — the absorbed wine taste is considered expired.
↓
Rav Ḥisda continues the discussion of individual fasts at the daf's close — the topic carries forward into Daf 12.
