📯 Daf 14: Stringencies, Disputes & Humility

The mishna's phrase "How are the last seven fasts more stringent?" triggers a chain of disputes about what sounding the alarm (מַתְרִיעִין) actually means.
↓
Rav vs. Rav Yehuda: Does 'sounding the alarm' mean reciting Aneinu or blowing shofarot? Resolution: both agree on shofarot; they dispute whether Aneinu also constitutes the alarm.
↓
Three contradictory baraitot about pregnant and nursing women are resolved by Rav Ashi: they fast only on the middle fasts — stricter than first, less severe than last.
↓
Rabbi Yehuda Nesia decreed 13 fasts without divine answer. Rabbi Ami rules: "Do not trouble the community excessively" — a foundational halachic principle on rabbinic authority.
↓
Rabbi Elazar: Only one certain of being answered — like Joshua or Jehoram — may prostrate himself or don sackcloth before the community. Humility must be genuine.

📯 What Does 'Sounding the Alarm' Mean?

Rav
The mishna's 'sounding the alarm' refers to reciting the Aneinu prayer — the verbal cry to God is the essence of the alarm on fast days.
Rav Yehuda
No — 'sounding the alarm' means literally blowing shofarot. The physical blast is the alarm; Aneinu alone does not fulfill it.
Gemara
Reconsider: on shofarot, both agree this constitutes 'sounding the alarm.' The dispute concerns only whether Aneinu also qualifies as such.
Conclusion
According to Rav: shofarot and Aneinu both constitute the alarm. According to Rav Yehuda: only shofarot qualify — Aneinu alone does not count as 'sounding the alarm.'
⚠️ Shabbat Emergency If a calamity strikes on Shabbat, they cry out — but with voices alone, never shofarot, since shofar-blowing is forbidden on Shabbat (even on Rosh HaShana that falls on Shabbat).

🤰 Pregnant & Nursing Women: Three Baraitot Reconciled

Baraita 1

  • Fast with community on first fasts
  • Do not fast on the last fasts
  • Seems to exempt them from the hardest fasts

Baraita 2

  • Fast on the last fasts
  • Do not fast on the first fasts
  • Directly contradicts Baraita 1!

Rav Ashi's Resolution

  • Fast only on the middle fasts
  • Middle = stricter than first, lighter than last
  • Baraita 1 calls them 'last' relative to the first set
  • Baraita 2 calls them 'first' relative to the final set
  • All three baraitot are thereby harmonized

🙏 Where Does the Individual Insert Aneinu?

Question: An individual fasting — where does he insert the Aneinu (עֲנֵנוּ) prayer in the Amida?
↓
Rabbi Shmuel bar Sasretai & Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi
Insert Aneinu as an independent blessing between Go'el Yisrael and Refa'enu
Alternative Approach
Fold Aneinu into the blessing of Shome'a Tefilla (Who Hears Prayer) — no separate blessing added
↓
No decisive proof was produced for either view. The Gemara records the dispute and moves on — the question is revisited later in the tractate.

⚖️ Not Troubling the Community Excessively

אֵין מַטְרִיחִין אֶת הַצִּבּוּר יוֹתֵר מִדַּאי
📜 Foundational Halachic Principle Rabbi Yehuda Nesia decreed 13 fast days during a communal crisis — yet received no divine answer. He considered imposing still more fasts.

Rabbi Ami intervened: "They said: One does not trouble the community excessively." This places a practical and moral limit on the number of fasts a leader may decree.

Rabbi Abba objected: Rabbi Ami ruled against the majority on his own authority. Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba citing Rabbi Yoḥanan held that the full fast-cycle restriction applies only in Eretz Yisrael, not Babylonia.

Parallel case: The people of Nineveh asked Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi about praying for rain in Tammuz. Rav Naḥman: Pray in the blessing of the years (Birkat HaShanim). Rav Sheshet: Pray in Shome'a Tefilla.
