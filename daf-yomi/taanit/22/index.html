🏪 Rabbi Beroka Meets Elijah in Bei Lefet Market

Rabbi Beroka regularly visited the market of Bei Lefet, where Elijah the Prophet would appear to him
↓
Rabbi Beroka asked Elijah: "Does anyone here have a share in the World-to-Come?"
↓
Elijah pointed to a jailer — no tzitzit, black shoes, indistinguishable from a gentile — as a hidden tzaddik
↓
The jailer explained: I dress to blend in among gentiles to protect Jewish women and intercede for Jews sentenced to death
↓
Then Elijah pointed to two jesters who cheered the sad and made peace between quarreling people
↓
Lesson: Hidden acts of kindness and protection — not outward piety — are what earn the World-to-Come

🌟 Three Hidden Tzaddikim of the Marketplace

The Jailer

  • Wore no tzitzit threads
  • Wore black shoes (gentile style)
  • Blended in to move freely among non-Jews
  • Protected Jewish women from harm
  • Interceded for condemned Jews awaiting execution
  • Asked Rabbi Beroka to keep silent about him

The Two Jesters

  • Professional badchanim (comedians)
  • Made a point of cheering those who were sad
  • Made peace between quarreling people
  • No outward sign of religious distinction
  • Both immediately identified by Elijah
  • Both merited the World-to-Come equally

The Common Thread

  • None wore visible markers of piety
  • All performed hidden acts of chesed
  • Each protected or uplifted vulnerable people
  • Overlooked or dismissed by casual observers
  • Known only to God — and revealed by Elijah
  • Model of righteousness beyond the letter of the law

⚔️ Josiah's Death — Why Was He Punished?

Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani
Josiah was punished because he went to war against Pharaoh Neco without consulting the prophet Jeremiah first — even though he had a seemingly valid Torah argument.
Pharaoh Neco's Warning
"What have I to do with you, king of Judah? God has commanded me to make haste — forbear from meddling with God Who is with me, lest He destroy you" (II Chronicles 35:21).
Josiah's Reasoning
Josiah said: Neco refers to his idol, not to the true God. The Torah commands: 'You shall not follow their gods' — so I need not heed this pagan king's warning.
Jeremiah at the Deathbed
Jeremiah saw Josiah's lips moving as he lay dying and feared he was complaining against Divine judgment. He leaned in — and heard Josiah accepting his fate with love and justifying God's decree.
The Lesson
Even a brilliant and righteous king must consult a prophet before going to war. Personal Torah reasoning, however compelling, cannot substitute for prophetic guidance in life-or-death decisions.
Josiah's Final Words "The Lord is righteous, for I have rebelled against His commandment" (Lamentations 1:18) — he accepted Divine judgment even in his last moments

🌧️ The Paradox of Too Much Rain Rabbi Yoḥanan rules: We do not pray for excess rain to stop in Eretz Yisrael — derived from Malachi 3:10: "Test Me now... if I will not open for you the windows of heaven and pour out for you a blessing until there is more than enough." One does not pray that overflowing Divine blessing should cease.

In Babylonia, however, the land is low-lying and flooding is genuinely dangerous — so they do sound the alarm.

Rabbi Eliezer's measure: Enough rain that a person standing at the great cliff of Keren Ophel can dip his feet in the water below. How tall is this cliff? Rabba bar bar Ḥana testifies: I saw it personally — an Arab on a camel holding a spear looked from above like a tiny worm.
