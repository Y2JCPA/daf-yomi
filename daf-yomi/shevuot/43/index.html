Bailees, Oaths & Collateral: The Framework

Exodus 22:6 & 22:9
Bailee oath laws derived via klal u'prat u'klal
↓
Oath applies to items similar to the detail:
Movable property with intrinsic monetary value
↓
Included
Silver, vessels, animals — movable items with inherent value
Excluded
Land, Canaanite slaves, documents, consecrated property
↓
Further requirement: Claim must be defined by size, weight, or number for oath to apply
↓
Special rules when collateral (mashkon) is involved — who swears first?
Core Principle The Torah's bailee laws apply only to movable property with intrinsic value, and oaths require precisely defined claims.

Grapes on the Vine: Attached to Land?

R' Meir
Grapevines laden with fruit are like movable property — they are subject to bailee oaths.
Rabbis
Anything attached to the land has the status of land — even fruit-laden vines are excluded.
Gemara
If R' Meir treats all attached items as non-land, why dispute over laden vines? Dispute over bare vines!
R' Yosei b. R' Chanina
R' Meir concedes attached items are generally like land. The dispute is only about grapes ready to harvest (b'tzir).
R' Meir (clarified)
Grapes ready for harvesting are as if already harvested — they have the status of movable property.
Rabbis (clarified)
Even ready-to-harvest grapes are still attached — their status remains that of land.
Nafka Mina If grapes are ready to pick, are they already "movable"? R' Meir says yes — the Rabbis say not until physically detached.

When Is a Claim Defined Enough for an Oath?

Claimant says: "I gave you a house full of produce"
↓
Is the claim defined by size, weight, or number?
Abaye
If he said "this house" — specific and defined → oath required.
If unspecified → exempt.
Rava
Even specifying "this house" is not enough.
Must claim a measurable quantity → otherwise exempt.
↓
Rava's proof: The mishna could have distinguished within its own case if Abaye were correct — it didn't.
↓
A baraita supports Rava: both claim and admission must be in defined quantities
Rava's Rule The defendant is never liable for an oath unless the claimant's demand AND the defendant's partial admission are both defined by size, weight, or number.

Shmuel's Collateral Rule vs. Challenges

Shmuel's Principle

  • One lent 1,000 dinars and took a sickle handle as collateral
  • If the handle is lost → creditor loses the entire 1,000
  • The collateral represents the full debt, regardless of its actual value
  • Rationale: creditor accepted it as security for the whole loan

Mishna's Challenge

  • Mishna has creditor and debtor disputing collateral's value
  • If lost collateral cancels the debt, why litigate value?
  • Answer: The mishna deals with a case where creditor explicitly stated he takes collateral only for its own value
  • Shmuel's rule applies when no explicit condition was stated
Mashkon When a creditor accepts collateral without conditions, the collateral is presumed to secure the entire debt — its loss cancels what is owed.

Multiple Pieces of Collateral: Three Opinions

📋 Shmuel: One handle lost from two → does not lose entire debt (loses only that handle's value)
↓
⚖️ Rav Nachman: One handle lost from two → loses half the debt; second lost → loses all
↓
🔄 Rav Nachman: Handle + silver piece — silver lost → does not lose half (relied on silver for repayment)
↓
🏛️ Sages of Neharde'a: Handle + silver — silver lost → loses half; handle then lost → loses all
↓
💡 Key principle: When multiple collateral items exist, each one's loss is assessed relative to the whole debt
Dispute The Amoraim disagree on whether collateral items split the debt equally or whether the more valuable item carries more weight.
