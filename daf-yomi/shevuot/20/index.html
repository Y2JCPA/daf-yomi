What Is a 'Bond' (Issar) in Torah Law?

Abaye
Bitui (clear utterance) = an oath. Issar (bond) = association with a preexisting oath — and association is like an explicit oath.
Rava
Issar is ambiguous — placed between vow and oath in the verse. Expressed as a vow → it's a vow. Expressed as an oath → it's an oath. But association is NOT like an explicit oath.
R' Yochanan
Agrees with Rava: a bond is not association but an oath itself. 'By my clear utterance I will not eat' or 'on my bond I will not eat' — both are oaths.
Core Question Does the Torah's term issar (bond) mean associating a new prohibition with an existing oath, or is it simply another word for oath?

Bitui (Clear Utterance) vs. Issar (Bond)

בִּטּוּי — Bitui

  • Means 'clear utterance'
  • Source: Leviticus 5:4 — 'to clearly utter with his lips'
  • Universally agreed: this is an oath
  • Numbers 30:7 — 'the clear utterance of her lips'

אִסָּר — Issar

  • Means 'bond' or 'binding obligation'
  • Source: Numbers 30:5 — 'a bond with which she has bound her soul'
  • Disputed: oath itself or association with oath?
  • Placed between vow and oath in Numbers 30:11
Key Verse Numbers 30:11 — 'Or bound her soul by a bond with an oath' — the bond is linked to a preexisting oath (Abaye) or is itself an oath (Rava).

Abaye vs. Rava: Is Association Like an Explicit Oath?

Abaye
A bond means associating an action with something already prohibited by oath. This association is like stating the oath explicitly — one is liable for offerings (unwitting) and lashes (intentional).
Rava
Association with an oath is NOT like stating an oath explicitly. The word issar is ambiguous — the Torah placed it between vow and oath. Its status depends on how it's expressed.
Gemara
Challenge to Rava: A baraita shows that associating a vow with another vow is valid — shouldn't the same apply to oaths?
Rava
The baraita discusses bonds of vows specifically. 'Vows a vow' (Numbers 30:3) teaches association works only for vows, not oaths.
Practical Difference If one says: 'I won't eat meat like on the day my father died' — according to Abaye, this works for both vows and oaths. According to Rava, only for vows.

TimeframeFuture — 'I will eat' / 'I will not eat'Past — 'I ate' / 'I did not eat'
NatureOath not fulfilledOath that is a lie
Torah SourceLeviticus 19:12 — 'shall not take an oath falsely'Exodus 20:7 — 'shall not take God's name in vain'
PunishmentLashes (intentional), Offering (unwitting)Lashes

Rav Dimi Reports R' Yochanan distinguishes: sheker = future oaths unfulfilled; shav = past oaths that are lies. The baraita that calls them 'one' means they were spoken in a single divine utterance.

Zakhor & Shamor — Spoken as One

זָכוֹר אֶת יוֹם הַשַּׁבָּת לְקַדְּשׁוֹ / שָׁמוֹר אֶת יוֹם הַשַּׁבָּת לְקַדְּשׁוֹ
Divine Utterance Just as 'Remember (זכור) the Shabbat' and 'Observe (שמור) the Shabbat' were spoken in a single utterance — beyond human capacity to speak or hear — so too the prohibitions of shevuat sheker and shevuat shav were given simultaneously.

Rav Adda bar Ahava: The pairing of zakhor/shamor teaches that whoever must observe (refrain from labor) must also remember (recite kiddush) — including women, who are obligated in kiddush by Torah law.
