Shevuot 11 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does Rabba argue that surplus incense must have inherent sanctity (kedushat haguf)?
A. Because it is disqualified by contact with a tevul yom (one who immersed that day)
B. Because it is disqualified by being left overnight
C. Because it was placed in a service vessel
D. Because the Torah explicitly states incense has inherent sanctity
Correct: A — Rabba cites a baraita teaching that incense placed in a mortar is disqualified by a tevul yom. Since only items with inherent sanctity are disqualified this way, the incense must have kedushat haguf.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Rabba say incense is NOT disqualified by being left overnight (linah), even though it has inherent sanctity?
A. Because the Torah exempts incense from linah
B. Because incense doesn't change form or spoil over time
C. Because incense is only used once a year
D. Because the mortar is not considered a service vessel
Correct: B — Rabba explains that linah only disqualifies substances that spoil over time. Since incense maintains its form unchanged throughout the year, overnight disqualification does not apply to it.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rabba, what is the mechanism that allows surplus public offerings to be sold?
A. The Torah permits selling any surplus offering
B. The court makes a tacit stipulation at the time of consecration
C. A prophet instructed that surplus offerings may always be sold
D. Surplus items automatically lose their sanctity at year's end
Correct: B — Rabba proposes that the court builds a condition into every public consecration: if the item is needed, it has inherent sanctity; if not needed, it has only value-sanctity and can be sold.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does Rabba say the court's stipulation does NOT help in the case of lost Yom Kippur offerings, but DOES help for the red heifer?
A. The red heifer has only value-sanctity by Torah law, while Yom Kippur offerings have inherent sanctity
B. Lost animals are uncommon and the court doesn't stipulate for uncommon cases — but the red heifer's great monetary value justifies an exception
C. Yom Kippur offerings are more holy than a red heifer
D. The red heifer is a communal offering while Yom Kippur offerings are individual
Correct: B — Rabba's rule is that the court stipulates for common cases. Lost animals are uncommon, so normally no stipulation applies. However, the red heifer's great expense motivates the court to stipulate even in this uncommon case to prevent major financial loss.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In the dispute about lost communal sin-offerings, what do Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon say should be done with the found animals?
A. They should be left to die
B. They should be immediately redeemed and sold
C. They should graze until blemished, then be sold with proceeds going to communal gift offerings
D. They should be offered as burnt-offerings instead
Correct: C — R' Elazar and R' Shimon hold that a communal sin-offering is not left to die. Instead, the animals graze until they develop a blemish, are then sold, and the proceeds fund communal nedavah (gift) offerings.
