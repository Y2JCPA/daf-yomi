Shevuot 41 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the first answer in the Gemara, what is the practical difference between a Torah oath and a rabbinic oath of inducement?
A. Whether the oath can be transferred to the other party
B. Whether the defendant can refuse to take the oath
C. Whether the oath requires witnesses
D. Whether the oath must be taken while holding a Torah scroll
Correct: A — The Gemara's first distinction is regarding transfer of the oath (gilgul): a Torah oath cannot be transferred to the claimant, while a rabbinic oath can be transferred.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rav Ashi, what happens to a defendant who refuses to take a rabbinic oath of inducement?
A. The court enters his property immediately
B. He is excommunicated for 30 days, then flogged if he still refuses
C. He automatically loses the case
D. The oath is transferred to the claimant
Correct: B — Rav Ashi modified the excommunication approach: the defendant is excommunicated for 30 days, and if he still refuses to take the oath or settle, he is flogged and then left alone.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does Rav Ashi distinguish between a creditor who vitiates his promissory note and one who does not?
A. A vitiated note is invalid and cannot be collected
B. When vitiated, the court demands an oath on its own; otherwise, only if the defendant requests it
C. A vitiated note requires two witnesses while a regular note requires three
D. The Torah scholar exemption only applies to vitiated notes
Correct: B — Rav Ashi explains that when a creditor vitiates his note (admits partial payment), the court itself imposes an oath. In Rav Pappa's case, the oath is administered only because the defendant specifically demanded it.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In the dispute about repaying before 'Reuven and Shimon,' why does Rava reject the repayment made before two other witnesses?
A. Because two random witnesses lack legal standing
B. Because the creditor specifically named those individuals so the debtor couldn't dismiss him with others
C. Because Reuven and Shimon were Torah scholars
D. Because the repayment was made on Shabbat
Correct: B — Rava holds that the creditor deliberately specified Reuven and Shimon by name precisely to prevent the debtor from later claiming he repaid before different, unverifiable witnesses.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In Rav Naḥman's case about repaying before 'learned people,' what was his ruling when the creditor claimed the money was only a deposit?
A. The creditor was exempt since the condition wasn't fulfilled
B. The debtor must pay again since the condition was never met
C. Since the creditor admitted receiving the money, it counts as repayment, and the condition can be fulfilled afterward
D. The case was dismissed because neither party had proper witnesses
Correct: C — Rav Naḥman ruled that the creditor's admission of receiving the money constitutes repayment. He cleverly offered to fulfill the condition himself, saying that he and Rav Sheshet qualify as people who have learned halakha.
