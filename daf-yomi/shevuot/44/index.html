Shevuot 44: Collateral, Bailees & Oaths

Central Question: What happens when a creditor loses the debtor's collateral?
↓
R' Eliezer
Debtor still pays full debt — creditor is unpaid bailee
R' Akiva
Creditor loses value of collateral — creditor is paid bailee
↓
Mishna: Certain claimants take an oath and receive payment — reversing the Torah's default
Key Theme This daf explores the financial responsibility that comes with holding another's property — whether as collateral or as a found item — and introduces the revolutionary concept of oaths that lead to payment rather than exemption.

Lost Collateral: Who Bears the Loss?

R' Eliezer
Even though the collateral was lost, the debtor must still pay the full debt.
R' Akiva
The creditor must deduct the value of the lost collateral from the debt.
Gemara
Do they disagree about Shmuel's principle — that lost collateral cancels the entire debt?
Gemara
No. Both reject Shmuel when collateral is worth less than the loan. They disagree about R' Yitzchak's principle of creditor acquisition.
Shmuel's Rule If a creditor loses the collateral (mashkon), the entire debt is canceled — a principle the Gemara tries to link to this Tannaitic dispute.

R' Yitzchak: Does a Creditor Acquire Collateral?

Deuteronomy 24:13 — Returning collateral is called 'righteousness' (tzedakah)
↓
If creditor doesn't own the collateral, where is the 'righteousness' in returning it?
↓
R' Yitzchak: A creditor acquires (koneh) the collateral — it is considered his property
↓
But does this apply to collateral taken at the time of the loan or only after?
At Time of Loan
R' Yitzchak does not necessarily say creditor acquires it
After the Loan
All agree R' Yitzchak's principle applies
Key Distinction The timing of when collateral is taken — at the loan or afterward — determines whether the creditor is considered its owner.

Bailee Status of Creditor Holding Collateral

Rabba → R' Eliezer

  • Status: Unpaid Bailee (shomer chinam)
  • Exempt if collateral is lost or stolen
  • Debtor must still pay full debt
  • Like one guarding a lost item — no payment received
  • If creditor uses collateral: not performing a mitzva

Rav Yosef → R' Akiva

  • Status: Paid Bailee (shomer sakhar)
  • Liable if collateral is lost or stolen
  • Creditor loses value of collateral from debt
  • Exemption from other mitzvot = 'payment'
  • Even if creditor uses collateral: still a mitzva
Mitzva Exemption = Payment? Rav Yosef's reasoning: while safeguarding collateral (part of the mitzva to lend), one is exempt from other mitzvot requiring expense — this saved money counts as 'payment' for bailment.

Shevi'it (Sabbatical Year) & Collateral

IssueRabban Shimon b. GamlielRabbi Yehuda HaNasi
Collateral worth half the loanShevi'it does not abrogate the debtShevi'it abrogates the debt
Collateral equal to loanShevi'it does not abrogateShevi'it does not abrogate
ReasoningCollateral represents the entire debtOnly the portion covered by collateral is protected
Gemara's challengeSeems to support Shmuel's principle—
Gemara's resolutionRefers only to portion commensurate with collateralCollateral is merely a reminder (siman)
Resolution The Gemara rejects the link to Shmuel: RSBG only means Shevi'it doesn't abrogate the portion matching the collateral's value. Rabbi holds even that portion is abrogated — the collateral was just a reminder.

Mishna: Who Takes an Oath and Receives Payment?

📜 Torah default: One takes an oath to exempt himself from paying
↓
🔄 Rabbinic innovation: Certain claimants swear and receive payment
↓
👷 Hired worker — swears wages were not received
↓
🏚️ Robbery victim — swears about what was taken
↓
🤕 Injured person — swears about compensation owed
↓
⚠️ Suspect oath-taker's opponent — the honest party swears instead
↓
📒 Storekeeper with ledger — swears based on his records
R' Yehuda's Qualification The hired worker's oath only applies when there is partial admission (modeh b'miktzat) — e.g., employer admits paying 25 dinars of 50 owed.
