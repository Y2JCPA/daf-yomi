The Gallnuts Price Dispute

Case
A debtor repaid with gallnuts (aftzei). He claims they were worth 6 dinars/kav; the creditor says only 4 dinars/kav.
Witnesses
They confirm the gallnuts were worth 4 dinars per kav at the time.
Rava
The debtor has the presumptive status of one who falsely denies his debts.
Rami bar Ḥama
But you yourself said: what is not incumbent upon a person is not on his mind! Perhaps he simply forgot the price.
Rava
People remember the standard, set market price. This is different from forgetting a minor detail.
PRINCIPLE Standard market prices are considered common knowledge that people remember, unlike incidental details.

'I Deem You Credible' — Stipulations in Debt

Abaye & Rava

  • Debtor said: 'I deem you credible whenever you say I didn't repay'
  • He then repaid before witnesses
  • Creditor denies being repaid
  • The creditor COLLECTS — the debtor's stipulation gave him ne'emanut (credibility)

Rav Pappa's Objection

  • The debtor made him credible over the debtor's own word
  • He did NOT make him credible over witnesses
  • Witnesses override the stipulation
  • Debtor is EXEMPT from further payment
HALAKHA Rav Pappa holds: 'I deem you credible' gives the creditor power against the debtor's word, but not against the testimony of witnesses.

Rabba's Theory: Why Does Partial Admission Require an Oath?

Rabba
A person does not exhibit insolence (eino me'iz panim) before his creditor who did him a favor. He wants to deny everything but can't bring himself to lie completely.
Rabba
He admits part because he can't be insolent, but denies part to buy time — 'I'll repay when I have money.' So the Torah imposes an oath to push him to full honesty.
R' Eliezer b. Ya'akov
The debtor would not be insolent even before the creditor's son. So admitting part of a claim to the son is still a standard partial admission → oath required.
Rabbis
One would not be insolent before the original creditor, but would be insolent before his son. So admitting to the son = returning a lost item → exempt from oath.
CORE DISPUTE Does the 'no insolence' presumption extend to the creditor's heir? R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov says yes (oath required); the Rabbis say no (treated as voluntarily returning a lost item).
