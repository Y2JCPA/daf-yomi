Shevuot 32: The Verbal Demand Requirement

Plaintiff pursues witnesses seeking testimony
↓
Did the plaintiff explicitly verbalize a demand to testify?
Yes — Explicit demand
Witnesses who deny knowledge are liable for false oath of testimony
No — Non-verbal intent only
Witnesses who deny knowledge are exempt (Shmuel)
Key Distinction Unlike the oath on a deposit (where any denial creates liability), the oath of testimony requires the plaintiff to explicitly articulate the demand to testify.

Must the Plaintiff Verbalize the Demand?

Questioner
If the plaintiff is pursuing the witnesses, isn't it obvious he wants them to testify? Why would they be exempt?
Shmuel
Even though the plaintiff's intent is for them to testify, witnesses are liable only if he tells them explicitly.
Questioner
But the mishna says: 'Where the plaintiff said to witnesses: Come testify' — implying verbal demand is required!
Gemara
Perhaps 'said' in the mishna is not precise — the tanna may use that language because it is how things typically occur, not as a strict requirement.
Baraita Support A baraita confirms Shmuel: if witnesses see the plaintiff pursuing them and deny knowledge without being asked directly, they are exempt.

Oath of Testimony vs. Oath on a Deposit

Oath of Testimony (שבועת העדות)

  • Plaintiff must explicitly verbalize demand to testify
  • Pursuing witnesses is not enough
  • Witnesses exempt if no verbal demand
  • Sliding-scale offering (קרבן עולה ויורד)

Oath on a Deposit (שבועת הפיקדון)

  • Bailee liable for any denial of the deposit
  • No requirement for owner's verbal demand
  • Leviticus 5:21 — 'deals falsely in a matter of deposit'
  • Guilt-offering (אשם)
Why the Difference? The oath on a deposit is about the bailee's false denial regardless of the claim's form, whereas the oath of testimony depends on a formal demand from the plaintiff.

Liability: In Court vs. Outside Court

Plaintiff administers oath to witnesses outside court
↓
Witnesses come to court
↓
Do they admit or deny knowledge in court?
Admit in court
Exempt — even if they swore falsely 5 times outside court
Deny in court
Liable for each oath administered outside court
Abaye's Source Leviticus 5:1 — 'If he does not utter it' — liability applies only where testimony would lead to a monetary payment, i.e., in court.

Rav Pappa Challenges Abaye on Court Requirement

Rav Pappa
If liability requires a court setting, maybe the oath itself must be taken in court — not just the denial?
Abaye
That cannot be, because a baraita teaches one is liable for each and every oath (Lev. 5:5). If the oath must be in court, how can there be multiple oaths?
Abaye
The oath can be administered outside court multiple times. It is only the denial — refusing to testify — that must occur in a place where testimony has legal effect.
Resolution The oath can be taken anywhere; the denial of knowledge must be in court to generate liability, because only there does testimony produce a monetary judgment.

Rabbi Abba's Case: The Snatched Silver Ingot

StepDetails
ClaimA man snatched a silver ingot from another
CourtCase came before Rabbi Ami (R' Abba present)
EvidencePlaintiff brought one witness to the theft
Defendant's claim"Yes, I snatched it — but it was mine"
ProblemCan't require payment (only 1 witness). Can't require oath (he admitted snatching = disqualified as robber)

Application If a witness in such a case falsely denies knowledge, he is liable for oath of testimony — because his testimony would have directly caused a monetary payment.
