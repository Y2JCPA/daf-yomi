Shevuat Ha'Edut: Who Is Obligated?

Shevuat Ha'Edut (Oath of Testimony) is administered
↓
Is the potential witness fit to testify?
Yes — Men, Non-Relatives, Fit
Liable for false denial
Brings sliding-scale offering
No — Women, Relatives, Unfit
Exempt from the oath entirely
Key Rule The oath of testimony applies only to those who are eligible witnesses — derived from 'the two men shall stand' (Deut. 19:17)

When Must the Denial Occur in Court?

R' Meir
The oath applies both in court and outside of court when one takes the oath on his own
R' Meir
But if others administer the oath and the witness neither swears nor answers amen — not liable unless he denies in court
Rabbanan
Whether one swears on his own or the oath is administered by others — not liable until denial is made in court
Dispute R' Meir distinguishes self-imposed vs. administered oaths; the Rabbis require court in all cases

Deriving Women's Exclusion from Testimony

Deut. 19:17 — 'Then the two men shall stand'
↓
Does 'two men' refer to witnesses or litigants?
Baraita 1
'Between whom the controversy is' already identifies litigants → 'two men' must mean witnesses
Baraita 2
Why specify 'two'? Can't 3 people go to court? → Must mean witnesses
Baraita 3
Why specify 'men'? Women can be litigants! → Must mean witnesses
Gezeira Shava All three baraitot also cite: 'Two men' here = 'two witnesses' in Deut. 19:15 — a textual parallel confirming the derivation

Standing in Court: Witnesses vs. Litigants

Ulla

  • Dispute (R' Yehuda vs. Rabbis) is only about litigants
  • Witnesses must ALWAYS stand
  • Source: 'The two men shall stand'

Rav Huna

  • Dispute is only during deliberation
  • At verdict: judges SIT, litigants STAND
  • Source: 'Moses sat… the people stood'
Practical Ruling Witnesses always stand; litigants stand at verdict time — the dispute concerns only the deliberation phase

The Duck Stratagem

אשתו של רב הונא הוה לה דינא קמיה דרב נחמן
Story Rav Huna's wife had a case before Rav Nachman. Dilemma: Rising for her honors a chaver's wife — but the other litigant may see it as judicial bias.

Solution: Rav Nachman told his attendant to cast a duck at him, forcing him to stand for an unrelated reason — fulfilling the honor without intimidating the other party.

At verdict time, he sat like one untying his shoe — neither fully standing nor sitting.
