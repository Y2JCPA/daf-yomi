Dilemma 1: Tarrying in the Airspace

Rava
If an impure person suspended himself in the airspace of the Temple courtyard long enough to bow down — what is the law?
Rava
Is the tradition of tarrying only for tarrying suited for bowing — i.e., standing on the ground?
Rava
Or perhaps the tradition applies to any tarrying, whether suited for bowing or not?
Gemara
תיקו — The dilemma shall stand unresolved.
CONTEXT This is the second in a series of dilemmas exploring limits of the shiheya (tarrying) tradition. Can you be liable for time spent in the Temple if you were never in a position to bow?

Rav Ashi's Two Dilemmas

Intentional Impurity in Temple

  • One intentionally made himself impure while already inside the Temple
  • Is tarrying only required for accidental (אונס) impurity?
  • Or does tarrying apply to intentional impurity too?
  • תיקו — UNRESOLVED

Nazirite at a Grave

  • A nazirite finds himself beside a grave (outside the Temple)
  • Is the tarrying tradition limited to inside the Temple?
  • Or does it extend to any impurity contracted beyond one's control?
  • תיקו — UNRESOLVED
SCOPE QUESTION Rav Ashi probes two new dimensions: intent (accidental vs. deliberate) and location (Temple vs. outside). Neither is resolved.

Short Route vs. Long Route

An impure person realizes he is in the Temple and must leave
↓
Short Route
Exempt — even walking heel-to-toe all day long
(Rava: continuous slow walking ≠ tarrying)
Long Route
Liable — brings a sliding-scale offering
Even if he runs! (Rabba to Abaye)
↓
Rava's further dilemma: Do multiple brief pauses combine to constitute tarrying?
↓
Distinction: Continuous slow walking ≠ stopping altogether
RAVA'S PRINCIPLE The short route exemption is about path, not speed. Walking heel-to-toe continuously on the shortest path = exempt. But stopping mid-walk raises a new question about combining pauses.

Abaye vs. Rabba: Running the Long Way

Abaye
If the impure person quickly ran out the longer way in the same time it would take to go the shortest way — is he exempt?
Abaye
Perhaps the tradition is about the measure of time, and he didn't exceed the minimum?
Rabba
No — the liability for the longer way was not given to be overridden. He chose the wrong path.
Rabba
He is liable even if he runs the long route, because the rule is about the route, not time spent.
PRINCIPLE The Torah's obligation attaches to the choice of route, not merely the duration of stay. Taking the wrong path creates liability regardless of speed.

Rav Huna: Turning Offerings with a Fork (צינורא)

ScenarioConsumed Without Turning?Ruling
Non-priest turns limb with forkWould NOT have burnedObviously liable — he performed actual service
Non-priest turns limb with forkWould have burned in 2 hours; now burns in 1 hourLiable — accelerating service IS service (Rav Huna's chiddush)
Non-priest turns limb with forkWould have burned at same rateNot liable — no meaningful service
R' ZEIRA'S APPLICATION This principle solves the puzzle: how can an impure priest serve and be liable for death at Heaven's hand but not karet? He exits via the shortest route (no tarrying = no karet) while briefly turning a limb with a fork (= valid service).
