Oaths on Utterance: When Is One Liable?

A person takes a shevuat bitui (oath on an utterance)
↓
Is the oath about an optional matter (reshut)?
Yes — Optional
Liable for breaking it (offering for unwitting, lashes for intentional)
No — Involves Mitzva
Exempt — whether oath was to perform or refrain from mitzva
Key Verse "To do evil, or to do good" (Lev. 5:4) — teaches that oath liability applies only to optional matters, not mitzvot.

Mishna: Oaths Involving Mitzvot

Type of OathExampleLiability
Oath to refrain from mitzva"I swear I will not eat matza on Pesach"Exempt — oath doesn't take effect
Oath to perform a mitzva"I swear I will eat matza on Pesach"Exempt — already sworn from Sinai
Oath about optional action"I swear I will/won't eat this loaf"Liable if broken
Oath to harm oneself"I swear I will fast today"Liable — optional matter
Oath to harm others"I swear I will strike so-and-so"Exempt — not in his power
Principle Oaths only generate liability when they concern matters fully within one's discretion — reshut (optional matters).

R' Yehuda ben Beteira vs. The Rabbis

R' Yehuda ben Beteira
Kal vachomer: If one is liable for breaking an oath about an optional matter (not sworn from Sinai)...
R' Yehuda ben Beteira
...how much more so for an oath about a mitzva, for which one IS under oath from Sinai!
Chakhamim
No! For optional oaths, Torah rendered one liable for both positive and negative oaths equally.
Chakhamim
But for mitzva oaths, the negative form (oath to refrain from mitzva) doesn't take effect — so there is no symmetry.
R' Yehuda ben Beteira
But look at oaths to do good to others — they have no valid negative inversion either, yet the Torah includes them!
Chakhamim
There, one can invert to 'I will NOT do good to others.' But with mitzvot — can one validly swear 'I will NOT perform a mitzva'? No!
Outcome The Rabbis prevail: one who swears to perform a mitzva and fails is exempt from the oath offering.

Deriving 'Optional Matters' from the Verse

Verse: "To do evil, or to do good" (Lev. 5:4)
↓
Could the verse refer to mitzva matters?
↓
If so: 'doing good' = performing mitzva, 'doing evil' = refraining from mitzva. But then 'doing evil' is really doing good (keeping mitzvot)!
↓
The juxtaposition creates a logical contradiction if applied to mitzvot
↓
Further proof: The word "or" (או) includes doing good to others — this inclusion is only necessary if the verse discusses optional matters
↓
Conclusion: The verse refers to reshut (optional matters) only
R' Yoshiya's View Even according to R' Yoshiya (who uses amplifications and restrictions), 'to do evil or to do good' serves to restrict and exclude mitzva oaths from liability.

Rava's Distinction: Oath Wording Matters

"I will not eat THIS LOAF"

  • Liable once he eats a kezayit (olive-bulk)
  • Oath applies to any part of the loaf
  • Each olive-bulk is a separate prohibition

"I will not eat IT"

  • Not liable until he eats the entire loaf
  • Oath applies to eating the whole thing
  • Partial eating does not trigger liability
Practical Impact If first oath is 'I will not eat it' (whole loaf) and second is 'I will not eat this loaf' (each piece), the second oath adds a new prohibition and takes effect — liable twice.

Multiple Oaths on One Loaf

1️⃣ First oath: "I will not eat this loaf" — takes effect
↓
2️⃣ Second oath: "I will not eat it" — cannot take effect (prohibition already exists)
↓
3️⃣ Third oath: "I will not eat it" — also does not take effect
↓
4️⃣ He eats the loaf → liable only once (for the first oath)
↓
5️⃣ But the redundant oaths are not void — if one dissolves the first oath via a chakham...
↓
6️⃣ ...the second oath takes its place (Rava's ruling)
Rava If a halakhic authority dissolves the first oath, the second oath retroactively takes effect — unless it too is dissolved.
