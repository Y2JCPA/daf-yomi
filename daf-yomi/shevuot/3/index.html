The 'Two That Are Four' Framework

Mishna opens: Four sets of halakhot share the pattern 'two that are four'
↓
Oaths
I will eat / I won't eat
+ I ate / I didn't eat
Temple Impurity
Ate sacrificial food / Entered Temple
+ unaware of food status / Temple identity
Shabbat
Poor person carries out / Homeowner carries out
+ Poor person brings in / Homeowner brings in
Leprosy
Se'et / Baheret
+ secondary of each
↓
Gemara asks: Why does Shevuot list all four sets, while Shabbat and Nega'im list only their own?
↓
Answer: Oaths and Temple impurity are written together in the Torah (Lev. 5:2–4) and both involve a korban oleh v'yored — so the mishna taught them together, then added all the rest
Key Insight The Torah's juxtaposition of oaths and Temple defilement explains why this tractate uniquely catalogs all 'two that are four' categories.

The Four Types of Oaths on an Utterance

OathTimeframeCategory
'I will eat'Future — positivePrimary (שתים)
'I will not eat'Future — negativePrimary (שתים)
'I ate' (but didn't)Past — false positiveAdditional (שהן ארבע)
'I did not eat' (but did)Past — false negativeAdditional (שהן ארבע)
Structure The two primary types involve future commitments; the additional two involve false statements about the past. All four can generate liability.

Temple Defilement: Two Awareness Lapses

Primary Two (שתים)

  • Unaware of impurity → ate sacrificial food (קודש)
  • Unaware of impurity → entered the Temple (מקדש)

Additional Two (שהן ארבע)

  • Aware of impurity but unaware the food was sacrificial
  • Aware of impurity but unaware the place was the Temple
Common Thread All four cases involve a lapse of awareness (he'elem) — either about one's impurity status or about what one encountered.

Whose Opinion Is the Mishna?

Gemara
The mishna is neither R' Yishmael nor R' Akiva!
Gemara
R' Yishmael holds liability applies only to future oaths — but the mishna includes past oaths too.
Gemara
R' Akiva holds no liability for unawareness of the Temple's identity — but the mishna includes that case.
Resolution
Option 1: It is R' Yishmael — some of the four carry liability, some carry exemption.
Resolution
Option 2: It is R' Akiva — similarly, the four are listed but not all generate a korban.
Problem This resolution is challenged: the mishna's parallel to leprous marks implies all four types carry liability, not just some.

Rava's Resolution: R' Yishmael and Lashes

New approach: The mishna is R' Yishmael
↓
R' Yishmael exempts past oaths from a korban — but not from malkot (lashes)
↓
Rava's teaching: The Torah amplifies shevuat sheker (false oath) to be like shevuat shav (vain oath) → both incur lashes
↓
All four oath types generate some liability — future oaths: korban; past oaths: lashes
Rava's Principle The Torah equates false oaths about the past with vain oaths, extending the punishment of lashes to cover all four categories.

The 'I Will Eat' Problem: Lav She'ein Bo Ma'aseh

Gemara
'I will not eat' + ate → lashes make sense — he performed an action violating his oath.
Gemara
But 'I will eat' + didn't eat — he violated by inaction! This is a לאו שאין בו מעשה.
Gemara
R' Yishmael must hold: one is flogged even for a prohibition without an action.
Gemara
But then R' Yochanan has a contradiction — he rules with unattributed mishnayot, yet says one is not flogged for inaction!
Core Tension If R' Yishmael's view is the unattributed mishna, R' Yochanan should follow it — yet he explicitly rules no lashes for inaction-based prohibitions.
