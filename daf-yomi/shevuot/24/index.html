When Does an Inclusive Prohibition Take Effect?

Reish Lakish
The mishna deals with an oath about less than a full measure (chatzi shiur). One is already under oath from Sinai even for sub-measures.
R' Yochanan
The mishna deals with an oath that includes permitted items along with forbidden ones — an issur kolel (more inclusive prohibition).
R' Shimon
A more inclusive prohibition does not take effect on existing prohibitions — even eating non-kosher on Yom Kippur is exempt from a separate offering.
Rava
If an inclusive prohibition takes effect: an oath on figs, then on figs and grapes — the second oath covers both, even the already-forbidden figs.
Core Issue Can an oath — a person-generated prohibition — function as an issur kolel and override a pre-existing Torah prohibition?

Two Readings of the Mishna

Reish Lakish

  • Mishna = oath about less than an olive-bulk (chatzi shiur)
  • R' Shimon: any amount triggers lashes from Sinai
  • Already under oath from Sinai for sub-measures
  • New oath cannot take effect on what Sinai already covers

R' Yochanan

  • Mishna = oath that includes permitted AND forbidden items
  • Oath not to eat = covers kosher and non-kosher
  • This is an issur kolel — more inclusive prohibition
  • Takes effect on non-kosher because it also adds new prohibitions
Key Difference Reish Lakish focuses on quantity (chatzi shiur), R' Yochanan on scope (inclusive prohibition).

R' Shimon's Logic: No Issur Kolel

A person eats non-kosher meat on Yom Kippur
↓
Is the Yom Kippur prohibition an issur kolel?
Rabbanan: Yes
Yom Kippur adds prohibition on all food → takes effect even on already-forbidden items → liable for Yom Kippur violation
R' Shimon: No
An inclusive prohibition does not override an existing one → exempt from Yom Kippur offering for non-kosher meat
Baraita R' Shimon: One who eats neveilah on Yom Kippur is exempt from a separate offering for the Yom Kippur violation.

Can an Oath Be 'Positive and Negative'?

Gemara
Leviticus 5:4 requires an oath to be invertible — 'to do evil or to do good' — positive ↔ negative.
Reish Lakish
Works easily: oath to eat any amount ↔ oath not to eat any amount. Both can take effect on sub-measures.
Gemara
Problem for R' Yochanan: an oath not to eat non-kosher works (issur kolel). But an oath to eat non-kosher cannot take effect — Torah already forbids it!
Gemara
Resolution: The mishna's oath is unspecified — 'I will not eat' (anything). Eating non-kosher = real eating, so the oath applies. Eating dirt = not real eating → exempt.
Rava's Principle If one swears 'I will not eat' and eats dirt, he is exempt — dirt is not considered 'eating.'

Issur Kolel vs. Issur Mosif (Expanded Prohibition)

FeatureIssur Kolel (Inclusive)Issur Mosif (Expanded)
What expands?More items forbidden for the same personMore people forbidden from the same item
ExampleYom Kippur prohibits ALL food (including already-forbidden)A bechor is born — forbidden to all, adds to existing issurim on that animal
Takes effect? (Rabbanan)Yes — adds scope of prohibitionYes — adds people to prohibition
Takes effect? (R' Shimon)No — existing prohibition blocks itYes — expanding to new people is different
R' Shimon's reasoningTwo distinct pieces (items) — we do not extendOne single piece (item) — adding people is valid
Rava's Distinction Even R' Shimon agrees issur mosif works because it adds people to one item. Issur kolel adds items — that he rejects.

Rava: Oath on Figs, Then Figs and Grapes

1️⃣ Person swears: 'I will not eat figs'
↓
2️⃣ Then swears: 'I will not eat figs and grapes'
↓
3️⃣ Second oath = issur kolel — adds grapes to the prohibition
↓
4️⃣ Since it takes effect on grapes, it also takes effect on figs
↓
5️⃣ Chiddush: Issur kolel applies even to person-generated prohibitions, not only Torah-level ones
Why Needed? You might think issur kolel only works for automatic prohibitions (like Yom Kippur). Rava teaches: it works for oath-based prohibitions too.
