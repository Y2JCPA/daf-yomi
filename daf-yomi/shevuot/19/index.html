Shevuot 19: Awareness, Uncertainty & the Two Paths

Core Theme This daf explores the boundaries of awareness of impurity (yedi'ah) required for liability when entering the Temple. The Gemara examines cases of dual lapses of awareness, the famous two-path scenario where impurity is certain but each individual entry is uncertain, and whether awareness of uncertain impurity functions like awareness of definite impurity for the purpose of separating sin-offerings.
3 / 9

What If He Forgot Both Impurity AND the Temple?

Rava
Asks Rav Naḥman: If one had a lapse of awareness of both his impurity and his being in the Temple — what is the halakha?
Rav Naḥman
He forgot his impurity — therefore he is liable.
Gemara
On the contrary! He forgot the Temple — so he should be exempt!
Rav Ashi
We observe: if he leaves when told he is impure → the lapse was about impurity → liable. If he leaves when told he is in the Temple → the lapse was about the Temple → exempt.
Ravina
No proof from behavior! He leaves only because he becomes aware of both simultaneously — there is no indication from here.
Key Insight When both types of awareness are absent, the Gemara cannot determine which lapse is primary — the question remains unresolved.

The Two Paths Scenario (Case 1)

Two paths — one impure (corpse buried), one pure
It is unknown which is which
↓
He walks on Path 1, does NOT enter the Temple
↓
He walks on Path 2, forgets his uncertain impurity, and enters the Temple
↓
He is certainly impure from one of the paths → LIABLE for a sin-offering
Logic Since he walked on both paths without purification, he is definitely impure — the only question is which path made him so.

Partial Awareness of Definite Impurity

First Tanna

  • Partial awareness of definite impurity IS counted as complete awareness
  • Even if he only remembers one path, the combined certainty creates liability
  • He knows enough to be considered as having had yedi'ah

R' Shimon ben Yehuda (in name of R' Shimon)

  • Partial awareness of definite impurity is NOT counted as complete awareness
  • Since he forgot walking Path 1, his awareness at each entry is only uncertain
  • Exempt even without purification between the two entries
Principle The dispute hinges on: does cumulative certainty from two uncertain events create the yedi'ah (awareness) needed for liability?

R' Yoḥanan vs. Reish Lakish: Uncertain Awareness After Purification

R' Yoḥanan
Since he certainly contracted impurity on one path and entered the Temple impure, the Sages made awareness of uncertain impurity like awareness of definite impurity.
Reish Lakish
This follows Rabbi Yishmael, who says we do not require any awareness at the beginning — it suffices if he later learns he was impure.
Why It Matters R' Yoḥanan establishes a broader principle: uncertain awareness can equal definite awareness. Reish Lakish limits the ruling to R' Yishmael's specific position.
