Shevuot 19 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Ashi, how do we determine whether one's lapse of awareness was about impurity or the Temple?
A. We observe whether he leaves when told about the impurity or about the Temple
B. We follow the majority — most lapses are about impurity
C. We assume the lapse was always about impurity since it is more severe
D. We ask him which he forgot first
Correct: A — Rav Ashi says we observe his behavior: if he leaves upon learning he is impure, the lapse was about impurity (liable); if he leaves upon learning he is in the Temple, the lapse was about the Temple (exempt). Ravina rejects this test.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In the two-paths scenario, why does R' Shimon exempt the person who purified himself between walking the paths?
A. Because the purification process was invalid
B. Because each time he entered the Temple, his impurity was only uncertain
C. Because he had full awareness of his impurity both times
D. Because only Kohanim are liable for this transgression
Correct: B — R' Shimon holds that since each individual entry involved only uncertain impurity — the first time he might not yet have become impure, the second time he might already have purified himself — there is no liability.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What does Rava explain about R' Shimon ben Yehuda's exemption even in the first case (no purification between paths)?
A. The person never actually entered the Temple
B. The person forgot walking the first path, so he had only partial awareness
C. The impurity from a path is only rabbinic, not biblical
D. Two uncertain impurities can never combine to create certainty
Correct: B — Rava explains that when walking the second path, he forgot having walked the first. His lapse of awareness at each point was only partial — and R' Shimon ben Yehuda holds partial awareness of definite impurity is NOT counted as complete awareness.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does R' Yoḥanan explain the liability in the two-paths case (with purification between entries)?
A. The purification was ineffective because it was based on uncertainty
B. The Sages made awareness of uncertain impurity equivalent to awareness of definite impurity
C. R' Yishmael's view requires no awareness at all
D. The person willfully ignored the possibility of impurity
Correct: B — R' Yoḥanan says that since the person certainly contracted impurity on one of the paths, the Sages treated awareness of uncertain impurity like awareness of definite impurity — creating liability.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to R' Yoḥanan's reading of R' Yehuda HaNasi, does awareness of uncertain forbidden-fat consumption separate acts for sin-offerings?
A. Yes — uncertain awareness separates for both sin-offerings and guilt-offerings
B. No — uncertain awareness separates only for provisional guilt-offerings, not sin-offerings
C. Yes — but only if the person ate from two different animals
D. No — awareness never separates acts; all acts are combined into one offering
Correct: B — R' Yoḥanan holds that R' Yehuda HaNasi's principle applies only to provisional guilt-offerings. Uncertain awareness does NOT separate acts for sin-offerings, because uncertain awareness is not equivalent to definite awareness.
