The Scope of Oath Liability: Past, Future, Lips & Heart

R' Yishmael
One is liable only for an oath about the future — 'to do evil or to do good' refers exclusively to future acts
R' Akiva
One is liable also for oaths about the past — 'whatsoever a man shall utter' amplifies the verse to include past oaths
R' Yoḥanan
Their dispute stems from their teachers' methods: R' Yishmael uses klal u'prat, R' Akiva uses ribbui u'mi'ut
Shmuel
Even after deciding to swear, one must express the oath with his lips for it to take effect
Key Verse Leviticus 5:4 — "Or if anyone take an oath clearly with his lips to do evil, or to do good, whatsoever it be that a man shall utter clearly with an oath"

R' Akiva vs R' Yishmael: Oaths About the Past

R' Yishmael
"To do evil or to do good" — the verse speaks exclusively of the future. One is liable only for breaking a forward-looking oath.
R' Akiva
If so, we'd only have liability for oaths involving evil/good. How do you extend to other matters?
R' Yishmael
From an amplification of the verse — the broader phrasing extends to other matters.
R' Akiva
If you amplify the verse for other matters, you must also amplify it for oaths about the past!
Gemara
R' Akiva's critique seems compelling — why does R' Yishmael disagree?
Core Dispute Is liability for shevuat bitui (oath on an utterance) limited to the future, or does it extend to the past as well?

Two Hermeneutical Methods — Two Interpretive Traditions

Klal u'Prat (כלל ופרט)

  • Teacher: R' Neḥunya ben HaKana
  • Student: R' Yishmael
  • Method: Generalization → Detail → Generalization
  • Rule: Refer only to items similar to the detail
  • Result: Detail is future oaths → only future oaths included
  • Excludes: Oaths about the past

Ribbui u'Mi'ut (ריבוי ומיעוט)

  • Teacher: Naḥum of Gam Zo
  • Student: R' Akiva
  • Method: Amplification → Restriction → Amplification
  • Rule: Include everything except what restriction specifically excludes
  • Result: Includes all oaths, past and future
  • Excludes: Only an oath to refrain from a mitzva
R' Yoḥanan Each sage follows the hermeneutical tradition of his teacher — their disagreement is methodological, not arbitrary

R' Akiva's Ribbui u'Mi'ut on Leviticus 5:4

Amplification (ריבוי)
"Or if anyone take an oath clearly with his lips"
→ Expands to include all possible oaths
↓
Restriction (מיעוט)
"To do evil, or to do good"
→ Narrows the scope
↓
Amplification (ריבוי)
"Whatsoever a man shall utter clearly with an oath"
→ Expands again
↓
Included
All oaths — past and future, whether involving good/evil or not
Excluded
An oath to refrain from a mitzva (a matter involving a commandment)
Principle Amplification → Restriction → Amplification = include everything except what the restriction specifically excludes

R' Yishmael's Klal u'Prat Analysis

Verse ElementFunctionEffect on Liability
"If anyone take an oath with his lips"Generalization (כלל)Broad category of oaths
"To do evil, or to do good"Detail (פרט)Future-oriented oaths specifically
"Whatsoever a man shall utter"Second Generalization (כלל)Extends to items similar to the detail
Generalization includes →Other matters (not evil/good)Even neutral future oaths are liable
Detail excludes →Past oaths (even evil/good)No liability for oaths about the past
Challenge The Gemara asks: why not reverse it — include past oaths and exclude non-evil/good matters? R' Yitzḥak answers: the detail resembles "he shall not break his word" (future), not "you shall not lie" (past)

What Makes an Oath About the Past "Unwitting" (שוגג)?

One swears falsely about the past
(e.g., "I ate" when he didn't)
↓
Knew it was false
Intentional (מזיד)
No offering permitted
Didn't know it was false
Circumstances beyond control (אונס)
Exempt from offering
↓
Rav Naḥman's solution:
He knows the oath is false and prohibited,
but doesn't know he's liable to bring an offering
↓
This constitutes shogeg (unwitting)
→ Liable to bring a sliding-scale offering
Novelty Oaths are unique in Torah law — one brings a sin-offering for a simple prohibition (no karet), so even the Rabbis agree ignorance of offering-liability counts as unwitting
