How Do the Four Shades of Leprous Marks Relate?

Rabbi Akiva
All four shades are ordered by brightness — each combines with its adjacent shade. Both secondary shades derive from sappaḥat and connect to se'et.
Rabbis (Mishna)
The four shades form two independent pairs: baheret + lime-colored mark, and se'et + egg-membrane mark. Each pair combines only within itself.
R' Yehoshua b. R' Akiva
If all shades combine freely, why not just say: "Any mark from egg-membrane brightness and above is impure"?
Rabbi Akiva
A priest who cannot distinguish and name the four shades is not authorized to inspect marks at all.
Core Dispute Do the four shades of nega'im form one continuous spectrum (R' Akiva) or two separate pairs (Rabbis)?

Which Shades Combine? R' Akiva vs. The Rabbis

Rabbi Akiva

  • All four shades ordered by brightness on one spectrum
  • Adjacent shades combine with each other
  • Both secondary shades derived from sappaḥat → connect to se'et
  • Egg membrane combines with se'et via sappaḥat derivation
  • Snow-white baheret & lime-colored mark each adjacent to se'et

Rabbis (Mishna)

  • Four shades split into two independent pairs
  • Baheret pairs with lime-colored mark only
  • Se'et pairs with egg-membrane mark only
  • Shades combine only within their own pair
  • A lime mark CANNOT combine with se'et or egg-membrane
Practical Impact If a person has a mark partly lime-colored and partly egg-membrane-colored — the Rabbis say they don't combine; R' Akiva says they may.

Why Specify Four Shade Names?

R' Yehoshua b. R' Akiva
Why did the Sages say "two types that are four" and specify their names?
Rabbi Akiva
What else should they say?
R' Yehoshua
Just say: Anything from egg-membrane brightness and above is impure!
Rabbi Akiva
They specified names so you know which shades combine — each with its adjacent one.
R' Yehoshua
Then say: egg-membrane and above is impure, and they combine!
Rabbi Akiva
A priest who cannot identify and name the four shades may not inspect nega'im at all.
Gemara's Inference R' Yehoshua used only a single formulation ('egg-membrane and above'), not a dual one — suggesting R' Akiva views all shades on one combined spectrum, not two separate pairs.

Scriptural Sources for the Four Shades (Amud B)

Baheret = intense white
Abaye: "And if it is a white baheret" (Lev. 13:4) — it alone is the brightest white
↓
Se'et = raised/wool-white
The word se'et means 'raised' — "upon all the raised up hills" (Isaiah 2:14)
↓
Sappaḥat = 'secondary/appended'
"Append me (sefaḥeni) to a priestly class" (I Samuel 2:36)
↓
Secondary to Se'et
Egg membrane — derived directly from sappaḥat in Lev. 14:56
Secondary to Baheret
Lime of Sanctuary — R' Zeira: gezera shava of 'white' linking se'et and baheret
Derivation The Torah placed sappaḥat between se'et and baheret (Lev. 13:2) to teach that just as se'et has a secondary shade, so does baheret.

Rabbi Ḥanina's Milk Analogy (R' Akiva's View)

🥛 Four cups of milk, each a slightly different shade of white
↓
🩸 2 drops of blood in cup 1 → dimmest white (egg membrane)
↓
🩸 4 drops in cup 2 → next shade (lime of Sanctuary)
↓
🩸 8 drops in cup 3 → brighter white (se'et / wool-white)
↓
🩸 12 drops in cup 4 (some say 16) → brightest (baheret / snow-white)
↓
📊 All cups on a single spectrum — each one above the next
Gemara's Challenge This analogy involves milk mixed with red (blood) — perhaps R' Akiva's ruling applies only to reddish-white marks (פתוך), not flawless white ones?
