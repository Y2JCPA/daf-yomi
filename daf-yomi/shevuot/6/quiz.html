Shevuot 6 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Akiva, why did the Sages specify the names of all four shades rather than just stating a brightness threshold?
A. So priests can identify and name all shades — those who cannot may not inspect marks
B. To teach that only the two brightest shades are truly impure
C. Because the Torah explicitly lists all four names
D. To establish that egg membrane marks are always pure
Correct: A — Rabbi Akiva told his son that a priest who cannot distinguish between and name all four shades is not authorized to inspect leprous marks.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does the word 'sappaḥat' teach about the system of leprous marks?
A. That baheret is the brightest shade
B. That there is a shade secondary to and appended to a primary shade
C. That only priests may inspect marks
D. That reddish marks are always impure
Correct: B — Sappaḥat is derived from 'sefaḥeni' (append me) in I Samuel 2:36, teaching that there is a secondary shade appended to a primary one.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does the Gemara reject Rabbi Ḥanina's milk-and-blood analogy as proof that Rabbi Akiva holds all shades combine on one spectrum?
A. Because the analogy uses only three cups, not four
B. Because milk mixed with blood resembles reddish-white marks — R' Akiva's view might apply only to mixed marks, not flawless white
C. Because Rabbi Ḥanina actually supports the Rabbis' view
D. Because the number of blood drops doesn't match the brightness order
Correct: B — The analogy involves milk mixed with red (blood), so perhaps R' Akiva's single-spectrum ruling applies only to marks mixed with red (befatukh), not to flawless white marks.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rava correct the analogies proposed for the Rabbis' view of the four shades?
A. He says all four shades are equal with no hierarchy
B. He uses two completely separate kingdoms (Persia and Rome), each with its own subordinate
C. He eliminates the need for analogies entirely
D. He proposes a single hierarchy with the Exilarch on top
Correct: B — Rava's analogy of King Shapur + subordinate and the Roman Emperor + subordinate properly represents TWO independent pairs, matching the Rabbis' view that each primary mark has its own secondary — not one continuous hierarchy.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does the Gemara derive that baheret has a secondary shade, given that 'sappaḥat' in the verse is adjacent to se'et?
A. From the position of sappaḥat between se'et and baheret in Leviticus 13:2
B. From a tradition of Moses at Sinai
C. From the numerical value (gematria) of the word baheret
D. From the fact that baheret appears more frequently in the Torah
Correct: A — A baraita teaches that the Torah placed sappaḥat between se'et and baheret to indicate that just as se'et has a secondary shade, so too does baheret. R' Zeira additionally derives this via a gezera shava of the word 'white.'
