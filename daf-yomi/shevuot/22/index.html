The Great Measure Debate: How Much Triggers Liability?

Rabbis
One who swears not to eat is liable only for eating an olive-bulk (k'zayit) — the standard Torah measure.
R' Akiva
One who swears not to eat is liable for any amount (kol shehu) — since when else does mere speech create an offering obligation?
Rava
The dispute applies only when he did not specify an amount. If he explicitly says 'any amount,' all agree he is liable for any amount.
Rav Pappa
The dispute is only about oaths. With konamot, since eating is not mentioned explicitly, all agree any amount triggers liability.
Core Issue Does a general oath not to eat automatically include the standard Torah measure of an olive-bulk, or does the oath-taker's speech create a new, stricter prohibition?

R' Akiva's Challenge: Where Else Does Speech Create an Offering?

R' Akiva
Where do we find one who merely speaks and must bring an offering? An oath-taker just speaks and brings an offering — so any amount should suffice!
Gemara
What about a blasphemer? He speaks and brings an offering!
R' Akiva
A blasphemer merely sins by speaking — he doesn't generate a new prohibition through his speech.
Gemara
A nazirite generates a prohibition through speech and brings an offering!
R' Akiva
The nazirite's offering is to permit wine again, not for violating the prohibition itself.
Gemara
Consecrated property (hekdesh) — one creates a prohibition by speech and brings an offering for misuse!
R' Akiva
Consecration creates a prohibition for the whole world. An oath is unique: it creates a prohibition only for oneself.
Key Point R' Akiva systematically distinguishes oaths from every other speech-based obligation, proving they are uniquely personal prohibitions — which is why any amount should trigger liability.

Konamot vs. Oaths: Rav Pappa's Distinction

Konamot (Vows)

  • Item is forbidden 'like an offering'
  • No explicit mention of 'eating'
  • Therefore: like specifying any amount
  • All agree: liable for any amount
  • Items from separate konamot CAN combine
  • Dispute on misuse of consecrated property (me'ilah)

Oaths (Shevuot)

  • Prohibition phrased as 'I will not eat'
  • Explicit mention of eating — has defined measure
  • Dispute: R' Akiva vs. Rabbis on minimum amount
  • Items from separate oaths do NOT combine
  • Distinct regarding sin-offerings (Rav Pineḥas)
  • Standard misuse rules do not apply
Rav Pappa With konamot, since the vow doesn't mention 'eating' but only that the item is forbidden like an offering, it is as if one specified any amount.

Rava's Unresolved Dilemmas (Teiku)

🤔 One swears: 'I will not eat dirt' — dirt is not normally eaten. Is any amount enough, or is olive-bulk still required?
↓
⚖️ On one hand: he said 'I will not eat' — eating means an olive-bulk
↓
⚖️ On the other hand: dirt is not food — no one eats an olive-bulk of dirt, so perhaps any amount suffices
↓
❓ TEIKU — The dilemma remains unresolved
↓
🍇 Second dilemma: 'I will not eat a grape seed' — it's eaten in a mixture but not alone. Any amount or olive-bulk?
↓
❓ TEIKU — Also unresolved
Pattern Both dilemmas pivot on the same tension: when the item is unusual to eat, does 'I will not eat' still invoke the standard olive-bulk measure?
