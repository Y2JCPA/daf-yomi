Shevuot 22 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava, when does everyone agree that an oath creates liability for eating any amount?
A. When he swears not to eat non-kosher food
B. When he explicitly specifies that any amount is forbidden
C. When he takes the oath in front of witnesses
D. When he swears regarding an item that has no standard measure
Correct: B — Rava teaches that if the oath-taker specifies that any amount is included, all agree he is liable for any amount — the dispute only applies when he did not specify.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Rav Pappa say that konamot always create liability for any amount?
A. Because konamot are more stringent than oaths by Torah law
B. Because the vow doesn't mention 'eating' — it just forbids the item like an offering
C. Because R' Akiva's opinion is accepted regarding konamot
D. Because konamot involve consecrated property
Correct: B — Since a konam merely declares the item forbidden 'like an offering' without mentioning eating (which has a defined measure), it is treated as if the person specified that any amount is forbidden.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Pineḥas, why don't items from separate oaths combine to produce an olive-bulk?
A. Because each oath requires its own witnesses
B. Because items from separate oaths are distinct regarding sin-offerings
C. Because the Torah requires each food to be measured independently
D. Because combining amounts is never permitted for oaths
Correct: B — Rav Pineḥas explains that since two items forbidden by a single oath are distinct with regard to sin-offerings (one is liable separately for each), their sub-measures also don't combine.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is Rava's dilemma regarding one who swears not to eat dirt?
A. Whether the oath takes effect at all since dirt is inedible
B. Whether he must eat an olive-bulk or any amount to be liable
C. Whether dirt is considered food for purposes of blessings
D. Whether he can nullify the oath through regret
Correct: B — Rava already established that one who simply swears 'I will not eat' and eats dirt is exempt. The dilemma is specifically about one who swears 'I will not eat dirt' — since dirt is not normally eaten, perhaps any amount suffices, or perhaps 'eat' still requires an olive-bulk.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does R' Akiva respond to the challenge from consecrated property (hekdesh), where speech creates a prohibition and an offering?
A. He says consecrated property is a Rabbinic institution, not Torah law
B. He says the offering for hekdesh is different from an oath offering
C. He says consecrating an item creates a prohibition for the whole world, not just oneself
D. He says consecrated property requires a court declaration, not mere speech
Correct: C — R' Akiva distinguishes oaths as uniquely creating a personal prohibition — only the oath-taker is affected. Consecrated property, by contrast, creates a prohibition for everyone in the world, making it fundamentally different.
