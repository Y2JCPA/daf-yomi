Shevuot 38 — Oath Formulations & Partial Admission

שְׁבוּעַת הַפִּקָּדוֹן
🔑 Core Theme This daf analyzes how the wording of an oath determines whether a defendant faces one liability or many when denying multiple claimants. It then turns to whether a false oath about rape/seduction fines creates guilt-offering liability, and concludes with the Mishna on partial admission — the minimum claim and admission thresholds that trigger a judicial oath.
3 / 9

Four Tannaitic Views: When Is Each Denial a Separate Oath?

R' Meir

  • One oath covering all → one liability
  • Specified each → liable for each
  • Dispute: Is 'nothing of yours' (no 'and') specific or general?

R' Yehuda

  • 'And nothing of yours, and nothing of yours' = specific
  • Each 'and' creates a separate denial
  • Liable for each claim individually

R' Eliezer

  • Oath word must come at the END
  • 'Nothing of yours… on my oath' = specific
  • Final oath retroactively covers each denial

R' Shimon

  • Must say 'on my oath' to EACH claimant
  • Only explicit oath per person = separate liability
  • Most stringent formulation requirement
Key Question Does the conjunction 'and' (וְ) between denials unify them into one oath or separate them into distinct liabilities?

Shmuel vs. R' Yochanan: Reading the Meir-Yehuda Dispute

Shmuel
Using 'and nothing of yours' (with conjunction) — R' Meir says it's general (one liability), R' Yehuda says it's specific (multiple liabilities)
Shmuel
Without the conjunction — R' Yehuda says general, R' Meir says specific. They flip positions!
R' Yochanan
All agree that 'and nothing of yours' (with conjunction) is specific — even R' Meir!
R' Yochanan
They only disagree about 'nothing of yours' without 'and': R' Meir = specific, R' Yehuda = general
R' Yochanan
R' Meir's only case of general denial: saying in the plural — 'nothing of yours (all of you) is in my possession'
Source of Dispute Shmuel derives his reading from the baraita (R' Yehuda's language implies he's correcting R' Meir). R' Yochanan derives from the Mishna (the unattributed ruling reflects R' Meir's view).

Rape/Seduction Oath: R' Shimon vs. the Rabbis

Father claims: 'You raped/seduced my daughter'
Defendant swears falsely: 'I did not'
↓
What is the father actually claiming?
R' Shimon
Father claims only the fine (kenas)
→ Fine is not paid on self-admission
→ Oath denial is meaningless
→ EXEMPT from guilt-offering
Rabbis
Father also claims humiliation & degradation (boshet/pegam)
→ These are monetary, not fines
→ Oath denial is meaningful
→ LIABLE for guilt-offering
Rav Pappa's Principle R' Shimon: A person doesn't forgo a fixed-value claim (fine) to claim something requiring appraisal (humiliation). Rabbis: One doesn't forgo a claim the defendant can't escape by admission to claim one he can escape.

Rava & Abaye: The Wheat-Barley-Spelt Analogy

Rava
R' Shimon's case is like: 'Return my wheat, barley, and spelt.' Defendant swears: 'Your wheat isn't with me' — true for wheat, false for barley/spelt → exempt, since the specific oath was truthful
Abaye
Not comparable! There, he only denied the wheat. Here, the defendant denies everything — 'I never raped or seduced her' — a blanket denial!
Abaye
Better analogy: 'Nothing of yours is with me at all' — a general denial that's partly false → should be liable! So this can't explain R' Shimon.
Ravin
R' Yochanan actually said: R' Shimon holds the father claims only the fine; the Rabbis hold he also claims humiliation/degradation. The dispute is about what's being claimed, not oath mechanics.
Abaye's Correction A blanket denial ('I never did it') is fundamentally different from a specific denial ('your wheat isn't here') — the analogy must match the scope of the denial.

R' Chiyya's Baraita: 20 Sin-Offerings

ElementDetails
Scenario5 claimants, each claiming: deposit, pledge, stolen item, lost item
Claims per person4 items × 5 people = 20 claims
Defendant's oathSpecifies to first claimant, then says 'and neither are yours' to each subsequent one
Ruling20 sin-offerings — one per false claim
SignificanceProves 'and neither are yours' is a specific denial — supports R' Yochanan over Shmuel
Proof If 'and nothing of yours' were general (as Shmuel reads R' Meir), the count would be far fewer than 20. The baraita supports R' Yochanan's reading.
