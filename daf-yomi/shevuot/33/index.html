Oath of Testimony: When Are Witnesses Liable?

Witnesses deny knowledge of testimony under oath
↓
Single General Denial
"We don't know any testimony" → One oath liability
Itemized Denial
"We don't know about deposit, loan, theft, lost item" → Liable for each
↓
But liability only applies to monetary claims — not status, not capital cases
Core Principle The oath of testimony (shevuat ha-edut) applies only to cases involving monetary claims, derived by comparing the word 'or' (o) in its verse to the oath on deposits.

Why Is the First Set of Witnesses Liable?

Challenge
The second set of witnesses is still available to testify — what loss did the first set cause by their denial?
Ravina
The second set were brothers-in-law — each married to one of two sisters — making them relatives and unfit to testify together.
Ravina
The sisters were moribund (gosesot). You might think: most who are moribund die soon, so they're already fit to testify.
Ravina
The tanna teaches: since now the wives have not yet died, the second set remains disqualified. The claim depends entirely on set one.
Key Insight We don't rely on statistical probability (rov) that moribund individuals will die — current legal status governs witness fitness.

Mishna: One Oath vs. Multiple Oaths

Single Oath Liability

  • Witnesses say: "We don't know any testimony for you"
  • General blanket denial
  • Even if claim includes deposit, loan, theft, and lost item
  • Liable for only one false oath

Multiple Oath Liability

  • Witnesses say: "We don't know about your deposit, loan, theft, lost item"
  • Itemized denial of each component
  • Each denial treated as a separate oath
  • Liable for each and every component
Parallel Case Same rule applies to types of grain: a general denial about wheat, barley, and spelt = one oath; itemizing each = multiple oaths.

Dilemma: Oath of Testimony Concerning a Fine

Gemara
If witnesses are asked to testify about an act that carries a fine (knas) and they falsely deny — are they liable for a false oath of testimony?
R' Elazar b'R' Shimon
Even if the offender admits liability, witnesses can still come and obligate him to pay the fine. So their testimony has monetary value — they are liable.
Rabbanan
One who admits a fine is exempt. So the defendant could always admit and escape payment — does this mean witnesses' testimony has no guaranteed monetary value?
Gemara
The dilemma applies only according to the Rabbanan. According to R' Elazar b'R' Shimon, witnesses clearly cause financial outcome.
The Dilemma Since the defendant can exempt himself by admitting, is the witnesses' testimony truly a 'monetary matter' (tvi'at mammon) or not?

Slander Case: Resolving the Baraita

Baraita: Witnesses to slander (motzi shem ra) deny testimony → liable for false oath
↓
But slander payment is a fine! Whose opinion is this?
↓
Must be R' Elazar b'R' Shimon: even after admission, witnesses can still obligate the fine
↓
Latter clause: "If he admitted, he's exempt" — seems like the Rabbanan?
↓
Resolution: Entire baraita is R' Elazar b'R' Shimon. Exemption applies only where there are no witnesses at all.
R' Elazar b'R' Shimon's View Admission exempts from a fine only when no witnesses exist. If witnesses are available, they can still testify and obligate the fine regardless of admission.
