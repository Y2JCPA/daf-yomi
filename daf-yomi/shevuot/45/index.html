Oaths in Monetary Disputes: Who Swears?

Monetary dispute arises between two parties
↓
One Party Suspect
The opposing party swears and collects
Both Suspect
R' Yosei: Oath returns to its place
R' Meir: They divide the amount
Neither Suspect
Standard oath rules apply based on type of claim
Key Theme This daf explores the architecture of monetary oaths — who swears, who collects, and what happens when the oath system breaks down due to suspect parties or competing presumptions.

The Storekeeper's Ledger: Three-Party Dispute

👨‍💼 Employer tells storekeeper:
"Give my laborers a sela in coins"
↓
🏪 Storekeeper says: "I gave it to them"
👷 Laborers say: "We didn't receive it"
↓
Mishna's Rule
Storekeeper swears he paid → collects from employer
Laborers swear they weren't paid → collect from employer
Employer bears the loss
Ben Nannas Objects
"One side must be lying!
How can both swear?"
→ Both collect without an oath
Ben Nannas Ben Nannas argues that requiring oaths where one party is certainly lying invites false oaths. Better to let both collect without swearing.

Customer vs. Merchant: Who Holds the Advantage?

Storekeeper Says: "Give Me the Dinar"

  • Customer claims: "I already paid you"
  • Customer takes an oath that he paid
  • The one making the payment swears

Customer Says: "Give Me the Produce"

  • Storekeeper claims: "I already gave it to you"
  • Storekeeper takes an oath that he delivered
  • The one making the delivery swears

R' Yehuda's View

  • Whoever has the produce in hand has the advantage
  • Possession creates presumption of truth
  • No oath needed for the one holding goods
Money Changer The same rules apply to money changers. R' Yehuda adds: a money changer would never give coins before receiving the dinar, so possession of coins proves payment was made.

The Hired Worker's Oath: Time Limits and Presumptions

Gemara
Why does the worker swear and collect his wages? Because the employer is distracted with many laborers and may forget to pay.
Questioner
If the employer forgets, then even regarding the amount of wages fixed, the employer should be forgetful — yet the baraita says the worker must bring proof!
Gemara
The amount fixed — he certainly remembers. He forgets only whether he paid, not how much.
Questioner
Then even after the deadline passes, the worker should still be able to swear and collect!
Gemara
Once the deadline for bal talin (timely payment) arrives, the employer takes it upon himself to remember. After the deadline, we presume he paid.
Questioner
Is the worker suspected of demanding wages twice?
Gemara
After the deadline, two presumptions support the employer: (1) he wouldn't violate bal talin, and (2) a worker wouldn't defer requesting his wages.
Key Window The worker can swear and collect only within the payment deadline (the night after working). After that, the dual presumption shifts credibility to the employer.

Miggo: 'Since He Could Have Said...'

Shmuel's Rule: A worker's oath applies only when hired with witnesses
↓
Was the worker hired in the presence of witnesses?
↓
Yes — With Witnesses
Employer cannot deny hiring
→ Worker swears and collects
No — Without Witnesses
Employer has miggo: could say 'I never hired you'
→ Believed when he says 'I already paid'
→ Worker cannot collect with oath alone
Miggo Principle If a person could have made a stronger claim (total denial) but instead makes a weaker one (partial admission), the weaker claim is deemed credible without an oath. R' Yochanan agrees with Shmuel's ruling.
