Can One Forbid Another's Property Through Idol Worship?

A person worships an animal belonging to someone else
Merely bows to it
The animal is permitted — one cannot forbid another's property
Performs a sacrificial rite upon it
The animal is forbidden — e.g., pouring a libation between its horns or slaughtering it
Core Principle A person cannot render another's property forbidden by mere worship, but performing an actual ma'aseh avodah (sacrificial rite) upon it does forbid it.

Chizkiyya's Verse: Revoking Idol Status

כְּאַבְנֵי גִר מְנֻפָּצוֹת לֹא יָקֻמוּ אֲשֵׁרִים וְחַמָּנִים
Isaiah 27:9 "When he makes all the stones of the altar as limestones that are beaten into pieces, so that the asherim and sun images shall rise no more."

If the stones are beaten to pieces → idol status is revoked.
If not beaten to pieces → they "shall rise" — status remains.

Worshipping Another's Animal Under Duress

Baraita 1
If one worships his own animal — forbidden. If another's — permitted.
Baraita 2
Any animal worshipped — even unwittingly, under duress, or willingly — is disqualified from sacrifice!
Rami bar Ḥama
"Under duress" means gentiles coerced him to bow to his own animal — no contradiction.
R' Zeira
But the Torah exempts one who acts under duress! (Deut. 22:26)
Rava
Duress in private — exempt. Duress in public — one must give one's life. This is chilul Hashem.
Resolution Rava reconciles the verses: privately under duress — not required to die, so worship doesn't forbid; publicly under duress — must sacrifice one's life, so worship does forbid.

When Does Worshipping Another's Property Forbid It?

One worships an animal belonging to another person
↓
What type of worship was performed?
Bowed to it
Permitted — mere prostration cannot forbid another's property
Poured libation ON its horns
Animal is a mere platform/altar — still permitted (Rav Adda bar Ahava)
Poured libation BETWEEN its horns (worshipping the animal)
Forbidden — sacrificial rite performed upon the animal itself
Key Distinction Pouring on the horns uses the animal as an altar (permitted). Pouring between the horns worships the animal itself (forbidden). — Rav Adda bar Ahava

Exchange of an Exchange for Idol Worship

View 1: Exchange of Exchange is Permitted

  • The item exchanged directly for the idol — forbidden
  • An item exchanged for that item — permitted
  • Derives from: "for IT is accursed" — only "it" (the direct exchange), not further exchanges
  • Idol worship & Sabbatical Year are two verses that come as one → cannot teach other cases (like orlah)

View 2: Exchange of Exchange is Also Forbidden

  • The item exchanged directly for the idol — forbidden
  • An item exchanged for that item — also forbidden
  • Derives from: "you shall become accursed LIKE IT" — anything acquired from the exchange is accursed
  • Uses "it" to exclude exchanges of orlah and kilei hakerem instead
Deut. 7:26 "You shall not bring an abomination into your house, that you should become accursed like it; you shall utterly detest it… for it is accursed."
