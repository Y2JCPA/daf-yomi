How Does God Judge Israel vs. the Nations?

God exercises judgment over the world
↓
Nations of the World
Punished for all transgressions at once — total ruin (Ezekiel 21:32)
Jewish People
Punished gently, like the pecking of a hen — a little at a time (Job 30:24)
KEY THEME This daf explores God's measured mercy toward Israel — through oaths, restrained anger, and gentle discipline — contrasted with decisive judgment of the nations.

The Future Sun: Wicked vs. Righteous

The Wicked

  • The sun will burn them like a furnace (Malachi 3:19)
  • Neither root in this world nor branch in the World-to-Come
  • All the proud and evildoers become stubble

The Righteous

  • The sun of righteousness with healing in its wings (Malachi 3:20)
  • Not only healed but rejuvenated
  • They will leap as calves of the stall
METAPHOR The same sun serves two opposite purposes — destruction for the wicked and renewal for the righteous.

Resolving Contradictions About God's Nature

Rav Ḥinnana bar Pappa
Contradiction: "The Almighty, Whom we have not found out His excellent power" (Job 37:23) vs. "Great is our Lord, mighty in power" (Psalms 147:5)
Gemara
At a time of judgment (mercy) → power hidden. At a time of war → power revealed.
R' Ḥama b. R' Ḥanina
Contradiction: "Fury is not in Me" (Isaiah 27:4) vs. "The Lord is a jealous and furious God" (Nahum 1:2)
Gemara
"Fury is not in Me" → toward Israel. "Jealous and furious" → toward the nations.
METHOD The Gemara resolves contradictions between verses by assigning each to a different context — God's behavior varies by audience and circumstance.

God's Judgment: The Pecking of a Hen

God judges the nations
↓
All transgressions punished at once
"A ruin, a ruin, a ruin" (Ezekiel 21:32)
God judges Israel
↓
Rava's Reading #1
Punishment like a hen's peck (kefid) — a tiny bit at a time, never total destruction
Rava's Reading #2
Even small mitzvot — like a hen pecking at a dunghill — combine into great merit
SOURCE "Surely none shall put forth his hand to a ruinous heap; neither because of these things shall help come in one's calamity" (Job 30:24) — reread as: befido = pecking; lahen shua = they are saved.

Balaam's Donkey Exposes Him

🏇 Princes of Moab ask: Why ride a donkey, not a horse?
↓
🗣️ Balaam: My horse is grazing in the meadow
↓
🫏 Donkey speaks: "Am not I your donkey?" — the one you always use
↓
🔄 Balaam: Only for carrying burdens! Donkey: "Upon which you have ridden"
↓
⏳ Donkey: "All your life long unto this day" — and I also serve you at night
↓
💡 Conclusion: Balaam couldn't know his donkey's mind — how could he know God's?
WORDPLAY "Hahasken hiskanti" (Numbers 22:30) is linked to "sokhenet" (I Kings 1:2, re: Abishag), implying the donkey alluded to an intimate relationship with Balaam.

God's Moment of Anger (רֶגַע)

QuestionAnswer
How long does God's anger last?One moment (רֶגַע) — the time to say the word
Source #1"His anger is but for a moment; His favor, for a lifetime" (Psalms 30:6)
Source #2"Hide yourself for a brief moment, until the anger passes" (Isaiah 26:20)
When does it occur?During the first three hours of the day (Abaye)
How to identify it?The rooster's crest turns completely white — no red streaks
Why during those hours?Kings place crowns on their heads and bow to the sun (R' Meir)
BALAAM'S POWER "Knows the knowledge of the Most High" means Balaam could pinpoint this exact moment of divine anger to time his curses — but God withheld His anger during all of Balaam's days.
