When Is Wine Left Near a Gentile Still Permitted?

A gentile is found near Jewish wine
↓
Could the gentile be seen or caught?
Yes — Fear of Detection
Wine is permitted
The gentile wouldn't risk being seen making a libation
No — Unobserved
Wine is forbidden
The gentile may have used it for idolatrous libation
KEY PRINCIPLE Rava's rulings throughout this daf consistently hinge on whether the gentile had reason to fear being caught — the psychological deterrent is what protects the wine.

Jew Leaves Wine to Go Pray or Observe Shabbat

Case 1
A Jew and gentile are drinking together. The Jew hears davening and leaves to pray at the synagogue.
Rava
The wine is permitted — the gentile thinks: 'Any moment now he will remember his wine and come back.'
Case 2
A Jew and gentile are on a ship. The Jew hears the Shabbat shofar, disembarks, and leaves for all of Shabbat.
Rava
Still permitted! The gentile doesn't realize the Jew truly won't return.
Issur the Convert
When we were gentiles, we used to say: Jews don't really keep Shabbat — if they did, how many wallets would be found in the marketplace?
Rava
They don't know the halacha of R' Yitzchak — one may carry a wallet in increments of less than 4 amot. Gentiles assume Jews would violate Shabbat for money.
INSIGHT The gentile's perception of Jewish behavior — not the reality — determines whether the wine is forbidden.

Thieves in Pumbedita — Following the Majority

Thieves broke into Pumbedita and opened many barrels of wine
↓
Who were the thieves — Jews or gentiles?
Rava's Ruling
Permitted — Most thieves in Pumbedita are Jews, and we follow the majority (rov)
Neharde'a Parallel
Shmuel similarly permitted wine when thieves came to Neharde'a
↓
Is this like R' Eliezer's rule — uncertainty of entry = pure?
↓
Since they opened many barrels, they were looking for money, not interested in wine — so it's like uncertainty of entry, not contact
PRINCIPLE When identity of the person is uncertain, we follow the majority (rov). When intent is uncertain, we assess the most likely purpose.

Key vs. Authorization — Gentile Woman & the Wine Shop

Transferring the Key

  • A Jewish shopkeeper gave her wine shop key to a gentile woman
  • R' Yitzchak citing R' Elazar: Wine is permitted
  • Transferring a key = responsibility for guarding only
  • Does NOT authorize entry into the shop

Parallel: Am Ha'aretz & Keys

  • Mishna (Teharot 7:1): Transferring keys to an am ha'aretz
  • Pure items remain pure
  • Same logic: key ≠ permission to enter
  • Abaye: If this is true for tumah, certainly for yayin nesekh!
ABAYE'S ARGUMENT Ritual purity laws (tahara) are MORE stringent than wine libation laws. If a key doesn't create impurity concerns, it certainly doesn't create libation concerns.

Courtyard Divided by a Low Partition (Meseifas)

Rav
If your neighbor across a low partition is an am ha'aretz — your pure items are impure.
But if a gentile neighbor — the wine is not rendered yayin nesekh.
R' Yochanan
Even with an am ha'aretz neighbor, your pure items remain pure.
Challenge to Rav
A baraita says: Inner courtyard of a chaver, outer of an am ha'aretz — produce is pure even if the am ha'aretz can reach it!
Rav
Different — there, if caught reaching in, the am ha'aretz would be accused as a thief. That deters him.
Support for Rav
Rabban Shimon b. Gamliel: If a chaver's roof is above an am ha'aretz's roof — if the am ha'aretz can reach up, items are impure.
R' Yochanan
Different — the am ha'aretz can excuse reaching up by saying 'I was just stretching.' No fear of being caught.
CORE DISPUTE Rav: Purity laws are stricter than libation laws — a low partition doesn't protect purity but does protect wine.
R' Yochanan: A partition protects even purity.
