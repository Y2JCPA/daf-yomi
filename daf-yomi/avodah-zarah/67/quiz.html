QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Shmuel and R' Yochanan, why is the mishna's leniency for vinegar in split beans limited to hot beans?
A. Because cold beans cannot absorb flavor at all
B. Because in hot beans the vinegar immediately detracts, while in cold beans it first enhances before detracting
C. Because cold beans with vinegar is a normal dish that people eat intentionally
D. Because the principle of bitul only applies to hot mixtures
Correct: B — When vinegar falls into cold beans, it first enhances the flavor. If the beans are later heated and the flavor deteriorates, the mixture is treated as 'first enhanced then detracted' (noten ta'am lishvach ul'vasof lifgam), which is forbidden because we follow the initial beneficial stage.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the practical difference between the two versions of Reish Lakish's statement?
A. The first version permits more cases than the second
B. The second version says we ignore other flavor flaws and focus only on whether the issur currently detracts
C. The first version requires a Gentile taster and the second does not
D. They disagree about whether the principle applies to wine
Correct: B — Version 1 (strict) requires the dish to be otherwise perfect — the only flaw is the forbidden substance. Version 2 (lenient, accepted as halakha) says that even if other factors also detract from the flavor, since the forbidden substance currently detracts, the mixture is permitted.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to R' Abbahu citing R' Yochanan, what is the status of a mixture where the forbidden food's flavor is present but its substance has dissolved completely?
A. Permitted, since there is no substance
B. Forbidden, and one receives lashes
C. Forbidden, but one does not receive lashes
D. It depends on whether the flavor enhances or detracts
Correct: C — R' Yochanan distinguishes three levels: flavor + substance = forbidden with lashes; flavor only (no substance) = forbidden but no lashes; flavor that detracts = permitted. The case described is the middle category.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is R' Meir's proof that even detrimental flavor (noten ta'am lifgam) should render a mixture forbidden?
A. The Torah explicitly states that all forbidden flavors are prohibited regardless of quality
B. Vessels of gentiles require purging even though they impart stale, detrimental flavor
C. The verse about neveilah applies to all cases of forbidden food mixture
D. A Rabbinic gezeirah was enacted to prevent confusion between enhanced and detracted flavors
Correct: B — R' Meir argues from the Torah's requirement to purge gentile vessels (Numbers 31:22-23). Since the absorbed flavor in these vessels is old and certainly detrimental, yet the Torah still requires purging, this proves that even detrimental flavor is prohibited.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does R' Shimon counter R' Meir's proof from vessels of gentiles?
A. He says the Torah's requirement for vessels is only Rabbinic, not Biblical
B. He says purging is required for cleanliness, not because of forbidden flavor
C. Following Rav Huna b. Rav Chiyya, the Torah only prohibits a pot used that very day (ben yomo), when the flavor still enhances
D. He argues that metal vessels are different from food mixtures
Correct: C — R' Shimon, following Rav Huna son of Rav Chiyya, limits the Torah's prohibition of gentile vessels to a pot used that very day (ben yomo), when the absorbed flavor is still fresh and enhances the food. A pot not used that day imparts stale, detrimental flavor and would not be prohibited by Torah law.
