Noten Ta'am Lifgam — Forbidden Flavor That Detracts

Forbidden food falls into permitted food
↓
Does the forbidden food impart recognizable flavor?
No flavor
Permitted — nullified (bitul)
Flavor + substance
Forbidden + liable for lashes
Flavor only (no substance)
Forbidden but no lashes
↓
If flavor present — does it enhance or detract?
Enhances
Forbidden — all agree
Detracts
R' Shimon: Permitted
R' Meir: Still forbidden
HALAKHA If a forbidden food imparts flavor to a permitted food to its detriment (noten ta'am lifgam), the mixture is permitted.

Vinegar into Split Beans: Hot vs. Cold

Forbidden vinegar (חומץ) falls into split beans (גריסין)
↓
Were the beans hot or cold when the vinegar fell in?
Hot beans
Vinegar detracts from flavor immediately → Permitted
Cold beans, then heated
Vinegar first enhanced flavor while cold → then detracted when heated → Forbidden
KEY PRINCIPLE A dish where an ingredient first enhanced then later detracted (נותן טעם לשבח ולבסוף לפגם) is forbidden — we follow the initial beneficial stage.

Shmuel & R' Yochanan on the Bean Ruling

Shmuel
The mishna's leniency for vinegar in beans applies only when vinegar fell into hot split beans — the flavor is immediately detrimental.
R' Yochanan
I agree: if the vinegar fell into cold beans and one later heated them, it is like a food that was first enhanced then detracted — and is forbidden.
Rav Dimi
R' Yochanan also said: In Tzippori, they would prepare split beans and vinegar on erev Shabbat and call it 'cress dish'.
AGREEMENT Shmuel, R' Yochanan (via Ravin and Rav Dimi) all concur: the mishna's leniency is limited to hot beans where the detriment is immediate.

Two Versions of Reish Lakish's Ruling

Version 1 — Strict

  • "Noten ta'am lifgam" = the dish is perfect in every way
  • It is only lacking because of the forbidden substance
  • Not because it is missing salt or has excess spices
  • Only then is it permitted as lifgam

Version 2 — Lenient

  • Even if the dish has other flaws (lacking salt, too much spice)
  • We do NOT say: the detriment is from those flaws, not the issur
  • Since the forbidden substance now detracts from flavor — permitted
  • This version is accepted as halakha
HALAKHA The Gemara rules in accordance with the lenient version of Reish Lakish: as long as the forbidden substance currently detracts, the mixture is permitted — regardless of other contributing factors.

R' Meir vs. R' Shimon: Core Dispute

R' Meir
Whether the forbidden food enhances or detracts from the flavor — the mixture is forbidden either way.
R' Shimon
If it enhances — forbidden. If it detracts (noten ta'am lifgam) — permitted.
R' Meir
My proof: vessels of gentiles (כלי גויים) require purging. The absorbed flavor is stale and detrimental, yet the Torah still forbids them!
R' Shimon
Following Rav Huna b. Rav Chiyya: the Torah only prohibits a pot used that very day (בן יומו), when the flavor still enhances — not stale flavor.
ROOT OF DISPUTE R' Meir derives from gentile vessels that even stale/detrimental flavor is forbidden. R' Shimon limits the Torah's prohibition to same-day vessels where flavor still enhances.
