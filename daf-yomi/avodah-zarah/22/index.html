The Saffron Growers' Partnership

Case
Saffron growers jointly owned a field — gentile worked Shabbat, Jew worked Sunday. Can they split profits equally?
Rava
Permitted. They may divide profits equally.
Ravina
But a baraita says: Without prior stipulation, the Jew may not say 'take your Shabbat share, I'll take a weekday.'
Resolution
It was revealed they had stipulated from the outset — so Rava ruled correctly all along.
Key Principle Prior stipulation (hitchilu) is what transforms a Shabbat labor arrangement from prohibited agency into a permissible independent division.

Yes — stipulated
✅ Gentile takes Shabbat profits, Jew takes weekday profits — Permitted
No — calculated later

What if they neither stipulated nor calculated?

R' Shimon ben Elazar: Samaritans vs. Gentiles

Renting to a Gentile

  • ✅ We can tell him not to work on certain days
  • He will comply with our instructions
  • No concern about lifnei iver

Renting to a Samaritan (Cuti)

  • ❌ He will NOT comply — claims greater knowledge
  • 'I am more learned than you — work is permitted!'
  • Violates lifnei iver AND field bears Jew's name
Double Reasoning R' Shimon ben Elazar gives two reasons to prohibit: (1) lifnei iver — enabling a commanded person to sin, and (2) marit ayin — the field is called by the Jewish owner's name.

Can We Buy Animals for Korbanot from Gentiles?

Baraita
One may purchase animals from gentiles for offerings — no concern for bestiality, idol worship, etc.
Gemara
But the Mishna says gentiles are suspected of bestiality! Shouldn't we worry the animal is disqualified?
Rav Sheila bar Avina / Rav
A gentile protects his own animal — bestiality causes barrenness (females) and weakens flesh (males).
Rav Kahana
Even male animals are protected — the owner won't risk deteriorating their livestock's value.
Resolution Self-interest protects one's own animals. The suspicion in the Mishna applies when the gentile has nothing to lose — such as with someone else's animal.
