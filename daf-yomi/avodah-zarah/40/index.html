Identifying Kosher Fish Products

Fish product from a gentile
Is it whole fish, pieces, brine, or roe?
Whole/Pieces
Head & spine must be recognizable
(Rav Pappa: each fish)
Brine
Must contain kilbit
(kosher fish indicator)
Roe/Intestines
Purchase only from an expert
or with fish present
KEY PRINCIPLE Different standards of identification apply depending on whether one is consuming the fish body, its brine, or its roe.

How to Identify Kosher Tarit

Rav Huna
Tarit is not permitted until both its head and spine are recognizable.
Rav Naḥman
It is permitted if either the head or the spine is recognizable.
Rav Yehuda / Ulla
Their dispute is only about dipping in brine. For eating the body, all agree both head and spine are needed.
Rav Pappa
Halakha: Tarit is prohibited until the head and spine of each and every fish is recognizable.
R' ZEIRA'S PRACTICE R' Zeira originally dipped in tarit brine, but once he heard Ulla's distinction — that the dispute is only about brine — he stopped dipping as well.

Pieces of Fish — Do They All Need Signs?

Pieces of fish brought by a gentile
Do they all need kosher signs?
↓
Baraita: If even one out of 100 pieces has a sign of kashrut, all are permitted
(Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's incident)
↓
Rav Pappa explains:
Only when the pieces correspond — they fit together, proving they're from one fish
↓
Why state this? Lest you say the fit was coincidental — the baraita teaches we are not concerned
RECONCILIATION Rav Pappa reconciles his strict ruling (each fish needs identification) with the lenient baraita (one sign suffices) by limiting the baraita to pieces from the same fish.

The Tzaḥanta Ship Incident at Sikhra

Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana

  • Saw scales on the fish
  • Permitted the entire shipment
  • Shofarot went out permitting the fish
  • Rav Pappa: He permitted even the bodies
  • Rav Pappi: He permitted only the brine

Rava

  • Objected: scales may be from prior kosher fish in the boat
  • Prohibited all the fish on the ship
  • Shofarot went out prohibiting them
  • Concern: common kosher fish in that region shed scales onto non-kosher fish
RAV ASHI'S CAUTION Rav Ashi neither permitted nor prohibited — he acknowledged both Rav Pappa's leniency and Ulla's distinction that the dispute applies only to brine, not bodies.

Fish Roe & Intestines — Expert Required?

AuthorityRuling on Fish Roe/Intestines
RavMay be purchased only from an expert
R' DostaiDelete 'roe' from Rav's statement — non-kosher fish don't have roe (they give live birth)
R' ZeiraDon't delete it — both kosher and non-kosher fish produce eggs
Rav YehudaIf seller says 'I salted them and they were kosher' — roe is permitted
Rav NaḥmanRoe is prohibited unless seller shows the actual kosher fish it came from
PRACTICAL HALAKHA Rav Yehuda instructed his attendant Adda: Once the seller says 'I salted them,' they are permitted — following his own lenient ruling.

Permitted Gentile Foods: Storeroom vs. Market

🫒 Ḥiltit leaf — Permitted; slivers on it came from the ground, not sliced with a non-kosher knife
↓
🫒 Rolled olive cakes — Permitted even when soft; softness is from their own oil, not from wine
↓
🫒 Overripe olives (shlashin) — R' Yosei prohibits; pit slips out when grasped due to softness
↓
🦗 Locusts from storeroom — Permitted; no wine sprinkled on stored goods
↓
🦗 Locusts from market basket — Prohibited; gentile sprinkles wine on displayed goods
↓
🍎 Apple wine from storeroom — Permitted; from market — prohibited (mixed with grape wine)
PATTERN Storeroom/storehouse goods are permitted because gentiles don't add wine to bulk storage; market goods are suspect because wine is mixed in for display or flavor.
