Does Meshikhah (Pulling) Acquire for a Gentile?

Ameimar
Meshikhah applies to gentiles. Persians send gifts and never retract — proving pulling alone acquires.
Rav Ashi
Meshikhah does NOT apply to gentiles. Persians don't retract out of haughtiness, not halakhic obligation.
Rav Ashi
Proof: Rav told wine merchants to take money first before measuring wine — if pulling acquired, this would be unnecessary.
Core Issue The mode of acquisition determines when the gentile owns the wine — and therefore whether the seller receives permitted or forbidden money.

A Jew sells wine
to a gentile

When was the price fixed?
Before Measuring
Wine still belongs to Jew when price set → Money is PERMITTED
After Measuring
Wine already poured into gentile's vessel → became yayin nesekh first → Money is FORBIDDEN
Key Principle The moment of price-fixing determines ownership — if the wine was still permitted when the price was set, the money is permitted.

Does Meshikhah Acquire for a Gentile?

Ameimar
Yes. Persians send pardashnei (gifts) and never retract — proving pulling acquires without payment.
Rav Ashi
No. They don't retract because of haughtiness — it's shameful, not legally binding.
Rav Ashi
Proof from Rav: He told wine merchants — take dinars first, then measure. If pulling acquired, the gentile would own the wine before it becomes forbidden!
Gemara
Rav's case may involve pouring into the gentile's vessel containing yayin nesekh — the wine is forbidden on contact, before pulling occurs.
Practical Stakes If meshikhah acquires, the gentile owns the wine before it becomes forbidden — and the seller's payment is permitted.

Craftsmen Receiving Libation Wine as Payment

Before Taking Possession

  • Wine has NOT entered the Jew's domain
  • Permitted to say: 'Give us money instead'
  • Not considered a sale of forbidden wine
  • The Jew never owned the yayin nesekh

After Taking Possession

  • Wine HAS entered the Jew's domain
  • Prohibited to ask for money instead
  • Would be tantamount to SELLING yayin nesekh
  • Deriving benefit from libation wine is forbidden
Mishna The critical moment is when the wine enters the Jew's possession — before that point, requesting monetary value is permitted.

ScenarioWine StatusAcquisitionResult
Poured into gentile's vessel (held in hand)Forbidden on contact with bottomGentile acquires in airspaceMoney permitted — acquired before forbidden
Poured into vessel on groundForbidden on contactNo acquisition by pullingMoney forbidden — wine forbidden before acquired
Remnant of yayin nesekh on jug mouthForbidden on contact with rimNever enters permittedMoney forbidden — each drop forbidden immediately
Conclusion The Gemara concludes: Nitzok is NOT a connection (chibur) — the stream doesn't render the entire flow forbidden. The problem is the remnant on the jug's mouth (kuzanta).

Rav's Instructions to Wine Shopkeepers

💰 Take dinars from the gentile FIRST
↓
🍷 Then measure and pour the wine for the gentile
↓
🚫 If gentile has no money available...
↓
🤝 Lend him dinars, then accept those dinars as payment
↓
📋 This way it is treated as a LOAN repayment, not a wine sale
↓
✅ The wine becomes yayin nesekh in the gentile's possession, not the Jew's
Rav's Concern Without this procedure, the wine becomes yayin nesekh while still in the Jew's possession — and accepting money for it would be deriving benefit from libation wine.
