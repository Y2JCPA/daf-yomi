Why Is Ḥilak (Sultanit) Prohibited?

Sultanit — small fish caught before scales develop
↓
Its small size causes it to be intermingled with non-kosher fish in the catch
↓
Cannot distinguish kosher from non-kosher in the mixture → Prohibited
IMPORTANT DISTINCTION Fish that will grow scales (sultanit, afiyatz) or that shed scales when caught (akunas, afuna) are intrinsically permitted. The problem is the mixture with non-kosher species, not the fish itself.

Small Fish of the Bav River

Abaye
The tzaḥanta (small fish) of the Bav River are permitted.
Gemara
Is it because the water flows rapidly and non-kosher fish can't survive?
Gemara
No — we see non-kosher fish do exist in fast-flowing rivers.
Gemara
Is it because the water is salty? No — non-kosher fish exist in salty water too.
Gemara
Rather, the mud of the Bav River is unsuitable for non-kosher fish to reproduce.
Ravina
Nowadays, the government built canals connecting rivers — the Goza and Gamda rivers spill non-kosher fish into the Bav. Inspection is now required.
PRINCIPLE Environmental presumptions of kashrut can change when physical conditions change — e.g., new canals connecting waterways.

One Seal vs. Two Seals (Deposited Foods)

Two Seals Required

  • Meat (בשר) — animal and fish
  • Wine (יין)
  • Fish pieces with no kashrut sign
  • Milk (חלב) — per Rav (disputed)

One Seal Sufficient

  • Fish stew (מוריס)
  • Ḥiltit (חילתית)
  • Bread (פת) — exchange is detectable
  • Cheese (גבינה) — low value, not worth forging
RAV vs. SHMUEL Rav includes milk (requires 2 seals) and fish separately. Shmuel says fish with no sign = meat (same category), and omits milk from his list entirely.

Ḥiltit: Why Is It Prohibited?

🔪 Gentiles slice ḥiltit with a knife that has non-kosher residue
↓
⚖️ Normally: a prohibited substance that worsens flavor (noten ta'am lifgam) is permitted
↓
🌶️ But ḥiltit is sharp — its sharpness enhances the taste of the non-kosher residue on the knife
↓
🚫 Therefore it is noten ta'am lishvaḥ (enhances flavor) → Prohibited
↓
✅ Exception: R' Levi's gentile slave sold ḥiltit — permitted because he was a Sage's slave
HALAKHA Sharp foods (ḥiltit, onions, etc.) absorb and enhance residual flavors from knives — a principle with broad kashrut implications.
