Broken Idols, Natural Detachment & Forbidden Figures

Reish Lakish
An idol that broke on its own is permitted — the worshipper has clearly abandoned it.
R' Yochanan
An idol that broke on its own is still forbidden — each fragment retains its status.
R' Yochanan
If Reish Lakish is right, why can't a Jew revoke an idol's status? It should be like a broken idol!
Rava
It's a rabbinic decree — lest the Jew first lift the idol and acquire it, making revocation impossible.
Core Tension Does breaking or reshaping an idol automatically remove its forbidden status? The daf explores multiple challenges to Reish Lakish's lenient position through cases of Jewish revocation, Mercury stones, tree worship, and bird's nests.

The Pit in Rimon: Certainty vs. Uncertainty

A violent person throws a non-viable newborn into a pit
↓
A kohen looks in to determine if male or female
↓
Is the kohen tamei (impure)?
Sages Rule: Tahor
Martens and polecats (bardelas) are common — they likely dragged the body away
Gemara Reframes
Perhaps it was congealed blood, not a baby — making it uncertainty vs. uncertainty, not uncertainty vs. certainty
Principle An uncertainty cannot override a certainty — so the Gemara reinterprets the case to avoid this problem.

Can a Jew Revoke an Idol's Status?

R' Yochanan
The Mishna says a Jew cannot revoke an idol's status. But according to Reish Lakish, bending it should be like breaking — and it should be permitted!
Abaye
The Mishna refers to where the Jew bent it without breaking — reshaping isn't the same as breaking.
R' Yochanan
But a Mishna on 53a says bending an idol does revoke its status, even without removing material!
Gemara
That applies only when a gentile bends it. A Jew's bending does not revoke the status.
Rava
Actually, a Jew's bending should work by Torah law. But the Sages decreed against it — lest the Jew first lift the idol, acquiring it. An idol in Jewish possession can never be revoked.
Rava's Decree The concern: if a Jew lifts an idol before attempting revocation, he acquires it — and an idol owned by a Jew can never have its status revoked.

Mercury Stones: Jew vs. Gentile

Gentile Repurposes Stones

  • Takes stones from Mercury (Markulis) worship heaps
  • Paves roads and builds theaters with them
  • ✅ Permitted — the gentile revoked their idolatrous status

Jew Repurposes Stones

  • Takes stones from Mercury worship heaps
  • Paves roads and builds theaters with them
  • ❌ Prohibited — a Jew cannot revoke idol status
  • Rava: Decree lest the Jew first lift the stones and acquire them
Key Distinction Only a gentile can revoke the idolatrous status of an idol. A Jew's physical actions on the idol are ineffective due to rabbinic decree.

Bird's Nest on an Ashera Tree

QuestionAnswer
Where did the nest's branches come from?The bird brought them from elsewhere, not from the worshipped tree itself
How do we know?The parallel case of a consecrated tree says no me'ila liability — proving the branches aren't consecrated
What about natural detachment (leaves falling)?Rav Huna b. R' Yehoshua: Natural growth processes cannot revoke idol status
Can you climb the ashera tree?No — but you may knock the nest off with a pole
What about chicks vs. eggs?Chicks that can fly: permitted. Eggs: prohibited
R' Abbahu says R' Yochanan says 'Knock off the nest' means knock off the chicks — but one may NOT benefit from the nest material itself.

Chicks, Eggs & the Mother Test

🌳 A bird builds a nest atop an ashera (worshipped tree)
↓
🪺 The nest contains eggs and chicks
↓
🐣 Chicks that can fly away — not bound to the tree → Permitted
↓
🥚 Eggs — dependent on the tree → Prohibited
↓
🐥 Chicks still needing their mother — Rav Ashi: treated like eggs → Prohibited
Rav Ashi's Addition The test isn't just whether they're eggs or chicks — it's whether they have independent mobility. Dependent chicks are still bound to the forbidden tree.
