Pagan Fairs: May a Jew Cause Benefit to Idol Worship?

Reish Lakish
Deriving benefit from idol worship is prohibited, but merely causing benefit (indirect support) is permitted.
R' Yochanan
Even causing benefit is prohibited — kal va'chomer: if deriving benefit is forbidden, surely causing benefit is too!
R' Natan
The wreath-and-tax scenario proves it: at a pagan fair, a Jew inevitably either derives or causes benefit to idolatry.
Reish Lakish
The Rabbis disagree with R' Natan. I follow their lenient view.
R' Yochanan
No one disagrees with R' Natan — all agree causing benefit is prohibited.
Core Issue Does buying at a pagan fair indirectly fund idol worship, and is such indirect benefit prohibited?

Deriving Benefit vs. Causing Benefit

Reish Lakish
Buying from stores adorned for idol worship: deriving benefit (נהנה) is forbidden, but causing benefit (מהנה) — where a portion of sales supports idolatry — is permitted.
R' Yochanan
Even stores adorned only with fruit are prohibited. Kal va'chomer: if deriving benefit is forbidden, how much more so causing benefit!
Gemara
A baraita of R' Natan challenges Reish Lakish — the wreath scenario shows causing benefit is also treated as prohibited.
Rav Mesharshiyya b. Rav Idi
Reish Lakish holds the Rabbis disagree with R' Natan. R' Yochanan holds everyone agrees with R' Natan.
Key Distinction נהנה (nehene) = deriving benefit FROM idolatry  |  מהנה (mehane) = causing benefit TO idolatry

R' Natan's Wreath-and-Tax Dilemma

A Jew is at a pagan fair. Announcement: wear a wreath of roses to receive a tax reduction.
↓
Wears the wreath ✓
He derives benefit from idol worship (tax discount)
Refuses the wreath ✗
He causes benefit to idol worship (pays full tax to them)
Conclusion One who does business at a pagan fair is trapped — all items acquired there must be destroyed or disposed of.

Disposing of Items from Pagan Fairs

🐄 Animals — Must be destroyed: cut hooves from the knee down
↓
🍎 Produce — Leave to decompose on its own
↓
👗 Clothing & Vessels — Leave to decompose on their own
↓
💰 Money & Metal Vessels — Cast into the Dead Sea (ים המלח)
↓
🔒 Consecrated Animals — Lock the door; let it starve (don't cut hooves)
Why Starve? Cutting hooves of a hekdesh animal would degrade sanctified property. Slaughtering risks someone eating it and misusing hekdesh.

Why Not Cut Hooves of Consecrated Animals?

Abaye's Reason

  • Cutting hooves = degrading consecrated animals (גנאי קדשים)
  • Based on: "You shall not do so to Hashem your God" (Deut. 12:4)
  • One may not actively destroy sacred items

Rava's Reason

  • It appears as inflicting a blemish (מום) on a sacrificial animal
  • Today (no Temple): only a rabbinic appearance issue
  • When Temple stood: actually prohibited by Torah law
  • Unlike a blemished animal, this one has no monetary value either
Key Point Without the Temple, the animal is unfit for sacrifice AND has no redemption value — so the prohibition is only mi'drabanan (it merely appears like blemishing).
