KEY THEME This daf navigates the tension between preserving life and maintaining religious boundaries, with circumcision and medical treatment as the two central cases.

Who May Circumcise a Jewish Child?

R' Meir
An Aramean (gentile) may circumcise a Jewish boy — but only if he is a recognized expert physician (מומחה) who would not risk his reputation.
R' Yehuda
A Samaritan may not circumcise a Jew, because Samaritans circumcise for the sake of Mount Gerizim — not for God's covenant.
R' Yosei
Where do we find that circumcision must be performed lishmah (for its own sake)? A Samaritan may circumcise Jews until his soul departs — no concern at all!
Gemara
The contradiction in R' Yehuda's views is resolved: the prohibition is actually R' Yehuda HaNasi, while the permission is R' Yehuda bar Ilai.
RESOLUTION Two different Tannaim named Yehuda — R' Yehuda HaNasi forbids gentile circumcision; R' Yehuda bar Ilai permits it.

Biblical Sources: Why Gentiles Cannot Circumcise

Daru bar Pappa / Rav

  • Source: "You shall keep My covenant, you and your seed" (Gen. 17:9)
  • Only Abraham's descendants may perform milah
  • Excludes ALL gentiles — even circumcised ones
  • A circumcised Arab still may NOT perform milah

R' Yochanan

  • Source: "Himmol yimmol" — he must be circumcised (Gen. 17:13)
  • Doubled verb teaches: only one who IS circumcised may circumcise others
  • Would PERMIT a circumcised Arab or gavnuni
  • Excludes uncircumcised gentiles only
NAFKA MINA The practical difference: a circumcised Arab or hill person (gavnuni). Rav forbids; R' Yochanan permits.

Lishmah Debate: Must Circumcision Be 'For God's Sake'?

R' Yehuda — Lishmah RequiredR' Yosei — No Intent Needed
Source Verse"Let all his males be circumcised to the Lord" (Ex. 12:48)"Himmol yimmol" — anyone may circumcise (Gen. 17:13)
Reading of Exodus 12:48"To the Lord" modifies circumcision — must be for God"To the Lord" modifies Passover — "keep Passover to the Lord"
Response to other verseDoubled verb is just Torah's literary style (דיברה תורה כלשון בני אדם)The doubling specifically teaches that ANY person may perform milah
Samaritan performing milahForbidden — they circumcise for Mount GerizimPermitted — intent is irrelevant
PRINCIPLE R' Yosei's reading separates 'to the Lord' from circumcision entirely — it applies to the Paschal offering mentioned earlier in the verse.

When May a Jew Receive Medical Treatment from a Gentile?

A Jew is ill and only a gentile physician is available
↓
Uncertain outcome
May live or die from ailment → MAY NOT be treated by gentile
(Concern: gentile may kill him)
Certain death without treatment
Will definitely die if untreated → MAY be treated
(Possible the gentile will save him)
↓
Objection: Even if certain to die — what about chayei sha'ah (temporal life)?
↓
We are not concerned with temporal life when there is a possibility of permanent recovery
PROOF The four lepers outside the besieged city (II Kings 7:4) chose to risk death by approaching the Aramean camp rather than wait to starve — showing we don't prioritize chayei sha'ah over potential full recovery.

The Story of Ben Dama

אשריך בן דמא שגופך טהור ויצאת נשמתך בטהרה
DRAMATIC INCIDENT Ben Dama, nephew of R' Yishmael, was bitten by a snake. Ya'akov of Sekhanya — a disciple of Jesus the Nazarene — came to heal him.

R' Yishmael refused to allow the treatment. Ben Dama pleaded: "Let him treat me! I can cite a verse to prove it's permitted!"

But before he could finish speaking, his soul departed.

R' Yishmael declared: "Fortunate are you, ben Dama — your body is pure! You did not transgress: 'He who breaks through a fence, a snake shall bite him'" (Ecclesiastes 10:8).
