Interpreting Rav Sheshet's Statement

Gemara
Rav Sheshet's ruling mixes finding vessels and forming figures — is this consistent?
Abaye
The first and last clauses discuss finding vessels; the middle clause discusses forming figures. They are simply different cases.
Rava
The entire statement refers to finding vessels. The middle clause follows Rabbi Yehuda, who adds the nursing woman and Sar Apis to the forbidden list.
R' Yehuda's Additions Nursing woman (מינקת) = symbolizes Eve who nurses the world. Sar Apis (שר אפיס) = symbolizes Joseph who ruled (שר) and appeased (מפיס) the world during famine.

R' Elazar HaKappar & the Dragon Ring

💍 R' Elazar HaKappar finds a ring on the road with a dragon figure
↓
👦 Encounters a minor gentile boy — says nothing to him
↓
🧔 Encounters an adult gentile — asks him to revoke the ring's idolatrous status
↓
❌ The gentile refuses to revoke it
↓
👋 R' Elazar HaKappar slaps the gentile, who then succumbs and revokes it
Three Halakhot Derived ① A gentile can revoke the status of another gentile's idol. ② Only one aware of idol worship (not a minor) can revoke. ③ A gentile can revoke status even against his will.

Why Not Rely on Owner's Despair?

R' Ḥanina
Why did R' Elazar HaKappar need a gentile to revoke the status? A lost object found in a public area — the owner has despaired of recovering it! The idol status should be automatically void.
Abaye
The owner despairs of the object, yes. But he does not despair of its idolatrous status (מאיסורא). He thinks: if a gentile finds it, he'll worship it. If a Jew finds it, he'll sell it to a gentile who will worship it.
Principle Despair of recovering a lost item (יאוש) does not automatically revoke its forbidden idolatrous status. Active revocation (ביטול) is still required.

Rabban Gamliel's Moon Diagrams — How Was This Permitted?

Rabban Gamliel had diagrams of moon phases to help witnesses identify the new moon
↓
But the Torah says: "You shall not make figures of My attendants" (Exodus 20:20) — including the moon!
↓
Answer 1
Others (gentiles) fashioned them for him — Jews may not form such figures but may possess them
Answer 2
As Nasi, the public was always with him — no suspicion of idolatry
Answer 3
The figures were not whole — assembled from pieces, so not truly forbidden forms
Context The Gemara offers multiple justifications because the general prohibition against forming celestial images is firmly established from Exodus 20:20.
