Why Can't We Sell Large Livestock to Gentiles?

Mishna: One may not sell large livestock, calves, or foals to gentiles
↓
What is the concern? Not bestiality — a gentile spares his own animal
↓
Shabbat labor! But he bought it — it's his animal now?
↓
Lending/Leasing
Animal still belongs to the Jew — gentile works it on Shabbat
Testing
Seller demonstrates animal near Shabbat — drives it by voice command
Key Principle The sale itself is permitted, but the Sages enacted a gezeirah (decree) against selling due to the risk of lending, leasing, or testing the animal — all of which involve a Jew's animal working on Shabbat.

Selling vs. Leaving an Animal with a Gentile

R' Elazar
Even where they prohibited leaving an animal in seclusion with a gentile, it is permitted to sell it to him.
R' Elazar
Why? A gentile spares his own animal from bestiality — he doesn't want it to become sterile.
R' Elazar
But with someone else's animal? He has no such compunction.
Rav (via Rav Taḥlifa)
Rav retracted his earlier view and agrees: a gentile spares his animal — he does not want it to become sterile.
Distinction Selling = animal becomes the gentile's own → he protects it. Seclusion = animal remains someone else's → no protection.

Three Rabbinic Concerns Behind the Prohibition

Mishna forbids selling large livestock to gentiles — not because of bestiality
↓
Concern: The gentile will put the animal to work on Shabbat
↓
But he bought it — it's his! Why is the Jew liable?
↓
1. Lending (שאילה)
Animal remains the Jew's property while the gentile uses it on Shabbat
2. Leasing (שכירות)
Leasing does not transfer ownership — still the Jew's animal
3. Testing (נסיון)
Seller commands animal near Shabbat to demonstrate fitness — driving laden animal
Rami b. Rav Yeiva At times one sells close to sunset on Erev Shabbat, says 'Go test it,' and the animal walks at the Jewish owner's voice — making him liable for a sin-offering.

Does Leasing Confer Ownership?

Proof SourceScenarioImplication
Mishna 21a — Renting a houseJew rents house to gentile who brings in idolsIf lessee acquired it, it's his house — why prohibit? → Idol worship is uniquely severe
Mishna Terumot 11:9Priest rents cow from Israelite — may not feed it terumaIf lessee acquired it, it would be the priest's cow → proves leasing does NOT confer ownership
ConclusionOne does not acquire an item through leasingAll three concerns (lending, leasing, testing) remain valid grounds for the decree
Key Proof Rav Yitzḥak b. Rav Mesharshiyya: If leasing conferred ownership, a priest who leased a cow could feed it teruma — but the Mishna says he cannot. Leasing ≠ ownership.

Assigning Innocent Motive: Shabbat vs. Shevi'it

Rav Huna / Beit Hillel

  • Rav Huna sold a cow to a gentile — claimed buyer intended to slaughter it
  • Proof: Beit Hillel permit selling a plowing cow in Shevi'it — buyer may slaughter it
  • Principle: Assume the permitted purpose when one exists

Rabba's Distinction

  • Shevi'it: No command to rest one's animal — only the land must rest
  • Shabbat: One is commanded to rest the animal — so the decree applies
  • The cases are not comparable

Rav Ashi's Rule

  • Rejects Rabba's distinction entirely
  • Where an innocent motive is plausible → assign it (even if commanded to rest)
  • Where no innocent motive exists → do not assign one (even if not commanded to rest)
  • Proof: Shevi'it vessels (plow, yoke) cannot be sold — no innocent use
Rav Ashi The key factor is not whether you must rest the item, but whether a plausible innocent use exists for the buyer.

Selling to a Jew Suspected of Reselling to Gentiles

Rabba
I sold a donkey to a Jew — even one suspected of reselling to gentiles.
Abaye
But he will go and sell it to a gentile!
Rabba
Is the only possibility that he sells to a gentile? Maybe he'll sell to a Jew! We cannot assume the worst.
Abaye
But a baraita teaches: In a place where they did not sell small livestock to Samaritans — it is forbidden. Samaritans aren't suspected of bestiality, so the concern must be reselling to gentiles!
Abaye
This proves: one who is suspected of reselling is treated as if he will sell to a gentile.
Principle Even selling to a middleman can be prohibited if there is reasonable suspicion that the item will end up with someone who will use it for a forbidden purpose.
