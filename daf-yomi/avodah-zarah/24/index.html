Can Jews Purchase Sacrificial Animals from Gentiles?

Rabbi Eliezer
A red heifer may not be purchased from gentiles — derived from the verse 'unto you' (Numbers 19:2) and also due to concern about bestiality.
Rabbis
It may be purchased — Dama ben Netina sold one to the Sages! Jews guarded it from birth.
Rabbi Eliezer
That proves nothing — Jews safeguarded that heifer from birth. And the purchase went through Jewish merchants as intermediaries.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Animals under three years old are permitted — gentiles won't risk making them barren through bestiality.
Core Issue The Gemara tests Rabbi Eliezer's double requirement (Jewish seller + no bestiality concern) against multiple biblical episodes of gentile-owned animals being offered.

Rabbi Eliezer's Two Requirements for the Red Heifer

Can a parah adumah (red heifer) be purchased from a gentile?
↓
Requirement 1: Verse
'Unto you' (Numbers 19:2) — must come from a Jewish source
Requirement 2: Concern
Suspicion of bestiality (רביעה) — disqualifies the animal for the altar
↓
Both conditions must be met: Jewish seller AND no bestiality concern
Key Point The Gemara clarifies that Rabbi Eliezer holds BOTH reasons — Sheila's claim that it was only the verse derivation is corrected by the baraita.

The Missing 'Vav' — Disconnecting the Ephod Stones

Gemara
If 'unto you' disqualifies gentile contributions, how were ephod stones purchased from Dama ben Netina (a gentile)?
Gemara
In Exodus 25:7, 'onyx stones' (avnei shoham) lacks the conjunction 'and' (vav) — this disconnects later items from the 'that they take' requirement.
Objection
But the next phrase 'and stones to be set' restores the conjunction — reconnecting all items to the restriction!
Resolution
The Gemara leaves this difficulty and pivots to the red heifer story from the same Dama ben Netina incident as a stronger challenge.
Textual Analysis The presence or absence of the conjunctive 'vav' (ו) in Torah verses carries legal significance — it can include or exclude items from a given law.

The Bestiality Concern — Mother and Offspring

🐄 Rava's Principle: Offspring of an animal involved in bestiality is also forbidden — as if both mother and offspring were involved
↓
❓ Challenge: Even if the heifer was guarded from birth, what about bestiality with the pregnant mother?
↓
🔄 Correction: Jews guarded the animal from the time of conception, not just birth
↓
❓ Further Challenge: What if bestiality occurred with the mother before conception? Rabbi Eliezer prohibits offspring of such animals!
↓
⚖️ Rava/Rav Naḥman: The dispute only applies when bestiality occurred while the animal was consecrated — if non-sacred, offspring are permitted
↓
⚠️ Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana/Rav Naḥman: The dispute applies when animals were non-sacred — if consecrated, all agree offspring are forbidden
Dispute Whether the concern about the mother's status before conception matters depends on a dispute between Rava and Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana regarding Rav Naḥman's teaching.

The Philistine Cattle — Hora'at Sha'ah (Provisional Edict)

Problems with the Episode

  • Animals belonged to Philistines (gentiles)
  • The cattle were female — burnt-offerings require males!
  • Both issues suggest extraordinary circumstances

Rav Adda bar Ahava's Defense

  • Perhaps offered on a private altar (bamah)
  • Female burnt-offerings are valid on a private altar
  • Proof: Samuel sacrificed a 'milking lamb' (I Samuel 7:9) — female

Textual Proof

  • The word vaya'alehu is read masculine
  • But written in the feminine form vaya'alah
  • This kri/ketiv teaches that females are valid on private altars
Morigim Tangentially, the Gemara defines 'morigim' (threshing instruments) from Araunah's verse: Ulla says it is a 'turbil bed' — a serrated threshing board (kurkesa), as in Isaiah 41:15.
