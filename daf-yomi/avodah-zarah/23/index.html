Can Animals Purchased from Gentiles Be Used as Offerings?

The Contradiction
Mishna: Gentiles are suspected of bestiality — so how can their animals serve as offerings?
Ravina
The mishna is ab initio (לכתחילה) — don't buy for offerings. The baraita is after the fact (בדיעבד) — if already purchased, it's valid.
R' Pedat
The mishna follows Rabbi Eliezer who forbids; the baraita follows the Rabbis who permit purchasing from gentiles.
The Gemara
R' Eliezer's concern is bestiality, not labor — because the yetzer hara overcomes rational financial calculation.
Core Tension The Gemara resolves how gentiles can be suspected of bestiality yet their animals remain valid for offerings — through multiple frameworks: ab initio/after the fact, tannaitic dispute, and economic reasoning.

Ravina's Resolution: Ab Initio vs. After the Fact

Contradiction: Mishna says gentiles suspected of bestiality, yet baraita permits their animals for offerings
↓
לכתחילה (Ab Initio)
Mishna's ruling — don't purchase animals from gentiles for offerings in the first place
בדיעבד (After the Fact)
Baraita's ruling — if already purchased, the animal is permitted as an offering
Ravina Many halakhic disputes resolve through the ab initio/after the fact framework — stricter rules for ideal behavior, more lenient when the act is already done.

The Captive Woman: Testing Ab Initio vs. After the Fact

Mishna (A.Z.)
A woman may not seclude herself with gentiles — they are suspected of forbidden relations.
Mishna (Ketubot)
A woman imprisoned for monetary matters is permitted to her husband — no presumption of rape.
The Gemara
This proves the ab initio/after the fact distinction! Seclusion is forbidden ab initio, but after the fact she is permitted.
Rejection
No proof! In Ketubot, the financial incentive restrains the captor — he fears losing ransom money if he rapes her.
Proof
Indeed — when imprisoned for capital offenses, she is forbidden, since money is no longer a restraint.
Key Insight The Gemara shows that the captive woman case isn't about ab initio vs. after the fact — it's about the captor's financial self-interest restraining him.

Rabbi Eliezer vs. The Rabbis: Red Heifer from Gentiles

Rabbi Eliezer

  • Red heifer may NOT be purchased from gentiles
  • Concerned the gentile engaged in bestiality
  • The yetzer hara overcomes financial logic
  • Derives from: "Children of Israel shall take" (Num. 19:2)
  • Extends prohibition to other offerings as well

The Rabbis (Chakhamim)

  • Red heifer MAY be purchased from gentiles
  • A gentile won't forfeit profit for fleeting desire
  • Cite Isaiah 60:7 — "All flocks of Kedar... upon My altar"
  • Animals from gentiles valid for ALL offerings
  • Economic rationality prevails over suspicion
R' Pedat The contradiction between the mishna and baraita reflects a tannaitic dispute — the mishna follows R' Eliezer, the baraita follows the Rabbis.

The Red Heifer: Altar or Maintenance?

חַטָּאת הִיא — It is a sin-offering (Numbers 19:9)
Critical Classification If bestiality disqualifies the red heifer, it must be קדשי מזבח (consecrated for the altar) — since items consecrated for Temple maintenance (קדשי בדק הבית) are not disqualified by bestiality.

The Gemara rejects this proof: The Torah calls the red heifer a חַטָּאת (sin-offering), giving it altar-like disqualifications even if classified as Temple maintenance.

But a caesarean-born animal is invalid for sin-offerings, yet R' Shimon deems a caesarean-born red heifer fit — showing the חַטָּאת label doesn't apply fully.
