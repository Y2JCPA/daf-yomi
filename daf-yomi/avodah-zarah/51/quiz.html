5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does R' Yehuda consider slaughtering a locust for an idol to create liability?
A. A locust's neck is similar to an animal's neck, so its slaughter resembles Temple slaughter
B. Any act of killing for an idol creates liability regardless of Temple resemblance
C. Locusts were actually offered in the Temple as a minor sacrifice
D. The locust is considered a form of meal offering, not animal slaughter
Correct: A — The Gemara clarifies that even R' Yehuda requires resemblance to Temple rites — he simply holds that a locust's neck is anatomically similar enough to an animal's neck that slaughtering it qualifies.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rav Naḥman (quoting Rav), why is a thrown stick NOT rendered prohibited even though the thrower is liable?
A. Throwing a stick lacks the 'scattering' quality needed to resemble blood sprinkling (zerikah)
B. A thrown stick is too small to be considered a real offering
C. The stick must be broken to count as an offering
D. Only natural objects like stones can become prohibited through throwing
Correct: A — Rav Naḥman explains that for an act to prohibit the offering, it must resemble a Temple rite. Throwing a stick doesn't scatter like blood sprinkling, so while the person is liable (it's the idol's typical worship), the stick itself isn't prohibited.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does the Gemara explain that stones thrown at a Mercury idol become prohibited if they don't scatter?
A. They become part of the idol itself — enlarging it — not prohibited as offerings
B. Stones shatter on impact, which counts as scattering
C. Mercury worship is an exception to all normal rules of idol worship
D. The stones are prohibited only by rabbinic decree, not Torah law
Correct: A — Rav explained that Mercury stones don't become prohibited as offerings (since there's no scattering). Instead, each stone becomes part of the idol itself — it enlarges the Mercury pile. Rav Ashi adds that each stone also serves as an offering to itself and to the preceding stone.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why are vine branches with grapes found at Mercury's head prohibited, even though grapes aren't sacrificed in the Temple?
A. Ulla explains: the grapes were picked specifically for idol worship — picking is comparable to slaughter
B. Grapes can be squeezed into wine, which IS a Temple offering
C. Any food item found near an idol is automatically prohibited
D. The vine branches serve as decoration for the idol
Correct: A — Ulla resolves the difficulty by explaining that the mishna refers to grapes picked initially for the purpose of idolatrous worship. The act of picking (harvesting) them for this purpose is comparable to slaughtering an animal, which renders them prohibited.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does the Gemara derive that money and garments found near an idol are permitted?
A. By comparing 'with them' (Deut. 29:16) to 'on them' (Deut. 7:25) — only decorative items are prohibited
B. A specific verse explicitly permits money found near idols
C. Money and garments can never become objects of idol worship
D. The Rabbis enacted a leniency for items of monetary value
Correct: A — The Gemara compares two verses: items 'with them' (near idols) follow the same rule as items 'on them' (decorating idols). Just as only decorative coverings on idols are prohibited, non-decorative items found nearby — like money or garments — are permitted.
