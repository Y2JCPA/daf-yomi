When Does Forbidden Flavor Prohibit a Mixture?

R' Meir
Once a forbidden food enhances flavor, the mixture is forever forbidden — even if the flavor later detracts
R' Shimon
A forbidden food that currently detracts from flavor does not render the mixture forbidden
Ulla
Their dispute is only where the forbidden food first enhanced, then detracted. If it detracted from the outset — all agree it's permitted
R' Yochanan
Their dispute extends even to where the forbidden food detracts from the outset
Core Issue Does the initial enhancement of flavor create a permanent prohibition, or do we judge by the current state of the mixture?

Ulla vs. Rav Ḥagga: Vinegar in Split Beans

Rav Ḥagga
A baraita says forbidden vinegar in split beans is disputed — but vinegar detracts from beans! This refutes Ulla's limitation!
Ulla
Ḥagga doesn't know what the Sages say! The case is where vinegar fell into cold beans, which it enhances, and then they were heated — so it enhanced first, then detracted
Rav Ḥagga
But in cold beans, vinegar improves the flavor…
Ulla
Exactly — enhanced then detracted, which is the scope of the Meir-Shimon dispute as I defined it
Note Ulla dismisses the challenge by reinterpreting the case, but R' Yochanan's broader reading is ultimately supported

Do Forbidden Substances Combine? (Mitztarfin)

Rabbis (R' Meir)

  • Orla + Kilei HaKerem combine to create a prohibited quantity
  • Forbidden leaven joins with permitted leaven to prohibit the dough
  • Even partial enhancement by forbidden substance creates prohibition

R' Shimon

  • Even two forbidden substances don't combine — each measured independently
  • Forbidden + permitted certainly don't combine
  • When both leavens enhanced together, both also detracted — so permitted
Mishna Me'ila 18a R' Shimon's position on non-combination (ein mitztarfin) is consistent across multiple areas of halakha

Abaye Explains Why All Three Clauses Are Needed

📜 Clause 3 (wine/vinegar) — Teaches that the dispute applies where forbidden flavor detracts from the outset
↓
📜 Clause 2 (teruma first) — Teaches that when forbidden flavor enhanced then detracted, all agree it's forbidden
↓
❓ Why is Clause 1 (both together) needed? Clause 3 already implies Rabbis would forbid it!
↓
💡 Clause 1 teaches R' Shimon's reasoning: when both enhanced, both enhanced; when both detracted, both detracted — no isolating the forbidden
Key Insight Each clause teaches a unique principle — the baraita is precisely structured with no redundancy
