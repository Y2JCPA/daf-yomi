When Does Gentile Contact Prohibit Wine?

Rava
A gentile who stirs wine without intent does not make it yayin nesekh — the barrel wine may be sold to gentiles.
Abaye
Shmuel and R' Yoḥanan both prohibited such wine. And the baraita of the agardamim proves benefit is forbidden!
Rava
Those rulings were for towns not well-versed in Torah. And the baraita only prohibits drinking, not benefit.
Abaye
If so, why doesn't the baraita say 'it may be sold'? The confiscator case does say that — proving the inspector case forbids benefit!
Gemara
Rava's opinion is conclusively refuted (תיובתא).
KEY OUTCOME Wine stirred by a gentile is prohibited even for benefit — Abaye's position stands.

Rava vs. Rav Huna bar Rav Naḥman at Meḥoza

Rav Huna b"R Naḥman
What is the halakha when a gentile stirs wine without intent to offer a libation?
Rava
It is prohibited even for benefit (be-hana'ah).
Rav Huna b"R Naḥman
But didn't you permit selling the wine in the barrel to gentiles?
Rava
I permitted selling the barrel's wine minus the value of the prohibited wine poured in — not benefiting from the prohibited wine itself.
CONTEXT Rava ordered the gates closed to focus on this question — showing its legal importance.

The Agardamim Baraita — Two Cases

ScenarioGentile's IntentRuling
Market inspector drills hole in barrel & tastes wine, or tastes from cup and returns residueKnows it is wine; intentional contactProhibited (even for benefit)
Confiscator reaches into barrel thinking it contains oil — finds wineNo intention to touch wine at allMay be sold to gentiles
DECISIVE PROOF If the inspector case only prohibited drinking, it would say 'may be sold' like the confiscator case. Since it doesn't — benefit is also forbidden. This conclusively refutes Rava.

The Night Pouring Incident

Narrator
R' Yoḥanan ben Arza and R' Yosei ben Nehorai were drinking wine. A stranger served them — then was revealed to be a gentile.
One Sage
Prohibited even for benefit — the gentile would assume great Sages drink wine, and may have poured it as a libation.
Other Sage
Permitted even for drinking — the gentile would assume they wouldn't ask a gentile to serve wine, so it must be shikhra (liquor).
R' Yehoshua ben Levi
Both rulings are valid — each Sage properly followed his reasoning.
KEY DETAILS It was nighttime (couldn't see), with new wine (no distinctive smell), and the gentile poured without touching — hence the dispute focused on the gentile's mental state.

Wine Diluted by a Gentile — R' Yoḥanan's Ruling

R' Asi's Question

  • A gentile mixed (מסכו) wine with water
  • He did NOT directly touch the wine
  • Should this be permitted?
  • Uses biblical term: mashkha (Proverbs 9:2)

R' Yoḥanan's Answer

  • Corrects terminology: say mezago not mesakho
  • Torah language ≠ Rabbinic language
  • The wine is PROHIBITED by rabbinic decree
  • Principle: 'Go, go nazirite — don't approach the vineyard!'
PRINCIPLE Even indirect gentile contact with wine is prohibited as a protective fence — just as a nazirite must avoid the entire vineyard, not just the grapes.
