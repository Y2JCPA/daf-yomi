Permitting Gentile Oil — A Permissive Court?

R' Yehuda Nesia
Simlai, you missed it — last night we permitted gentile oil.
R' Simlai
In our days, you will permit gentile bread as well!
R' Yehuda Nesia
Then they'll call us a permissive court — like they called Yosei ben Yo'ezer 'Yosef the Permissive'!
R' Simlai
He permitted three things. You've only permitted one. Even with another, that's still only two.
R' Yehuda Nesia
I have already permitted another matter — the conditional get.
CONTEXT R' Yehuda Nesia (grandson of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi) led the court that relaxed the prohibition on gentile oil, a significant communal ruling.

The Conditional Get — "If I Don't Return"

Husband says: "This is your get if I don't return within 12 months"
↓
Husband dies within the 12 months
↓
Standard View
Not a valid get — takes effect only after death, which is too late
R' Yehuda Nesia's Court
Permitted her to marry — follows R' Yosei: date on document proves when it takes effect (retroactively)
NOTE R' Yehuda HaNasi (the grandfather) had also ruled this way, but the Sages did not concede to him during his lifetime.

Abaye's Framework: Language of Conditions in a Get

"When the sun emerges"

  • Future-tense language = takes effect at that moment
  • If husband dies during the night → posthumous get
  • GET IS INVALID — he was dead when it took effect

"On condition that the sun emerges"

  • Conditional language = retroactive from now
  • Per Rav Huna: 'on condition' = retroactive effect
  • If husband dies at night → condition fulfilled
  • GET IS VALID — took effect retroactively while alive
PRINCIPLE Rav Huna: Anyone who states a condition using 'on the condition that' (al menat) — the document takes effect retroactively from the time it was given.

Temple slaughterhouse liquidsThey are ritually pureRabbis had decreed impurity on liquids; he exempted Temple liquids
Touching a corpseOne who touches is impureActually lenient — restored Torah law re: secondary contact (see below)
IRONY The third testimony sounds stringent ('he is impure'), but it actually removed a rabbinic stringency — earning him the title 'Yosef the Permissive.'

Temple Slaughterhouse Liquids: Rav vs. Shmuel

Rav
These liquids are actually ritually pure. Liquid impurity is entirely rabbinic, and the Sages never decreed it for Temple liquids.
Shmuel
They are pure only in that they don't transmit impurity to other items. But they themselves can become impure.
Shmuel
Liquid impurity itself is Torah law. Only the capacity to transmit impurity is rabbinic — and that decree excluded Temple liquids.
CORE DISPUTE Is the ritual impurity of liquids entirely rabbinic (Rav) or is the impurity itself from Torah while transmission is rabbinic (Shmuel)?
