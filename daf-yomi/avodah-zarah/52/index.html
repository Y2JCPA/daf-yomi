When Do Idols Become Forbidden & What Happens to Desecrated Temple Vessels?

R' Yishmael
A gentile's idol is not forbidden until worshipped — derived from the juxtaposition (hekesh) of 'their gods' to 'the places' in Deut. 12:2
R' Akiva
The word "et" separates 'their gods' from 'places' — so no hekesh. A gentile's idol is forbidden immediately upon carving (Deut. 7:25)
R' Yishmael
A Jew's idol is forbidden immediately — logical reasoning: if even a gentile's idol (whose status can be revoked) needs worship, a Jew's (whose status cannot be revoked) must be forbidden from creation
R' Akiva
A Jew's idol is not forbidden until worshipped — 'set it up in a hidden place' (Deut. 27:15) means rites performed in secret must occur first
Core Dispute R' Yishmael and R' Akiva reach opposite conclusions for Jewish vs. gentile idols — each using different hermeneutical principles to derive the timing of prohibition.

When Does an Idol Become Forbidden?

R' Yishmael's View

  • Gentile's idol: NOT forbidden until worshipped (hekesh from Deut. 12:2)
  • Jew's idol: Forbidden IMMEDIATELY upon making (logical reasoning + Deut. 9:21, 27:15)
  • Uses juxtaposition (hekesh) as hermeneutic tool
  • Deut. 7:25 teaches that a gentile can revoke (posel) idol status

R' Akiva's View

  • Gentile's idol: Forbidden IMMEDIATELY upon carving (Deut. 7:25 — 'graven images')
  • Jew's idol: NOT forbidden until worshipped (Deut. 27:15 — 'set it up in a hidden place')
  • The word 'et' separates terms — rejects hekesh
  • Derives revocation from Shmuel's reading of Deut. 7:25
Mirror Image Each Tanna reaches the exact opposite conclusion about Jewish vs. gentile idols — a striking symmetry driven by their hermeneutical principles.

R' Akiva's Source: Deut. 7:25 & Shmuel's Reconciliation

Ulla
R' Akiva derives from 'graven images (פסילי) of their gods' (Deut. 7:25) — from the time a gentile engraves the stone, it becomes a god and is forbidden
Gemara
But the verse also says 'take it for yourself' — implying the gold/silver is permitted! How do we reconcile 'you shall not covet' with 'take it for yourself'?
Shmuel
Both are true: If the gentile carved it as a god → 'you shall not covet' applies (forbidden). If the gentile revoked (pesalo) its status → 'take it for yourself' applies (permitted)
Gemara
R' Yishmael uses the same verse differently — to derive that a gentile can revoke an idol's status (Rav Yosef's baraita)
Wordplay The root פ-ס-ל does double duty: פסילי (graven images) and פסלו (revoked its status) — carving creates prohibition, but revocation removes it.

Vessels of the Temple of Onias

R' Yosei b. Shaul
May vessels used in the Temple of Onias be used in the Jerusalem Temple? The Temple of Onias was not idolatrous — it served God but violated the prohibition of sacrificing outside Jerusalem.
R' Yehuda HaNasi
The vessels are prohibited. We had a verse proving this, but I have forgotten which one it is.
R' Yosei b. Shaul
But II Chron. 29:19 says King Ahaz's idolatrous vessels were 'prepared and sanctified' for Temple use — implying even idolatrous vessels can be reused!
R' Yehuda HaNasi
Blessed are you! You returned my lost verse! 'Prepared' = we buried them (genizah); 'sanctified' = we sanctified other vessels in their place. This proves they are forbidden!
Beautiful Exchange R' Yehuda HaNasi's forgotten verse is recovered through the very objection raised against him — the student's challenge restores the teacher's knowledge.
