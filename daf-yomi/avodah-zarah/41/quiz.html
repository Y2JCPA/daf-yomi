Avodah Zarah 41 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabba's corrected statement, where does the dispute between R' Meir and the Rabbis about statues apply?
A. Only in villages
B. Only in cities
C. Both cities and villages equally
D. Only at crossroads
Correct: B — Rabba's corrected statement places the dispute in cities, where statues might be ornamental. In villages, all agree statues are made for worship and are forbidden.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why was a sword in a statue's hand initially NOT considered a sign of idol worship?
A. Swords were too common
B. It was thought to depict a bandit
C. Swords symbolized peace
D. It was considered a Roman military decoration
Correct: B — The Sages initially thought a statue with a sword merely depicted a bandit (listes), not an idol. They later reasoned it symbolizes the idol's power to kill the entire world.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Shmuel distinguish between permitted fragments and forbidden hand/foot figures?
A. By the material they are made from
B. By whether they are found near a temple
C. By whether they stand on pedestals
D. By their size relative to a full statue
Correct: C — Shmuel explains that hand/foot figures standing on pedestals were designed as independent objects of worship, not mere broken fragments. Fragments without pedestals are permitted.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Reish Lakish respond to R' Yochanan's proof from the story of Dagon?
A. He says Dagon was not really an idol
B. He says the Philistines abandoned Dagon and worshipped the threshold instead
C. He says the verse is not meant literally
D. He says the Philistines eventually stopped all worship
Correct: B — Reish Lakish argues that the Philistines' avoidance of the threshold shows they transferred their worship FROM the broken Dagon TO the threshold — proving they did abandon the broken idol.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What principle does the Gemara invoke to argue against Reish Lakish's position that self-broken idols are permitted?
A. We follow the stringent opinion in Torah prohibitions
B. An uncertainty does not override a certainty
C. The burden of proof is on the one who permits
D. We follow the majority opinion
Correct: B — The Gemara argues: the idol was CERTAINLY worshipped, and its revocation is only UNCERTAIN. The principle 'ein safek motzi midei vadai' — uncertainty cannot override certainty — should keep the idol forbidden.
