Can Nature Become an Idol?

First Tanna
Mountains and hills worshipped as gods are permitted — you cannot forbid what God created. But gold or silver coatings on them are forbidden.
R' Yosei HaGelili
Deuteronomy 12:2 teaches: 'Their gods upon the mountains' — not the mountains themselves. The same applies to hills.
R' Akiva
The verse isn't limiting definitions — it's giving indicators: wherever you find a high mountain, elevated hill, or leafy tree, know that idol worship is there.
Core Principle Natural objects attached to the ground cannot become forbidden as idols, but human additions to them — coatings, plantings — can be.

Natural vs. Human-Made Objects of Worship

Mountains & Hills (Natural)

  • Created by God, not by humans
  • Cannot be forbidden even if worshipped
  • Permitted for planting, harvesting, etc.
  • Derived from Deut. 12:2 — 'their gods upon the mountains' but not the mountains themselves

Coatings & Ashera (Human)

  • Gold/silver coatings are human additions
  • Forbidden per Deut. 7:25 — 'you shall not covet the silver or gold on them'
  • Ashera trees involve human planting
  • Anything with יד אדם (human involvement) is forbidden
Key Distinction The Torah distinguishes between what God made (permitted) and what humans added or created for idol worship (forbidden).

What Differs Between the First Tanna and R' Yosei HaGelili?

Gemara
Both the First Tanna and R' Yosei HaGelili seem to say the same thing — mountains are permitted, what's on them is forbidden. What is the difference?
Reish Lakish
They disagree about ציפוי (coating): the First Tanna says coating is not like the mountain (forbidden). R' Yosei HaGelili says coating is like the mountain (permitted).
Rav Sheshet
Rejected! Everyone agrees coating is forbidden. They actually disagree about a tree planted and then worshipped: the First Tanna permits it, R' Yosei HaGelili forbids it.
Resolution Rav Sheshet's reading prevails: the real dispute concerns trees planted before worship began.

Scriptural Sources for Idol Destruction Laws

VerseWhat It Teaches
Deut. 7:25 — 'You shall not covet the silver or gold on them'Gold/silver coatings on idols are forbidden
Deut. 12:2 — 'Their gods upon the high mountains'Mountains themselves are not forbidden as gods
Deut. 12:2 — 'Under every leafy tree'R' Akiva: indicator of where idols are found
Deut. 12:3 — 'Burn their asherim with fire'Trees initially planted for worship must be burned
Deut. 7:5 — 'Hew down their asherim'R' Yosei b. R' Yehuda: trees planted then worshipped are forbidden
Hermeneutical Method R' Yosei b. R' Yehuda uses the technique of 'if it were not stated' (אילו לא נאמר) to derive that 'hew down' refers to trees planted before worship.

Tree Planted Then Worshipped — The Core Dispute

Rabbis (Majority)
A tree planted for ordinary purposes and only later worshipped remains permitted. 'Burn their asherim' refers only to trees initially planted for idol worship.
R' Yosei b. R' Yehuda
Even a tree planted and subsequently worshipped is forbidden. 'Hew down their asherim' (Deut. 7:5) — which tree has a forbidden trunk but permitted root? One planted then worshipped!
Rav Sheshet
This matches R' Yosei HaGelili in the Mishna — he too forbids a tree planted and subsequently worshipped, which is why he adds 'anything with human involvement is forbidden.'
Practical Distinction The trunk (above ground) is forbidden, but the roots remain permitted — because only the visible part was the object of worship.
