Conditional Sale: Must a Price Be Fixed?

Case
A man told another: If I sell this land, I will sell it to you — and they performed a kinyan (formal acquisition).
Rav Yosef
The first person acquired it. The kinyan is binding.
Abaye
But he did not fix a price! Without an agreed price, there is no valid acquisition.
Proof
From the Mishna: A Jew who sells wine to a gentile — if he fixed a price before measuring, the money is permitted. If he measured before fixing a price, the money is forbidden — showing acquisition depends on price.
Rav Huna
Resolved from a baraita: Even after unloading produce into the buyer's house, if no price was fixed, both parties can retract.
HALAKHA An acquisition is not complete until a price has been fixed — even if a formal kinyan was performed. Abaye's position is upheld.

Drew seller's donkey-drivers into his houseBefore measuring❌ No acquisition — both can retract

Unloaded produce into his houseBefore measuring✅ Acquired — neither can retract
Unloaded produce into his houseAfter measuring❌ Both can retract — no fixed price
PRINCIPLE Merely drawing the seller's laborers into one's house is never sufficient. Unloading the goods is the act of acquisition — but only if a price was fixed beforehand.

Two Conditional Land Sales

Case 1: No Price Stated

  • "If I sell, I'll sell to you"
  • Kinyan performed, no price set
  • Seller sold to someone else
  • Rav Yosef: First buyer acquired
  • Abaye: No — no price = no acquisition
  • Halakha follows Abaye

Case 2: Price of 100 Dinars

  • "If I sell, I'll sell to you for 100 dinars"
  • Kinyan performed with set price
  • Seller sold to another for 120 dinars
  • Rav Kahana: First buyer acquired
  • Rav Ya'akov: The extra 20 dinars compelled the sale
  • Halakha follows Rav Ya'akov
BOTTOM LINE In both cases, the first buyer does not acquire. Case 1: no price was fixed. Case 2: the higher offer (120 vs. 100) shows the seller was motivated by the price difference, not bad faith.

Assessment Rules: "As Assessed" vs. "As Stated By"

📋 Seller says: I'll sell as it is assessed by 3 people — majority (2 of 3) suffices
↓
🗣️ Seller says: As stated by 3 people — all 3 must agree
↓
👥 Seller says: As assessed by 4 people — all 4 must agree (stricter with 4)
↓
🔄 Buyer requests more proficient assessors — Rav Pappa: He may demand new assessors
↓
⚖️ Rav Huna b. Rav Yehoshua objects: How do you know the new ones are better? Perhaps the first are more proficient!
↓
✅ Halakha: Follows Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua — buyer cannot demand replacement assessors
NOTE The language matters: "as assessed" implies majority rule; "as stated by" requires unanimity. With 4 assessors, even "as assessed" requires all four.

The Nitzok Debate: Is a Stream a Connection?

Mishna: A Jew measures wine through a funnel into a gentile's jug, then into a Jew's jug
↓
Wine remnant in the funnel is forbidden. By what mechanism?
Nitzok = Connection
The stream going down into the gentile's vessel connected the funnel to it, rendering the remnant forbidden
R' Ḥiyya's Answer
The wine in the gentile's jug rose up and reached the funnel — direct contact, not stream
↓
Additional proofs from pouring between vessels and a baraita about barrels — all rejected
↓
Rav Sheshet: The baraita refers to a gentile pouring — forbidden due to ko'aḥ (his force), not nitzok
UNRESOLVED The Gemara attempts multiple proofs that nitzok (a stream) constitutes a halakhic connection (ḥibbur), but each is deflected. The fundamental question remains unresolved.
