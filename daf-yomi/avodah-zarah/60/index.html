When Does Gentile Contact Prohibit Wine?

Gentile interacts with Jewish wine
↓
Direct Contact
Prohibited for drinking and benefit
Indirect / Incidental
Depends on manner, intent, and circumstances
No Contact at All
Permitted — even for drinking
Key Principle The Gemara systematically examines every variation of gentile interaction with wine — direct force, indirect force, full vs. empty containers, and intent — to determine when wine becomes prohibited.

Wine Above & Below the Stopper

Rav Pappa (Version 1)
Wine touching the stopper area is prohibited, but the rest of the barrel is permitted.
Rav Pappa (Version 2)
Wine above the stopper is prohibited; wine below it is permitted.
Rav Yeimar
Rav Pappa's ruling reflects a Tannaitic dispute — the first Tanna says contact at any point renders it all impure.
R' Yehuda
Only the wine that was actually touched is rendered impure — the rest remains pure.
Connection Version 1 of Rav Pappa aligns with R' Yehuda — only the wine in contact with the touched area is affected, not the entire barrel.

Pouring Wine: Gentile vs. Jew

Wine is being transferred from barrel to beaker (kuva)
↓
Gentile Pours, Jew Holds Beaker
❌ Prohibited — wine flows by gentile's force
Jew Pours, Gentile Holds Beaker
✅ Permitted — wine flows by Jew's force
↓
But if the gentile tilts the beaker to the side...
↓
❌ Prohibited — the gentile's action now affects the wine
Rav Pappa The critical factor is whose force moves the wine. Even passive holding is fine — but actively tilting the beaker crosses the line.

Rav Ashi's Leniency Rav Ashi permits the wineskin even when not full, reasoning that shaking wine inside a sealed skin is not a recognized form of libation offering.

Winepress: Direct Force vs. Indirect Force

Rav Pappi — Lenient

  • Gentile operates beam-press without touching wine
  • Permits wine pressed by gentile's indirect force
  • Some say: even direct force through a mechanism is OK

Rav Ashi / Rav Shimi — Strict

  • Prohibits wine even when gentile uses beam-press
  • Direct force of gentile = definitely prohibited
  • Some say: dispute is only about direct force

Rav Yaakov (Nehar Pekod)

  • Ruled strictly in a real case
  • Prohibited wine pressed by force of his force (ko'aḥ ko'ḥo)
Key Concept Ko'aḥ ko'ḥo (כח כחו) — 'the force of his force' — is a major halakhic concept: does indirect causation carry the same status as direct action?

Gentile Found Near the Wine Vat (Mishna)

🏺 A gentile is found standing near a Jew's wine collection vat
↓
💰 Does the Jew owe the gentile a loan with a lien on the wine?
↓
✅ No lien → Wine is permitted — gentile has no reason to touch it
↓
❌ Yes, lien exists → Wine is prohibited — gentile feels entitled to the wine
↓
📜 Shmuel clarifies: a general loan is not enough — it must be specifically secured against that wine
↓
🔍 Rav Ashi proves this from the Mishna about Beit She'an: prohibition only when gentile won't let Jew remove the wine
Practical Rule A gentile's mere proximity to wine doesn't prohibit it. Only when the gentile has a specific proprietary claim — a lien on the wine itself — do we assume he would touch it.
