Daf 63: Payment, Timing & Prohibition

Core Question: When does a payment become forbidden — at the time of promise, transfer, or use?
↓
Etnan Zonah
Payment to a prostitute: does timing of sacrifice vs. intercourse affect the prohibition?
Sabbatical Produce
Employer's laborers buy forbidden produce: is the employer liable based on how he pays?
↓
Unifying Principle: The designation of specific items or coins as payment creates the prohibition — vague obligations do not
Key Insight Both sugyot revolve around the same mechanism: a prohibition attaches only when a specific item is designated, not when an abstract debt exists.

When Can a Prostitute Sacrifice Her Payment?

R' Eliezer
The baraita refers to a case where she sacrificed the payment first, before the intercourse took place.
Gemara
If he said 'Acquire from now' — it's obviously permitted, it's just a gift! Why would the baraita need to teach this?
Gemara
If he did not say 'Acquire from now' — she can't consecrate it! One can only consecrate what is in one's possession (Lev. 27:14).
Resolution
He said: 'Let it stay with you until intercourse; if you need it meanwhile — acquire it from now.' A conditional transfer.
Principle An item can only be consecrated (hekdesh) by someone who has legal possession of it — derived from "when a man shall sanctify his house."

Rav Hoshaya's Dilemma: Consecrated vs. Sacrificed

She consecrated the animal (verbal declaration) before intercourse, but has not yet sacrificed it
↓
Permitted?
"Declaration to the Most High = transfer to an ordinary person" (אמירתו לגבוה כמסירתו להדיוט)
→ Consecration should equal sacrifice
Forbidden?
The animal is still physically extant at the time of intercourse
→ It remains "payment" and is forbidden
↓
Attempt to resolve from R' Eliezer's statement: he said sacrificed, implying consecration alone is insufficient
↓
❓ Rejected — Perhaps R' Eliezer mentioned sacrifice because it was obvious, but was uncertain about consecration
תיקו — The dilemma stands unresolved
Teyku The Gemara cannot determine whether verbal consecration alone removes the prohibition of etnan — the question remains unresolved.

Payment After Intercourse: Two Contradictory Baraitot

Baraita 1: Permitted

  • He engaged in intercourse first
  • Gave payment afterward
  • Payment is PERMITTED for sacrifice

Baraita 2: Forbidden

  • He engaged in intercourse first
  • Gave payment afterward — even up to 3 years later
  • Payment is FORBIDDEN for sacrifice
Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak / Rav Chisda Forbidden = he said "in exchange for this lamb" (specific, designated item — acquired immediately).
Permitted = he said "in exchange for a lamb" (unspecified — whatever he later gives is not deemed payment).

Jewish prostitute, lamb in her courtyardCourtyard acquisition (kinyan chatzer)She acquires automatically — forbidden
But if already in her courtyard…It was acquired before intercourse!Not a case of 'payment after intercourse'
Resolution: AppoteikiLamb is collateral; if he doesn't pay dinars by deadline, she keeps itAcquisition is conditional and deferred — fits the baraita
Appoteiki A designated-collateral arrangement: "If I bring you dinars by such-and-such day, return the lamb; if not, keep it as payment." Acquisition occurs only upon default.

Sabbatical-Year Produce: Employer & Laborers

Baraita
If employer says 'Go eat with this dinar' — no concern. The laborers acquire food on their own volition.
Baraita
If he says 'Go eat and I will reimburse you' — he must be concerned about shevi'it, ma'aser, and yayin nesekh.
Rav Sheshet
This contradicts R' Yannai's school! They borrowed Sabbatical produce and repaid later — that's also paying after the fact!
Rav Chisda
The baraita refers to a storekeeper who regularly gives credit — the debt arises immediately when laborers receive food. Not comparable to R' Yannai's case.
Objection The Gemara challenges: even without regular credit, Rava's guarantor principle says one who commits to pay creates an immediate liability!
