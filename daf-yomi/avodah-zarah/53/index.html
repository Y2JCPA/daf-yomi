When Is an Idol's Status Revoked?

Gentile has an idol
Can its forbidden status be removed?
↓
Physical Defacement
Cut ear, nose, finger, or crushed its face → Status Revoked
Temporary Scorn
Spat, urinated, dragged, threw excrement → NOT Revoked (may return to worship)
Sale / Mortgage
Dispute: R' Yehuda HaNasi vs. Rabbis — depends on buyer's likely intent
Key Principle A gentile can revoke the status of his own idol, but a Jew can never revoke the status of a gentile's idol — and a Jew's own idol worship can never be revoked.

Partnership in an Idol: Can Status Be Revoked?

R' Hillel b. R' Volas
When a gentile has partnership in an idol, it teaches that only a Jew cannot revoke a gentile's idol — but a gentile can revoke his own.
R' Shimon b. Menasya
A Jew's object of idol worship can never be revoked — the word 'never' emphasizes: even if a gentile partner revokes his share, the Jew's share remains forbidden.
R' Hillel b. R' Volas
The emphasis of 'never' applies to the partnership case: the Jew worships based on his own intentions, so the gentile's revocation cannot help.
Principle A gentile's revocation of his share in an idol does not affect the Jew's share — the Jew's idolatrous intent is independent and irrevocable.

Selling an Idol: The Dispute

Gentile sells or mortgages his idol
Is its status revoked?
↓
R' Yehuda HaNasi
Selling revokes the idol's status — the sale shows he no longer values it as a god
The Rabbis
Selling does NOT revoke its status — the buyer may continue worshipping it
↓
Key clarification: R' Yehuda HaNasi concedes his colleagues are correct when sold to a gentile smith (for worship); the Rabbis concede R' Yehuda HaNasi is correct when sold to a Jewish smith (for destruction)
Resolution R' Yehuda HaNasi himself said: My opinion is correct when sold for destruction (Jewish smith); my colleagues' opinion is correct when sold for worship (gentile smith).

Why Each 'Non-Revocation' Case Is Necessary

Borrowed Against It

  • Gentile used idol as collateral
  • Did not sell or abandon it
  • Still intends to reclaim it
  • Status: NOT revoked

Rockslide Fell On It

  • Owner thinks: 'It's safe under the rocks'
  • 'Whenever I want it, I'll take it'
  • No urgency to clear rubble
  • Status: NOT revoked

Robbers Stole It

  • Owner thinks: 'If a gentile took it — he'll worship it'
  • 'If a Jew took it — he'll sell it to a gentile'
  • Either way, worship continues
  • Status: NOT revoked

Owners Went Overseas

  • Left the idol behind
  • Like the war of Joshua — intend to return
  • Abandonment was involuntary
  • Status: NOT revoked
Why All Cases? Each case is needed because you might have thought one shows revocation — the Gemara explains why in every scenario, the owner still intends to reclaim or expects continued worship.

The Golden Calf & Agency in Idol Worship

🧱 A Jew sets a brick upright intending to bow to it, but does not actually bow
↓
🙇 A gentile comes and bows to it
↓
🐂 R' Elazar: This is like the Golden Calf — Israel revealed they were amenable to idol worship
↓
📜 'These are your gods (plural), O Israel' (Exodus 32:4) — they desired many gods, not just the Calf
↓
🤝 The gentile acts as the Jew's agent (sheluḥo) — the brick becomes forbidden
↓
🌳 This explains why the asherim in Eretz Yisrael had to be destroyed, not merely revoked
Logical Chain Eretz Yisrael belonged to the Jews (inheritance from Abraham) → gentiles can't forbid what isn't theirs → unless Jews showed amenability to idolatry via the Golden Calf → gentiles acted as their agents → asherim = Jewish idol worship → status can never be revoked.
