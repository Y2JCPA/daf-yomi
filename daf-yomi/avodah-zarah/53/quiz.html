Avodah Zarah 53 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Mishna, which physical action does NOT revoke an idol's status?
A. Cutting off the tip of its nose
B. Crushing its face with a hammer
C. Dragging it on the ground
D. Cutting off its fingertip
Correct: C — Dragging the idol is considered temporary scorn — the gentile may resume worshipping it afterward. Physical defacement (cutting, crushing) permanently alters its form and revokes its status.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why can't a Jew's share in a co-owned idol be revoked even when the gentile partner revokes his share?
A. Because the Jew paid more for it
B. Because the Jew worships based on his own independent intentions
C. Because Jewish law requires a court proceeding
D. Because the idol must be completely destroyed first
Correct: B — R' Hillel b. R' Volas explains that the Jew worships based on his own intentions — the gentile's revocation of his share has no effect on the Jew's independent idolatrous intent, which can never be revoked.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
In the dispute about selling an idol, when does R' Yehuda HaNasi concede the Rabbis are correct?
A. When sold to a Jewish smith
B. When sold to a gentile smith
C. When sold at a discount
D. When the idol is made of wood
Correct: B — R' Yehuda HaNasi said his colleagues are correct when the idol is sold for the purpose of worship — i.e., to a gentile smith who will presumably continue worshipping it rather than melting it down.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does the Gemara derive from the Golden Calf that a gentile can render a Jew's brick forbidden by worshipping it?
A. The verse says 'these are your gods' in plural, showing Israel desired many forms of idolatry — so the gentile acts as the Jew's agent
B. The Golden Calf was made of gold, which is the same material as bricks
C. Moses explicitly prohibited all bricks after the Calf incident
D. The gentile becomes the legal owner of the brick by worshipping it
Correct: A — The plural 'gods' in Exodus 32:4 shows Israel was amenable to all idol worship, not just the Calf. When a Jew shows intent (setting up the brick) and a gentile acts on it, the gentile functions as the Jew's agent, making the brick forbidden as a Jew's idol — whose status can never be revoked.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why is an idol abandoned in wartime still prohibited?
A. Because it was damaged in the fighting
B. Because the owner fled under duress and did not voluntarily relinquish it
C. Because wartime idols are always more dangerous
D. Because the conquering army now owns it
Correct: B — Like the Canaanites in the war of Joshua who intended to return for their idols, wartime abandonment is involuntary — the owner did not willingly give up the idol, so there is no implicit revocation of its status.
