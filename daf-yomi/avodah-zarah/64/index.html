Maintaining Diverse Kinds (Kilayim)

Rabbi Akiva
Even one who merely maintains diverse kinds (מקיים כלאים) without actively planting them is flogged.
Rabbis
Only one who sows or covers diverse kinds with earth is flogged — merely maintaining them is permitted.
Rabbi Akiva
The verse says "lo…kilayim" — there should be no diverse kinds in your field at all, even passively.
Gemara
Even Rabbi Akiva would permit maintaining kilayim temporarily to reduce impropriety (משום מיעוט תיפלה) — e.g., being paid by a gentile to uproot them.
KEY PRINCIPLE Even a prohibition as strict as kilayim may be temporarily maintained when the goal is to eliminate the violation — "reducing impropriety" (מיעוט תיפלה).

Proceeds from a Gentile's Sale of an Idol

A gentile sells an object of idol worship and receives money
↓
Does the forbidden status of the idol transfer to the money?
↓
Rav Naḥman
The proceeds are permitted — an idol doesn't transfer forbidden status to money in a gentile's hands
Proof
Rabba bar Avuh told converts: Sell your idols first, then convert — the proceeds will be permitted
↓
Gemara rejects: Converts had already mentally revoked the idols' status before selling
💡 RESOLUTION The proof ultimately comes from a baraita: if a gentile sells an idol and then repays a Jewish creditor, the money is permitted — because the forbidden status doesn't transfer in a gentile's possession.

When Are Proceeds from Idol Sales Forbidden?

ScenarioStatusReason
Gentile sells idol, then brings payment to Jew✅ PermittedProceeds already in gentile's hands — no transfer of forbidden status
Gentile says "Wait while I sell the idol" to pay you❌ ForbiddenThe Jew desires the preservation of the idol (since it must be sold to repay him)
Convert & gentile brother divide inherited idols: convert takes money✅ PermittedDivision of inheritance — idol's value distributed among shards
Same division after property entered convert's possession❌ ForbiddenConvert now has ownership stake in forbidden objects
⚖️ PRINCIPLE The critical factor is whether the Jew has a vested interest in the idol's continued existence (ניחא ליה בקיומיה). If so, even indirect benefit is forbidden.

Who Is a Ger Toshav (גר תושב)?

Rabbi Meir

  • One who accepts before 3 ḥaverim
  • Not to worship idols
  • Minimal requirement — rejection of idolatry alone

Rabbis (Sages)

  • One who accepts all seven Noahide mitzvot
  • Including: no murder, theft, blasphemy, etc.
  • Broader ethical commitment required

Others

  • A convert who eats neveilot (unslaughtered carcasses)
  • Accepts all Torah mitzvot except this one
  • Essentially a full convert minus one prohibition
📜 NOTE Despite the dispute over definition, all agree that a ger toshav does not engage in idol worship — which raises the question of whether he can revoke an idol's status.

Can a Ger Toshav Revoke an Idol's Status?

Rav Naḥman
One who worships idols can revoke idol status, but one who does not worship them cannot. A ger toshav therefore cannot revoke.
Objection (Baraita)
A gentile can revoke his own or another's idol — whether he worships it or not. Doesn't this include a ger toshav?
Rav Naḥman
No — both clauses refer to regular gentiles: one who worships Peor can revoke another's Mercury idol. Both are still idolaters.
Objection
But the baraita says: "In all other matters, a ger toshav is like a gentile" — doesn't that include revoking idol status?
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
No — that phrase refers to eruv law: a ger toshav's domain is treated like a gentile's for purposes of joining courtyards on Shabbat.
UNRESOLVED The Gemara does not reach a definitive conclusion. Rav Naḥman's position — that a non-idolater cannot revoke — withstands the objections but is not definitively proven.
