How Must Idols Be Destroyed? & Bathing Before Aphrodite

R' Yosei
Grinding or breaking idols into pieces is sufficient — proven from Moses, Asa, Hezekiah, and David
The Rabbis
Each proof can be refuted — special circumstances explain every case
Rabban Gamliel
I may bathe before Aphrodite — the bathhouse preceded the statue, and people treat it with disrespect
R' Oshaya
Rabban Gamliel gave a deceptive answer to deflect Proclus
Rav Ḥama bar Yosef
No — his answers were truthful and legally sound when properly understood
Key Theme This daf weaves together two major threads: the proper method of idol destruction (Amud A) and when proximity to an idol is permitted (Amud B).

Grinding the Golden Calf: Was It Enough?

R' Yosei
Moses ground the Golden Calf to dust and cast it into a brook — this proves grinding suffices to destroy an idol! (Deut. 9:21)
The Rabbis
That's not proof! A different verse says Moses made Israel drink it (Ex. 32:20) — he ground it to test them like a sota woman, not merely to destroy the idol
R' Yosei
Then what about King Asa? He cut down Maacah's ashera and burned it at the Kidron River (I Kings 15:13) — no concern for fertilization!
The Rabbis
The Kidron River does not grow vegetation in that area — so no fertilization concern existed
Sota Parallel Just as a sota drinks water with dissolved scroll ink to determine guilt or innocence, Moses made Israel drink the dissolved calf to identify the guilty idol worshippers.

David scattered (vayyissa'em) Philistine idolsII Sam. 5:21A parallel verse says they were burned (I Chron. 14:12) — vayyissa'em means 'took away,' not 'scattered'
Pattern Each time R' Yosei brings a biblical proof that grinding/breaking suffices, the Rabbis find a special circumstance that explains it away.

The Brazen Serpent: Why Breaking Sufficed

Hashem tells Moses: "Make yourself a serpent" (Num. 21:8)
↓
"Make yourself" = from Moses' own property → serpent belongs to Moses
↓
Principle: A person cannot render forbidden an item that is not his
↓
Israel worshipped it, but could not give it idol status since it wasn't theirs
↓
Why Destroy It?
Hezekiah saw Israel straying after it — destroyed as a precaution, not an obligation
Why Just Breaking?
Since it lacked true idol status, breaking into pieces was sufficient
Key Principle Ownership matters: An idol fashioned from someone else's property cannot acquire forbidden status, because one cannot prohibit what doesn't belong to them.

Rabban Gamliel's Three Answers to Proclus

Answer 1: Priority

  • "I did not come into its domain"
  • "It came into my domain"
  • The bathhouse existed before the Aphrodite statue was erected

Answer 2: Adornment

  • People say: "Make Aphrodite as an adornment for the bathhouse"
  • Not: "Make a bathhouse for Aphrodite"
  • The statue is secondary to the bathhouse

Answer 3: Disrespect

  • You wouldn't enter before your god naked or after seminal emission
  • This statue stands on a sewage pipe
  • People urinate before it — it's not treated as a deity
Verse "Their gods" (Deut. 12:2) — only a statue treated as a deity is forbidden; one treated with disrespect is permitted.

Were Rabban Gamliel's Answers Deceptive?

R' Oshaya
Rabban Gamliel gave a deceptive response (teshuvat ramaut) to Proclus
Rav Ḥama bar Yosef
I say the response was not deceptive but truthful
Challenge (via Rava)
Urinating before an idol doesn't negate its status — Peor was worshipped through defecation and remained forbidden!
Rav Ḥama bar Yosef
Peor's standard worship involved defecation. Aphrodite's did not — so disrespect here genuinely negates its status
Abaye
The deception was in "it came into my domain" — even if the idol preceded the bathhouse, one may still use it without showing favor (Mishna 51b)!
Rav Ḥama bar Yosef
An esteemed visitor like Rabban Gamliel merely visiting is equivalent to showing favor — so the priority argument was necessary
Insight The Gemara explores whether Rabban Gamliel crafted answers a gentile would accept, or whether they reflect genuine halakhic reasoning.
