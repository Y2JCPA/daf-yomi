Wheat Worshipped, Then Ground to Flour

Mar Zutra / Rav Naḥman
The flour case is like an animal that became pregnant first and then was used for bestiality — the offspring (flour) is forbidden since it existed in earlier form (wheat) when worship occurred.
Gemara Challenge
The cases are not comparable! A fetus is already an animal — the womb is just a 'closed door.' But wheat becoming flour is a totally new form (shinui).
Conclusion
No proof can be derived from the mishna about offspring for the case of transformed worshipped items.
Principle Transformation of form (shinui) may break the chain of prohibition — a fetus-to-animal is not the same as wheat-to-flour.

Reish Lakish's Lulav Dilemma

Can its lulav be used for the mitzvah?
↓
Tree planted for idolatry initially?
→ Forbidden even for ordinary use. No dilemma.
↓
Tree planted normally, then worshipped?
R' Yosei bar Yehuda
Forbidden even for ordinary use — no dilemma needed
Rabbanan
Permitted for ordinary use, but is it repulsive (ma'us) to use for a mitzvah?
Rav Dimi's Version Reish Lakish asked about an ashera whose idol status was revoked: Is there permanent disqualification for mitzvot, or does revocation restore fitness?

Is Disqualification for Mitzvot Permanent?

Gemara
Proof from covering blood: If wind covers the blood and it's re-exposed, one must cover it again — showing disqualification is not permanent.
Rav Pappa
אין פסול למצוות — There is no permanent disqualification for mitzvot. Once the cause of exemption is removed, the obligation returns.
Gemara
Perhaps Rav Pappa's rule only applies le-chumra (to be stringent, like requiring re-covering), but not le-kula (to permit the lulav)?
Conclusion
Teiku — The dilemma remains unresolved.
Unresolved The Gemara cannot determine if 'no disqualification for mitzvot' applies symmetrically to both leniencies and stringencies.

Three Types of Idolatrous Houses

Built for Idolatry

  • House constructed initially for idol worship
  • Entirely forbidden — cannot derive benefit
  • Rav adds: even bowing to a house forbids it
  • Based on: detached-then-attached = still detached

Plastered/Cemented for Idolatry

  • Existing house modified for idol worship
  • Remove the added layer only
  • Rest of the house is permitted
  • Added material absorbs the prohibition

Idol Placed Temporarily

  • Idol brought in and then removed
  • House is permitted once idol is gone
  • Lightest level of contact with idolatry
  • No permanent effect on the structure
Rav's Addition Bowing to a house also renders it forbidden — the mishna's categories are not exhaustive but grouped by shared revocation rules.

R' Ami's Qualification Plastering must be done within the stone (carving/engraving) to render it forbidden — mere external ornamentation does not forbid it. This differs from a house, where plaster between bricks counts as 'within.'
