Daf 46: Worshipped Objects — What Becomes Forbidden?

Object worshipped by a gentile
Attached to ground (mountain, growing tree)
Permitted for benefit — not a human product
Detached (carved idol, cut tree)
Forbidden for benefit
↓
But is a worshipped attached item unfit for Temple use?
Rava
Kal v'chomer from prostitute's payment → Forbidden for the Most High
Rav Huna b. Rav Yehoshua
Reverse kal v'chomer → Permitted for the Most High
Core Tension The daf explores the boundary between natural objects (mountains, animals) that cannot be forbidden by worship and the stricter standards of Temple fitness.

Mitzva to Give Idols Negative Nicknames

What Idolaters Call It

  • Beit Galya — House of Elevation
  • Ein Kol — The All-Seeing Eye

What Jews Should Call It

  • Beit Karya — House of Digging (lowliness)
  • Ein Kotz — Eye of a Thorn (pain)
Source R' Akiva derives from "You shall detest it and abhor it" (Devarim 7:26) that the nickname must be specifically negative, not merely neutral.

Dislodged Boulders: Permitted or Forbidden?

Sons of R' Ḥiyya / R' Yoḥanan
One side says boulders naturally dislodged from a mountain and then worshipped are permitted — their status is like the mountain itself.
Other Side
They are forbidden — once detached, they are no longer "attached to ground."
Gemara
A mountain is attached → permitted. An animal is detached → also permitted. Common element: not products of human involvement.
Objection
Mountains and animals were not altered from natural form. A dislodged boulder has been moved!
Resolution
Derive instead from a blemished animal (altered, detached) + mountain → common element still = not man-made → permitted.
Method The Gemara uses the tzad ha-shaveh (common element) method, trying multiple pairs until it finds two sources whose combined leniencies cover all objections.

↓

Rava vs. Rav Huna b. Rav Yehoshua: Competing Kal V'Chomer

Rava
Kal v'chomer to stringency: Payment to a prostitute is permitted to an ordinary person when detached, yet forbidden for the Temple even when attached. Idol worship is forbidden even to an ordinary person when detached — surely it's forbidden for the Temple when attached!
Rav Huna b. Rav Yehoshua
Kal v'chomer to leniency: A worshipped object, though forbidden when detached for anyone, is permitted when attached even for the Temple — "their gods upon the mountains, but not the mountains themselves." So prostitute's payment attached should also be permitted!
Rava
When two opposing kal v'chomer arguments exist, we apply the stringent one.
Rav Pappa
Is that always true? R' Akiva infers a leniency against R' Eliezer regarding sprinkling purification water on Shabbat for the Paschal offering!
Rava's Principle Wherever there is an option for a stringent or lenient kal v'chomer, we infer the stringency. Rav Pappa challenges this from the Pesachim dispute.
