When Does Bishul Akum Apply?

Food cooked entirely by a gentile
↓
Sura Version
Prohibited if cannot be eaten raw
Pumbedita Version
Prohibited only if fit for a king's table
↓
Practical difference: small fish, mushrooms, porridge — not raw-edible, but not royal fare
↓
Sura
❌ Prohibited
Pumbedita
✅ Permitted
KEY PRINCIPLE The prohibition of bishul akum is rabbinic, not biblical — the verse is cited only as an asmakhta (support).

Two Versions of Rav's Rule

Sura Academy

  • Any food eaten raw is exempt from bishul akum
  • Focus: Is the food edible without cooking?
  • Small fish, mushrooms, porridge → Prohibited
  • Stricter standard

Pumbedita Academy

  • Any food not served at a king's table is exempt
  • Focus: Is the food dignified enough to matter?
  • Small fish, mushrooms, porridge → Permitted
  • More lenient standard
BOTH CITE RAV Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak transmits both versions in Rav's name — the academies simply preserved different traditions.

The Kasa Deharsena Problem

Rav Asi
Small salted fish are not subject to bishul akum — they can be eaten raw.
Rav Yosef
If a gentile roasted these fish, a Jew may even use them for eruv tavshilin.
Rav Yosef
But if a gentile made kasa deharsena (fish fried in oil and flour) — it is prohibited.
Gemara
Isn't that obvious? Why would it be permitted?
Rav Yosef
You might have thought the fish is the essential component. I teach that the flour is essential — and flour cannot be eaten raw.
PRINCIPLE When multiple ingredients are combined, the essential component determines whether bishul akum applies — here the flour, not the fish.

Intent Matters: Gentile's Unintentional Cooking

🔥 Gentile performs an action that incidentally cooks food
↓
Singeing Animal Head
R' Yoḥanan: Permitted — even the fully-cooked ear tip. Intent was to remove hair, not to cook.
Burning a Meadow
Rav Beruna / Rav: Locusts in the meadow are prohibited
↓
Why are the locusts prohibited if the gentile only intended to clear the field?
↓
✅ Answer: The case involves a mixture of kosher and non-kosher species — not bishul akum at all
RECONCILIATION Both cases actually agree: when a gentile has no intent to cook, bishul akum does not apply. Rav Beruna's case is about species identification, not gentile cooking.

Joint Jew-Gentile Cooking: What's Permitted?

ScenarioHalakhaSource
Jew places meat on coals, gentile turns it over✅ PermittedShmuel
Gentile places meat on fire, Jew turns it over✅ PermittedR' Yoḥanan (kal va-ḥomer)

Gentile lights oven, Jew bakes bread✅ PermittedRavina
Jew lights oven, gentile bakes bread✅ PermittedRavina
Gentile lights & bakes, Jew stokes coals✅ PermittedRavina
RULING R' Yoḥanan: Food is prohibited only when cooking from beginning to end is performed by a gentile. Any Jewish participation in the process is sufficient.

Salted Fish & Roasted Eggs: The Great Dispute

Ḥizkiyya
Fish salted by a gentile — permitted.
Bar Kappara
Egg roasted by a gentile — permitted.
R' Yoḥanan
Both are prohibited as bishul akum.
R' Ḥiyya of Parva
Two against one — the halakha follows Ḥizkiyya and bar Kappara!
Rav Zevid
Don't listen to him! Abaye said the halakha follows R' Yoḥanan.
Gemara
The Exilarch's attendants gave Rav Zevid spiced vinegar to drink for his stringency — and he died.
HALAKHA Despite the dramatic consequences, Abaye rules the halakha follows R' Yoḥanan — salted fish and roasted eggs prepared by gentiles are prohibited.
