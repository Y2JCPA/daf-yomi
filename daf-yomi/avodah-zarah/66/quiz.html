QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Abaye, why is new libation wine that falls onto grapes forbidden by any amount?
A. Because we follow the flavor — wine and grapes taste the same, making it min b'mino
B. Because we follow the name — both are called 'grape products'
C. Because new wine has not fermented and is considered identical to grape juice
D. Because all libation wine is forbidden by any amount regardless of the mixture
Correct: A — Abaye holds we follow the flavor (ta'am) when classifying mixtures. Since new wine and grapes share the same flavor, it is min b'mino (same type with same type), and any amount of the forbidden substance renders the mixture forbidden.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In the case of wine vinegar mixed with malt vinegar, how do Abaye and Rava each rule?
A. Both agree it's forbidden by any amount since both are vinegar
B. Abaye: noten ta'am (different flavors); Rava: any amount (same name 'vinegar')
C. Abaye: any amount (same flavor); Rava: noten ta'am (different names)
D. Both agree it's forbidden only if one imparts flavor to the other
Correct: B — Abaye follows flavor — wine vinegar and malt vinegar taste different, so it's min b'she'eino mino (forbidden only if noten ta'am). Rava follows name — both are called 'vinegar,' so it's min b'mino (forbidden by any amount). Their positions reverse compared to the wine-on-grapes case.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Rava's proof that smell (rei'aḥ) is halakhically insignificant?
A. A baraita about spices with different names that do not combine
B. The mishna about bread baked in an oven fueled by teruma cumin stalks — the bread is permitted
C. The principle that min b'she'eino mino requires noten ta'am
D. R' Yehuda's ruling that hot bread over teruma wine is permitted
Correct: B — Rava cites the mishna (Terumot 10:4) that bread baked in an oven lit with teruma cumin stalks is permitted, since it absorbed only the smell and not the actual flavor — proving that smell alone does not render food forbidden.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Abaye counter Rava's proof from the cumin-stalk oven?
A. He says the cumin stalks were not actually teruma
B. He says that case is different because the forbidden substance was burned — smell from a non-extant source is not forbidden
C. He says the bread actually is forbidden and the mishna is not authoritative
D. He says cumin smell is too weak to be considered substantial
Correct: B — Abaye distinguishes: in the cumin case, the forbidden substance was consumed by fire before its smell entered the bread. Smell from a non-extant (burned) source is not forbidden. But smell from an extant source — like wine still in a barrel — is forbidden.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In the case of forbidden wine poured into vinegar, what is the key factual disagreement between Abaye and Rava?
A. Whether the wine is actually forbidden or merely suspect
B. Whether a liquid that smells like vinegar but tastes like wine is classified as vinegar (Abaye) or wine (Rava)
C. Whether the vinegar barrel was properly sealed
D. Whether the wine had already turned to vinegar before being poured
Correct: B — Abaye holds that a liquid whose smell is vinegar and taste is wine is classified as vinegar — so the wine became min b'mino upon entering the barrel's airspace. Rava holds such a liquid is still classified as wine — making it min b'she'eino mino, forbidden only if it imparts flavor.
