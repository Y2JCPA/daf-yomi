Flavor vs. Name & The Significance of Smell

Abaye
When classifying substances in mixtures, we follow the flavor (ta'am). New wine and grapes share the same flavor → min b'mino → forbidden by any amount.
Rava
We follow the name (shem). Wine has one name, grapes have another → min b'she'eino mino → forbidden only if wine imparts flavor.
Abaye
Regarding smell (rei'aḥ): it is a substantial matter (milta hi). Smelling gentile wine through a bunghole is prohibited.
Rava
Smell is nothing (rei'aḥ lav milta hi). Proof: bread baked in an oven lit with teruma cumin stalks is permitted.
Core Dispute Two major Abaye-Rava disputes on this daf: (1) Do we classify mixture components by flavor or name? (2) Is smell halakhically significant?

The Core Dispute: Flavor vs. Name

Abaye — Follow the Flavor (Ta'am)

  • New wine + grapes = same flavor → min b'mino
  • Min b'mino: forbidden by any amount (kol shehu)
  • Wine vinegar + malt vinegar = different flavors → min b'she'eino mino
  • Min b'she'eino mino: forbidden only if noten ta'am (imparts flavor)

Rava — Follow the Name (Shem)

  • New wine + grapes = different names → min b'she'eino mino
  • Forbidden only if wine imparts flavor to grapes
  • Wine vinegar + malt vinegar = both called 'vinegar' → min b'mino
  • Min b'mino: forbidden by any amount
Note Abaye and Rava reach opposite conclusions on each case — their positions are mirror images depending on whether flavor or name controls the classification.

Abaye's Proof: Combining Spices

Abaye
A baraita teaches: forbidden spices with different names but of the same species combine to render a dish forbidden.
Abaye
Ḥizkiyya explains: they combine because they are all sweet spices — same flavor. This proves we follow flavor, not name!
Rava
That baraita follows Rabbi Meir, who holds that all forbidden foods combine regardless of name or flavor.
Rava
R' Meir derives this from 'Lo tokhlu kol to'eivah' (Devarim 14:3) — all prohibitions are treated as a single category.
R' Meir's Principle All Torah-forbidden foods that fall into a mixture combine to render it forbidden, derived from the general prohibition in Deuteronomy 14:3.

Is Smell (Rei'aḥ) Substantial?

Abaye — Rei'aḥ Milta Hi

  • Smell is a substantial matter
  • A Jew smelling gentile wine through a bunghole (bat tiha) is prohibited
  • The oven-cumin proof is different: that smell came from a burned substance
  • Smell from an extant source is forbidden

Rava — Rei'aḥ Lav Milta Hi

  • Smell is nothing — insignificant
  • Smelling gentile wine through a bunghole is permitted
  • Proof: bread baked with teruma cumin stalks as fuel is permitted
  • This shows smell alone does not render food forbidden
Bat Tiha The bunghole (בת תיהא) is a hole in a barrel used for smelling the wine. All agree a gentile smelling a Jew's wine through it does not forbid the wine.
