QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Reish Lakish, why is mixing oil outside the Temple courtyard valid?
A. The meal offering does not require a service vessel at this stage
B. From the removal of the handful onward, pouring and mixing are solely a mitzva of the priesthood, not location-dependent
C. Oil may be mixed anywhere as long as the handful was removed inside
D. The verse explicitly permits mixing outside the courtyard after the handful
Correct: B — Reish Lakish derives from Lev 2:6 that after the handful is removed, subsequent acts are solely a priestly mitzva — once the priestly requirement is met, location no longer disqualifies.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does Rabbi Yoḥanan permit regarding a meal offering that became lacking BEFORE the removal of the handful?
A. The priest may remove the handful from whatever flour remains
B. The offering must be replaced entirely with a new dedication
C. The owner may bring flour from home to refill the missing amount
D. The offering is automatically disqualified and no action may be taken
Correct: C — Rabbi Yoḥanan permits supplementing the flour before the handful is removed, since the handful has not yet established the offering — the owner retains the ability to complete it.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What does Rav Malkiyu conclude from his analysis of the two verses in Leviticus 2?
A. Only one verse is needed; the other is a redundant amplification
B. Both verses together prove that a lacking meal offering is disqualified at all stages, refuting Rabbi Yoḥanan in both disputes
C. One verse applies to voluntary offerings, the other to obligatory ones
D. The two verses prove that Reish Lakish is wrong about oil mixing outside the courtyard
Correct: B — Rav Malkiyu argues that one verse addresses lacking before the handful (cannot refill) and the other addresses lacking after the handful (cannot burn the handful) — both positions of Reish Lakish are Scripturally supported, refuting Rabbi Yoḥanan.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does Rav Pappa note that Reish Lakish's silence was problematic when Rabbi Yoḥanan cited the shewbread baraita?
A. Reish Lakish should have pointed out that the baraita is a minority opinion
B. Reish Lakish could have distinguished communal offerings — where ritual impurity was permitted — from individual offerings
C. Reish Lakish should have cited a counter-baraita about the shewbread
D. The shewbread baraita actually supports Reish Lakish's position
Correct: B — Rav Pappa notes that shewbread is a communal offering, and ritual impurity was overridden for communal offerings — making it an invalid comparison to individual meal offerings. Reish Lakish's failure to raise this distinction appears to be a concession.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Rabbi Zeira derive that using the left hand for removing the handful disqualifies the meal offering?
A. A direct verse in Leviticus explicitly prohibits left-hand service in the Temple
B. The verse about the leper specifying "left hand" implies that normal priestly service requires the right hand
C. A ruling from Sinai transmitted through oral tradition
D. The verse about Aaron's inauguration describes right-hand service as mandatory
Correct: B — Rabbi Zeira uses the leper's purification rite, where 'left hand' is explicitly stated as an exception. Since 'left hand' appears four times in that passage, the principle of restriction-after-restriction confirms that ordinarily the right hand is required for priestly service.
