⚖️ Three Disputes: Rabbi Yoḥanan vs. Reish Lakish

Rabbi Yoḥanan
Mixing oil outside the courtyard disqualifies — meal offering performance requires a service vessel and must occur inside the Temple courtyard.
Reish Lakish
Mixing oil outside is valid — from the removal of the handful onward, pouring and mixing are solely a mitzva of the priesthood, not location-dependent.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
If lacking before handful removal: owner may refill from home — the handful has not yet established the offering, so one may always supplement.
Reish Lakish
If lacking before handful removal: disqualified, may not refill — the verse "of its fine flour" indicates a lacking meal offering is invalid.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
If the remainder is lacking after handful removal: burn the handful anyway — Rabbi Yehoshua confirms any portion of remainder suffices.
Reish Lakish
If the remainder is lacking after handful removal: handful may not be burned — the priests' portion must be whole at the time of burning.
Core Question Does meal offering service require both priestly performance AND Temple courtyard location — or only priestly performance once the handful is removed?

📉 Meal Offering Lacking Before Handful Removal

Rabbi Yoḥanan
Refilling is permitted. The handful establishes the offering — before removal, the owner may always supplement from home. Proof: the leper's log of oil may be refilled before the priest pours.
Reish Lakish
Refilling is forbidden. The verse states "of its fine flour" — a lacking meal offering is disqualified. The leper's case is different: the pouring itself establishes it.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
The handful does establish the offering to the extent it can be disqualified — but before that moment, the owner retains the ability to complete it.
Reish Lakish
The leper's log: lacking before pouring → bring more. Lacking after pouring → disqualified. The same logic applies here from the moment of dedication.
מקור — Nega'im 14:10 If the leper's log lacked full measure before the priest poured from it, the priest brings more oil and pours. If it became lacking after the priest poured, it is disqualified.

📜 Rav Malkiyu: Two Verses, Two Rulings

ויקרא ב:ב — Leviticus 2:2

  • "And he shall remove… of its fine flour"
  • The word "of" implies a lacking offering is disqualified
  • Applies: before removal of the handful
  • Teaching: one may not remove the handful from a lacking meal offering
  • → Refilling is impossible; disqualified from the start

ויקרא ב:ג — Leviticus 2:3

  • "That which is left of the meal offering shall be Aaron's and his sons'"
  • Priests receive the remainder only if it is intact and complete
  • Applies: after removal of the handful
  • Teaching: if the remainder became lacking before burning, the handful cannot be burned
  • → Both Reish Lakish rulings are supported by Scripture

🍞 The Shewbread Objection and Reish Lakish's Silence

לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים — שֻׁלְחַן הַפָּנִים
🍞 Baraita — Shewbread Breaks Before Detachment A baraita teaches: if the shewbread broke before the priest detached it from the Table, the bread is unfit for consumption — yet the frankincense bowls are burned normally.

Rabbi Yoḥanan's proof: Even Rabbi Eliezer only permits burning the frankincense because the bread merely broke — it still exists in some form. If the bread had been completely burned or destroyed, even Rabbi Eliezer would forbid burning the frankincense. This refutes Reish Lakish.

Reish Lakish's silence: Rav Pappa notes he should have responded — communal offerings differ from individual offerings because ritual impurity was permitted in communal sacrifices. But Reish Lakish did not offer this distinction, implying he conceded the point.

✋ Derivation: Left Hand Disqualifies the Handful

Mishna ruling: If the priest removed the handful with his left hand, the meal offering is unfit — but from where is this derived?
↓
Rabbi Zeira's source: The verse about the metzora (leper) states the priest pours oil "into his left hand" (Lev 14:26) — the explicit mention of the left hand is an exception, implying the right hand is normally required.
↓
Challenge: That verse is needed for itself — to teach that specifically for the leper's log, oil is poured on the left! The Gemara answers: a principle of amplification applies; both the standard law and the exception are derived.
↓
Counter-challenge: Perhaps the opposite is true — since "left" is specified for the leper, the left hand is the normal requirement generally as well!
↓
Resolution: The word "left hand" appears four times in the leper passage. Multiple restrictions indicate an exception, not the rule. Normal priestly service requires the right hand; the left is reserved solely for the leper's oil.
