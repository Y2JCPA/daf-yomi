Arakhin 12 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Huna, why can a lacking remainder NOT become piggul even according to Rabbi Akiva?
A. The handful was not fully burned on the altar
B. The remainder's deficiency is intrinsic to it, not caused by an external factor
C. Rabbi Akiva holds piggul only applies to blood offerings
D. A lacking remainder is entirely exempt from all ritual law
Correct: B — Rav Huna draws a key distinction: when meat leaves the Temple, its disqualification is external. But a lacking remainder is deficient from within itself. Rabbi Akiva's leniency (allowing piggul despite external disqualification) does not extend to intrinsic deficiency.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Abaye, why does Rabbi Ḥiyya's mishna omit 'or burn an olive-bulk of its handful outside the Temple'?
A. Because an olive-bulk measure never applies to burning the handful
B. Because the mishna discusses a case where the remainder was already lacking
C. Because it follows Rabbi Elazar, who requires the full handful — not just an olive-bulk — to be burned
D. Because burning outside is never considered improper intent for piggul
Correct: C — Abaye argues Rabbi Ḥiyya's mishna follows Rabbi Elazar, who holds that only burning the complete handful creates liability. Therefore one cannot speak of 'an olive-bulk of the handful' — it is all or nothing. This refutes Rava's proof that the mishna was dealing with a lacking remainder.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
The mishna rules: intent to eat half an olive-bulk + intent to burn half an olive-bulk = the offering is fit. Why?
A. The olive-bulk measure applies only to eating, never to burning
B. Eating by a person and burning on the altar are distinct categories that cannot join
C. Both acts must occur outside the Temple for them to combine
D. The handful and remainder are measured independently from each other
Correct: B — The Rabbis hold that eating (by a person) and burning (on the altar) are fundamentally different categories of consumption. Half an olive-bulk of each cannot join to form a single olive-bulk of improper intent. Only intents within the same category can combine.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yehuda, when does having both time-related AND area-related improper intent render an offering piggul?
A. Always, regardless of the order of the intents
B. Only when both intents occur simultaneously during the same rite
C. Only when the time intent (next day) preceded the area intent (outside)
D. Only when the area intent preceded the time intent
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehuda rules that order determines the outcome. If the intent regarding time (eating next day) came first, the offering is piggul with karet liability. If the area intent came first, the offering is not piggul. This is because piggul requires the specific disqualification of time to be primary.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rava ultimately bases his ruling that a lacking remainder CAN become piggul on which source?
A. Rabbi Ḥiyya's version of the mishna omitting 'or an olive-bulk'
B. A baraita discussing loaves of shewbread that left the Temple
C. The explicit ruling of Rabbi Akiva in the Mishnah
D. The principle that external factors cannot disqualify sacred items
Correct: B — After Abaye refutes his proof from Rabbi Ḥiyya's mishna, Rava turns to a baraita about shewbread. The baraita implies that sprinkling the blood is effective in removing items from misuse of consecrated property even for those that left — and Rava uses this to establish an analogy for the lacking remainder case.
