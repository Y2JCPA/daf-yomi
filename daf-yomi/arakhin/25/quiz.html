QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Abaye, why does the tzitz NOT effect acceptance for offerings that leave the Temple courtyard?
A. Leaving the courtyard makes an offering completely invalid with no possible remedy
B. The verse 'accepted before the Lord' requires that the general prohibition be permitted inside the Temple — leaving the courtyard is never permitted before the Lord
C. Rabbi Zeira conceded this point before Abaye even answered
D. The verse 'Aaron shall bear the sin' specifically excludes spatial violations
Correct: B — Abaye derives from 'accepted before the Lord' that the tzitz only covers violations whose general prohibition is sometimes permitted in the Temple context. Leaving the courtyard is never permitted before the Lord — so the tzitz cannot cover it.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the tzitz NOT atone for blemished animals that were sacrificed?
A. Blemished animals are not considered sacred items under any circumstances
B. The High Priest does not wear the tzitz during such offerings
C. The Torah states explicitly 'it shall not be accepted' regarding blemished animals — ruling out any possibility of acceptance
D. Blemished animals can only be redeemed, not sacrificed, so the tzitz is irrelevant
Correct: C — Rav Ashi cites two verses about blemished animals: 'It shall not be accepted' and 'it shall not be acceptable for you.' The Torah's explicit rejection of acceptance means the tzitz cannot override this — there is simply no mechanism for acceptance to take hold.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Ravina resolve the contradiction between the two baraitot about impure blood?
A. One baraita discusses individual offerings and the other communal offerings
B. The determining factor is how the impurity was contracted: unwitting contraction = accepted; intentional contraction = not accepted
C. The determining factor is how the sprinkling was done: unwitting sprinkling = accepted; intentional sprinkling = not accepted
D. One baraita follows Rabbi Eliezer and the other follows Rabbi Yosei — no real contradiction
Correct: B — Ravina resolves by focusing on how the impurity was contracted. If the impurity came about unwittingly, the tzitz effects acceptance regardless of how the sprinkling was performed. If the impurity was contracted intentionally, the tzitz does not help.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
The Mishna states that if the handful became ritually impure and was sacrificed, the tzitz effects acceptance. What is the practical result?
A. The entire offering is disqualified and burned outside the Temple
B. The offering is accepted and the remainder is eaten by the priests
C. The offering must be repeated with a new pure handful
D. The High Priest must bring a personal sin-offering to atone
Correct: B — The Mishna explicitly states that if the handful became impure and was sacrificed despite this, the frontplate effects acceptance — meaning the offering is valid and the remainder (shirayim) is eaten by the priests as usual.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to the Gemara, what is the core dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yosei regarding the frontplate?
A. Whether the frontplate must be worn continuously or only during the avodah
B. Whether the frontplate effects acceptance for the impure blood of portions to be eaten when sprinkled intentionally
C. Whether the frontplate can atone for sins committed by non-priests
D. Whether the frontplate covers blemished animals in emergency situations
Correct: B — The Gemara attributes two contradicting baraitot to these rabbis: one holding the tzitz effects acceptance even for intentionally sprinkled impure blood (Rabbi Eliezer, lenient), and one holding it does not (Rabbi Yosei, strict — the Sages penalized intentional violations).
