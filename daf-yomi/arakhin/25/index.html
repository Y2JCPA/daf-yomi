📜 Mishna: The Frontplate Saves the Offering

הציץ מרצה על הטומאה — ונשא אהרן את עון הקדשים
משנה — הציץ מרצה If the handful (kometz) became ritually impure and the priest sacrificed it anyway — the tzitz (frontplate) effects acceptance and the remainder is eaten by the priests. Similarly, if the handful left its designated area. The frontplate worn by the High Priest bears the sin of the sacred items, rehabilitating impure offerings so they count.

🤔 Defining the Frontplate's Scope

Baraita
The tzitz atones for ritual impurity in offerings — the general impurity prohibition is sometimes permitted (e.g., communal Passover offering overrides impurity).
Rabbi Zeira
Why not include offerings that leave the courtyard? That prohibition too is sometimes permitted — communal offerings don't disqualify even if they exit!
Abaye
The verse says 'accepted before the Lord' — only a sin whose prohibition is permitted before the Lord (inside the Temple) qualifies. Leaving the courtyard is not permitted before the Lord.
Rabbi Ile'a
Then why not atone for left-hand service? That too is sometimes permitted — specifically in the water-drawing service of Yom Kippur!
Abaye
The verse says 'sin committed with the sacred items' — the sin must be with the offering itself, not in the manner of service. Left-hand is a manner-of-service sin and is excluded.
Rav Ashi
Left-hand is also excluded because the verse says hakodashim (plural) — frontplate atones only for sins involving the offerings as objects, not procedural violations. And blemished animals? The Torah says explicitly 'it shall not be accepted' — no acceptance possible at all.
Two-Part Criterion (1) The prohibition must be generally permitted before the Lord in some circumstances, AND (2) the sin must have been committed with the offering itself — not in the manner of service.

⚖️ Rabbi Eliezer vs. Rabbi Yosei on the Frontplate

Rabbi Eliezer

  • Frontplate effects acceptance for impure blood of portions to be eaten
  • Even if sprinkled intentionally — the Sages did not penalize
  • Holds that sprinkling is effective; impurity is borne by the tzitz
  • Consistent with his view that leaving the courtyard does not disqualify sprinkling
  • More lenient on the tzitz's scope of acceptance

Rabbi Yosei

  • Frontplate does not effect acceptance for impure blood of portions eaten
  • If one violated intentionally — Sages did penalize; offering not accepted
  • Distinguishes between inadvertent and willful violations strictly
  • The frontplate saves only where sin was truly accidental and unavoidable
  • More stringent on the tzitz's scope of acceptance

🔍 Two Resolutions to the Contradicting Baraitot

The Problem: Baraita A says the tzitz accepts impure blood even sprinkled intentionally. Baraita B says it does not accept intentionally sprinkled impure blood. Contradiction!
↓
Rav Yosef's Approach: Baraita A = Rabbi Yosei (does not penalize intentional violations); Baraita B = Rabbi Eliezer. But the Gemara challenges: can we really attribute these exact positions to these rabbis?
↓
Rav Ḥisda's Approach: Reverse the attributions — Baraita A = Rabbi Eliezer; Baraita B = Rabbi Yosei. Challenged similarly — we need more proof of their consistent positions.
↓
Ravina's Resolution: Divide by how impurity was contracted. Unwitting contraction → accepted always. Intentional contraction → not accepted, regardless of how sprinkling was performed.
↓
Rav Sheila's Opposite Resolution: Divide by how sprinkling was performed. Unwitting OR intentional sprinkling → accepted (if impurity was contracted unwittingly). Intentional contraction of impurity → never accepted.
