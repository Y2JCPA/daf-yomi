QUESTION 1 OF 5
What are the two possible reasons Rabbi Shimon considers a meal offering valid when the handful was removed for the sake of another meal offering?
A. The baraita permits it; or the gezera shava on 'iniquity' permits it
B. Removing for another meal offering is like removing for itself; or one law applies to all meal offerings (Lev 6:7)
C. The word 'it' permits it; or Rabbi Shimon follows his own unrecorded tradition
D. Intent toward any offering is sufficient; or the surplus money rule implies validity
Correct: B — The Gemara presents two theories: (A) removing for another meal offering is functionally 'for its own sake,' since the intent centers on a valid meal offering, or (B) the verse 'And this is the law of the meal offering' (Lev 6:7) unifies all meal offerings under one law.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
From which offering is the disqualification of the meal offering of jealousy derived, and by what hermeneutical method?
A. From the burnt offering, via the word 'fire-offering'
B. From the guilt offering, via the word 'his iniquity [avono]'
C. From the sin offering, via a verbal analogy (gezera shava) on the word 'iniquity [avon]'
D. Directly from an explicit verse in Numbers 5 that forbids wrong intent
Correct: C — The word 'iniquity' (avon) appears in both the verse about the meal offering of jealousy (Numbers 5:15) and the sin offering (Leviticus 10:17), creating a gezera shava. A guilt offering is excluded because it uses the different form 'his iniquity [avono].'
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why is the Omer meal offering excluded from the mishna's rule that wrong-intent offerings are valid but give no credit?
A. Because a special verse in Leviticus explicitly disqualifies it
B. Because the Omer uses barley, which has stricter rules than wheat
C. Because the Omer is both a communal offering and has a fixed time (16 Nisan), placing it outside the mishna's scope
D. Because the Omer's handful is not removed by a priest
Correct: C — The mishna's leniency applies only to individual offerings (not communal) and those without a fixed time. The Omer is communal AND time-bound (16 Nisan), so it falls entirely outside the mishna's framework — it is not merely 'no credit' but categorically excluded.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yirmeya, why are the guilt offerings of a nazirite and leper disqualified when slaughtered not for their sake?
A. They share the word 'it' with the sin offering
B. The Torah is more stringent with guilt offerings that render fit than with those that merely atone
C. They are communal offerings like the Omer
D. A gezera shava links them to the meal offering of jealousy
Correct: B — Rabbi Yirmeya teaches that the Torah distinguishes guilt offerings by function: those that atone (robbery, misuse of property) remain valid with wrong intent, but those that 'render fit' (enabling status change for nazirite/leper) are subject to stricter rules and are disqualified.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What does Rav Huna cite in Rav's name regarding the word 'it' (הוּא) written by the guilt offering (Lev 7:5)?
A. It disqualifies a guilt offering slaughtered not for its sake, just like a sin offering
B. A guilt offering whose owner died or whose transgression was atoned must graze until blemished, then the proceeds go to communal burnt offerings
C. It teaches that a guilt offering and sin offering share the same laws in all respects
D. It permits slaughtering a guilt offering on behalf of a different person's atonement
Correct: B — Rav Huna citing Rav: the word 'it' by the guilt offering is not an exclusion (as by the sin offering) but teaches that when a guilt offering's owner dies or their transgression is otherwise atoned, the animal grazes until blemished, is sold, and the money funds communal burnt offerings.
