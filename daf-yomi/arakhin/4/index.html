🤔 Why Does Rabbi Shimon Validate Wrong-Intent Handful Removal?

Theory A
Intent-based: Removing a handful for the sake of another meal offering is functionally equivalent to removing it for its own sake — the focus remains on a valid meal offering
Theory B
Law-based: "And this is the law of the meal offering" (Lev 6:7) — one unified law governs all meal offerings, making wrong-intent removal valid across all cases
Why the Distinction Matters Theory A limits the leniency to wrong-intent removal between meal offerings specifically. Theory B broadens it to all meal offerings under a single unified law — with wider halakhic consequences for edge cases.

📜 Does the Word 'It' (הוּא) Disqualify Guilt Offerings Too?

Gemara Challenge
The guilt offering also uses the word "It is a guilt offering" (Lev 7:5) — shouldn't it likewise be disqualified when slaughtered not for its own sake, just like the sin offering?
Baraita
The word "it" by the sin offering creates an exclusion for that offering only. By the guilt offering, the same word serves a different purpose entirely — taught by Rav Huna in the name of Rav.
Rav Huna citing Rav (Zevachim) A guilt offering whose owner died or whose transgression was atoned — the animal is set out to graze until blemished, then sold; the proceeds purchase communal burnt offerings. The word 'it' preserves the animal's sacred status for this process.

🏥 Two Categories of Guilt Offerings: Atoning vs. Rendering Fit

Regular Guilt Offerings

  • Guilt offering for robbery
  • Guilt offering for misuse of consecrated property
  • Purpose: atone for a specific sin
  • ✅ Valid even if slaughtered not for their sake
  • Owner receives no credit, but offering is not disqualified
  • Covered by general rule of Zevachim 2a

Guilt Offerings That Render Fit

  • Guilt offering of a nazirite who became ritually impure
  • Guilt offering of a leper
  • Purpose: render person fit to enter camp / partake of offerings
  • ❌ Disqualified if slaughtered not for their sake
  • They failed to achieve their purpose of rendering fit
  • Rabbi Yirmeya: Torah is more stringent with 'rendering fit'

🌅 Why the Omer Is Excluded From the Mishna's Leniency

Mishna's general rule: Meal offerings from which the handful was removed not for their sake are valid, but the owner does not fulfill their obligation
↓
Rav's teaching: The omer meal offering — if the handful was removed not for its sake — does not satisfy the communal obligation (stronger than mere 'no credit')
↓
First reason for exclusion: The mishna speaks only of offerings brought by an individual. The omer is a communal offering brought on behalf of all Israel
↓
Second reason: The mishna covers only offerings with no fixed time. The omer has a fixed time — the 16th of Nisan — placing it in a categorically different class
↓
Rav extends the principle: Same logic applies to guilt offerings of nazirite and leper — they too fall outside the general leniency due to their unique purpose of rendering fit
