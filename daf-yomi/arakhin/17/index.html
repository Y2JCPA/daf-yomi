🔥 Does Burning the Handful with Intent to Burn Frankincense = Piggul?

Pumbedita Sages
Burning does render burning piggul — burning the handful with intent to burn the frankincense the next day fully invalidates the meal offering.
Rava
Proof from the mishna (12a): burning is listed alongside removal, placement in vessel, and conveyance — all treated identically for piggul purposes.
Rav Ḥisda citing Rav
Burning does not render burning piggul. The handful is the permitting factor for the remainder, not for the frankincense — so frankincense-intent is irrelevant.
Rav Menashya bar Gadda
Rabbi Meir's piggul rule applies only when the handful is burned with intent to eat the remainder the next day — not when intent involves burning the frankincense.
Gemara Resolution Rava's proof from the mishna is rejected: removal/placement/conveyance differ from burning because both 'eat remainder' and 'burn frankincense' intents render them piggul, while only 'eat remainder' intent is relevant to burning.

🏛️ Rabbi Ḥanina's Complex Ruling (via Rav Hamnuna)

Rabbi Ḥanina
If one burned the handful with intent both to burn the frankincense tomorrow and to eat the remainder tomorrow — the offering is piggul and one is liable for karet.
Gemara Challenge
Why require both conditions? If burning renders piggul — state only the frankincense intent. If not — the eating intent alone suffices. What does combining them add?
Rav Adda bar Ahava
Rabbi Ḥanina holds: burning alone does not render piggul. But with both intents, the eating intent creates piggul, while the burning intent adds karet liability.
Rav Hamnuna
This teaching was equivalent to all the rest of my learning — its novelty: two simultaneous intents each serve a distinct halakhic function.
Halakhic Novelty Two concurrent improper intents are not redundant — one establishes piggul status and the other elevates the penalty to karet.

🍞 Mishna (17b): Improper Intent on Unusual Items

Rabbi Eliezer

  • Intent to consume an item not usually consumed (e.g., the handful itself) beyond its time → Unfit (פסול), no karet
  • Intent to burn an item not usually burned beyond its time → Unfit
  • Derives from verse: Torah equates two verbal forms to expand scope
  • Torah-level invalidation even for unusual-item intent

The Rabbis

  • Intent to consume an item not usually consumed → No halakhic effect
  • Intent to burn an item not usually burned → No halakhic effect
  • Only intent regarding the normal use of each item is significant
  • Verse teaches measure (olive-bulk) and equates eating/burning — not unusual items

All Agree (Either View)

  • Intent to consume usual item the next day → Piggul + karet
  • Intent to burn usual item the next day → Piggul + karet
  • Handful burned with intent to eat remainder tomorrow → Piggul
  • No improper intent → Valid offering

📖 The Key Verse — אָכֹל יֵאָכֵל

אָכֹל יֵאָכֵל — שתי לשונות: אחת לאכילת אדם ואחת להקטרת המזבח
Vayikra 7:18 אִם הֵאָכֹל יֵאָכֵל מִבְּשַׂר זֶבַח שְׁלָמָיו
'If the flesh of his peace offering is eaten on the third day...'

The Torah uses a doubled verbal form with two distinct conjugations:

Rabbis interpret: The two forms equate burning on the altar with eating by a person — intent to burn something after its time is as invalid as intent to eat it. Also teaches the minimum measure of an olive-bulk.

Rabbi Eliezer interprets: The two different verb forms (he'akhol / ye'akhel) equate intent on unusual items with intent on usual items — each invalidates the other.
