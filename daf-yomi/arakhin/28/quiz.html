QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava, how are the two contradictory baraitot about sprinkling 'not for its own sake' reconciled?
A. One baraita follows Rabbi Eliezer; the other follows the Rabbis — two different opinions
B. One baraita means the offering is valid for eating; the other means the mitzva of sprinkling was not properly fulfilled
C. One baraita discusses blood of the guilt-offering; the other discusses the oil of the leper
D. One baraita refers to the First Temple; the other to the Second Temple
Correct: B — Rava says both baraitot follow the Rabbis, but 'valid' means the offering may be consumed (the eating-mitzva is satisfied), while 'invalid' means the mitzva of sprinkling itself was not properly fulfilled — two different halakhic standards in the same act.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why is a Candelabrum fashioned from fragments of gold unfit, while one fashioned from other metals is fit?
A. Gold fragments cannot be properly melted and fused together
B. The verse 'of beaten work will the Candelabrum be made' requires a single block; a separate 'will be made' includes other metals
C. Fragments of gold are considered ritually impure for Temple use
D. The Torah explicitly lists silver and copper as alternatives but not gold fragments
Correct: B — The word 'beaten work' requires fashioning from a single solid block — fragments of gold violate this requirement. However, the passive phrase 'will the Candelabrum be made' is interpreted as a separate inclusion permitting other types of metal entirely.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the total height of the Candelabrum, according to the tradition of Shmuel in the name of a certain elder?
A. Ten handbreadths
B. Fifteen handbreadths
C. Eighteen handbreadths
D. Twenty-four handbreadths
Correct: C — Shmuel in the name of an elder specifies that the Candelabrum was 18 handbreadths tall, detailing the breakdown: 3 for the base and flower, 2 bare, then three knobs each preceded by a bare handbreadth, then a final bare handbreadth topped by a goblet, knob, and flower.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why were Moses's trumpets unfit for future generations, unlike all other vessels he fashioned?
A. Silver tarnishes and could not be repaired once consecrated
B. The phrase 'make for yourself' (לְךָ) designates the trumpets specifically for Moses, not for posterity
C. Trumpets required re-consecration each generation by a new High Priest
D. Moses fashioned the trumpets before the Tabernacle was erected, making them conditionally valid
Correct: B — The verse says 'Make for yourself two silver trumpets' — the word לְךָ ('for you') indicates they were designated for Moses's era alone. The Gemara considers alternative readings of לְךָ but confirms that for the trumpets it carries a restrictive temporal meaning.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the core hermeneutical difference between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Rabbi Yosei son of Rabbi Yehuda regarding Candelabrum materials?
A. R. Yehuda HaNasi requires pure gold; R. Yosei son of R. Yehuda permits silver only
B. R. Yehuda HaNasi applies kelal u'frat restricting the Candelabrum to metals; R. Yosei applies ribui u'mi'ut permitting even wood
C. R. Yehuda HaNasi requires beaten work; R. Yosei permits cast metal
D. R. Yehuda HaNasi says gold fragments are valid; R. Yosei requires a single block
Correct: B — R. Yehuda HaNasi uses kelal u'frat u'khlal (general-specific-general), concluding only items similar to the specific — metals — are valid; wood is unfit. R. Yosei b. R. Yehuda uses ribui u'mi'ut u'ribui (amplification-restriction-amplification), concluding any material is valid — even wood, as the Hasmoneans in fact used.
