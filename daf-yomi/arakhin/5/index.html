🌾 Omer Handful Removed Not for Its Own Sake

Rav
An omer meal offering from which a handful was removed not for its own sake is disqualified — improper intent invalidates it entirely.
Reish Lakish
It is valid — the handful may be burned upon the altar, though another omer must be brought whose remainder may be eaten by priests.
Rav Adda bar Ahava
Reish Lakish holds: an offering is not considered 'before its time' if it is to be brought on that very same day.
Rav Pappa
Reish Lakish's real reasoning: the illumination of the eastern horizon alone permits the new crop — so the omer handful was never truly untimely.
Central Tension If the handful is valid, how can it be sacrificed before the omer has permitted the new crop for consumption? The timing principle is the key to unlocking this puzzle.

🐦 Birds vs. Meal Offerings — Unique Halakhot (Baraita)

Unique to Birds

  • A zav, zava, or post-partum woman may bring a bird offering before their atonement offering — valid even without yet achieving atonement
  • Melikah (pinching the nape) is forbidden for ordinary consumption, yet this very act consecrates the bird as a valid Temple offering
  • The general prohibition against birds was 'permitted' through the act of sanctification for the altar

Unique to Meal Offerings

  • Require placement in a service vessel before any further rite
  • Require tenufah (waving) and hagashah (bringing to the altar corner)
  • Require kemitzah (removal of the handful) as a distinct rite — no parallel in bird offerings
  • Baraita states: meal offerings have NO case where their general prohibition was permitted through sanctification

🦟 Leper Purification: Correct Order and Violations

Step 1: Slaughter the guilt offering (אָשָׁם) first — prescribed order in Leviticus 14
↓
Step 2: Place blood from the guilt offering on the leper's right thumb and big toe
↓
Step 3: Place oil on top of the blood on the leper's right thumb and big toe
↓
Violation — oil before blood: The oil placement must be repeated. Reason: Torah writes יִהְיֶה ('shall be') — the order must remain as prescribed
↓
Rav Sheshet's objection: If 'brought that day' negates 'before its time,' why redo the oil? The blood will be placed later that same day!
↓
Rav Pappa's answer: Leper halakhot are categorically different — the 'shall be' expression mandates exact sequence, independent of the general timing principle

🐄 Can Tereifa's Disqualification Be Derived A Fortiori?

Premise: A blemished animal — permitted for ordinary consumption — is disqualified as an offering
↓
A fortiori attempt: A tereifa is forbidden even for ordinary consumption — surely it is disqualified as an offering!
↓
Objection A
Fat and blood are forbidden yet offered on the altar — but they come from a permitted animal
Counter
Melikah of birds: entirely forbidden yet valid for the altar
Rebuttal
Melikah's prohibition arises through sanctification — tereifa was forbidden before consecration
↓
Conclusion: The a fortiori inference cannot stand — a specific Torah verse is required to derive that tereifa is disqualified as an offering

🌅 Reish Lakish's Ruling on New Crop Before the Omer

Mishna (68b)
After the omer but before the two loaves, one may not bring offerings from the new crop — the two loaves are 'first fruits' and must precede all others.
Reish Lakish
A meal offering brought from new crop before the omer is disqualified only if brought on the 14th or 15th of Nisan. If brought on the 16th itself — it is valid!
Rav Pappa
This ruling proves Reish Lakish's true view: illumination of the eastern horizon (עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר) permits the new crop — so once dawn breaks on the 16th, an offering is no longer 'early.'
Rav Yitzḥak
Reish Lakish's position was not stated explicitly — it is derived by inference from this very ruling about what is disqualified on the 14th and 15th versus what is valid on the 16th.
Key Inference If the 16th-of-Nisan offering is valid even brought before the omer ceremony, Reish Lakish must hold that dawn itself — not the omer sacrifice — triggers the new crop's permissibility.
