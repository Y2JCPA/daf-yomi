⚖️ The Core Dispute: Does Partial Piggul Intent Disqualify?

Rabbi Meir
Intent of piggul during the burning of either the handful or the frankincense renders the meal offering piggul — even one of two permitting factors suffices.
Rabbis
The offering is only piggul if the priest had piggul intent during both permitting factors — partial intent, covering only the handful or only the frankincense, is insufficient.
Rav
This dispute applies only when one permitting factor was performed in silence and the other with piggul intent. If the handful was burned first with piggul intent, all agree the offering is piggul.
Shmuel
The dispute applies even when both permitting factors were performed with piggul intent — Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis disagree about the required threshold in all such configurations.
Universal Exception For the sinner's meal offering and the sotah offering, all agree that piggul intent during the handful alone renders the offering piggul — because the handful is the sole permitting factor; there is no frankincense.

🔥 Two-Stage Burning: How Piggul Intent Is Distributed

Remove (קְמִיצָה): Priest removes the kometz (handful) from the meal offering — the first critical act
↓
Convey (הוֹלָכָה): Priest carries the handful and the frankincense vessel toward the altar — debated whether this triggers piggul
↓
Burn the handful (הַקְטָרַת הַקֹּמֶץ): Placed on the altar fire — Permitting Factor #1
↓
Burn the frankincense (לְבוֹנָה): Placed on the altar fire — Permitting Factor #2
↓
Together the two burnings permit the remainder for priestly consumption — both are required to complete the permitting process
↓
Rav's ruling: If Stage 3 had piggul intent, the offering is already disqualified before Stage 4 — all agree it is piggul. Dispute only when one stage was silent.

🚶 Conveying the Handful: Does It Create Piggul?

Reish Lakish
Conveying (הוֹלָכָה) is only part of the permitting process — since frankincense must also be conveyed and burned, piggul intent during carrying the handful alone does not render the offering piggul.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
The halakha of conveying is like that of removing the handful. Just as removal alone creates piggul (being the sole removing act), so too conveying the handful alone creates piggul — each act is judged within its own category.
Underlying Logic Conveying is not itself a permitting factor — it merely transports the handful to the altar. Reish Lakish: examine the full set of permitting factors (handful + frankincense). Rabbi Yoḥanan: any non-permitting rite is judged by its own rite-category, not the overall permitting set.

🌿 Sesame-Seed Burning: A New Piggul Scenario

Scenario: Priest burns only a sesame-seed amount of handful + frankincense, each time intending to eat a sesame-seed of remainder the next day
↓
Can we derive the ruling from the existing three-way dispute (Rabbi Meir / Rabbis / Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi)?
↓
Opinion: Piggul
The manner of consumption of this offering is in small portions — so burning in small portions with such intent is valid piggul
Opinion: Not Piggul
A minimum olive-bulk is required to trigger piggul — a sesame-seed amount is fundamentally insufficient regardless of intent
↓
Gemara's conclusion: The sesame-seed case cannot be resolved from existing disputes — each sage may hold their position only for their specific case. The question remains open (תֵּיקוּ).

🐑 Shavuot Lambs & Loaves: Piggul Intent Across Offerings

Lamb slaughtered re: Two Loaves

  • Slaughter one lamb intending to eat the two loaves tomorrow
  • Rabbi Meir: PIGGUL — lamb's slaughter is a permitting factor for the loaves
  • Rabbis: NOT piggul — slaughter alone is not the complete permitting act for loaves
  • Rava: slaughter itself sanctifies the loaves; blood-sprinkling merely permits them

Lamb slaughtered re: Other Lamb

  • Slaughter Lamb A intending to eat Lamb B tomorrow
  • Result: BOTH lambs remain fit offerings
  • Reason: piggul intent must concern the offering whose permitting act is being performed
  • Cross-offering piggul intent has no legal effect

Frankincense Bowl re: Shewbread

  • One of two frankincense bowls burned with intent to eat shewbread tomorrow
  • Rabbi Meir: PIGGUL — the bowl is a permitting factor for the shewbread
  • Rabbis: NOT piggul — both bowls are permitting factors; burning only one is incomplete
  • Abaye's challenge to Rava: this case tests the 'partial permitting factor' theory
