📍 Where on the Doorpost? Height Dispute

Shmuel
It is a mitzva to place the mezuza at the beginning of the upper third of the doorpost — this is the ideal location.
Rav Huna
Raise the mezuza one handbreadth from the ground, or distance it one handbreadth from the crossbeam. The entire space between is valid.
Gemara
Rav Huna follows Rabbi Yehuda (baraita). But Shmuel — whose opinion does he follow? Not Rabbi Yehuda, not Rabbi Meir…
Rav Huna bar Rav Natan
Shmuel follows Rabbi Yosei. 'Upper third' means the maximum — do not place it more than a third's distance from the crossbeam.
Resolution Both Shmuel and Rav Huna can align with Rabbi Yosei: the upper third sets an upper limit, not a single mandatory spot.

👑 God Protects from Outside

מִדַּת בָּשָׂר וָדָם אֵינָהּ כְּמִדַּת הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא
Rabbi Ḥanina The attribute of flesh and blood is not like the attribute of the Holy One, Blessed be He.

A human king sits inside his palace — his servants stand outside to guard him.

But Israel dwells inside, and the Holy One guards them from outside.

As it is written: "God will guard your going out and your coming in" — He stands at the door, mezuza-like, shielding those within.

✅❌ Valid vs. Invalid Mezuza Affixing

✅ Valid

  • Written on one sheet of parchment
  • Affixed vertically on the doorpost
  • On the right side by entry direction
  • Within the upper third of the doorpost
  • Carved into reed post (tube method — reed entrance)

❌ Invalid

  • Written on two separate sheets
  • Affixed horizontally like a bolt (Rav)
  • Wedged into a hole in the doorpost
  • Dug one handbreadth deep into the post (Rav Yosef son of Rava)
  • Placed more than upper third from crossbeam

🔩 The Bolt-Mezuza Case: Challenge and Resolution

Rav rules: A mezuza placed like a bolt — wedged into a hole or affixed horizontally — is unfit.
↓
Challenge (Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef): He saw Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's study hall entrance with a mezuza placed horizontally — doesn't this contradict Rav?
↓
Answer: Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's doorpost was exceptionally wide — a diagonal placement counted as both horizontal and vertical simultaneously. A special case.
↓
Second challenge: Rav Yitzḥak also said Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's entrance had no mezuza at all — but Rav Huna always passed through a different entrance that did have one.
↓
Conclusion: Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's specific entrance was exempt (e.g., temporary or lacking proper form). Rav's ruling stands: horizontal affixing is unfit.
