🏅 Is the Frontplate (Tzitz) the Subject of the Rabbi Yehuda / Rabbis Dispute?

Rabbis
The frontplate effects acceptance for items normally consumed by priests that became impure — the blood sprinkling is fully valid and permits the loaves.
Rabbi Yehuda
The frontplate only effects acceptance for items that ascend upon the altar — it does not work for priest-consumed items.
Rav Huna b. Rav Natan
Challenge! Rabbi Yehuda himself concedes the frontplate works for altar items (e.g., impure frankincense bowls). So their dispute cannot be about the frontplate at all!
Rav Ashi + Ravina
Additional proofs from the Pesachim mishna and the two-loaves mishna confirm: the Rabbi Yehuda / Rabbis dispute is not about the Tzitz.
📌 Gemara's Conclusion Multiple proofs (baraita, Pesachim mishna, two-loaves mishna) establish that the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis does NOT concern the frontplate. Their disagreement lies elsewhere.

🍞 How a Thanks Offering Renders Its Loaves Piggul

Kohen slaughters the thanks offering with intent to consume it after its permitted time (next day)
↓
The thanks offering itself becomes piggul — invalid, subject to karet if eaten
↓
The accompanying loaves are also rendered piggul — they depend on the offering's blood for permission
↓
✅ Works (offering → loaves):
Intent at slaughter of offering renders loaves piggul
❌ Does NOT work (loaves → offering):
Intent regarding the loaves alone cannot render the offering piggul

🔄 Two Parallel Cases of Asymmetric Piggul

Thanks Offering + Loaves

  • ✅ Thanks offering renders its loaves piggul
  • ❌ Loaves do NOT render the thanks offering piggul
  • Why? Loaves are only permitted by the offering's blood — full dependency
  • Source: Rav Kahana — loaves sanctified only through the blood rite of the offering

Shavuot Lambs + Two Loaves

  • ✅ Lambs render the two loaves piggul
  • ❌ Two loaves do NOT render the lambs piggul
  • Why? Same asymmetric dependency — loaves derive their sanctity from the lambs
  • Both cases are necessary: neither alone teaches the other (different structures)

🤔 Rabbi Elazar's Dilemma: Does Combined Intent Across Offering + Loaves Work?

Rabbi Elazar
Dilemma: A kohen slaughters the thanks offering intending to eat an olive-bulk from the offering AND from its loaves the next day. Does the intent for the offering combine with intent for the loaves to render both piggul?
Rav
Even here: the loaves are rendered piggul — but the thanks offering is NOT rendered piggul. The asymmetry still holds.
Gemara Challenge
Kal va-chomer objection: if the thanks offering itself — which CAN render loaves piggul — is not rendered piggul, surely the loaves (which cannot render the offering piggul) should also not be rendered piggul!
Resolution
We do NOT apply this a fortiori. Proof from the seeds-in-vineyard incident: Torah-prohibited items (hemp/arum) are forbidden; rabbinically-prohibited seeds are permitted — kal va-chomer does not automatically transfer stringencies.
Key Rule Piggul intent regarding the loaves can render them piggul via their own independent rite — even when identical intent does not render the accompanying offering piggul.

📜 Rava's Explanation of Rabbi Meir (Amud Bet)

Setup: Rabbis challenge Rabbi Meir — a person may bring his offering today and libations up to 10 days later, so the blood clearly does NOT permit the libations at the time of slaughter
↓
Rabbi Meir's Response: I too speak only about libations that were already designated at slaughter — these become fixed to this offering and may not be used with another
↓
Rava's Clarification: Rabbi Meir holds that designated libations are fixed (קבועים) to the offering at slaughter — therefore piggul intent during slaughter reaches them
↓
Parallel Case — Leper's Oil: Same dispute applies: Rabbi Meir says oil is fixed to its guilt offering; Rabbis say it can be substituted, so it is not bound
↓
Practical Implication: According to Rabbi Meir, liability for eating piggul extends to libations and leper's oil when the primary offering had piggul intent during its qualifying rite
