Mishna: Pairs and groups of ritual items are mutually indispensable — two Yom Kippur goats, two Shavuot sheep, shewbread arrangements, nazirite loaves, lulav species, seven red heifer sprinklings

🌿 Must the Lulav Be Bound Together with the Other Species?

Rabbi Yehuda
The lulav MUST be bound with the myrtle and willow — if it is not bound, it is unfit. I derive this via gezerah shavah: 'taking' written for the four species (Lev. 23:40) parallels 'taking' written for the hyssop bundle at the Paschal offering (Ex. 12:22). Just as the Paschal hyssop must be a bundle, so too the four species must be taken as a bound bundle.
Rabbis
Binding is a mitzvah — it fulfills hiddur mitzvah — but the lulav is perfectly valid even if not bound. The gezerah shavah does not create an absolute requirement; 'taking' of the lulav and 'taking' of hyssop are not comparable in this way.
Rav Ḥanan bar Rava
The mishna's rule that the four species are mutually indispensable applies only when one does NOT have all four. If one possesses all four species, failure to take any one of them does not disqualify the others — each is independently valid.
Baraita
All four species must be taken as one unified bundle — compared to the Jewish people in prayer: their repentance is not accepted until they are all bound together as a single unit, as it is stated: 'He builds His upper chambers in the heaven and has established His bundle upon the earth' (Amos 9:6).
Resolution The dispute over whether binding is required vs. merely a mitzva is itself a tannaitic controversy — the baraita follows Rabbi Yehuda's position, while the mishna reflects the Rabbis' lenient view.

✨ Hiddur Mitzvah — Glorifying the Commandment

זֶה אֵלִי וְאַנְוֵהוּ — "This is my God and I will glorify Him" (Exodus 15:2)
Core Principle The Rabbis hold that binding the lulav together fulfills hiddur mitzvah — the obligation to beautify and glorify the commandments. This is derived from Exodus 15:2: "This is my God and I will glorify Him" — the Rabbis interpret: beautify yourself before God through the commandments themselves. A beautiful lulav, a beautiful sukkah, beautiful tzitzit, a beautiful Torah scroll — even where strict law is already satisfied, one should seek to honor the mitzva with beauty.

🩸 Red Heifer Sprinklings — When Are They Valid?

Priest performs seven sprinklings of red heifer blood opposite the Sanctuary entrance
↓
Done NOT lishmah (not for their own sake)
Invalid — sprinklings must be performed with proper intention; performed otherwise they have no effect
Done NOT precisely toward the entrance
Disputed — two baraitot contradict each other; Rava resolves this as a tannaitic dispute
Done properly — lishmah + facing the entrance
Valid — purification takes effect; any single missing sprinkling invalidates the entire set
↓
Inner sin offerings (bull and goat of Yom Kippur, bull of the anointed priest): different rules apply — Rava's resolution distinguishes outer from inner sprinklings

🏛️ Unauthorized Entry into the Holy of Holies — Two Views

Rabbis

  • "Into the holy place" and "within the Curtain" describe one combined prohibition
  • Both the Sanctuary and Holy of Holies fall under a single prohibition
  • Penalty for unauthorized entry: lashes
  • "Before the Ark Cover" (el penei) teaches that the Yom Kippur sprinkling must directly face the Ark — it does not add a separate death penalty
  • The term el is not always strictly directional — it can mean 'near' or 'before'

Rabbi Yehuda

  • "Into the holy place" = Sanctuary alone → penalty: lashes
  • "Within the Curtain" = Holy of Holies → penalty: death at Heaven's hand
  • Two areas, two distinct prohibitions, two different penalties
  • "Before the Ark Cover" means the Yom Kippur sprinkling must be directed precisely toward the Ark — el means specifically in front of
  • The verse must write both phrases to establish separate penalties for each space
