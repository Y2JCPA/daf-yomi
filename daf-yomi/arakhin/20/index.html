⚖️ Rav Yosef & Abaye: Is Salting Indispensable?

Rav Yosef
Rav holds that only sacrificial rites repeated in a second verse are indispensable. He follows the tanna of our mishna: if one did not salt the meal offering, it is still fit.
Questioner
But salting IS mentioned in a second verse — 'And every meal offering of yours you shall season with salt' (Lev. 2:13)! How can Rav say it is not repeated?
Rav Yosef
Since the word 'covenant' (בְּרִית) is written about salt, it is considered as though the requirement were repeated in another verse. 'Covenant' itself makes it indispensable — equivalent to a repeated verse.
Abaye
Could it even enter your mind that a non-priest would approach the altar to salt the handful? A non-priest may not enter the altar area! This scenario is inconceivable and cannot be counted among the rites.
Core Dispute What elevates a ritual requirement to 'indispensable'? Repetition in a verse — or does covenantal language (בְּרִית) serve the same function? Rav says yes to both, making salting obligatory even by his own standard.

🔥 Why Limbs of Burnt Offering Are Included but Blood Is Not

Limbs of Burnt Offering

  • ✅ Other items come as a requirement (wood needed for burning)
  • ✅ Prohibited to eat while ritually impure — like meal offering
  • ✅ Subject to me'ila (misuse of consecrated property)
  • ✅ Burned entirely on the altar
  • ✅ INCLUDED in the salt requirement

Blood

  • ❌ No other items come as a requirement for blood
  • ❌ Impurity prohibition does not apply in the same way
  • ❌ Not brought in the same vessel as libations (unlike frankincense with meal offering)
  • ⚠️ Does render offering permitted — but this alone is insufficient
  • ❌ EXCLUDED from the salt requirement

🤝 The Eternal Covenant of Salt

בְּרִית מֶלַח עוֹלָם הִוא לִפְנֵי ה׳ לְךָ וּלְזַרְעֲךָ אִתָּךְ — "It is an everlasting covenant of salt before the Lord, for you and your descendants" (Numbers 18:19)
בְּרִית מֶלַח Salt preserves and never corrupts — the Torah uses it as the symbol of eternal covenant. Two key verses invoke this symbolism: Leviticus 2:13 commands salting every meal offering, and Numbers 18:19 calls the priestly gifts 'an everlasting covenant of salt before the Lord.' Rabbi Yehuda derives the obligation from the covenant language in Leviticus; Rabbi Shimon uses a gezera shava linking both verses. Either way, the covenant framing transforms salting from a culinary act into a theological statement: as salt endures, so does the bond between Israel and God. Rav adds: covenantal language is legally equivalent to a repeated verse — making the salt requirement fully indispensable.
