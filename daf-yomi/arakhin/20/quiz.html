QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav (as explained by Rav Yosef), what is the halakhic status of a meal offering that was not salted?
A. Invalid — salting is always indispensable
B. Valid — unsalted rites are fit unless the verse is explicitly repeated
C. Invalid only if the handful was not salted
D. Valid only for an Israelite's offering, not a priest's
Correct: B — Rav holds that only rites repeated in a second verse are indispensable. He follows the tanna of our mishna: an unsalted meal offering is still fit. Although the 'covenant' language eventually leads Rav to count salting as indispensable, the mishna's tanna represents his baseline position.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Abaye argue that a non-priest salting the handful cannot be used to challenge Rav's position?
A. Because non-priests are permitted near the altar for salting
B. Because the scenario is inconceivable — non-priests cannot enter the altar area
C. Because salting is always performed before the offering reaches the altar
D. Because Rav explicitly excluded all non-priest ritual actions from his count
Correct: B — Abaye points out that a non-priest physically cannot enter the area near the altar. Since the scenario is inconceivable, it cannot be listed among the rites Rav argues are non-indispensable — and thus it cannot serve as a challenge to Rav's view.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
In the klal/prat/klal derivation, what phrase serves as the 'detail' (prat) that limits the scope?
A. "You shall sacrifice salt with all your offerings" — the second generalization
B. "Every offering" — the broadest possible term
C. "Your meal offering" (minchatecha) — specifically the meal offering
D. "The handful" (kometz) — the portion burned on the altar
Correct: C — The phrase 'your meal offering' (minchatecha) is the prat — it narrows the generalization to the meal offering specifically. The second generalization then re-expands the law, but only to items similar to the detail: things burned on the altar that share characteristics with the meal offering.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Which set of characteristics justifies including the limbs of a burnt offering in the salt requirement while excluding blood?
A. The burnt offering is more expensive and more common than blood
B. The burnt offering requires wood for burning, is subject to me'ila, and has an impurity prohibition — blood lacks all of these
C. The burnt offering is brought by priests while blood is handled by Levites
D. The burnt offering is fully consumed while blood can be partially redeemed
Correct: B — The Gemara's mnemonic lists traits shared by the burnt offering and meal offering but absent in blood: other items (wood) come as requirements for it, it is subject to the impurity prohibition, and it is subject to me'ila (misuse of consecrated property). Blood lacks these characteristics and is therefore excluded.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What does the word בְּרִית ('covenant') written in connection with salt teach, according to Rav's final position in the Gemara?
A. That salt may be replaced by other preserving agents in an emergency
B. That the salt requirement applies only during the First Temple period
C. That the salt requirement is considered as if stated in a second verse, making it indispensable
D. That the salt covenant applies only to communal offerings, not individual ones
Correct: C — Rav ultimately holds that covenantal language (בְּרִית) is legally equivalent to a repeated verse. This means that even by Rav's own standard — that only rites repeated in verses are indispensable — salting qualifies as indispensable, because the covenant language effectively 'repeats' the requirement.
