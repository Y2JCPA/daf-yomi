🌾 The Four Kometz Rites

1. Removal (Kemitza)
Priest removes a handful of flour from the meal offering with three middle fingers
↓
2. Placement in Vessel
Handful placed into a sanctified service vessel (kli shareit)
↓
3. Conveyance to Altar
Priest carries the kometz to the altar
↓
4. Burning (Haktara)
Kometz burned on altar; remainder (shirayim) then permitted to the priests

📜 The Core Mishna Ruling

כָּל הַמְּנָחוֹת שֶׁנִּקְמְצוּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן — כְּשֵׁרוֹת, אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא עָלוּ לַבְּעָלִים לְשֵׁם חוֹבָה
MISHNA All meal offerings from which a handful was removed not for their sake (shelo lishmah) are valid — but they do not fulfill the owner's obligation. Exception: the meal offering of a sinner (minchat chatat) and the meal offering of jealousy (minchat sotah) are fully disqualified if any rite was performed shelo lishmah.

🔁 Why Does the Mishna Add 'But Did Not Satisfy'?

The Problem: We already know the offering is valid — why add that it 'did not satisfy the obligation'?
↓
Teaching #1 — the offering is locked in: The only deficiency is non-fulfillment; the meal offering itself is permanently valid and cannot be re-offered with different intent
↓
Proof via Logic: Just because one deviated from protocol once does not permit deviating again — the owner must bring a new offering
↓
Proof via Verse (Deut. 23:24): 'That which has gone out of your lips you shall observe and do' — a vow binds you to proper fulfillment; deviation once does not release the vow

⚖️ Meal Offerings vs. Slaughtered Offerings: Shelo Lishmah

🌾 Meal Offerings

  • Each type has a unique preparation method
  • Sinner's offering: coarse barley flour
  • Voluntary offering: fine wheat flour
  • Different species = wrong intent is recognizable
  • ∴ Wrong intent CAN disqualify (Rabbi Shimon)

🐂 Slaughtered Offerings

  • One manner of slaughter for all offerings
  • One manner of blood collection for all
  • One manner of blood sprinkling for all
  • Wrong intent is not externally recognizable
  • ∴ Wrong intent does NOT disqualify (all agree)

🔥 The Contradiction in Rabbi Shimon — and Three Resolutions

Rabbi Shimon
A voluntary meal offering sacrificed not for its sake is valid AND satisfies the owner's obligation — it is like a guilt offering (asham) which is valid even without proper intent.
Rabbi Shimon
But a voluntary meal offering removed for the sake of another meal offering does NOT satisfy the owner's obligation — just as a guilt offering does not effect acceptance when misidentified.
Rabba
No contradiction: 'Satisfies' = change of sanctity type (e.g., for the sake of a different category). 'Does not satisfy' = removed for another specific offering of the same category.
Rav Ashi
No contradiction: 'Satisfies' = change of name (e.g., called by a different offering's name). 'Does not satisfy' = change of owner (sacrificed for another person). The Mishna follows Rabbi Shimon under this reading.
Rava
No contradiction: 'Satisfies' = change of offering type entirely. 'Does not satisfy' = removed for another meal offering of the same type but different owner.
KEY QUESTION Only Rav Ashi's resolution allows the Mishna to follow Rabbi Shimon. Both Rabba's and Rava's resolutions leave the Mishna unexplained under Rabbi Shimon's view.

🗺️ When Does Shelo Lishmah Disqualify a Meal Offering?

A rite of the meal offering is performed shelo lishmah (not for its sake)
↓
Minchat Chatat or Sotah
DISQUALIFIED entirely
Like a sin offering slaughtered shelo lishmah — these offerings require lishmah at every stage
Voluntary Meal Offering
VALID but does not fulfill owner's obligation
Owner must bring a second offering to satisfy the vow
↓
The offering itself may not be re-offered with corrected intent — it is permanently valid as-is (proven by logic and Deut. 23:24)
