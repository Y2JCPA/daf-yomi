QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yoḥanan's resolution of the dispute, what does Rabbi Yosei hold about piggul intent and the two Shavuot loaves?
A. If the priest intends an olive-bulk from both loaves combined, both are piggul; if intent was only on one loaf, only that loaf is piggul
B. The two loaves are always two separate bodies, so intent on one never affects the other
C. The priest must intend the full olive-bulk from each loaf separately for either to become piggul
D. The two loaves follow Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's view that 'bishteihen' requires intent on both simultaneously
Correct: A — Rabbi Yoḥanan resolves: Rabbi Yosei holds the loaves are one body. 'Mixed' intent (olive-bulk from both) renders both piggul; 'separated' intent (only one loaf) renders only that loaf piggul — since the priest divided them mentally.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does Levi teach about intentions occurring during the four sacrificial rites (slaughter, receiving, carrying, sprinkling)?
A. Intentions during all four rites always combine to create piggul liability
B. Intentions during the four sacrificial rites do NOT combine to render an offering piggul
C. Only intentions during slaughter and sprinkling — the two primary permitting factors — combine
D. Intentions combine only if all four rites are performed by the same priest
Correct: B — Levi's baraita explicitly teaches that intentions during the four sacrificial rites do not combine to render an offering piggul. Each rite stands independently — combined intent across rites is insufficient for biblical piggul.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does Abaye argue that the case of two lambs each slaughtered with intent for half an olive-bulk from a different loaf does NOT result in any disqualification — not even rabbinic?
A. Because split intent is explicitly permitted by Torah law in this case
B. Because Rabbi Yosei never addressed the specific case of two lambs
C. Because there is no analogous parallel case from which the Rabbis could issue a decree of disqualification
D. Because the two lambs are considered two separate bodies under all opinions
Correct: C — Abaye's core argument: rabbinic decrees disqualifying offerings require an analogous 'mirror case' to serve as the basis for the decree. For frankincense/handful there are clear parallels; for two lambs with split intent, no such parallel exists — so no decree applies and the offerings are valid.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In the mishna at the end of Daf 14, what is Rabbi Yehuda's ruling when one of the two Shavuot loaves becomes ritually impure before the sprinkling of the blood?
A. Only the impure loaf is burned; the pure loaf is offered and eaten normally
B. Both loaves must be taken to the place of burning, as a communal offering cannot be divided
C. The sprinkling proceeds and the tzitz (frontplate) atones for the impurity of the loaf
D. The entire Shavuot sacrifice is postponed until a new set of loaves can be prepared
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda rules both loaves must be burned — a communal offering (korban tzibur) cannot be divided. If one part is invalidated before the permitting act (sprinkling), the whole offering is compromised and neither part may be offered or eaten.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rav Ashi refutes Rav Huna by citing a baraita in Rabbi Yosei's name about a priest who slaughters an offering with intent to pour its remaining blood the next day. What principle does Rav Ashi derive?
A. Intent to perform a rite outside the Temple always invalidates the offering entirely
B. Piggul intent can render an element piggul even without direct intent on that specific element — intent to pour blood renders the meat piggul
C. Rabbi Yosei holds that the two loaves on Shavuot are always considered two separate bodies
D. Intent expressed during slaughter always overrides and nullifies intent expressed during sprinkling
Correct: B — Rav Ashi's proof: Rabbi Yosei rules that intent to pour remaining blood tomorrow renders the MEAT piggul — even though the priest's intent was never directly about the meat. This demonstrates that piggul intent can spread beyond the directly intended element, refuting Rav Huna's narrow reading of the baraita.
