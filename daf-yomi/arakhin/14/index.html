🧩 The Great Piggul Debate: Can Intent Combine?

Rav Huna
The baraita about the two loaves follows Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, not Rabbi Yosei. We cannot infer Rabbi Yosei's view from it. The phrase 'bishteihen' means one from each loaf — not an olive-bulk combined from both together.
Rav Ashi
Refutation: A baraita in Rabbi Yosei's name rules that intent to pour remaining blood tomorrow renders the MEAT piggul — even though his intent was never about the meat itself. Piggul intent clearly spreads beyond the directly intended element!
Ravina
Another refutation from the mishna: Removing a handful with intent to burn it the next day renders the remainder piggul — even though the handful is not the consumed portion. Intent on the thigh should certainly spread to the other thigh!
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Resolution: Rabbi Yosei holds the two thighs are one body, so piggul intent on one renders the other piggul too. For the two loaves: if intent covers an olive-bulk from both combined, both are piggul. If intent was only on one loaf, only that loaf is piggul.
Core Principle Whether piggul intent 'spreads' turns on whether paired offerings constitute one unified body (גוף אחד) or two independent bodies — a question at the heart of this entire daf.

🔍 Rabbi Yoḥanan's Resolution: The Two Loaves

Question: Does piggul intent regarding ONE of the two Shavuot loaves render the OTHER loaf piggul as well?
↓
Rabbi Yoḥanan: Rabbi Yosei holds the two loaves are one body (גוף אחד) — the verse binds them together. The outcome depends entirely on how the priest expressed his intent.
↓
Intent Mixed — Combined
Priest intends to consume an olive-bulk from BOTH loaves together during the same rite — he did not distinguish between them
Intent Separated — One Loaf Only
Priest intends to consume an olive-bulk specifically from ONE loaf — he mentally separated them during the rite
↓
Both Loaves Piggul
Since the verse binds them as one body, combined intent renders both loaves piggul with full kareit liability
Only That Loaf Piggul
Only the loaf associated with the intent becomes piggul; the other loaf is unaffected and remains valid for eating

⚖️ Combining Intentions: When Do They Merge?

Intentions COMBINE

  • Intent for an olive-bulk spread across both loaves together during one rite
  • The verse treats paired offerings as one unified body (גוף אחד)
  • Some say: intent during slaughter + sprinkling (both permitting factors) combine
  • Full measure of consumption intended — even split between two parts of one body

Intentions Do NOT Combine

  • Levi's baraita: Intentions across the four sacrificial rites do NOT combine for piggul
  • Intent during slaughter of one lamb + different rite for another lamb = separate
  • Half an olive-bulk from one loaf during one lamb's rite + half from another loaf during another lamb's rite
  • Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: Intent must be for an olive-bulk from both loaves combined (bishteihen)

Rabbinic Decree Cases

  • Handful + frankincense: intent on one disqualifies the other by decree (Rabbi Yosei)
  • Two lambs/loaves with split intent: Rabbis disqualify by decree — but no kareit liability
  • Single frankincense bowl with intent on both shewbread arrangements: disqualified
  • Decrees apply only where an analogous parallel case justifies extending the rule

🗣️ Abaye vs. Rava bar Rav Ḥanan: The Logic of Decrees

Rava bar Rav Ḥanan
Challenge to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: If he holds that intent during a full permitting factor + half a consumption measure combines for piggul — then when one slaughters BOTH lambs, each with intent for half an olive-bulk from a different loaf, shouldn't both loaves be fully piggul?
Abaye
No — Rabbi Yosei and the Rabbis each issued decrees in their respective analogous cases. Rabbi Yosei decreed frankincense handful disqualifies because it resembles the handful of meal offering. The Rabbis likewise decreed the sinner's meal offering follows the standard meal offering.
Rava bar Rav Ḥanan
Fine for those cases — but where is the analogous 'other case' that would justify a rabbinic decree for two lambs slaughtered with split intent? What parallel case gives the Rabbis grounds to issue such a decree here?
Abaye
There IS no analogous case — and that is precisely the point! The two-lambs-split-intent scenario is entirely unique. With no parallel, there is no basis for any decree. The offerings are therefore simply not piggul — not even rabbinically disqualified.
Abaye's Principle Rabbinic decrees disqualifying offerings require an analogous parallel case. Without such a parallel, the offering is neither biblically piggul nor rabbinically disqualified.

🏛️ Mishna: One of the Two Loaves Becomes Impure

שתי הלחם — אחת נטמאת
Tannaitic Dispute — End of Daf 14 The Case: One of the two Shavuot loaves becomes ritually impure before the sprinkling of the lambs' blood (the act that permits the loaves for consumption).

Rabbi Yehuda: Both loaves must be taken to the place of burning — a communal offering (korban tzibur) cannot be divided. If one is invalidated, the other may not be offered separately.

The Rabbis: The pure loaf is offered and eaten normally; only the impure loaf is disqualified and burned.

Rabbi Elazar clarifies: This dispute applies specifically when the loaf became impure before sprinkling. After sprinkling, the loaves are already rendered permitted — impurity at that stage cannot retroactively invalidate the pure loaf according to any view.

Rav Pappa adds: The Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda also disagree about whether the tzitz (High Priest's golden frontplate) operates to atone for the impurity of communal offerings in this context.
