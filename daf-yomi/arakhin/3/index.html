🎭 The Contradiction and Rabba's Resolution

Gemara
Two baraitot contradict each other regarding Rabbi Shimon's opinion: one says a meal offering brought not for its own sake fulfills the owner's obligation, and one says it does not!
Rabba
It is not difficult. Here (valid): the false intent is recognizable — observers see it is not for its own sake. There (invalid): the false intent is concealed — no one can tell anything is wrong.
Gemara
But why does recognizable false intent get disregarded? A pan meal offering removed for a deep-pan — someone who sees it will think the priest is acting improperly!
Rabba
There is no problem. If the owner vowed a pan offering and brought it in a pan, it is valid even if done for a deep-pan — because preparation mode proves the offering's identity. Meal offerings are unlike slaughtered offerings in this regard.
Rabbi Shimon
Precisely: מנחות שאני — meal offerings are different. Each type's preparation reveals its identity. Slaughtered offerings all look the same at slaughter.
Key Term נראה כשוטה (nireh kohen shoteh) — "appears as a foolish priest" — the standard for recognizably false intent that is disregarded

🏺 Rabba's Principle: Recognizable vs. Concealed Intent

Priest performs rite with improper intent
(e.g., removes handful from pan mincha for sake of deep-pan)
↓
Intent is RECOGNIZABLE
Observers can identify the offering by its preparation — the false intent is obvious (nireh shoteh)
Intent is CONCEALED
Observers cannot detect the improper intent — the offering looks perfectly normal
↓
→ Owner Fulfilled
False intent is disregarded; the offering's mode of preparation proves its true identity
→ Owner Not Fulfilled
False intent invalidates fulfillment; no outward sign contradicts the improper purpose
↓
Challenge: Goat sin offering (black hair) for guilt offering (white-wooled ram) — isn't the difference clearly recognizable?
Answer: Differences in appearance are not always "on people's minds"

🍞 Meal Offerings vs. Slaughtered Offerings

Meal Offerings (מנחות)

  • Each type has a unique preparation mode (pan, deep-pan, dry, with oil)
  • "And this is the law of the meal offering" (Lev. 6:7) — one unified law for all minchahs
  • Mode of preparation proves what offering it is — false intent more detectable
  • Rava: removing handful for different mincha type = owner fulfilled
  • Rav Ashi: intent about vessel type alone is inconsequential

Slaughtered Offerings (זבחים)

  • All offerings slaughtered in the same manner — no visual distinction
  • Blood sprinkled identically regardless of offering type
  • "And slaughter it for a sin offering" (Lev. 4:33) — 'it' means for its own sake only
  • Slaughter in north or south can be ambiguous as to intent
  • Improper intent typically invalidates fulfillment of obligation

🔬 Rava and Rav Ashi's Resolutions Tested

Rava's basis: "And this is the law of the meal offering" (Leviticus 6:7) — the singular word 'law' implies one unified rule for all meal offerings, so improper intent for another mincha type is valid
↓
Challenge to Rava: But Rabbi Shimon himself states his reasoning is that each mincha's mode of preparation proves its identity — not the verse! How can Rava use the verse as the basis?
↓
Resolution: Rava reads Rabbi Shimon's statement differently — the verse provides the rule, and the preparation-proves-identity principle explains the contrast with slaughtered offerings (where one uniform slaughter applies to all)
↓
Rav Ashi's basis: When a priest says 'for the sake of a deep-pan' while handling a pan offering, he only has intent regarding the vessel — and such intent is inconsequential
↓
Challenge to Rav Ashi (Rav Aḥa son of Rava): If vessel-intent is inconsequential, why does Rabbi Shimon validate removing a handful from a dry mincha for the sake of an oil mincha? That difference is not about the vessel!
↓
Final challenge: If 'for the sake of peace (shelamim)' intent is inconsequential, why does slaughtering a burnt offering 'for the sake of a peace offering' invalidate it? Answer: The offering itself is called shelamim — so the intent refers to the offering-type, not a mere word
