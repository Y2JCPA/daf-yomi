QUESTION 1 OF 5
What is the key distinction Rabba makes to resolve the contradiction between the two baraitot about Rabbi Shimon's opinion?
A. Whether the priest verbally declared his intention before performing the rite
B. Whether the false intent is recognizable to observers (nireh shoteh) or is concealed
C. Whether the offering is a communal or individual obligation
D. Whether the owner is present in the Temple courtyard at the time of the rite
Correct: B — Rabba distinguishes between recognizable and concealed false intent. When observers can see the improper intent (it 'appears as a foolish priest'), it is disregarded and the owner fulfills his obligation. When the intent is hidden with no outward sign, the offering does not fulfill the obligation.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rava, what verse does Rabbi Shimon use to validate a meal offering whose handful was removed for the sake of a different type of meal offering?
A. "And slaughter it for a sin offering" (Leviticus 4:33)
B. "Who sacrifices the blood of the peace offering" (Leviticus 7:33)
C. "And this is the law of the meal offering" (Leviticus 6:7)
D. "You shall bring the meal offering that is made of these things" (Leviticus 2:8)
Correct: C — Rava says Rabbi Shimon derives from "And this is the law of the meal offering" (Lev. 6:7) that there is one unified law for all types of meal offerings — hence removing a handful for a different mincha type is still valid, as all share the same halakhic rule.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Ashi, why does a pan meal offering whose handful was removed 'for the sake of a deep-pan' still fulfill the owner's obligation?
A. Because the verse 'one law for all meal offerings' covers this case
B. Because the intent is recognizable to observers in the Temple courtyard
C. Because intent regarding only the vessel type is inconsequential — only offering-type intent invalidates
D. Because the owner's original vow encompassed both vessel types
Correct: C — Rav Ashi holds that the priest's intent was solely about the vessel (pan vs. deep-pan), not about a fundamentally different type of offering. Such vessel-focused intent is halakhically inconsequential. Only intent to perform the rite for a different category of offering would invalidate.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
The Gemara challenges Rabba's principle by citing a goat sin offering slaughtered for the sake of a guilt offering. What makes this a strong challenge?
A. A goat cannot halakhically serve as a sin offering under any circumstances
B. A guilt offering must come from a female animal, making the sex easy to verify
C. A goat has black hair while a guilt offering is a white-wooled ram — the difference is visually obvious, yet Rabbi Shimon seems to invalidate such cases
D. The slaughter location (north) proves the offering's identity, making intent irrelevant
Correct: C — The Gemara challenges: since a goat has black hair and a guilt offering is a white-wooled ram, observers would clearly recognize the mismatch. By Rabba's principle, this recognizable false intent should be disregarded and the offering should be valid — yet Rabbi Shimon appears to invalidate it.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rav Aḥa son of Rava challenges Rav Ashi's resolution. What is the substance of his challenge?
A. Rav Ashi's reading of Rabbi Shimon contradicts the plain meaning of Leviticus 6:7
B. If only vessel-type intent is inconsequential, why does Rabbi Shimon validate removing a handful from a dry meal offering for the sake of an oil meal offering — since that difference is not about the vessel?
C. Rav Ashi's principle would render all slaughtered offerings valid regardless of improper intent
D. Rav Ashi ignores the explicit ruling of the Rabbis in the cited mishna (102b)
Correct: B — Rav Aḥa son of Rava asks: if Rav Ashi's principle is that only vessel-type intent is inconsequential, then removing a handful from a dry mincha for the sake of an oil mincha should NOT be valid — yet Rabbi Shimon rules it is. The dry vs. oil distinction is a difference in substance, not vessel type.
