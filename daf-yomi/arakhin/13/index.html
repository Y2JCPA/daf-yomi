⚖️ The Frankincense Piggul Debate

Rabbi Yosei
If a priest removes the handful intending to burn the frankincense the next day — the meal offering is unfit (pasul) but does NOT incur piggul liability. The frankincense is a permitting factor for itself, not for the meal offering.
Rabbis
We disagree — the offering IS rendered piggul! Just as intent during slaughter regarding altar portions renders an animal offering piggul, intent regarding frankincense renders the meal offering piggul.
Rabbi Yosei
In an animal offering, the altar portions permit the priests to eat — they are the permitting factor for the offering. But frankincense permits itself to be burned, not the meal offering. These are fundamentally different categories.
Reish Lakish
The same logic extends to the two bowls of frankincense of the shewbread — a permitting factor cannot render another permitting factor piggul. Intent on one bowl does not render the shewbread piggul.
Core Principle מַתִּיר אֵינוֹ מְחַיֵּיב מַתִּיר — A permitting factor does not render another permitting factor liable to piggul

🤔 Abaye's Logical Chain: Why the Mishna is Needed

Question: Abaye asks — if we can derive from a mishna elsewhere that intent to consume does not join with intent to burn, why do I need this mishna to repeat it?
↓
Suggestion 1: The mishna teaches that two half-olive-bulk consume-intents for the next day combine to create piggul.
↓
Rejected: This is derivable from animal offerings — no novel teaching is needed in a meal-offering mishna.
↓
Suggestion 2: The mishna teaches that consuming an item typically consumed + consuming an item not typically consumed do not join for piggul.
↓
Accepted: The mishna is needed for the case where one intends to consume half an olive-bulk and burn half an olive-bulk. Though each intent matches its item's typical manner, they still do not combine for piggul.

⚖️ Meal Offering vs. Animal Offering: Piggul Rules

Animal Offering

  • Slaughtered with intent to sacrifice altar portions the next day → piggul
  • The altar portions are the permitting factor allowing priests to eat the meat
  • Improper intent during slaughter — a single act — creates piggul for the entire offering
  • One set of altar portions serves as the permitting factor for one offering unit

Meal Offering

  • Handful removed with intent to eat remainder next day → piggul (all agree)
  • Handful removed with intent to burn handful itself next day → piggul (Rabbi Yosei concedes)
  • Handful removed with intent to burn frankincense next day → pasul only (Rabbi Yosei) vs. piggul (Rabbis)
  • Non-priest collecting frankincense: invalid and disqualifies the entire offering (Rabbi Yannai)

🙏 Non-Priest Collecting Frankincense: Sources and Rejection

Rabbi Yannai
The collection (קִבּוּץ) of the frankincense from a meal offering, when performed by a non-priest, is invalid and disqualifies the entire meal offering.
Rabbi Yirmeya
The reason: the verse states וְקָמַץ — 'and he shall take a handful' — just as handful-taking must be done by a priest, so too the collection of frankincense must be done by a priest.
Rav Mari
We can also derive this from the mishna (12a): The mishna renders improper intent during placing the handful into a vessel capable of creating piggul. This shows vessel-placement is a full-fledged priestly rite — and similarly, frankincense collection.
Gemara
Rejected: The vessel-placement creates piggul not because it parallels blood collection, but because it is indispensable — the handful cannot reach the altar without it. Both rites involve a service vessel sanctifying a permitting factor.
Conclusion קִבּוּץ הַלְּבוֹנָה — Frankincense collection is a priestly rite; non-priest performance disqualifies the offering

🍞 Shavuot Loaves: Does Piggul Intent Spread?

Priest slaughters the two Shavuot lambs with intent to eat one of the two accompanying loaves the next day
↓
Rabbis (Mishna)
Both loaves are rendered piggul — the two loaves form a single unit; intent on one affects both
Rabbi Yosei
Only the loaf for which there was improper intent is piggul — the other loaf is entirely unaffected
↓
Rav Huna elaborates Rabbi Yosei's position: Even intent regarding the right limb alone of a Shavuot lamb does not render the left limb piggul — analogous to ritual impurity, which in one limb does not spread to another
↓
Rav Naḥman objects from a baraita: The Rabbis require piggul intent regarding an olive-bulk from both loaves to incur karet — suggesting that under the Rabbis' view, both must be part of the intent for full liability
