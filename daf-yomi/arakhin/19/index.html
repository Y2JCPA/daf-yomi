⚖️ Rav's Principle: Law & Statute Signal Indispensability

A sacrificial rite of the meal offering is described in the Torah
↓
Does the verse use חוק (statute) or תורה (law)?
↓
If either term appears → the rite is indispensable to the offering's validity
↓
Omitting or invalidly performing that rite disqualifies the entire offering
↓
Exception: if the term refers to eating rather than the sacrificial act itself, the rule does not apply

📖 Does a Verse Refer to Both What Precedes and What Follows It?

Rabbis
"With his finger" (Leviticus 16) refers to BOTH the collection of blood AND its placement — the verse governs both the act before and after the mention of the finger.
Rabbi Shimon
The term 'hand' is not stated with regard to collection of blood — only for placement. A verse is interpreted by what precedes AND succeeds it only when both halakhot are textually connected.
Abaye
This is precisely where Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis disagree: whether a single verse simultaneously governs two separate halakhot based on its surrounding context.
Key Principle at Stake Can one verse govern the halakha that precedes it AND the halakha that follows it — or does it apply to only one direction?

🔤 Mnemonic Cases Challenging Rav's Principle (נ-ת-צ / י-ק-מ-ל)

Rav's Rule: Where Torah writes חוק (statute) or תורה (law), omitting the rite invalidates the offering
↓
Nazirite (נ)
Only law written — yet its offerings are indispensable. Supports Rav.
Thanks Offering — Toda (ת)
Only law written — yet its four bread types are indispensable. Supports Rav.
Yom Kippur (י)
Only statute written — yet the two goats must both be brought. Supports Rav.
↓
Resolution: Rav's rule means either term alone suffices — 'law AND statute' was rhetorical, not a requirement for both

🤝 Rav vs Shmuel: Are Repeated Rites Indispensable?

Rav

  • Every rite repeated in the Torah is repeated only to teach it is indispensable
  • "His handful" repeated → taking the handful by hand (not a utensil) is indispensable
  • Proof: Aaron's service on the eighth day of the Tabernacle consecration repeats this rite — establishing it for all generations
  • Repetition in Torah = signal of invalidating significance

Shmuel

  • Repeated rites are not necessarily indispensable based on repetition alone
  • The eighth-day verse is a temporary, one-time command — not applicable to all generations
  • One cannot derive an eternal halakha from a situational act
  • Exception: the offerings of the twelve princes — repeated 12 times — are sufficient to establish a permanent rule

🏛️ Where Is the Meal Offering Brought at the Altar?

לִפְנֵי ה׳ — Before the Lord | לִפְנֵי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ — In front of the altar
Placement Dispute: Southwest Corner The verse (Leviticus 6:7) states two phrases:
• "In front of the altar" — implies the southern side
• "Before the Lord" — implies the western side

Rabbi Eliezer: Both phrases must be fulfilled simultaneously — the southwestern corner satisfies both requirements at once. You cannot fulfill one and nullify the other.

Rav Ashi adds: Rabbi Eliezer holds the altar stood in the northern part of the Temple courtyard — making the altar's southwestern corner visible both 'before the Lord' (westward, toward the Holy of Holies) and 'in front of the altar' (southward).
