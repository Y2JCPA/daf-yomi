📜 How Must a Mezuza Be Written?

R. Shimon ben Elazar
Leave a space above and below the text, and write the passages in the open manner (petucha) — begin the second passage on the line following the end of the first.
Rav
The halakha follows R. Shimon ben Elazar — but only regarding the required spaces above and below, not necessarily the open manner of writing.
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
It is a mitzva ab initio to write the passages in the closed manner (setuma). If written in the open manner it is still permitted — b'dieved.
Abaye
But Rav holds one must follow established custom — proven from his ḥalitza ruling! Custom carries binding force and cannot be uprooted even by a new ruling.
Bottom Line L'chatchila write the mezuza in the closed manner with spaces above and below; b'dieved the open manner is valid. Rav's ruling on spaces follows R. Shimon ben Elazar.

🔄 Can Worn Tefillin Become a Mezuza?

Worn tefillin parchment — may it be repurposed as a mezuza? (Reducing sanctity: tefillin → mezuza)
↓
Supports Permitting
A Torah scroll (closed passages) may be converted — evidenced by the baraita on reducing sanctity levels downward
Complication
Baraita: changing between klaf and dokhsostos renders the item unfit — tefillin and mezuza use different parchment sides!
↓
Resolution: The 'change of material' prohibition applies to tefillin written on dokhsostos on the flesh side. A mezuza written on klaf from worn tefillin parchment remains valid.

📋 Writing Requirements: Tefillin vs. Mezuza vs. Torah Scroll

Tefillin

  • Written on klaf (hair side)
  • Scoring (sirtut): not required
  • Must copy from a written text
  • Passages: closed manner (setuma)

Mezuza

  • Written on klaf — dokhsostos is invalid
  • Scoring (sirtut): required — halakha l'Moshe miSinai
  • May be written from memory (no source text needed)
  • L'chatchila: closed; b'dieved open is valid

Torah Scroll

  • Written on klaf (hair side)
  • Scoring (sirtut): required
  • Must copy from a written text
  • Passages: closed manner — more stringent

🚪 What Makes a Mezuza Valid?

Material — Klaf: Written on the hair side of parchment. Using dokhsostos (flesh side) renders it unfit.
↓
Scoring (Sirtut): The parchment must have ruled lines scored into it — a binding halakha for mezuza specifically.
↓
Intent (Lishmah): Must be written for the sake of the mitzva. Writing it casually 'like a letter to a friend' renders it invalid.
↓
Passage style: L'chatchila in the closed manner (setuma); b'dieved the open manner (petucha) is permitted.
↓
Affixed to the doorpost: Must be attached to the post itself. Hanging on a stick in the entranceway is invalid — 'write them on your doorposts.'
↓
Placement — within the airspace: Must be inside the entrance, not on the exterior — fulfilling 'upon your gates.'

👟 Ḥalitza: Can Custom Ever Be Overridden?

Rav (Version 1, via Rav Ḥananel)
If Elijah says one may not perform ḥalitza with a shoe — the Sages listen. If he says one may not do it with a sandal — they do not listen, for we are already accustomed to using a sandal.
Rav (Version 2, via Rav Kahana)
If Elijah says one may not do ḥalitza with a sandal — they do not listen, because it is an established custom. We do not uproot what communities have accepted as binding practice.
Abaye
The practical difference: whether a shoe may be used ab initio. But both versions agree — Rav treats established minhag as binding, which informs how we understand his mezuza ruling.
Key Principle — מנהג Rav holds that established custom (minhag) carries binding halakhic force. Even prophetic guidance cannot override what a community has accepted — this principle applies across domains, from ḥalitza to mezuza writing.
