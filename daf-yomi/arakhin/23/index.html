⚖️ The Great Nullification Dispute: Rav Hisda vs. Rabbi Hanina

Rav Hisda
Follow the potentially nullifying substance (the larger quantity). If the larger quantity can attain the status of the smaller, the two are identical and the smaller is not nullified.
Rabbi Hanina
Follow the potentially nullified substance (the smaller quantity). Only if the smaller can attain the status of the larger is it considered the same type and not nullified.
Rav Hisda
Example: Carcass meat mixed into a majority of slaughtered meat — the slaughtered meat can become carcass (the animal could die), so the two are identical; carcass is not nullified.
Rabbi Hanina
Example: Slaughtered meat mixed into a majority of carcass — slaughtered meat cannot become carcass on its own, so it is nullified by the larger quantity.
📚 Foundation This dispute builds on Rabbi Yehuda's principle that a substance is not nullified by the same type of substance. Rav Hisda and Rabbi Hanina disagree on which party's potential to change determines whether the two are 'the same type.'

🫙 Oil in the Sinner's Handful — Fit or Unfit?

Rabbi Yochanan
If oil was added to the handful of a sinner's meal offering (which is forbidden from having oil), the offering is unfit — מנחת חוטא עם שמן פסולה.
Reish Lakish
The offering remains fit — the priest shall sacrifice it despite the added oil.
Rabbi Yochanan
Objection (baraita): A dry meal offering that became intermingled with an oil-mixed offering — Rabbi Yehuda says sacrifice it; the Rabbis say do not. This proves oil causes disqualification!
Reish Lakish
That baraita refers only to libation meal offerings for bulls or rams intermingled with each other — an entirely different case from the sinner's offering.
Rabbi Yochanan
Counter-objection: Another baraita teaches the libation case as a separate, independent halakha — proving the first baraita must refer to the sinner's offering after all!
📖 Leviticus 5:11 "He shall put no oil upon it, neither shall he put any frankincense" — the Gemara clarifies this verse forbids the owner from adding oil, not the priest; and it may speak to initial prohibition, not post-facto disqualification.

🔥 Rava's Dilemma: Contiguous Substances and the Altar

Rava's Case: The priest squeezed the handful's oil onto the wood of the altar first, then placed the handful itself on the wood to burn
↓
❓ Are substances contiguous to altar items halakhically considered part of those items?
↓
Rabbi Yochanan — YES
Contiguous substances are considered part of altar items. Proof: a bone adds to the olive-bulk measure of meat for liability when offered outside the Temple.
Reish Lakish — NO
Contiguous substances are not considered part of altar items. The bone does not complete the olive-bulk measure with the meat.
↓
Rav Ashi: The dilemma stands unresolved for both opinions — the circumstances of the bone/meat dispute differ from the oil/handful case. Each Amora may rule differently in Rava's exact scenario.

⚖️ Rav Hisda vs. Rabbi Hanina: Two Nullification Frameworks

Rav Hisda

  • Follow the larger quantity (potential nullifier)
  • If the majority can attain the status of the minority → same type → minority not nullified
  • Slaughtered meat (majority) can become carcass → carcass minority not nullified
  • Carcass meat (majority) cannot become slaughtered → slaughtered minority is nullified
  • Supported by: mishna case where the remainder can become a 'handful-not-yet-removed' offering

Rabbi Hanina

  • Follow the smaller quantity (potential nullified)
  • If the minority can attain the status of the majority → same type → minority not nullified
  • Carcass meat can be made kosher (slaughtered) → it can become slaughtered → not nullified
  • Slaughtered meat cannot become carcass → is nullified in carcass majority
  • Supported by: mishna case where the handful itself could become a remainder after ascending the altar

🧩 Rabbi Zeira's Verbal Analogy: The Torah Edict on Handfuls and Remainders

Term 1: The word burning (הַקְטָרָה) appears with respect to the handful (kometz) removed from the meal offering
↓
Term 2: The same word burning appears with respect to the burning of the Passover sacrifice remainder
↓
Gezerah Shavah (Verbal Analogy): The shared term creates a halakhic linkage between the two burning contexts
↓
Inference: Just as two handfuls mixed together do not nullify each other — both are burned — so too a handful does not nullify a remainder, and a remainder does not nullify a handful
↓
Result — Independent Torah Edict: Regardless of the Rav Hisda / Rabbi Hanina dispute, handfuls and remainders never nullify each other. This is a חוק מן התורה — a decree of the Torah itself.
