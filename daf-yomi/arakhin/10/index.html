⚖️ When Does a Rite Require the Right Hand?

Reish Lakish
Any place in Torah where an action is performed with a finger or by the priesthood — only the right hand may be used.
Rava
Either indicator alone (finger or priesthood) disqualifies the left — but only for rites that preclude atonement.
Abaye
Challenge: Conveyance of burnt-offering limbs to the ramp — priesthood is written yet both hands are permitted!
Rabbi Shimon
The right hand is mandatory only when both finger and priesthood appear in the verse together.
Daf's Central Question How many textual triggers — and what type of rite — are needed before the left hand is disqualified from Temple service?

📜 Why Both Leper-Oil Verses Are Needed

"Upon the blood" (Lev. 14:17)

  • Wealthy leper — oil placed on top of the guilt-offering blood
  • Teaches: oil is valid even while the blood is still present
  • Alone: I might think oil can only be placed when blood is there

"Upon the place of the blood" (Lev. 14:28)

  • Poor leper — oil placed where the blood once was
  • Teaches: oil is valid after the blood has been wiped away
  • Alone: I might think oil is placed only after blood is removed

🔗 Rava's Verbal Analogies from the Leper Passage

"Hand" (leper) → "Hand" (handful removal)
Derives that kemitza — removing the handful from a meal offering — requires the right hand
↓
"Foot" (leper, 14:17) → "Foot" (yavam)
Verbal analogy links leper purification to chalitza, the rite of the childless brother's widow
↓
"Ear" (leper, 14:17) → "Ear" (Canaanite slave)
Verbal analogy connects the leper's ear-anointing to the ear-boring rite of a permanent slave
↓
"Left hand" (poor leper) → Disqualifies priest's right hand
The extra mention of the left hand teaches a priest may not use his right hand for this rite — left is required

👆 Reish Lakish's Rule (as Clarified by Rava)

כל מקום שנאמר אצבע וכהונה — אינו כשר אלא בימין
Rabba bar bar Ḥana citing Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish Wherever Torah states that a rite is performed with a finger or by a priest, use of the right hand is mandatory and the left hand disqualifies. Rava's clarification: Either indicator alone suffices — but the rule is limited to rites that are indispensable to atonement. Rites that are not essential (e.g., conveying limbs to the altar ramp) are exempt even when priesthood is mentioned.
