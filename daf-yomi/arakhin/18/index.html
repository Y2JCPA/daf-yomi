🩸 What Did Rabbi Yehuda Teach Yosef the Babylonian?

Gemara Question
If Rabbi Yehuda taught that all agree an offering is unfit when one intends to leave the blood overnight — what was the point of Yosef's question to Rabbi Elazar? Why did the answer 'restore what he had lost'?
First Attempt
Perhaps Rabbi Yehuda taught Yosef that the Rabbis deem it fit and only Rabbi Eliezer deems it unfit — so Yosef was surprised when Rabbi Elazar ruled it fit as well?
Rejection
That cannot work — Rabbi Elazar said the ruling was only because Rabbis disagreed with Rabbi Eliezer, implying it was the lenient Rabbinic view. The pieces do not fit.
Resolution
Rabbi Yehuda did teach that all agree it is unfit. Yosef's 'lost thing' was simply that Rabbi Elazar confirmed the same ruling Rabbi Yehuda had given — restoring Yosef's confidence in his teacher's tradition.
Key Insight The Gemara resolves the tension by showing that Rabbi Elazar's confirmation was not a new ruling but a validation of what Yosef already knew — an act of scholarly solidarity across generations.

📖 Yosef the Babylonian: Three Questions, One Answer, and Tears

אַשְׁרֵיכֶם תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה חֲבִיבִין עֲלֵיכֶם בְּיוֹתֵר
Agadic Interlude — Love of Torah Yosef the Babylonian asked Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua the same halakhic question three times: once during the day, once that evening, and once the following morning. Each time Rabbi Elazar answered: "The offering is fit."

After the third time, Rabbi Elazar realized his repeated answer might have been wrong. His eyes streamed with tears as he declared:

"Happy are you, Torah scholars, for whom matters of Torah are exceedingly dear!"

Yosef replied: "My teacher, you have returned to me that which I had lost." — confirming the ruling and honoring his own teacher Rabbi Yehuda's original tradition.

👐 Who May Pour Oil on a Meal Offering?

Rabbis — Non-Priest May Pour

  • The verse says: 'He shall pour oil upon it… and bring it to Aaron's sons'
  • Pouring precedes bringing to the priests — it is a pre-priestly act
  • Therefore a non-priest may perform it without invalidating
  • Oil pouring is not listed among the exclusively priestly rites
  • Rabbi Zeira: mixing is a mitzva but not indispensable

Rabbi Shimon — Priest Required

  • 'Among the sons of Aaron' encompasses all rites entrusted to priests
  • Oil pouring is a rite of the meal offering and thus priestly
  • A priest who accepts non-priestly pouring as valid loses his share in priestly gifts
  • Baraita lists pouring, mixing, breaking, salting, waving — all priestly rites
  • Derived from the obligation incumbent on priests exclusively

⚖️ Rav Naḥman vs. Rava: Priests' vs. Israelites' Meal Offerings

Rav Naḥman
No contradiction between the mishna and baraita: the baraita (Rabbi Shimon) discusses a priest's meal offering, while the mishna discusses an Israelite's meal offering — different rules apply.
Rava
But the obligation to pour oil in a priest's meal offering is derived from the Israelite's — so they must share the same rules! You cannot distinguish them on this basis.
Rav Naḥman (alt.)
Then the distinction is: the mishna concerns offerings from which a handful is removed; the baraita concerns those from which it is not removed (wholly-consumed priestly offerings).
Rava (alt.)
Same problem — the obligation for the latter is derived from the former! The rule for pouring travels with the source text, so no distinction can be drawn.
Left Unresolved The Gemara records both versions of Rav Naḥman's attempted distinction and Rava's refutations, leaving the reconciliation of Rabbi Shimon's baraita with the mishna as an ongoing difficulty.

🩸 Piggul Intention: The Blood-Overnight Question

Priest performs blood presentation at the altar
↓
Intent: drink blood on the next day
Improper intention but not a standard act of service
Intent: leave blood overnight without presenting
Intention mirrors an actual disqualifying act (linah — overnight delay)
↓
Rabbis: FIT | Rabbi Eliezer: UNFIT
Rabbis: drinking blood is not a priestly rite, so the intent is irrelevant
All Agree: UNFIT
Leaving blood overnight is itself a disqualifying act — intending it = piggul-like invalidity
