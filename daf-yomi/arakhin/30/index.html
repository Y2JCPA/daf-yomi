✍️ Who Wrote the Last 8 Verses of the Torah?

Rabbi Yehuda
Joshua wrote the last 8 verses describing Moses' death. It is impossible that Moses himself wrote: 'And Moses died there' — a living person cannot write of his own death.
Rabbi Shimon
The Torah scroll cannot be missing even one letter! God commanded Moses: 'Put this Torah scroll by the Ark' — it was already complete. Therefore Moses wrote every word, including his own death.
Rabbi Yehuda (clarification)
For the rest of the Torah, God dictated and Moses repeated aloud as he wrote. For these final verses, God dictated and Moses wrote them — like any scribe recording what he is told.
Rabbi Shimon (clarification)
Until the death passage: God dictated and Moses repeated aloud. From the death passage onward: God dictated and Moses wrote in silence, with tears — a unique mode of prophetic writing.
Rav's Ruling (connected to this debate) The last 8 verses (Deut. 34:5–12) must be read by a single individual in the synagogue — they may not be divided between two readers, since they form a unified prophetic passage.

🕊️ Correcting an Omitted Divine Name

Scribe omits the Divine Name and writes the next word in its place by mistake
↓
Rabbi Yosei
Scrape off the mistaken word

Suspend the scraped letters above the line

Write the Divine Name in the correct place below
Rabbi Shimon Shezuri
May suspend the entire Divine Name above the line

But may NOT suspend only part of the Name — all or nothing
Rabbi Meir (via R. Shimon b. Elazar)
May write the Name neither suspended above the line nor in the lower margin — must redo the page entirely
↓
Final ruling — Rav Ḥananel citing Rav: The halakha follows suspending the Divine Name above the line (Rabbi Yosei's position)

📰 Torah Scroll Sheet: Column Rules

Minimum: 3 Columns

  • Every sheet must contain at least 3 columns
  • Fewer columns gives the appearance of a private notebook (pinkas) — undignified
  • Exception: The final sheet of the scroll may have even 1 verse or 1 column

Maximum: 8 Columns

  • No sheet may have more than 8 columns
  • Too many narrow columns gives the appearance of a missive/document — undignified
  • Width of each column must remain proportionally substantial

Splitting a 9-Column Sheet

  • DO: Split as 4 columns + 5 columns
  • DO NOT: Split as 3 + 6 — one sheet would be borderline
  • Both resulting sheets must independently meet the 3-column minimum

🛒 The Mitzva of Writing a Torah Scroll

הלוקח ספר תורה בשוק — כחוטף מצוה מן השוק
Rav's Teaching on Writing vs. Purchasing One who purchases a Torah scroll in the marketplace is likened to one who snatches a mitzva in the marketplace — hurried, transactional, and lacking the full spiritual merit of the act. The proper fulfillment of the mitzva is to write the Torah scroll oneself. One who merely corrects a single letter in an existing scroll is considered as if he wrote the entire Torah. This reflects the principle that the process of sacred labor carries intrinsic religious value beyond the finished product.
