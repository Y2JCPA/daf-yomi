Arakhin 30 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav, how must the last 8 verses of the Torah be read in the synagogue?
A. They may be divided between any number of readers
B. They must be read by a single individual without division
C. They are skipped during the weekly Torah reading
D. They must be read by the rabbi specifically
Correct: B — Rav rules that the last 8 verses (Deut. 34:5–12) must be read by a single individual and cannot be divided between two readers, since they form a unified passage.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does Rabbi Shimon explain Moses' writing of the final verses describing his death?
A. Moses had Joshua write them on his behalf
B. Moses dictated them aloud as God spoke, like all other verses
C. Moses wrote them in silence, with tears, after God dictated
D. Moses left blank spaces that God later filled in
Correct: C — Rabbi Shimon holds that for the final verses, Moses departed from his usual practice of repeating aloud and instead wrote in silence, with tears (בדמע), a unique mode of prophetic transmission.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
If a scribe acquires a parchment sheet with space for 9 columns, how must it be divided?
A. Into 3 columns and 6 columns
B. Into 4 columns and 5 columns
C. It must not be divided — use it as one sheet
D. Into 2 columns and 7 columns
Correct: B — The sheet must be divided into 4 and 5 columns. Splitting into 3 and 6 is forbidden because both sheets must independently have at least 3 columns.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the required size of the lower margin of a Torah scroll, and why is it larger than the others?
A. 3 finger-widths; to allow space for commentary
B. 2 finger-widths; standard boundary requirement
C. 1 handbreadth (tefaḥ); so the reader's arm does not rest on the writing
D. Half a handbreadth; to balance the upper margin
Correct: C — The lower margin must be one full handbreadth (tefaḥ) — the largest margin — specifically to prevent the reader from inadvertently resting his arm on the sacred text.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the halakhic ruling regarding one who purchases a Torah scroll rather than writing one?
A. It is preferable to purchase — scribal errors are reduced
B. It is equivalent in merit to writing one personally
C. It is like snatching a mitzva in the marketplace — the full merit is lost
D. Purchasing is forbidden; one must always write personally
Correct: C — Rav teaches that purchasing a Torah scroll is like snatching a mitzva — the act lacks the full dignity and merit of writing it oneself. Even correcting one letter is considered like writing the entire Torah.
