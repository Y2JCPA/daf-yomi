⚖️ The Central Dispute: Does 'Lo Ye'achel' Ban Benefit?

Ḥizkiya
Any verse saying lo ye'achel ('shall not be eaten') prohibits both eating AND deriving benefit — the passive form implies total prohibition.
Rabbi Abbahu
Lo ye'achel prohibits eating only. A separate verse is always required to extend the ban to benefit — every prohibition needs its own explicit source.
Ḥizkiya
Then why does the Torah add 'for you' (lachem) in certain verses? That phrase permits benefit — proving that without it, lo ye'achel would have banned benefit already.
Rabbi Abbahu
'For you' teaches other lessons in each case — ownership, identity of the owner, or contextual limits. Lo ye'achel alone never bans benefit without additional derivation.
The Practical Stakes This dispute governs whether one may sell or otherwise benefit from leavened bread, animal carcasses, creeping animals, and more — affecting major areas of practical halakha.

🐛 Sheratzim: The Creeping Animal Paradox

Gemara
Creeping animals (sheratzim): the Torah says 'it shall not be eaten' and also 'for you' — suggesting benefit is permitted. So selling them to gentiles should be allowed ab initio?
Mishna
No. The Mishna rules that one may not deliberately trap creeping animals for sale to gentiles — even if incidental benefit is technically permitted.
Ḥizkiya
According to my view: why does the Torah need both 'it shall not be eaten' AND 'for you'? The first bans benefit — the second carves out an exception. Why not simply write the exception alone?
Resolution
The redundancy teaches that even within permitted benefit, active pursuit (trapping specifically to sell) differs from incidental benefit. The Torah addresses both scenarios separately.
Nuance Where benefit is permitted, actively engineered benefit (like deliberately trapping sheratzim for commercial sale) may still be restricted — the Torah's dual language delineates passive from active benefit.

🍞 Chametz on Pesach: Rabbi Yosei HaGelili vs. The Rabbis

Rabbi Yosei HaGelili

  • 'Leavened bread shall not be eaten' — eating only is prohibited
  • Deriving benefit from chametz on Pesach is permitted
  • First 'with you' (imcha): you may not see your own chametz
  • Second 'with you': you may see chametz belonging to others
  • Selling chametz before Pesach: fully permitted under this view

The Rabbis

  • 'Lo ye'achel' prohibits both eating and benefit from chametz
  • Deriving benefit from chametz on Pesach is forbidden
  • First 'with you': you may not see your own chametz
  • Second 'with you': you may not maintain a gentile's chametz if he is under your control
  • The doubled phrase covers all chametz in one's domain

🧵 Gid HaNasheh & Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's General Principle

Foundation: Rabbi Yosei HaGelili proves benefit is permitted from chelev nevelah (fat of a carcass) — the Torah explicitly allows it with 'for you'
↓
A Fortiori (Kal Va-Chomer): Fat is forbidden to eat even from a living animal, yet its benefit is permitted from a carcass. The sciatic nerve (gid hanasheh) is only forbidden from a living animal — so its benefit should certainly be permitted!
↓
Rabbi Shimon's Refutation: Fat is unique — it was once permitted (before Sinai) and thus has a lenient foundation. The sciatic nerve has always been forbidden; the inference from fat does not apply
↓
Rabbi Yosei's Response: The a fortiori applies only to domestic animals (behema), where fat and sinew are comparable cases. Wild animals (chaya) operate under different rules
↓
Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's Master Principle: How do we know ALL Torah prohibitions carry a benefit ban? From the fact that nevelah requires an explicit permit — implying the default for every prohibition is: benefit banned
↓
Source for the General Rule: Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi follows Rabbi Yehuda, who derives it from 'you shall be sacred men unto Me, therefore you shall not eat flesh torn in the field' (Exodus 22:30) — sanctity implies total abstention
