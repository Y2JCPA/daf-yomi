⚖️ Rava's Rule: Rabbinic Decrees Upheld vs. Waived

Sages UPHELD Decree (karet notwithstanding)

  • Gentile convert on Erev Pesach (Beit Hillel) — cannot eat Korban Pesach
  • Sprinkling purification waters (haza'ah) — does not override Shabbat
  • Bringing circumcision scalpel through public domain on Shabbat — prohibited

Sages WAIVED Decree (positive mitzva wins)

  • Acute mourner (onen) — immerses and eats Korban Pesach; night-oneness is only rabbinic
  • Metzora on 8th-day purification — enters Temple despite tevul yom status
  • Beit haperas — examined by blowing dust; rabbinic impurity waived for pilgrims

✂️ Convert Who Converted on Erev Pesach: May He Eat?

Beit Shammai
He immerses and eats the Paschal lamb that evening — just as an uncircumcised Jew circumcised on Erev Pesach may do. Circumcision removes the disqualification; there is no further impediment.
Beit Hillel
One who separates from the foreskin is like one who separates from a grave — ritually impure. He must wait. Furthermore, we decree: next year he may contact a corpse before Pesach and reason, 'last year I was in a similar state and still ate' — a dangerous error.
R. Shimon ben Elazar
This dispute is only about the gentile convert. Regarding an uncircumcised Jew circumcised on Erev Pesach — Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did NOT disagree: all agree he immerses and eats that evening!
Rava
The Sages upheld their decree even when failing to do so incurs karet. Beit Hillel disqualify the convert despite this enormous stake — yet they themselves waived the rabbinic prohibition for the onen, where the night-mourning is only rabbinic.
Key Distinction Uncircumcised Jew vs. uncircumcised gentile convert: the Jew's circumcision removes a Torah disqualification; the convert's status triggers an additional rabbinic decree (גזירה) per Beit Hillel.

🗣️ Rav Naḥman vs. Rav Sheshet: Distant Traveler Who Arrives in Time

Rav Naḥman
If one was on a distant journey and others slaughtered and sprinkled on his behalf, then he arrived before eating — he does NOT need to observe Pesach Sheni. The mishna says 'did not observe,' implying he is only exempt if he had absolutely no part in the first Pesach.
Rav Sheshet
Look at the mishna's latter clause: it groups those who 'unwittingly forgot or were coerced' alongside the impure and distant-journey cases. The mishna must be teaching them together — and adds the intentional neglector who is liable for karet. 'Did not observe' cannot mean the traveler who had it done on his behalf is off the hook.
Rav Ashi
The mishna is precisely formulated: 'These are exempt from karet; those are liable.' This juxtaposition proves the mishna addresses those who fully failed to observe — proving Rav Sheshet's reading that intentional neglect is included alongside impure and distant-journey cases.
Rav Naḥman
I can still say: the mishna discusses only intentional neglect alongside impure and distant travelers — and the onen is a separate case the mishna should have mentioned but did not, for brevity. My reading of 'did not observe' stands.
Core Dispute Does "did not observe" in the mishna mean the person had zero involvement in the first Pesach — or simply that he missed it for any reason? The answer determines whether the distant traveler whose Pesach was performed by proxy still needs Pesach Sheni.

🌾 Halakha: Beit HaPeras Leniency for Pesach Pilgrims Beit haperas — a field plowed over a grave, scattering bone fragments — renders one impure by rabbinic decree. Normally one who passes through it is considered impure.

Special Leniency: Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel agree that we examine a beit haperas lying on the path to Jerusalem for Pesach pilgrims.

Practical Method (Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel): Before each step, one blows the dust on the path and inspects for bone fragments. If none are found underfoot, one may proceed and remain ritually pure.

Why waived? Beit haperas impurity is rabbinic in origin; the Sages chose not to let it block the positive mitzva with karet of the Korban Pesach when a practical workaround exists.
