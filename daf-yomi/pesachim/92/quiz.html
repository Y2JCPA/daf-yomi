QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Beit Hillel, why may a gentile convert circumcised on Erev Pesach NOT eat the Korban Pesach that evening?
A. He has not yet completed his conversion and is not yet Jewish
B. He is considered impure like one who left a grave, and there is a decree he may err after future corpse contact
C. Only a Kohen may eat the Korban Pesach on the day of his circumcision
D. He must wait thirty days before participating in any sacrificial meal
Correct: B — Beit Hillel give two reasons: (1) one who separates from the foreskin is likened to one separating from a grave — ritually impure; (2) a preventive decree that he might contact a corpse next year before Pesach and incorrectly reason from this year's experience.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
On which case do Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel AGREE, as confirmed by Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar?
A. A gentile convert circumcised on Erev Pesach may eat the Korban Pesach that evening
B. An uncircumcised Jew circumcised on Erev Pesach may immerse and eat the Korban Pesach that evening
C. Sprinkling purification waters overrides Shabbat when Erev Pesach falls on Shabbat
D. An acute mourner must abstain from the Korban Pesach even on Pesach night
Correct: B — Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar states explicitly in a baraita: Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not disagree about an uncircumcised Jew — all agree he may immerse and eat that evening. Their dispute is limited to the gentile convert.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Which of the following is one of Rava's cases where the Sages WAIVED their rabbinic decree despite the mitzva involving karet?
A. A gentile convert circumcised on Erev Pesach (per Beit Hillel)
B. Sprinkling purification waters on Shabbat when Erev Pesach falls on Shabbat
C. A metzora completing his purification on Pesach who enters the Temple courtyard
D. Bringing a circumcision scalpel through the public domain on Shabbat for a bris
Correct: C — The metzora on his 8th purification day is one of Rava's waived cases — the Sages allowed him to enter the Temple despite his tevul yom status because the positive mitzva with karet overrides the rabbinic restriction. The other three are cases where the Sages upheld their decrees.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does one traverse a beit haperas on the path to Jerusalem for Pesach while avoiding ritual impurity?
A. Walk only on large stones placed across the field
B. Recite a special formula of nullification before entering the field
C. Blow the dust before each step to inspect for bone fragments
D. Obtain explicit rabbinical dispensation and enter with closed footwear
Correct: C — Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel: one blows the dust on the path before taking each step. By exposing the ground, one can check for bone fragments. If none appear, one may step there and remain ritually pure — enabling Pesach pilgrims to proceed.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In the debate between Rav Naḥman and Rav Sheshet, what is the practical consequence if Rav Naḥman's view is accepted?
A. One who intentionally missed Pesach is also exempt from Pesach Sheni
B. One on a distant journey whose Korban Pesach was performed by others and who arrived in time to eat does not need to observe Pesach Sheni
C. An acute mourner is exempt from both the first and second Pesach
D. Sprinkling of purification waters must be performed before Pesach Sheni as well
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman reads 'did not observe' in the mishna strictly: if others performed the korban on the traveler's behalf and he arrived in time to eat, he had some participation in the first Pesach and is therefore not obligated in Pesach Sheni. Rav Sheshet disputes this reading.
