🥬 Competing Lists of Valid Maror Species

Mishna

  • Ḥazeret (lettuce)
  • Tamkha (chervil)
  • Ḥarḥavina (field eryngo)
  • Olashin (endives)
  • Maror (bitter herbs)

Bar Kappara

  • Endives
  • Chervil
  • Ḥarḥallin
  • Field eryngo
  • Ḥazeret

School of R. Samuel

  • Ḥazeret
  • Endives
  • Chervil
  • Field eryngo
  • Ḥarginnin
  • Hardofannin

✅ What Qualifies as a Valid Bitter Herb?

Candidate plant for Maror — מָרוֹר
↓
Is it a vegetable?
Not fish bile (kufya) — must resemble matza in origin
Fit for human consumption?
Excludes harzipu — a poison plant that kills animals
↓
Does it taste bitter?
Baraita: anything without the taste of bitter herbs cannot fulfill the obligation
↓
✅ Valid Maror
Ḥazeret, endives, chervil, field eryngo — fulfills the obligation
❌ Invalid
Boiled herbs, fully dried leaves, poisonous or non-edible plants

🌿 Which Bitter Herb Is Most Preferred?

Rav Aḥa b. Rava
I am searching specifically for merirata to use as my bitter herb — it seems to be the most intensely bitter plant available.
Ravina
Why search for merirata? If you seek the most bitter plant, look at what the mishna lists first — ḥazeret. Whatever is enumerated first is the most preferred.
Ravina
And the name ḥassa (חַסָּא) hints that God showed us mercy (חַס) — just like lettuce: bitter at the root, soft at the top. So too Egypt was harsh at first, then softened toward Israel at the end.
R. Yoḥanan (via R. Shmuel b. Naḥmani)
The Egyptians are compared to bitter herbs because they 'embittered' Israel's lives — yet like lettuce they ended soft, ultimately allowing Israel to leave.
Key Insight ḥazeret is preferred not just for its bitterness but for its symbolic resonance — the bittersweet arc of the Exodus narrative encoded in a single plant

🌱 Kilayim: May Bitter Herb Species Be Planted Together?

Rav's Ruling

  • All maror vegetables may be planted in one garden bed
  • They are vegetables, not seeds
  • Vegetable kilayim rules are less stringent
  • Confirmation: mishna, Bar Kappara, and school of Shmuel all call them 'vegetables'

Mishna Kilayim Context

  • 6×6 handbreadth bed: 4 species on four sides + 1 in middle
  • Spatial separation permits multiple species
  • Ḥazeret and ḥazeret gallin (wild) are not kilayim with each other
  • Wild and garden varieties count as the same species

Exception (R. Yosei b. R. Ḥanina)

  • A stalk of cabbage that has hardened…
  • …requires a beit rova of space — treated like a seed
  • Once hardened, its character shifts toward seed-like status
  • Supports Rav: normal bitter herbs are vegetables, not seeds

🫙 Second Mishna: Chametz Prevention Laws (39b)

Forbidden: Soaking coarse bran in water for chickens — contact with water may leaven the bran before it is used
↓
Permitted: Pouring boiling water onto bran — rapid scalding denatures the dough potential and prevents leavening
↓
R. Yosei b. R. Yehuda: Continuous dripping of water onto flour does NOT cause leavening — the ceaseless flow inhibits fermentation from taking hold
↓
Vatika pastry (school of R. Sheila): Permitted on Passover — prepared with a mixture of liquids that prevent the leavening process
↓
Mar Zutra warns: Do not line a pot edge with roasted flour — some grains may be insufficiently roasted and will leaven inside the hot pot
