QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why did Ulla retract his criticism of Babylonians and instead praise them for eating dates?
A. He learned the dates were actually imported from Israel and very expensive
B. He suffered indigestion from the dates and realized Babylonians learn Torah despite eating harmful food
C. He discovered that the dates had special medicinal properties that aided Torah study
D. He was told by the local scholars that dates were a required part of Shabbat observance
Correct: B — After eating a large basket of dates, Ulla suffered severe indigestion. He then praised Babylonians: even though the dates are like 'a basketful of lethal poison,' they somehow maintain Torah study on them — a testament to their dedication.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rabbi Elazar, which Patriarch's name for the Temple will be used in the messianic future?
A. Abraham — who called it 'The Mountain of the Lord'
B. Isaac — who called it 'The Field'
C. Jacob — who called it 'The House of God'
D. David — who called it 'The Palace of the King'
Correct: C — Isaiah 2:3 states 'the House of the God of Jacob' — specifically invoking Jacob's name. Rabbi Elazar derives that the Temple's final, messianic designation will be Jacob's term: בֵּית אֱלֹהִים, the House of God.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rava, what does a person's act of slaughtering their own Paschal lamb demonstrate?
A. That they have extra financial resources and can afford a second offering
B. That they forgot their master or father had already slaughtered one on their behalf
C. That they are protesting and do not wish to be included in their master's or father's lamb
D. That they are acting in error and their offering is invalid
Correct: C — Rava rules: when a person slaughters their own Paschal lamb, 'you have no greater protest than this.' The act itself demonstrates unambiguously that they intend to partake of their own offering, not the one slaughtered on their behalf.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In the case where a slave forgot which animal his master specified, AND the master also forgot — what is the outcome?
A. The master eats from whichever animal he prefers; the slave takes the other
B. Both animals are burned at the place of burning; neither fulfills the Paschal obligation
C. The lamb is always used as the default Paschal offering and the kid is a peace offering
D. The case is brought before a rabbinic court which determines the likely original intention
Correct: B — When both master and slave have forgotten the original specification, no stipulation can save the situation. Neither animal can be confidently designated as the valid Paschal offering, so both are sent to the place of burning. The obligation goes unfulfilled.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Regarding five people whose Paschal lamb hides were mixed up — a wart disqualifying one was found — when does Abaye say they are ALL exempt from the second Pesach?
A. Always, because the mixing of hides itself creates an unavoidable circumstance of duress
B. Only if they all brought their animals from the same flock
C. Only when the hides were mixed up after the blood of each animal had already been sprinkled on the altar
D. Only if none of them knew about the blemish at the time of slaughter
Correct: C — Abaye rules they are exempt only when the hides were mixed after sprinkling. At the moment each animal's blood was sprinkled, all five were still identifiable and valid — each person fully discharged their Paschal obligation at that moment. The subsequent mixing of hides is therefore irrelevant.
