⚖️ The Great Mixture Dispute

Rav Dimi
Permitted substance does NOT join together with prohibited substance to form the requisite olive-bulk measure — except for a nazirite.
Abaye
But what about teruma soup — a non-priest who eats it is flogged! Doesn't this prove permitted and prohibited DO join?
Rav Dimi
No — 'olive-bulk' in that mishna means one eats an actual olive-bulk of teruma within the eating-time. The non-sacred oil and garlic do not combine.
Abaye
If eating an olive-bulk of prohibited within the eating-time is Torah law, why are the Rabbis lenient about Babylonian kutaḥ on Passover? It clearly contains leaven!
Rav Dimi
The teruma soup flogging IS because permitted joins prohibited — but modern teruma is only rabbinic law since the exile. Hence leniency with kutaḥ.
Resolution Post-exile teruma applies only by rabbinic decree — this explains why the Rabbis are lenient about chametz mixtures that would otherwise trigger Torah-level liability

🔀 Does Permitted Substance Join Prohibited?

Question: Two foods mixed together — one permitted, one forbidden. Does the permitted food count toward the olive-bulk minimum?
↓
General Rule
NO — only the forbidden portion counts
Rav Dimi's position, applying across all Torah prohibitions
Nazirite Exception
YES — permitted bread + forbidden wine combine
Derived from the word 'soaked' in Numbers 6:3
↓
Practical result: A nazirite who soaks bread in wine and eats an olive-bulk of the combined mixture is liable — even though much of what he ate was bread (permitted)

🍷 Sources for Ta'am K'ikar — Who Uses What?

Rabbis
We derive ta'am k'ikar from the nazirite verse: 'he shall drink no vinegar of wine… nor shall he drink anything soaked in grapes.' The word 'soaked' teaches that absorbed taste is forbidden like the substance itself.
Rabbi Akiva
I derive ta'am k'ikar from the purging of Midianite vessels (Numbers 31). The Torah requires boiling out absorbed taste — proving taste carries the same legal force as actual substance.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
The nazirite source is too unique — permitted substance joins prohibited there. You cannot derive a general rule about taste from a case with its own special combinability logic.
Rabbi Akiva
And meat in milk cannot serve as the source — it is a novelty. Soaking meat in milk all day is permitted, yet brief cooking is forbidden. Novelties cannot generate general principles.
Core Dispute Both Rabbis and Rabbi Akiva accept ta'am k'ikar as law — they dispute only which verse is the valid derivational source and which source is too anomalous to generalize from

🍖 Why Meat in Milk Cannot Source Ta'am K'ikar

Normal Taste Logic

  • Soaking transfers taste = forbidden
  • Cooking transfers taste = forbidden
  • Long contact = short contact in effect
  • Consistent: same cause, same result

Meat in Milk — The Novelty

  • Soaking meat in milk all day → PERMITTED by Torah
  • Cooking meat briefly in milk → FORBIDDEN
  • Each ingredient alone = permitted
  • Cooking specifically triggers the prohibition

Why This Matters

  • A novelty (chiddush) cannot generate general rules
  • Rabbis and R. Akiva both reject it as a source
  • Rabbis turn to the nazirite verse instead
  • R. Akiva turns to Midianite vessels instead

👅 Ta'am K'ikar — The Foundational Principle

טַעַם כְּעִיקָּר — מִינָּהּ שָׁמְעִינַן לְכָל הַתּוֹרָה כֻּלָּהּ
טַעַם כְּעִיקָּר "The legal status of the taste of a forbidden food is like that of its substance itself."

Once derived — whether from the nazirite verse (Rabbis) or Midianite vessels (R. Akiva) — this principle applies across the entire Torah.

Just as with a nazirite: the prohibition against eating grapes extends to anything that absorbs grape taste — so too with every prohibition: any food that absorbs a forbidden taste becomes forbidden.

This became the cornerstone of all kashrus law: prohibiting foods cooked in non-kosher pots, meat absorbing milk flavor, and fish absorbing meat flavor.
