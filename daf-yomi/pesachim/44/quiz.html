QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Dimi, in which case does a permitted substance join together with a prohibited substance to form the requisite minimum measure?
A. Chametz on Passover
B. Teruma eaten by a non-priest
C. A nazirite who ate a mixture containing wine
D. Meat cooked together with milk
Correct: C — Rav Dimi rules that as a general principle, permitted substances do NOT join prohibited substances — with the nazirite as the sole exception. The Torah uses the word 'soaked' (Numbers 6:3) to indicate that even a mixture containing wine counts for a nazirite's liability.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does Rav Dimi ultimately explain why the Rabbis are lenient about Babylonian kutaḥ (a dip containing leaven) on Passover?
A. The leaven in kutaḥ is less than an olive-bulk
B. Kutaḥ is a rabbinic food category exempt from Passover
C. Teruma in the post-exile period applies only by rabbinic law, not Torah law
D. The flour in kutaḥ is not considered true chametz
Correct: C — Rav Dimi resolves the contradiction by noting that once the Jewish people were exiled from the Land of Israel, the laws of teruma apply only by rabbinic decree — not Torah law. This is why the Rabbis are lenient about chametz mixtures that would otherwise seem to trigger Torah-level punishment.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
From where do the Rabbis derive the principle of ta'am k'ikar — that the taste of a forbidden substance has the same legal status as the substance itself?
A. From the prohibition of meat cooked in milk
B. From the nazirite verse using the word 'soaked' (Numbers 6:3)
C. From the requirement to purge Midianite vessels (Numbers 31)
D. From the prohibition of chametz on Passover
Correct: B — The Rabbis derive ta'am k'ikar from the nazirite verse: 'he shall drink no vinegar of wine… nor shall he drink anything soaked in grapes.' The word 'soaked' teaches that absorbed taste is forbidden just like the substance itself — and from this case, the principle extends to the entire Torah.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why can the prohibition of meat cooked in milk NOT serve as the source for ta'am k'ikar?
A. Meat in milk is only a rabbinic prohibition
B. The verse about meat in milk is ambiguous and disputed
C. It is a novelty — soaking meat in milk all day is permitted, while even brief cooking is forbidden
D. Meat in milk has no minimum measure requirement
Correct: C — Both the Rabbis and Rabbi Akiva agree that meat in milk cannot source ta'am k'ikar because it is anomalous (a 'novelty'): normally, prolonged soaking transfers taste just as cooking does, yet the Torah permits soaking meat in milk all day while forbidding even brief cooking. We cannot derive general principles from cases that defy normal logic.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
From where does Rabbi Akiva derive the principle of ta'am k'ikar, and why do the Rabbis reject this source?
A. From chametz on Passover — Rabbis say Passover is too strict to generalize from
B. From the nazirite — Rabbis say the nazirite has unique combinability rules
C. From purging Midianite vessels — Rabbis say this is also a novelty since vessel-absorbed taste normally has no legal force
D. From teruma laws — Rabbis say teruma is only rabbinic today
Correct: C — Rabbi Akiva derives ta'am k'ikar from Numbers 31, where the Torah requires Israelites to purge absorbed taste from captured Midianite vessels. The Rabbis reject this because it is itself a novelty: under normal circumstances, taste absorbed into a vessel's walls has no legal significance ('pagum' — degraded). A novelty cannot generate general rules applicable across the Torah.
