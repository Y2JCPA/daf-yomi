🏙️ When Must You Follow Local Custom?

Person travels from one community to another
↓
Intends to return home
May maintain home customs — da'atei l'mihadar (Rav Ashi re: Rabba bar bar Ḥana)
Does NOT intend to return
Must adopt local stringencies fully — both the strict customs of home and of the new place
↓
Either way: do not visibly deviate from local practice — מִפְּנֵי הַמַּחֲלֹקֶת (due to conflict)

🚶 The Traveler's Two-Way Obligation

Mishna: One who travels between communities with different Passover-eve customs
↓
Lenient place → Strict place
Adopts local stringency: may not perform labor — even though it is permitted at home
Strict place → Lenient place
Retains home stringency: still may not perform labor — even though locals do
↓
Ruling: Both the stringencies of home and the stringencies of destination apply simultaneously

🏛️ Special City Customs — Local Decrees

City of Birei

  • Wide shoes (resembling slippers) are generally permitted on Shabbat
  • Birei community: custom to prohibit wearing wide shoes
  • Yehuda and Hillel, sons of Rabban Gamliel, observed this restriction there

City of Akko

  • Sitting on gentile merchandise-display stools permitted generally on Shabbat
  • Akko community: custom to prohibit (looks like commercial activity)
  • Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel personally observed this restriction in Akko

City of Kabul

  • Two brothers bathing together generally permitted
  • Kabul decreed: brothers may not bathe together
  • Concern: resembles bathing with sister's husband, which is prohibited by baraita

⚖️ Abaye vs. Rava — Which Clause Does 'Conflict' Explain?

Abaye
The principle 'do not deviate due to conflict' applies only to the first clause: one arriving at a strict place adopts the local stringency. That is where conflict arises — locals would object to someone performing labor they have forbidden.
Rava
The principle applies to both clauses. Even one who travels from a strict place to a lenient place may not perform labor openly — the strict traveler would create an appearance of laxity and friction among locals unaware of his origin.
Core Dispute If a traveler from a strict community visits a lenient community — is there any potential 'conflict' in performing labor? Does the prohibition against deviating apply even when traveling toward leniency?

🥩 Rabba bar bar Ḥana: Intent to Return

דַּעְתֵּיהּ לְמֶיהְדַר — his intent was to return
Case Study — Rav Ashi's Resolution Rabba bar bar Ḥana traveled from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia and ate the fat above the straight portion of the stomach — permitted in Eretz Yisrael but treated as forbidden in Babylonia.

Challenge: Doesn't the mishna require adopting local stringencies when traveling?

Rav Ashi's Answer: The requirement to adopt local custom applies only when one intends not to return. Since Rabba bar bar Ḥana intended to return to Eretz Yisrael, he was not obligated to adopt Babylonian stringencies.
