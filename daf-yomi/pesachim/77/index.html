⚖️ The Central Dispute: What Makes an Offering Valid When Impure?

Rabbi Eliezer
Blood alone atones — even if the meat became ritually impure or was lost entirely, one sprinkles the blood and fulfills the obligation. The verse 'the blood of your offerings shall be poured out' shows blood stands on its own.
Rabbi Yehoshua
Both blood AND meat must remain valid. If the meat is impure or lost, do not sprinkle the blood. The verse links them: 'you shall eat the flesh AND the blood shall be poured out.'
Rabbi Eliezer
The phrase 'you shall eat the flesh' teaches only that meat must be eaten through its proper means — not that the absence of meat invalidates the blood rite.
Rabbi Yehoshua
However, after the fact (בדיעבד): if the sacrificial limbs already went up onto the altar in a state of impurity, the frontplate (ציץ) appeases — they are not brought down. I distinguish לכתחילה from בדיעבד.
Driving Question Does the tzitz (frontplate) appease only for impure limbs that already ascended the altar, or also for the impurity of the blood itself? The answer determines whether the mishna follows Rabbi Yehoshua.

📜 Why Every Communal Offering Needs Its Own Derivation

Base Principle: Communal offerings override Shabbat and ritual impurity
Derived from: "These you shall sacrifice to the Lord in your appointed times" (Num. 28:2) — the word מועדים implies obligation regardless of circumstances
↓
Daily Offering (תמיד)
✔ Frequent — but no karet for neglect. Cannot derive from Paschal lamb.
Paschal Lamb (פסח)
✔ Karet for neglect — but not frequent. Cannot derive from daily offering.
Omer & Two Loaves
✔ Permit eating new grain/Shavuot bread — but are meal-offerings, not animal sacrifices. Cannot derive from either.
↓
Conclusion (Gemara): No single offering contains all stringencies of the others. Each derivation is indispensable — the Torah wrote them all explicitly.
Had only one been written, we could not extend the ruling to the others.

🏛️ Does the Tzitz Permit Impure Communal Offerings?

Rabbi Yehuda
Ritual impurity is wholly permitted for the public (טומאה הותרה בציבור). There is simply no prohibition when the community must bring offerings — the tzitz is irrelevant to this question.
Rabbi Shimon
The High Priest's frontplate on Yom Kippur proves otherwise — even when it is not on his forehead, it still appeases God for impure communal offerings. The tzitz mechanism is essential.
Rabbi Yehuda
Set aside Yom Kippur entirely! The tzitz there atones only for individual offerings brought in impurity — which are forbidden. For the public, impurity is simply permitted without needing tzitz atonement.
Gemara
All agree: the tzitz appeases for impure sacrificial limbs that went up onto the altar. The dispute is whether it also appeases for the impurity of the blood itself — and this determines compatibility with the mishna.
ציץ — The Golden Frontplate Engraved 'Holy to God' (קדש לה'), worn on the High Priest's forehead. Exod. 28:38: 'It shall be on Aaron's forehead and Aaron shall bear the iniquity of the holy things.' Its precise scope of atonement is the daf's hinge question.

🩸 Rabbi Eliezer vs. Rabbi Yehoshua: Essentials of a Valid Offering

Rabbi Eliezer

  • Blood is the primary vehicle of atonement — it stands alone
  • If meat became impure or was entirely lost → still sprinkle the blood
  • Verse: 'The blood of your offerings shall be poured out' — blood is listed independently
  • 'You shall eat the flesh' teaches only the manner of eating, not that meat loss invalidates
  • The tzitz appeases for impure limbs ascending the altar

Rabbi Yehoshua

  • Both blood AND meat must be valid — they are equally essential
  • If meat is impure or lost → do NOT sprinkle the blood (ab initio)
  • Verse links them: 'you shall eat the flesh… and the blood shall be poured out'
  • A fortiori: just as sacred meat cannot be eaten before sprinkling, blood cannot be sprinkled if meat is gone
  • After the fact (בדיעבד): tzitz atones if limbs already ascended in impurity

🔄 Gemara's Attempts to Reconcile the Mishna with Rabbi Yehoshua

Opening Tension: The mishna rules that certain impure communal offerings are valid (the tzitz atones). Rabbi Yehoshua requires both meat and blood to be valid — so how does the mishna fit his view?
↓
Attempt 1: The mishna follows Rabbi Yehuda, who holds impurity is wholly permitted for the public — not Rabbi Yehoshua at all.
Rejected: Rabbi Yosei explicitly confirms both Rabbi Eliezer AND Rabbi Yehoshua are correct in their respective domains, implying Yehoshua's view must also be representable in a mishna.
↓
Attempt 2: Rabbi Yehoshua holds the tzitz atones for both impure animal limbs and impure portions of meal-offerings (omer, two loaves).
Rejected: A baraita proves Rabbi Yehoshua requires both the handful (kometz) and the remainder to be valid — he doesn't exempt meal-offerings from the dual requirement.
↓
Attempt 3: Rabbi Yehoshua holds the tzitz atones broadly — for all portions that 'go up' to the altar AND for portions of meal-offerings that are processed.
Rejected: Another baraita shows Rabbi Yehoshua invalidates offerings when meat is lost — the term 'disqualified' implies even after the fact, not just ab initio.
↓
Final Resolution: Rabbi Yehoshua distinguishes לכתחילה (ab initio — both meat and blood must be valid before bringing) from בדיעבד (after the fact — once the offering is brought impure, the tzitz atones). The mishna addresses the after-the-fact case. Proven from a baraita that explicitly records his leniency post facto.

📋 Pesachim 77 — Summary Map

Multiple Torah derivations establish that communal offerings override Shabbat and impurity — no single offering can serve as the model for all
↓

Impurity wholly permitted (הותרה) — no need for tzitz mechanism

Tzitz atones for communal impurity — it is דחויה (pushed aside), not הותרה (permitted)
↓
Core dispute: If the meat is impure or lost, can one still sprinkle the blood?
Eliezer: Yes — blood atones alone. Yehoshua: No — both must be valid ab initio.

Final Resolution: Rabbi Yehoshua distinguishes לכתחילה (both required) from בדיעבד (tzitz atones for what already went up). The mishna addresses the after-the-fact scenario and is fully compatible with his view.
