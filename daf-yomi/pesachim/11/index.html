⚖️ When Does Rabbi Yehuda Issue a Decree?

Rava
Rabbi Yehuda only decrees against chametz — because people naturally distance themselves from it. For omer grain and oil, which are familiar, he does not decree.
Rav Ashi
No — the distinction is about the Jerusalem grain market: once grain entered the market as flour or toasted grain, it lost its new-grain identity and R. Yehuda's different ruling is explained that way.
Gemara
Rav Ashi's view is rejected: even after processing, new grain retains its identity since one can distinguish it by taste. His suggestion is a mistake.
Conclusion
Rava's principle stands: Rabbi Yehuda decrees only when the prohibited item is something people habitually avoid — chametz is the paradigm case.
Core Principle Rabbi Yehuda's gezeira logic is psychological: he only decrees when the prohibited substance is one that people naturally keep their distance from.

🔍 Rabbi Yehuda's Gezeira Logic (Rava's Principle)

Is the prohibited item one that people naturally distance themselves from?
✅ Yes — e.g., chametz
Rabbi Yehuda issues a decree
People are already vigilant; a protective fence reinforces that instinct
❌ No — e.g., new grain, oil
Rabbi Yehuda does not issue a decree
The item is familiar; an extra fence would be ignored anyway
↓
Exception — Firstborn Animal: R. Yehuda does decree despite general prohibition, because the owner's extreme agitation over the animal dying may drive him to act rashly

🍞 Three Opinions: Chametz on Erev Pesach

Rabbi Meir

  • Eat chametz: all of 5th hour
  • Burn chametz: start of 6th hour
  • Assumes people know exact time
  • No buffer zone — no intermediate stage

Rabbi Yehuda

  • Eat chametz: all of 4th hour
  • 5th hour: ⚠️ suspend (neither eat nor burn)
  • Burn chametz: start of 6th hour
  • Two disqualified Temple loaves as public indicator

Rabban Gamliel

  • Non-sacred food: entire 4th hour
  • Teruma: entire 5th hour (mitzva gets extra time)
  • Burn all chametz: start of 6th hour
  • Three-tier system — stringency tracks sanctity

🐄 Firstborn Animal: Let Blood or Not?

Rabbi Yehuda
One may let blood from a firstborn animal even where it does not cause a blemish — because the owner, seeing the animal near death, is emotionally agitated and may slaughter it prematurely without permission.
The Rabbis
One may let blood provided it does not create a blemish. If you prohibit all intervention, you actually push the owner to sin — so permit the safe option.
Gemara's Challenge
But doesn't Rabbi Yehuda permit scratching animals on Yom Tov? If he is so concerned about agitation over property, why no decree there?
Resolution
The firstborn case is unique: without action, the animal will die — total loss. That extreme, irreversible agitation warrants a special ruling. Ordinary Festival concern about an animal's discomfort does not reach that level.
Key Distinction Agitation over certain total loss (animal dying) differs from ordinary concern about property — only the former triggers Rabbi Yehuda's special decree logic.

🕐 Abaye: One Dispute Explains Two Halakhot

אמר אביי: כי מעיינת בה, משכחת לה — לדברי רבי מאיר אדם טועה לא מידי, לדברי רבי יהודה אדם טועה שעה אחת ומשהו
Abaye's Unification Rabbi Meir holds a person does not err in estimating time — hence witnesses who say 2nd hour vs. 5th hour are irreconcilable, and chametz may be eaten safely until the full 5th hour.

Rabbi Yehuda holds a person errs by one hour and a bit (alternative version: half an hour) — so witnesses two hours apart may still be reconciled, and the chametz deadline is moved earlier to build in a safety buffer against time-estimation error.

The same disagreement about human accuracy in telling time underlies both the testimony laws and the chametz deadlines.
