QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Gemara's resolution of the baraita, does Rabbi Meir permit completing labor on the fourteenth only for Festival-purpose labor, or for any labor?
A. Any labor begun before the 14th may be completed, regardless of Festival purpose
B. Only Festival-purpose labor begun before the 14th may be completed on the fourteenth
C. Rabbi Meir prohibits all labor on the 14th — no exceptions
D. Rabbi Meir permits only the three crafts: tailors, barbers, and launders
Correct: B — The baraita explicitly limits Rabbi Meir: 'With regard to any labor that is for the purpose of the Festival, one may complete it on the fourteenth.' The phrase 'for the purpose of the Festival' is the operative restriction.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the core dispute between Rav Huna and Rabbi Ami regarding restoring a runaway brooding hen?
A. Whether placing a hen on eggs constitutes prohibited labor on Erev Pesach
B. Whether eggs under a hen become muktzeh on Yom Tov
C. Rav Huna: only within 3 days (heat retained); Rabbi Ami: even after 3 days (prevent major loss)
D. Whether the owner must compensate his neighbors if the hen damages their property
Correct: C — Rav Huna holds restoration is only effective and permitted within three days while the hen's body heat persists. Rabbi Ami permits restoration even after three days, out of concern for the major financial loss of ruined eggs.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does the Gemara resolve the contradiction between the mishna (taking vessels to a craftsman permitted) and the baraita (taking vessels to a craftsman prohibited)?
A. The mishna is Rabbi Meir; the baraita is the Rabbis — a tannaitic dispute
B. The mishna refers to Judea and the baraita refers to Galilee
C. The mishna refers to the 14th of Nisan; the baraita refers to Chol HaMoed
D. There is no resolution — the Gemara leaves it as תיקו (unresolved)
Correct: C — The Gemara resolves: bringing FROM a craftsman refers to the 14th of Nisan (permitted). Taking TO a craftsman refers to Chol HaMoed, where only retrieving completed items is allowed — not bringing new work.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rabbi Yoḥanan explain the apparent contradiction between two mishnayot — one treating Erev Pesach labor as a mere custom and another treating it as a binding rule?
A. The first mishna reflects Rabbi Meir's view; the later mishna reflects Rabbi Yehuda's view
B. The first mishna refers to Galilee; the later mishna refers to Judea
C. The first mishna refers to Erev Pesach; the later mishna refers to Tisha B'Av
D. Rabbi Yoḥanan reverses the attribution of opinions in one of the sources
Correct: A — Rabbi Yoḥanan explains: the earlier mishna (treating it as a custom) reflects Rabbi Meir's lenient position, while the later mishna (treating it as a binding rule in Judea) reflects Rabbi Yehuda's stricter position.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Which three crafts does the baraita specifically permit to perform labor until midday on Passover eve, and what is the rationale?
A. Bakers, butchers, and wine merchants — they prepare food for the Festival
B. Potters, glassmakers, and dyers — they produce Festival vessels
C. Tailors, barbers, and launders — laypeople cannot do these tasks themselves and need them for the Festival
D. Scribes, shoemakers, and blacksmiths — their work cannot be interrupted once begun
Correct: C — The baraita specifies tailors (חַיָּטִין), barbers (סַפָּרִין), and launders (כּוֹבְסִין) because a person cannot easily perform these tasks on himself, and they are necessary for proper Festival preparation.
