⚖️ Deriving the Prohibition of Benefit from Torah Verses

Many Torah prohibitions use "lo yei'achel"
"It shall not be eaten" — does this include benefit, or only eating?
↓

🗣️ The Verse-by-Verse Battle: Abaye, Rav Pappa, and Ravina

Abaye
Derive the benefit prohibition from: "shall not be eaten; it shall be burnt" (Leviticus 6:23). The burning clause renders the eating clause free to teach that benefiting is also forbidden.
Rav Pappa
Rejected! That verse may be needed to designate a negative mitzva for the act of burning itself — so one who burns the sin-offering outside the Temple is flogged. You cannot use it for the benefit prohibition.
Rav Pappa
Instead, derive it from: "Flesh that touches any impure thing shall not be eaten; it shall be burnt with fire" (Lev. 7:19). That eating prohibition is derivable elsewhere, so the verse is free to teach the prohibition of benefit.
Ravina
But perhaps that verse teaches that eating impure consecrated meat violates two separate prohibitions — two sets of lashes! We have clear precedent from the ant and hornet cases of stacking multiple lashes from one act.
Core Tension Every proposed proof-verse is challenged as already serving another halakhic purpose. The Gemara must find a verse with no other function than teaching the benefit prohibition.

📏 Rabbi Yoḥanan's Usual Manner Rule

כָּל אִסּוּרִין שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה — אֵין לוֹקִין עֲלֵיהֶן אֶלָּא דֶּרֶךְ אֲכִילָתָן
הלכה כללית Rabbi Abbahu said in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan: For all prohibitions against eating in the Torah, one is flogged only if one eats the forbidden item in its usual manner of consumption.

The same applies to benefit: one is flogged only if benefit is derived in the usual manner. Example: placing the fat of a stoned ox (shor haniskal) on a wound for medicinal purposes — exempt, because this is not the usual way fat is used.

🌱 Usual Manner Rule vs. the Kilayei Kerem Exception

Standard Prohibited Items

  • Flogged only when benefit is taken in the usual manner
  • Unusual use (e.g., fat as medicine) → exempt from lashes
  • Rationale: the Torah's concern targets normal benefit
  • Applies to: forbidden fats, tereifa, orlah, and most other prohibitions

Kilayei Kerem (Vineyard Mixtures)

  • Flogged even for benefit taken in an unusual manner
  • Source: Deut. 22:9 — "pen tukdash" (lest you become entirely forfeited)
  • The word tukdash signals absolute, total forfeiture of the item
  • Any form of benefit — normal or not — incurs lashes

🥩 Meat Cooked in Milk — Building the Complete Prohibition

Step 1 — Isi ben Yehuda's comparison: In Deuteronomy 14, the Torah juxtaposes "you are a holy people" with the command not to cook a kid in its mother's milk — just as it does with the prohibition of tereifa. This creates a textual equation between the two.
↓
Step 2 — Eating prohibition established: Just as tereifa (unkosher meat) is forbidden to eat, so too is meat cooked in milk. This is derived through the literary parallel, not from an explicit verse about eating.
↓
Step 3 — Benefit prohibition via kal v'chomer: If tereifa — whose prohibition is in some ways more lenient — also prohibits deriving benefit, then meat cooked in milk should certainly prohibit benefit as well.
↓
Step 4 — Connection to the daf's main theme: This case exemplifies the Gemara's larger question: can a benefit prohibition be derived by inference (kal v'chomer) alone, or must there be a scriptural verse? The Gemara leaves this as the live question.
