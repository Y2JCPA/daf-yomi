QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why is a Paschal lamb roasted in a swept oven (embers remain but no active flame) considered invalid?
A. Sweeping the oven on Pesach violates the prohibition of labor
B. The Torah writes 'roasted in fire' twice, requiring active fire — residual heat without flame is excluded
C. A swept oven cannot reach a high enough temperature for valid roasting
D. Only open-flame pits were permitted for the Paschal lamb by rabbinic decree
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan taught (via Rav Ḥinnana the Elder and Rabbi Asi): the double 'roasted in fire' in the Torah teaches that active fire is required. A swept oven retains only embers without live flame and therefore does not meet the standard.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does Rav Aḥadvoi bar Ami resolve the apparent contradiction between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi (coals = fire) and the baraita that requires a derivation to include coal-burns under leprosy law?
A. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's ruling applies only to the Paschal lamb, not to leprosy
B. The baraita's derivation ('a burn, a burn') is needed for red-hot metal — wood coals that are still burning are obviously fire and need no derivation
C. Coal-burns were intentionally excluded from leprosy law as an extra stringency
D. The leprosy verse was written before the Paschal lamb law and does not reflect later halakhic development
Correct: B — The derivation from 'a burn, a burn' is necessary to include red-hot metal (which is heated by fire but is not fire itself). Red-hot wood coals, while still actively burning, are self-evidently fire and require no special inclusion.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to the Gemara's conclusion, why must the execution of a priest's daughter be carried out with molten lead rather than burning bundles of branches?
A. Molten lead is hotter and therefore more completely fulfills the 'fire' requirement
B. A gezerah shavah from Nadav and Avihu shows that 'burning' means the fire acts internally — i.e., molten lead poured down the throat
C. The Sanhedrin had no access to sufficient firewood during the Second Temple period
D. External burning would publicly disgrace the priestly family, which is forbidden
Correct: B — A verbal analogy (gezerah shavah) connects the 'burning' of the priest's daughter to the deaths of Nadav and Avihu, who were burned internally while their bodies remained intact. This requires an internal burning method — molten lead.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Abaye, what does the phrase גַּחֲלֵי אֵשׁ ('coals of fire') teach about what the High Priest brings into the Holy of Holies on Yom Kippur?
A. Either smoldering or flickering coals are acceptable — both are valid options
B. Only smoldering coals may be used — open flame is too dangerous in the Holy of Holies
C. The coals must be actively burning (flickering), not smoldering — and a pure disembodied flame without coal is also insufficient
D. The coals must be taken specifically from the outer altar, not from any other source
Correct: C — Abaye: 'coals' alone would imply smoldering; 'fire' alone would permit a pure flame. The combination 'coals of fire' requires actively flickering coals — neither smoldering coals nor a pure flame without coal suffices.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
If a Paschal lamb belonging to a group of Israelites was basted with teruma oil and is already fully roasted, what must be done before the group may eat it?
A. The entire lamb must be discarded since it absorbed forbidden teruma throughout
B. The outer layer of the lamb must be peeled off before eating
C. Nothing — the teruma oil becomes nullified through the roasting process
D. The lamb must be transferred to a priestly group who may eat teruma
Correct: B — The mishna rules: once the lamb is fully roasted and the teruma oil has been absorbed into the outer layer, Israelites (who may not eat teruma) must peel off the outer surface before consuming it.
