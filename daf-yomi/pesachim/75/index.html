🔥 What Counts as 'Roasted in Fire'?

Torah: "Roasted in fire" (Exodus 12:8–9)
Written twice — what does each instance teach?
↓
Swept oven (embers only)
Active flame gone after sweeping
Invalid — excluded by double 'in fire'
Active burning coals
Coals still glowing and burning
Valid — R. Yehuda HaNasi: coals = fire
Red-hot metal
Metal heated to glow, no flame
Invalid — radiating heat, not fire itself
↓
Rule: Only direct fire or actively burning coals qualify as 'fire'
Residual heat and secondary sources are excluded

🏺 The Orla Oven Dilemma

Rav Ḥinnana bar Idi
If an oven was fired using peels of orla fruit, and one baked bread in it — is the bread forbidden?
Rav Adda bar Ahava
I have no ruling — bring a parallel case.
Rav Ḥinnana bar Idi
Rabbi Yoḥanan ruled: a swept oven (embers only) — a Paschal lamb roasted in it is invalid, since the Torah writes 'roasted in fire' twice. So indirect heat is not 'fire.'
Rav Adda bar Ahava
That verse teaches a universal principle — only direct fire is 'roasting' in all halakha. From it we learn the orla bread is forbidden.
Rav Ḥinnana bar Idi
Or — the swept oven ruling is unique to the Paschal lamb. Without the double 'in fire,' even swept-oven heat would have been valid. So the orla bread might actually be permitted.
Core Issue Does forbidden fuel (orla) transfer its prohibition to food baked in the oven? The answer depends on whether secondary heat carries 'forbidden benefit.'

⚗️ Are Coals 'Fire'? — The Contradiction Resolved

R. Yehuda HaNasi:
Coals = fire for Paschal lamb
Cutting and placing on coals is valid roasting
↓
❓ Contradiction from leprosy laws:
"A burn from fire" — word burn written twice to include coal-burns
If coals were obviously fire, why derive it?
↓
Rav Aḥadvoi bar Ami
Burning wood coals = obviously fire
Derivation needed only for red-hot metal (not inherently fire)
Ravina
No contradiction —
contexts differ fundamentally; Paschal lamb and leprosy rules operate independently
↓
✅ Conclusion: Burning coals = fire
Red-hot metal and swept-oven embers do not qualify

⚖️ Execution of a Priest's Daughter — Which Method?

Proposed: Burning with Branches

  • "In fire she shall be burned" — includes all fire-based burning
  • Surround her with bundles of branches and burn her?
  • ❌ Rejected via gezerah shavah (verbal analogy)
  • Analogy to Nadav and Avihu: fire burned them internally, not externally
  • Requires internal burning → molten lead

Proposed: Boiling Water

  • Fire heated the water — indirect fire-death?
  • ❌ Rejected by Rav Naḥman
  • "Love your fellow as yourself" (Lev. 19:18)
  • Choose the most merciful death available
  • Boiling water is more agonizing than instant molten lead

✅ Ruling: Molten Lead

  • Molten lead poured down the throat
  • Fulfills 'in fire she shall be burned'
  • Source: sons of Aaron — fire entered their bodies
  • Most merciful qualifying method
  • Confirmed by both verbal analogy and Rav Naḥman's principle

🫙 Mishna: Paschal Lamb — Contact, Drips, and Teruma Oil

Lamb touched the oven wall?
Peel off that spot — it was cooked by indirect radiant heat from the earthenware, not direct fire.
↓
Gravy dripped onto the oven floor and splashed back?
Peel the area that the gravy re-touched. If the entire lamb fell into the oven, peel its entire outer surface.
↓
Basted with teruma oil — who is in the group?
↓
👨‍✡️ Group of priests: May eat it as-is. Priests are permitted to eat teruma oil and no peeling is required.
↓
👨‍👩‍👧 Group of Israelites — lamb still raw: Wash off the teruma oil before roasting. Israelites may not eat teruma.
↓
👨‍👩‍👧 Group of Israelites — lamb already roasted: Peel off the outer layer before eating, as it has absorbed the forbidden teruma oil.
