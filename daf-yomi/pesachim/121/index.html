💧 Sprinkling vs Pouring: One Mitzvah or Two?

Rabbi Yishmael
Sprinkling is included in the general category of pouring. They are one unified mitzvah — the blessing over the Paschal lamb therefore exempts the Festival offering blessing as well.
Rabbi Akiva
Sprinkling and pouring are two entirely separate mitzvot. Neither is subsumed within the other — each offering demands its own distinct blessing.
Practical Ruling According to Rabbi Yishmael, one blessing for the Paschal lamb suffices for both. According to Rabbi Akiva, a separate blessing must be recited for the Festival offering.

🥩 Meat Impurity: Olive-bulk or Egg-bulk?

Rav Huna
An olive-bulk (k'zayit) of sacrificial meat is sufficient to contract ritual impurity — consistent with the standard halachic measure used throughout Torah law.
Rav Ḥisda
Only an egg-bulk (k'beitza) of meat conveys ritual impurity — a larger measure is specifically required in this context of sacrificial flesh.
Context This dispute governs the minimum quantity of sacrificial meat through which one becomes ritually impure by contact or carrying — with significant practical consequences for Temple purity.

⚠️ Piggul and the Decree Against Priestly Enmity

פִּגּוּל — an offering invalidated by improper intent regarding time of consumption
Rabbinic Protective Decree The Sages feared that a priest harboring enmity toward an offering's owner might deliberately intend invalid timing during the service — thereby rendering the sacrifice piggul (forbidden, invalid). To deter such corruption, the Sages instituted a protective measure ensuring that personal animosity could not weaponize the Temple service against worshippers.

Rabbi Yishmael

  • Sprinkling is subsumed under pouring

  • One blessing covers the Paschal lamb and Festival offering
  • Blessing for Paschal lamb exempts reciting a second blessing

Rabbi Akiva

  • Sprinkling and pouring are two distinct mitzvot
  • Neither act is included within the other
  • Each offering requires its own blessing
  • Paschal lamb blessing does NOT exempt the Festival offering

👶 Pidyon HaBen — Who Recites Sheheḥiyanu?

Rabbi Simlai attended a pidyon haben (redemption of the firstborn son) and a halachic dilemma arose among those present
↓
The question: Who recites sheheḥiyanu — the father fulfilling the mitzvah, or the priest receiving five sela?
↓
Case for the priest: He benefits financially by receiving five sela coins — and sheheḥiyanu is normally recited by whoever derives the benefit
↓
Case for the father: He is the one performing the mitzvah and redeeming his son — the primary obligated party should pronounce the blessing of gratitude
↓
Broader principle at stake: Does sheheḥiyanu follow financial benefit or mitzvah obligation?
