📖 Interruptions, Kiddush & the Bundle Problem

Rabbi Yehuda
If diners left for shul and returned: foods that require an after-blessing in their place (e.g., bread) need no new blessing. Foods without such a required after-blessing do require a new blessing.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
A change of location never obligates a new blessing upon returning — regardless of what food is being eaten. The original blessing always covers resumed eating.
Rabbi Eliezer
When Shabbat is sanctified mid-meal: remove the table, recite kiddush over one cup, then return to eating.
Rabbi Yosei
Finish the meal first, then bring two cups — one for Grace after Meals, and a separate cup for kiddush. One may not bundle two sanctifications over a single cup.
Key Rule (Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak) מִצְווֹת חֲבִילוֹת חֲבִילוֹת — Mitzvot must not be performed in bundles. Each mitzva deserves its own dedicated cup and recitation.

🚪 Does Leaving the Meal Require a New Blessing on Return?

Baraita (Rabbi Yehuda)
Friends left for synagogue/study hall and returned: foods that require a blessing after them in their place (like bread) need no new blessing on return — the original blessing holds.
Gemara's Inference
By implication: foods that do not require an after-blessing in place (like wine or fruit) DO require a new blessing — their departure fully uprooted the meal context.
Rabbi Yoḥanan's Rebuttal
This baraita does not refute me. Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning was that the original blessing was conditional on returning — it was cast broadly to cover the resumed meal regardless of food type.
Rav Ḥisda (Baraita)
Friends drinking wine: if they moved and returned to their original places → no new blessing. If they returned to new places → a new blessing is required.
Rav Ḥisda's Ruling The deciding factor is the location of return: original seat = no new blessing; new seat = new blessing required.

🥂 When Can Two Mitzvot Share One Cup?

Birkat HaMazon + Kiddush — Two Cups Required

  • Grace after Meals and Kiddush are two entirely different matters
  • Birkat HaMazon concludes the meal; Kiddush sanctifies the day
  • Bundling them on one cup violates: מִצְווֹת חֲבִילוֹת חֲבִילוֹת
  • Rabbi Yosei: finish meal, then use two separate cups
  • Each mitzva must receive individual honor

Havdala + Kiddush — One Cup Permitted

  • Havdala and Kiddush are one matter — both mark and distinguish the sanctity of sacred days
  • When Yom Tov follows Shabbat, both can be recited over a single cup
  • This is the basis of the YaKNeHaZ formula
  • No violation of 'bundling' since they share a unified theme
  • Conceptually: the transition from Shabbat to Yom Tov is a single sanctity-event

Havdala on Motzei Shabbat — Standard Order

  • 1. Wine (יַיִן) — blessing over the cup
  • 2. Candle/Light (נֵר) — blessing over the flame
  • 3. Spices (בְּשָׂמִים) — blessing over fragrance
  • 4. Havdala (הַבְדָּלָה) — separation blessing
  • Multiple elements but one unified act of havdala — not bundling

✡️ YaKNeHaZ (יקנה"ז) — Festival Night Following Shabbat

י — Yayin (יַיִן): Recite the blessing Borei peri ha-gafen over wine — wine always opens
↓
ק — Kiddush (קִדּוּשׁ): Sanctify the Yom Tov — the primary mitzva of the evening
↓
נ — Ner (נֵר): Blessing over the flame — Borei me'orei ha-eish — havdala component acknowledging Shabbat's end
↓
ה — Havdala (הַבְדָּלָה): Distinguish between Shabbat and Yom Tov — the transition blessing
↓
ז — Zman (זְמַן): Sheheḥiyanu — Who has given us life, sustained us, and brought us to this time — recited on first night of Festival
↓
Key Gemara inference: Sheheḥiyanu is only recited here because this is the first night of a Festival — proving we deal with a seven-day holiday (Pesach/Sukkot). On the second night of a two-day diaspora Yom Tov, this formula applies with a separate sheheḥiyanu question.
