⚖️ Core Dispute: Erring While Performing a Mitzva

Rabbi Eliezer
One who errs while intending to perform a mitzva is liable to bring a sin-offering — even if the error was reasonable. The Paschal lamb overrides Shabbat, yet slaughtering it for a different purpose incurs liability.
Rabbi Yehoshua
One who errs while intending to perform a mitzva is exempt — the mitzva context fundamentally changes the nature of the act. Intentional uprooting of designation and mitzva-error are two entirely different cases.
The Underlying Question When one violates Shabbat while genuinely trying to perform a commandment — does the mitzva intent provide any legal protection, or is the Shabbat violation fully actionable?

⚔️ The A Fortiori Exchange: Offerings & The Limit

Rabbi Eliezer
The Paschal lamb overrides Shabbat for its own purpose, yet one who slaughters it for a different purpose is liable. How much more so communal offerings — which also override Shabbat — render one liable for slaughtering beyond their fixed number!
Rabbi Yehoshua
The cases are incomparable! Your first example involves intentionally uprooting the designation — mine involves erring while pursuing a mitzva. Communal offerings have a limit, so one who exceeds it with mitzva-intent is still liable — but that is a special case.
Rabbi Eliezer
Communal offerings do have a fixed limit, and even mitzva-intent does not exempt one who exceeds it. Wherever there is a limit, one who errs is liable — regardless of mitzva motivation.
Rabbi Yehoshua
Circumcision on Shabbat also has a limit — only the baby whose eighth day falls on Shabbat. Yet if one circumcised the wrong baby on Shabbat, I exempt him! A fixed limit alone does not automatically impose liability.
Rabbi Ami's Resolution Rabbi Yehoshua only exempts the circumciser when the Shabbat-baby still needs circumcision — i.e., Shabbat still stands to be overridden. If the Shabbat-baby was already circumcised first, both agree the actor is liable.

✂️ The Two-Baby Circumcision Problem

Baby B → circumcise on Shabbat

Baby B (Shabbat-baby) still needs circumcision → Shabbat still stands to be overridden
Scenario 2: Circumcised Baby B on Shabbat eve first (error)
Baby A still needs circumcision, but no Shabbat-circumcision remains → Shabbat no longer stands to be overridden

Scenario 1 Ruling
R. Eliezer: Liable
R. Yehoshua: Exempt — acted in context of genuine Shabbat-mitzva

All agree: Liable
No mitzva context remains — the act was pure Shabbat violation

🐄 Rabbi Meir's Exemptions: How Far Does It Reach?

Exempt (Rabbi Meir)

  • Slaughtered a fit offering for Paschal offering — resembles the korban pesach
  • Slaughtered a calf of a peace-offering for Paschal — even though calf ≠ lamb (Rav Beivai bar Abaye / R. Elazar)
  • Slaughtered an unconsecrated animal for Paschal — if preoccupied with mitzva (Rav Naḥman)
  • Reason A: Could be confused with a valid offering
  • Reason B: Actor was preoccupied with performing a korban

Liable (Rabbi Meir concedes)

  • Slaughtered a blemished animal on Shabbat — never sacrificed as an offering (R. Yoḥanan)
  • Unconsecrated animals are never sacrificed — similar in principle to blemished
  • Neither reason applies: animal cannot be confused AND actor not mitzva-preoccupied
  • Rav Naḥman: Both reasons operate independently — only neither triggers liability

📜 Whose Opinion Does Rabbi Yoḥanan Follow?

The Question: Rabbi Yoḥanan exempts one who errs while performing a mitzva (e.g., yibum). Which Tanna's position does this reflect?
↓
❌ Not Rabbi Yosei — Rabbi Yosei exempts one who picks up a lulav on Shabbat by mistake, but that case involves being pressed for time (lulav applies only one day/year). A unique situational factor, not a broad principle.
↓
❌ Not Rabbi Yehoshua (offerings) — Rabbi Yehoshua exempts mitzva-errors for communal offerings, but this is specifically tied to the Paschal/communal offering framework, not a universal leniency.
↓
❌ Not Rabbi Yehoshua (circumcision) — Exemption only applies when Shabbat still stands to be overridden. Too narrow and contextual to account for Rabbi Yoḥanan's yibum ruling.
↓
✅ Rabbi Yehoshua (teruma) — A disqualified priest who ate teruma need not repay its value. Teruma is avodah (sacred service), and the Torah validates service performed by one of priestly lineage. Rabbi Yoḥanan's reasoning: one acting within a mitzva framework, even if technically disqualified or mistaken, retains a degree of validity.
