Pesachim 72 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Abbahu, the first clause of the mishna (one who slaughters the Paschal lamb for a different purpose) refers to which type of actor?
A. One who erroneously thought it was a different type of offering
B. One who intentionally uprooted the animal's designation
C. One who forgot entirely what day it was
D. One who acted under communal pressure
Correct: B — Rabbi Abbahu explains that the first clause deals with one who intentionally uprooted the animal's designation, while the latter clause deals with one who erred while intending to perform a mitzva. This resolves the apparent contradiction between the two clauses of the mishna.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In the two-baby circumcision case, Rabbi Ami explains that Rabbi Yehoshua only exempts the circumciser when:
A. Both babies had already been circumcised before Shabbat
B. Shabbat still 'stands to be overridden' — the Shabbat-baby still needs circumcision
C. The circumciser acted in complete ignorance of which baby was which
D. Only one baby was supposed to be circumcised on Shabbat
Correct: B — Rabbi Ami clarifies that Rabbi Yehoshua's exemption applies only when the Shabbat-circumcision-baby still needs to be circumcised — i.e., Shabbat still 'stands to be overridden.' If the Shabbat-baby was already circumcised on Shabbat eve (first scenario of the Rabbis of R. Yannai), all agree the actor is liable.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Naḥman's reconciliation, what are Rabbi Meir's two independent reasons for exempting someone who slaughtered for the Paschal offering?
A. The animal was consecrated, and the slaughter was performed in the Temple courtyard
B. The actor was preoccupied with performing a mitzva, OR the animal could be confused with a valid offering
C. The animal was fit for sacrifice, and Shabbat had not yet ended
D. The actor intended purely for Heaven, and no damage was caused to consecrated property
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman reconciles seemingly contradictory rulings: Rabbi Meir exempts if EITHER (a) the actor was preoccupied with performing a mitzva (even if the animal cannot be confused with the correct one), OR (b) the animal could be confused with a valid offering. Only when neither applies — such as a blemished animal — does Rabbi Meir concede liability.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Rabbi Yoḥanan rules that one who had intercourse with his sister-in-law (in a yibum context) and she turned out not to be niddah is:
A. Liable for a double sin-offering because yibum is rabbinically discouraged
B. Exempt from a sin-offering because he was performing the mitzva of yibum (levirate marriage)
C. Liable because conjugal intent alone does not create a mitzva exemption
D. Exempt only if the act was completed before witnesses informed him
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan exempts him because he was performing the mitzva of yibum. Since he was actively engaged in a mitzva and erred — the woman turned out not to be niddah — no sin was committed at all, and there is no basis for liability.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
The Gemara ultimately suggests Rabbi Yoḥanan's ruling on mitzva-errors aligns most closely with which case?
A. Rabbi Yosei's lulav ruling — pressed for time due to once-a-year nature of the mitzva
B. Rabbi Yehoshua's circumcision ruling — Shabbat still standing to be overridden
C. Rabbi Yehoshua's teruma ruling — a disqualified priest who ate teruma need not repay
D. Rabbi Eliezer's communal offering ruling — a fixed limit always imposes liability
Correct: C — The Gemara concludes Rabbi Yoḥanan's position best aligns with Rabbi Yehoshua's ruling on teruma: a disqualified priest who ate teruma need not repay the principal, because teruma is considered 'sacred service' and the Torah validates service performed by one of priestly lineage even when technically disqualified. This broad mitzva-validity principle underpins Rabbi Yoḥanan's leniency.
