🌙 First Pesach vs. Second Pesach (Pesach Sheni)

First Pesach (14 Nisan)

  • Prohibited to own leavened bread (bal yera'eh)
  • Hallel recited while eating
  • Overrides Shabbat
  • Overrides communal ritual impurity
  • Leavened bread prohibited in entire domain

Second Pesach (14 Iyar)

  • Leavened bread permitted alongside it
  • Hallel NOT recited while eating
  • Also overrides Shabbat
  • Also overrides impurity (per R. Yehuda)
  • Eaten roasted with matzah and bitter herbs

⚖️ Deriving Pesach Sheni Laws: Rabbis vs. Isi ben Yehuda

Rabbis
"They shall perform it" (Numbers 9:12) limits the verse to mitzvot on the body of the offering. But since the verse adds "with matzah and bitter herbs," apparently it is not specific — so mitzvot not related to the body also apply!
Isi ben Yehuda
"They shall perform it" already limits to body-related mitzvot — no extra derivation needed. The phrase "nor break a bone in it" is then free to teach that breaking bones is prohibited even for bones without marrow.
Rabbis
We use "They shall perform it" differently: it teaches that the Paschal lamb on Pesach Sheni is NOT slaughtered on behalf of groups that include ritually impure members mixed with pure ones.
Point of Agreement Both Rabbis and Isi ben Yehuda agree that matzah and maror apply on Pesach Sheni — they only dispute the derivation and what residual teaching remains.

🎵 Hallel During Preparation — Why?

מניין לפסח שני שטעון הלל בשעת עשייתו — מתוך שישראל עוסקין במצוה, שרויין בשמחה
Gemara Explanation The Gemara asks: why must Hallel be recited while preparing the Paschal lamb on Pesach Sheni? Because when Israel is engaged in performing a mitzva, they are in a state of joy — and Hallel is an expression of that sacred joy during the act itself. During eating, however, Hallel is only obligatory on the first Pesach, whose meal is the direct celebration of the Exodus from Egypt. The second Pesach lacks this primary commemorative dimension.

💀 Communal Impurity: Who May Eat the Paschal Lamb?

Scenario: The majority of Israel are ritually impure from contact with a corpse (tum'at met) on 14 Nisan
↓
Result: The Paschal lamb is slaughtered in a state of communal impurity — the offering proceeds for the entire nation
↓
Who may eat? Those impure from tum'at met — yes, they may eat
Zavim, zavot, niddot, yotzet leida — no, they may NOT eat
↓
Why the distinction? The communal dispensation was triggered by corpse-impurity; it only extends to that same category — not to zav-level impurity which is a separate and more severe status
↓
Rabbi Eliezer: Even if zavim or lepers unlawfully push into the courtyard, they are exempt from karet — because the general permission granted for corpse-impure people provides a conceptual cover
↓
Rav Yosef's Question / Rava's Answer: If corpse-impure people push into the Sanctuary itself — Rava: "from the camp" (Numbers 5:2) implies the rule applies even to part of the camp; the dispensation does not extend inside the Sanctuary
