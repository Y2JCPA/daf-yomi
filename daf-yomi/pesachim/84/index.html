🦴 Two Mishnas: Edible Parts & Bone-Breaking

אֵין שׁוֹבְרִין בּוֹ אֶת הָעֶצֶם
Daf Overview Pesachim 84 contains two mishnas: first, which parts edible in an adult ox (through cooking) may also be eaten in a young kid (through roasting) — the Gemara concludes: ends of ribs and cartilage; second, breaking a bone of a pure Paschal lamb incurs 40 lashes, while the same act on an impure offering does not. The Gemara spends the bulk of the daf debating exactly which disqualifying factors remove the prohibition, with seven amoraim each proposing a different edge case.

🐄 Edible Parts: Adult Ox vs. Young Kid

Adult Ox

  • Parts edible through cooking set the standard
  • Ends of the ribs (cartilaginous tips)
  • Cartilage (soft connective tissue)
  • These become the model for transfer to the kid

Young Kid (Paschal Lamb)

  • Same parts edible through roasting
  • Rava: the mishna equates cooking↔roasting
  • Ends of ribs and cartilage are permitted
  • Baraita confirms Rava's reading explicitly

What Does NOT Transfer

  • Parts not eaten in an ox at all
  • Hard sinews — once hardened, not food
  • Soft sinews that will harden: disputed
  • Rabbi Yoḥanan retracted — may NOT register

🧬 Soft Sinews That Will Eventually Harden

Rabbi Yoḥanan
One may register for sinews that will eventually harden but are currently soft — since they are edible now, they qualify as a valid registration portion for the Paschal lamb.
Reish Lakish
One may NOT register for such sinews. Since they will become hard and inedible, their future state defines them — they are not a real eating portion.
Reish Lakish (proof)
The baraita lists only ends of ribs and cartilage as transferable — future-hardening sinews are conspicuously absent, proving they are excluded.
Rabbi Abbahu
"Whoever asked you this was not concerned for his flour" — Rabbi Yoḥanan retracted his original leniency and agreed one may NOT register for such sinews.
Resolution Rabbi Yoḥanan ultimately retracted. The halakha follows Reish Lakish: one may NOT register for currently-soft sinews that will eventually harden into non-food.

🦴 Mishna: The Bone-Breaking Prohibition

Rule 1: Break a bone of a ritually pure Paschal lamb → 40 lashes. This is an active Torah prohibition (לאו שיש בו מעשה).
↓
Rule 2: Leave over part of a pure Paschal lamb → no lashes. Reason: it is a prohibition with no action (לאו שאין בו מעשה) — the transgression is purely passive.
↓
Rule 3: Break the bone of a ritually impure Paschal lamb → no lashes. The prohibition does not apply to a disqualified offering.
↓
Biblical source: "In one house shall it be eaten… and you shall not break a bone in it" — the word בוֹ limits the prohibition to a valid offering only.
↓
Dispute: Does בוֹ exclude any offering that is disqualified, or only one that was never fit to be eaten? This generates the extended amoraic debate on amud bet.
