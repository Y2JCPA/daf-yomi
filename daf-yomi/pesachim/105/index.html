⏰ Shabbat Creates Binding Obligations: Kiddush and Havdala

Rav's Core Principle: Just as Shabbat establishes the obligation to tithe untithed produce eaten casually, so it establishes kiddush at its start — and, by extension, havdala at its conclusion
↓
Shabbat begins → immediate obligation for kiddush before eating; one may not continue a casual meal without reciting kiddush
↓
Shabbat ends (nightfall) → havdala obligation is created
↓
Key ruling: One need NOT interrupt an ongoing meal for havdala — but may NOT begin a new meal after nightfall without first reciting havdala
↓
Similarly: wine and beer are forbidden before havdala; water is permitted (though Rav Huna warns even against water due to the ailment istanis)

🔍 Ulla's Opinion on the Havdala Blessing

Questioner
This is difficult for Ulla! He rules that one who began havdala but did not conclude with 'Blessed' has not fulfilled the obligation. Yet the Gemara wants to compare havdala to blessings over mitzvot — which do not require that full closing formula. How can these be reconciled?
Ulla
Havdala is itself considered like a blessing over a mitzva — and such blessings do not require concluding with 'Baruch.' Therefore, beginning the blessing is enough to establish the obligation, even without the formal closing formula. There is no contradiction.
📌 Classification Havdala = mitzva blessing (ברכת המצוות), not a standard praise formula — no 'Baruch' closing is required to validate it

🌅 Shabbat Onset and Conclusion: Must You Stop Eating?

You are mid-meal as Shabbat begins or ends
↓
Shabbat begins (Friday night)
Kiddush is immediately obligatory. Shabbat establishes kiddush — you must stop eating to recite kiddush before continuing the meal
Shabbat ends (Saturday night)
Need NOT stop the meal for havdala. An ongoing meal is not interrupted — but you may not begin a new meal without havdala
↓
After nightfall: Cannot start any new meal or drink significant beverages (wine, beer) before havdala is recited
↓
Source: Rav Hamnuna the Elder citing Rav — Shabbat establishes both kiddush and havdala obligations, just as it establishes tithes

💧 What May Be Consumed Before Havdala?

🍷 Wine & Beer — Prohibited

  • Significant beverages — treated like a full meal
  • Forbidden after nightfall until havdala is recited
  • Applies even if you have not yet eaten
  • Rav Huna's warning applies here as well

💧 Water — Permitted (mostly)

  • Gemara: 'We have no problem with water'
  • Not a significant beverage — no prohibition
  • May drink before havdala without issue
  • But: Rav Huna warned against drinking even water before havdala — fear of ailment called istanis (stomach disorder)

🕍 Ravina vs. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak: Is Kiddush Available All Day?

Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
One who did not recite kiddush on Friday night (Shabbat eve) may recite kiddush at any point during the Shabbat day — the obligation extends throughout the entire day of Shabbat.
Ravina (Objection 1)
The Tosefta says kiddush is a mitzva 'on the nights of Shabbat.' If missed-night kiddush could be recited all day, then Shabbat and Yom Tov would effectively have a mitzva of kiddush during the day as well — which implies nighttime is primary!
Ravina (Objection 2)
A baraita says 'the honor of the day takes precedence over the honor of the night.' If we don't apply the principle that a mitzva is beloved in its proper time, then the day-kiddush cup would be wasted — it could be used for havdala instead!
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
We do NOT apply the principle 'a mitzva is beloved in its proper time' universally. As proof: the baraita itself says to use the one cup for day-kiddush over Grace after Meals — not wait for the 'better' time. Night kiddush missed can be made up during the day.
⚖️ Resolution If kiddush was not recited Friday night, it may be recited during Shabbat day — we do not apply the principle that a mitzva is superior in its proper time (מצוה חביבה בזמנה) in this case

📜 Eight Halakhot from the Havdala Baraita (Amud B)

#Halakha LearnedDerived How
1One who recites havdala in Ma'ariv must still recite havdala over a cupThe baraita prescribes a cup upon returning home even after prayer
2Havdala requires a cup even when said in prayerPrayer alone does not exempt the cup requirement
3Birkat HaMazon (Grace after Meals) requires a cup of wineThe baraita shows a cup used for Grace at the end of the meal
4A cup of blessing requires a minimum measure (shiur)Otherwise one cup could serve both havdala and Grace — yet baraita implies two cups are needed
5One who recites a blessing must taste what he blesses overThis is why one cup cannot serve double duty after being tasted
6Once tasted, the cup is 'used' and may not serve a second blessingTasting at havdala disqualifies the cup from also being used for Grace
7Havdala and Grace after Meals each require their own separate cupBoth require a cup and one cup cannot serve both
8Havdala blessings follow a fixed order: wine → light → spices → havdalaThe baraita specifies this exact sequence upon coming home
