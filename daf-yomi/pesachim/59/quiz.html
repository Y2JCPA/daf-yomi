QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why is the Paschal lamb sacrificed after the daily afternoon offering, according to the Gemara's derivation?
A. It is a rabbinic enactment to keep the Paschal lamb close to nightfall when it is eaten
B. The afternoon Tamid is described only as 'in the afternoon,' while the Paschal lamb is described as both 'in the evening' and 'in the afternoon' — the item with a later time-designation follows the item with only an earlier one
C. A special verse in Deuteronomy explicitly mandates this sequence
D. The Paschal lamb's blood would be invalidated if sprinkled before the afternoon Tamid's blood
Correct: B — The Gemara derives the order by comparing verse language: the daily afternoon offering is described only with 'in the afternoon,' while the Paschal lamb is described with both 'in the afternoon' and 'in the evening.' An offering connected to the later time (evening) is sacrificed after one connected only to the earlier time (afternoon).
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does the word 'oto' (אוֹתוֹ, 'it') in the verse about lighting the lamps teach?
A. The lamps must be kindled by the High Priest personally, not a regular kohen
B. The lamps are the last Temple rite of the day — explicitly excluded from early kindling
C. The lamps must be kindled simultaneously with the afternoon incense, not before or after
D. The lamps may not be kindled on Shabbat or Yom Tov
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that 'oto' emphasizes that the lamps — specifically — are kindled last. This word excludes the possibility that lamps could be kindled before other afternoon rites. The lamps are the final avodah of the day.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Ravina (citing Rav Ḥisda) explain why a mechussar kippurim can successfully sacrifice after the afternoon Tamid on Passover eve?
A. His offerings are treated as communal sacrifices, which are always permitted after the Tamid
B. We deal with a poor leper whose sin-offering is a bird — birds are entirely consumed on the altar, so no priestly eating is required and the mitzva of completion is satisfied immediately
C. The positive commandment of Passover overrides all restrictions for every type of lacking-atonement person
D. The leper's atonement is not time-sensitive and can be completed the next morning
Correct: B — Ravina specifies a poor leper whose sin-offering is a bird (not an animal). Bird sin-offerings are entirely consumed by fire on the altar — there is no meat for priests to eat. Therefore the 'mitzva of completion' (which requires priestly consumption) is not impeded, and the offering is halakhically complete without delay.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is Rava's ruling about whether a leper achieves atonement if the standard order of sin-offering before burnt-offering is reversed?
A. The leper loses his atonement entirely if the order is reversed — the sequence is indispensable
B. The leper must bring a replacement burnt-offering the next day to complete atonement
C. The verse 'the priest shall offer the burnt-offering' implies it was already offered — the leper achieves atonement even if the order is reversed
D. Only a zav, not a leper, may reverse the order of his offerings
Correct: C — Rava derives from the verse in Leviticus 14:20 ('and the priest shall offer the burnt-offering') that the language implies the burnt-offering 'was already offered' in a sense — from here the Gemara learns that the leper's atonement is complete even when the normal order (sin-offering before burnt-offering) is reversed, making the leper's case categorically different.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is Rav Pappa's dilemma about the Mishna's ruling on mixed intent (lishmah and shelo lishmah) for the Paschal lamb?
A. Whether mixed intent applies only to the slaughter or also to the sprinkling of blood
B. Whether the Mishna refers to dual intent expressed during one single rite, or dual intent spread across two separate rites — since this determines which Tannaic opinion the Mishna follows
C. Whether the invalidating intent must be stated aloud or may be a silent mental reservation
D. Whether a non-kohen's stated intent can invalidate a sacrifice performed by a kohen
Correct: B — Rav Pappa asks: when the Mishna says 'for its own purpose and not for its own purpose,' does this mean the priest expressed both intentions simultaneously during one rite (one act, mixed intent — pointing to Rabbi Yosei), or that he performed different rites with different intentions (two separate acts — possibly pointing to Rabbi Meir)? The answer changes which Tannaic authority underlies the Mishna.
