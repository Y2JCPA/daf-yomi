🍞 What Qualifies as Bread?

Fulfills Matza Obligation

  • Fine flour matza
  • Coarse flour matza
  • Half-baked matza (b'di'avad)
  • Pot-baked matza (b'di'avad)
  • Figured matza (b'di'avad)

Exempt from Ḥalla

  • Sponge/honey/spiced cakes (Reish Lakish: pot-stew)
  • Me'isa — Beit Shammai
  • Ḥalita — Beit Hillel
  • Second-tithe dough (Rabbi Meir: consecrated)

Obligated in Ḥalla

  • Oven-baked bread (all agree)
  • Ḥalita — Beit Shammai
  • Me'isa — Beit Hillel
  • Pot-baked then oven-baked (disputed)

🏋️ How Thick Can Matza Be?

Rav Huna
Matza may be baked up to a handbreadth thick — just as the shewbread (lechem hapanim) was a handbreadth thick in the Temple.
Rav Yosef
Shewbread was prepared by diligent priests using dry summer wood. These exceptional conditions prevented leavening. We cannot apply that analogy to ordinary Passover baking!
Rav Yosef (cont.)
If dry-wood temple baking is required for safety, ordinary households using moist wood or imprecise technique would face real leavening risk with thick dough.
Outcome Rav Yosef rejects thick matza for household use — the Temple shewbread analogy fails because Temple conditions were unreplicable. Thickness increases leavening risk.

🎨 Figured Matza — Ruling and Exceptions

B'di'avad: One who eats figured matza on Passover has fulfilled the obligation — it counts as matza
↓
L'khatchila prohibited: Do not bake figured matza on Passover — shaping causes delay, risking leavening
↓
Rav Yehuda's leniency: A woman using a mold could press and place immediately, with no delay at all
↓
Rejected: People will not understand the distinction and will assume all shaped matza is permitted, even when baked slowly
↓
Rabbi Tzadok's exception: Expert professional bakers work quickly and efficiently — the prohibition does not apply to them (cf. Rabban Gamliel's home)

💧 Me'isa vs Ḥalita — Ḥalla Dispute

Me'isa (מְעִיסָה)

  • Dough boiled directly in water
  • Beit Shammai: Exempt from ḥalla
  • Beit Hillel: Obligated
  • R. Yishmael b. R. Yosei: Exempt (in father's name)

Ḥalita (חֲלִיטָה)

  • Boiling water poured over the dough
  • Beit Shammai: Obligated
  • Beit Hillel: Exempt
  • R. Yishmael b. R. Yosei: Exempt (in father's name)

Rabbi Yehuda's Principle

  • Bread = only what is baked in an oven
  • Source: "Ten women shall bake your bread in one oven" (Lev. 26:26)
  • Pot-boiled stew: always exempt
  • Exception: if subsequently baked in oven (disputed)
