📜 Whose Mishna Is It?

Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov
The mishna follows Rabbi Yehuda. He derives the eating prohibition from the seeing prohibition — just as seeing a gentile's chametz is permitted, eating chametz of a gentile after Pesach is also not punished.
Rava
The mishna follows Rabbi Shimon, who permits benefit from chametz after Pesach even if owned by a Jew. The mishna's verse proof (lo yera'eh) applies only to the first clause about seeing, not to the penalty for eating.
The Core Question Why does the mishna forbid benefit from a Jew's chametz after Pesach? Which Tanna's opinion does it reflect — and what are the implications for eating chametz belonging to a gentile?

⚔️ Gentile's Chametz After Pesach: Flogged?

Rava
One who eats chametz of a gentile after Pesach is flogged, per Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yehuda derives the prohibition from an independent verse — not from the seeing prohibition — so the gentile's chametz is independently forbidden and carries a lash.
Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov
One who eats chametz of a gentile after Pesach is not flogged, per Rabbi Yehuda. Since he derives eating from seeing (lo yera'eh), and one may see a gentile's chametz, one is also not punished for eating it.
Plot Twist Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov later retracted his position based on a baraita about consecrated chametz and the laws of me'ilah.

⚖️ Consecrated Chametz During Pesach — Me'ilah?

One eats consecrated chametz (hekdesh) during Passover
↓
First Opinion — Guilty of Me'ilah
Consecrated chametz retains monetary value because it can cause financial loss (one burns it to avoid a lashing). An item that causes financial loss is deemed valuable.
Some Say — Not Guilty of Me'ilah
Chametz during Pesach has no monetary value and cannot be redeemed even to feed dogs. Since it cannot be redeemed, it is not subject to me'ilah.
↓
Rabbi Yochanan identifies the "Some Say" as Rabbi Neḥunya ben HaKana, who structures Yom Kippur liability like Shabbat — accidental violations require atonement; intentional ones incur karet, not execution

🕍 Rabbi Neḥunya ben HaKana's Principle

רבי נחוניא בן הקנה היה עושה את יום הכיפורים כשבת לתשלומין
Yom Kippur = Shabbat for Liability Rabbi Neḥunya ben HaKana equated Yom Kippur with Shabbat:

• Shabbat: Accidental desecration → chatat; Intentional with warning → death (stoning); Intentional without warning → karet

• Yom Kippur: Accidental eating → chatat; Intentional → karet (not execution)

This principle also affects how consecrated chametz on Pesach is treated — since the chametz itself is forbidden like a Yom Kippur prohibition, its liability structure shifts accordingly.

🥣 Three Opinions on Chametz Mixed Into Food

Rav

  • During Pesach: Forbidden — any amount, same or different type
  • After Pesach: Same type forbidden; different type permitted
  • Logic: Issued a decree for different-type mixtures during Pesach due to the severity of chametz; no decree needed after Pesach

Shmuel

  • During Pesach: Same type forbidden; different type permitted
  • After Pesach: Permitted regardless of type
  • Logic: No additional decree for different-type mixtures; after Pesach chametz is wholly permitted

Rabbi Yochanan

  • During Pesach: Forbidden only when chametz gives flavor (≥ 1/60 ratio), same or different type
  • After Pesach: Permitted regardless
  • Logic: Chametz during Pesach follows standard flavor-imparting rules; no special stringency beyond that
