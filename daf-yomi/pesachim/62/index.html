🩸 Which Impurity Does the Baraita Discuss?

Mar Zutra
The baraita compares circumcision to impurity — just as impurity of the people does not disqualify on behalf of others, so too with circumcision status.
Gemara
But uncircumcised people do not disqualify the offering! As the mishna states: one who slaughters for both circumcised and uncircumcised — it is valid.
Resolution
The baraita must refer to impurity of the meat — partial impurity does not spread to make the whole offering invalid.
Counter
But the latter clause then fails — where do we ever find impure sacrificial meat permitted? Only communal people-impurity allows deferral!
Final Resolution Yes, the two clauses of the baraita address different types of impurity: the first clause speaks of meat impurity, the latter of people impurity — and this is no contradiction.

✂️ Can an Uncircumcised Person Achieve Atonement via the Korban Pesach?

Rav Ḥisda
Yes — since he is obligated to bring a Paschal lamb, he is considered eligible for its atonement. The verse 'for him and not for his fellow' (Lev. 1:4) does not exclude him.
Opponent
No — 'for him and not for his fellow' excludes an uncircumcised person who is forbidden to eat the Paschal lamb and cannot receive atonement through it.
Gemara
Does Rav Ḥisda truly accept the 'since' argument? A contradiction arises from Rabba's ruling about baking on a Festival without guests...
Rabba
Since if guests arrive the bread would be permitted, he is not flogged. But this case differs: no prohibited action is performed — only thought. In the Paschal case, blood is actually sprinkled.
Key Distinction Rav Ḥisda accepts 'since' only when no prohibited act is committed. Sprinkling blood on behalf of an uncircumcised person is an actual deed — a different category entirely.

🩸 Pesach Impurity — Navigating the Baraita

Baraita: Circumcision and impurity both disqualify the Pesach — compare the two categories
↓
Impurity of the People
Majority of community impure → Pesach Sheni permitted for all. Partial group impurity does not spread to all.
Impurity of the Meat
Fats impure, meat pure → blood may be sprinkled.
Meat impure, fats pure → blood may NOT be sprinkled.
↓
The baraita's two clauses each address a different type of impurity — this resolves the apparent contradiction

🐑 Dual-Purpose Sacrifice: Order of Intent Matters

For Its Own Purpose THEN Different Purpose

  • Disqualification is in the offering itself
  • Impossible to identify which part is prohibited — no separation
  • Applies in all four rites: slaughter, receiving blood, carrying blood, sprinkling
  • Rav Ashi: arguments 1 and 2 above are actually one and the same point

For Different Purpose THEN Its Own Purpose

  • Disqualification is in the person — the offerer transgressed
  • The valid and invalid parts can be identified separately
  • Does not apply uniformly across all four rites
  • The offering itself may retain a degree of validity

📚 Rabbi Simlai, Rabbi Yoḥanan, and the Book of Genealogies

Rabbi Simlai approaches Rabbi Yoḥanan: "Would the Master teach me the Book of Genealogies [Sefer Yuchsin] in three months?"
↓
Rabbi Yoḥanan throws a clod of dirt at him: "Berurya, wife of Rabbi Meir, studied 300 laws from 300 teachers daily — yet could not master it in three years. You want three months?"
↓
As Rabbi Simlai departs, he asks: "What is the difference between one who slaughters the Pesach for its own purpose AND a different purpose, versus for a different purpose AND its own purpose?"
↓
Rabbi Yoḥanan recognizes his scholarship and gives a three-part answer: (1) where disqualification resides, (2) whether the prohibition is identifiable, (3) which rites it applies to.
↓
Rav Ashi later concludes: arguments (1) and (2) are one and the same thing — stating two facets of the identical principle.

📜 The Hidden Book of Genealogies

מִיּוֹם שֶׁנִּגְנַז סֵפֶר יוּחֲסִין — תָּשַׁשׁ כֹּחָן שֶׁל חֲכָמִים וְכָהָה מְאוֹר עֵינֵיהֶם
Rami bar Rav Yuda citing Rav "From the day the Book of Genealogies was hidden and no longer available to the Sages, the strength of the Sages was weakened and the light of their eyes was dimmed." Mar Zutra added in hyperbole: the Book's exposition of Chronicles was so vast that the verses from Azel to Azel (I Chronicles 8:38–9:44) required 400 camels' worth of interpretations to carry.
