⚗️ Core Dilemma: Does Meat That 'Went Out' Become Impure?

First View
The Sages decreed ritual impurity on meat that exited its permitted boundary — just as they decreed on notar (leftover sacrificial meat)
Counter View
No such decree was made for meat that went out of its boundary — it is forbidden to eat, but carries no ritual impurity
Outcome The Gemara raises and rejects multiple proofs from baraita — the dilemma remains unresolved. Attempts via the peeling-from-bone halakha and the Paschal group-transfer law all fail to settle the question.

A bone of the Paschal lamb — broken or cracked

⚠️ Why Not Peel Meat from a Bone Standing Alone?

Abaye
One may not peel the meat off a lone bone — we are concerned the entire bone will crack. If another part of the bone still has meat on it, cracking violates the bone-breaking prohibition.
Ravina
This ruling was stated specifically about a thigh bone (ירך), which holds much marrow and cannot be peeled without cracking. Other bones may be peeled normally.
Context The mishna rules: when a limb partially exits its permitted boundary, cut to the bone and peel back the meat that stayed inside. The Gemara asks: why not just peel the inside-meat from the bone directly?

🧪 Rav Huna vs. Rav Ḥisda — Why Decree Impurity on Piggul & Notar?

Rav Huna

  • Decree applied to piggul (invalid intent offering)
  • Reason: prevent priests from treating forbidden sacred food carelessly
  • Since it cannot be eaten, people might handle it dismissively
  • Minimum amount for decree: olive-bulk (כזית)
  • The decree deters disrespectful handling of consecrated matter

Rav Ḥisda

  • Decree applied to notar (leftover sacrificial meat)
  • Reason: impurity deters priests from accidentally eating it
  • A tamei priest cannot eat — the decree functions as a deterrent
  • Minimum amount for decree: egg-bulk (כביצה)
  • The sages calibrated to the reasoning, not merely mimicking notar's rules

🚪 Carrying Paschal Meat to Another Group — Liability Chain

Torah rule: "In one house shall it be eaten; you shall not carry out any of the flesh outside" (Exodus 12:46) — the Paschal lamb belongs to one registered group in one location
↓
Violation: Moving meat from one group's location to another group's location transgresses a Torah prohibition
↓
Rabbi Ami: One is not liable until he places the meat down in the second group's location — mere carrying is insufficient for liability
↓
Reasoning: The verb 'carrying out' (הוצאה) is complete only upon placement — analogous to Shabbat domain laws where liability requires rest in another domain
↓
Purity status: The moved meat remains ritually pure and technically permitted — the person sinned, but the meat itself is not invalidated
