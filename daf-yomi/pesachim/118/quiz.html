Pesachim 118 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why is Psalm 136 called the 'Great Hallel' according to Rabbi Yoḥanan?
A. It is the longest psalm and contains the most verses
B. It describes God sitting in the heights of the universe and dispensing food to every creature
C. It was sung at the splitting of the Red Sea by Moses himself
D. It contains the greatest number of divine names and attributes
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan explains that the Hallel is 'great' because it praises God for the most universal act of providence: feeding all flesh from His exalted position above all creation. This surpasses even the praise for specific historical miracles.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
To what do the 26 mentions of 'hodu' (give thanks) in Psalm 136 correspond?
A. The 26 books of the Hebrew Bible
B. The 26 letters of a complete Hebrew alphabet cycle
C. The 26 generations from Adam to Moses who lived without Torah, sustained by God's chesed alone
D. The 26 miracles performed during the Exodus from Egypt
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi teaches that the 26 'hodu' correspond to 26 generations from Creation until the giving of Torah at Sinai. These generations had no Torah to merit God's sustenance — they survived purely through divine loving-kindness (chesed).
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Yoḥanan conclude that providing a person's food is more difficult than redemption?
A. Food must be provided daily whereas redemption is a one-time event
B. Jacob's blessing uses 'the angel who redeemed me' for redemption but 'God who has fed me' for food — food requires God Himself
C. The Torah devotes more verses to food laws than to the laws of redemption
D. Providing food involves overcoming the curse of Adam, which was harsher than slavery
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan derives this from Jacob's deathbed blessing (Gen. 48:15–16): redemption is attributed to a mere angel, while food provision is attributed to God Himself. The direct divine involvement shows that sustaining life requires a greater miracle than redemption.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Who served as the guarantor when God promised to repay the ministering angel of the sea?
A. The Jordan River, which parted for Joshua
B. The angel Gabriel, who serves as Israel's protector
C. The Kishon River, which later swept Sisera's army into the sea
D. Moses, whose merits upheld the covenant
Correct: C — When the sea demanded a guarantor before returning the Egyptian dead, God said 'The Kishon River will be My guarantor.' This was fulfilled when Sisera's 900 iron chariots — one and a half times Pharaoh's 600 — were swept by the Kishon into the sea after the battle with Deborah and Barak.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Which nation's gift to the Messiah is rejected in the future, and what is the reason given?
A. Egypt, because they enslaved Israel for four hundred years and cannot be forgiven
B. Kush, because they are too distant geographically to bring a genuine gift
C. Rome (Edom), because they do not come to honor the Messiah but to destroy Jerusalem's Temple
D. Persia, because of Haman's attempted genocide against the Jewish people
Correct: C — Despite Rome's a fortiori argument ('If Egypt and Kush were accepted, surely we — Israel's brothers — will be'), God rejects Rome because their true intent is destruction of the Temple, not honor. Rome is called the 'beast of the reeds' — the pig — whose wickedness is described with a single quill: one-dimensional and irredeemable.
