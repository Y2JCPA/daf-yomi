⚖️ Can Second-Tithe Matza Fulfill the Passover Obligation?

Rabbi Meir
Second-tithe produce is consecrated property — one cannot fulfill the matza obligation with it, just as consecrated property cannot discharge personal obligations.
Rabbis
Second-tithe produce is permitted for eating — one can fulfill the matza obligation with it, as it is not consecrated in the strict sense.
Rav Pappa
Objection: The challah obligation applies to second-tithe dough — this proves it is treated as non-sacred dough. Why then does Rabbi Meir prohibit it for matza?
Parallel Dispute The identical Rabbi Meir vs. Rabbis disagreement applies to second-tithe citron for the Four Species on Sukkot (Lev. 23:40 — 'take for yourselves' implies ownership).

🙏 Why Can't Thanks-Offering Loaves Fulfill the Matza Obligation?

Rabba
'Guard the matzot' (Exodus 12:17) — matza must be guarded specifically for the matza commandment. Thanks-offering loaves were guarded for sacrificial purposes, not for matza.
Rav Yosef
'Seven days you shall eat matzot' (Exodus 12:15) — matza must be permitted in any habitation. Thanks-offering loaves may only be eaten in Jerusalem.
Practical Difference Between Rabba and Rav Yosef Loaves baked for the market: Rabba excludes them (merchant had no intent to guard for matza), but Rav Yosef permits them (they are unrestricted in location — the merchant may change his mind before baking).

🤔 Reish Lakish's Dilemma & Rabbi Eliezer's Ancient Tradition

הֲלָכָה לְמשֶׁה מִסִּינַי — A law transmitted to Moses from Sinai
Dilemma → Resolution → Sinai Tradition Reish Lakish asked: Can one fulfill matza with challah separated from second-tithe dough in Jerusalem?

• Per Rabbi Meir: Second-tithe is consecrated — but challah itself is terumah. Does that double restriction bar it, or does challah's terumah status stand alone?

• Per Rabbis: Since impure second-tithe can be redeemed and eaten anywhere, apply the 'since' principle — it is permitted.

Rava rules: Challah from second-tithe is more lenient; the 'since' argument applies — one can fulfill the matza obligation with it.

Rabbi Eliezer (baraita): The distinction between loaves prepared for oneself vs. for the market is a halakha l'Moshe miSinai — transmitted from Moses at Sinai!
