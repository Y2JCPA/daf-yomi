QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the Gemara reject comparing meat in milk to chametz when arguing one should be flogged for deriving benefit?
A. Chametz is only forbidden during Passover, so the analogy is too narrow
B. Chametz carries karet, making it more stringent — you cannot derive leniencies from a harsher prohibition
C. Meat in milk never had a permitted time, unlike chametz
D. The verse for chametz explicitly permits deriving benefit before Passover
Correct: B — The Gemara rejects the chametz analogy because chametz is punishable by karet (excision) — a uniquely severe penalty. One cannot use a stricter prohibition as a source to apply leniencies to a less severe one like basar b'chalav.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Abaye, why is one flogged for unusual benefit from kilayim b'kerem?
A. Because kilayim b'kerem never had a permitted time, like orla
B. Because a verse explicitly lists all forms of benefit as forbidden
C. Because the Torah intentionally omitted the word 'eating,' implying even unusual benefit is prohibited
D. Because kilayim is analogized to neveilah, which also includes unusual benefit
Correct: C — Abaye argues the Torah's omission of the word 'eating' in the kilayim b'kerem prohibition is deliberate — it signals that flogging applies even to unusual forms of benefit, going beyond the normal 'eating' paradigm.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yochanan, from which prohibited substance may one NOT heal oneself even to save one's life?
A. Orla fruit
B. Neveilah (non-kosher carcass)
C. Wood of an asheira (idolatrous tree)
D. Kilayim produce
Correct: C — Rabbi Yochanan rules that pikuach nefesh overrides all prohibitions except idolatry, forbidden sexual relations, and bloodshed. Wood of an asheira falls under avodah zarah — one of the three yehareg v'al ya'avor prohibitions.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does the Gemara derive that one must give up one's life rather than commit murder?
A. From the verse 'with all your soul,' interpreted to include self-sacrifice
B. From a juxtaposition with the case of the betrothed young woman in Deuteronomy
C. From a logical argument: one life is not preferable to another
D. From a baraita that equates murder with idolatry
Correct: C — The Gemara uses sevara (logical reasoning) to derive that murder requires yehareg v'al ya'avor: since one life is not inherently more valuable than another, one cannot justify killing someone else to preserve oneself. No verse is needed.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon about unintentional benefit from prohibited items, what scenario is their core point of disagreement?
A. When one cannot avoid the situation and intentionally benefits
B. When it is possible to avoid the situation and one does not intend to benefit
C. When the prohibited item is food rather than an object
D. When the benefit is derived indirectly through a non-Jew
Correct: B — The core dispute is when one could have avoided the situation but did not, yet had no intent to benefit. Rabbi Yehuda prohibits this (action overrides intent), while Rabbi Shimon permits it (lack of intent is decisive). When avoidance is impossible, even Rabbi Yehuda agrees it is permitted.
