⚖️ The Zeh V'Zeh Gorem Principle

Rabbi Eliezer
If bread was baked using asheira wood, it is prohibited even if the oven also received heat from permitted wood. The prohibited cause renders the entire result forbidden.
The Rabbis
When both this and that cause (זֶה וְזֶה גּוֹרֵם) produce the result — permitted and prohibited fuel together — the bread is permitted. The prohibited element does not dominate.
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
An oven or pot formed with prohibited wood is prohibited, even if permitted causes also shaped it. His view aligns with Rabbi Eliezer on zeh v'zeh gorem.
Abaye
Rabbi Eliezer's stringency may be unique to idolatry, whose prohibition is exceptionally severe. We cannot generalize his ruling to other Torah prohibitions.
Core Question זֶה וְזֶה גּוֹרֵם — When both a permitted and a prohibited cause together produce a result, is that result forbidden or permitted?

🌳 Bread Baked with Asheira Wood — What Is the Law?

Oven is fueled with wood from an asheira (tree worshipped as an idol)
↓
Direct flame bakes bread
Bread is prohibited. If mixed with other bread, then that with more — all prohibited (Rabbi Eliezer)
Glowing coals bake bread
Disputed: Rav Yehuda/Shmuel — prohibited; Rabbah bar bar Chana — permitted
↓
Gemara's difficulty: If coals permit, when do the Rabbis ever forbid such bread? The case where flame is directly opposite the bread is needed to find a prohibited scenario.

⚡ Three Types of Prohibited Oven Fuel Compared

Asheira Wood

  • Wood from a tree worshipped as an idol
  • Benefit from it is biblically prohibited
  • Bread baked with it is forbidden (R. Eliezer)
  • Stringency may be unique to idolatry (Abaye)
  • Oven and pot formed with it: prohibited (R. Yehuda HaNasi)

Orla / Kilayim

  • Orla: fruit of a tree within its first 3 years
  • Kilayim: grain from a mixed vineyard
  • New oven lit with these: must be shattered (Shmuel)
  • Old oven: permitted once cooled down
  • Bread from old oven after cooling: permitted

Hekdesh Wood

  • Wood consecrated for Temple/sacred use
  • Intentional use = me'ilah (misuse of hekdesh)
  • Me'ilah transfers item to non-sacred status
  • Wood for peace-offerings: lesser sanctity (Rav Pappa)
  • Unintentional burning: wood remains consecrated

💬 Glowing Coals from Asheira: Prohibited or Permitted?

Rav Yehuda (Shmuel)
Bread baked in an oven heated with glowing coals from asheira wood is prohibited. There is still improvement derived from the prohibited wood in the baking process.
Rabbah bar bar Chana
Bread baked with glowing coals from asheira is permitted. The coals represent a sufficiently removed stage from the prohibited source — the direct benefit is gone.
Gemara
If Rabbah bar bar Chana permits coals, then according to the Rabbis (who disagree with R. Yehuda HaNasi), where is there any case where bread is actually prohibited? The Gemara needs to identify the limiting scenario.
Resolution The prohibited case according to the Rabbis is when the flame itself is directly opposite the bread — only then is there sufficient direct benefit from the prohibited wood to forbid the bread.

🍞 Rabbi Yehuda's Case for Burning Chametz

Rabbi Yehuda's ruling: בִּיעוּר חָמֵץ — removal of chametz — can only be accomplished through burning. Crumbling, scattering, or dissolving does not fulfill the obligation.
↓
First derivation (kal v'chomer): Leftover offerings (notar) must be burned; chametz is also prohibited to eat — therefore chametz must be burned too. Rejected by Rabbis: a derivation whose initial rule is stringent but whose consequences are lenient is invalid.
↓
Second derivation (mah matzinu): Notar is prohibited to eat AND to benefit from, and requires burning. Chametz is also prohibited to eat AND to benefit from — therefore it too must be burned.
↓
Rabbis counter with neveilah: An animal carcass is prohibited to eat — yet does not require burning. The double prohibition is not the determining factor.
↓
Rabbis counter with shor haniskal: A stoned ox is prohibited both to eat and to benefit from — yet does not require burning. This directly undermines Rabbi Yehuda's derivation. Rabbi Yehuda responds: there is a distinction… (continued next daf)

Central Question: Prohibited + permitted cause together → is the product forbidden?
↓
Rabbi Eliezer / R. Yehuda HaNasi
PROHIBITED
The prohibited cause is not nullified; applies especially to idolatry
The Rabbis
PERMITTED
When both causes contribute equally, the prohibited is overridden; stricter only when flame directly contacts bread
