🗣️ One Rite or Two? — The Gemara's Proofs and Refutations

Gemara
Come and hear from the mishna: 'A Paschal lamb slaughtered for a different purpose' — this first clause must mean one rite, since the last clause 'for a different purpose and for its own purpose' must be one rite too!
Refutation
No — the last clause may speak of two rites and was included merely for stylistic parallelism with the first clause. The two clauses need not be parallel in structure.
Gemara
Come and hear from the next mishna: those who cannot eat vs. those who can eat — the latter clause clearly speaks of one rite, so the former must also!
Refutation
Are the two mishnayot comparable? This case is as it is and that case is as it is — there is no requirement that adjacent mishnayot use identical structures.
Conclusion
All proofs are refuted. The question of one rite vs. two rites is left unresolved (תֵּיקוּ).
Key Term מַעֲשֶׂה / Ma'aseh — Each of the four sacrificial acts: slaughter (שְׁחִיטָה), receiving the blood (קַבָּלָה), carrying the blood (הוֹלָכָה), and sprinkling the blood (זְרִיקָה)

🐑 The Four Rites: When Is the Paschal Lamb Valid?

Paschal Lamb on Erev Pesach
Four rites must be performed with correct intent
↓
Slaughter: wrong purpose
❌ Disqualified
First rite with wrong intent taints the offering — even if later rites are correct
Slaughter: own purpose; later rites: different purpose
✅ Valid offering
Slaughter is the defining act — correct intent there is paramount
All four rites: different purpose
❌ Disqualified
No rite was performed with correct intent
↓
Principle: Intent at slaughter (שְׁחִיטָה) is most critical — it determines the essential sacrificial character of the offering

📅 Rav Dimi's Dilemma: Off-Season Slaughter with Mixed Intent

Setup: A Paschal lamb slaughtered on a day other than Erev Pesach — with intent both for its own purpose (as Pesach) and for a different purpose (e.g., as a shelamim / peace-offering)
↓
Rav Dimi's Reasoning: Since improper intent disqualifies the Paschal lamb at its proper time (Erev Pesach), it should also disqualify off-season — the same logic that makes it invalid at the right time should apply at the wrong time too
↓
Rabbi Yirmeya's Refutation: Not comparable! The disqualification at the proper time is powerful because it applies equally to all offerings — it is a universal rule. Off-season slaughter is a different category and you cannot simply extend the analogy
↓
Rav Adda bar Ahava Challenges Rava: Perhaps a case where one explicitly states mixed intent differs from a case where he is merely silent? An explicit statement of purpose may carry greater legal weight
↓
Rava's Resolution: The Paschal lamb's identity is inherently unstable — people may withdraw from and register for it at any time. Therefore there is always a possibility it will not serve its original owner. This distinguishes it and the mixed-intent ruling stands: Disqualified

⚖️ Proper Time vs. Off-Season: Comparing the Rules

🗓️ Proper Time — Erev Pesach

  • Slaughter for different purpose → Disqualified
  • Slaughter for own purpose only → Valid; owner fulfilled obligation
  • Mixed intent in one rite → Core dispute in mishna
  • Mixed intent across two rites → Debated (Rabbi Meir vs. Sages)
  • Wrong intent is judged against the Pesach's identity as Pesach

📆 Off-Season — Other Days of Year

  • Slaughter for own purpose only → Becomes shelamim (valid)
  • Mixed intent (own + different) → Rava: Disqualified
  • Improper intent nullifies valid intent — explicit statement matters
  • Rav Pappa raises: does shinui ba'alim similarly disqualify?
  • Rava: ownership is fluid; sanctity-change is more fundamental
