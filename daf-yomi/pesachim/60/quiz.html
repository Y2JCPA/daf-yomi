Pesachim 60 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
What is the central unresolved question the Gemara debates in the opening of Daf 60?
A. Whether a non-priest may slaughter the Paschal lamb in an emergency
B. Whether the mishna's conflicting intentions occur in one rite or across two separate rites
C. Whether Rabbi Meir or the Sages hold the majority ruling on Paschal intent
D. Whether sprinkling intent is more legally significant than slaughter intent
Correct: B — The Gemara repeatedly attempts to prove from the mishna whether the disputed case of conflicting intentions occurs within a single sacrificial rite or across two different rites. Every proof is refuted, leaving the question unresolved.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rava, what is the ruling for a Paschal lamb slaughtered off-season with intent both for its own purpose and for a different purpose?
A. Valid as a shelamim since it was slaughtered partly for its own purpose
B. Valid as a Paschal lamb since the proper-time rules do not apply off-season
C. Disqualified — improper-purpose intent nullifies the valid intent
D. Depends on which intent was stated first at the time of slaughter
Correct: C — Rava rules the offering is disqualified. The intent to slaughter it as a different offering type (e.g., shelamim) comes and nullifies the presumed valid Paschal intent — the improper intent prevails.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why did Rabbi Yirmeya reject Rav Dimi's analogy between off-season slaughter and proper-time slaughter?
A. Because off-season a Paschal lamb has no sanctity whatsoever
B. Because the disqualification at the proper time applies universally to all offerings, making it a special category that cannot simply be extended to off-season
C. Because Rav Dimi was reasoning from a Babylonian tradition that did not apply in Eretz Yisrael
D. Because the mishna explicitly exempts off-season slaughter from intent-based disqualification
Correct: B — Rabbi Yirmeya argued that improper intent disqualifies at the proper time because that rule applies equally to all offerings — it is a universal, categorical rule. Off-season slaughter is a different legal context and the analogy cannot be assumed.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rava distinguish shinui kodesh (change of sanctity) from shinui ba'alim (change of owner) when arguing they are not analogous?
A. Shinui kodesh is a rabbinic rule while shinui ba'alim is biblical in origin
B. Shinui kodesh is an intrinsic flaw in the offering's very sanctity; shinui ba'alim is external since ownership is fluid — people may withdraw and re-register at any time
C. Both are equally disqualifying in all legal contexts
D. Shinui ba'alim only applies to communal offerings, not individual ones like the Pesach
Correct: B — Rava holds that a change of sanctity is a flaw within the sacrifice itself. Ownership, by contrast, is inherently unstable for the Paschal lamb — the mishna explicitly teaches that people can withdraw from or register for a Pesach at any time — so the owner-change analogy does not hold.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
If one slaughters a Paschal lamb on Erev Pesach for its own purpose but then carries and sprinkles the blood with intent for a different purpose, what is the ruling?
A. Disqualified — wrong intent at any of the four rites invalidates the offering entirely
B. Valid — intent at slaughter is the defining act; later rites with wrong intent do not disqualify
C. Disqualified only if the wrong intent occurred at sprinkling, not at carrying
D. Valid for the owner and the priest bears personal liability for the wrong intent
Correct: B — The Gemara establishes that correct intent at slaughter is the most critical factor. If slaughter was performed with correct intent, subsequent rites with wrong intent do not disqualify the offering itself, though the owner may not have fully discharged their personal obligation.
