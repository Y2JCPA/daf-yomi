Pesachim 22 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Abbahu, what does the phrase 'lo ye'achel' (it shall not be eaten) indicate?
A. A prohibition against eating only; benefit remains permitted
B. Both a prohibition against eating and against deriving benefit
C. A rabbinic restriction that can be waived for monetary loss
D. A prohibition that applies only to Jews, not to selling to non-Jews
Correct: B — Rabbi Abbahu holds that 'lo ye'achel' prohibits both eating and benefit. Special verses (like 'throw it to the dog' for tereifa, or 'like water' for blood) are required to permit benefit in specific cases.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the basis for Rabbi Shimon's view that benefit may be derived from the sciatic nerve of a neveilah?
A. A specific verse explicitly permits the sciatic nerve of a neveilah
B. The sciatic nerve is only rabbinically prohibited, not biblically
C. Sinews do not impart flavor, so the Torah's carcass-permit includes them
D. Rabbi Shimon holds that the sciatic nerve is not forbidden at all
Correct: C — Rabbi Shimon holds that sinews are flavorless (ein ba'asim b'noten ta'am). Therefore, when the Torah permits benefit from a neveilah, this includes all its tasteless sinews — the gid hanasheh among them.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
From what verse does the Gemara derive that benefit from blood is permitted?
A. "Cover it with dust" — blood must be buried like waste
B. "No soul shall eat blood" — eating only, not benefit
C. "Pour it on the earth like water" (Deut. 12:24) — benefit permitted like water
D. "The blood is the life" — blood belongs to God, all use forbidden
Correct: C — The comparison 'like water' teaches that just as ordinary water may be used freely for benefit, so too blood may be used for benefit. Challenges (water for libations, water for idols) are rejected — we compare to the most common, unrestricted use of water.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does the Gemara derive that benefit from the stoned ox (shor haniskal) is prohibited, including its hide?
A. From the verse 'its flesh shall not be eaten' alone, which covers all benefit
B. From the extra phrase 'the owner of the ox shall be naki (clear),' which extends prohibition to benefit
C. From a rabbinic decree enacted after a specific incident
D. From the analogy to orla, which is fully prohibited
Correct: B — The Gemara notes that 'lo ye'achel' alone could only prohibit eating. The Torah's additional phrase 'but the owner shall be naki' is expounded to extend the prohibition to deriving benefit — even from the ox's hide.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What unusual ruling did Shimon HaAmmassoni make regarding his lifelong practice of interpreting the word 'et' in the Torah?
A. He ruled that every 'et' teaches inclusion of the next broader category
B. He abandoned all his 'et' interpretations upon reaching 'et Hashem Elokecha tira' (you shall fear God), saying he could not include something equal to God
C. He ruled that 'et' before a prohibition always extends it to benefit
D. He concluded that 'et' only has legal force in monetary matters
Correct: B — Shimon (or Nechemya) HaAmmassoni interpreted every 'et' in the Torah to include an additional object. When he reached 'et Hashem Elokecha tira,' he abandoned all his interpretations, since nothing can be added as an equal object alongside God. Later, Rabbi Akiva resolved this: 'et' here includes Torah scholars.
