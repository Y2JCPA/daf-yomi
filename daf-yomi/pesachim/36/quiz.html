QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the repetition 'matzot matzot' include second tithe matza but NOT first fruits matza?
A. First fruits are too holy to be used for an ordinary mitzva
B. Second tithe has a redemption path enabling it to be eaten 'in all your habitations'; first fruits are excluded by that very phrase (Exodus 12:20)
C. First fruits taste different and do not resemble leḥem oni
D. The Torah explicitly names second tithe but not first fruits in the Passover passage
Correct: B — The amplification of matzot matzot expands inclusion, but the verse 'in all your habitations you shall eat matzot' limits it: second tithe can be redeemed and consumed anywhere, while first fruits are confined to Jerusalem — so they are implicitly excluded by 'all your habitations.'
QUESTION 2 OF 5
On the first night of Passover, Rabbi Akiva forbids matza kneaded with wine, oil, or honey. Why does he permit it from the second night onward?
A. The prohibition of chametz ends after the first night
B. From night 2, eating matza is voluntary — not a Torah obligation — so the leḥem oni requirement does not apply
C. Wine and honey become less flavorful after Passover begins
D. Beit Hillel permits it throughout, and Rabbi Akiva follows Beit Hillel only from night 2
Correct: B — The Torah obligation to eat matza — which must be leḥem oni — applies specifically to the first night. From the second night onward, eating matza is a positive act but not obligatory; since one is not fulfilling a mitzva of leḥem oni, enriched dough is permitted.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is an 'ashisha' and why does it fail to qualify as leḥem oni?
A. A type of grain found only in Egypt, not among the five species
B. A large, important cake (one-sixth of an eipha of flour) — a portion of significance, not poor man's food
C. A matza boiled in hot water after baking, making it soft
D. A matza baked too thick, as forbidden by Beit Shammai
Correct: B — The Gemara derives from David's feast at the Ark's arrival (II Samuel 6:19) that ashisha denotes something substantial and celebratory. Rav Ḥanan bar Abba specifies it as a cake from one-sixth of an eipha — a notable portion. Such a cake is the opposite of leḥem oni (poor man's bread).
QUESTION 4 OF 5
The Gemara distinguishes between soaking omer wheat (forbidden) and kneading meal-offerings (permitted by non-priests). What is the key distinction?
A. Soaking involves more water and thus greater leavening risk than kneading
B. Kneading takes place inside the Temple courtyard — the place of diligence — while soaking occurs outside, where we cannot rely on that environment
C. The omer is more sacred than regular meal-offerings and must be guarded more strictly
D. Only priests may guard the omer from leavening, and they don't handle the soaking stage
Correct: B — The Gemara's principle: kneading, although performed by non-priests, is done within the Temple precincts — 'the place of the diligent' — so leavening won't occur. Soaking happens outside the Temple, where no such environmental guarantee exists, and the risk of leavening is real.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rabbi Yosei HaGelili and Rabbi Akiva dispute whether an acute mourner (onen) may eat first fruits. How does this affect the leḥem oni debate?
A. If an onen can eat first fruits, then first fruits resemble matza and should be valid for the obligation
B. If an onen cannot eat first fruits, then first fruits are unlike the mourning-associated leḥem oni, supporting R. Yosei HaGelili's position that leḥem oni must exclude items an onen cannot eat
C. The onen's diet is irrelevant to the matza debate — they address entirely separate issues
D. Rabbi Akiva uses the onen comparison to include all matza, regardless of first fruits
Correct: B — R. Yosei HaGelili holds that leḥem oni alludes to mourning (oni = affliction/mourning), and since an onen cannot eat first fruits, first fruits cannot be leḥem oni. Rabbi Shimon disputes this, holding that an onen CAN eat first fruits — which is why Rabbi Yosei HaGelili must use a different verse ('in all your habitations') to exclude first fruits.
