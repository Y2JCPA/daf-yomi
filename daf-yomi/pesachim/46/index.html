🤝 The 'Since' Principle: Does Potential Festival Use Permit the Action?

Rav Ḥisda
One who bakes on a Festival day for weekday use is flogged. We do NOT say 'since guests might come, the bread is fit for Festival use.' That possibility does not retroactively permit the baking.
Rabba
He is not flogged. We DO say: since guests might arrive unexpectedly, the bread is fit for the Festival itself — so the baking was permissible from the outset.
Rav Ḥisda
Then how is baking on a Festival for Shabbat ever permitted? Answer: Only via Eruv Tavshilin — a Rabbinic institution with a Torah basis.
Rabba
Can a Rabbinic institution (Eruv Tavshilin) permit a Torah prohibition? According to you, without Eruv Tavshilin, baking Festival→Shabbat would be a Torah violation — that is untenable!
Core Issue Does the mere possibility that food could be used on the Festival day make its preparation permitted — even if it was intended for a weekday?

🦻 Deaf Dough (עיסת חרש) — Determining Leavening Status

Definition: Dough whose leavening status cannot be determined — like a deaf-mute (חרש) who cannot communicate its internal state
↓
Primary test: Is there similar dough kneaded at the same time with the same water ratio that has visibly leavened? If yes → the deaf dough has also leavened
↓
If no comparison dough exists: What is the halakha? The Gemara seeks clarification from Rabbi Abbahu citing Reish Lakish
↓
Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish: According to the Sages, leavening occurs in the time it takes a person to walk from Akko to Tzippori — i.e., one mil (~18–24 minutes)
↓
Practical result: Dough left untended longer than a mil-walk is presumed leavened. The teaching incidentally records the distance between Akko and Tzippori as one mil.

🫓 Separating Challah on a Passover Festival Day

Sages

  • Separate challah from the dough immediately
  • Allow it to become ritually impure (no priest will eat it anyway)
  • Burn the impure challah right away — no risk of leavening
  • The challah 'belongs' to the priest, so: It Shall Not Be Seen does not apply to the owner

Rabbi Eliezer

  • Do not designate challah before baking
  • Reason: 'since' she could give it to a priest to eat immediately, it is as if she owns it — making It Shall Not Be Seen apply
  • Wait until all dough is baked — baked bread cannot further leaven
  • Then separate challah from the finished bread safely

💸 Monetary Loss as a Permitting Factor

Rabba
Objection from a baraita: A dangerously ill animal may be slaughtered on a Festival only if there is enough time to eat an olive-bulk of its meat while it is still day. This limits 'since' — it cannot apply unconditionally!
Rav Ḥisda
The animal is permitted due to monetary loss (הפסד ממון) — not the 'since' principle. Monetary loss to the owner is a separate permitting factor on Festival days.
Rabba
Does monetary loss alone override a Torah prohibition? Surely financial concern cannot simply suspend a Festival law!
Rav Ḥisda
Yes — because monetary loss leads the owner to decide to eat an olive-bulk of meat on the Festival itself. That genuine intent makes the slaughter retroactively valid for Festival use.
Key Tension הפסד ממון (monetary loss) vs. כיון ש- ('since' principle) — two distinct mechanisms for permitting otherwise-prohibited Festival-day actions.
