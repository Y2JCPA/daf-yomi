QUESTION 1 OF 5
What is 'deaf dough' (עיסת חרש) and why is it called that?
A. Dough kneaded by a deaf-mute person, which has a special legal status
B. Dough that has definitely leavened but cannot be burned
C. Dough whose leavening status is uncertain — like a deaf-mute who cannot communicate its state
D. Dough that is ritually impure and cannot be given to a priest
Correct: C — Deaf dough is dough that cannot 'communicate' whether it has leavened — just as a deaf-mute (חרש) cannot communicate verbally. The dough shows no clear signs either way, making it comparable to a person who cannot convey information.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, how long does leavening take, and how is this time measured?
A. Half an hour, based on a Talmudic tradition
B. The time to walk from Jerusalem to Jericho — four mil
C. The time to walk from Akko to Tzippori — one mil
D. The time to recite the entire Shema — approximately nine minutes
Correct: C — Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish (cited by Rabbi Abbahu) said leavening takes the time to walk from Akko to Tzippori, which is one mil (~18–24 minutes). This teaching also incidentally records that the distance between those two cities is exactly one mil.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the core halakhic dispute between Rav Ḥisda and Rabba on this daf?
A. Whether Eruv Tavshilin is a Torah or Rabbinic institution
B. Whether baking on a Festival day for weekday use incurs lashes — hinging on the 'since' principle
C. Whether monetary loss can ever override Festival restrictions
D. Whether deaf dough is forbidden or permitted on Passover
Correct: B — Rav Ḥisda holds that baking on a Festival for weekday use incurs lashes because we reject the 'since' principle (guests might come). Rabba holds one is not flogged because we say 'since guests might arrive, the bread is fit for Festival use' — making the baking retroactively permitted.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Rabbi Eliezer's opinion on separating challah during the Passover Festival day is:
A. Separate challah before the dough is kneaded
B. Separate challah from the dough immediately, even in impurity
C. Do not separate challah at all during Passover
D. Wait until all the dough is baked, then separate challah from the finished bread
Correct: D — Rabbi Eliezer holds that since the owner could hypothetically give the challah to a priest to eat right away, it is as if the owner possesses it — making the prohibition of 'It shall not be seen' apply. Therefore she must wait until the bread is baked before separating challah, at which point there is no leavening risk.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Rav Ḥisda explain why slaughtering a dangerously ill animal on a Festival is permitted, without invoking the 'since' principle?
A. Because the animal will die anyway, making slaughter a neutral act
B. Because of the principle that saving an animal's life overrides Festival law
C. Because monetary loss leads the owner to decide to eat an olive-bulk of meat on the Festival itself — creating genuine Festival intent
D. Because the 'since' principle does apply here, contrary to Rabba's objection
Correct: C — Rav Ḥisda explains that the permit comes from monetary loss (הפסד ממון): facing the loss of the animal's value, the owner decides to eat an olive-bulk of its meat on the Festival. That real intention to consume it on the Festival day — not a mere theoretical possibility — makes the slaughter valid. This is distinct from the 'since' principle.
