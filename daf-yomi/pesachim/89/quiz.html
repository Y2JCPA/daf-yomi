QUESTION 1 OF 5
What ultimately prevents making a valid stipulation between a Korban Pesach and a Shelamim?
A. Stipulations (tenai) are never valid in the laws of sacrificial offerings
B. Irreconcilable legal distinctions — blood application, semicha, breast and thigh, number of presentations
C. The animal must be designated before the fourteenth of Nisan with no conditions
D. Only the Kohen Gadol may conditionally designate an offering
Correct: B — The Gemara rejects each proposed stipulation by identifying a legal distinction between a Korban Pesach and a Shelamim: the breast and thigh go only to the kohen from a Shelamim; semicha is required only for Shelamim; blood is poured at the base for Pesach but sprinkled on corners for Shelamim; and the required number of presentations differs. Each distinction alone defeats a conditional designation.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yochanan, why did the father in the mishna create a competition for his children?
A. To establish bererah so that the child's ownership would be retroactively clarified
B. To save money by giving only the winner a full share of the Pesach
C. To enthuse and motivate his children to perform mitzvot with speed and alacrity (l'zarzam)
D. To comply with a rule that only one child may eat the Pesach in each household
Correct: C — Rabbi Yochanan explains that the father actually registered all his children from the outset of slaughter. The competition ('first to enter Jerusalem wins') was purely l'zarzam — a motivational device to spur his children toward zealous, speedy fulfillment of the mitzvah. No bererah was employed or needed.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Until when does Rabbi Shimon permit a registered person to withdraw from the Korban Pesach?
A. Until the animal is purchased in the marketplace
B. Until the animal is slaughtered
C. Until the kohen sprinkles the blood on the altar
D. Until the animal begins to be roasted over the fire
Correct: C — Rabbi Shimon derives from the phrase 'miheyot' (מיהיות) that withdrawal is permitted during the animal's 'being' — which he interprets as extending past slaughter all the way until the kohen sprinkles the blood. The Rabbis disagree, reading 'meiḥayutei' as meaning only while the animal is still alive.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
If one group member unilaterally registers an additional person into his own portion of the Korban Pesach without the group's consent, what may the other group members do?
A. Invalidate the entire Korban Pesach and require a new animal
B. Force the unauthorized registrant to bring his own separate Korban Pesach
C. Restrict the member who added someone to receiving only his own individual portion
D. Nothing — once registered, membership in a Pesach group cannot be altered
Correct: C — The mishna rules that while one may register another into his own portion, the rest of the group need not accommodate the addition from their shares. They may give the registering member only his own individual portion, ensuring the newcomer eats only from what the registering member is entitled to.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What halakhic status does money have that someone collected from others to register them onto his Korban Pesach or Festival peace-offering?
A. It becomes hekdesh (Temple-consecrated) and must be used to purchase a communal offering
B. It is non-sacred money, but falls to the free-will offerings fund as a rabbinic fine
C. It must be returned immediately to the payers with interest
D. It is split equally between the kohanim who performed the service
Correct: B — The baraita rules the registration/sale is invalid, making the money non-sacred. However, Rava explains that the Sages imposed a fine (kenas): the money falls to the fund for free-will offerings (nedavah). This rabbinic penalty discourages people from treating participation in sacred offerings as a commercial transaction.
