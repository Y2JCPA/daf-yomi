🔬 Korban Pesach vs. Shelamim — The Legal Distinctions That Matter

קרבן פסח — Korban Pesach

  • Blood poured at base of altar (yesod)
  • No semicha (hand-placing) required
  • Breast and thigh NOT given to kohen
  • One blood presentation sufficient
  • Eaten only by pre-registered members
  • Surplus Pesach cannot be directly designated

שלמים — Shelamim (Peace-Offering)

  • Blood sprinkled on all four altar corners
  • Semicha (hand-placing) required for men
  • Breast and thigh go to the kohen
  • Requires multiple blood presentations
  • Open to a wider circle of eaters
  • Can be brought as free-will offering anytime

🔄 Does the Father-and-Children Mishna Prove Bererah?

Gemara
The father slaughters before knowing which child will enter Jerusalem first — yet it is valid retroactively for that child. This proves bererah (ברירה) — retroactive clarification — is operative!
R' Yochanan
No bererah is needed. The father registered ALL his children from the moment of slaughter. The competition was only l'zarzam (לזרזם) — to energize them toward zealous mitzvah performance.
Gemara
The mishna's language supports R' Yochanan: 'He acquires on behalf of his brothers their portions together with him' — together, implying all were registered simultaneously from the start.
Baraita
Incident confirms the goal: A woman ran so fast that her sandal strap broke. The Sages praised her eagerness — showing the competition succeeded in inspiring zealous mitzvah performance.
Bottom Line R' Yochanan: All children were registered from slaughter. The 'winner' only takes priority in eating from an already-jointly-registered Pesach — no bererah invoked.

🍖 The 'Fine Hands' Problem — Can a Group Restrict a Large Eater?

Dilemma posed: A group member has etzba'ot yafot (אצבעות יפות) — 'fine hands,' meaning he always grabs more than his share. Can the group say: 'Take your portion and leave'?
↓
From the Mishna: If one registers others into his portion without consent, the group limits him to his own portion — showing groups can legally restrict members' portions.
↓
Tosefta rules explicitly: Yes — group members may tell the fine-hands colleague: 'Take your allotted portion and leave.' This is legally permitted.
↓
Corollary — may groups divide? Since telling someone to eat alone is allowed, the Tosefta implies groups may subdivide into smaller groups, even a group of one.
↓
Rav Pappa & Rav Huna anecdote: Rav Pappa ate 8 slices for every 1 of Rav Huna's. Rav Huna joked: 'Better to eat with 100 Pappas than one Ravina!' — Ravina ate even faster.
