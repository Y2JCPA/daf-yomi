🔥 Festival Baking: Proximate vs. Non-Proximate Shabbat

Rabbis
Temple baking on a Festival overrides the rabbinic decree only for the proximate Shabbat — the Shabbat immediately following the Festival
Rav Ḥisda
Agreed: one may not bake on a Festival for a Shabbat separated by intervening weekdays — the Festival cannot be used to prepare for a non-adjacent day
Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel
In the name of R. Shimon (son of deputy High Priest): Shewbread baking overrides a Festival — but does not override Yom Kippur
Gemara Resolution
No fundamental dispute: both agree baking is permitted for proximate Shabbat; they differ on whether the verse 'for you' (Exodus 12:16) also covers non-proximate cases
Core Tension Can Festival preparations extend beyond the immediately following Shabbat? The principle of הואיל ('since / since it is possible') is tested throughout this daf.

🌾 Shtei HaLechem — Two Loaves of Shavuot

Rav Mari — Objection
The two Shavuot loaves are eaten no sooner than the 2nd day and no later than the 3rd day from baking — how can baking on a Festival for a later day be permitted?
Gemara — Answer
The verse says: 'save that which every man must eat' (Exodus 12:16) — this permits baking even for Temple items that cannot be eaten on the Festival itself
Normal Case
Baked on erev Shavuot; eaten on Shavuot itself → 2nd day
Shabbat Erev Shavuot Case
If erev Shavuot falls on Shabbat: baked on Friday, cannot bake on Shabbat → eaten on Shavuot = 3rd day
Biblical Derivation אַךְ אֲשֶׁר יֵאָכֵל לְכָל נֶפֶשׁ — Exodus 12:16 permits Festival baking even for Temple offerings not consumed immediately. R. Shimon ben Gamliel: the phrase 'for you' (לָכֶם) excludes items the Festival community cannot eat.

⚖️ Eight Prohibitions from Plowing One Furrow

Who Is Plowing

  • A Kohen (priest)
  • Who is also a Nazir (nazirite)
  • Plowing over a burial site (source of ritual impurity)

Time & Agricultural Violations

  • Sabbatical Year (Shemitah) — forbidden labor
  • Festival Day (Yom Tov) — forbidden labor
  • Diverse seeds in vineyard (Kilayei Kerem)
  • Mixed animals at the plow (Kilayei Behema)

Personal Status Violations

  • Kohen: prohibition #1 on contracting corpse-impurity
  • Kohen: prohibition #2 (entering impure area)
  • Nazir: prohibition on contracting corpse-impurity
  • Nazir: second nazirite-impurity prohibition

🥩 Five Prohibitions — Gid HaNasheh in Milk on Yom Tov

Cooking Prohibition 1: Cooking the gid hanasheh (sciatic nerve) — forbidden even without eating
↓
Cooking Prohibition 2: Cooking meat in milk — basar b'chalav — separate Torah prohibition
↓
Cooking Prohibition 3: Cooking on Yom Tov for an inedible result — both gid and milk-meat are forbidden to eat, so this cooking serves no valid Yom Tov purpose
↓
Eating Prohibition 4: Eating the sciatic nerve — a distinct prohibition from the cooking ban
↓
Eating Prohibition 5: Eating meat cooked in milk — separate eating violation
↓
Debate: Abaye lists 'kindling fire' as one of the five. Rabba removes it (fire may be transferred on Yom Tov) and substitutes muktzeh wood — derived from 'prepare' (הכינו) in Exodus 16:5

🔄 The הואיל (Ho'il) Principle — 'Since' Logic on Yom Tov

Question posed by Rav Ḥisda to Rabba:
Does הואיל ('since') apply?
Can a theoretical permissibility make an actual act permitted?
↓
Ho'il Applies
Since guests could theoretically arrive and eat, cooking for oneself is permitted — the food is 'potentially communal'
Challenge (Abaye)
The 8-prohibition furrow case shows that the act's forbidden nature cannot be dissolved by theoretical permissibility — each violation stands independently
↓
Ongoing resolution: Ho'il applies only where genuine permissibility exists for that specific item on that day — not where the item itself is intrinsically forbidden
