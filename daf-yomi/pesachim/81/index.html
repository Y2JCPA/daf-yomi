⚖️ Does Retroactive Impurity Cancel the Korban Pesach?

Questioner
Rabbi Yosei exempts a zav from the second Pesach — doesn't this prove retroactive impurity applies by Torah law, invalidating the first Pesach entirely?
Gemara
No! Rabbi Yosei's retroactive impurity is only rabbinic. By Torah law the offering was valid at the time — hence the Tzitz appeases and no second Pesach is required.
Rabbi Yochanan
If a new discharge cancels the clean days retroactively, shouldn't all those days count against the zav as discharge days?
Rabbi Oshaya
I disagree with you — but know that Rabbi Yosei, who exempts from the second Pesach, actually agrees with your position on retroactivity.
Gemara
But didn't Rabbi Yosei say otherwise in a different context? This contradiction must be resolved carefully.
Core Issue Is the retroactive tum'ah of a zav/zava Torah-level or rabbinic? The answer determines whether the Tzitz can appease God for their Korban Pesach — and whether a second Pesach is owed.

🪬 The Tzitz (Frontplate) — What Does It Appease and What Doesn't It?

✅ Tzitz Appeases

  • Tum'at ha-tehom (impurity of the deep) — corpse unknown to all
  • Rabbinic-level retroactive tum'ah of zav/zava on their seventh day
  • Community offerings brought in tum'ah (tum'at tzibur)
  • Uncertain impurity where Torah-level validity still holds

❌ Tzitz Does NOT Appease

  • Known, definite Torah-level tum'ah (tum'ah vadait)
  • Impurity contracted from a creeping creature (sheretz) — explicitly excluded
  • A priest knowingly bringing an offering in avoidable impurity
  • Cases where the Tzitz is not worn by the Kohen Gadol

🕳️ Tum'at HaTehom — Impurity of the Deep

טֻמְאַת הַתְּהוֹם — כָּל שֶׁאֵין אָדָם יוֹדֵעַ בָּהּ אֲפִלּוּ בְּסוֹף הָעוֹלָם
Halakha l'Moshe miSinai Tum'at ha-tehom is a Sinai-transmitted leniency: if a nazirite or one bringing the Korban Pesach unknowingly became impure from a corpse, and no one in the world was aware of that corpse, the offering or nazirite vow remains valid. Rabbi Yochanan: Derived from lakhem (Numbers 9:10) — 'unto you, clearly known' — impurity is 'of the deep' only until two people know (lakhem is plural). Reish Lakish: Derived from the word derekh (road) juxtaposed with 'impure' — just as a road is open and known to all, so too impurity must be publicly known; if unknown to everyone = tum'at ha-tehom. Gemara's conclusion: Both amoraic derivations are mere asmakhtot (supports) — the core rule is an oral tradition from Sinai.

🔥 Mishna: Burning an Impure Korban Pesach

Whole or majority of the Korban Pesach became ritually impure
↓
Burned before the Temple (habira) — publicly, with wood from the altar arrangement given to the owners for this purpose
↓
Why before the Temple? Rabbi Yosei bar Chanina: to embarrass those responsible for allowing it to become impure
↓
Only a minority became impure — or part was left over as notar
↓
Burned in the owners' own courtyards with their own wood — privately, no public shame
↓
Key rule: Majority impure → public burning (embarrassment) | Minority impure → private burning (no embarrassment needed)
