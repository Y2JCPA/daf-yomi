QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Gemara's resolution, what is the nature of Rabbi Yosei's ruling that a zav/zava renders objects impure retroactively on their seventh day?
A. It is a full Torah-level prohibition that invalidates the Korban Pesach entirely
B. It is only a rabbinic decree, so the Korban Pesach remains valid by Torah law
C. It applies only to the zava's immersion, not to the zav
D. It is a minority opinion disputed by all other Tannaim
Correct: B — The Gemara resolves that Rabbi Yosei's retroactive impurity ruling is mid'rabbanan (rabbinic). By Torah law, the offering was valid at the time of slaughter — hence the Tzitz appeasement applies and no second Pesach is required.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why must the majority-impure Korban Pesach be burned specifically before the Temple (habira) rather than anywhere else?
A. Because the Temple has a specially designated fire for this purpose
B. To ensure the wood comes from the altar's wood arrangement
C. To embarrass those responsible for allowing the impurity to occur
D. Because an impure offering cannot be transported outside the Temple mount
Correct: C — Rabbi Yosei bar Chanina explains: the public burning before the Temple is specifically meant to embarrass those who allowed the Korban Pesach to become impure. When only a minority is impure, it is burned privately in the owners' courtyards without public shaming.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the precise halakhic definition of tum'at ha-tehom (impurity of the deep) according to the baraita?
A. Any impurity contracted in or near a body of water
B. Impurity from a corpse buried more than a tefach underground
C. Impurity from a corpse of which no person anywhere in the world is aware
D. Impurity that occurred while the person was unconscious or asleep
Correct: C — The baraita defines tum'at ha-tehom as impurity from a corpse that no one — even at the ends of the earth — is aware of. If even one person anywhere in the world knows of it, it is NOT tum'at ha-tehom and disqualifies the nazirite or Korban Pesach.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rava derive that tum'at ha-tehom is permitted for one facilitating the daily tamid offering, as it is for the Korban Pesach?
A. From a gezeirah shavah linking 'its appointed time' in the Pesach verse (Numbers 9:13) to 'its appointed time' in the tamid verse (Numbers 28:2)
B. From a kal va-chomer: if Korban Pesach is lenient, the tamid certainly is
C. From an explicit Mishnaic statement equating the two offerings
D. From the principle that the Tzitz appeases for both offerings equally
Correct: A — Rava uses a gezeirah shavah (verbal analogy): the phrase be-mo'ado ('its appointed time') appears in both the Pesach passage (Numbers 9:13) and the tamid passage (Numbers 28:2). This verbal parallel equates their laws, including the leniency of tum'at ha-tehom.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
If a traveler finds a corpse lying across the full width of a road but the corpse is broken and dismembered, what is his impurity status?
A. Impure — he must have passed over at least one part of it
B. Pure — we say perhaps he passed between the separated parts without overshadowing any of them
C. Impure only if he was riding an animal or carrying a heavy burden
D. A safek (doubtful) impurity requiring immersion but not invalidating teruma
Correct: B — When the corpse is dismembered, the passerby is ruled pure. We invoke the possibility that he walked between the separated parts without overshadowing or touching any piece. A whole corpse allows no such doubt and would render him impure.
