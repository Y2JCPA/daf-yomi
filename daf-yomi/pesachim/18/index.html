💧 The Great Liquid Impurity Dispute

Rabbi Yehuda
The purification waters (mei chatat) are nullified entirely in the cow's innards — they transmit no impurity whatsoever to the cow's flesh.
The Gemara
Even if they don't cause severe impurity, shouldn't they at least render the cow's flesh impure at a lesser level?
Rabbi Yehuda (reread)
'Nullified' means only nullified from severe impurity. The waters still render food impure — the mishna is incomplete and must be read carefully.
Rav Ashi
Actually, the waters are nullified entirely — this question is independent of whether liquid impurity derives from Torah or rabbinic law.
Core Issue Whether liquids transmit ritual impurity by Torah law (d'oraita) or only by rabbinic decree (d'rabbanan) shapes how the mei chatat function after being drunk, and how far impurity can propagate through food-liquid-vessel chains.

📖 Rabbi Akiva's Derivation from Leviticus 11:34

Leviticus 11:34: "From all food upon which water has come shall be impure [יִטְמָא]… and all drink that may be drunk shall be impure [יִטְמָא]"
↓
Why does the verse say 'shall be impure' twice? Each usage must teach something distinct.
↓
First 'shall be impure'
Food transmits impurity to liquid
When impure food contacts liquid, the liquid becomes impure
Second 'shall be impure'
Liquid transmits impurity to food
When impure liquid contacts food, the food becomes impure
↓
But NOT liquid → vessel: excluded by a fortiori reasoning + dayyo principle

⚖️ The Dayyo Principle in Action

Challenger
A fortiori: If an impure vessel transmits impurity to liquid — then liquid (which received impurity from the vessel) should transmit to another vessel!
Response
Dayyo! It is sufficient that the conclusion equal the premise. Since a vessel cannot transmit to another vessel, the liquid derived from it also cannot.
Challenger
What about liquids impurified directly by a creeping animal (sheretz)? Shouldn't those transmit to vessels — since sheretz is stronger than a vessel?
Response
Since even the basic halakha of sheretz-touched liquids derives via a fortiori inference, dayyo applies to the inference itself — no stringencies beyond the source case.
Dayyo Principle — דַּיּוֹ לַבָּא מִן הַדִּין לִהְיוֹת כַּנִּדּוֹן A conclusion derived by a fortiori inference cannot be more stringent than its source case. The derived rule is 'sufficient' to equal — never to exceed — the original premise.

🔄 Rabbi Yosei vs. Rabbi Akiva on Liquid Impurity

Rabbi Akiva

  • Liquid impurity is Torah law (d'oraita)
  • Non-sacred food reaches third-degree impurity status
  • Consecrated items reach fourth-degree impurity
  • Derives from Lev. 11:34 — two 'shall be impure' phrases
  • Susceptibility of liquids: they become impure without prior wetting

Rabbi Yosei

  • Liquid impurity is rabbinic only (d'rabbanan)
  • Non-sacred food does not reach third-degree impurity
  • Teaches Rabbi Akiva's Torah derivation — but only in his teacher's name
  • Personally holds liquids are too weak to transmit impurity by Torah law
  • Disagrees with Rabbi Akiva on the severity of liquid-food impurity chains

🕍 Degrees of Impurity for Consecrated Items

וְהַבָּשָׂר אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע בְּכָל טָמֵא לֹא יֵאָכֵל (ויקרא ז:יט)
Rav Ashi's Final Analysis Third-degree impurity (שְׁלִישִׁי) for consecrated items is derived directly from Torah scripture. Fourth-degree impurity (רְבִיעִי) is then derived by a fortiori inference from the third degree. The dayyo principle does not block this step — because the Torah itself established third-degree, the a fortiori to fourth-degree is valid. However, no fifth degree can be inferred. Rabbi Yosei's baraita: one who lacks atonement (e.g., a zav after immersion) is permitted in the machane Leviya yet disqualified from consecrated food — proving the stricter rules of consecrated items operate on their own track.
