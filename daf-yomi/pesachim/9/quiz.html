QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the mishna, when does one need to re-search a house after bedikat chametz due to a marten?
A. Always, whenever a marten is seen anywhere near the house
B. Only if one actually saw the marten drag leaven out of the house
C. Never — the marten is assumed to have eaten the bread it took
D. Only on the 14th of Nisan, not on the 13th
Correct: B — The mishna rules that we are only concerned if we witnessed the marten dragging leaven from the house. Without such direct observation, we do not assume it returned the chametz and do not re-search.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Zeira say we must be concerned when a marten took bread (but not flesh)?
A. Bread is too heavy for a marten to eat quickly in one sitting
B. Martens are halachically forbidden to eat bread, so they discard it
C. A marten eats flesh immediately, but stores bread in a hole — raising real concern it deposited chametz elsewhere
D. Bread becomes ritually impure when a marten's saliva touches it
Correct: C — Rabbi Zeira distinguishes: flesh is consumed on the spot (no concern of deposit), whereas bread is hoarded in the marten's hole — creating genuine concern that it deposited chametz inside a house already searched.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rava respond to Abaye's distinction between the 13th and 14th of Nisan?
A. Rava accepts the distinction but limits it to urban areas with many bakeries
B. Rava rules that both mishnas actually refer to the same day — the 14th
C. Rava says the 13th is the more stringent day regarding chametz concerns
D. Rava ridicules it: is the marten a prophetess that knows the date and adjusts its behavior accordingly?
Correct: D — Rava rejects Abaye's answer by mocking its premise: a marten cannot know it is the 14th and that no one will bake until evening. Instead, Rava reinterprets the two mishnas based on whether the leaven was found or left over after the search.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In the case of nine piles of matza and one pile of chametz, a mouse takes a morsel from an unknown pile. What is the ruling?
A. Follow the majority — assume the mouse took from the matza; no chametz concern
B. The house must be fully re-searched since chametz may have been scattered
C. All ten piles must be destroyed since we cannot determine which is contaminated
D. The ruling is unresolved (teiku) because two equal uncertainties conflict
Correct: A — We apply the principle of majority (rov): nine of the ten piles are matza, so we assume the mouse took from the majority. This parallels the case of nine kosher stores and one non-kosher store — any separated item follows the majority.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What reason does Rav Mari give for hiding leftover chametz in a concealed place after the bedika?
A. So that non-Jews do not take it and return it to the house during Pesach
B. Because chametz left in the open becomes halachically ownerless before the zman
C. As a decree lest one place ten pieces before the bedika and find only nine afterward — and spend Pesach searching for the missing piece
D. To prevent children from eating it before the formal bittul chametz declaration
Correct: C — Rav Mari explains it as a rabbinic safeguard: if one places ten pieces and subsequently finds only nine, he will conclude that chametz remains hidden in the house and spend the entire Pesach holiday in futile searching. Careful concealment and counting prevents this anxiety.
