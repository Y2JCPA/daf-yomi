QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak rule that a torch may not be used for bedikat chametz?
A. A torch is too dim to illuminate chametz in dark corners
B. A torch cannot be inserted into holes and crevices due to its large flame
C. The Sages issued a specific decree permitting only wax candles
D. A torch poses a fire hazard and might accidentally burn the chametz
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak clarifies the issue is not brightness but size — a small lamp can be inserted into holes and crevices where chametz hides; a torch, with its large flame, cannot.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why do oil storages NOT require bedika, while wine storages do?
A. Chametz cannot become absorbed into oil and is easily visible
B. Oil storages are typically sealed and inaccessible during Pesach
C. A person measures and brings all needed oil before the meal begins, so the storage is not entered during eating
D. The Sages only required searching liquids that are drunk, not used for cooking
Correct: C — The Gemara explains that oil has a fixed, pre-measured quantity — a person knows exactly how much he needs and brings it before the meal, so he never re-enters the storage during eating, making chametz there unlikely.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to the Gemara's final answer, what 'danger' exempts one from searching holes near a shared wall with a gentile neighbor?
A. Scorpions commonly nest in such walls and may sting the searcher
B. The wall may be structurally unstable and could collapse
C. The gentile neighbor may suspect the Jew of using sorcery, creating physical danger
D. Chametz belonging to the gentile might be accidentally touched, causing a prohibition
Correct: C — Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak, citing Pelimu, explains that reaching into a shared wall hole may cause the gentile to suspect the Jew of casting spells upon him — a suspicion that was genuinely life-threatening in this era.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Rabbi Elazar teaches that those performing mitzvot are protected from harm. Why does this NOT apply to searching dangerous holes?
A. Bedikat chametz is a rabbinic mitzva and the protection only covers Torah obligations
B. The protection applies only when going to perform the mitzva, not when already engaged in it
C. Where danger is commonplace, one may not rely on miraculous mitzva protection
D. The protection is void when there is any personal benefit mixed into the mitzva performance
Correct: C — The Gemara distinguishes: Rabbi Elazar's principle protects in ordinary circumstances, but where danger is מצוי (commonplace and predictable), such as a known hostile neighbor, one cannot rely on a miracle and must avoid the danger.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the core dispute between Rav and Shmuel in explaining Beit Hillel's ruling about 'two upper rows' in a wine cellar?
A. Rav holds both rows are on the front face (top two vertically); Shmuel holds the top front row plus one row going inward horizontally
B. Rav holds one searches top to bottom; Shmuel holds one searches front to back
C. Rav holds only the very top row; Shmuel holds the top two rows of the entire cellar
D. Rav and Shmuel agree on the rows but disagree on whether the cellar needs searching at all
Correct: A — Rav understands 'upper rows' as the top row of the front face plus the row below it (vertical pair on the front). Shmuel understands it as the top front row plus the next row going inward into the cellar (horizontal pair at the top level).
