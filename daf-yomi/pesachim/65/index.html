🧹 May Priests Rinse the Temple Floor on Shabbat?

Rabbis
Rinsing, sweeping, and sprinkling water on the floor are prohibited on both Shabbat and Festivals — both prohibitions are rabbinic decrees. There is no difference between them.
Rabbi Yehuda
On a Festival it is a biblical prohibition; on Shabbat only rabbinic. The Mishna permits rinsing on Shabbat because the Rabbis waived their own decree for the Paschal service.
Rav Ashi
Even granting the Rabbis' view, the Mishna can follow Rabbi Natan: the Temple service authority may override rabbinic decrees — priests rinsed even against the Rabbis' wishes.
Context The Temple floor was flooded with sacrificial blood from thousands of Paschal lambs. Could priests wash it on Shabbat, or was this forbidden labor?

🩸 Which Blood Is Fit for the Altar?

Spurting Blood ✓ Kosher

  • Blood that leaves the animal with life — initial spurts after slaughter
  • This is the blood through which atonement is made
  • Rabbi Yehuda: priests were vigilant to receive this in a vessel
  • Valid for sprinkling on the altar — the essential rite
  • Source: 'For the blood is the life' (Deut. 12:23)

Draining Blood ✗ Unfit

  • Blood that drains after spurting stops — dam hanikarin
  • Life does not leave with this blood
  • Even Rabbi Yehuda concedes: does not make atonement
  • Cannot be used on the altar — completely unfit
  • Rabbi Elazar cites Rabbi Yehuda explicitly on this point

כיצד היו מהלכין? על גבי דפים — How did they walk? On wooden platforms
🌊 Ankle-Deep Blood — and How Priests Remained Valid Rabbi Yehuda challenged the Rabbis: If the pooled blood was unfit, why did they plug the Temple drains on Passover eve? The blood rose to ankle height.

Problem 1 — Interposition (חציצה): Did blood between foot and floor invalidate the rite? Answer: Blood is moist — liquids do not constitute a halakhic interposition.
Problem 2 — Soiled garments: Priests in soiled garments perform an invalid rite. Answer: Priests walked on wooden platforms raised above the blood-covered floor.
Problem 3 — Carrying limbs to the ramp: How did they navigate? Answer: The same elevated platforms enabled movement throughout the courtyard.

⚖️ New Mishna: Rabbi Eliezer vs. Rabbi Yehoshua

Mishna
Slaughter, blood sprinkling, cleaning intestines, and burning fats override Shabbat. But roasting the lamb and rinsing its innards do NOT override Shabbat.
Rabbi Eliezer
A fortiori: If slaughter — a biblical prohibition — overrides Shabbat for the Paschal lamb, surely sprinkling — which is only rabbinic — also overrides Shabbat! I need no other proof.
Rabbi Yehoshua
Your proof is weak! You cannot derive from optional Festival activities that Shabbat prohibitions are overridden. What applies to permitted discretionary acts cannot teach us about obligatory rites.
Rabbi Eliezer
I reject your Festival comparison entirely. My a fortiori stands on its own: slaughter overrides Shabbat → sprinkling certainly does. The logic is self-contained.
At Stake The debate determines the logical source for sprinkling's Shabbat override — is it derived independently, or from the Festival analogy?
