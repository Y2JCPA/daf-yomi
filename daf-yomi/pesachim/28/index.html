⚖️ When Does the Chametz Prohibition Begin?

Rabbi Yehuda
Three separate verses teach three stages: chametz is forbidden from the 6th hour on Erev Pesach, fully prohibited during Pesach, and benefit is forbidden even after Pesach for a Jew.
Rabbi Shimon
Only during Passover is chametz prohibited. Before and after its time — no prohibition at all. The verse 'you shall eat no leavened bread with it' applies only during the Pesach meal.
Rabbi Yosei HaGelili
One verse specifically teaches about the Passover in Egypt — leavened bread was forbidden that year only. Future generations derive their prohibition from different verses.
The Mishna's Authorship Problem The mishna (gentile chametz after Pesach = permitted; Jewish chametz = forbidden) does not align with Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Shimon, or Rabbi Yosei HaGelili — raising the Gemara's central question: who authored it?

💬 Three Sages, One Lesson: Undone by Your Own Logic

פסחים כ"ח.
Folk Wisdom Applied to Rabbi Yehuda's Refutation Rav Yosef: "In the spoon the carpenter made, the mustard will burn his palate." — The strongest refutation of Rabbi Yehuda's argument comes from his own analogy.

Abaye: "He who made the stocks [saddana] shall sit in the stocks — he is repaid through his own handiwork."

Rava: "He who made the arrows shall be killed with his own arrows — he is repaid through his own handiwork."

All three sayings make the same point: Rabbi Yehuda's comparison to notar (leftover sacrifices) is defeated by the very cases he relied upon.

🌊 How Must Chametz Be Disposed Of Before Pesach?

Rabba
Idols thrown into the Dead Sea need no grinding — there is no fear they will be retrieved. But chametz thrown into ordinary rivers must be crumbled first, since those waters are accessible and the chametz could be recovered and used.
Rav Yosef
Chametz thrown into any sea — including the Dead Sea — must be crumbled beforehand. No distinction between bodies of water. Crumbling is always required to ensure the chametz is truly nullified.
Two Baraitot in Tension One baraita supports Rabba (no crumbling needed for inaccessible waters), and another supports Rav Yosef (crumbling always required). The Gemara notes the difficulty each ruling poses for the opposing opinion.

🏛️ Chametz After Pesach: Gentile vs. Jew

Gentile's Chametz

  • ✅ Permitted to derive benefit from
  • Leavened bread owned by a non-Jew over which Passover elapsed
  • The biblical prohibition does not apply to chametz in a gentile's domain
  • A Jew may purchase and use it freely after Pesach

Jew's Chametz

  • ❌ Forbidden to derive any benefit from
  • Leavened bread a Jew owned through Pesach
  • Rabbinic penalty: benefit permanently forbidden as fine
  • Biblical basis: bal yeira'eh / bal yimatzei — it should not have remained

⚖️ Who Authored the Mishna?

Mishna: Gentile chametz after Pesach = permitted; Jewish chametz after Pesach = forbidden for benefit
↓
Rabbi Yehuda?
He forbids chametz even before Pesach from the 6th hour — yet the mishna only addresses post-Pesach chametz. Mismatched scope.
Rabbi Shimon?
He holds chametz before and after Pesach carries no prohibition at all — directly contradicts the mishna's ruling that Jewish chametz is forbidden.
Rabbi Yosei HaGelili?
His views on Passover in Egypt vs. later generations do not account for the mishna's distinction between Jewish and gentile ownership.
↓
🔍 The Gemara leaves the authorship unresolved — the mishna may reflect a view not fully articulated in the known tannaitic debate
