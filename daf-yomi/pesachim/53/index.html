🐑 Was Theodosius of Rome Great or Violent?

Position A
Theodosius was a great Torah scholar — the Sages refrained from ostracizing him out of deference to his Torah learning.
Position B
Theodosius was a violent man — the Sages refrained from ostracizing him only because they feared his power and influence.
Evidence for Scholarship
He transmitted the profound teaching of Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah's kal v'chomer from the frogs — a sophisticated Torah insight suggesting genuine learning.
Rabbi Yosei bar Avin
Theodosius channeled profits from merchandise into the purses of Torah scholars, lending them money and partnering in business — this is praiseworthy. The dilemma remains unresolved.
The Verdict The Gemara leaves the question open — Theodosius's status as scholar or strongman is unresolved, yet his Torah teachings stand on their own merit.

🏘️ Three Local Customs — One Principle: Follow Your Community

Selling Small Livestock to Gentiles

  • Some places: permitted
  • Some places: prohibited
  • Reflects varying concern about indirect harm
  • Each community follows its own minhag

Roasted Meat on Passover Eve

  • Some places: may eat roasted meat
  • Some places: may NOT eat roasted meat
  • Concern: resembles Paschal lamb (marit ayin)
  • Rav: forbidden to say 'this meat is for Passover'

Kindling Lamp on Yom Kippur Eve

  • Some places: kindle a lamp
  • Some places: do NOT kindle
  • Both aim to distance people from sin
  • Conjugal relations forbidden on Yom Kippur

🐑 The Theodosius Goat Ruling — Marit Ayin Logic

Theodosius of Rome instituted a custom:
The Jewish community roasts whole goats on Passover eve
↓
Sages' reaction: Were it not that he is Theodosius, we would consider ostracizing him
↓
Goat Roasted Whole
Prohibited
Resembles the Paschal lamb — misleads people into thinking consecrated meat was eaten outside the permitted area
Goat Not Roasted Whole
Permitted
No appearance of Paschal lamb; does not create a false or misleading impression

🔥 Hananiah, Mishael & Azariah — Kal V'chomer from the Frogs

The Question: What led Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah to willingly enter the fiery furnace of Nebuchadnezzar rather than bow to an idol?
↓
Step 1 — The Source: They derived an a fortiori (kal v'chomer) inference from the plague of frogs in Egypt
↓
Step 2 — The Premise: Frogs are not commanded regarding kiddush Hashem, yet they leaped willingly into hot ovens: "And the river shall swarm with frogs, which shall go up... into your ovens" (Exodus 7:28)
↓
Step 3 — The Inference: If frogs — who have no commandment of sanctifying God's name — sacrificed themselves, how much more so should we, who ARE commanded regarding kiddush Hashem?
↓
Conclusion: They entered the furnace willingly to sanctify God's name — and were miraculously saved. This is the paradigmatic act of kiddush Hashem.

🕯️ Blessing Over Fire — Havdalah Rules

Shmuel
Only at Havdalah (Motzei Shabbat) does one recite בּוֹרֵא מְאוֹרֵי הָאֵשׁ — because fire was first created at the conclusion of the very first Shabbat, when Adam struck two stones together.
Yom Kippur Case
At the conclusion of Yom Kippur falling on Shabbat: one recites the blessing. At the end of Yom Kippur on a weekday: one does NOT — fire was never 'rested' on Yom Kippur as it was on Shabbat.
Ulla
Ulla had heard a tradition but forgot who taught it. He turned and looked at Rabba bar bar Ḥana — who brought teachings from Eretz Yisrael — and understood he was the transmitter from Rabbi Yoḥanan.
Rav Yosef
Citing Proverbs: "Counsel in the heart of man is like deep water, but a man of understanding will draw it out" — Ulla drew out the deep wisdom from Rabba bar bar Ḥana like water from a well.
Halakhic Rule בּוֹרֵא מְאוֹרֵי הָאֵשׁ — The blessing over fire is recited only at Havdalah (end of Shabbat), not at the end of Yom Kippur on a weekday.
