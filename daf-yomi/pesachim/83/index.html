⚖️ Is a Base for Leftover Legally Significant?

Gemara
The mishna says bones must be burned — but why? If they're hollow bones with no marrow, we should just discard them! The Torah only requires burning actual leftover flesh, not mere bones.
Rav Mari bar Avuh
Bones that served as a base for leftover (basis l'nevelah) render hands impure — just as the leftover sacrificial flesh itself does. They take on the legal status of the leftover!
Gemara's Proof
Granted — if basis is significant, bones burn like leftover. But if basis is NOT significant, why burn empty bones at all? The mishna's ruling proves basis must be legally significant!
Rebuttal
No proof needed — the tanna of the mishna holds that lo tishbor (do not break a bone) applies even to a disqualified Paschal lamb. Hence bones cannot be casually discarded either way — no inference about basis.
Resolution via Rabbi Ya'akov The prohibition of lo tishbor applies only to a Paschal lamb that had a valid time — one disqualified before nightfall is exempt. This settles which bones require burning.

🔍 Finding Bones in Piles — Two Approaches

Baraita
Only Paschal lamb bones require burning. Bones of other offerings — even if they were a base for leftover — need not be burned, just discarded. How do we reconcile this with the basis principle?
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
We're dealing with bones from which marrow was already removed. Regular offerings: marrow removed = done while eating, before leftover set in. Paschal lamb: perhaps marrow was removed after it became leftover — so it burns!
Rav Zevid
Different scenario: bones found in piles, some with marrow removed. For regular offerings — assume owner removed all marrow at once (no leftover). For Paschal lamb — perhaps only some were drained. Doubt → we burn them all.
Both Answers Agree The key question is always: was the marrow removed before or after the prohibition set in? Rav Naḥman focuses on certainty of timing; Rav Zevid focuses on the ambiguity when bones are mixed together in a pile.

💪 Three Categories of Sinews on the 16th

Meat Sinews

  • Rav (via Rav Yehuda): all sinews = like meat
  • Can register to eat the Paschal lamb via them
  • Can fulfill the mitzvah by eating them
  • Exception: neck sinews — too tough, not edible
  • If leftover → must burn on 16th Nisan

Gid HaNasheh (Sciatic Nerve)

  • Torah forbids it (Genesis 32:33)
  • Applies only to one thigh — logic: right side
  • Rabbi Yehuda: uncertain which thigh is forbidden
  • Rav Ika: both nerves became mixed together
  • Must burn both — forbidden regardless of leftover

Fat of the Outer Sciatic Nerve

  • Shmuel: two nerves — inner (near bone) is forbidden
  • Outer nerve = Torah-permitted
  • But fat of outer nerve = rabbinically forbidden
  • Rav Ashi / Ravina: mishna requires burning this fat
  • Burned on 16th as rabbinic stringency

🔥 Why Doesn't Burning Leftover Override Shabbat?

Question
If the 16th of Nisan falls on Shabbat, we postpone burning leftover to the 17th. But burning leftover is a mitzvah — just as the Paschal offering overrides Yom Tov, why doesn't this override Shabbat?
Chizkiya
Exodus 12:10: 'Burn what remains of it until morning.' Since we cannot burn on Shabbat morning, the verse itself teaches the burning is deferred — to the next morning, the 17th.
Abaye
Numbers 28:10: 'The burnt-offering of Shabbat on its Shabbat.' This teaches that only the designated Shabbat offerings may be offered on Shabbat — not other mitzvot like burning leftover.
Rava
Exodus 12:16: 'Only what each person will eat may be done for you.' Food preparation overrides Yom Tov — but Shabbat is excluded from this dispensation entirely. Burning is not food preparation, so it certainly cannot override Shabbat.
All Three Agree Chizkiya, Abaye, and Rava reach the same practical ruling — burning leftover is deferred to the 17th when the 16th is Shabbat. They disagree only on which verse is the source.

🌙 Tracing the Sciatic Nerve Ruling Step by Step

Torah source (Genesis 32:33): Jacob's thigh was struck — Israel does not eat the gid hanasheh. The prohibition applies to one thigh, not both.
↓
Which thigh? Logic dictates the right side (more significant). But Rabbi Yehuda was uncertain — so practically, both nerves must be treated as forbidden.
↓
Rav Ika's answer: Rabbi Yehuda actually knew it was the right side — but both nerves became mixed together and can no longer be identified. Therefore both are burned.
↓
Rav Ashi adds: The mishna's ruling about sinews also covers the fat surrounding the sciatic nerve — forbidden not by Torah but by rabbinic custom (minhag).
↓
Ravina finalizes: Shmuel ruled — inner nerve (near bone) = Torah-forbidden; outer nerve = Torah-permitted. But the fat of the outer nerve is rabbinically forbidden and must be burned on the 16th.
