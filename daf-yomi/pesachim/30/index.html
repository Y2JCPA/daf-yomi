🏺 Earthenware Pots: Break or Retain?

Rav
Earthenware pots in which chametz was cooked during Passover must be broken — the absorbed chametz flavor cannot be expelled and renders all subsequent food forbidden.
Shmuel
They need not be broken. Simply retain them until after Passover — once the festival ends, the absorbed flavor is no longer prohibited and the pots may be used normally.
Shmuel's Hidden Leniency Shmuel warned pot merchants not to raise prices after Passover, hinting he could rule pots remain usable. He withheld the ruling publicly to prevent merchants from using it to justify gouging consumers.

🍞 Chametz Mixtures & Rabbi Shimon's Penalty

Rava
A chametz mixture during Passover is forbidden — whether mixed with its own type or a different type of food.
Gemara
But didn't Rava say Rabbi Shimon imposed a penalty forbidding any benefit from chametz that a Jew owned over Passover?
Resolution
The penalty applies only to pure unadulterated chametz — not to a mixture. A chametz mixture is permitted after Passover; the penalty does not extend that far.
Key Distinction Pure chametz → rabbinic penalty applies after Passover (Rabbi Shimon). Chametz absorbed into a mixture → no penalty, permitted after Passover.

🔥 Earthenware Vessels: Inside vs. Outside Kindling

Earthenware vessel absorbed chametz flavor — can it be purged for Passover use?
↓
🏠 Oven — kindled from INSIDE
Fire burns within the vessel itself → consumes the absorbed flavor → wall is purged and permitted
🍳 Pot / Griddle — kindled from OUTSIDE
Heat source is external → absorbed flavor is not consumed → vessel remains prohibited
↓
Special case: Fill an earthenware griddle with burning coals inside → it now functions like an inside-kindled oven → permitted

🧰 Vessel Materials & Passover Purging

Metal Vessels

  • Purge via hagalah — immerse in boiling water
  • Fully restorable for Passover use
  • Knives: Rav Ashi forged brand new ones
  • Most flexible material

Wooden Ladle

  • Purge in boiling water in a primary vessel
  • Principle: absorbed hot → expelled hot (כבולעו כך פולטו)
  • Ruling of Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua

Plain Earthenware

  • Rav: must be broken after chametz use on Passover
  • Shmuel: retain until after Passover, then use normally
  • Cannot undergo hagalah — Torah requires breaking (Lev. 6:21)
  • Bowls of Meḥoza: treated as full leaven containers

Glazed Earthenware

  • Green glaze: clearly absorbs liquid → prohibited for Passover
  • Black or white glaze used for wine libations: permitted
  • Ameimar: if liquid seeps through to outside → vessel absorbs → prohibited
  • Torah rule on earthenware still applies

🔪 Step-by-Step: Preparing Utensils for Passover

Step 1 — Identify material: Is the vessel metal, wood, or earthenware?
↓
Step 2 — Assess prior use: Was chametz cooked hot in it, or only stored cold?
↓
Step 3 — Cold use only: Rinsing and scrubbing (merika u'shtifa) suffices — no absorbed flavor
↓
Step 4 — Hot use, metal or wood: Perform hagalah — immerse fully in a boiling kli rishon (primary vessel)
↓
Step 5 — Hot use, earthenware: Cannot be purged — must be discarded or broken per Torah law
↓
Step 6 — Glazed earthenware: Check for seepage; if liquid penetrates glaze, treat as plain earthenware and discard

📜 Mishna: The Gentile Creditor & Chametz Collateral

נָכְרִי שֶׁהִלְוָה אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל עַל חֲמֵצוֹ — מֻתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה לְאַחַר הַפֶּסַח
Mishna Ruling A gentile lent money to a Jew and the Jew deposited chametz as collateral. The gentile holds the chametz through Passover, and afterwards retains it as full payment. This chametz is permitted to derive benefit from after Passover — because it was effectively in the gentile's domain, the prohibition of bal yera'eh (a Jew may not own chametz over Passover) never applied. The Gemara then debates: does a creditor retroactively acquire collateral (Abaye) or merely hold a lien (Rava)?
