🌙 How Is 'Or' Proven to Mean Night?

Mishna uses אור לארבעה עשר
Search on or of the 14th — but what does or mean?
↓
Proof 1
Yom Kippur: recite 7 blessings on or of Yom Kippur — clearly the evening service
Proof 2
Peace offering: burn on or of third day — verse says burn by day, so or = preceding night
Proof 3
School of Shmuel: explicitly replaces or with "the evening of the 14th"
↓
Or = Evening / Night
But then: why not just say 'night'? → Answer: Euphemism (לשון נקייה)

🤔 Why Not Simply Say 'Night'?

Gemara
We've established that or means night. But why does the mishna use the unusual word or (light) instead of the common word laylah (night)?
Answer
The tanna employed lashon nekiya — dignified, euphemistic speech. The word or (light) is more refined than laylah, which can evoke improper nocturnal activity.
Rav Pappa
Proof that the Torah values euphemism: instead of writing tameh (impure), the Torah wrote asher lo yihye tahor — 'who is not ritually pure' — adding nine letters to avoid the crude term!
School of R. Yishmael
A person should always speak euphemistically — just as the Torah itself models by choosing dignified language whenever possible, even at the cost of brevity.
Core Principle The mishna's word or teaches lashon nekiya (לשון נקייה) — refined, dignified speech is a Torah value, not mere politeness

🗣️ Lashon Nekiya: Torah's Own Euphemisms

Direct / Crude Term

  • לילה (laylah) — night
  • טמא (tameh) — ritually impure
  • רוכבת (rokhevet) — she rides [a saddle]
  • יושבת (yoshevet) — she sits [on a seat]
  • מקרה לילה — nocturnal emission (direct)

Refined / Dignified Expression

  • אור (or) — light — used in the mishna!
  • אשר לא יהיה טהור — 'who is not pure' (+9 letters!)
  • המרכב (ha-merkav) — the saddle/thing-ridden
  • המושב (ha-moshav) — the seat/place-sat-upon
  • אשר לא יהיה טהור מקרה — indirect circumlocution

🐑 Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira's Clever Trap

A gentile would travel to Jerusalem each Passover, claim to be Jewish, and illegally eat from the sacred Paschal lamb
↓
He would return home and boast openly about eating the Paschal lamb — a sacrifice forbidden entirely to non-Jews
↓
Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira (in Netzivim/Nisibis, far from Jerusalem) heard of this and devised a subtle plan
↓
He told the gentile: "Next time, make sure to request the fat tail (alya) — that is truly the choicest portion!"
↓
The gentile arrived in Jerusalem and demanded the fat tail — which is forbidden cheilev (sacrificial fat), never distributed to anyone
↓
The suspicious priests investigated his lineage, discovered he was a gentile, and expelled him — permanently ending his infiltrations
↓
The Sages sent Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira a message: "Peace upon you! Even from Netzivim you guard the sanctity of Jerusalem!"
