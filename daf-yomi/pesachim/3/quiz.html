Pesachim 3 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
What does the Gemara ultimately conclude the word 'or' (אור) in the mishna refers to?
A. The morning of the 14th of Nisan
B. The evening/night before the 14th
C. The afternoon preceding Passover
D. The full day of the 14th
Correct: B — Multiple proofs establish that 'or' = evening/night. The School of Shmuel's baraita is decisive: it explicitly replaces 'or of the 14th' with 'the evening of the 14th.'
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why did the tanna of the mishna write 'or' instead of the plain word 'night' (לילה)?
A. Because or is technically more precise in halachic contexts
B. Because laylah refers only to late night, not early evening
C. To employ lashon nekiya — dignified, euphemistic speech
D. To follow the terminology established by Beit Shammai
Correct: C — The Gemara answers that the tanna used a euphemism. The principle of lashon nekiya — preferring refined language — led him to choose 'or' (light) over 'laylah' (night).
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Pappa, how many extra letters did the Torah use to avoid writing 'tameh' (impure) directly?
A. Three letters
B. Five letters
C. Seven letters
D. Nine letters
Correct: D — Rav Pappa demonstrates the Torah's commitment to lashon nekiya: instead of the 3-letter word 'tameh,' the Torah wrote 'asher lo yihye tahor' — adding nine letters to use an indirect, dignified expression.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How did Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira expose the gentile who was secretly eating the Paschal lamb?
A. He reported him to the Temple guards directly
B. He told him to request the fat tail, which is forbidden sacrificial fat
C. He had his students follow the man back to his home city
D. He sent a letter to the Sanhedrin describing the man's appearance
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira cleverly told the gentile to demand the 'alya' (fat tail) as the choicest portion. Since the fat tail is forbidden cheilev offered on the altar and never distributed, the priests were immediately suspicious and investigated his identity.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the teaching of the School of Rabbi Yishmael regarding everyday speech?
A. One should speak as concisely as possible at all times
B. One should always speak euphemistically, as the Torah models
C. One must use precise halachic terminology in all discussions
D. One should avoid discussing matters of ritual impurity in public
Correct: B — The School of Rabbi Yishmael explicitly teaches: 'A person should always converse euphemistically' — and supports this from Torah verses in Leviticus that change grammatical forms to avoid naming crude or negative concepts directly.
