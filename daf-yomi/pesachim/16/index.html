⚖️ The Great Liquid Impurity Debate

Rav
Yosei ben Yo'ezer ruled that Temple liquids are completely pure — they neither become impure nor transmit impurity to others.
Shmuel
No — they do not transmit impurity to others, but they can themselves become impure. Liquid impurity is rabbinic and lacks the force to spread.
Rav Sheisha b. Rav Idi
Liquids are like fourth-degree impurity in consecrated items — they can become impure but do not transmit it further. This resolves the dispute!
Gemara
Multiple proofs from Leviticus and the tzitz demonstrate that liquid impurity is Torah law — yet even Torah-level impurity may lack the power to spread.
Core Question Is the ritual impurity of liquids (טומאת משקין) derived from the Torah or rabbinic enactment — and if Torah, does impure liquid transmit impurity further?

🔵 Rav vs. Shmuel: Temple Liquids

Rav
Yosei ben Yo'ezer declared Temple liquids tahor — completely pure. They do not become impure at all, and certainly do not transmit impurity.
Shmuel
Tahor here means only that they do not transmit impurity — but they can still receive impurity themselves. Liquid impurity is rabbinic and does not spread.
Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana
Challenge for Rav Pappa: Shmuel himself said elsewhere that blood transmits impurity. How can he hold that liquids generally do not transmit impurity?
Rabbi Zeira
Blood that flows freely like water renders produce susceptible. Blood received in a vessel for altar-sprinkling does not render produce susceptible — that is the distinction.
יוסי בן יועזר מצרידה Yosei ben Yo'ezer of Tzereida's testimony established a special leniency for Temple liquids. The amoraim disputed exactly how far this leniency extends.

📜 Is Liquid Impurity Torah Law? Tracing the Proofs

Question: Is the impurity of liquids (משקין) derived from the Torah or only a rabbinic decree?
↓
Proof 1 — Leviticus 11:34
"All drink that may be drunk in any vessel shall be impure" — the Torah explicitly renders liquids impure
Proof 2 — Leviticus 11:36
"A spring or cistern shall be pure" — implying all other water can become impure; Biblical in origin
Proof 3 — Tzitz atones
The frontplate atones for blood/meat/fat that became impure. If liquid impurity were only rabbinic, the tzitz would not need to address it
↓
Conclusion: Liquid impurity is Torah law (דאורייתא) — yet debate remains whether impure liquids transmit that impurity to other items

🩸 Blood: When Does It Render Food Susceptible (הכשר)?

Renders Susceptible ✓

  • Blood that flows freely like water (e.g., from slaughter, poured on ground)
  • Source: Deut 12:23 — "the blood is the soul" — blood treated like water
  • Blood that is poured out without being designated for altar use

Does NOT Render Susceptible ✗

  • Blood received in a vessel to be sprinkled on the altar
  • Blood squeezed out after the initial spurt — even from non-sacred animals
  • Rabbi Zeira: squeezed blood is exceptional and never renders susceptible

Underlying Logic

  • Blood treated like ordinary water → renders susceptible
  • Blood elevated to sacred altar function → removed from ordinary impurity rules
  • Rav Shmuel bar Ami accepted Rabbi Zeira's resolution with a Scriptural proof

🌊 Why Two Verses? Detached vs. Attached Water

Verse 1 — Detached water: "From all food that may be eaten, on which water has come shall be impure" (Lev 11:34) — water separated from its source renders food susceptible
↓
Verse 2 — Attached water: A second verse establishes that water still connected to its source (a spring, a river) also renders food susceptible to impurity
↓
Why detached-water verse alone is insufficient: One might have thought only moving, detached water renders susceptible — stationary spring water perhaps does not
↓
Why attached-water verse alone is insufficient: One might have thought only in-place water (which is significant where it stands) renders susceptible — once detached, perhaps not
↓
Conclusion: Both verses together establish that all water — whether attached or detached from its source — renders food susceptible to ritual impurity
