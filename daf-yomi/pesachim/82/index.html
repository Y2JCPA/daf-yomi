🪵 Who May Use Wood from the Temple Arrangement?

Rav Ḥama bar Ukva
The mishna allowing Temple arrangement wood refers to a visitor who has no wood in Jerusalem. A resident, who can obtain his own wood, may not use Temple wood.
Rav Pappa
Both cases concern a visitor. The distinction is whether he already set out on the road (permit Temple wood — it is difficult to collect) or is still at home (must get his own).
Rav Zevid
I side with Rav Ḥama: visitor vs. resident is the key. Even a resident who has not yet begun his journey must use his own wood, since he could easily obtain it.
Background The Tosefta rules that individuals may not burn their impure Paschal lamb in private courtyards using wood taken from the Temple arrangement — but why, and who exactly is affected?

🫂 Why Must All Use Temple Wood? Two Explanations

Rava's Reason

  • Fear of me'ilah — misuse of Temple property
  • If one brings wood home and leftover remains after burning, he might use it for personal benefit
  • This constitutes forbidden benefit from sacred Temple property
  • Practical difference: reeds or palm branches (unfit for arrangement) — Rava would permit; Rav Yosef would still forbid

Rav Yosef's Reason

  • To avoid embarrassing the poor who lack their own wood
  • If private wood were permitted, only indigent people would rely on Temple wood
  • They would be publicly identified as paupers
  • Uniform Temple wood usage preserves everyone's dignity equally

📜 Deriving the Obligation to Burn Disqualified Sacrifices

Where does the Torah require burning of disqualified sacred offerings?
↓
Impure meat (lesser sanctity)
Lev. 7:19: 'flesh that touches any impure item shall be burned in fire' — explicit verse
Meat taken outside boundary
Derived from Moses rebuking Aaron on the 8th day of inauguration — Torah logic
↓
What about disqualified offerings of most sacred order? (spilled blood, left overnight, piggul...)
↓
Rabbi Shimon's exposition (Lev. 6:23): any sin-offering whose blood enters the Tent of Meeting must be burned → extended by kal va-chomer and gezera shava ('iniquity'/'iniquity') to all disqualified sacred offerings

👥 Owners' Impurity: Before or After Blood Sprinkling?

Rav Yosef
The Tanna Kamma vs. Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka dispute applies only when owners became impure before blood sprinkling — the meat was never fit for eating. If they became impure after sprinkling, all agree: burn immediately.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Even when owners became impure after sprinkling, the dispute still applies. Acute mourning (aninut) is the proof — it disqualifies only the owners from eating, not the offering itself, yet Rabbi Neḥemya says Aaron's sin-offering was burned for this reason.
Rabbi Neḥemya
Aaron's sin-offering on the 8th day was burned due to acute mourning — a post-sprinkling owner disqualification — proving such cases can demand burning, not just decay of form.
Core Principle Disqualification 'in itself' → immediate burning. Disqualification 'in the owners or blood' → decay of form (Tanna Kamma). Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka: all owner-disqualifications require immediate burning.
