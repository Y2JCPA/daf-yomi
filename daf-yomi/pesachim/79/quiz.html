5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yehoshua, if the meat of an offering became impure but the fat remains pure, what is the halakha regarding the blood?
A. The blood may not be sprinkled — the meat's impurity invalidates the offering
B. The blood may be sprinkled, since an olive-bulk of pure fat for the altar suffices
C. The owner must replace the offering before sprinkling
D. The blood is sprinkled but the offering has diminished sanctity
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehoshua rules that any olive-bulk of matter fit for its designated purpose — fat for the altar, meat for eating — is sufficient to permit sprinkling the blood. Pure fat fulfills this requirement.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is Rav's ruling when exactly half the community is ritually impure on Pesach?
A. Only the pure half sacrifices, on the first Pesaḥ
B. Both halves sacrifice on the first Pesaḥ — each treated as having majority status
C. Both halves are deferred to the second Pesaḥ
D. The impure half is entirely exempt from the Paschal lamb obligation
Correct: B — Rav holds 'half and half is like the majority' — since neither group is a minority, both invoke the communal context and sacrifice on the first Pesaḥ, the impure doing so through the tumah dispensation.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Ḥisda, the communal dispensation to sacrifice in impurity requires which specific condition?
A. Any impurity affecting even a single member of the community
B. The Paschal lamb itself having become impure before slaughter
C. The slaughtering knife became impure through corpse-impurity, rendering the slaughterer's body impure
D. The High Priest personally declaring the service to be in a state of tumah
Correct: C — Rav Ḥisda distinguishes: corpse-impurity via the knife renders the slaughterer's body impure (a severe, primary-level tumah), invoking the communal dispensation. Impurity from a sheretz only affects the meat, not the person — insufficient.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to the latter version of Rav Kahana's opinion, what is the fate of the impure half in a 50/50 split?
A. They sacrifice on the first Pesaḥ in tumah alongside everyone else
B. They are deferred to the second Pesaḥ like any impure minority
C. They perform the Paschal lamb on neither the first nor the second Pesaḥ
D. They are permanently exempt from the Paschal lamb that year
Correct: C — In Rav Kahana's latter version, half-and-half is neither a majority (so no first-Pesaḥ dispensation) nor a standard minority (so no second-Pesaḥ deferral). They fall between categories and sacrifice on neither occasion.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Rav reconcile the baraita ruling that in a half-and-half situation the impure sacrifice on the second Pesaḥ?
A. Rav rejects this baraita as contradicting the mishna
B. The men were split 50/50, but women (mostly pure) joined the pure side, making the impure a true minority
C. This baraita addresses only the case where the Kohanim are entirely pure
D. Rav reads 'second Pesaḥ' as a voluntary make-up offering, not an obligation
Correct: B — Rav explains: the men were evenly divided, but women — whose Pesach participation is optional, so they are counted on whichever side they join — added to the pure side. This tipped the balance, making the impure a true minority who are deferred to the second Pesaḥ.
