QUESTION 1 OF 5
When two groups' Paschal lambs are intermingled, the mishna's solution involves each group sending four of its five members to register with the other group. Why does Rav Ashi say this number is precise for Rabbi Yehuda's opinion?
A. Four is the minimum needed to form a valid group according to Rabbi Yehuda
B. Sending four leaves two people behind, satisfying Rabbi Yehuda's requirement of at least two per group
C. Only groups of nine or more may slaughter, so four must join the other group's five
D. Rabbi Yehuda requires exactly five per group, so four must be sent away
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda holds that the Paschal lamb may not be slaughtered for an individual alone — there must be at least two registered members. By sending four away, each group retains exactly one member, but Rav Ashi's point is that with groups of five, sending four still leaves a valid group of two via the cross-registrations. The mishna's specification of 'five each' thus precisely accommodates Rabbi Yehuda.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rav Huna, why does the mishna need to specify that one may not eat on the eve of Passover near mincha time?
A. Because people might forget it is Passover and eat chametz
B. Because Rabbi Yosei generally permits eating until dark on other eves, so the mishna must specify that Passover eve is different
C. Because the Paschal lamb must be eaten on a full stomach
D. Because Rabbi Yehuda is lenient about Passover and the mishna must override him
Correct: B — Rav Huna explains that the mishna is teaching according to Rabbi Yosei. Rabbi Yosei normally permits eating until dark on Shabbat and Festival eves — so one might assume the same applies to Passover eve. The mishna must explicitly state that Passover eve is an exception, even for Rabbi Yosei, because of the matza obligation.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Rav Pappa's reason for why the prohibition against eating on Passover eve is stricter than the prohibition on the eve of other Festivals?
A. Chametz is forbidden on Passover, so eating any food is problematic
B. On other Festival eves only a large meal is forbidden; on Passover eve even a small snack is forbidden so that matza is eaten with a hearty appetite
C. The Paschal lamb must be sacrificed before mincha, so eating beforehand shows disrespect
D. Passover begins at nightfall while other Festivals begin at sunset, making the timing different
Correct: B — Rav Pappa holds that even if the mishna follows Rabbi Yehuda (who already bans a full meal before other Shabbat/Festivals), Passover eve goes further: even a small snack (אכילת עראי) is forbidden. The unique reason is that one must eat the matza with genuine appetite (תאבון), a consideration that does not apply to Shabbat meals in the same way.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
The mishna states that even the poorest of Jews must eat the Passover Seder in a specific posture. What is that posture, and what does it symbolize?
A. Standing, to remember the haste of the Exodus
B. Seated upright, to show attentiveness during the Haggadah
C. Reclining, to symbolize freedom and the posture of free people
D. Kneeling, to show gratitude for the Exodus
Correct: C — The mishna rules that even the poorest Jew must recline (מסובין) while eating at the Seder. Reclining was the posture of free, aristocratic people in the ancient world — it symbolizes that on this night every Jew relives and embodies the freedom of the Exodus, regardless of their current circumstances.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Mar Zutra's contribution to the Gemara's discussion is unusual. What does he say?
A. He rules definitively that eating on Shabbat eve is permitted until dark
B. He questions whether the baraita being cited is even textually reliable
C. He introduces a new distinction between Shabbat and Yom Tov eves
D. He rules that one may eat on Passover eve until the fifth hour
Correct: B — Mar Zutra steps back from the whole debate and challenges the premise: he questions whether the version of the baraita being cited — which bans eating from nine hours onward on Shabbat eve — is even a reliable text. This is a skeptical move that challenges the very foundation of the preceding amoraic discussion, though the Gemara does not resolve it definitively.
