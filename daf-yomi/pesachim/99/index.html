⚖️ Two Disputes, One Evening

Amud A
When two groups' Paschal lambs are intermingled, each sends representatives to join the other's group — but Rabbi Yehuda requires a group of at least two to slaughter.
Amud B
On the eve of Passover one may not eat from adjacent to mincha time until dark — so that matza will be eaten with a hearty appetite. Even the poorest Jew must recline and drink four cups.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Even according to Rabbi Yehuda, the mishna works: since he permits an individual to appoint another as his agent, two groups can each send four members to join the other — leaving two valid groups of five.
Rav Pappa
On Shabbat/Festival eves, you may eat a small meal before dark. On Passover eve specifically, even a small meal is forbidden — the matza obligation demands a fully unburdened appetite.
Core Principle The Seder night mitzvot — matza, maror, four cups, reclining — are obligatory for every Jew regardless of poverty, and eating restrictions on Passover eve are stricter than those of other Shabbat/Festival eves precisely because appetite matters.

🐑 Intermingled Paschal Lambs: The Solution

Problem: Two groups each have one lamb; the lambs got mixed up and no one knows which belongs to whom
↓
Each group sends four members to formally join the other group as registered participants
↓
Group A slaughters
Group A's four members are now also in Group B — they can eat from whichever lamb is slaughtered for Group B
Group B slaughters
Group B's four members are now also in Group A — they can eat from whichever lamb is slaughtered for Group A
↓
Result: Each remaining individual (the one who stayed behind) slaughters his own group's lamb — both offerings are valid and everyone is covered

⚖️ Does the Mishna Follow Rabbi Yehuda?

Objection
The mishna may not follow Rabbi Yehuda! He holds one may not slaughter the Paschal lamb for an individual alone — there must be at least two registered members per group.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Even Rabbi Yehuda agrees an individual may appoint an agent. So when each group's four members join the other group, the remaining individual can still appoint one of those four as his agent — valid under Rabbi Yehuda!
Rav Ashi
The mishna is also precisely worded for Rabbi Yehuda: it specifies 'five groups of five each.' Only when each group has five can it afford to send four away and still have a group of two remaining — exactly Rabbi Yehuda's minimum.
Conclusion
Both Rabbi Yoḥanan's logical reconciliation and Rav Ashi's textual precision confirm: the mishna is consistent with Rabbi Yehuda's position.
📜 Talmudic Principle Shתיקה יפה לחכמים, קל וחומר לטיפשים — Silence is fitting for the wise; all the more so for fools. (Opening of Amud A)

🌅 Passover Eve vs. Shabbat/Festival Eve: Eating Restrictions

Shabbat & Festival Eve

  • May not eat from 9 hours onward (≈3 hours before dark)
  • Restriction: enter Shabbat/Yom Tov with appetite
  • A small snack (אכילת עראי) may be permitted
  • Rabbi Yehuda: stop eating before dark
  • Rabbi Yosei: may continue until dark itself
  • Source: Baraita cited in Gemara

Passover Eve

  • May not eat from adjacent to mincha time (~2.5 hrs before dark)
  • Restriction: must eat matza that night with full appetite
  • Even a small snack is forbidden — stricter than Shabbat
  • All opinions agree: no eating before the Seder
  • Rav Pappa: the matza obligation makes it categorically stricter
  • Source: Mishna (Pesachim 99b)

🛋️ Seder Night Obligations: Even the Poorest Jew

Recline while eating — even the poorest Jew must eat in a reclining posture, the posture of free men (חֵרוּת)
↓
Four cups of wine (ארבע כוסות) — the charity fund (קופה של צדקה) provides wine for those who cannot afford it
↓
Eat matza — the central biblical obligation of the night; hence all pre-Seder eating restrictions exist to protect one's appetite for it
↓
Maror (bitter herbs) — commemorating the bitterness of Egyptian slavery; obligatory alongside matza
↓
Tell the story of the Exodus (הגדה) — the Seder's narrative framework, fulfilled in the context of matza, maror, and wine
↓
No exceptions for poverty: the communal fund ensures that financial hardship does not excuse any Jew from these four obligations on the night of Passover
