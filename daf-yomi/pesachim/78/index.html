⚖️ Does the Frontplate Appease for Impure Offerings?

Rabbi Eliezer
The frontplate (tzitz) appeases God for the impurity of portions that should be burned. Blood is accepted even if meat became impure — the offering is valid.
Rabbi Yehoshua
If the meat was lost or burned before sprinkling, the offering is disqualified. Ab initio, do not sprinkle blood when valid meat is unavailable.
Rabbi Yosei
I see as correct the statement of Rabbi Eliezer (after the fact) and also the statement of Rabbi Yehoshua (ab initio) — these are context-dependent, not contradictory.
Resolution (78b) Rabbi Yosei agrees with Eliezer בדיעבד (after the fact) and with Yehoshua לכתחילה (ab initio) — one addresses retroactive validity, the other proper procedure.

🩸 Rabbi Eliezer vs. Rabbi Yehoshua: Blood and Meat

Rabbi Eliezer

  • Frontplate (tzitz) appeases for impure portions to be burned on altar
  • Blood is accepted even without meat — atonement through blood alone
  • Offering remains valid even if meat became ritually impure
  • Individual offering valid after the fact (bedieved)

Rabbi Yehoshua

  • Frontplate does NOT appease for impure portions to be eaten
  • Meat is essential — without it, blood cannot be accepted
  • If meat was lost or burned, the offering is disqualified
  • Ab initio: do not sprinkle blood without valid meat available

🤔 Rav Pappa & Abaye: Resolving Rabbi Yosei's Dual Ruling

Rav Pappa
Rabbi Yosei is like a document that awards something to two conflicting parties — he agrees with both Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua. How can that be?
Abaye
He did not issue contradictory rulings. He said what was reasonable in context: for animal offerings he found Eliezer compelling; for meal-offerings the reasoning of Yehoshua applied.
Rav Pappa
That works for animal vs. meal-offerings — but what about the two loaves of Shavuot? They are entirely eaten and nothing is brought on the altar. How can they be brought impure per Rabbi Yosei?
Gemara (Final Answer)
Rabbi Yosei agrees with Eliezer after the fact (meat became impure) and with Yehoshua ab initio (offering lost/burned). These address entirely different cases.
Key Distinction בדיעבד vs. לכתחילה — impure meat (Eliezer's case) vs. lost/burned meat (Yehoshua's case) are distinct scenarios, not the same ruling applied differently.

🍖 Rabbi Natan: Is Eating Essential to the Paschal Lamb?

The Verse (Exodus 12:4): "According to every man's eating you shall make your count" — seems to require eating as essential to the offering's validity
↓
Rabbinic view: Eating IS essential. If meat cannot be eaten, the offering is invalid and owners must bring a Second Pesaḥ (פסח שני)
↓
Rabbi Natan's view: Eating is NOT essential — it is a separate מצוה (commandment) to eat, but failure to eat does not invalidate the blood-sprinkling or the owners' obligation
↓
Proof from baraita: Two groups registered for one lamb with insufficient meat for all — Rabbi Natan rules both groups are exempt from Second Pesaḥ; the blood-sprinkling already fulfilled their obligation
↓
Practical ruling (Rav): The mishna's prohibition on sprinkling when meat is impure applies only לכתחילה (ab initio). Bedieved — if sprinkled, the offering is accepted. This follows Rabbi Natan.

📋 Pesachim 78 — Summary Map

Core Question: Is the Paschal lamb valid when its meat is ritually impure?
↓
Rabbis: Eating is essential → impure meat = invalid offering → Second Pesaḥ required
Rabbi Natan: Eating is not essential → blood-sprinkling alone fulfills the obligation even with impure meat
↓
Rav rules (per Rabbi Natan): לכתחילה — do not sprinkle if meat is impure. בדיעבד — if sprinkled, the offering is accepted.
↓
Rabbi Yosei's dual position: Agrees with R. Eliezer (after the fact / impure meat case) and with R. Yehoshua (ab initio / lost meat case) — context-dependent, not contradictory.
