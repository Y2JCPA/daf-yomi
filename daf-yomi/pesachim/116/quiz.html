Pesachim 116 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav, what is the 'disgrace' that begins the Maggid?
A. Our ancestors were slaves to Pharaoh in Egypt
B. Our ancestors were idol worshippers in Mesopotamia
C. Our ancestors had no food during the forty years in the desert
D. Our ancestors violated the covenant at Sinai
Correct: B — Rav says the Haggadah should open with spiritual disgrace: 'At first our forefathers were idol worshippers' (Joshua 24:2). Shmuel by contrast says to start with physical disgrace — 'We were slaves to Pharaoh.' Both views are preserved in the Haggadah.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Ami say charoset is brought to the Seder, even though eating it is not technically a mitzva?
A. To fulfill the mitzva of remembering the apple orchards
B. To recall the sweetness of redemption
C. To neutralize the poison of the bitter herbs
D. To symbolize the mortar used in Egypt
Correct: C — Rabbi Ami says charoset is brought due to the poison — the sharp, bitter herbs were considered potentially toxic, and the thick charoset paste neutralizes this danger. Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok adds it is a mitzva in its own right as a remembrance of mortar.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Beit Hillel, until which point does one recite Hallel before the Passover meal?
A. End of Psalm 112
B. End of Psalm 113 (113:9 — 'joyful mother of children')
C. End of Psalm 114 (114:8 — 'rock into a pool of water')
D. End of Psalm 118
Correct: C — Beit Hillel rules one recites until the end of Psalm 114, which culminates with the splitting of the sea and the trembling of the earth before God. Beit Shammai stops one psalm earlier, at 113:9.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Rav Naḥman uses the analogy of a freed slave to explain what obligation at the Seder?
A. Eating maror to recall the bitterness of slavery
B. Reciting Hallel and thanking God for redemption
C. Drinking four cups of wine
D. Reclining at the table like a free person
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman asks his servant Daru: what should a freed slave say to the master who freed him and gave him gold and silver? He must thank and praise him. This exemplifies the obligation of Hallel — praising God who took us from slavery to freedom.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does the Gemara resolve the challenge to Rav Aḥa bar Yaakov's ruling that a blind person is exempt from the Haggadah?
A. Rav Yosef had partial vision and could see the matza
B. Rav Yosef held matza today is rabbinic, and Sages model their laws on Torah — so he recited it rabinically
C. A later ruling overturned the exemption entirely
D. Rav Yosef only recited the blessing, not the full Haggadah
Correct: B — The resolution: Rav Yosef held matza nowadays is only rabbinic (since there is no Pesach offering). Everything the Sages enacted, they enacted in the pattern of Torah law — so a blind person fulfills the rabbinic version even without being able to point at the matza.
