⚖️ Ben Teima vs. the Rabbis: How Similar Is the Chagigah to the Paschal Lamb?

Rabbis
The Festival peace-offering of the 14th is a regular peace-offering — eaten for two days and a night, may come from the herd, and bones may be broken.
Ben Teima
The Chagigah of the 14th is fully assimilated to the Paschal lamb — eaten that night only, roasted, from the flock only, subject to the bone-breaking prohibition.
Rabbis
Our mishna states it is eaten two days and a night — this directly refutes Ben Teima's sweeping comparison.
Ben Teima
"Neither shall the offering of the feast of Passover be left to the morning" (Exodus 34:25) — this verse encompasses both the Paschal lamb and its accompanying Chagigah.
Baraita Resolves A baraita confirms Ben Teima: the Chagigah of the 14th is 'like the Paschal offering in every respect' — from the flock, not left overnight, no bone-breaking permitted.

🗓️ When Is the Festival Peace-Offering of the 14th Brought?

Core rule: The Chagigah of the 14th is not a Torah obligation — it is voluntary, not compulsory
↓
It is brought when the Paschal lamb's portions are too small to satisfy the registered members of the group (less than an olive-bulk per person)
↓
Does NOT override Shabbat — lacking obligatory status, it cannot push aside Shabbat; proof: it is absent when portions are large
↓
Scriptural basis for non-override: "You shall celebrate it as a Festival for the Lord, seven days" (Leviticus 23:41) — the Chagigah spans seven days and need not override any single Shabbat
↓
If consecrated on the 14th but not slaughtered that day: may be offered the next day to fulfill the mitzva of rejoicing (שִׂמְחָה)
↓
Special case: Comes in ritual impurity — when the whole community is impure, a Chagigah is NOT brought alongside the Paschal lamb

🔥 Is the Festival Peace-Offering Eaten Roasted?

Dilemma
According to Ben Teima, who fully compares the Chagigah to the Paschal lamb — is the Chagigah also eaten roasted like the Paschal lamb, or not?
Proof Attempt
The Passover Seder question: "On all other nights we eat meat roasted, boiled, or stewed — on this night only roasted." If the Chagigah exists, it too must be roasted!
Rejection
This Haggadah question does not prove roasting — it merely distinguishes this night. The Chagigah itself might not require roasting.
Baraita
Conclusive: "The Festival peace-offering that comes with the Paschal offering on the 14th is like the Paschal offering in every respect." This settles it — it is eaten roasted.
Conclusion (Ben Teima) The Chagigah of the 14th is eaten roasted. The all-encompassing baraita resolves all three dilemmas at once: roasted, from the flock, and no bone-breaking.

🗡️ The Knife (and Cleaver) Found on the 14th: Whose Mishna?

A knife found on the 14th in Jerusalem — may one use it immediately for slaughter?
↓
Yes — no concern for impurity on the eve of Passover (Jerusalem presumed pure)
↓
But what about the cleaver? Must it be immersed before the Chagigah?
↓
Rabbis: bones may break
Cleaver IS needed for Chagigah → must be immersed if found impure
Ben Teima: no bone-breaking
No cleaver needed at all → even if found, immersion is required precisely because Chagigah prohibits breaking bones
↓
Gemara's conclusion: The mishna reflects Ben Teima's view — the cleaver requires immersion because the Chagigah shares the Paschal lamb's bone-breaking prohibition

📊 Paschal Lamb vs. Chagigah of the 14th: Two Views

🐑 Paschal Lamb (All Agree)

  • From the flock (sheep or goats) only
  • Eaten roasted — not boiled or stewed
  • Not left until morning (אַל תּוֹתִירוּ)
  • No bone-breaking permitted (עֶצֶם לֹא תִשְׁבְּרוּ)
  • Overrides Shabbat (communal obligation)
  • Eaten by registered group members only

🕊️ Chagigah 14 — Ben Teima

  • From the flock only — NOT from the herd
  • Eaten roasted (fully like Paschal lamb)
  • Not left until morning
  • No bone-breaking permitted
  • Does NOT override Shabbat (voluntary)
  • Eaten on night of 14th only

🕊️ Chagigah 14 — Rabbis

  • May come from the herd as well
  • May be boiled or stewed
  • Eaten two days and the intervening night
  • Bones may be broken
  • Does NOT override Shabbat (voluntary)
  • Normal peace-offering rules apply

🏃 Ben Dortai: The Dissenter Who Fled South

וְזָבַחְתָּ פֶּסַח לַה' אֱלֹקֶיךָ צֹאן וּבָקָר (דברים טז:ב)
Historical Controversy Yehuda ben Dortai separated himself from the Rabbis and relocated to the south, placing himself too far from Jerusalem to be obligated in the Paschal offering. His reasoning: The verse "slaughter the Paschal offering from the flock and from the herd" (Deuteronomy 16:2) means the Chagigah is obligatory — and therefore must override Shabbat like all communal obligations. Rather than violate his conviction, he left. The Rabbis' rebuttal (Rav): The phrase "from the herd" does not refer to the Paschal lamb itself — it teaches that an accompanying Chagigah peace-offering is brought from the herd. But this Chagigah is still voluntary and does not override Shabbat. Rav Ashi's sharp dismissal: "Must we arise and explain the reasons of those who separated themselves from the sages?" Ben Dortai broke from the entire scholarly community — his opinion need not be engaged.
