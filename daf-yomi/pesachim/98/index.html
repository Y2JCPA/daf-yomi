⏰ Three Halakhot Derived by Rav Huna bar Rav Yehoshua

שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ חַיָּה נִדְחֵית — שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ דְּחִיָּה מֵעִיקָּרָא הֲוֵי דְּחִיָּה
שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תְּלַת — Conclude Three from This From the mishna about one who separates an unfit female sheep as a Paschal lamb, Rav Huna derives three binding rules:

1️⃣ Living animals can be permanently deferred (נִדְחֵית)
A live animal is not merely suspended — it is fully rejected from its designated purpose.

2️⃣ Deferral at the outset is a permanent deferral
Even if the animal was never fit, its initial consecration is still treated as a real — and permanently rejected — designation.

3️⃣ Deferred animals go to pasture
They graze until they develop a blemish (מוּם), then are sold and proceeds go to nedavah (voluntary offerings).

🌅 When Did the Father Die? — Five Amoraic Opinions on the Tosefta

Rava
Father died before midday. The animal was not yet a Paschal lamb. 'Bring it as a Paschal lamb' means the son registers himself as new owner and brings it for himself.
Abaye
The Tosefta is disjunctive — two separate cases: (1) father died after midday, son was registered → Paschal lamb; (2) father died after midday, son was NOT registered → peace-offering.
Rav Sherevya
Father died after midday, but the father was oleh regel — on his way up to Jerusalem. His death does not permanently reject the animal, so a peace-offering remains valid.
Rav Ashi
After midday — but the Tosefta follows Rabbi Shimon, who holds that living animals are never permanently deferred. Midday does not lock the sacrifice irreversibly.
Ravina
Animal was separated after midday and owners died after midday; follows Rabbi Zeira — midday alone does not determine status, so the animal can still be offered as a peace-offering.
The Core Tension If the father died before midday — why does the Tosefta say 'bring it as a Paschal lamb' (it wasn't one yet)? If after midday — why allow a peace-offering (it should be fully locked in)?

🔀 Paschal Lamb Intermingled with Firstborn Animals

Rabbi Shimon

  • Permits causing offerings to be disqualified (לְגָרֵם פְּסוּל)
  • Priests: eat all intermingled animals on Passover night under Paschal lamb rules
  • Firstborn is treated more strictly (eaten indoors, at night) — but priests eat it regardless
  • No loss: priests eat the firstborn anyway, and Paschal rules simply govern when and where
  • Opinion: the stricter rules do not waste the offering, they merely restrict its consumption

The Rabbis

  • Forbids causing offerings to be disqualified
  • Treating a peace-offering under Paschal lamb rules shortens its permitted eating window — leading to notar (leftover that must be burned)
  • This constitutes an active disqualification, which is prohibited
  • Rava's ruling: Wait until the animal develops a blemish (מוּם)
  • After the blemish: redeem it, treat proceeds as ordinary funds, eat the animal without restrictions
