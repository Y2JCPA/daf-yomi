🌊 The Impurity of Temple Liquids

Rav
The priests erred — they should have said the oil becomes impure. Haggai's dilemma concerned 4th-degree (revi'i) impurity: the garment itself was impure and touched the bread.
Shmuel
The priests did not err. Haggai's dilemma concerned 5th-degree (chamishi) impurity: the garment was only a sheini, and the chain of contact was more distant.
The Core Question Haggai 2:12 — Can consecrated food (kodesh) contract impurity through indirect contact? Four items tested: bread, stew, wine, and oil.

🏛️ Rav vs Shmuel: Parsing Haggai's Words

Rav
Four items (bread, stew, wine, oil) are written because a 4-step chain is what creates 4th-degree impurity for consecrated food. This validates the revi'i reading.
Shmuel
The verse says 'v'naga kanafō' — "its corner touched", not that the primary impurity touched. The indirect phrasing implies the garment was already secondary — pointing to a 5th-degree chain.
Rav Naḥman
(Rabba bar Avuh)
The priests were expert in corpse-impurity (the severe dilemma in verse 13) but uncertain about remote degrees. Their accurate answer in verse 13 proves competence — not error.
Ravina
The two dilemmas differ in severity: verse 12 concerns 4th-degree; verse 13 (corpse-impurity person touching food) concerns 3rd-degree — a lesser chain. Priests knew the stricter case.
חגי ב:יג "And Haggai said: If one who is impure with corpse-impurity touches any of these, shall he be impure? And the priests said: He shall be impure."

🍶 Yosei ben Yo'ezer's Testimony: Two Interpretations

Rav's Version

  • Testimony concerns liquids of the slaughterhouse (blood and water)
  • These liquids are actually ritually pure — they cannot become tamei at all
  • Wine and oil (altar liquids) can still become impure under normal rules
  • Decree is specific to beit hamitbachayim (the slaughterhouse area)

Levi's Version

  • Testimony concerns liquids of the altar (wine and oil)
  • These liquids do not transmit impurity to other consecrated items
  • Even if the liquids become impure themselves, they cannot render kodesh pasul
  • Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi limits this: only in their place (inside the Temple)

Rav Pappa's Resolution

  • Purity of slaughterhouse liquids = halakha l'Moshe mi-Sinai
  • A tradition from Sinai, not derived from Torah text
  • Challenged by Rav Huna b. Rav Natan: cannot derive rulings from Sinai-traditions
  • Challenged by Ravina: Rabban Shimon's ruling implies this is not a Sinai tradition

📜 Rabbi Yehuda on Liquid Impurity: Retraction or Distinction?

Original ruling (baraita): Rabbi Yehuda says liquids that contacted impure items are impure in all cases — implying liquid impurity is by Torah law
↓
Challenge from Mishna: Rabbi Yehuda himself rules that a vessel impure on its exterior (from liquids) does not render its interior impure — but if Torah law, why distinguish interior vs exterior?
↓
Ravina's defense: The mishna addresses liquids that became impure through a zav (a special rabbinic addition) — not a general retraction of the Torah-law position
↓
Gemara's counter: If so, the mishna should have said "in what case?" differently — the phrasing suggests a real limitation, not just a scope clarification
↓
Open dilemma: Did Rabbi Yehuda retract only regarding liquids-to-vessels, or also regarding liquids-to-foods? Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak cites the parah adumah cow (drank purification water) — daf ends unresolved
