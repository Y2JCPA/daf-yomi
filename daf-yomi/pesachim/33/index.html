🔥 Intentional Misuse: Death Penalty or Offering?

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
One who intentionally misuses consecrated items is liable for death at the hand of Heaven — derived via gezerah shavah from the laws of teruma.
The Rabbis
Intentional misuse does not carry the death penalty. The offender brings an offering, but there is no divine death sentence.
Rav Pappa
How do you know Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi follows the Rabbis' olive-bulk rule? Perhaps he follows Abba Shaul: both conditions are required — worth a peruta AND an olive-bulk in volume.
Mar son of Rabbana
Read the baraita differently: for most mitzvot, unintentional ≠ intentional — but for misuse of consecrated property, even unintentional misuse incurs liability akin to intentional.
📜 The Gezerah Shavah The word חֵטְא (sin) appears both in the laws of teruma and in the laws of me'ila (misuse of consecrated property), creating a verbal analogy that equates their punishments and minimum measures.

🍇 Do Liquids Absorb Inside a Grape?

Rabbi Yoḥanan (via Rav Aḥa)
Press impure grapes less than an egg-bulk at a time — the wine that flows is kosher, since liquid impurity requires an egg-bulk to transfer.
Rav Ḥisda
Who will accept this? Where did the impurity go? The liquid was inside the impure grape all along — pressing cannot purify what was already impure.
Rav Aḥa bar Rav Avya
Proof from the mishna: squeezing an exact egg-bulk of impure olives/grapes causes liability — implying less than egg-bulk does not convey impurity to the juice!
Rav Ḥisda (reply)
That mishna deals with grapes not yet rendered susceptible to impurity — they had no contact with liquids. Unsusceptible produce cannot convey impurity to its juice.
⚖️ Key Distinction The dispute turns on whether juice inside a fruit shares the fruit's susceptibility status or is treated as an independent liquid already susceptible.

📐 Me'ila vs. Teruma: The Gezerah Shavah

מְעִילָה — Misuse of Consecrated Property

  • Word חֵטְא (sin) in the Torah passage on me'ila
  • Minimum measure: olive-bulk (derived by analogy)
  • Intentional: death at hand of Heaven (Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi)
  • Unintentional: ram brought as guilt offering
  • Abba Shaul: BOTH a peruta's worth AND olive-bulk required

תְּרוּמָה — Priestly Tithe

  • Word חֵטְא (sin) in the Torah passage on teruma
  • Minimum measure: olive-bulk for punishment
  • Intentional eating by non-priest: death at hand of Heaven
  • Unintentional eating: pays principal + one-fifth
  • Worthless items (no market value) cannot be designated teruma

🕯️ Using Impure Teruma Permissibly

Premise: Teruma that became ritually impure may not be eaten by the priest — its primary permitted use is lost.
↓
Permitted use: One may light lamps with bread and oil of impure teruma — it is consumed in a halachically sanctioned manner rather than wasted.
↓
Abaye (citing Ḥizkiya) and Rava (citing Sages from the school of Rav) both limit this ruling — not all circumstances permit it.
↓
Rava adds: A rabbinic decree may prohibit storing impure teruma fruit to press for oil — lest one stumble and eat it accidentally.
↓
Principle: Impure teruma retains sacred status. It cannot be carelessly discarded — it must be destroyed or used in a defined, dignified halachic manner.
