QUESTION 1 OF 5
From where does Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi derive that intentionally misusing consecrated items incurs death at the hand of Heaven?
A. An explicit verse in Leviticus stating the penalty directly
B. A gezerah shavah using the word חֵטְא in both teruma and me'ila passages
C. The principle that all Temple-related sins carry the death penalty
D. A tradition transmitted orally from Moshe at Sinai
Correct: B — Rabbi Abbahu explains that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi uses a verbal analogy (gezerah shavah): the same word חֵטְא appears in the laws of teruma — where intentional eating by a non-priest brings death — and in the laws of me'ila, equating their punishments.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Abba Shaul, what two conditions must both be met for full liability in misuse of consecrated property?
A. Intent and prior warning by witnesses
B. Damage to Temple property and monetary gain
C. Worth at least a peruta AND at least an olive-bulk in volume
D. Awareness of the prohibition and intentional action
Correct: C — Abba Shaul requires two independent thresholds: the misused item must be worth at least a peruta (monetary minimum) AND must be at least an olive-bulk in volume (physical minimum). Both must be met simultaneously for full liability.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does Rav Ḥisda reject the ruling that pressing impure grapes less than an egg-bulk at a time produces kosher wine?
A. Because grapes themselves cannot contract ritual impurity
B. Because liquid impurity is stricter than solid impurity in all cases
C. Because the juice was stored inside the impure grape and should already be considered impure
D. Because Rabbi Yoḥanan's ruling applied only to olives, not grapes
Correct: C — Rav Ḥisda challenges: where did the impurity go? Since the juice resided inside the impure grape, pressing it out cannot purify it. He holds that liquids stored inside fruit share the fruit's impure status from the moment impurity is contracted.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What does the verse 'The first fruits of your grain, your wine, your oil…' (Devarim 18:4) teach about what can be designated as teruma?
A. That only the finest produce may be designated as teruma
B. That the item must retain recognizable value — a worthless item cannot become teruma
C. That only grain, wine, and oil are subject to the teruma obligation
D. That teruma must be given before all other priestly gifts
Correct: B — Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua derives that the designated item must remain recognizable as an Israelite's valued possession — it must have the character of 'first fruits.' A completely worthless item therefore has no valid teruma status.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What permitted use exists for teruma oil or bread that has become ritually impure?
A. It may be sold to a non-Jew at a reduced price
B. It must be buried in the ground without delay
C. It may be used as fuel to light lamps
D. It may be eaten by the priest after immersing in a mikveh
Correct: C — The Gemara teaches that one may light lamps with ritually impure teruma bread and oil. Since impure teruma cannot be eaten, burning it as lamp fuel is a legitimate halachic use — it is consumed respectfully rather than discarded, preserving the honor of its sacred status.
