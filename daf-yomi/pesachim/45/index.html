⚖️ Can Two Verses Yield a General Principle?

Rabbi Akiva
The nazirite and sin-offering cases are two verses that come as one — they teach the same rule (taste = substance) and therefore cannot extend that rule to other cases by analogy.
The Rabbis
Both verses are necessary: nazirite teaches permitted + prohibited combining, sin-offering teaches prohibited + prohibited. Each is unique — they are not two verses that come as one, and the principle applies broadly.
Rabbi Akiva
Both cases equally teach that a permitted substance joins with a prohibited one. They are two verses that come as one — ta'am ke'ikar cannot be extended beyond these specific cases.
Rav Kahana
The nazirite verse is still needed! Permitted + prohibited substances combine only when eaten simultaneously, whereas prohibited + prohibited join together even at different times — they remain distinct lessons.
Key Term Ta'am ke'ikar (טַעַם כְּעִיקָּר) — the legal status of the taste of a prohibited substance is equivalent to the substance itself. Derived from nazirite (Numbers 6:4) and sin-offering (Leviticus 6:20).

🔩 Contradictory Baraitot: Lenient vs. Stringent

Rav Huna
Delete the first lenient baraita — the stringent one is correct and the lenient baraita is simply erroneous.
Rav Yosef
Have you removed the tannaim from the world?! It is a legitimate tannaitic dispute — the lenient baraita reflects Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's view.
R. Shimon ben Elazar
A hardened mass of leaven designated for sitting upon — not for consumption — is nullified. It is no longer considered food and loses its forbidden status.
First Tanna
An olive-bulk of leaven cannot be nullified regardless of designation — once it was food, it remains forbidden throughout Passover.
Rav Naḥman's Ruling The halakha follows Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar — a mass of leaven covered with mortar or designated for non-food use is nullified. (Though a dispute between amoraim on Rav's precise opinion is noted.)

🧵 Half-Olive Portions: Location Changes Everything

In a Kneading Bowl

  • Two half-olive portions stuck to separate sides of bowl
  • Isolated — not obligated to remove
  • A string of dough connects them across the bowl
  • If pulling the string would bring portions together → obligated to remove
  • If string would not bring them together → not obligated
  • Rav Naḥman citing Shmuel

In a House

  • Two half-olive portions scattered in the house
  • Always obligated to remove — even without a connecting string
  • Reason: while cleaning, pieces may roll together and combine
  • Combined = full olive-bulk of leaven = biblical violation
  • Stricter standard for open spaces than for fixed bowls
  • Ulla — stricter ruling for open areas

Unresolved Dilemmas

  • One piece in house, one in upper story
  • One piece in house, one in a portico
  • Do physically separated spaces count as one domain?
  • Can they combine to create an olive-bulk obligation?
  • Teiku — let these stand unresolved
  • Dilemmas raised by Sages of Eretz Yisrael (Ulla)

🥩 Deriving Ta'am Ke'ikar: From Verse to Principle

Source 1 — Nazirite (Numbers 6:4): "He shall eat nothing made of the grapevine, from the grape skins to the grape seed" — even the taste of grape products is forbidden, demonstrating that taste carries the same legal status as substance.
↓
Source 2 — Sin-Offering (Leviticus 6:20): Ordinary meat that contacts sin-offering meat "shall become consecrated" — it absorbs the status of the sin-offering through contact/taste, a second derivation for ta'am ke'ikar.
↓
The Rabbis' position: Nazirite teaches permitted+prohibited combining; sin-offering teaches prohibited+prohibited combining. These are distinct lessons — both verses are necessary, so the principle extends broadly to all cases.
↓
Rabbi Akiva's position: Both verses teach the identical rule — a permitted substance joins with a prohibited one. They are therefore two-verses-as-one (שְׁנֵי כְּתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד) and yield no general derivation.
↓
Rav Ashi to Rav Kahana: But the nazirite verse also teaches prohibited+prohibited combining — so even Rabbi Akiva should need it separately from the sin-offering case.
↓
Rav Kahana's resolution: Permitted+prohibited substances combine only when eaten simultaneously; prohibited+prohibited combine even at different times. The nazirite verse teaches the former, sin-offering the latter — genuinely distinct, confirming the Rabbis' position.
