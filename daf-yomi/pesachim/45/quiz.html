QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Akiva, why can the principle of ta'am ke'ikar not be derived generally from the nazirite and sin-offering verses?
A. The two cases contradict each other and cancel each other out
B. They are two verses teaching the same principle, so no general rule can be extended from them
C. The nazirite case applies only to wine, not to other prohibitions
D. Rabbi Akiva holds that taste is never prohibited — only the substance itself
Correct: B — Rabbi Akiva applies the rule of 'shnei ketuvim haba'im ke'echad' — when two verses independently teach the same principle, that principle cannot be extended by analogy to other cases.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
When is one NOT obligated to remove even an olive-bulk of dough from the cracks of a kneading bowl, according to Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel?
A. When the dough has hardened and is no longer edible by humans
B. When the dough serves to reinforce the structural integrity of the bowl
C. When the dough was placed there less than three days before Passover
D. When the bowl belongs to a non-Jew who left it with the Jew
Correct: B — Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel rules that dough serving to reinforce the kneading bowl is considered structurally part of the vessel and is nullified — one is not obligated to remove it even if it reaches an olive-bulk.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How did Rav Yosef respond to Rav Huna's suggestion to simply delete the lenient baraita that contradicted the stringent one?
A. He agreed that the lenient baraita was a scribal error and should be deleted
B. He said the lenient baraita refers to a different type of kneading bowl
C. He objected that it represents a legitimate tannaitic dispute — specifically Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's opinion
D. He said both baraitot are correct but apply to different times of Passover
Correct: C — Rav Yosef famously objected 'Have you removed the tannaim from the world?!' — arguing the contradiction reflects a genuine tannaitic dispute, with Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar holding that leaven designated for non-food use (e.g., sitting upon) is nullified.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Ulla, why must one remove two separate half-olive portions of leaven found in a house, even without any string connecting them?
A. Because the laws of nullification (bitul) never apply in a private domain
B. Because Passover inspection requires removing all visible traces of leaven regardless of size
C. Because while cleaning the house one may inadvertently push the pieces together, forming a full olive-bulk
D. Because the two pieces together have the combined smell of a full olive-bulk of leaven
Correct: C — Ulla reasons that in an open house, a person sweeping and cleaning may cause the two pieces to roll adjacent to each other, at which point they would constitute a full olive-bulk — therefore preemptive removal is required.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the fundamental distinction Abaye draws between leaven removal law and interposition law in immersion?
A. Leaven laws are biblical while interposition laws are only rabbinic in origin
B. Leaven removal depends on objective quantity (olive-bulk), while interposition depends on the owner's subjective particularity
C. Leaven laws apply only to edible food; immersion laws apply to all objects including inedible ones
D. The kneading bowl is subject to leaven laws but actually exempt from immersion interposition laws
Correct: B — Abaye clarifies that the mishna's comparison is actually a contrast: for leaven, the obligation depends on objective quantity (the olive-bulk threshold), while for immersion, the question of whether dough constitutes an interposition depends on whether the owner is particular about it — a purely subjective standard.
