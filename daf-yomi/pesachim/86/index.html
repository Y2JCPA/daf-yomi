📜 The Great Ketiv/Kri Dispute

Rabbi Yehuda
The consonantal text (ketiv) is authoritative. "Upon the houses" is written in plural — so one Paschal lamb may be eaten in two separate groups in one house.
Rabbi Shimon
The vocalized text (kri) is authoritative. "Upon the houses" is read in singular — so one person who eats the Paschal lamb may eat it in two different locations.
The Verse "Upon the houses wherein they shall eat it" (Exodus 12:7) — ketiv: בָּתִּים (batim, plural); kri: בַּיִת (bayit, singular)

🐑 One Lamb in Two Groups vs. One Person in Two Places

Rabbi Yehuda
Follows ketiv (written form). Plural batim = one Paschal lamb may be eaten in two separate groups. But they must be in the same house — a single animal shared by two distinct groups sitting apart.
Rabbi Shimon
Follows kri (vocalized form). Singular bayit = one individual person may eat the Paschal lamb in two different places — moving from location to location during the meal.
Mishna Rule Two groups eating one Paschal lamb in one house: each group turns their faces away from the other while eating. The attendant warming himself by the fire may rejoin either group.

🪞 Partitions: Splitting and Joining Paschal Groups

Partition Erected Mid-Meal

  • One group becomes two distinct groups
  • R. Yehuda: each sub-group needs its own lamb registration
  • R. Shimon: counts as eating in a new place — permitted for individuals
  • Rav Kahana taught this as a clear ruling
  • Rav Ashi challenged: what if erected and immediately removed?

Partition Removed Mid-Meal

  • Two groups merge into one shared space
  • R. Yehuda: now one group, the joint eating is valid
  • R. Shimon: new location created — person may now eat here
  • Removing partition = creating a new unified location
  • Group identity tied to physical space, not just intent

🍷 Proper Table Conduct: Rav Huna at Rav Naḥman's House

Hosts ask his name. Rav Huna replies with his title — "Rav Huna" — without false modesty.
↓
Hosts puzzled: Isn't stating one's own title a sign of conceit and arrogance?
↓
Rav Huna explains: I have been called this name since my youth. I am stating my identity, not boasting. Changing it now would be deceptive.
↓
Hosts ask: Why did you recline immediately when you arrived, without waiting to be seated?
↓
Rav Huna explains: One follows the host's directive — you invited me in, so I acted on that invitation as instructed.
↓
Hosts ask: Why did you accept the cup of wine immediately, without politely declining first?
↓
Rav Huna explains: One may defer to a lesser person, but one may not refuse a great person. Refusing an important host's offer is itself disrespectful.
