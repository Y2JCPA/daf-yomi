💡 The Central Dispute: What Does אוֹר Mean?

Rav Huna
אוֹר = light. The Mishna says to search by candlelight — fitting because the search begins at the start of night when one lights candles to see
Rav Yehuda
אוֹר = evening/night. The Rabbis use a euphemism — they call darkness 'light,' just as they call a blind person sagi nahor (one full of light)
Why It Matters The entire opening of Pesachim turns on one word: "Or of the 14th, one searches for chametz by candlelight." Whether אוֹר means the preceding night or the morning of the 14th affects when the search — and the entire Pesach preparation — begins.

📜 The Mishna: Bedikat Chametz

When: On or (אוֹר) of the 14th of Nisan — the eve before Passover
↓
How: Search by candlelight (נר) — a candle reaches crevices better than a torch or bonfire
↓
Where: Every location in the home where chametz is ordinarily brought
↓
Exemption: Places where chametz is never taken do not require searching — we don't burden the public unnecessarily
↓
Rabbinic basis: The candle requirement signals the Rabbis' concern for thorough, careful inspection before Pesach

💡 Rav Huna vs. Rav Yehuda: The Meaning of אוֹר

Rav Huna
אוֹר = light. The Mishna is describing nightfall — the moment the 14th begins, when candles are lit and one searches by their light
Gemara
Objection: If אוֹר means 'light' (i.e., daytime), why is a candle needed? One does not light candles to search in broad daylight!
Rav Yehuda
אוֹר = evening. The Rabbis use a euphemism for darkness — just as the blind are called sagi nahor (abundant in light), the night is called אוֹר
Gemara
Support: The candle is needed precisely because it is night. This clinches that the search takes place at nightfall — consistent with Rav Yehuda's interpretation
Key Inference The Mishna's stipulation of candlelight (נר) is the decisive clue: one searches by candle because it is dark — אוֹר means evening/night.

⚖️ R. Eliezer ben Ya'akov vs. R. Yehuda: Labor on Erev Pesach

R. Eliezer b. Ya'akov
Labor is forbidden on Erev Pesach from אוֹר of the 14th — meaning from nightfall at the start of the 14th
R. Yehuda
Labor is forbidden only from sunrise on the 14th. Therefore אוֹר as used by R. Eliezer ben Ya'akov must mean evening/night — confirming our view!
R. Eliezer b. Ya'akov
Precedent exists for a night distinct from its following day: the night of Passover itself has unique laws separate from Passover day. Such distinctions are established.
R. Yehuda
The 14th itself proves my point! During part of it eating chametz is permitted, during part it is forbidden. Even a single day can be internally divided.
R. Eliezer b. Ya'akov
That division is only rabbinic — the Torah forbids chametz only from midday. The Rabbis can legislate a preventive fence; such decrees exist for Torah-level laws.
Gemara's Conclusion From R. Yehuda's position (labor forbidden from sunrise), the Gemara infers that R. Eliezer ben Ya'akov uses אוֹר to mean evening — the entire debate presupposes this reading.
