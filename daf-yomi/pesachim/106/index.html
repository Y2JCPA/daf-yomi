🍷 Rav Ashi: Are 'Tasting' and 'Minimum Measure' One Ruling or Two?

The Baraita
Two rules are stated: (1) once someone has tasted the cup, it is disqualified as a cup of blessing; (2) a cup of blessing requires a minimum measure. These appear to be two independent laws.
Rav Ashi
Upon close examination, these are actually one matter. The reason tasting disqualifies the cup is precisely because a cup of blessing requires a minimum measure — once someone drinks from it, the remaining wine may fall below that required amount.
The Baraita
But the baraita also permits reciting two entirely unrelated blessings over a single cup — suggesting cups can serve multiple purposes?
Rav Ashi
That is a separate teaching. The unifying point is about measure: so long as the full requisite amount remains in the cup, it may be used. Tasting from it creates the risk of shortfall — hence the disqualification.
Practical Stringency Rabbi Ya'akov bar Idi refused to recite kiddush or havdala with a pitcher from which anyone had already sipped. Rav Idi bar Sheisha was stringent about any blemished vessel.

🕯️ Kiddush: Friday Night vs. Shabbat Day

Night Kiddush — Friday Eve

  • Source: 'Remember the Shabbat' — recite at its entry
  • The essential fulfillment of the kiddush mitzvah
  • Includes Vaykhulu and full kiddush text
  • Must be recited before any eating on Friday night
  • Tasting food beforehand disqualifies one from reciting it
  • Washing hands before kiddush creates an interruption problem

Day Kiddush — Shabbat Morning

  • Derived from 'the day' in the verse — a secondary obligation
  • Recited before the Shabbat morning meal
  • Rav Yehuda: simply recite Borei peri hagafen over wine
  • No extended kiddush text — the wine blessing suffices
  • Residents of Meḥoza called this the 'great kiddush' (Rav Ashi was puzzled)
  • If night kiddush was missed, day kiddush serves as makeup

זָכוֹר אֶת יוֹם הַשַּׁבָּת לְקַדְּשׁוֹ — שמות כ:ז
📖 The Biblical Derivation — מְקוֹר The Gemara investigates the verse: "זָכוֹר אֶת יוֹם הַשַּׁבָּת לְקַדְּשׁוֹ" — 'Remember the day of Shabbat to sanctify it'. The Sages derive: remember it over wine — kiddush must be verbal, spoken over a cup. The Gemara initially asks: night kiddush seems obvious from the verse, so why derive it? It corrects itself: on the contrary, night kiddush is the essential obligation (one must sanctify Shabbat as it enters). The real derivation is for day kiddush — from 'the day' in the verse. Thus both times are obligated, with night being primary. The word זָכוֹר (remember/mention) specifically implies verbal declaration over wine.
