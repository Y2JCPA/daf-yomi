🏠 One Pile, Two Houses — Where Did the Mouse Go?

Scenario
One pile of leaven sits between two searched houses. A mouse takes a morsel. We don't know which house it entered.
Rabbi Yehuda
If each homeowner asks a Sage separately, each retains his presumptive purity — we judge them individually.
Rabbi Yehuda
But if they both come to ask at the same time, they are both impure — the doubt must resolve against someone.
Rava
If they came simultaneously — everyone agrees: both impure. If independently: everyone agrees each is pure. The dispute only applies to a middle case.
Key Principle Simultaneous doubt forces us to assign impurity; sequential doubt allows each person's presumptive status to stand.

⚖️ Nine Morsels vs. Ten — Who Added the Extra?

Placed 9, Found 10

  • Mouse added a morsel from outside
  • Akin to: person left 100 dinars, found 200
  • Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: the original 100 retain second-tithe status
  • Rabbis: all money is non-sacred — we can't identify the original
  • Practical ruling: must search/remove all ten morsels (stricter view applies to chametz)

Placed 10, Found 9

  • Mouse removed a morsel — one is missing
  • Akin to: person left 200 dinars, found 100
  • Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: remaining 100 are second-tithe
  • Rabbis: all non-sacred — whoever took some presumably took all
  • For chametz: uncertainty whether leaven remains in house requires further concern

🕯️ The Three Times for Bedikat Chametz (Rabbi Yehuda)

1st opportunity: Evening of the 14th of Nisan — the primary, preferred time for bedikat chametz (searching by candlelight)
↓
2nd opportunity: Morning of the 14th — before the prohibition on eating chametz takes effect (before the 5th or 6th hour)
↓
3rd opportunity: Time of bi'ur (removal) — when chametz must be burned/destroyed, one may search and immediately remove
↓
After these three times have passed: Rabbi Yehuda — no further searching is permitted
↓
The Rabbis disagree: If one did not search before Pesach, search during the Festival; if not then, search after — though that chametz is already forbidden from benefit
↓
Rav Ḥisda & Rabba bar Rav Huna: Rabbi Yehuda's three times correspond to three required searches — not merely three permitted windows

📜 Mishna Dispute: When Must One Search?

Rabbi Yehuda
One must search on the eve of the 14th, on the morning of the 14th, and at the time of bi'ur. These are the only valid windows.
The Rabbis
If one did not search before Pesach — search during the Festival. If not during — search after the Festival, even though the leaven is already forbidden.
Rav Yosef
Objection: Even Rabbi Yehuda agrees one need not conduct three searches! He merely lists three permissible times — any one suffices.
Mar Zutra
In my version of Rav Yosef's statement: Rabbi Yehuda says explicitly — 'Anyone who did not search at one of these three times may no longer search.' This proves they are a fixed deadline, not three mandatory searches.
Gemara Conclusion
Rabbi Yehuda agrees: miss the first opportunity, use the second or third — but once all three have passed, no further searching is valid.
Core Dispute Rabbi Yehuda: prohibition on eating chametz creates a deadline — one may not search once eating is forbidden. Rabbis: the obligation to search persists even during Pesach.
