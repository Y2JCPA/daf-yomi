Pesachim 10 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
If two homeowners each ask a Sage separately about a mouse that may have carried leaven into one of their houses, what is the ruling?
A. Both are impure, since the leaven is a Torah prohibition
B. Each retains his presumptive purity, since the doubt is evaluated individually
C. The one who asked first is pure; the second is impure
D. Both must burn their chametz immediately
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda rules that when each person asks separately, each retains his presumptive status of purity. The doubt is evaluated on its own terms for each individual.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Rava ruled that if a mouse enters a house with a loaf but only crumbs are found, the house requires further searching. Why?
A. Crumbs indicate the mouse ate the loaf and may have left impurity
B. A mouse does not typically generate crumbs, so the original loaf is probably still there
C. Crumbs are themselves chametz and must be removed
D. The presence of crumbs proves a second mouse entered
Correct: B — Rava's reasoning is that mice do not normally produce crumbs — they eat or carry whole pieces. Therefore, the crumbs cannot account for the original loaf, which is presumed still somewhere in the house.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
The case of 'placed nine morsels and found ten' is compared to which financial scenario?
A. A person who lost his wallet and found a larger one
B. A person who left 100 dinars of second-tithe money and returned to find 200
C. Two merchants disputing ownership of the same coin
D. A person who placed money in escrow and found it doubled
Correct: B — The Gemara explicitly compares it to someone who left 100 dinars of second-tithe money and found 200. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi says the original 100 retain their status; the Rabbis say all is non-sacred since you cannot identify the original.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yehuda, what happens if one did not search for chametz at any of the three designated times?
A. He must sell all his chametz to a non-Jew immediately
B. He may search at any point during Pesach
C. He may no longer conduct a valid search after those three windows have passed
D. He is obligated to fast for missing the mitzvah
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehuda holds that the three times (eve of 14th, morning of 14th, time of bi'ur) are the only valid windows. Once all three have passed — particularly once eating chametz is prohibited — no further search is valid.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is Rava's unresolved dilemma about a loaf of bread found on a high beam?
A. Whether the beam is considered part of the house for purposes of the search
B. Whether one is obligated to climb a ladder to remove it, or if the Sages did not require such exertion
C. Whether leaven on a beam is subject to the same laws as leaven on the ground
D. Whether the beam's owner or the house's owner is responsible for the leaven
Correct: B — Rava left unresolved whether the obligation to remove chametz before Pesach extends to exerting significant effort such as climbing a ladder. The concern is that if it falls, one might come to eat it — but the Sages may not have imposed such physical exertion.
