Pesachim 19 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Akiva's testimony, consecrated items (kodashim) can assume impurity up to which degree?
A. Second degree
B. Third degree
C. Fourth degree
D. Fifth degree
Correct: C — Rabbi Akiva's baraita explicitly states: fourth degree, yes; fifth degree, no — this applies to fine flour, incense, frankincense, and coals used in the Temple.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why did the Gemara (Rava) conclude that the knife used to cut meat containing the Temple needle is pure?
A. Because hands and vessels are exempt from impurity in the Temple
B. Because the knife never actually touched anything impure
C. Because Rabbi Akiva testified that knives are always exempt
D. Because the needle had already been purified by the water
Correct: B — Rava explained that the meat itself (from a living animal) carries no impurity, and the needle was embedded inside the meat — so the knife had no direct contact with any impure object.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rav Ashi classify the Temple courtyard with respect to laws of uncertain ritual impurity?
A. As a private domain — doubt makes the object impure
B. As a sacred domain fully exempt from impurity laws
C. As a public domain — doubt leaves the object pure
D. As requiring individual priestly rulings case by case
Correct: C — Rav Ashi rules that the Temple courtyard has the status of a public domain for safek tuma — therefore uncertainty resolves to pure, not impure.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the purity status of spittle found in the Upper Market of Jerusalem?
A. Pure, because all Jerusalem spittle is assumed pure
B. Impure, because the Upper Market is frequented by gentiles
C. Requires immersion but presumed pure until used
D. Disputed between Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Yosei ben Rabbi Ḥanina
Correct: B — The mishna teaches: all spittle in Jerusalem is pure except for the Upper Market, which is frequented by gentiles — shifting the majority and rendering the spittle impure.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why can the usual 'asking' rule (used for the Sotah in a private domain) not be applied to the Temple needle?
A. Because Temple objects follow different Biblical rules
B. Because metal utensils are always classified as pure in Temple precincts
C. Because a needle is an inanimate object with no knowledge (daat) and cannot be questioned
D. Because the needle's impurity was already confirmed by witnesses
Correct: C — The Gemara notes that the private-domain rule allowing questioning applies to a person (who has daat). A needle has no knowledge and cannot be asked — yet this is moot anyway, since the Temple courtyard is a public domain where doubt = pure.
