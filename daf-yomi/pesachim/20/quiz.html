QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why was chibbat hakodesh (esteem for sacred objects) ultimately invoked to explain how the meat containing the impure needle became susceptible to impurity?
A. Because blood of consecrated offerings always renders food susceptible to impurity
B. Because slaughterhouse water is brought against the owner's will and therefore renders food susceptible
C. Because both slaughter blood and consented water were ruled out as sources of susceptibility, leaving only chibbat hakodesh as the explanation
D. Because the Torah explicitly states that sacred meat is always susceptible to impurity
Correct: C — Rabbi Yoḥanan eliminates slaughter blood (it does not render consecrated food susceptible), and Rabbi Yosei b. Rabbi Ḥanina eliminates consented slaughterhouse water. With all Torah-level sources ruled out, the Gemara invokes chibbat hakodesh as a rabbinic mechanism.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is Reish Lakish's unresolved dilemma regarding chibbat hakodesh?
A. Whether a needle found inside a live animal transmits impurity to the surrounding meat
B. Whether a mass of meal-offering, due to chibbat hakodesh, itself becomes a source from which first- and second-degree impurity are counted upon contact
C. Whether slaughterhouse water counts as a liquid that renders food susceptible by Torah law
D. Whether blood of non-consecrated animals can render sacred food susceptible to impurity
Correct: B — Reish Lakish asks: since chibbat hakodesh makes the offering susceptible to impurity, does that also mean that contact with a meal-offering mass creates first- and second-degree impurity in others? The Gemara does not resolve this.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Ḥisda's resolution, whose opinion does Rabbi Yehoshua's ruling (that teruma in abeyance may be burned with impure teruma) actually reflect?
A. Rabbi Akiva, who held that oil disqualified by a tevul yom may be burned in an impure lamp
B. Rabbi Eliezer, who permits burning different grades of teruma together on Shabbat
C. Rabbi Shimon, who holds that burning involves only passive contact and not active rendering impure
D. Rabbi Meir, who follows Rabbi Ḥanina the deputy High Priest in all matters of teruma
Correct: C — Rav Ḥisda resolves the contradiction by explaining that Rabbi Yehoshua's lenient ruling aligns with Rabbi Shimon's position: burning together is considered passive contact, not actively rendering impure — which even stringent authorities might permit.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rabbi Elazar resolve the apparent contradiction between Rabbi Yehoshua's general prohibition on actively rendering teruma impure and his ruling permitting it in the broken-barrel case?
A. Rabbi Yehoshua only applies his prohibition to first-degree impurity, not second-degree
B. The broken-barrel case involves the potential loss of non-sacred produce — this great financial loss justifies actively rendering the teruma impure
C. Rabbi Yehoshua's ruling about the broken barrel is a minority view that was later retracted
D. In the broken-barrel case the teruma was already definitively impure, so no new act of rendering impure occurs
Correct: B — Rabbi Elazar explains that the broken-barrel scenario is unique: if the teruma wine flows into the lower press, it will ruin the non-sacred wine there. This potential for significant financial loss to others is a special circumstance that justifies the active rendering impure.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does the Gemara conclude that impure teruma OIL may not be actively rendered impure, even though impure teruma WINE sometimes may be?
A. Oil has a higher level of sanctity than wine and therefore stricter rules apply
B. Oil spoils faster and cannot be used for any purpose once impure, unlike wine
C. Wine has a secondary permissible use (sprinkling) that creates real risk of accidental consumption; impure oil placed in a repulsive vessel will not be accidentally drunk
D. The Torah explicitly prohibits actively rendering oil impure but is silent regarding wine
Correct: C — Shmuel taught that wine is used for both drinking and sprinkling — a legitimate secondary use that creates concern people might accidentally consume impure teruma wine. Impure oil, placed in a repulsive vessel, will not be drunk. This practical difference drives the stricter rule for oil.
