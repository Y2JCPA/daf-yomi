🎉 Simcha, Sacrifice, and Shabbat Liability

Ulla
Peace-offerings slaughtered on the 14th of Nisan fulfill the mitzva of simcha (rejoicing) on the Festival itself.
Rav Yosef
A baraita states: one fulfills shalmei simcha with that offering, but does not fulfill the primary mitzva of rejoicing — contradicting Ulla!
Rav Ashi
The baraita speaks of an offering slaughtered on the 15th, not the 14th — consistent with Ulla's ruling about the 14th.
Rava
On Sukkot, hallel and rejoicing span 8 days — if fresh slaughter is required for simcha, how does this work on Shabbat when slaughter is forbidden?
Core Question Can a Shalmei Chagiga slaughtered before the Festival fulfill the mitzva of simcha on the Festival day itself? And what are the Shabbat liability rules for wrong-purpose Paschal offerings?

🕊️ Ulla's Position and the Amoraic Challenges

Ulla
Peace-offerings slaughtered on the 14th (erev Pesach) fulfill simcha on the Festival itself — the offering need not be slaughtered on the Festival day.
Rav Yosef (from Baraita)
One fulfills shalmei simcha with a 14th-offering, but does not fulfill the mitzva of rejoicing — for it is an obligation and everyone must bring it. Direct contradiction!
Rav Ashi
The baraita must refer to an offering slaughtered on the 15th itself — if it were the 14th, the tanna's position would be untenable. Ulla stands.
Ravin (tradition from Eretz Yisrael)
Rabbi Elazar: erev-Festival peace-offerings DO fulfill simcha — yet the baraita shows the first night of Pesach has no simcha obligation because nothing was set aside in advance.
📜 Key Verse (ושמחת) "And you shall be but joyous" — the baraita derives: this includes the last night of the Festival in simcha; the first night is excluded.

📅 Deriving the Eating Period for the Festival Peace-Offering

Deuteronomy 16:4: "Nor shall any of the meat sacrificed on the first day remain overnight until the morning" — what does this mean?
↓
First Reading
Eaten only until the first morning → just one day and one night (15th Nisan to morning of 16th)
Accepted Ruling
Eaten for two days and one night — 15th and 16th Nisan; may not remain until morning of 17th
↓
Festival peace-offering of the 14th: eaten until morning of the 16th (two days, one night from slaughter)
↓
Rav Kahana: Sacrificial parts of the Festival offering of the 15th are disqualified at the first morning — word "ראשון" (first) teaches this is the first morning after slaughter, not the second

🔥 Three Responses to Rava's Sukkot Challenge

Rava's Challenge

  • Hallel and simcha observed on Sukkot for 8 days
  • If fresh slaughter is required for simcha each day…
  • …how does simcha work on Shabbat during Sukkot?
  • Slaughter of non-obligatory offerings is forbidden on Shabbat!

Rava's First Response

  • Festival goats (male goats of the musaf) are eaten raw, not roasted
  • Roasting is a non-essential act — forbidden on Shabbat
  • Therefore those goats cannot be used for simcha on Shabbat
  • Pre-slaughtered offerings fill the gap — consistent with Ulla

Broader Principle

  • Simcha does not require same-day slaughter in all cases
  • When Shabbat falls during a Festival, prior offerings serve
  • "ושמחת" includes last night — shows simcha can span time
  • First night excluded: no offering yet designated for the Festival

⚖️ The Kal V'Chomer Debate: Rabbi Eliezer vs. Rabbi Yehoshua

Premise: The Paschal lamb is permitted to be slaughtered on Shabbat for its own purpose — it overrides Shabbat by biblical law.
↓
Ruling: If one slaughters the Paschal lamb on Shabbat for a different purpose (changed intent) — he is liable for a sin-offering. The offering is disqualified.
↓
Rabbi Eliezer's Kal V'Chomer: If even the Paschal lamb — normally permitted on Shabbat — makes one liable when purpose is changed, then other offerings (forbidden on Shabbat) should certainly incur liability when slaughtered for Paschal purposes!
↓
Rabbi Yehoshua's Rebuttal: The Paschal case is unique — it was permitted for its own purpose; changing that purpose strips the permission entirely. Other offerings are forbidden regardless, so intending a permitted mitzva (Paschal) provides exemption.
↓
Rabbi Eliezer's Counter: Communal offerings (tamid, musaf of Shabbat/Yom Tov) are also permitted on Shabbat — yet changing their purpose incurs liability. This refutes Rabbi Yehoshua's principle!
↓
Rabbi Meir's Corollary: Per Rabbi Yehoshua's logic, one who slaughters other offerings for communal purposes beyond the daily limit should also be exempt — a further implication of his principle worth examining.
