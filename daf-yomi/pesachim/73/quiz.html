QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why is one who slaughters a blemished Paschal lamb on Shabbat liable for a sin-offering, according to the Gemara?
A. He was unaware of the blemish, making his act fully intentional regarding Shabbat
B. Any slaughter of a consecrated animal on Shabbat incurs liability automatically
C. The slaughter is an improvement: sacrificial parts that ascend the altar need not come down
D. A blemished animal's slaughter completes its dedication to the Temple
Correct: C — The Gemara explains that even disqualified sacrificial parts, once placed on the altar, need not be removed (מה שעלה לא ירד). This makes the Shabbat slaughter of a blemished animal a meaningful 'improvement,' incurring liability.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why is one who slaughters a tereifa Paschal lamb on Shabbat EXEMPT from a sin-offering?
A. A tereifa is already legally dead, so slaughter is not a prohibited Shabbat act
B. The slaughter provided no improvement — a tereifa cannot be offered or benefit from the altar rule
C. Errors regarding terminal illness are treated leniently in halacha
D. The Paschal offering has special exemptions not shared by other korbanot
Correct: B — The Gemara's logic: one is liable for Shabbat violation only when the act constitutes an improvement. A tereifa (an animal with a fatal condition) provides no halakhic benefit from slaughter — hence no improvement, no liability.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What does Rav Huna say in the name of Rav regarding a guilt-offering whose owner already achieved atonement through a different offering?
A. It must be left to graze until it develops a blemish, then redeemed for monetary value
B. It is immediately disqualified and must be buried
C. Once consigned to grazing and then slaughtered without specification, it is valid as a burnt-offering
D. It reverts to being ownerless property (hefker) and may be used by anyone
Correct: C — Rav rules that the guilt-offering, once consigned to grazing (an act of uprooting its status) and slaughtered without specification (שלא לשמה), is valid as a burnt-offering — its original guilt-offering purpose has been explicitly removed.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Rav Pappa suggested the baraita (requiring immediate burning) follows Rabbi Eliezer. Why does the Gemara reject this?
A. Rabbi Eliezer never ruled on Paschal offering cases
B. Rabbi Eliezer would also hold the slaughterer liable for a sin-offering, contradicting the mishna's exemption
C. Rabbi Eliezer's opinion was already rejected by the Sanhedrin
D. Rabbi Eliezer requires a later baraita, not the earlier mishna
Correct: B — Rabbi Eliezer holds that erroneous slaughter cannot uproot a designated offering's status — meaning the animal would still be a Paschal lamb, and slaughtering it on Shabbat would incur liability. But the mishna exempts him, creating a contradiction.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Whose opinion does the Gemara ultimately conclude the baraita follows, requiring immediate burning of the ownerless Paschal offering on weekdays?
A. Rabbi Eliezer, who requires full status uprooting
B. Rav Huna bar Yehoshua, based on the midday-designation principle
C. Rabbi Yishmael son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka
D. Yosef ben Ḥonai, who rules that others' Paschal lambs do not override Shabbat
Correct: D — After rejecting Rav Pappa's suggestion (Rabbi Eliezer) and examining Rav Ashi's answer, the Gemara concludes 'it is clear that it is as he answered at the beginning' — the baraita follows Yosef ben Ḥonai, whose ruling about Paschal offerings is consistent with the mishna's exemption and the baraita's burning requirement.
