🔄 The Uprooting Debate: Must an Offering's Status Be Explicitly Changed?

Rav Huna (citing Rav)
A guilt-offering (אשם) consigned to grazing after the owner achieved atonement — if then slaughtered without specification — is valid as a burnt-offering. Explicit uprooting of its original status IS required.
Rav Ḥisda (objection)
If uprooting is required, then when owners withdrew from the Paschal lamb and it was slaughtered on Shabbat, it should still have Paschal status — yet the mishna exempts him! How?
Rav Huna b. Yehoshua
The mishna deals with a case designated before midday — by midday it was firmly established as a Paschal offering, and then the owners withdrew, leaving it purposeless.
Gemara (rejection)
Rejected — Rav himself holds living creatures cannot be permanently rejected (מחוסר זמן). The animal was always fit; rejection logic cannot apply here.
Rav Yosef / Rav Ashi
Two alternative answers: Rav Pappa says the baraita follows Rabbi Eliezer; ultimately Rav Ashi says Rav follows Rabbi Yishmael b. Yoḥanan ben Beroka.
Final Conclusion The mishna and baraita follow Yosef ben Ḥonai: on weekdays the ownerless offering is burned immediately; on Shabbat it waits until nightfall per Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi.

✨ The Altar Improvement Principle

כל הפסולין שעלו — לא ירדו
Talmudic Rule: מה שעלה — לא ירד Even parts of a disqualified offering that are placed upon the altar do not come back down — "What has gone up shall not come down." This rule transforms the act of slaughtering a blemished Paschal lamb on Shabbat into a meaningful improvement (תיקון), making the Shabbat desecration purposeful and therefore incurring liability for a sin-offering. Without this principle, there would be no reason to distinguish the blemished case from the tereifa case.

🔄 The Guilt-Offering After Atonement: Four Opinions

Rav (original ruling)
A guilt-offering consigned to grazing (רועה), once slaughtered without specification (שלא לשמה), is valid as a burnt-offering. Uprooting its guilt-offering status first is required — but once uprooted, it may become an עולה.
Rav Pappa
The baraita requiring immediate burning of a disqualified Paschal offering follows Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that erroneous designation cannot automatically uproot an offering's status.
Gemara (rejects Rav Pappa)
But Rabbi Eliezer would also make the slaughterer liable for a sin-offering — contradicting the mishna's exemption! This cannot be Rabbi Eliezer's view.
Rav Ashi
Rav's ruling about the guilt-offering follows Rabbi Yishmael son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, who holds that offerings in this state require decay of form (נפילת צורה) before changing status.
Conclusion Ultimately confirmed: the baraita follows Yosef ben Ḥonai, whose ruling about others' Paschal offerings on Shabbat resolves all difficulties.

📜 Tannaitic Opinions on Offering Status & Uprooting

Yosef ben Ḥonai

  • Other peoples' Paschal lambs slaughtered on their behalf on Shabbat do NOT override Shabbat
  • A disqualified ownerless offering is burned immediately on weekdays
  • Final answer: mishna and baraita follow this opinion
  • Shabbat case: waits until nightfall (per Rav Yehoshua ben Levi)

Rabbi Eliezer

  • Erroneous act CANNOT automatically uproot an offering's designated status
  • Baraita requiring immediate burning fits his view (Rav Pappa's suggestion)
  • Would hold slaughterer liable for sin-offering — creates contradiction
  • Ultimately rejected as source for this baraita

R. Yishmael b. R. Yoḥanan ben Beroka

  • Rav's ruling about guilt-offering follows this opinion (Rav Ashi)
  • Requires decay of form (נפילת צורה) before status changes
  • Aligns with school of Rabba bar Avuh on related matters
  • Disqualification inherent; uprooting still required

Rav (Amora)

  • Explicit uprooting IS required for guilt-offering status change
  • Living creatures cannot be permanently rejected (אין מוקצה לחי)
  • Guilt-offering consigned to grazing + slaughtered = valid burnt-offering
  • His ruling: corrected by Gemara to mean only after consignment
