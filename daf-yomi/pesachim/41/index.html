🌿 Ḥaroset vs. Mustard: Rav Kahana's Distinction

Rav Kahana
The dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis applies only to mustard with flour. But flour added to ḥaroset — everyone agrees it is permitted on Pesach.
Unnamed Sage
I did not hear this distinction. I make no difference between ḥaroset and mustard — in my view the Rabbis permit eating ḥaroset with flour on Pesach regardless.
Rav Ashi
It is reasonable to rule in accordance with Rav Kahana! Shmuel ruled the halakha does not follow Rabbi Yosei — implying vinegar does not cause grain to leaven, which supports this distinction.
Gemara (Rejection)
No proof! Perhaps Shmuel meant only that vinegar neither shrinks nor leavens grain according to the Rabbis — this says nothing about whether ḥaroset differs from mustard.
Practical Difference Whether flour mixed into ḥaroset on Pesach is forbidden under Rabbi Meir's strict view, or if Rabbi Meir's dispute with the Rabbis is limited to mustard alone

🍖 Two Derivations for the Boiling Prohibition

Verse: "You shall not eat it partially roasted, nor boiled in any way [bashel mevushal] in water" (Exodus 12:9)
↓
Rabbis — Kal V'Ḥomer
Water doesn't absorb the food's taste, yet boiling in it is forbidden.
All other liquids (which do absorb taste) are certainly forbidden.
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
"In water" → only water.
Bashel mevushal (double expression) → all other liquids and dry pot-roasting without any liquid.
↓
Practical Difference: Meat roasted in a pot — no liquid, only its own juices
Rabbis
Permitted — kal v'ḥomer addresses only liquid; dry pot not covered
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
Forbidden — bashel mevushal explicitly extends to this case

♨️ Hot Springs of Tiberias: Shabbat vs. Korban Pesach

Shabbat Ruling

  • Rav Ḥisda: Cooking in hot springs on Shabbat → Exempt
  • Shabbat cooking requires an act of fire or fire-derivative
  • Hot springs are not derived from fire
  • No Torah-level Shabbat violation

Korban Pesach Ruling

  • Korban Pesach cooked in hot springs → Liable
  • Rava: 'Liable' means violated the positive mitzva: 'Roasted with fire'
  • The lamb was not prepared as commanded — no fire used
  • Rav Ḥiyya bar Rav Natan confirms this is Rav Ḥisda's own interpretation

🌙 Day vs. Night — When Does the Prohibition Activate?

During the day (14 Nisan, before nightfall): One who eats an olive-bulk of partially roasted Korban Pesach is exempt from lashes
↓
After dark (beginning of 15 Nisan): One who eats partially roasted meat receives lashes — the prohibition is now fully active
↓
A fortiori argument considered: If liability exists at night when one is obligated to eat, shouldn't there also be liability by day?
↓
Gemara rejects this: The prohibition and the mitzva are linked. At the time one is obligated to eat roasted Pesach (night), the prohibition against improper eating is triggered. By day — no mitzva, no prohibition.
↓
Verse confirms: 'And they shall eat the meat on that night, roasted with fire' (Exodus 12:8) — eating the Korban Pesach during the day violates a positive mitzva inference, not a standard prohibition
