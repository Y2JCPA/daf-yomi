✍️ Two Letters, Two Lapses of Awareness

Rabban Gamliel
Liable. There is no concept of yedia (awareness) that applies to only half a measure. The intervening awareness cannot exempt him — the two letters combine into one completed act.
Rabbis
Exempt. The awareness between the two letters fully breaks the sequence. Each letter alone is only half a measure, and awareness separates them into two incomplete, non-liable acts.
The Core Principle Does intervening awareness of Shabbat between two half-acts exempt a person? Rabban Gamliel: No. The Rabbis: Yes. This mirrors the broader question of whether yesh yedia l'chatzi shi'ur.

אָנֹכִי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ — שמות כ:ב
🔤 Notarikon — Torah's Hidden Abbreviations אָנֹכִי (Anokhi) — the opening word of the Ten Commandments — encodes a message:

Rabbi Yoḥanan: אנא נפשי כתיבת יהבית — "I Myself wrote and gave [the Torah]"
The Rabbis: אמירה נעימה כתיבה יהיבה — "A pleasant statement, written and given"

Other examples: יִרַל (Num. 22:32) = יָרֵא לוֹ — "was dangerous to him"; נִצְטַדַּק (Gen. 44:16) = נְכוֹנִים, צַדִּיקִים, טְהוֹרִים, דַּכָּאִים — "honest, righteous, pure, contrite"

🧵 Weaving at the Start — How Many Threads?

Rabbi Eliezer
Beginning a new weave: 3 threads (per the mishna).
Adding to an existing fabric: 1 thread.
Along the edge of the warp: liable for even 1 thread.
R. Yitzḥak reported from Eretz Yisrael that R. Eliezer's real ruling for beginning is 2 threads — reconciling the texts.
The Rabbis
Both beginning and adding: 2 threads.
Whether starting a new fabric or weaving onto an existing one, the minimum measure is uniform — 2 threads.
A single thread is never sufficient for liability under any weaving scenario.
Reconciliation Rabbi Yitzḥak's transmission from Eretz Yisrael resolves the apparent contradiction: R. Eliezer's mishna figure of 3 threads applies to a specific context; his core position on beginning a weave may also be 2 threads.

😤 Tearing on Shabbat — When Liable, When Exempt

Destructive Tearing

  • Tears garment in anger
  • Tears garment in anguish over the dead
  • No constructive purpose intended
  • Result: Exempt — destructive acts without repair intent do not satisfy the standard of melacha
  • Parallel: all destructive labors on Shabbat are exempt

Constructive Tearing

  • Tears in order to sew two stitches
  • Example: a pocketlike protrusion impedes sewing — one tears the fabric and tucks the protrusion under the seam
  • Intent is to repair, not to destroy
  • Result: Liable — constructive intent transforms tearing into a prohibited act
  • Measure: sufficient to enable two stitches

😢 Rending Garments in Mourning — Obligated or Exempt?

One rends a garment on Shabbat — who is it for?
↓
Close Relative (Obligated Mourner)
Mourning obligation exists — rending is a required act, not purely destructive
Non-Obligatory Dead
No formal obligation to rend — purely an expression of grief or honor
Upright Person / Torah Scholar
All present may be obligated to rend — as one who witnesses the soul departing
↓
Mishna (Exempt)
Refers to cases where no obligation exists — the rending is purely destructive grief
Baraita (Liable)
Refers to obligatory mourning rending — a constructive act fulfilling a religious duty
Resolution
The apparent contradiction dissolves: obligation determines liability, not the emotion itself
