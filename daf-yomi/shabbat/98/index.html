📏 The Tamim Dispute: How Thick Were the Tabernacle Beams?

Rabbi Yehuda
The beams were one cubit thick at both bottom and top — they did not taper. Tamim means complete and whole in full dimensions throughout their entire height.
Rabbi Neḥemya
The beams narrowed from one cubit at the bottom to a fingerbreadth at the top. Tamim means bring whole beams — not planks spliced together — but tapering is still required.
Rabbi Yehuda
If the tops remain a full cubit wide, the curtains account for the beam thickness: 4 cubits hang down on each side of the Tabernacle, reaching close to the ground.
Rabbi Neḥemya
If beams taper to nearly nothing at the top, the curtains lose almost no width to beam thickness: ~5 cubits hang on each side, covering more of the Tabernacle walls.
📖 The Disputed Verse "And they shall match at the bottom, and together they will be ended [tamim] at the top toward one ring" (Exodus 26:24) — Does tamim describe shape or material integrity?

Slide 1: The Covered Public Domain Exemption

רשות הרבים המקורה — פטור, דלא דמי לדגלי מדבר
⚖️ Rav's Ruling One who carries four cubits in a covered public domain is exempt from Shabbat liability — even if the space is otherwise large enough to qualify as a public domain. The halakhic standard for reshut ha-rabbim is derived from the open-sky flags of the Israelite camp in the desert. A covered space fundamentally fails this comparison and cannot reach the threshold of liability.

Slide 2: Do Levite Wagons Create a Public Domain Below Them?

Wagon dimensions: 5 cubits long × 2.5 cubits wide
Used to transport Tabernacle beams through the desert
↓
Each beam: 1 cubit wide × 1 cubit deep
Three stacks placed across = 4.5 cubits + 0.5 cubit empty space
Four stacks tall → 1 cubit open space distributed among stacks
↓
🪵 Wagon Loaded with Beams
Gaps between beams < 3 handbreadths → beams merge into wagon structure → underside is NOT a public domain
🔲 Empty Wagon Frame (Rav Kahana)
Narrow stakes on empty wagon leave open space > 3 handbreadths → space is unenclosed → underside IS a public domain
↓
Shmuel adds: Stakes of the Tabernacle (before beams were placed) were especially narrow — gaps exceeded 3 handbreadths and thus constituted public domain beneath them

Slide 3: The Two Sets of Tabernacle Curtains

🧵 Inner Linen Curtains

  • 10 curtains total (Exodus 26:1–2)
  • Each curtain: 28 cubits long × 4 cubits wide
  • Total combined width: 40 cubits (10 × 4)
  • 30 cubits covers roof of Tabernacle
  • 10 cubits remain for the walls
  • R. Yehuda: beam tops = 1 cubit → 4 cubits hang each side
  • R. Neḥemya: beam tops ≈ 0 → ~5 cubits hang each side

🐐 Outer Goat-Hair Curtains

  • 11 curtains total (Exodus 26:7–8)
  • Each curtain: 30 cubits long × 4 cubits wide
  • Total combined width: 44 cubits (11 × 4)
  • 30 cubits covers roof of Tabernacle
  • 14 cubits remain for the walls
  • 1 cubit folded at front entrance
  • 1 cubit hangs over the rear
  • ~6 cubits hang on each side

Slide 5: How Were the Tabernacle Beams Constructed and Installed?

Step 1 — Harvest whole acacia trees: Per Rabbi Neḥemya, tamim requires bringing entire whole beams — not planks cut and rejoined. Structural integrity from a single piece of wood.
↓
Step 2 — Shape the beam: Carve to 10 cubits tall × 1.5 cubits wide × 1 cubit deep. Bottom left at full cubit width; top tapered (Neḥemya) or kept full cubit (Yehuda).
↓
Step 3 — Groove the base: The bottom of each beam was carved with a groove (mortise joint) designed to slot precisely into the silver socket below it.
↓
Step 4 — Set the silver sockets: Hollow silver sockets were placed on the ground along the Tabernacle perimeter. Each socket received the grooved base of one beam.
↓
Step 5 — Drape the curtains: The linen curtains (28 × 4 cubits, 10 total) were draped over the standing beams. Whether 4 or 5 cubits hung on each side depended on the beam's top width — the heart of the R. Yehuda / R. Neḥemya dispute.
↓
Step 6 — Add the middle bar: A single cedar bar miraculously bent into a U-shape, threading through all the beams from one end to the other to lock the structure together.
