QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why is one who carries four cubits in a covered public domain exempt from Shabbat liability according to Rav?
A. Covered spaces are always classified as private domains regardless of size
B. The space is not similar to the flags of the Israelite desert camp, which was open to the sky
C. The four-cubit rule only applies to throwing, not carrying
D. Roofed structures automatically reduce the width below the required minimum
Correct: B — Rav derives the standard for public domain from the Israelite camp in the desert. Since that camp was open to the sky, a covered space — no matter how large — does not meet the halakhic benchmark and cannot generate liability.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rav Kahana, under what condition was the underside of a Levite wagon considered a public domain?
A. When fully loaded with Tabernacle beams aligned in four stacks
B. At all times, since wagons traveled through the public Israelite camp
C. Only when the wagon was empty and only its narrow frame and stakes were visible
D. When the wagon carried the Ark and was surrounded by Kohanim
Correct: C — Rav Kahana clarified that public domain status applied only to the empty wagon frame. The narrow stakes left open spaces greater than three handbreadths, qualifying the underside as a public domain. Loaded beams closed off this space.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the core disagreement between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Neḥemya about the Tabernacle beams?
A. Rabbi Yehuda: cedar wood; Rabbi Neḥemya: acacia wood
B. Rabbi Yehuda: beams were 1 cubit thick top to bottom; Rabbi Neḥemya: beams tapered to a fingerbreadth at the top
C. Rabbi Yehuda: ten beams per side; Rabbi Neḥemya: eight beams per side
D. Rabbi Yehuda: beams were hollow; Rabbi Neḥemya: beams were solid
Correct: B — Both agree the beams were 1 cubit thick at the bottom. The dispute concerns the top: Rabbi Yehuda holds they remained 1 cubit wide throughout; Rabbi Neḥemya holds they narrowed to a fingerbreadth. This affects how the curtains draped over the structure.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
The inner linen curtains of the Tabernacle each measured 28 × 4 cubits. Given 10 curtains, how many cubits remained after covering the 30-cubit roof?
A. 4 cubits
B. 8 cubits
C. 10 cubits
D. 14 cubits
Correct: C — Ten curtains of 4 cubits width each = 40 total cubits. Subtracting 30 cubits for the roof leaves 10 cubits to be distributed across the two sides of the Tabernacle walls — the exact amount disputed by Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Neḥemya.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How were the Tabernacle beams physically secured in their upright position at the base?
A. They were lashed together with goat-hair cords tied to the ground stakes
B. The beams were embedded directly into packed desert sand at each campsite
C. Grooved beam bases (mortise joints) were inserted into hollow silver sockets set on the ground
D. Two beams were always joined together with a copper clamp at the base
Correct: C — A baraita teaches that the bottoms of the beams were carved with grooves (mortise joints) and the silver sockets were hollow. The grooved base of each beam was inserted into its corresponding socket, stabilizing the entire Tabernacle structure.
