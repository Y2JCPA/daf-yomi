⚖️ Ambiguous Births, Definite Strictures

טומטום ואנדרוגינוס — תשב לזכר ולנקבה
Core Theme Niddah 28 confronts birth events where identity is unclear: a shaped limb discharged, a fetus that extended and retracted its hand, a child of indeterminate sex. The Rabbis apply maximum stringency for impurity while carefully distinguishing between liability — which requires certainty — and precautionary stringency — which covers every possibility. The tumtum and hermaphrodite case drives a deep debate about how the verse "both male and female you shall send out" carves out Temple liability.

🤲 Shaped Limb Discharge: Is There a Period of Purity?

Baraita
A woman who discharges a shaped hand or foot (fingers discernible) is impure with the impurity of a woman after childbirth — the limb certainly came from a formed fetus.
Rav Ḥisda & Rabba bar Rav Huna
She observes the period of impurity of a woman after childbirth — but we do not give her the days of purity that follow. We cannot confirm the full fetus was discharged.
Rav Yosef
Objection from the mishna: It says she observes strictures of both male and female birth — if she had no days of purity at all, why would the mishna need to specify both sexes?
Abaye
Had the mishna said only that she observes strictures of a menstruating woman, one might think she entirely loses childbirth status. Mentioning both sexes teaches: she retains the impurity designation but lacks the purity days.
Ruling A shaped limb confers childbirth impurity but the subsequent days of purity are not granted — the partial discharge is too uncertain to bestow the full benefit.

🚪 Fetus Extends Then Retracts Its Hand

Fetus extends its hand outside the womb, then retracts it — birth never completed
↓
Rav Huna: Mother is impure with childbirth impurity — derived from Tamar: "And it came to pass as he drew back his hand" (Gen. 38:29), implying emergence alone suffices
↓
Rav Yehuda objects
Baraita states: if a fetus extended its hand, the mother need not be concerned about childbirth impurity at all
Rav Naḥman resolves
The baraita means no concern about any specific childbirth matter — she still observes general stringencies of childbirth impurity
↓
Abaye finalizes: She observes general childbirth stringencies but is not held liable for specific childbirth prohibitions

⚧️ Birth Impurity: Male vs. Female vs. Tumtum

Male Birth (זכר)

  • 7 days of impurity
  • 33 days of purity
  • Total cycle: 40 days
  • Shorter impurity period

Female Birth (נקבה)

  • 14 days of impurity
  • 66 days of purity
  • Total cycle: 80 days
  • Double the male period

Tumtum / Androgynos (⚧️)

  • Observe BOTH male and female strictures
  • 14 days impurity (female standard)
  • 66 days purity (female standard)
  • Sex determined at conception — Rabbi Yitzḥak
  • Twin cases: depends on co-twin's sex

🏛️ Tumtum Enters Temple While Impure — Are They Liable?

Rav
A tumtum or hermaphrodite who becomes impure is not liable for entering the Temple. From "Both male and female you shall send out" (Num. 5:3) — only a definite male or female bears this liability.
Challenger
But if they emit both white ziva AND red blood simultaneously, they are definitely impure regardless of sex! Why still no Temple liability?
Rav (continued)
Liability requires knowing the exact type of impurity contracted. Since sex is uncertain, we cannot definitively assign them to zav or zavah liability. The verse carves them out.
Further objection
Then we should also not burn teruma they touch! The verse for zav/zavah impurity seems to apply to them and make it real impurity.
Gemara's answer
That verse is needed for Rabbi Yitzḥak's halakha (including male lepers). Rav's Temple exemption comes from Num. 5:3 alone — teruma is still burned stringently, but no formal Temple liability attaches.
Key Distinction Temple entry: liability requires certain sex → tumtum exempt | Teruma: precautionary stringency applies → burned on contact
