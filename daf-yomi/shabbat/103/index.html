🕳️ Drilling a Hole: Which Prohibition Applies?

Rav
One who drills a hole on Shabbat is liable due to Building (בונה). Any functional opening in a structure is a complete act of building, regardless of size.
Objection
But if the prohibition is Building, why is any size hole liable? Normally Building requires a significant, substantial act!
Resolution
Even a tiny hole functions as a complete structural feature. The prohibition attaches to the function created, not the dimension of the act.
Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel
One who strikes an anvil with a sledgehammer is also liable — the blow finishes the vessel (מכה בפטיש), completing it as a usable tool.
Core Principle Liability attaches to the completion of function — any act that finalizes a structure or vessel triggers the prohibition.

📜 Three Tannaitic Opinions on Writing Liability

Rabbi Yehuda

  • Liable for any two letters
  • Even identical letters (e.g., alef-alef)
  • Even a small name within a larger name counts
  • Shem (שם) written from Shimon (שמעון) = liable
  • Derives: 'from one' (מאחת) = partial act is significant

Rabbi Yosei

  • Liable due to marking, not writing per se
  • Based on Tabernacle beams: symbols carved to identify matching beams
  • Even a single meaningful mark can create liability
  • 'And did one of these' (ועשה אחת מאלה) → one act + these acts
  • Restriction + amplification: liable even for partial significant act

Rabbi Shimon

  • Liable only for writing a complete, enduring name
  • 'One' (אחת) = one complete prohibited act
  • Partial names insufficient unless the part stands independently
  • Shem from Shimon: liable only because Shem is itself a complete name
  • Emphasizes the endurance of the written form

📖 Deriving Partial Labor Liability: The Exegetical Chain

Verse (Lev. 4:22): 'When a leader sinned and performed one of any of the commandments which the Lord his God commanded not to do, and is guilty'
↓
Straightforward reading: 'One' (אחת) = one complete prohibited act required — writing the full name Shimon creates liability
↓
Expansive reading: 'From one' (מאחת) = even a part suffices — writing only Shem (שם) from Shimon (שמעון) also creates liability
↓
Rabbi Yosei ben Ḥanina's analysis: 'From' (מ) = restriction; 'of these' (מאלה) = amplification. Together they teach: liable even for partial acts that have independent meaning
↓
Both readings reconciled: 'From one' → partial name (Shem within Shimon); 'One' → complete name (Shimon alone). Both cases are liable.
↓
Practical ruling: Writing שם (Shem) — a name in its own right — even as part of שמעון (Shimon) creates full Shabbat liability for writing

🔤 Ketav Shalem — Perfect Writing for Mezuzah

וּכְתַבְתָּם עַל מְזוּזוֹת בֵּיתְךָ (דברים ו:ט) — וּכְתַבְתָּם = כְּתָב שָׁלֵם
כְּתָב שָׁלֵם — Perfect Script The verse 'And you shall write them on your doorposts' (Deut. 6:9) is read as ukhtavtam = ketav shalem (perfect, complete writing). Rules of letter form: • Do not write bent letters (כ, נ mid-word form) as straight final-form letters, or vice versa • A final mem (מ sofit, closed) written as an open mem remains valid — per Rav Ḥisda • From the fact that open→closed is valid, closed→open is also valid (kal va-chomer) • Rav Ḥisda is supported by Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira's baraita about the Sukkot libations Key debate: Does the mezuzah become invalid if letter forms are imperfect? The Gemara's final position leans toward validity when the letter remains recognizable.
