QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav, why is one who drills any size hole on Shabbat liable?
A. Due to the prohibition of Finishing (makeh b'patish)
B. Due to the prohibition of Building (boneh)
C. Due to the prohibition of Writing (kotev)
D. Due to the prohibition of Demolishing (soter)
Correct: B — Rav holds that drilling a hole falls under Building (boneh) — any functional opening in a structure is a complete act of building, regardless of how small the hole is.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why is one liable for writing with the LEFT hand on Shabbat, according to the Gemara's resolution?
A. Because Beit Shammai ruled that left-hand writing is typical for left-handed people
B. Because the mishna follows Rabbi Yosei, who bases liability on marking (siman), not typical writing
C. Because the Tabernacle workers used both hands when carving symbols on beams
D. Because the Rabbis issued a rabbinic decree (gezerah) to equalize both hands
Correct: B — Rav Ya'akov bar Bat-Ya'akov explains: the mishna follows Rabbi Yosei, who holds liability derives from marking (as workers marked Tabernacle beams to identify pairs). Since marking has nothing to do with which hand is used, left-hand writing also creates liability.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the minimum amount of plowing for which one is liable on Shabbat, and what is the Talmud's justification?
A. A furrow at least one amah (cubit) long, based on standard field measurements
B. Enough to plant a full row of wheat, corresponding to Tabernacle bread production
C. Any amount at all, even enough for a single pumpkin seed
D. A furrow the length of a standard ox's stride in the Tabernacle courtyard
Correct: C — The Gemara rules any amount of plowing creates liability. The justification: even the tiniest furrow is suited for a single pumpkin seed. In the Tabernacle context, plowing was done to plant even a single stalk of the dye-producing herb used for the Tabernacle.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
When Rabbi Yehuda says one is liable for writing 'Shem' (שם) as part of 'Shimon' (שמעון), what verse and word does the Gemara cite to support this?
A. 'And you shall write them' (ukhtavtam) from Deuteronomy 6:9 — implying any writing counts
B. 'From one' (me'achat) in Leviticus 4:22 — indicating even a partial prohibited act creates liability
C. 'And he performed one of these' (Leviticus 4:2) — interpreted by Rabbi Shimon as requiring completion
D. 'And is guilty' (ve'ashem) in Leviticus 4:22 — guilt attaches to any identifiable act
Correct: B — The Gemara derives Rabbi Yehuda's ruling from 'from one' (מאחת) in Leviticus 4:22. The prefix 'from' (מ) indicates that even a partial act — here, writing the sub-name Shem within Shimon — creates liability, since Shem is an independent, recognizable name.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Regarding ketav shalem (perfect writing) and mezuzah, what does Rav Ḥisda rule about a final mem (closed mem) written as an open mem?
A. It invalidates the mezuzah because final forms must always be closed
B. It is valid only if the scribe corrected it before the ink dried
C. It remains valid, supported by Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira's baraita and a kal va-chomer from open letters rendered closed
D. It depends on a dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel unresolved in the Gemara
Correct: C — Rav Ḥisda rules the mezuzah remains valid. The Gemara supports this with Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira's baraita (related to Sukkot libation verses) and a kal va-chomer: since an open letter rendered closed is valid, a closed letter rendered open is certainly valid.
