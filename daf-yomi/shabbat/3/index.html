⚖️ The Sota Analogy: Ritual Bath Uncertainty

Rabbis
Public domain doubt → Pure. Private domain doubt → Impure. Like sota: uncertainty in public = pure; seclusion in private = definite impurity. Same logic applies to an uncertain ritual bath.
R' Shimon
I derive differently. I do not rely on sota at all — I derive the end of impurity from the beginning of impurity. Doubt in public domain = pure at start; so too doubt about immersion in public = pure at end.
Key Limit Why can't we apply sota's private-domain absolute-impurity rule to ritual baths? Sota requires a ḥazakah — husband's prior warning + specific seclusion. No such basis exists for ritual bath cases.

🔀 Sota Analogy: How Domain Determines Ruling

Uncertainty: Was the ritual bath valid at time of immersion?
↓
Public Domain
Ritually Pure ✓
Like sota in public: doubt → pure. All agree.
Private Domain
Ritually Impure ✗
Rabbis: like sota in private → definite impurity
↓
Gemara's challenge: If we follow the sota analogy, why not apply the sota rule that private-domain seclusion = definite impurity to ALL private-domain cases?
Answer: Sota requires ḥazakah (prior warning + specific seclusion). Without that basis, the analogy cannot be extended beyond public/private domain distinction.

🩸 Shammai's Three Explanations for Non-Retroactive Impurity

Reason 1: Bodily Sensation

  • A woman senses menstrual flow within her body
  • If felt only now → flow did not begin earlier
  • Objection: What of a sleeping woman?
  • Answer: Discomfort would awaken her
  • Objection: Mentally incompetent woman?
  • Answer: Shammai addresses normal women only; 'all women' excludes R' Eliezer's broader view

Reason 2: Blood Would Have Flowed Out

  • Prior blood does not remain blocked — it flows out
  • If blood existed earlier, it would have emerged then
  • Objection: Absorbent cloth during intercourse?
  • Abaye: Cloth can block flow — Shammai's reasoning fails here
  • Rava: Cloth does not hermetically seal womb — blood still flows out
  • This dispute between Abaye and Rava generates differing practical rulings

Reason 3: p'ru u'rvu (Rava's Version)

  • Retroactive impurity deters husbands from intimacy
  • This violates the mitzva to be fruitful and multiply
  • Torah protects procreation by limiting retroactivity
  • Even Hillel concedes this concern has merit
  • Shammai: Even a safeguard rendering pure items impure should not be imposed if it undermines p'ru u'rvu
  • Scrupulous husband may separate from wife entirely if she is always retroactively impure

🤝 Hillel vs. Shammai: The Basket Challenge

Hillel
Don't you concede that if a creeping animal carcass is found in a basket of pure produce, all the produce is retroactively impure? So too, a woman who discovers her flow should be retroactively impure! Why is she different?
Shammai
You are mistaken. My reason is due to p'ru u'rvu. But even by your own logic about the basket: a basket carcass can sit unnoticed — there is no equivalent 'would have emerged' argument. And for women, imposing retroactivity would devastate procreation — that decree must not be made.
Hillel's Concession Hillel acknowledges Shammai's p'ru u'rvu argument has force, but maintains some form of retroactivity is warranted as a safeguard. The Gemara records both sides as having merit.

🔄 Rabbi Shimon's Start-to-End Derivation

Core Principle: Derive rules for the end of impurity from established rules for the beginning of impurity
↓
Beginning of impurity (established): Uncertain contact with impurity in a public domain → ritually pure. The person retains their presumptive status of purity.
↓
Application to end of impurity: Uncertain whether immersion in a ritual bath was valid, occurring in a public domain → ritually pure. The person retains presumptive impure status only in private domain.
↓
Rabbis' objection: The analogy fails — at the beginning, the person was definitely pure before the doubt arose; at the end, the person was definitely impure before the immersion. The presumptive statuses are opposite.
↓
Result: Rabbi Shimon's independent derivation reaches the same practical ruling (public = pure, private = impure) but via a different logical path than the sota analogy used by the Rabbis.
