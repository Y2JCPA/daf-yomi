QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Adda bar Ahava, what should be done if it is uncertain whether a baby born on Shabbat is viable?
A. Do not circumcise until after Shabbat to avoid any possible transgression
B. Circumcise him regardless — if viable the mitzva is fulfilled; if not, no Shabbat prohibition was violated
C. Wait 30 days to confirm viability before performing circumcision
D. Consult two Sages before proceeding with any circumcision under doubt
Correct: B — Rav Adda bar Ahava rules that one circumcises the baby either way: if he is viable, the circumcision fulfills the mitzva; if he is a stillborn (nefal), cutting flesh does not constitute a Shabbat violation — so there is no reason to abstain.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does Mar son of Ravina reconcile Rav Adda's ruling with the baraita that prohibits desecrating Shabbat for an uncertain baby?
A. The baraita overrides Rav Adda completely — no Shabbat activity is permitted
B. The baraita only restricts circumcision; other Shabbat needs may be addressed
C. The baraita restricts secondary Shabbat activities (e.g., calling a doctor), but circumcision itself remains permitted
D. The baraita refers only to eight-month babies, not seven-month babies
Correct: C — Mar son of Ravina explains that the baraita's prohibition targets incidental Shabbat desecrations such as summoning a physician — activities beyond the circumcision itself. The act of circumcision remains permitted under Rav Adda's logic.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
In Abaye's final reformulation, where exactly do Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the Rabbis disagree?
A. A baby that yawned and died — Rabban Shimon says alive, Rabbis say dead from the outset
B. A baby that yawned and died — all agree it was dead; dispute is about a baby that fell off a roof, with Rabbis saying alive and Rabban Shimon saying dead
C. Both agree a baby that fell off a roof was alive; they dispute only the yawning case
D. They agree on all cases — the dispute is only theoretical
Correct: B — Abaye's final formulation: when a baby merely yawned and died, all agree it was dead from the outset (never viable). The dispute concerns a baby that fell off a roof or was eaten by a lion — Rabbis hold it was alive and died from the accident; Rabban Shimon holds it was presumed non-viable from the start.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the practical halakhic consequence when a baby dies within 30 days and the mother's levirate status is uncertain?
A. The mother is automatically exempt from levirate marriage since the baby's status is doubtful
B. The mother must perform full levirate marriage (yibbum) with no alternative
C. The mother performs ḥalitza due to doubt — but this bars her from marrying a kohen
D. There is no halakhic consequence until the Beit Din rules definitively on viability
Correct: C — Because it is uncertain whether the baby was viable (which would exempt the mother from levirate obligations), she performs ḥalitza as a safeguard. The resulting stringency: having undergone ḥalitza, she may no longer marry a kohen — even though the underlying obligation may not have applied at all.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rav Ḥisda limits Rabbi Yehuda's ruling that a hermaphrodite is 'male.' To what single area does Rabbi Yehuda's classification apply?
A. To all areas of halakha — the hermaphrodite is fully treated as a male in every respect
B. Only to circumcision on Shabbat — but not to valuations (arakhin) or other gendered laws
C. Only to firstborn inheritance status, not to circumcision
D. To both circumcision and valuations, but not to levirate marriage
Correct: B — Rav Ḥisda specifies that Rabbi Yehuda's male classification is limited to the permissibility of circumcising a hermaphrodite on Shabbat. For valuations (arakhin), the Torah's gendered language ('for the male… for the female') excludes the hermaphrodite entirely, as derived from the Sifra on Leviticus.
