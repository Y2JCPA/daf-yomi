⚖️ Does Caesarean Birth Trigger Childbirth Impurity?

Rabbis
A caesarean birth does NOT impose days of impurity/purity or the obligation to bring a korban. The verse says "emitted seed" — requiring the child to emerge through the birth canal, as seed is emitted.
Rabbi Shimon
A caesarean birth DOES carry the full halakhic status of regular childbirth. The verse says "if she gives birth to a female" (Lev. 12:5) — any birth, without specifying the manner.
Rabbis
"Emitted seed" is needed to include births of a tumtum (indeterminate sex) or hermaphrodite — Rabbi Shimon cannot use that phrase for caesarean and these edge cases.
Rabbi Shimon
I derive tumtum and hermaphrodite from bar Livai's baraita on "when she is cleansed" (Lev. 12:8). That frees me to use "if she gives birth" exclusively to include caesarean.
Key Verse "If a woman emits seed and gives birth to a male" (Lev. 12:2) — does 'emitted seed' require vaginal delivery?

🔍 Rabbis' Derivation: Why Caesarean ≠ Halakhic Birth

Lev. 12:2: "If a woman emits seed and gives birth" — what does the extra word teach?
↓
Rabbis interpret
"Emitted seed" = child must emerge through the birth canal. Caesarean bypasses this → no impurity days, no korban
Rabbi Shimon interprets
"Emitted seed" = even a fetus that fully decomposed into fluid still triggers childbirth impurity. Caesarean is covered by "if she gives birth"
↓
Both positions require a specific verse to anchor their reading — the debate is about which phrase carries which exclusion

🐑 Consecrated Animals Born by Caesarean — Rabbi Yoḥanan's Ruling

Rabbi Yoḥanan
Even Rabbi Shimon concedes: a consecrated animal born by caesarean section does not acquire sanctity and may not be offered on the altar.
Gemara Challenge
Why not derive by verbal analogy (gezeirah shavah) from human childbirth? The word "birth" appears in both contexts — Rabbi Shimon should extend sanctity to caesarean-born animals!
Resolution
It is more logical to derive consecrated animals from firstborn animals. They share five characteristics: both use "its mother," both are sacrificial animals, both specify male, both involve offspring, both are the same species.
Counter-challenge
But human births share four characteristics too: both are non-firstborn, both apply to either sex, both apply across species, and both use the word "birth." Why does five beat four?
Gemara's Tension The analogy contest reflects a deeper question: do we count shared features, or do qualitative similarities outweigh quantitative ones?

🔥 The Altar Principle: Disqualified Offerings (Amud B)

Base Rule (Rabbi Yehuda): A valid burnt offering that ascended the altar shall not be taken down — from Lev. 6:2: "That is the burnt offering that goes up on the pyre upon the altar."
↓
Three Exclusions (Rabbi Yehuda): Offerings slaughtered at night; offerings whose blood spilled before sprinkling; offerings that became impure before sprinkling — these do come down even if they ascended.
↓
Rabbi Shimon Expands: "This is the law of the burnt offering" — the verse includes all items that ascend the altar. Even disqualified offerings shall not be taken down once they have ascended.
↓
Rabbi Shimon's Exceptions: An animal that copulated with a person; an animal used for bestiality; an animal set aside for idol worship; an animal that was actually worshipped as an idol — these do come down even after ascending.
↓
Open Question: Rabbi Shimon himself asks — "What did you see?" What principle determines which disqualified offerings remain on the altar and which must descend?
