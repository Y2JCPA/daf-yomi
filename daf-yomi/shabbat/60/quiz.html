QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav, when may a blood stain found on a loaned garment be attributed to a gentile woman borrower?
A. Always — any gentile woman suffices as a potential source
B. Never — a stain cannot be attributed to a gentile woman under any circumstances
C. Only if the gentile woman had previously experienced a menstrual flow
D. Only if the garment was loaned for more than twenty-four hours
Correct: C — Rav infers from the mishna that the gentile woman is compared to a niddah: just as a menstruating woman must have seen blood, so too the gentile woman must have a prior history of menstrual blood before a stain can be attributed to her.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, if a woman lends her garment to both a lesser zava and a woman counting seven clean days without immersing, how many women's statuses are 'ruined'?
A. Neither woman's status is affected by the attribution
B. Both women's statuses are ruined
C. Only the woman counting seven clean days is ruined
D. Only the lesser zava's status is ruined
Correct: D — Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel explicitly states: 'therefore the status of only one woman is ruined.' The baraita clarifies this is the lesser zava, whose day-for-day count is invalidated. The woman counting seven clean days must recount, but this is addressed separately.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Rabbi Neḥemya rules that three women who sat on a stone bench are pure despite finding a stain. What is the basis of his ruling?
A. Stone is cold and prevents blood from being retained long enough to matter
B. Any item not susceptible to ritual impurity cannot convey impurity via a blood stain
C. With three women present, there is insufficient certainty to render any one impure
D. The stain must be at least three by three fingerbreadths to have legal significance on hard surfaces
Correct: B — Rabbi Neḥemya's principle is that susceptibility to ritual impurity (קבלת טומאה) is a prerequisite. Stone, the exterior of an inverted earthenware vessel, and small rags are not susceptible — therefore a stain found upon them cannot render women impure.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In the mishna's case of three women sleeping in one bed where blood is discovered, and one woman examines herself and finds she is a niddah, what is the ruling?
A. All three remain impure since blood was found beneath all of them
B. She is impure and the other two are pure
C. She is impure and the other two must undergo a precautionary immersion
D. All three must count seven clean days before immersing
Correct: B — The mishna explicitly rules: if one of them examined herself and found she was impure due to menstruation, she is impure and the other two are pure — the identified source accounts for all the blood discovered.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the core dispute between Rav (via bar Padda) and Rabbi Oshaya regarding a woman who discovers she is a niddah?
A. Whether she must bring her own offering or rely on her husband's
B. Whether bar Padda's three time periods apply only after intercourse or at all times
C. Whether her husband's obligation to bring a sin offering determines the retroactive impurity of items she handled
D. Whether she requires immersion before or after the full twenty-four hour onah period
Correct: C — Rav holds (following bar Padda) that if her husband is liable to a sin offering — meaning she discovered blood within the onah of intercourse — items she handled are retroactively impure. Rabbi Oshaya disagrees entirely: her husband's offering has no bearing on the purity status of objects she touched.
