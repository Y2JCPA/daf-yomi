💤 Did Rav Rule While Dozing? — The Gentile Woman Dispute

Rav Sheshet
Rav stated that halakha while he was dozing — it is an error! The baraita explicitly teaches: if a woman loaned her garment to a gentile woman and found a blood stain, she may not attribute the stain to the gentile at all.
Rava
How can you read Rabbi Meir as being lenient? The first tanna permits attribution to any gentile woman — Rabbi Meir restricts it to only a gentile woman who previously experienced menstrual blood. Rav is correct!
Rav Sheshet
But a further baraita states: 'A woman who loaned her garment to a gentile may not attribute the stain to her' — that is the base rule with no qualification!
Rava
That baraita is the first tanna being stringent. Rabbi Meir permits attribution — but only to a gentile woman who has already seen a flow. The halakha is: just as a menstruating woman must have seen blood, so too this gentile woman must have a prior history of blood.
Ruling Rav's halakha stands: a blood stain may be attributed to a gentile woman borrower only if she has previously experienced a menstrual flow (דמים ראתה).

⏳ Retroactive Impurity of Handled Items: Rav vs. Rabbi Oshaya

Rav (via Bar Padda)

  • Three time periods: examination period (בדיקת עונה), intermediate period, full onah (24 hrs)
  • If husband is liable to a sin offering → items she touched are retroactively impure
  • Physical logic: male organ prevented blood from exiting — she was definitively impure during intercourse
  • Husband's offering status is the legal indicator of retroactive impurity for her handled items

Rabbi Oshaya

  • Husband's offering obligation has no bearing on purity of items she handled
  • Even if he brings a sin offering, items she touched remain pure
  • Parable: child and old man walk a road — child delays them while traveling, but once they stop, the child's pace is irrelevant
  • Conclusion: presence of husband prevented blood during intercourse only — does not extend retroactively to everything she touched

🏺 Rabbi Neḥemya: Susceptibility as Prerequisite for Stain-Impurity

Mishna: Three women share a garment and sit on a stone bench (אבן) or bathhouse bench — Rabbi Neḥemya declares all pure
↓
Rabbi Neḥemya's principle: Any item not susceptible to ritual impurity (אינו מקבל טומאה) cannot convey impurity through a blood stain found upon it
↓
Rav Huna cites Rabbi Ḥanina: Extended to the exterior of an inverted earthenware vessel — pure, since earthenware becomes impure only through its interior airspace, not its outer surface
↓
Abaye adds: Small rags under 3×3 fingerbreadths — pure, as they are not useful to rich or poor and thus not susceptible to impurity in the first place
↓
Rav Ḥiyya bar Rav Mattana rules: Halakha follows Rabbi Neḥemya — but Rav Naḥman challenges from an incident brought before the Sages who did not rule leniently
↓
Resolution: It is a tannaitic dispute — a baraita records both that items not susceptible to impurity are pure from a stain, and a contrary ruling; neither is unanimously followed
