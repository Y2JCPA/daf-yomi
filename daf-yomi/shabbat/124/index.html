⚖️ What Does לְצֹרֶךְ ('For a Purpose') Actually Mean?

Rabba
For a specific purpose (לְצֹרֶךְ): Moving a permitted-use vessel to use the vessel itself (גּוּפוֹ) or to use its place (מְקוֹמוֹ).
Never permitted: Moving a prohibited-use vessel under any circumstances.
Rava
For a specific purpose: Moving a permitted-use vessel for body or place — both permitted.
Not for a specific purpose: Moving a prohibited-use vessel for body or place — also permitted.
Only forbidden: moving for neither body nor place (e.g., to prevent theft).
Practical Difference Moving a hammer to free up space: Rabba forbids (prohibited-use vessel). Rava permits (moving for its place). Moving a hoe only to prevent theft: both forbid — this is neither body nor place.

⚖️ Rabba vs. Rava: The Core Dispute on Muktzeh

Rabba
Permitted-use vessel: May move for body (גּוּפוֹ) or place (מְקוֹמוֹ) ✓
Prohibited-use vessel: May never be moved ✗

Abaye challenges: 'How do we carry empty bowls?' Rav Safra answers: Like a chair — permitted to be moved for a person to sit.
Rava
Permitted-use vessel: Move for body or place ✓
Prohibited-use vessel: Move for body or place ✓
Either type: Move for neither — forbidden ✗

Abaye objects: A mortar without garlic is forbidden per baraita — yet Rava would permit moving it!
Gemara's Conclusion Rava's view is upheld: even Rav rules that moving a hoe only to prevent theft is forbidden — confirming the 'body or place' requirement is the operative principle.

🏛️ Three Positions: When May Vessels Be Moved on Shabbat?

Rabbis (Majority)

  • All vessels may be moved
  • For the vessel's use (גּוּפוֹ) ✓
  • To free up its space (מְקוֹמוֹ) ✓
  • Even prohibited-use vessels — for body or place
  • No requirement to need the vessel specifically

Rabbi Neḥemya

  • Vessels may only be moved for their designated use
  • A mortar without garlic → forbidden
  • Empty bowls → problematic
  • Resolution (Rav Safra): like a chair — may move for a person
  • Stricter than Rabbis on all vessel movement

Rava's Practical Rulings

  • Felt cushions left in sun → permitted to bring in
  • Hoe moved to prevent theft → forbidden
  • Net: 'remove it from before Kahana' = moving for place ✓
  • Brooms of fine wool → permitted (permitted use)
  • Brooms of date-palm fronds → debated

🧱 Practical Rulings: Moving Miscellaneous Objects

Brooms of fine wool: Permitted to move on Shabbat — their designated use (sweeping) is permitted
↓
Brooms of date-palm fronds: Rabbi Elazar permits; Gemara debates whether this is for body or for place
↓
Leftover construction bricks: Rav Naḥman permits when scattered — suited for sitting. If piled up (designated for building = prohibited labor) → forbidden
↓
Small earthenware shard in courtyard: Permitted — vessels there need covering. In a karmelit: forbidden — no typical use for it in the street
↓
Clay seal of broken jug: Permitted to move; shards useful for covering vessels. Rava demonstrated this in Meḥoza's streets when servant wiped his shoes with a shard
↓
Cloak thrown in garbage: Rav Pappa objects to any prohibition — the cloak is still fit for use; its status is not determined by the owner's intent or where it was placed
