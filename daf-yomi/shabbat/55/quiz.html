QUESTION 1 OF 5
What is the practical halakhic difference between Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yoḥanan regarding corpse flesh?
A. Whether moist flesh transmits impurity at all
B. Whether flesh so dry that it crumbles is still impure
C. Whether bones require an olive's bulk to transmit impurity
D. Whether a zav's flesh transmits impurity like a corpse
Correct: B — Both agree that dry flesh is impure. They differ only about flesh so desiccated that it crumbles into powder — Reish Lakish: pure (no longer 'flesh'); Rabbi Yoḥanan: still impure (retains corpse status).
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rav Adda bar Ahava's final ruling, what two criteria must an item meet to transmit corpse impurity like a bone?
A. It must be visible to the naked eye and remain attached
B. It must contain marrow and be at least an olive's bulk
C. It must be created at birth AND its root must not renew itself when removed
D. It must have been part of the person for at least thirty days
Correct: C — Rav Adda's rule: like a bone — (1) created with the person from birth, and (2) if removed, the root does not regenerate. Teeth, hair, and nails all regrow, so detached they do not transmit corpse impurity.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does Rav Yehuda of Diskarta say the verse 'his issue, it is impure' (Lev. 15:2) was necessary?
A. To teach that ziva is impure only after three emissions
B. To counter the inference from the scapegoat — which causes others' impurity yet is itself pure
C. To establish that ziva transmits impurity only when moist
D. To clarify that a zav's garments also become impure
Correct: B — Without this explicit verse, one might argue: just as the scapegoat renders others impure yet is itself pure, so too ziva might cause impurity to the person without itself being impure. The verse teaches that ziva itself is intrinsically impure.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Of the nine fluids of the zav listed in the baraita, which three are always ritually pure?
A. Tears, milk of a zava, and blood from wounds
B. Mucus, phlegm, and nasal fluids
C. Sweat, secretion from an infected wound, and excrement
D. Saliva, urine, and phlegm when dry
Correct: C — The baraita explicitly states that sweat, foul secretion from an infected wound, and excrement of a zav are ritually pure — they are not included in any impurity derivation.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the dispute between Rav and Levi regarding 'nasal fluids' (ניחורין) in the baraita?
A. Rav says they are impure only when the zav is also experiencing a skin disease; Levi disagrees
B. Rav says they are fluids emitted via the mouth containing saliva traces; Levi says they are genuine nasal discharge
C. Rav says nasal fluids are pure entirely; Levi says they transmit severe impurity
D. Both agree they are impure but disagree about whether the zav must be male
Correct: B — Rav holds 'nasal fluids' in the baraita refers to mouth-pathway fluids carrying saliva traces, making them impure as an extension of saliva. Levi holds they are actual nasal discharge — a separate category. This dispute also affects why tears are (or are not) listed as severely impure.
