🐟 Sandal Fetus — Which Twin is the Firstborn?

A sandal fetus and its full-fledged twin are born
The sandal (סַנְדָּל) is a flattened, compressed fetus shaped like a sandal sole
↓
Which fetus exited the womb first?
↓
Rav Pappa
They do not lie side by side. The full-fledged offspring presses into the middle of the sandal → sandal exits first
Rav Huna bar Tachlifa (Rava)
They do lie side by side. Reverse Rav Pappa: full-fledged offspring exits first
↓
Result (Rav Pappa)
Twin is firstborn for inheritance (פִּדְיוֹן — no), since sandal opened the womb first
Result (Rav Huna)
Twin is firstborn for redemption (פִּדְיוֹן — yes), since it was born first

🏠 Does Afterbirth in a House Transmit Corpse-Impurity?

Rabbis (Tanna Kamma)

  • An afterbirth in the house renders everything ritually impure
  • Reason: a fetus is generally found within the afterbirth
  • The afterbirth acts as a tent over a corpse (אֹהֶל הַמֵּת)
  • Even if no fetus is visible, its presence is presumed
  • The house and all objects under the roof are impure

Rabbi Shimon

  • The house does not become a tent over a corpse
  • The offspring dissolved into blood before emerging from the womb
  • Once dissolved, the fetus is negated by the majority — it is blood, not a fetus
  • No intact fetus remains → no corpse-impurity
  • The afterbirth alone does not impart ohel impurity

📅 Attributing Afterbirth to an Offspring — The Three-Day Rule

Rav (via Rav Yehuda): For all three days after a woman gives birth, if she discharges an afterbirth, we attribute it to that earlier offspring — even if we do not see a direct connection.
↓
Challenge (Rav Kahana): But Rav himself also said that one offspring cannot remain in the womb after another has already been born! These two statements contradict each other.
↓
Resolution: The three-day rule applies to a case where she first discharged a non-viable newborn (נֵפֶל). Since a nefal is not considered a full-fledged birth, a viable fetus could still remain in the womb alongside it.
↓
Shmuel's Version (Rav Yosef bar Menashya of D'vil): Rav said — an afterbirth is attributed only to a viable offspring. If she discharged only a nefal, the afterbirth is not attributed to it.
↓
Practical Difference: If the afterbirth is attributed to the birth → no concern about additional viable offspring remaining in the womb. If not attributed → she may need to be examined further.

🐦 Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul's Questions to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi

Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul
Question 1: A woman discharged an item in the form of a crow (דְּמוּת עוֹרֵב) and also an afterbirth. Is the afterbirth attributed to the crow-shaped item — or do we worry a viable offspring remains inside?
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi (Rebbi)
The afterbirth is not attributed to the crow-shaped item. We are concerned that there may be a viable offspring still in the womb, and the crow-shaped form is not considered a complete birth.
Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul
Question 2: What if the afterbirth is physically tied to the crow-shaped discharge? Does that prove they are connected, and the afterbirth belongs to this item alone?
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi (Rebbi)
You have asked about something that does not exist in reality — an afterbirth cannot be tied to a crow-shaped discharge. This scenario is anatomically impossible.
What is a 'crow-shaped' discharge? A דְּמוּת עוֹרֵב is an abnormal fetal form resembling a crow. The question is whether such a form is halakhically recognized as a 'birth,' allowing the afterbirth to be attributed to it — or whether it is so anomalous that we must still worry about a viable fetus inside.
