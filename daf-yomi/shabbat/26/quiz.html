QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Pappa, why is the twin of a sandal fetus considered a firstborn for inheritance but not for redemption?
A. Because the sandal fetus itself takes the firstborn status for redemption
B. Because the full-fledged twin presses into the sandal, causing the sandal to exit the womb first — opening it for the twin
C. Because a sandal fetus always invalidates any firstborn status for its twin
D. Because only the mother's first pregnancy determines firstborn status
Correct: B — Rav Pappa explains that the fetuses do not lie side by side; the full-fledged offspring presses into the middle of the sandal fetus. This means the sandal — not the full-fledged twin — opens the womb and exits first. Therefore, the twin that follows is a firstborn (peter rechem) for inheritance purposes, but since the sandal technically opened the womb first, redemption from a priest does not apply.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why do the Rabbis rule that an afterbirth in a house renders it impure with tent-over-corpse (ohel ha-met) impurity?
A. Because the afterbirth itself is considered a corpse under Torah law
B. Because blood from the afterbirth has the status of a meit
C. Because a fetus is generally presumed to have been present within the afterbirth
D. Because Rabbi Shimon concedes that some afterbirths contain intact fetuses
Correct: C — The Rabbis hold that a fetus is typically found within an afterbirth. Since an offspring (even a dissolved one) may have been present, the afterbirth functions as a tent (ohel) covering the equivalent of a corpse, thereby rendering everything in the house ritually impure.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Which of the following items is explicitly excluded from Rabbi Oshaya's list of five items with a minimum measure of one handbreadth?
A. The spine of a lulav
B. A cross beam (korah) over an alleyway entrance
C. The wall of a sukka
D. A shofar for Rosh HaShana
Correct: B — The Gemara points out that a cross beam (קוֹרָה) over an alleyway must also be one handbreadth wide. However, Rabbi Oshaya does not include it in his list because it is subject to a halakhic dispute — and Rabbi Oshaya only lists items whose minimum measure is universally agreed upon.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does the Gemara resolve the apparent contradiction in Rav's statements — that afterbirth is attributed for three days, yet an offspring cannot remain after another offspring is born?
A. Rav only meant the three-day rule for cases of twins, not single births
B. The three-day rule applies when the first discharge was a non-viable newborn (nefal), not a full-fledged birth
C. Rav's three-day rule was retracted in favor of Shmuel's view
D. The contradiction was left unresolved — it is a teku
Correct: B — Rav Kahana resolves the contradiction by clarifying that the three-day attribution rule applies specifically when the first birth was a non-viable newborn (נֵפֶל). A nefal is not considered a complete birth, so a viable fetus can still remain in the womb. The afterbirth discharged afterward can therefore be attributed to that viable fetus.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
When Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul asked Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi about an afterbirth 'tied' to a crow-shaped discharge, what was the response?
A. The afterbirth is fully attributed to the crow-shaped discharge, exempting her from further concern
B. It depends on whether the crow-shaped item had identifiable limbs
C. You are asking about something that cannot exist in reality
D. The woman must bring both a doubt-offering and a definite offering
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi dismissed the question by saying Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul was asking about something that does not exist — it is anatomically impossible for an afterbirth to be tied to a crow-shaped discharge. This was not a theoretical rejection but a practical one based on physiological reality.
