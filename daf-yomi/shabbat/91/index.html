🌱 Seed Measures: Sowing vs. Eating

Seeds for Eating

  • Measure: dried fig-bulk (כגרוגרת)
  • Standard rule for most food items carried on Shabbat
  • Mishna aligns with this as the default position
  • Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar: same measure applies to all people

Seeds for Sowing

  • Measure: even a single wheat seed (Rabbi Meir)
  • Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel transmits this stringent ruling
  • Sowing intent grants significance to the smallest quantity
  • Stricter than eating because the act can yield unlimited growth

🔄 Rava's Dilemma: Changing Intent Mid-Carry

One carries a dried fig-bulk of seeds — originally for eating
↓
Mid-carry: he reconsiders and decides to use them for sowing
Eating → Sowing
Liable — original eating measure was already met; sowing intent doesn't reduce liability
Sowing → Eating
Liable — original sowing intent was valid at the moment the act began
↓
⚠️ Unresolved (תיקו): half a fig-bulk carried for sowing — seeds then become moist and swell in transit. Does swelling complete the measure retroactively?

🧺 Basket of Produce on the Outer Threshold

Ḥizkiya
The ruling that the entire basket must be carried out for liability applies only to cucumbers and gourds — bulky items where each piece is independently significant, so the basket is the natural unit.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
The ruling applies to any produce that needs the basket to be carried. Liability only arises when the entire basket is transferred at once — the basket defines the unit of measure.
Rabbi Zeira
The mishna's language does not precisely align with either opinion — both Ḥizkiya and Rabbi Yoḥanan must strain to read their view into the text.
Gemara
Each sage resolves the mishna's internal tension through his own reasoning. The very contradiction within the mishna is the basis for their respective positions.
Key Principle Liability for carrying requires both the act and the measure to be complete simultaneously — a basket on a threshold interrupts the act before it is finished.

💰 Purse Theft on Shabbat

הגונב כיס בשבת חייב — שבת צא.
Dual Violation — Merged Punishment Principle One who steals a purse on Shabbat is liable for the theft. The Gemara invokes the principle: one who incurs two punishments receives only the greater one — meaning the Shabbat death penalty could theoretically override theft payment. Yet the Rabbis still hold him liable for theft to ensure restitution.

Critical distinction: If he carried the purse out through its mouth (opening) → liable for theft. If through the underside → different analysis applies, since that is not the normal mode of using the object.
