🔄 Mi'un: The Minor's Refusal and Levirate Marriage

Mishna (Yevamot)
A yavam who performs levirate marriage with one of his brother's wives renders the other wives forbidden to him — they remain 'his brother's wife'
Rabbi Eliezer
The court persuades a minor wife to perform mi'un (refusal), dissolving her marriage — freeing the surviving brother to then perform yibum with her adult sister
Gemara
Proof from Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava: he testified the court persuades 'minor girls' (plural) — implying two scenarios, confirming the ruling applies broadly
Rav
We instruct the minor to refuse, so the surviving brother may then enter into levirate marriage with the adult sister, the widow of his childless brother
Key Term מיאון (Mi'un) — a minor's formal refusal to continue a marriage arranged by her mother or brothers. Dissolves the marriage without a get (bill of divorce). Only valid while she is a minor.

🌱 Three Types of Virgins — Why the Niddah Case Stands Apart

👤 Virgin Human (בתולת אדם)

  • A woman who has never engaged in intercourse
  • Relevant: ketubot payment, niddah blood status at marriage
  • Does NOT revert — menstrual blood eventually comes (Ḥanina's principle)
  • A husband IS particular about her blood (baraita: blood is beneficial for a woman)
  • Excluded from the baraita's list: her 'virginity' has a qualifier — defined by absence of blood, not just absence of intercourse

🌍 Virgin Ground (בתולת קרקע)

  • Ground never previously worked or tilled
  • Relevant: eglah arufah (Deut. 21) — broken-necked heifer ritual requires a nachal eitan in untilled land
  • Identified by: raises hard clumps, no loose dirt, no earthenware sherds
  • Does not revert to worked state on its own — included in the list
  • No modifier on its virginity — simply 'never worked'

🌳 Virgin Sycamore (בתולת שקמה)

  • Sycamore that has never been felled or cut for growth promotion
  • Relevant: commercial transactions — 'virgin sycamore' as a sales stipulation
  • Included in the baraita's list despite one possible objection
  • Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak: included because the term 'sycamore' bears no modifier
  • Rabbi Ḥanina b. Rav Ika: debated — does it revert after cutting?

🤰 When Is Pregnancy Legally Recognized?

Mishna's rule: A pregnant woman's special niddah status begins from the time her pregnancy is known to all who see her
↓
Gemara asks: How much time must pass for the fetus to be publicly known?
↓
Sumakhos answers: Three months — from the verse about Tamar (Genesis 38:24): 'After three months' when her pregnancy was reported to Judah
↓
Rabbi Meir objects: That verse is only a remez (allusion), not explicit proof — some women give birth at 7 months and show pregnancy earlier than 3 months!
↓
Accepted threshold: Three months remains the standard — after this point, fetal presence is evident to outside observers
↓
Edge case: A woman with presumptive pregnant status who miscarried 'air' (non-viable matter) retains her pregnant status — even incomplete pregnancies count halakhically

🕯️ Where Does Havdala Belong in the Amida?

Rabbi Eliezer
Recite havdala within the fourth blessing of the Amida — 'Who graciously grants knowledge' (Atah Chonen da'at) — since da'at is the vessel of discernment between holy and profane
Rabbi Akiva
Recite havdala as a fourth independent blessing — a standalone prayer appended to the Amida, not embedded within another blessing
Gemara
Difficulty: this ruling of Rabbi Eliezer would be a 5th case where halakha follows him — contradicting Rabbi Elazar's limit of four cases!
Rabbi Abba
That case is different — Rabbi Eliezer stated that ruling in the name of Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel. It is not his own independent position!
Gemara
But Rabbi Eliezer was older than Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel — how could he have cited him? Rather: we rule in accordance with Ḥanina on his own merits, not because Eliezer endorsed him
Yom Kippur Context On Yom Kippur the Amida has only 7 blessings (per one opinion) — so where havdala is recited becomes a unique question separate from a regular weekday Amida of 18 blessings
