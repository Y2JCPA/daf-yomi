🏠 Rava's Dilemma: Two Courtyards

Rava
One stands in a courtyard holding fruit and extends his hand into an adjacent courtyard. Both are private domains — in what way are they halakhically different? Why should this be prohibited?
Rav Naḥman
(Jokingly) When you come to eat at my table, I will explain it to you. The two courtyards belong to different people — they are legally distinct spaces despite both being private.
Gemara
The baraita's prohibition on returning the object refers specifically to a different courtyard. Even between two private domains, the transfer is prohibited — legal personhood of the domain matters.
Takeaway Two private domains are not interchangeable. Moving an object between separate private courtyards constitutes a prohibited act of carrying on Shabbat.

🌬️ Airspace Debate: Rabbi Akiva vs. Rabbis

Rabbi Akiva

  • Object in airspace is considered at rest
  • No minimum of 4×4 handbreadths required for a 'place'
  • Even a small area constitutes a halakhic domain
  • Passing through airspace = halakhic placement → liability
  • Mishna's liability rulings reflect this lenient-threshold view
  • Derives throwing rules from the halakha of passing-through

Rabbis (Majority)

  • Object in airspace is NOT considered at rest
  • Minimum of 4×4 handbreadths needed for a defined place
  • Mere transit through airspace does not trigger liability
  • Object must land and rest in the domain to count
  • Throwing and placing are governed by distinct rules
  • Stricter threshold for what counts as a 'domain'

⚖️ Throwing Through a Private Domain — How Many Violations?

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
One who throws from a public domain to a public domain, passing through a private domain in between, is liable for two sin-offerings — one for entering the private domain and one for exiting it.
Rav Yehuda (Shmuel)
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds two distinct prohibitions were violated: (1) carrying from public → private, and (2) carrying from private → public. Each is a separate act of liability.
Rav / Shmuel
Both Rav and Shmuel stated an objection to deriving the mishna's ruling from this case. The proof is ultimately rejected by the Gemara.
Subsidiary Principle "Cast its boughs after its trunk" (אחר עיקרו) — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds a tree's branches follow the trunk's domain status, making the whole tree a private domain.
