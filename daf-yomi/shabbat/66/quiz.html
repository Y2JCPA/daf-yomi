Shabbat 66 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the final resolution in the Gemara, which Tanna permits an amputee to go out with a wooden leg on Shabbat?
A. Rabbi Meir
B. Rabbi Yosei
C. Rabbi Akiva
D. Shmuel
Correct: B — After the disputed reading was clarified by Rav Ḥanan bar Rava's testimony, the Gemara concludes: Rabbi Meir prohibits (the wooden leg is not a shoe), and Rabbi Yosei permits it (treating it as a shoe).
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Rava disagrees with Abaye about the wooden leg with a pad-receptacle. What impurity does Rava say it receives that Abaye denies?
A. Corpse impurity (tumat met)
B. Treading impurity (tumat midras)
C. Impurity from a zav's touch
D. No impurity whatsoever
Correct: B — Abaye holds the wooden leg receives corpse impurity only (as a vessel), not treading impurity. Rava disagrees: since the amputee leans his full body weight on the leg, it also receives treading impurity — like a shoe or chair.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What did Rabbi Abbahu identify as 'loketamin'?
A. Pads cushioning an amputated limb stump
B. Wooden toys shaped like a donkey carried on the shoulders
C. Knee supports worn by bilateral amputees
D. Straw sandals worn by plasterers
Correct: B — Rabbi Abbahu explains that loketamin are wooden donkey-shaped toys carried on one's shoulders, giving the appearance that the donkey is riding the person. They are ritually pure because they are not functional vessels.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Avin bar Huna taught the real meaning of 'knots' boys wear on Shabbat. What are they?
A. Madder plant garlands for childhood illness
B. A copper amulet sealed with sixty seals
C. A strap from the right shoe tied to the boy's left arm
D. White thread twisted upward around the neck
Correct: C — Avin bar Huna citing Rav Ḥama bar Gurya explains the knots as straps from the right shoe tied to the left arm — a remedy for a young boy who has longing for his father and difficulty separating. This explains why the mishna specifies young boys specifically.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Abaye's foster mother taught a rule about incantations that name a person. Whose name is used?
A. The patient's own name
B. The father's name
C. The mother's name
D. The name of the healer performing the incantation
Correct: C — Abaye reports his foster mother's teaching: all incantations are intoned using the name of the mother of the person requiring healing — not the father's name. This was a standard rule for folk-healing incantations.
