QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Huna, why is there no contradiction between this mishna (permitting rescue of a basket of 100 meals) and the earlier mishna limiting rescue to 3 meals?
A. The 3-meal limit was a minority opinion and was later rejected
B. The 3-meal limit applies to carrying item by item in vessels; this mishna permits rescuing full sealed containers
C. The 3-meal limit applies only on Yom Tov, not Shabbat
D. The basket of 100 meals was a special rabbinic exception enacted after a famous fire
Correct: B — Rav Huna resolves the apparent contradiction: the 3-meal limit governs transferring individual food items in vessels, while this mishna deals with rescuing entire sealed containers (like a full basket or barrel) — a different halakhic category entirely.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does Rabbi Shimon ben Nannas permit regarding a moist goat's hide during a fire, and what is his reasoning?
A. Spreading it over burning items, because it singes but does not actually burn — slowing the fire without direct extinguishing
B. Wrapping it around garments to prevent them from catching fire in the first place
C. Using it to directly smother flames, since animal hides are not susceptible to ritual impurity
D. Soaking it in water and placing it in the path of the fire as a permitted firebreak
Correct: A — Rabbi Shimon ben Nannas permits spreading a moist goat's hide over a burning box or chest. His reasoning: the wet hide singes but does not ignite — so no direct melacha (extinguishing or burning) is performed. This is the basis for the broader discussion of grama.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the rule of psik reisha and how do Abaye and Rava apply it to Rabbi Shimon's general leniency?
A. Psik reisha means any forbidden act done accidentally is exempt; Abaye and Rava reject this view
B. Psik reisha means that when a forbidden result is inevitable, the act is prohibited even if unintended — and Abaye and Rava say even Rabbi Shimon concedes this
C. Psik reisha is a leniency allowing otherwise prohibited acts in cases of fire emergency
D. Psik reisha applies only to Rabbinic prohibitions; Abaye and Rava extend it to Torah prohibitions
Correct: B — Rabbi Shimon normally permits unintended consequences of permitted acts. But Abaye and Rava teach: where the forbidden result is inevitable — like cutting off an animal's head and expecting it to survive — even Rabbi Shimon agrees the act is prohibited. The prohibition follows from the certainty of the outcome.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the fundamental dispute between the Rabbis and Rabbi Yosei, as the Gemara concludes?
A. Whether one may call bystanders to rescue items for themselves during a fire
B. Whether the moist goat's hide constitutes a direct or indirect act
C. Whether indirect extinguishing (grama) is permitted or prohibited on Shabbat
D. Whether new earthenware vessels are considered muktzeh on Shabbat
Correct: C — The Gemara concludes that Rabbi Yosei prohibits grama (indirect extinguishing) while the Rabbis permit it. This is illustrated by the case of new earthenware vessels filled with water: the Rabbis allow placing them near a fire (they will burst and extinguish it indirectly); Rabbi Yosei forbids it.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Ben Beteira disagrees with the First Tanna about where rescued items may be carried. What is his ruling?
A. Ben Beteira permits rescuing more food; the First Tanna limits it to three meals
B. Ben Beteira permits carrying to a courtyard even without an eiruv; the First Tanna requires an established eiruv
C. Ben Beteira permits calling others to help; the First Tanna requires one to act alone
D. Ben Beteira permits carrying to the public domain; the First Tanna limits it to a courtyard
Correct: B — The First Tanna limits carrying rescued items to a courtyard where an eiruv was established — normal Shabbat carrying rules apply. Ben Beteira goes further: the distress of a fire permits carrying even to a courtyard without an eiruv, relaxing standard Shabbat carrying restrictions due to the emergency.
