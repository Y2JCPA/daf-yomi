🧴 Is Borit Sulphur? A Talmudic Proof

Initial Assumption
The mishna lists borit among laundry-cleaning materials used on Shabbat, alongside urine and cimolian earth — perhaps borit is simply sulphur?
Objection
If borit is sulphur, why does a baraita on the Sabbatical year list it alongside wormwood, bulbs of ornithogalum, and aloe? Sulphur is a mineral — it would never be subject to Sabbatical year restrictions on plants!
Sages
Correct — borit must be a plant-derived scouring agent, not sulphur. Its inclusion in the Sabbatical year list proves its botanical origin.
Rav Yehuda
Regarding cimolian earth in the mishna: this is what people call shelof dotz (pull-out stick) — a specific fine clay used for whitening cloth.
Ruling Borit is a plant-based cleanser, not sulphur — confirmed by Sabbatical year law. Cimolian earth = shelof dotz.

🛍️ The Merchant's Basket & Sacred Scrolls

Merchant's Basket Rule: One who carries out a basket containing many types of spices and jewelry on Shabbat is liable for only one sin-offering.
↓
Why? He performed a single act of hotza'ah (carrying out). The multiplicity of items does not multiply the transgression — one act = one offering.
↓
Sacred Scrolls: The measure for carrying out Torah scrolls or their coverings destroyed by the mekek insect is any amount — even a tiny scrap is preserved and thus significant.
↓
Two types of mekek: One destroys the scrolls themselves; another destroys only the coverings. Each has its own minimum measure — any amount — because people store even damaged remnants.
↓
Unifying Rule: The measure is always the minimum amount a normal person would consider worth storing or preserving — objective use or subjective stored intent both suffice.

⚙️ Metals and Temple Vow Halakhot

כל שהוא — any amount — applies wherever the smallest quantity serves a defined purpose
Gemara Digression From the ruling that any amount of metal creates Shabbat liability, the Gemara derives a related Temple law: one who vows 'metal for the Temple' without specification must give enough to fashion a small functional object — even a tiny pin or hook. The smallest piece of metal is halachically significant both for Shabbat and for Temple consecration. Shmuel adds: he asked all the seafarers about the mysterious eshlag listed in the mishna and could not identify it — it remains unknown.
