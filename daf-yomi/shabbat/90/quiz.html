QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the Gemara rule that any amount of pepper creates Shabbat carrying liability?
A. Because pepper is an expensive commodity worth protecting
B. Because even the tiniest amount can dispel mouth odor
C. Because pepper is used to dye small garments
D. Because pepper is required for Temple incense offerings
Correct: B — The Gemara explicitly answers: for what use is any amount of pepper suited? To dispel mouth odor — even one grain is useful, so any amount creates liability.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does the Gemara prove that borit is NOT sulphur?
A. The Gemara states directly that borit is a plant, not a mineral
B. A baraita lists borit alongside Sabbatical year plants — sulphur as a mineral would not be subject to those restrictions
C. Sulphur is too expensive to be a common laundry agent in Talmudic times
D. Shmuel asked seafarers and they confirmed borit is not sulphur
Correct: B — The proof is from the Sabbatical year baraita, which lists borit alongside wormwood and aloe. Since Sabbatical year restrictions apply only to plants, borit must be plant-derived, not mineral sulphur.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
One carries out a merchant's basket containing ten types of spices and five types of jewelry on Shabbat. How many sin-offerings is he liable for?
A. Fifteen — one per item type
B. Two — one for spices and one for jewelry
C. Ten — one per spice type only
D. One — because he performed a single act of carrying out
Correct: D — The mishna rules he is liable for only one sin-offering. He performed a single act of hotza'ah (carrying out). Multiplicity of contents does not multiply the transgression.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
A person stored a single cucumber seed specifically to use as a medicinal remedy. If he carries it out on Shabbat, what is his liability?
A. No liability — one seed is below any reasonable minimum measure
B. Liable only if the seed weighs more than a dried fig
C. Liable for carrying out any amount, because his stored intent makes it significant to him
D. Liable only if a doctor prescribed the specific seed
Correct: C — The mishna teaches: one who stores a seed for medicinal purposes and carries it out is liable for any amount. By storing it, he demonstrates it is significant — personal intent overrides the standard minimum measure.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to the baraita cited in the Gemara, what is the minimum measure of date pits that makes one liable if they were carried out specifically for use as fuel?
A. One pit
B. Two pits
C. Enough to cook a small egg
D. Any amount — fuel use makes even one pit significant
Correct: C — The baraita distinguishes: for planting — two pits; for fuel — enough to cook a small egg (the standard cooking measure); for children's play — one pit. Purpose determines the minimum measure.
