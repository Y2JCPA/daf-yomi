⚖️ Two Central Disputes of Daf 125

Rabbi Meir
Shards of an old oven may be moved on Shabbat — they still serve a function and are not fully stripped of vessel status.
Rabbi Yehuda
Shards of an old oven may not be moved — the Torah commanded their breaking, which removes their vessel status entirely.
Rabba
To permit moving a stone as part of a vessel, an action is required (e.g., tying it so it doesn't fall). Mental designation alone is insufficient.
Rav Yosef
No physical action is needed — mental designation alone suffices to integrate a stone into a vessel and permit its movement.
Core Question What transforms a broken or separate object into a permitted vessel — function, designation, or physical action?

🔥 The Oven Shards Debate: Three Positions

Abaye
The dispute concerns shards that serve some function but not similar to their original use. Rabbi Yehuda requires similarity to the original function; Rabbi Meir does not.
Rava
Objection: If that were true, why debate an oven specifically? Any shards would raise this question. The dispute must hinge on something unique to oven shards.
Rav Ashi
The dispute is about shards used as a ceramic board (tapka). Rabbi Meir: this resembles the oven's function. Rabbi Yehuda: it does not — an oven bakes standing and fires from within; a shard fires from without and bakes lying.
Key Verse (Vayikra 11:35) "They shall be broken in pieces… they are impure" — does commanded breaking permanently strip an object of vessel status?

🪨 Stone in Gourd: Designating an Object as a Vessel

Rabba — Action Required

  • A physical action must designate the stone as part of a vessel
  • Proof from mishna: stone must not fall out when filling — only then is it integrated
  • Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi told people to go tie the beams — action was essential
  • Mental intent alone does not suffice

Rav Yosef — Intent Suffices

  • Mental designation alone is sufficient — no action needed
  • Proof from mishna: stone that falls = prohibited, because it was never mentally designated
  • The prohibition flows from lack of intent, not absence of a physical act
  • Intent alone can include or exclude an object from vessel status

🪟 Window Shutter: When Is It Permitted on Shabbat?

The Scenario: A window shutter exists before Shabbat. May one close the window with it on Shabbat, or is this prohibited as boneh (building)?
↓
Rabbi Eliezer: Permitted only if the shutter is tied and hanging from the window — not touching the ground. In this case it remains part of the window structure and closing it is not an act of building.
↓
The Rabbis: Permitted in both cases — whether tied/hanging or touching the ground — as long as the shutter pre-existed Shabbat.
↓
Rabba bar bar Ḥana (R. Yoḥanan): All agree one may not construct a new shutter on Shabbat. The entire debate is about using a pre-existing shutter, not building from scratch.
↓
Rabbi Abba (Rav Kahana): 'Both in this case and in that case' means: whether the pre-existing shutter has a handle or not — the Rabbis permit shuttering in either scenario.
