💬 'Let Me Borrow' vs 'Loan Me' — Why Does Phrasing Matter?

Rava bar Rav Ḥanan
What is different about 'let me borrow' (השאילני) that makes it permitted on Shabbat, while 'loan me' (הלויני) is forbidden? They both describe the same transaction!
Abaye
Loan language implies a formal monetary arrangement — the kind that one might write down. 'Borrowing' implies a neighborly exchange with no written record expected.
Rava bar Rav Ḥanan
But on weekdays, people often say 'let me borrow' when they actually mean a loan, and the lender still writes it down! Your distinction is imperfect and will not prevent Shabbat violations.
Abaye
Even an imperfect verbal distinction serves its purpose. The changed phrase creates a mental reminder not to record the transaction. The Rabbis asked for a signal, not a guarantee.
Principle Language shapes intention. Even imperfect verbal distinctions distance us from prohibited acts on Shabbat by interrupting habitual patterns.

🦴 Treating a Dislocated Limb on Shabbat

✅ Permitted

  • Rinse the dislocated hand or foot in cold water in the normal manner
  • If it heals as a result — it heals (unintended healing is not prohibited)
  • Reset a broken bone (per Shmuel's version of the Mishna, as the accepted ruling)

❌ Forbidden

  • Vigorously move the limb about in water as deliberate therapeutic exercise
  • Performing any act whose primary purpose is healing (refuah) on Shabbat
  • Rav Avya's demonstration before Rav Yosef — doing various maneuvers with the dislocated hand

🪣 Drawing Water on a Festival — When Shinui Is Impossible

General rule: On Festivals, we alter our manner of performing acts from weekday practice as much as possible — this is called שינוי (shinui)
↓
Women draw water from rivers or streams on Festivals to fill large pitchers — identical to what they do on weekdays
↓
Suggestion 1: Have her spread a cloth over the vessel while drawing — rejected because she may come to squeeze the wet cloth, which is a separate prohibition
↓
Suggestion 2: Cover the vessel with a lid — rejected because sometimes no lid is available, making this unreliable
↓
Conclusion: When every alternative to a forbidden act generates a new prohibition or is practically unworkable, the Sages do not impose a shinui requirement

👁️ Witnessing Public Sin — When to Rebuke and When to Stay Silent

מוטב שיהיו שוגגין ואל יהיו מזידין
הנח להם לישראל — Leave Israel Alone Rava bar Rav Ḥanan noted that people clap hands, slap their bodies, and dance on Festivals — all rabbinically prohibited — yet the Sages said nothing to stop them. Abaye ruled: Leave Israel alone. It is better they sin unknowingly (שוגגין) than presumptuously (מזידין). If rebuke will not be heeded, silence prevents the sin from becoming intentional. A common misreading: Some thought this applies only to rabbinic prohibitions. The Gemara corrects this — the principle applies equally to Torah-level prohibitions when rebuke will not be accepted.
