⚖️ Abaye vs. Rava: Does Intent Define the Transgression?

Abaye
One who intends to lift a detached plant but instead severs an attached plant is exempt — he performed a completely different act than intended, belonging to a different legal category.
Rava
Agrees he is exempt — but derives this from the baraita's logic: the unique structure of Shabbat liability means that performing an unintended forbidden labor without awareness of that specific labor yields no liability.
Stakes of the Dispute Both exempt the person, but the reasoning diverges — Abaye focuses on the mismatch of object/act; Rava focuses on absence of mental engagement with the forbidden labor. The difference determines how the baraita about Shabbat strictures is to be read.

🌾 Abaye vs. Rava — Intent and Shabbat Labor

Abaye
Intended to cut a detached plant (permitted); accidentally severed an attached plant (reaping — forbidden).

Ruling: Exempt.
The act performed was categorically different from the intended act — it is as if he did something entirely uninstructed.
Rava
Same ruling — exempt — but derived from the baraita:

Shabbat has a stricture (many labors, multiple sin-offerings) AND a leniency (performing a labor without awareness of that specific prohibition → exempt). This leniency explains the exemption here.
Why the Reasoning Matters If Abaye is right, the baraita about Shabbat strictures cannot be proven from this case. If Rava is right, the baraita actively supports the exemption and reveals the unique structure of Shabbat liability.

📊 Shabbat vs. Other Mitzvot — The Baraita's Four-Part Analysis

Shabbat — Stricture

  • 39 prohibited labors all fall under one prohibition
  • Yet each distinct labor performed in one lapse of awareness carries its own sin-offering
  • No parallel in other mitzvot: a single forgotten prohibition generating multiple distinct atonements

Shabbat — Leniency

  • Performing a labor without awareness that it specifically is forbidden → exempt
  • Intent mismatch (intended permitted act, performed forbidden one) → exempt
  • Contrast with other prohibitions where the act alone can suffice for liability

Idolatry — Stricture

  • Bowing due to love or fear — even without worship intent — is liable
  • The outward act of prostration is idolatry; internal intent cannot fully negate it
  • Stricter than Shabbat in this dimension

Idolatry — Leniency

  • All modes of idolatrous worship fall under one prohibition
  • Single lapse of awareness covering all worship acts → one sin-offering
  • Unlike Shabbat's per-labor accounting

כורע מאהבה ומיראה — חייב
🙇 The Bowing Case — כורע מאהבה ומיראה One bows before an idol due to love of the person who asked him to bow, or due to fear of one coercing him. He believes: 'Unless I wholeheartedly intend to worship, I have not sinned.'

The Gemara rules: he is liable. The act of bowing before an idol is itself the prohibition — his mistaken belief that intention is required does not constitute ones (duress) and cannot exempt him. The act speaks louder than the motive.

📋 Shabbat 72 — Summary Map

Core Question of Daf 72:
When does awareness (ידיעה) or intent transform a prohibited act into a separate liability?
