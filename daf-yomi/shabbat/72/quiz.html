Shabbat 72 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
If a man has relations with a designated maidservant (שפחה חרופה) five times, and becomes fully aware of his transgression between each act, how many guilt-offerings must he bring?
A. Five — one for each act
B. Two — awareness after the first act divides the liability
C. One guilt-offering total
D. None — awareness after the fact creates retroactive permission
Correct: C — The Gemara rules explicitly that awareness is insignificant (אין ידיעה מחלקת) with regard to the designated maidservant — the entire series of acts counts as a single transgression requiring only one guilt-offering.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rava, why is one who intends to lift a detached plant but accidentally severs an attached plant on Shabbat exempt?
A. Accidental acts are always exempt on Shabbat
B. He performed a different act than intended, which is a different legal category entirely
C. He lacked awareness of the specific labor of reaping as a Shabbat prohibition
D. Severing attached plants is only rabbinically prohibited
Correct: C — Rava's reasoning (distinct from Abaye's) is that the baraita's leniency of Shabbat applies here: one is only liable for a Shabbat labor if his mind was engaged with that specific forbidden labor. Since he was thinking about a permitted act, he had no awareness of performing reaping.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the unique 'stricture of Shabbat' described in the baraita that does not apply to other mitzvot?
A. Shabbat violations carry karet (excision) while other mitzvot do not
B. Many distinct forbidden labors fall under one prohibition, yet each carries its own separate sin-offering liability
C. Shabbat requires two witnesses for all forms of punishment
D. Children are liable for Shabbat violations just as adults
Correct: B — The baraita identifies Shabbat's stricture as the combination of one overarching prohibition containing 39 labors, yet each labor performed in a single lapse of awareness generating its own sin-offering — a structure unlike any other prohibition in the Torah.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
One bows before an idol because a friend asked him to and he was afraid of embarrassing that friend. He believes that since he had no worshipful intention, he did not sin. What does the Gemara rule?
A. Exempt — lack of worshipful intent is a complete defense to idolatry
B. Liable — the physical act of bowing constitutes idolatry regardless of his motivation
C. Liable only by rabbinic decree, not biblically
D. It is a dispute between Abaye and Rava left unresolved
Correct: B — The Gemara rules he is liable. The act of bowing before an idol is itself prohibited — his mistaken belief that sincere worshipful intent was required does not exempt him. The outward, public act of prostration carries the full weight of the prohibition.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rava raises a dilemma before Rav Naḥman about someone who is unaware of both the existence of Shabbat and the individual prohibited labors. What specifically is Rava uncertain about?
A. Whether such a person is liable at all, given his complete ignorance
B. Whether he is liable to one or to many sin-offerings for the labors he performed
C. Whether his status is like an infant or a gentile
D. Whether he requires atonement from Yom Kippur or a personal sacrifice
Correct: B — Rava's dilemma concerns the count of sin-offerings: given that this person had a single 'lapse of awareness' that covered both Shabbat itself and all its labors, is that one undivided state of ignorance — yielding one sin-offering — or do the multiple labors performed each demand their own offering?
