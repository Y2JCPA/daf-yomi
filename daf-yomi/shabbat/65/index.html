🦷 Gold Tooth: Permitted or Prohibited on Shabbat?

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
A woman may go out with a false tooth or a gold tooth. Removing it would embarrass her — so she will not remove it in public. No concern of carrying in the public domain.
The Rabbis
A gold tooth is attractive — she may be tempted to remove and show it to friends. This creates a real concern of carrying. It is prohibited.
Rabbi Zeira
The dispute applies only when she has no other tooth to replace it. If she has another tooth, even Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi would agree it is prohibited — no embarrassment in removing it.
Abaye
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, Rabbi Eliezer, and Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar all share one principle: anything that makes her unappealing when removed — she will not remove it. Therefore it is permitted.
Core Rule If removing an item would embarrass a woman, she will not remove it in public — and therefore no rabbinic concern of carrying exists.

🧵 Four Cloths a Woman May Carry on Shabbat

Cloth in Ear

  • Permitted only if tied to the ear
  • Rami bar Yeḥezkel: tying prevents her from coming to carry it
  • Rabbi Yoḥanan went out with one; colleagues disagreed — his was untied
  • Rabbi Yannai went out; he held even untied is permitted

Cloth in Sandal

  • Permitted if tied to the sandal
  • Rami bar Yeḥezkel: tying is the essential condition
  • Untied — prohibited due to concern of carrying

Menstrual Cloth

  • Rava: permitted even without tying between thighs
  • Her modesty prevents her from removing it in public
  • Rabbi Yirmeya's dilemma: what if she uses a handgrip to hold it? — left unresolved (תיקו)

Coin on Foot Wound

  • Tzinit: wound on the sole of the foot
  • A sela coin is tied onto it for healing
  • The coin's rust is medicinal — not any hard object
  • An earthenware shard is an insufficient substitute

🌿 Shared Principle: Modesty as the Legal Safeguard

Rabbi Eliezer
A woman who went out with a bundle of fragrant herbs or a flask of balsam oil is exempt from a sin-offering. These are adornments — she will not remove something that enhances her appearance.
Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar
Anything worn beneath the hair-net may be taken into the public domain. A woman will not uncover her hair in public — so there is no concern she will remove and carry it.
Abaye
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi (false tooth), Rabbi Eliezer (herbs/balsam), and Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar (under the net) all hold the same principle: social embarrassment or modesty removes the concern of carrying.
Principle — אַיִן מְשַׁחֲקֶת Anything that makes her unappealing when removed — she will not remove it to show others. The behavioral norm itself is the legal protection.

🍬 Pepper & Salt: Medical Items Worn in the Mouth

Pepper in the mouth — placed to prevent mouth odor; a recognized medical remedy treated as part of the body
↓
Grain of salt in the mouth — placed to treat a toothache; similarly a bodily remedy, not an object being carried
↓
Ruling: Permitted on Shabbat — since they serve a medical purpose and remain part of the body while in use
↓
Concern addressed: Could she spit it out and come to carry it? The medical need keeps her from removing it unnecessarily — same logic as the 'embarrassment' principle
↓
Broader application: Small medical items that a person needs do not create carrying concern — the person's own interest ensures the item stays in place

🦵 Wooden Leg & the Nut-as-Button Dilemma

Man with amputated leg — may he go out with a wooden leg on Shabbat?
↓
Rabbi Meir: Permitted — the wooden leg has the legal status of a shoe (worn on the body, not carried in the hand)
↓
Abaye's Dilemma: A woman fastens her cloak on a nut to carry the nut to her child in the public domain — permissible artifice or prohibited?
↓
Argument for Permitting
Wearing garments is the normal way to transport them (as in fire case). Fastening a cloak on a nut is also normal dress behavior — perhaps permitted even by those who forbid artifice.
Argument for Prohibiting
In a fire, one must use artifice or lose everything. Here the woman acts purely for personal gain. The artifice is calculated circumvention — perhaps prohibited even for those who permit it in fires.
↓
Unresolved (תיקו) — the dilemma stands; Gemara offers no final ruling
