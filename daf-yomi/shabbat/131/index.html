🌿 Rabbi Eliezer vs. the Rabbis: Does Lulav Override Shabbat?

Rabbi Eliezer
The mitzva of lulav and all its facilitators override Shabbat. The verse says 'on that day' — meaning even on Shabbat one must fulfill it!
Rabbis
'On that day' is needed to teach that lulav must be performed by day, not by night. It cannot also be used to permit Shabbat overrides.
Rabbi Eliezer
I derive 'daytime only' from a separate verse — 'You shall rejoice before God seven days' — so 'on that day' remains free and available for Shabbat override.
Rabbis
That derivation was already needed to prevent conflating the seven days of lulav with the seven days of Pesaḥ. It is not free for your purpose either.
Hermeneutic Key A gezerah shavah (verbal analogy) is only valid when the shared term is 'free' — i.e., superfluous in its own context. If both sides are needed for something else, the analogy cannot be built.

📿 Which Mitzvot Override Shabbat? Rabbi Eliezer's Ruling

✅ Override Shabbat (Rabbi Eliezer)

  • ✂️ Circumcision — explicit Torah verse (Gen. 17)
  • 🌾 Omer offering — gezerah shavah with circumcision
  • 🌿 Lulav — 'on that day' + gezerah shavah chain
  • 🪵 Sukka — 'seven days' gezerah shavah with lulav
  • 🫓 Matza — 'fifteenth' gezerah shavah with Sukkot
  • 🎺 Shofar — 'on that day' + gezerah shavah chain

❌ Do NOT Override Shabbat

  • 🧵 Tzitzit — attaching fringes to a garment
  • 📜 Mezuza — affixing to a doorpost
  • Rav Yosef's reason: No fixed time — the mitzva has no deadline
  • Challenge: Every moment IS its time — it's perpetual!
  • Resolution: Fixed time means an irreplaceable deadline — later is still possible for tzitzit

⚖️ Rabbi Yoḥanan's Limiting Principle — Step by Step

Rabbi Yoḥanan states: Rabbi Eliezer did not say that all mitzva preparations override Shabbat — only specific, enumerated ones.
↓
Question: What does this statement come to exclude? Which mitzvot are being ruled out?
↓
Rav Adda bar Ahava answers: It excludes attaching tzitzit to a garment and affixing a mezuza to a doorpost.
↓
Baraita confirms: These two mitzvot do not override Shabbat — consistent with Rabbi Eliezer's actual view.
↓
Rav Yosef explains why: Tzitzit and mezuza have no fixed time — they are not time-bound. One can fulfill them at any moment.
↓
Counter-challenge: Since one is obligated at every moment, perhaps every moment is its fixed time — making it perpetually urgent?
↓
The underlying principle: 'Fixed time' requires a deadline after which the opportunity is lost forever. Tzitzit missed today can be done tomorrow — but circumcision on day 8, or lulav on the first day of Sukkot, cannot be made up.

🏠 Opening Sugya — Alleyway Without Courtyards The standard alleyway (mavui) eruv requires: at least two courtyards opening into the alleyway, and at least two houses per courtyard. A case arises where there are houses but no courtyards — the standard eruv rules don't apply. Proposed solutions: (1) Residents relinquish rights to one person, converting all into a single domain. (2) Split the day — one person owns from morning to noon, another from noon to night, creating two legal 'houses.' The Gemara rejects the time-split solution: this creates an awkward hybrid arrangement, not a genuine second house. The discussion segues into the major theme of the daf: when do Shabbat prohibitions yield to mitzva performance?
