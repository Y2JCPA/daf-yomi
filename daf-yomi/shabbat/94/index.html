🛏️ Carrying a Person: First Tanna vs. Rabbi Natan

First Tanna
One who carries out a living person on a bed is exempt — even for the bed itself — because the living person lightens their own load by shifting weight.
Rabbi Natan
Agreed in principle — but a bound person who cannot move cannot lighten the load. In that case one is liable for the bed as well!
Rav Adda bar Mattana → Abaye
What about Persians who are always considered as if bound and rigid? Would Rabbi Natan make one liable whenever carrying them on Shabbat?
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Rabbi Natan's rule is precise: the exemption only applies when the carried person can actively adjust their weight. Remove that capacity and liability follows.
Core Principle A living body is self-supporting — it shifts weight to ease the carrier and is therefore considered to carry itself. A corpse or bound person cannot, and the carrier bears full liability.

💅 Fingernail Removal: Scope of the Dispute

Mishna
One who removes fingernails with one another or with teeth: Rabbi Eliezer deems liable; the Rabbis deem exempt.
Rabbi Elazar (Gemara)
The dispute only applies to removal by hand. Using a utensil? Everyone agrees one is liable — this is even explicit in the mishna!
Rabbi Elazar (continued)
Further: the dispute only applies when removing nails for oneself. Removing nails for another person — everyone agrees one is exempt.
Gemara Challenges
Both points are obvious from the mishna! Why does Rabbi Elazar bother stating them? The Gemara struggles to identify the non-obvious content he is adding.
Ruling Matrix Utensil on self → all agree liable. Hand on self → disputed. By hand for another → all agree exempt. The mishna's debate lives in the narrow middle case.

✂️ Minimum Measures for Shabbat Liability

Hair / Nail Removal

  • Liable when removing enough to fill the opening of scissors
  • = Two hairs (Rabbis' minimum)
  • Rabbi Eliezer: liable for even one hair
  • Tosefta and baraita both confirm the two-hair standard
  • Applies to head hair, mustache, and beard

Corpse-Impurity Carrying

  • Olive-bulk (כזית) of corpse → liable
  • Olive-bulk (כזית) of animal carcass → liable
  • Lentil-bulk (כעדשה) of creeping animal → liable
  • Each measure is its own Torah minimum for that category
  • Rav Sheshet derives his position from inferring the larger measure

Partially Severed Nails (R. Shimon ben Elazar)

  • Majority of nail already severed + facing upward (causing pain) → permitted l'chatchila
  • Majority severed + facing downward (no pain) → permitted b'diavad only
  • Same rule applies to shreds of skin (skin-tags)
  • Minority severed → forbidden in all cases

🏙️ The Corpse of Derokera — Rav Naḥman's Ruling

הָיָה מֵת בִּדְרוֹקַרְת, וְהִתִּיר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק לְהוֹצִיאוֹ לְכַרְמְלִית
Halakha in Practice A corpse was stranded in the city of Derokera on Shabbat and could not remain where it was. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak permitted carrying it into a karmelit (semi-public domain), invoking rabbinic leniency to prevent the disgrace of an unattended corpse (כבוד המת).

Moving into a karmelit is only a rabbinic prohibition — not Torah-level — making leniency more accessible. Rabbi Yoḥanan, brother of Mar son of Rabbana, raised questions about the limits of this principle, probing how far human dignity can override Shabbat restrictions.

Lesson: Rabbinic prohibitions may be suspended for pressing human dignity concerns; Torah-level carrying restrictions require stricter conditions.
