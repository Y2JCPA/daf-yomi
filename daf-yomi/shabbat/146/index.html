🐍 The Snake's Contamination — זוהמת הנחש When the snake seduced Eve and she ate from the Tree of Knowledge, it infected all humanity with moral contamination (zuhama). When Israel stood at Mount Sinai, their contamination ceased — but not that of the nations of the world.

Rabbi Abba bar Kahana offers a stricter view: even for Israel, contamination persisted for three generations. Abraham fathered Ishmael (of lowly character); Isaac fathered Esau. Only with Jacob, whose twelve sons were all righteous, did the contamination fully cease.

🪣 Breaking Open a Barrel on Shabbat

Mishna
One may break a barrel on Shabbat to eat dried figs from it — provided one does not intend to make a vessel from the shards.
Rabbi Oshaya
This applies only when figs are pressed together, requiring a utensil to separate them. Since a utensil is needed anyway, the barrel fragments may serve as one — no new vessel-making intent is required.
Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel
One may bring a barrel of wine and cut off its top with a sword, placing it before guests on Shabbat — with no concern even for loose contents.
Gemara (reconciling)
Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel follows Rabbi Neḥemya who permits even incidental vessel-making through a destructive act. The Mishna follows the majority opinion — hence Rabbi Oshaya limits it to pressed figs only.
The Core Principle A destructive act (mekalkel) that incidentally creates a vessel — is it prohibited as makeh b'patish (the finishing blow)?

🪈 Rav vs. Shmuel: Barrel Modifications on Shabbat

Rav — Stringent

  • ❌ Inserting a reed spout into a barrel hole: prohibited
  • ❌ Sealing a barrel hole with oil: prohibited
  • ❌ Wrapping in felt cloaks: restricted (ruling by inference from conduct)
  • Reason: these acts complete or modify a vessel's structure on Shabbat

Shmuel — Lenient

  • ✅ Inserting a reed spout into an existing hole: permitted
  • ✅ Sealing a barrel hole with oil: permitted
  • ✅ Felt cloaks may be worn and transferred on Shabbat
  • Exception — Tavut citing Shmuel: inserting a myrtle leaf as spout is prohibited (appears like fixing a utensil)

🍲 Insulating and Cooling Food on Shabbat

Mishna permits: Placing a cooked dish into an empty pit on Shabbat to protect it from heat
↓
Why state the obvious? Lest one think a rabbinic decree against insulating to add heat might extend to neutral insulation — the Mishna clarifies it does not
↓
Also permitted: Submerging a vessel of good potable water into bad water to cool it — the good water is improved, not ruined
↓
Garments that fell in water: One may put them back on while wet and walk — no concern about wringing or laundering
↓
Drying restriction: One may dry garments in the sun, but not visibly before the masses — it would appear as laundering on Shabbat (marat ayin)
