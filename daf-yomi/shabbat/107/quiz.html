Shabbat 107 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav, if a bird flies under one's clothing on Shabbat and cannot escape, what is the correct course of action?
A. Immediately release the bird to avoid any trapping violation
B. Sit and secure it until dark, then take it
C. Ask a non-Jew to remove the bird
D. Walk to a public domain so the bird escapes on its own
Correct: B — Rav rules one may sit and secure the bird until Shabbat ends, then take it. This falls under Shmuel's category of actions that are Torah-exempt AND rabbinically permitted — one of only three such cases.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is Shmuel's key principle about actions that are 'exempt' on Shabbat?
A. All exempt actions are fully permitted on Shabbat
B. Exempt actions are only prohibited if done with intent
C. All exempt actions are rabbinically prohibited, except for three specific cases that are permitted
D. Exempt actions become prohibited only when performed publicly
Correct: C — Shmuel teaches that 'exempt' does not mean 'permitted' — all Torah-exempt Shabbat acts remain rabbinically forbidden. Only three exceptional cases are both Torah-exempt and rabbinically permitted.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the core dispute between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish regarding the eight creeping animals?
A. Whether the eight sheratzim can be trapped at all on Shabbat
B. Whether their skin is distinct from their flesh, affecting liability for wounding
C. Whether killing them is permitted for purposes of hygiene
D. Whether they are classified as 'animals' or 'insects' for Shabbat law
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan holds the sheratzim possess distinct skin, making wounding them a Shabbat liability. Reish Lakish holds their skin is indistinguishable from their flesh, which complicates or eliminates wound liability.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
When does Rav (citing Rabbi Shimon) say that trapping a snake on Shabbat is exempt?
A. Only when the snake is non-venomous
B. Only when trapping it indoors rather than in a public area
C. When trapping it to prevent being bitten, as opposed to for medicinal purposes
D. Only when less than a tefach of the snake's body has emerged from its hole
Correct: C — Rav rules that trapping a snake defensively — so it does not bite — is a purposeless/protective act and therefore exempt. But trapping it for medicinal use is purposeful and constitutes a liable melacha.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
From where does Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi derive that a halakhic 'wound' is defined as something irreversible?
A. From the construction of the Mishkan, where reddened ram skins were permanently dyed
B. From the verse 'Can a Cushite change his skin, or a leopard its spots?' (Yirmiyahu 13:23)
C. From the laws of bodily damages outlined in Bava Kamma
D. From the story of Ya'akov's permanent limp after wrestling the angel
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi answers Levi's dilemma by citing Yirmiyahu 13:23 — 'Can a Cushite change his skin, or a leopard its spots (ḥavarburotav)?' — proving that a wound or mark is inherently permanent and irreversible.
