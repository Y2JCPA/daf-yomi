⚖️ Which Bleeding Days Allow Birth to Purify?

Levi
Birth purifies a woman only if she bled during the eleven days fit for becoming a greater zava — bleeding on menstruation days alone does not count.
Abba Shaul (Rav)
Birth purifies even if she bled on days fit for a lesser zava. 'Days' (ימים) and 'many days' (ימים רבים) are both written — one for lesser, one for greater zava.
Gemara
Challenge to Levi: Rabbi Meir says labor pains can begin forty or fifty days before birth — how does Levi account for such a long span without ziva?
Levi
The mishna doesn't say she is pure throughout all those days — only that blood emitted specifically due to labor pains is pure from ziva. She may still have regular niddah periods in between.
Core Principle דם חבלה — blood of labor pains — is attributed by the Torah to birth, not to ziva. Birth 'consumes' the potential ziva impurity, provided the right conditions are met.

🩸 Rav vs. Levi: Conditions for Birth to Purify

Levi

  • Requires bleeding during the 11 days of ziva
  • Specifically days fit for becoming a greater zava (3 consecutive days)
  • Stricter — fewer scenarios where birth purifies
  • Menstruation-day bleeding alone does NOT qualify

Rav (via Abba Shaul)

  • Requires bleeding on days fit for lesser zava too
  • Derives from dual language: 'days' + 'many days' in the verse
  • More lenient — a broader set of qualifying scenarios
  • Lesser zava: 1–2 days of bleeding in the 11-day period

Rabbi Meir's Scenario

  • Labor pains can begin 40–50 days before birth
  • Blood during this period: pure from ziva entirely
  • Constructed a theoretical 150-day span without greater ziva
  • Used sequencing of menstruation, ziva, and post-birth purity

📅 Rabbi Meir's 150-Day Calculation

Days 1–2: She bleeds on non-menstruation days — start of 11-day ziva window; two days only, not yet a zava
↓
Day 3 (Menstruation begins): Bleeding on day 3 falls within her seven days of menstruation, breaking the three-consecutive-day ziva sequence
↓
Days 4–9: Remaining six days of menstruation with blood — she is a niddah, not a zava
↓
Days 10–20: Eleven days of ziva follow — she experiences labor pains with blood; attributed entirely to birth, not ziva
↓
Days 21–27 + 28–41: Another menstruation cycle, then 40 days of purity after a male birth (80 after female), totaling 141 days
↓
Grand total: ~150 days of blood during labor-attributed pain — never once rendering her a greater zava, per Rabbi Meir

🕍 Why Only Wednesday? The Chasidim Rishonim

ויבא אליה ויתן ה׳ לה הריון — רות ד:יג
Early Pious Men's Practice The חסידים הראשונים (early generations of pious men) would engage in marital relations only on Wednesday (Revi'i). Their concern: if a woman gives birth after a full nine months (271–273 days), relations on Sunday, Monday, or Tuesday would result in birth falling on Shabbat — requiring the midwife to desecrate it. Wednesday relations meant birth fell on Wednesday, Thursday, or Friday — safely before Shabbat.

Mar Zutra derives 271 days from Ruth 4:13: 'He went in unto her, and God gave her conception' — the phrase corresponds to 271 words of Hallel, hinting at the full gestational period.

📜 Rabbi Eliezer vs. Rabbis: The Dayo Principle

Rabbis
A fortiori: if blood emitted at rest before birth renders a woman a zava, then blood during labor — which is more intense — should certainly render her a zava during her days of purity!
R. Eliezer
Dayo (דיו) — it is sufficient! The conclusion of a kal va'chomer need only equal its source, not exceed it. The verse was lenient about labor blood — that leniency stands here too.
Rabbis
We reframe: where the verse was strict about resting blood, it was lenient about labor blood. So where it was lenient about resting blood (days of purity), it should certainly be lenient about labor blood — she should be fully pure!
R. Eliezer
Even if you construct such inferences all day, dayo holds. The leniency is only from greater ziva — she may still become a niddah or lesser zava from labor blood.
Rava's Verdict Rabbi Eliezer triumphed: the Rabbis' own source verse ('And if a woman…') confirms the Torah specifically addressed labor blood — pure from ziva, but niddah and lesser-zava impurity can still apply.
