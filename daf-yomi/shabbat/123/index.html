⚖️ Abaye Challenges Rabba on Vessel Movement

Rabba
Using a vessel for a permitted purpose — even one not its designated use — is permitted on Shabbat. The key is that a permitted action is being performed.
Abaye
Objection! The Tosefta says a mortar may only be moved if it still has garlic in it — implying it cannot be moved for any other permitted purpose!
Rabba
That Tosefta follows Rabbi Neḥemya's strict minority view: a vessel may only be moved for its designated use. The accepted majority opinion is more lenient.
Abaye
Second objection! Beit Shammai prohibit using a large pestle to cut meat — even for a permitted Festival purpose. That's not Rabbi Neḥemya!
Rabba
Beit Shammai here also follow Rabbi Neḥemya's stringency. The accepted halakha permits moving any vessel for any permitted need using the object itself.
📌 Core Principle Using an object whose primary function is prohibited, for the purpose of utilizing the object itself for a permitted action, is permitted on Shabbat — this is the majority ruling.

🪡 The Broken Needle: Rava vs. Abaye

Rava bar Rabba
May one move a needle whose eye or point has been removed? It can still extract a thorn — is it still a vessel for Shabbat purposes?
Rav Yosef
The mishna answers: one may take an ordinary needle to extract a thorn. What does the thorn care whether the needle is whole or broken?
Rava bar Rabba
But in laws of tumah, a needle missing its eye or point is not a vessel and becomes ritually pure — shouldn't Shabbat follow the same logic?
Abaye
You cannot derive Shabbat from tumah! Tumah requires a functional utensil; Shabbat only asks whether moving it serves a genuine human need.
Rava
The objection is valid! Since it is not a vessel for tumah, it is also not a vessel for Shabbat — and therefore may not be moved as a utensil.
⚖️ Resolution A baraita explicitly permits moving any needle whether perforated or not. Abaye reconciles: unfinished needles may still be repurposed. But once both eye AND point are gone, it is no longer a utensil at all.

📜 Three Stages of Muktze Leniency (Amud B — Abaye)

Original Restriction: Only three items were ever permitted to move on Shabbat: a knife for cutting dried figs, a spoon-fork for cleaning a pot, and a small key. Everything else was muktze.
↓
Stage 1 — First Leniency: Permitted to move any vessel whose primary function is permitted, for the purpose of utilizing the object itself in a permitted action.
↓
Stage 2 — Second Leniency: Even a vessel whose primary function is prohibited may be moved for the purpose of utilizing the object itself for a permitted action.
↓
Stage 3 — Rava's Full Leniency: All utensils — whether primary use is permitted or prohibited — may be moved even to free up their place, with no restriction.
↓
Historical Context (Rabbi Ḥanina): The stringent mortar-rule was enacted in the days of Nehemiah ben Hacaliah, when severe Shabbat decrees were in force (Neh. 13:15). Later authorities relaxed these restrictions progressively.

🔨 Cracking Nuts on Shabbat: Which Hammer?

Spice Merchant's Hammer

  • Primary use: crushing permitted spices
  • Both disputing opinions agree it may be used to crack nuts
  • Serves as the baseline, less controversial case
  • Owner is not overly particular about the hammer

Goldsmith's Hammer

  • Primary use: goldsmithing (מכה בפטיש — prohibited on Shabbat)
  • Rabbi Yoḥanan: permitted to crack nuts — a fortiori from spice hammer
  • If spice hammer permitted despite typical use, goldsmith's surely is too
  • Owner is more particular, yet vessel still usable per this view

Restrictive Opinion

  • Only spice merchant's hammer is permitted for cracking nuts
  • Goldsmith's hammer: owner's particularity sets it aside mentally
  • The owner's attitude toward the object affects its Shabbat status
  • Particularity (פרישות הבעלים) creates a form of muktze
