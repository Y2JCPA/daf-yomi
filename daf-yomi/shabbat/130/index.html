✂️ The Great Circumcision-on-Shabbat Debate

Rabbi Eliezer
One may carry the scalpel openly on Shabbat — and may even cut down trees to make charcoal to forge tools for circumcision! The mitzva overrides all Shabbat labor.
Rabbi Akiva
Any prohibited labor that could have been performed on Shabbat eve does NOT override Shabbat — including transporting the scalpel. Only what is impossible to do beforehand may be done on Shabbat.
🔑 Governing Principle Rabbi Akiva's rule prevails: Shabbat preparations possible on Friday must be done on Friday. Only the circumcision act itself — which can only happen on Shabbat at the appointed time — genuinely overrides the prohibition.

🤔 Why Must the Scalpel Be Carried Uncovered? — A Talmudic Dilemma

Opinion A — Affection
חיבוב מצווה — Affection for the mitzva. The scalpel is carried openly to publicize love of circumcision and to honor it. Rabbi Levi and a supporting baraita both confirm this is Rabbi Eliezer's true reason.
Opinion B — Suspicion
Avoiding suspicion. Carrying something covered on Shabbat looks secretive — people might suspect a forbidden act. Uncovered carrying demonstrates the item's lawful purpose.
✅ Resolution Rabbi Levi rules: Rabbi Eliezer's reason is affection for the mitzva. A baraita confirms: even if one could cover the scalpel, one should still carry it openly as a demonstration of love for the commandment.

🕊️ Elisha — Man of Wings

מה יונה כנפיה מגינות עליה — אף ישראל מצוות מגינות עליהם
Roman Persecution & Phylacteries Rome decreed: anyone caught wearing tefillin would have their brain pierced. Elisha donned tefillin in defiance. Spotted by a Roman officer, he fled — when caught and confronted, Elisha said he held dove's wings. The soldier opened his fist: a dove flew free. From that day forward, Elisha was called איש כנפיים — Man of Wings.

The Gemara notes: Israel is compared to a dove — just as a dove is protected by its wings, Israel is protected through its mitzvot.

💛 Mitzvot: Accepted with Joy vs. Under Persecution

Circumcision — Embraced with Joy

  • Accepted joyfully from the very beginning
  • Still practiced with enthusiasm in every generation
  • Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: mitzvot received with joy endure joyfully
  • "I rejoice over Your word like one who finds great spoil" (Ps. 119:162)
  • A city that followed Rabbi Eliezer: its people died at their appointed time — reward for affection for the mitzva

Tefillin — Practiced Under Duress

  • Observed heroically under Roman decrees of persecution
  • Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar: mitzvot for which Jews sacrificed lives endure with great strength
  • Donning tefillin requires a 'clean body' — compliance under fear was extremely difficult
  • Divine miracles protected those who sacrificed — the story of Elisha's dove
  • Yet tefillin observance was not universal in the era of persecution
