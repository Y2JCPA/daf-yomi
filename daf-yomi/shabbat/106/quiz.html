Shabbat 106 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Abbahu's baraita, which two labors remain liable on Shabbat even when performed destructively?
A. Plowing and building
B. Carrying and grinding
C. Inflicting a wound and kindling a fire
D. Sowing and reaping
Correct: C — The mishna teaches that destructive labors are generally exempt. However, Rabbi Abbahu's baraita carves out exceptions: wounding and kindling remain liable because both retain an inherent constructive dimension — a wound heals the body and fire used in execution achieves justice.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Rav Ashi say one who kindles a fire on Shabbat is liable even if done destructively?
A. Fire always causes public damage regardless of intent
B. Burning used in the execution of a priest's daughter is a constructive act of justice
C. Kindling was singled out in the Torah without regard to intent
D. All fire-related acts were treated strictly by the Sages
Correct: B — Rav Ashi derives that since the Torah prohibited kindling in the context of the execution by burning — which is a constructive act of justice — this proves that kindling retains its liable status even in seemingly destructive contexts.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Rav Ashi's criterion for distinguishing a 'small' from a 'large' enclosure?
A. Whether it has walls on all four sides
B. Whether the animal species is domesticated
C. Whether one can run after the animal and catch it in one lunge
D. Whether the enclosure is inside or outside a building
Correct: C — Rav Ashi defines a small enclosure as one where a person can chase the animal and catch it in a single lunge. In such a space the trapping is truly complete and one is liable. A large enclosure — where the animal can still evade — results in exemption.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
If two people lock a door to trap a deer and neither could have done it alone, what is the ruling?
A. Both are liable because they acted together
B. Only the person who touched the door last is liable
C. Both are exempt — neither performed a complete individual act of trapping
D. One is liable and one is exempt based on intent
Correct: C — When neither person could have locked the door alone, both are exempt. The principle is that liability for a Shabbat labor requires that one person perform a complete act. Joint action where neither individual suffices results in mutual exemption.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
One traps a sleeping deer on Shabbat. What is the ruling?
A. Exempt — a sleeping animal does not require effort to trap
B. Exempt — it is like a sick or lame deer that is already immobilized
C. Liable — a sleeping deer can wake and flee; real trapping was performed
D. Liable only if the deer was trapped in a cage, not a house
Correct: C — A sleeping deer is capable of fleeing once it wakes. Because it can still escape, genuinely trapping it constitutes a complete labor and one is liable. This contrasts with lame or sick deer — which cannot flee at all — making them already 'pre-trapped' and their confinement exempt.
