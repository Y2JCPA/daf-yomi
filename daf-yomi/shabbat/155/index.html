🐄 Animal Care on Shabbat: What Labor Is Permitted?

Performing an act of labor to assist an animal on Shabbat
↓
Untying bundles
2-knot bundles may be untied; 3-knot bundles may be spread; cedar branch bundles (zirin/kifin) forbidden as muktzeh
Processing food
Soft foods (pumpkin): permitted. Hard foods (carobs, hay): prohibited — constitutes labor rendering food edible
Force-feeding
Halata (gentle placement): permitted. Hamra'a (forcible overfeeding): prohibited

🧺 Eiruv Basket Hung on a Tree — Height Matters

Person drives stake into a tree and hangs a basket containing eiruv food
↓
📏 Above 10 handbreadths
Eiruv is INVALID
Removing bread from a tightly-tied narrow-mouthed basket inevitably moves the tree — constitutes forbidden use of its sides
📏 Below 10 handbreadths
Eiruv is VALID
Below 10 handbreadths is effectively ground level — does not constitute use of the tree itself
↓
Rav Pappa: A narrow-mouthed basket tightly tied to the tree is the key — removing the bread moves the tree, using its tzidei etz (sides)
↓
Rav Ashi: Since using a tree's sides is prohibited, one may not lean a ladder against the palm tree itself — that too is prohibited use of the tree's sides

🌾 Rav Huna vs. Rav Yehuda: What Are Peki'in, Kifin & Zirin?

Rav Huna
Peki'in and kifin are the same category — grain bundles. Peki'in = tied with 2 knots (may be untied). Kifin = tied with 3 knots (may be spread but not fully untied). Zirin = cedar branches; forbidden to move on Shabbat entirely.
Rav Yehuda
Peki'in and zirin are the same category — grain bundles. Peki'in = 2 knots (may untie). Zirin = 3 knots (may spread). Kifin = cedar branches; forbidden to move on Shabbat at all.
Gemara Summary
Both Amoraim agree: 2-knot bundles may be untied; 3-knot bundles may only be spread. They disagree only on which name belongs to which count, and which term refers to the forbidden cedar branches.
Practical Ruling One may untie 2-knot grain bundles and spread 3-knot bundles before animals on Shabbat. The cedar branch bundles — by whichever name — remain forbidden as muktzeh and may not be moved at all.

🥬 Preparing Food for Animals on Shabbat: Hard vs. Soft

✅ Permitted

  • Chopping soft pumpkins before an animal (soft, little effort)
  • Chopping animal carcass before dogs (already soft meat)
  • Crumbling straw and mixing it with alfalfa (Rav Ḥanan of Neharde'a)
  • Any exertion involving foods already soft and ready to eat
  • Rabbi Yehuda: crushing carobs specifically for a small animal

❌ Prohibited

  • Crushing hard hay for any animal — large or small
  • Crushing hard carobs for a large animal (Rabbis vs. Rabbi Yehuda)
  • Any processing that renders hard, inedible food edible through significant effort
  • Grinding or mashing foods that constitute a melacha (prohibited labor)
  • Using a tool to break down fibrous or dried plant material

🐪 Force-Feeding Animals: Hamra'a vs. Halata Defined

Rav Yehuda
Hamra'a = feeding a camel until a trough-like depression forms inside its throat from overfeeding. This is forbidden. Halata = simply placing food into its mouth. This is permitted.
Rav Yosef (objection)
Tosefta states: one may mehalketin chickens but may NOT malkitin doves in a dove-cote. If halata = hand placement (permitted), why would placing food before doves be forbidden?
Rav Yehuda (response)
Mehalketin = feeding by hand (permitted for chickens who cannot find their own food). Malkitin = throwing food before them. Doves in a dove-cote can find their own food — even throwing is unnecessary and thus forbidden.
Baraita (confirms)
Hamra'a: forcibly laying the animal down, forcing open its mouth, and feeding vetch and water under extreme pressure. Halata: gently placing food into the animal's mouth without force.
Key Distinction The prohibition is about compulsion and excess — Hamra'a involves harmful, forcible overfeeding. Halata is gentle assistance that the animal requires. The test is whether the animal's own capacity is bypassed by force.

🐕 Feeding Dogs, Bees & Kneading Bran on Shabbat

Rabbi Yona (at the Nasi's house): 'The righteous man takes knowledge of the cause [נפש] of his animal' (Prov. 12:10) — a person must understand and attend to his animal's needs even on Shabbat
↓
Rav Hamnuna: It is the way of the world to throw a piece of meat before a dog — even the Holy One, Blessed be He, concerns Himself with the dog's sustenance. One gives a dog enough food for the full day.
↓
Doves in dove-cotes and bees: One may NOT place water before them — they are capable of finding their own food and water. Providing it is unnecessary Shabbat labor.
↓
Doves outside a cote: One MAY place water before them — unlike cote-doves, they cannot easily find water themselves and require human assistance.
↓
Kneading bran: One may add water to bran (permitted) — but may NOT knead the mixture. Kneading is a prohibited melacha even for animal feed.
↓
Abaye quoting Rabba: The mishna follows Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda — true kneading liability requires actual kneadable material. Bran cannot truly be kneaded. Rejected: Even bran-mixing is forbidden rabbinically.
↓
Sweet flour (solet) from unripe grain: Dispute — Tanna Kamma: may not knead. Some say: may knead. Rav Ḥisda identifies whose lenient opinion this is.
