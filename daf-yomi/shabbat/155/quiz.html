QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Pappa, why is an eiruv basket hung above ten handbreadths on a tree invalid?
A. The basket is too high for the person to reach comfortably
B. Removing bread from a narrow-mouthed, tightly-tied basket causes the tree to move — forbidden use of the tree's sides
C. A basket must rest on the ground to serve as a valid eiruv location
D. Above ten handbreadths the tree is considered public domain
Correct: B — Rav Pappa explains that a narrow-mouthed basket tightly tied to the tree means that when one reaches in to retrieve the eiruv bread, the tree inevitably moves. This constitutes using the tree's sides (tzidei etz), which is rabbinically prohibited.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rav Huna, which bundle type is forbidden to move on Shabbat at all?
A. Peki'in — bundles tied with two knots
B. Kifin — bundles tied with three knots
C. Zirin — bundles of cedar branches
D. All three types are equally forbidden
Correct: C — Rav Huna holds that zirin are bundles of cedar branches, which are muktzeh and entirely forbidden to move on Shabbat. Peki'in (2 knots) may be untied and kifin (3 knots) may be spread before animals.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Which food preparation for animals is explicitly permitted on Shabbat according to the Gemara?
A. Crushing hard carobs for a large animal
B. Crushing hard hay for a small animal
C. Chopping soft pumpkins before an animal
D. Grinding dried grain into flour for feeding
Correct: C — Chopping soft pumpkins is permitted because the food is already soft and the action involves minimal effort — no significant prohibited labor is performed. Hard foods like carobs and hay require crushing to become edible, which constitutes a forbidden melacha.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does the Baraita define hamra'a in the context of feeding a camel?
A. Gently placing food into the camel's mouth with one's hand
B. Throwing food before the camel from a distance
C. Forcibly laying the animal down, forcing open its mouth, and feeding it under extreme pressure
D. Mixing water into the camel's dry feed before placing it before the animal
Correct: C — The Baraita confirms Rav Yehuda's position: hamra'a involves forcibly laying the camel on the ground, prying open its mouth, and stuffing in vetch and water under duress until overfeeding occurs. This is the prohibited form of Shabbat feeding. Halata — gentle placement of food — is permitted.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does the mishna prohibit placing water before doves in a dove-cote on Shabbat?
A. Water is muktzeh and may not be handled on Shabbat
B. Doves in a dove-cote are capable of finding their own food and water, making human provision unnecessary labor
C. Dove-cotes are privately owned and one may not carry into them
D. Providing water would cause the doves to fly away from the cote
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that doves kept in a dove-cote (and bees) are capable of foraging for their own sustenance. Therefore, providing them water on Shabbat constitutes unnecessary exertion — a rabbinically forbidden act. By contrast, doves not in a cote may receive water since they cannot easily find it on their own.
