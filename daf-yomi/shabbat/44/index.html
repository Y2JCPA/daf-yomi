👶 The One-Day-Old as Full Legal Person

בן יומו — 'a child of his day' — full halakhic status from the very first day of life
Core Theme The Gemara systematically derives from individual Torah verses that a one-day-old baby (בן יומו) has complete halakhic personhood: subject to all ritual impurities (niddah, ziva, tzara'at, corpse-impurity), affecting inheritance, yibum, and teruma, and protected under murder law. The daf concludes with the mishna on a three-year-old girl's legal capacity for betrothal through intercourse.

👶 Legal Capacities of a One-Day-Old Baby

Ritual Impurity (Tumah)

  • Baby girl becomes a niddah — derived from 'if a woman has an issue'
  • Baby girl becomes a zava after 3 consecutive days of discharge
  • Baby boy becomes a zav — from 'whether male or female' (Lev. 15:33)
  • Any baby receives impurity of leprous marks (tzara'at)
  • Any baby can receive corpse-impurity (tum'at met)

Civil & Personal Status Law

  • Creates a yibum (levirate) bond for his childless brother's widow
  • Exempts his widowed mother from yibum obligation
  • Enables/disqualifies mother's right to eat teruma
  • Inherits mother's estate and bequeaths to paternal brothers
  • Reduces the firstborn's double inheritance portion
  • One who kills him is liable for murder (Lev. 24:17)

📜 Sura vs. Pumbedita: Rava on the Firstborn's Double Portion

Sura (Mar b. Rav Yosef / Rava)
A one-day-old baby reduces the firstborn's double portion: since the firstborn receives double of each heir's share, adding one more heir shrinks every individual share — and thus shrinks the firstborn's bonus too.
Pumbedita (same tradition)
A firstborn born after his father's death does not receive a double portion — the verse says 'the firstborn of his sons' (Deut. 21:16), requiring the father to have a living son at the time of death.
Gemara's Ruling
The halakha follows all versions of Mar b. Rav Yosef in the name of Rava: both rulings apply — the one-day-old baby reduces the firstborn's share, and a posthumous firstborn has no double portion.
Two Academies, One Conclusion Sura and Pumbedita preserved different emphases of the same tradition — the Gemara validates both as practical halakha

🌾 Daughter of a Priest: When May She Eat Teruma?

Bat Kohen who was married to a Yisrael and is now widowed or divorced — normally returns to father's house and eats teruma (Lev. 22:13)
↓
Has a child from Yisrael
May NOT eat teruma — derived from 'and has no child' (Lev. 22:13): child from non-priestly man disqualifies her
Is pregnant by Yisrael
May NOT eat teruma — derived from 'as in her youth' (Lev. 22:13): pregnancy has changed her status from her youth
↓
Why does the mishna specify a one-day-old baby rather than simply 'a child'?
↓
Rav Sheshet: To teach that the baby also disqualifies the father's Canaanite slaves from eating teruma — since the child holds a share in the father's inheritance, the slaves are no longer exclusively the Kohen's

⚖️ Murder Liability and Mourning for a One-Day-Old

Source for murder liability: 'And he who smites any man (כל נפש אדם) mortally shall be put to death' (Lev. 24:17) — the phrase 'any man' (kol nefesh adam) includes even a one-day-old infant
↓
The principle extends: just as Shabbat is violated to save a fetus born after full gestation, it is violated to save a one-day-old — their life has equal sanctity
↓
Mourning obligation: A one-day-old who dies — one's relationship to him is like that of a full-fledged groom: relatives observe full shiva mourning rites
↓
Whose opinion? This is not in accordance with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who held that a child must survive thirty days to be considered fully alive — our mishna treats even one day as sufficient
↓
The Torah's equating of infant and adult life reflects a foundational principle: בן יומו — the child of his very first day — is a full human being in the eyes of halacha
