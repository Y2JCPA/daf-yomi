⚖️ Who May Attribute a Stain — and How Large?

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel
She may only attribute a stain if she actually killed a louse — but no size limit. Problem: every sheet has lice, so no woman would ever be impure!
The Rabbis
She may attribute a stain even without killing a louse. Problem: no sheet lacks lice entirely — so no woman would ever be impure either!
Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus
She may attribute a stain to a louse even without killing it — but only up to the area of a split bean (כַּגְּרִיס). This is the balanced ruling.
Endorsed Conclusion Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel declares Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus's ruling more correct than both his own and the Rabbis' — a fixed size limit resolves both extremes

⚖️ Three Positions on Louse Attribution (כִּנָּה)

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel

  • Requires she actually killed a louse
  • No upper limit on stain size
  • ⚠️ Problem: no woman stays pure — every sheet has lice
  • Self-admittedly leads to impossible stringency

The Rabbis

  • No need to have killed a louse
  • Any stain may be attributed freely
  • ⚠️ Problem: no woman is ever impure — no sheet is louse-free
  • Self-admittedly leads to impossible leniency

Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus

  • No need to have killed the louse
  • Stain must be ≤ area of a gris (split bean)
  • ✅ Balanced: limits both impurity and purity extremes
  • Endorsed by Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel as most correct

🫘 What Does 'Up to a Split Bean' Mean — Inclusive or Exclusive?

Rav Huna
'Up to (עַד) a split bean' means less than a split bean — the exact size of a split bean is NOT included in the leniency. A stain precisely that size renders her impure.
Rav Ḥisda
'Up to a split bean' includes the split bean's exact area. A stain precisely that size may still be attributed — she remains pure. The term is inclusive here.
Rav Ḥisda's Principle When 'up to' leads to stringency, it is inclusive of the measure. When it leads to leniency (as here — permitting attribution), it is exclusive. A stain exactly the size of a split bean → impure.

🔪 Attributing a Stain to an External Source

Discovery: Woman finds a blood stain on her body or garment
↓
Identify a source: Was she near slaughtered animals, a butcher's marketplace, a wounded area on her body, or a town with pigs?
↓
Plausibility check: The source must be realistic — she must have been in contact with it (passed through marketplace, had a wound, killed a louse)
↓
Size check (louse): If attributing to a louse, stain must not exceed the area of a split bean (כַּגְּרִיס) per Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus
↓
Rabbi Akiva's principle: 'The Sages stated the laws of stains not to impose stringency but to purify Israel' — attribute generously when plausible
