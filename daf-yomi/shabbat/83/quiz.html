Shabbat 83 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Akiva, why was idolatry juxtaposed to a menstruating woman rather than an animal carcass?
A. Because a carcass and idolatry have identical impurity laws in all respects
B. To teach that idolatry transmits impurity through carrying, which carcass alone would not establish
C. To teach that idolatry only transmits impurity through direct contact
D. To establish that idolatry requires a minimum size equal to a full carcass
Correct: B — Rabbi Akiva argues that a carcass could have taught carrying-impurity on its own. The Torah's choice of niddah (menstruating woman) instead signals an additional lesson — specifically carrying-impurity in a context where the niddah comparison uniquely applies.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the key distinction between gentiles and idolatry in Rav Ashi's reading of the baraita?
A. Gentiles transmit impurity only through movement; idolatry through contact alone
B. Idolatry transmits through carrying; gentiles do not
C. Gentiles transmit via themselves, movement, AND heavy stone; idolatry does NOT transmit via its heavy stone
D. There is no difference — both transmit identically as full zav equivalents
Correct: C — Rav Ashi explains that gentiles (like a zav) transmit impurity via themselves, their movement, AND their heavy stone. Idolatry transmits via itself and its movement, but critically NOT through its heavy stone — a meaningful halakhic difference.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the ruling regarding idolatry that is less than an olive-bulk in size?
A. It still transmits impurity through carrying but not through contact
B. It transmits impurity only through direct physical contact
C. It has no ritual impurity whatsoever
D. It depends on whether the object was actively worshipped
Correct: C — A baraita cited by Rav Avya (some attribute it to Rabba bar Ulla) rules explicitly: idolatry smaller than an olive-bulk carries NO impurity at all. This is derived from the verse 'he brought out the Asherah,' which implies the whole object is required.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to the view that a ship's purity derives from the verse 'a ship in the midst of the sea,' what is the ruling for an earthenware ship?
A. Pure, because all ships share the sea's immunity regardless of material
B. Pure, because earthenware is never susceptible to impurity
C. Impure, because earthenware vessels always receive impurity and this verse doesn't override that
D. Impure, because earthenware ships are too small to qualify as sea-vessels
Correct: C — This is one of the key practical differences between the two reasons for a ship's purity. If purity derives from the verse (ship-status), then an earthenware ship is still subject to earthenware vessel rules and becomes impure. But if derived from 'like the sea,' it would be pure.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
From which verse does Rabbi Yonatan derive that one must never stop studying Torah, even at the moment of death?
A. "You shall meditate on it day and night" (Joshua 1:8)
B. "This is the Torah: a person who dies in a tent" (Numbers 19:14)
C. "And you shall teach them to your children" (Deuteronomy 11:19)
D. "The Torah of your mouth is better to me than thousands of gold and silver" (Psalms 119:72)
Correct: B — Rabbi Yonatan reads Numbers 19:14 — originally about corpse-impurity in a tent — as a mandate for lifelong Torah study. The 'tent' represents the beit midrash, and the verse implies one should remain in that tent of Torah learning until the moment of death itself.
