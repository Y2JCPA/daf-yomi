QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the mishna, if one throws an object between domains and it is caught by another person before landing, what is the ruling?
A. Liable, because the throw was already completed when it left his hand
B. Exempt, because the object did not come to rest on the ground
C. Liable, because catching still counts as the object reaching its destination
D. Exempt only if the catcher was unaware of the throw
Correct: B — The mishna teaches that if the object is caught, burned, or rests in a dog's mouth — i.e., does not land normally on the ground — the thrower is exempt. The act was not completed in its intended manner.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Rava's position on one who becomes aware mid-throw is that he is liable. What is Rava's reasoning?
A. Awareness always breaks the chain of unwitting action regardless of circumstance
B. Since the object will inevitably land once thrown, mid-flight awareness is irrelevant to liability
C. He follows Rabban Gamliel who holds there is no awareness for half a measure
D. The thrower should have caught the object himself to stop it
Correct: B — Rava argues that since the moment the object left his hand its landing was inevitable, the entire act is treated as completed unwittingly. Awareness mid-flight cannot retroactively change what will happen.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does the mishna rule that one who builds on Shabbat is liable even for the smallest amount? What example does Rabbi Yirmeya give?
A. Because building in the Tabernacle was always done on a grand scale
B. Because a poor person digging even a tiny hole in his floor to hide coins performs a complete, useful act
C. Because the prohibition of building is rabbinic and has no minimum measure
D. Because fixing even a single stone constitutes repair of a public space
Correct: B — Rabbi Yirmeya explains that even a tiny hole dug by a poor person to hide coins is a complete, useful act of construction — proving that in building, any amount constitutes a meaningful act warranting liability.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Rav and Shmuel disagree about chiseling. What is Shmuel's position on which prohibited labor category chiseling falls under?
A. Building (בונה), because shaping stone prepares it for a structure
B. Plowing (חורש), because chiseling breaks apart a surface
C. Striking a Blow with a Hammer (מכה בפטיש), because it is a craft-completion act
D. Demolishing (סותר), because material is removed in the process
Correct: C — Shmuel classifies chiseling as a sub-category of 'Striking a Blow with a Hammer' — the prohibited labor representing the final finishing act that completes a crafted object — rather than Building.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What does Shmuel rule about one who takes a building stone and fixes it in place on the ground on Shabbat?
A. Exempt, because he did not attach it with mortar or cement
B. Liable for the prohibited labor of Building
C. Liable for Striking a Blow with a Hammer, not Building
D. Exempt, because placing a single stone does not constitute building
Correct: B — Shmuel rules that placing and fixing a stone in position on the ground constitutes the prohibited labor of Building (בונה), even without the use of mortar — the act of positioning it in place is itself an act of construction.
