📖 Overview

שַׁבָּת קמ"א
🗝️ Daf Overview Daf 141 traverses a series of Shabbat rulings around muktzeh, personal hygiene decrees, and the laws of carrying. The sugya moves from straw on beds and buried radishes, through bathing etiquette, a striking four-way dispute about wiping mud off one's foot, detailed shoe-related prohibitions (new, torn, old, on lasts), and culminates in Rava's analysis of carrying a living baby with a purse — invoking Rabbi Natan's principle that 'a living being carries itself' and Rabbi Shimon's rule that no prohibited labor exists without a minimum measure.

👣 The Mud-on-Foot Dispute — Four Positions

Abaye
Wipe on the ground ✅
NOT on a wall ❌
Wall-wiping looks like building (plastering)
Rava
Wipe on the wall ✅
NOT on the ground ❌
Ground-wiping may level holes — forbidden smoothing/plowing
Mar b. Ravina
❌ Both are prohibited
Solution: wipe on a beam lying on the ground
Rav Pappa
✅ Both are permitted
Neither concern rises to the level of prohibition
📌 Four Amora'im, Four Rulings This rare four-way split reflects genuine uncertainty about which rabbinic decree applies — 'appearance of building' vs. 'leveling holes.' No single ruling was universally adopted.

💧 Bathing on Shabbat — Rav Yehuda's Decree

Person bathes in a river on Shabbat
↓
Ready to exit the water
↓
❌ Ascends without drying
Carries water droplets 4 cubits through a karmelit
→ Rabbinic prohibition of carrying
✅ Dries off immediately, then ascends
No water carried on body
→ Permitted
↓
❓ Objection: What about descending into the water? His force propels water 4 cubits!
↓
Answer: When descending, the water moves by its own force (the river current carries it away) — not considered the person's carrying act. Permitted.

👟 Shoes on Shabbat — Permitted vs. Prohibited

❌ Prohibited

  • Oversized shoe on a small person — may fall off → he carries it
  • Woman in a torn shoe — mocked, removes it → carries it
  • New shoe not yet broken in — won't fit → removed and carried
  • Scraping mud from an old shoe — removes a worn layer = smoothing (מְמַחֵק)
  • Smearing oil on foot inside the shoe or sandal
  • Removing shoe from a tight-fitting last (according to Abaye)

✅ Permitted

  • Oversized cloak — won't fall off, no carrying concern
  • Scraping mud from a new shoe — no worn layer to remove
  • Using the back of a knife to scrape (unusual manner, shinui)
  • New shoe already broken in before Shabbat
  • Removing shoe from a loose last — shoe removed without moving muktzeh last
  • Smearing oil on foot outside the shoe (to polish, not tan)

👶 Rava: Carrying a Baby with a Purse on Shabbat

Case 1 — Living baby with a purse hanging around its neck carried to public domain
↓
Liable for the baby? ➜ No. Rava follows Rabbi Natan: חַי נוֹשֵׂא אֶת עַצְמוֹ — a living being carries itself. The carrier bears no liability for it.
↓
Liable for the purse? ➜ Yes. The purse belongs to and serves the adult owner — it is NOT nullified by the baby. It retains independent object status.
↓
Why isn't the purse nullified like a bed carrying a person? The bed serves the living person being carried (secondary to him). The purse does not serve the baby — it serves its adult owner. Different nullification logic.
↓
Case 2 — Dead baby with purse around its neck carried out
↓
Liable for the baby? ➜ No. Rava follows Rabbi Shimon: no liability without a minimum measure — and there is no defined minimum size for a corpse.
↓
Liable for the purse? ➜ No. When the baby itself generates no liability (exempt), the purse is nullified relative to it. Exempt for both.
