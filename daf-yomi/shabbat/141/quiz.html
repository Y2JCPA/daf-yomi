QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava, how should one wipe mud from one's foot on Shabbat?
A. On the ground only — wiping on wall looks like building
B. On a wall only — wiping on ground may level holes
C. On either wall or ground — both are permitted (Rav Pappa)
D. On a beam on the ground — safest option (Mar b. Ravina)
Correct: B — Rava holds one may wipe on a wall but NOT on the ground, due to concern about leveling holes in the ground, which resembles the prohibited labor of plowing or smoothing. Note that Abaye holds the opposite, and Mar b. Ravina prohibits both.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Rav Yehuda rule that a bather must dry off before ascending from the water on Shabbat?
A. To avoid wetting the ground and forming mud
B. To prevent cooling down and becoming ill on Shabbat
C. To avoid inadvertently carrying water droplets four cubits in a karmelit
D. Because wet clothing constitutes carrying a prohibited load
Correct: C — The concern is that one might carry water droplets on one's body for four cubits through a karmelit (a semi-public domain like a riverbank), violating the rabbinic prohibition of carrying in such a domain.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why is one who carries a living baby with a purse liable for the purse but not the baby?
A. The baby weighs less than the minimum shiur for carrying liability
B. Carrying a person is considered a mitzva and therefore exempt
C. A living being carries itself (Rabbi Natan), so only the purse counts as a carried object
D. The baby is considered an extension of the carrier's own body
Correct: C — Rava follows Rabbi Natan's principle: חַי נוֹשֵׂא אֶת עַצְמוֹ — a living being carries itself. Therefore the carrier is not liable for the baby, but is fully liable for the purse as an independently carried object.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why is a bed that carries a living person nullified (making the carrier exempt), but a purse around a baby's neck is NOT nullified?
A. The bed serves the person being carried and is secondary to him; the purse serves its adult owner, not the baby
B. A bed is too large to count as a discrete 'carried object'; a purse is small and discrete
C. The bed is not muktzeh; the purse is muktzeh and therefore has its own prohibited status
D. A living person can consent to being carried on a bed; a baby cannot consent to wearing a purse
Correct: A — The bed is entirely in service of and secondary to the living person being carried — it is nullified by him. The purse does not serve the baby; it belongs to and serves its adult owner. It therefore retains its independent object status and is not nullified.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why may one scrape mud off a new shoe on Shabbat, but not off an old shoe?
A. New shoes are designated for a mitzva and receive special leniency
B. Scraping an old shoe removes a layer of worn leather, constituting the prohibited labor of smoothing (מְמַחֵק)
C. Old shoes are muktzeh because they are no longer usable; new shoes are not muktzeh
D. New shoes are more expensive and scraping them would be a financial loss, which overrides the concern
Correct: B — Rabbi Yannai ruled that scraping an old shoe causes a worn layer of leather to peel off, which constitutes the prohibited Torah labor of smoothing/finishing (מְמַחֵק). A new shoe has no such worn surface layer, so there is no smoothing concern.
