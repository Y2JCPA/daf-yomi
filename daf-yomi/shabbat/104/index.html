✨ The Miraculous Letters of the Tablets

מ"ם וסמ"ך שבלוחות — בנס היו עומדין
Rav Ḥisda The letters mem (מ) and samekh (ס) in the stone tablets were chiseled all the way through. The enclosed interior of the samekh (a closed circle) and the final mem had no physical connection to the surrounding stone — yet they stood miraculously in place.

Furthermore, the writing was readable from both faces of the tablets — from the inside and from the outside — performing a second miracle in the very act of divine inscription.

📝 Writing on Shabbat: Liable vs. Exempt

LIABLE ✓

  • Deyo (deyota) — permanent ink from soot
  • Sam (samma) — yellow-tinged arsenic pigment
  • Sikra (sikreta) — red lead-based paint
  • Komos (koma) — gum/resin ink
  • Kankantom — copper sulfate
  • Any substance that makes a permanent mark
  • One letter that completes a book (baraita)
  • One thread that completes a garment (baraita)

EXEMPT ✗

  • Fruit juice or liquids (not permanent)
  • Road dust or scribes' drying powder
  • Writing with the back of the hand
  • Writing with the foot, mouth, or elbow
  • Scratching letters on flesh — Sages
  • Writing over existing letters
  • A single standalone letter (no larger context)
  • Any substance where writing does not endure

⚖️ Scratching Letters on Flesh — Rabbi Eliezer vs. the Sages

Rabbi Eliezer
One who scratches letters into their flesh on Shabbat is liable for a sin-offering. Proof: Did not Elijah the prophet engrave letters on his arm at Carmel? Writing on flesh is a valid act of writing!
The Sages
He is exempt. Elijah's act on Carmel was a sha'at hadechak — an emergency situation — and no halakhic ruling can be derived from emergency conduct. The mishna follows Rabbi Yehoshua ben Beteira.
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
The mishna's exemption aligns with Rabbi Yehoshua ben Beteira: scratching on flesh creates no permanent mark on a durable surface — two conditions required for the melakha of writing.
Core Principle The melakha of writing requires a permanent mark on a durable surface. Flesh fades and is not a standard writing medium — hence the Sages' exemption.

🌍 Edge Cases in Writing Liability

Base Rule (Mishna): Two letters written in one lapse of awareness = liable for melakhat kotev
↓
Completing a Work (Baraita): One letter that completes a book, or one thread that completes a garment = liable — the meaningful completion triggers liability
↓
Rabbi Ami: One letter written in Tiberias + one letter written in Tzippori = liable — a full act of writing lacking only proximity; bringing the papers together completes the word
↓
Emending a Letter (Rav Sheshet): Correcting a single malformed letter = liable — the corrected letter is now a complete, valid letter, equivalent to writing it anew
↓
Writing Over Writing: Exempt — no new surface is being marked; the marks already exist beneath the pen
