Shabbat 104 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why did Rav Ḥisda say the letters mem and samekh in the stone tablets were miraculous?
A. They glowed with divine fire visible from a distance
B. They were carved in both Hebrew and a celestial script
C. Their enclosed interiors had no physical support yet remained suspended in place
D. They could only be read by Moses, not by ordinary Israelites
Correct: C — The final mem (ם) and samekh (ס) are closed letters. When chiseled through stone, their interior segments would float free with no support — yet they stood miraculously in place.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In the children's alphabet midrash, what do the spellings of שֶׁקֶר (falsehood) and אֱמֶת (truth) teach about their respective qualities?
A. Falsehood predates truth in creation, as the alphabet proves
B. Falsehood is easily found (letters adjacent); truth is hard to find (letters spread across the alphabet)
C. Truth has more letters because it requires more effort to speak
D. Falsehood and truth are equally accessible — only intention distinguishes them
Correct: B — Shin, kuf, reish are consecutive in the alphabet — falsehood is readily at hand. Alef, mem, tav are spread from beginning to middle to end — truth requires far greater effort to attain.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rabbi Ami, if one writes one letter in Tiberias and a second letter in Tzippori on Shabbat, what is the ruling?
A. Exempt — writing was not completed within a single domain
B. Exempt — two separate acts in two cities cannot combine into one melakha
C. Liable — bringing the papers together completes a full act of writing
D. Liable only if both letters were written with permanent ink
Correct: C — Rabbi Ami rules liable because he performed a complete act of writing lacking only proximity. When the two pieces of paper are brought together, a full word exists — the spatial separation does not negate the writing.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Which writing material in the mishna is identified in the Gemara as yellow-tinged arsenic?
A. Deyo (deyota) — permanent soot-based ink
B. Komos (koma) — a gum or resin compound
C. Sam (samma) — a yellow arsenic pigment
D. Kankantom — copper sulfate solution
Correct: C — The Gemara explains that sam is samma, a yellow-tinged arsenic pigment used as a writing substance, making its use on Shabbat a liable act of writing.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rav Sheshet rules that emending a single malformed letter on Shabbat makes one liable. What is the underlying reasoning?
A. Emending is always a more skilled act than writing and therefore more severe
B. The emended letter becomes a properly formed, complete letter — equivalent to writing the whole letter anew
C. Any contact with a writing instrument on Shabbat constitutes the melakha
D. Rabbinic decree without a stated rational basis
Correct: B — When one corrects a malformed letter, the result is a fully valid, complete letter. The Gemara treats this as equivalent to writing the entire letter from scratch — hence liability despite only one letter being involved.
