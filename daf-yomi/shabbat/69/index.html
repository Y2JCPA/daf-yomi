🤔 What Makes a Sin 'Unwitting'?

Munbaz
Even one who knew the action was forbidden but was unaware the punishment is karet qualifies as an unwitting sinner and brings a chatat.
Rabbis
One must be completely unaware the act is prohibited at all. Awareness of the prohibition — even without knowing the penalty — disqualifies a chatat.
R. Yoḥanan
For Shabbat: unwitting means unaware that the punishment is karet, even if aware the act is prohibited — this sides with Munbaz.
Reish Lakish
For Shabbat: unwitting means unaware that the act is prohibited at all — this sides with the Rabbis.
The Stakes This dispute determines who must bring a sin-offering (chatat) for violating Shabbat or other Torah prohibitions, and what level of ignorance is required.

🗓️ The Desert Traveler: Which Day Is Shabbat?

Rav Huna
Count six days from the moment you realize you've lost track, then observe the seventh as Shabbat — recreating the creation week from your current position.
Ḥiyya bar Rav
Observe the very first day as Shabbat, then count six working days — because zachor (remember) precedes shamor (observe).
Rava
Each day he prepares only enough food to sustain himself. On his designated Shabbat he eats from the day before — if he has nothing, others may feed him, for pikuach nefesh overrides Shabbat.
Rava (add.)
If he remembers how many days passed since he departed (but not the weekday), he may perform labor throughout his departure day each week — even if it falls on actual Shabbat — since he has partial knowledge.
Core Principle Sacred obligation to observe Shabbat persists even in total uncertainty. The traveler must reconstruct holiness from whatever information he has.

⚖️ Unwitting Violations Across Three Domains

Shabbat

  • Forgot Shabbat exists → one offering total
  • Forgot which labors forbidden → one per labor
  • Per R. Yoḥanan: unaware of karet = unwitting
  • Per Reish Lakish: unaware of prohibition = unwitting
  • 39 melachot enumerated to clarify liability boundaries

Oath on a Statement

  • Novel halakha: only Torah prohibition with chatat for unwitting violation
  • Per Abaye: must be unaware of the prohibition to be 'unwitting'
  • One who knew it was an oath but forgot the prohibition → liable
  • All authorities agree on the awareness standard here
  • High bar because the oath obligation itself is unusual

Teruma

  • Ate teruma unwittingly → repay principal + one-fifth
  • Per Abaye: only liable if unaware it was teruma
  • Follows the Rabbis' standard: must be unaware of prohibition
  • Awareness of teruma-status = willful, not unwitting
  • Consistent principle across Abaye's rulings

🏜️ The Desert Traveler's Ruling — Full Summary

מִי שֶׁהָיָה מְהַלֵּךְ בַּדֶּרֶךְ אוֹ בַּמִּדְבָּר וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ אֵימָתַי שַׁבָּת — מוֹנֶה שִׁשָּׁה וּמְשַׁמֵּר אֶחָד
Practical Halakha A traveler who completely loses track of Shabbat in the wilderness must nonetheless observe one day in seven:

1. Each day he prepares only enough food for that day
2. The day before his designated Shabbat he prepares extra food for that rest day
3. If he has no food on his Shabbat, others may provide — pikuach nefesh overrides
4. If he recalls how many days since departure, his departure day is exempt from Shabbat restrictions each week

The Gemara derives from two verses that the distinction between forgetting Shabbat exists vs. forgetting specific labors is biblical in origin — shaping all liability calculations.
