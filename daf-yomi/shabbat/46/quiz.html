QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava's final ruling, if a boy develops two pubic hairs during his 13th year (the 'time'), what is his legal status?
A. He is a full adult (gadol) by Torah law
B. He is still considered a minor — the hairs are treated as if before the time
C. He is an adult only for vow purposes but a minor for all other halakhot
D. The matter is left unresolved as a tannaitic dispute
Correct: B — Rava rules explicitly that two hairs developing during the transition year are treated as having appeared before the time — they do not render the child an adult. This is the accepted halakha.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
A 12-year-old girl is examined and no pubic hairs are found. Why can she NOT simply perform mi'un (refusal)?
A. A 12-year-old is always considered an adult and can never perform mi'un
B. Mi'un requires the presence of her father, who in this case is deceased
C. We are concerned that hairs may have grown and then fallen out — she may actually be an adult
D. Mi'un is only valid before age 10 according to all opinions
Correct: C — Even when examined and no hairs found, we are concerned that the hairs may have grown and fallen out, leaving her legal status uncertain. Since she might be an adult, mi'un is unavailable.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the basis for Rav Huna's view that vows of a discriminating minor 'during the time' are valid by Torah law?
A. Numbers 6:2 — 'when a man or woman shall clearly utter a vow' equates such a minor to an adult for vows
B. A rabbinic enactment (takkanah) designed to protect minors from accidental violations
C. Numbers 30:2 — 'this is the matter' which extends vow law to all who can articulate intent
D. The principle that any act requiring kavanah (intent) is valid when a minor demonstrates that intent
Correct: A — Rav Huna derives from Numbers 6:2 — 'when a man or woman shall clearly utter a vow' — that if a minor on the brink of adulthood articulates that the vow is in God's name, his vow is Torah-valid.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish, if a minor consecrated a food item and others ate it, what is the ruling?
A. They are not flogged but must bring a guilt-offering (asham)
B. They are flogged with Torah-prescribed lashes
C. They are not flogged and need not bring any sacrifice
D. The consecration is invalid, so there is no prohibition at all
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish hold that a discriminating minor on the brink of adulthood is treated as a full adult — his consecration is Torah-valid, and those who eat it are liable to flogging.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Rabba bar Livai resolve the concern that a husband's nullification of his minor wife's vow will be invalid once she grows up?
A. The nullification must be explicitly repeated on the day she becomes an adult
B. The husband nullifies her vow each and every moment, so at the moment of majority the nullification is Torah-valid
C. The vow automatically lapses at majority without requiring any nullification
D. A rabbinic court ratifies the original nullification retroactively upon her reaching adulthood
Correct: B — Rabba bar Livai explains that the husband nullifies continuously — moment by moment. When she reaches majority and the marriage becomes fully Torah-valid, the ongoing nullification takes effect as a valid Torah-level act at precisely that moment.
