⚖️ Do Signs During the Transition Year Confer Adulthood?

Rav Hamnuna
If two hairs develop during the transition year, they are a valid sign of puberty — equivalent to developing after the time, and render the child an adult.
Rabbi Zeira
Objection from a baraita: the verse applies 'man' only to one already fully adult. Signs during the time cannot confer adult status — the word 'man' would be superfluous otherwise.
Rav Naḥman
This is a tannaitic dispute — one baraita treats 'during the time' as after the time; another treats it as before. Both positions have tannaitic support.
Rava
Final halakha: Two hairs that develop during the transition year are treated as if they developed before the time — they do not render the child an adult.
The Time (זְמַן) For a girl: the 12th year (age 11+1 day through 12). For a boy: the 13th year. Signs appearing in this window are legally ambiguous — Rava rules they confer no adult status.

🔍 Determining the Legal Status of a 12-Year-Old Girl

Girl reaches age 12 years and 1 day
↓
Was she examined for pubic hairs?
Not examined
Presumed adult (hairs assumed present) → cannot perform mi'un (refusal)
Examined — hairs found
Definite adult → ḥalitza valid; mi'un impossible
Examined — no hairs found
Concern hairs may have fallen out → status uncertain
↓
Rava's resolution: If unexamined — presume adult (no mi'un). If no hairs found — cannot do ḥalitza (might be minor) but also cannot do mi'un (might be adult). She is stuck between two statuses.

💬 Do We Suspect That Hairs Fell Out?

One Opinion
We are concerned that pubic hairs may have grown and then fallen out. A girl examined at 12 with no hairs found may still be an adult — we cannot rule out prior growth.
Other Opinion
We are not concerned that hairs fell out. If she was examined and none were found, she is a definite minor and can perform mi'un freely.
Rav Dimi of Neharde'a — Final Ruling We ARE concerned that hairs fell out — but only when her husband betrothed her before she was 12 and consummated the marriage after she turned 12. In that scenario, her uncertain adult status affects ḥalitza and mi'un alike.

📜 Minor's Vows: Rav Huna vs. Rava

Rav Huna — Vows Valid by Torah Law

  • A discriminating minor during the time who knows in Whose name he vows — vows are fully valid
  • Derived from Numbers 6:2: 'when a man or woman shall clearly utter a vow' — equates the brink-of-adulthood minor to an adult
  • Applies to both Nazirite vows and vows of prohibition
  • Supported by Rav Huna bar Yehuda's baraita

Rava — Vows Valid by Rabbinic Law Only

  • Numbers 30:2–3 states 'this is the matter' — only cancellation of vows applies fully to minors; vow-making itself is only rabbinically valid
  • A baraita confirms: the minor is equated to an adult for the prohibition against profaning his word, but is not flogged (lav she-ein bo ma'aseh)
  • Court must prevent the minor from violating vows, but no lashes
  • Rava's ruling is the accepted halakha

💍 Nullifying the Vows of a Minor Wife — Step by Step

Situation: A minor orphan girl whose brothers accepted betrothal on her behalf vowed while married. Her marriage is valid only rabbinically (she is a minor).
↓
Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel: The husband may nullify her vows — if the marriage is rabbinic, so are her vows; he nullifies both on the same level. If it turns out to be Torah-valid, he nullifies as a full husband.
↓
Problem 1: When she grows up, she may eat what she vowed forbidden, relying on the earlier nullification — but that nullification may not have been Torah-valid.
↓
Rabba bar Livai's answer: The husband nullifies her vow each and every moment, continuously — so when she reaches majority, the nullification at that instant is Torah-valid.
↓
Problem 2: A husband cannot nullify pre-marriage vows. Since she is his Torah-wife only upon majority, the vow she made as a minor predates the valid marriage.
↓
Resolution: The continuous nullification means he nullifies on the very day she becomes a full adult — within the halakhic window of that day, nullification is valid.
