⚖️ How Sages Determine Shabbat Measures: Common vs. Uncommon Use

🍷 Wine — Common Use Governs

  • Common use: drinking (larger amount needed)
  • Uncommon use: healing (smaller amount needed)
  • Measure set by: drinking (common)
  • Result: larger measure = higher threshold

💧 Water — Stringency Overrides

  • Common use: drinking (larger amount)
  • Uncommon use: rubbing on eye bandage (smaller)
  • Measure set by: healing (uncommon!)
  • Why? Healing measure is smaller = more stringent

📐 Abaye's Rule

  • When two uses differ in required amount:
  • Follow the more common use as baseline
  • But apply stringency: choose the smaller measure
  • Water's healing measure is smaller → used for liability

💧 Abaye's Principle: Why Does Water Deviate from the Rule?

Abaye
For any substance with both a common and uncommon use, the Sages follow the more common use to set the measure of Shabbat liability.
Challenger
But look at water! Its common use is drinking — yet the mishna sets the measure as 'enough to rub on an eye bandage,' which is its uncommon healing use. Your rule seems broken!
Abaye
The Sages apply stringency by always choosing the smaller of the two measures. Wine's drinking measure is smaller than its healing measure — so drinking governs. Water's healing measure is smaller than its drinking measure — so healing governs.
Principle
In both cases, the Sages choose the measure that is most stringent — the lowest threshold for liability. Common vs. uncommon is secondary; smaller is the decisive factor.
תכלית הכלל The Sages set the smallest useful measure as the Shabbat liability threshold — whether it comes from common or uncommon use. Stringency always drives the selection.

💸 Abaye Unpacks the Repaid Promissory Note Debate

The Situation: A debt was repaid but the creditor still holds the original promissory note. He carries it on Shabbat.
↓
Universal Agreement: It is prohibited to keep a repaid promissory note. It could be fraudulently used to collect the same debt a second time.
↓
The Core Question: Does the document still have enough legal value to make carrying it on Shabbat liable?
↓
First Opinion (Exempt): After repayment, the note is legally dead. The debtor can defeat any claim by saying 'I already paid.' No value → no liability.
↓
Rabbi Yehuda (Liable): The creditor might still need it — if the debtor admits he signed the note, the creditor could potentially collect again. Residual value = Shabbat liability.
↓
Abaye's Resolution: The dispute hinges on whether a debtor's admission ('I wrote it') grants the creditor power to collect. If yes → note has value → carrying is liable (Rabbi Yehuda). If no → exempt.

Waste Water and the Quarter-Log

וּמוֹדִין חֲכָמִים לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּמוֹצִיא מַיִם שֶׁאֵינָן רְאוּיִין — שִׁיעוּרָן בְּרְבִיעִית
🗑️ Special Case The Rabbis agree with Rabbi Shimon: one who carries out waste water (שׁוֹפְכִין) to the public domain is liable at a measure of a quarter of a log. The Gemara asks: what is waste water useful for? Answer: it is used to soften and wash flax fibers — a practical purpose that gives it Shabbat-relevant utility. The quarter-log is the minimum amount needed for that task, and thus becomes the liability threshold. Even 'useless' liquids can carry Shabbat significance when a practical application exists.
