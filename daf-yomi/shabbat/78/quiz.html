Shabbat 78 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Abaye, what is the decisive factor when the Sages choose between a substance's common-use measure and its uncommon-use measure for Shabbat liability?
A. They always follow the common use, regardless of measure size
B. They always follow the healing/medicinal use as a safeguard for health
C. They choose whichever measure is smaller, applying it as a stringency
D. They take the average of the two measures
Correct: C — Abaye teaches that the Sages apply stringency by choosing the smallest useful measure — whether it comes from common or uncommon use. This sets the lowest possible threshold for Shabbat liability.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the mishna set water's liability measure based on its healing use (rubbing an eye bandage) rather than its more common drinking use?
A. Drinking water on Shabbat is permitted, so it has no drinking-related liability
B. The healing measure (eye bandage) is smaller than the drinking measure, making it more stringent
C. In Talmudic times water was used primarily for healing, not drinking
D. The mishna follows Rabbi Shimon's opinion that only healing use matters
Correct: B — The eye-bandage measure is smaller than the amount needed for drinking. Since Shabbat law applies stringency by using the lowest threshold, the smaller healing measure becomes the liability marker — even though drinking is more common.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
A Tosefta teaches that the measure of a tax receipt is 'enough to write two letters.' What does this mean in context?
A. Two letters of the Hebrew alphabet written as abbreviations for tax categories
B. A two-sentence formal legal declaration
C. Two full words in the standard tax document format
D. The name of the taxpayer written in two characters
Correct: A — The Tosefta specifies that a tax receipt requires enough space to write two letters of the alphabet — a minimal meaningful notation. This is the minimum useful paper size that constitutes a real document for purposes of Shabbat liability.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yehuda, why does carrying a repaid promissory note on Shabbat still incur liability?
A. He holds that all written documents always have inherent Shabbat liability
B. The note can still be used if the debtor admits he signed it, allowing the creditor to collect again
C. The creditor is obligated by law to retain the note as proof of the original debt
D. Rabbi Yehuda rules that intent to destroy the note is required before it loses its status
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda holds that even after repayment, the creditor might need the note: if the debtor admits he signed it, the creditor could theoretically collect again based on that admission. This residual utility makes carrying it on Shabbat liable.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
For what practical purpose does the Gemara say waste water has utility, justifying a quarter-log as its Shabbat liability measure?
A. Watering small vegetable gardens near the home
B. Extinguishing embers in a hearth safely
C. Softening and washing flax fibers for textile production
D. Rinsing wine vessels before Shabbat use
Correct: C — The Gemara answers that waste water can be used to soften and wash flax — a real practical application that grants it utility and justifies applying the standard quarter-log liquid measure as the Shabbat liability threshold.
