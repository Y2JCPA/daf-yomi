⚰️ Death, Life, and Shabbat

כָּל צׇרְכֵי הַמֵּת עוֹשִׂין בְּשַׁבָּת — All the needs of the dead may be performed on Shabbat (Mishna)
Core Theme Daf 151 traces the boundaries of Shabbat observance when confronted with death. The Gemara examines waiting at the Shabbat boundary for a corpse's needs, the permitted and forbidden acts when caring for the dead, and the grave prohibition against hastening death by closing a dying person's eyes. It closes with stirring teachings: even a day-old baby outweighs a dead king in Shabbat obligations, and mitzvot must be performed now — before old age, poverty, or death remove the opportunity.

🏛️ Rav vs. Shmuel: When May a Jew Use a Gentile's Shabbat Work?

Rav
The prohibition depends on the city's majority. If most residents are Jewish, we assume the gentile made the item for a Jew — a Jew may not use it after Shabbat.
Shmuel
The prohibition depends on specific intent. Only if the gentile made it explicitly for a named Jew is it forbidden. If he changed his mind and offered it to another, it is permitted.
Mishna (supports Shmuel)
If a gentile made a coffin or dug a grave for a specific person then changed his mind, another Jew may be buried in it. This implies intent, not population, is the factor.
Baraita (supports Rav)
In a city with a gentile majority, a Jew may bathe immediately after Shabbat. With a Jewish majority, he must wait. This turns on population composition.
Resolution Each opinion is supported by a different source. The halakha follows the principle that specific intent toward a Jew is what triggers the prohibition — Shmuel's view is corroborated by the mishna's precise language.

🧴 What May (and May Not) Be Done for the Dead on Shabbat

✅ Smear oil on the body and rinse with water — both explicitly permitted
↓
❌ Do NOT move any limbs — a corpse is muktzeh (set-aside); moving limbs is a Shabbat violation
↓
✅ Tie the jaw — not to restore its position, but only to prevent it from continuing to open
↓
✅ Remove pillows from beneath the body; place it on sand or salt to slow decomposition
↓
✅ Close a skylight shutter or place metal on the body to keep it cool — indirect preservation is permitted
↓
✅ Wait at the Shabbat boundary and instruct a non-Jew to fetch a coffin and shrouds from a nearby location

👁️ Closing a Dying Person's Eyes

כָּל הַמַּעֲצִים עֵינָיו עִם יְצִיאַת הַנְּשָׁמָה — הֲרֵי זֶה שׁוֹפֵךְ דָּמִים
One who closes the eyes as the soul departs — this person is a murderer (Gemara)
⚠️ Grave Prohibition The Gemara states emphatically: One who shuts a person's eyes while the soul is departing is considered a murderer.

The analogy: A lamp is gradually going out — it could burn a little longer. If someone places a finger on the flame, it is immediately extinguished. Closing the eyes of one still dying is precisely that act.

One must wait until the person has fully died before attending to the body. Even on a weekday, one may not shut the eyes while the soul departs.

👶 The Infinite Value of Life: Baby vs. King

Living Day-Old Baby

  • ✅ Desecrate Shabbat to save his life
  • ✅ No need to protect him from weasels or mice — they flee from a living person
  • A single breath of life overrides all Shabbat law
  • Source: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel

Dead King David

  • ❌ Do NOT desecrate Shabbat — no amount of past greatness changes this
  • ❌ Even Og, king of Bashan — dead — requires protection from weasels
  • Death strips away all worldly rank and privilege
  • Source: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel

📜 Perform Mitzvot While You Still Can (Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar)

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar teaches:
Perform mitzvot while you find opportunity, while you have means, and while you are under your own control
↓
✅ While Alive
Health, wealth, and free will are available — the window for mitzvot is open
❌ At Death / Old Age
"The silver cord is snapped, the golden bowl shattered" (Ecclesiastes 12:6) — all faculties fail; the window closes
↓
Shmuel's addition: The only difference between this world and the Messianic era is subjugation to foreign kingdoms — ordinary human obligations, including mitzvot, continue unchanged in that era too
