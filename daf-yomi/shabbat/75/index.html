🩸 Kesem on a Garment — Does Location Determine Impurity?

Rava
A blood stain (kesem) found on the inner side of a garment renders the woman impure retroactively, because the location indicates it came from her body.
Abaye
Even a stain on the outer side is suspect unless she can attribute it to an external source — we cannot presume it came from elsewhere without evidence.
Rav Pappa
The size threshold applies equally: if the stain is smaller than a gris (bean), it is pure regardless of location on the garment.
Rav Huna
We must also consider the color — only the five shades enumerated by the Sages render impure. A brown or faded stain that does not match the prescribed colors is pure.
Core Principle A kesem creates retroactive impurity only when it meets three conditions: minimum size (gris), prescribed color, and cannot be attributed to an external cause.

📖 Background — What is a Kesem? A kesem (כֶּתֶם) is a blood stain discovered on a woman's garment or body. Unlike re'iyah (an actual flow of blood she perceived), a kesem is found without sensation. The Rabbis instituted that certain stains create retroactive impurity — rendering her niddah from the point the stain could have originated.

🎨 The Five Shades of Blood That Render Impure

אָדֹם — Red

  • The primary impure color
  • Includes bright red and deep red
  • No dispute among the Sages

שָׁחֹר — Black

  • Treated as red that darkened
  • Rabbi Akiva: black is most stringent
  • Impure even when dull in tone

קַרְתִּי / כְּרֹכֶּמֶת — Saffron / Crocus

  • Yellow-orange like saffron water
  • A distinctive disputed shade
  • Accepted as impure by all opinions

אֲדֻמְדַּם — Diluted Red

  • Like the color of wine mixed with water
  • Reddish hue, less saturated
  • Included in the five impure shades

תְּכֵלֶת / כְּמַיִם — Doubt Cases

  • Blue-black and water colors are pure
  • Not among the five shades
  • Scholars may be consulted on borderline cases

📅 The Seven Clean Days — Shiva Nekiyim Process

Day 0: Woman sees blood (or has a discharge that renders her a zava). She waits for the flow to cease entirely.
↓
Day 1 of Seven: She performs a bedika (self-examination with a cloth) in the morning to confirm she is clean. The cloth must come back without impure blood.
↓
Days 2–6: She examines herself each day. The examination need not be at a fixed time, but daytime is preferred for accuracy of color determination.
↓
Day 7: She performs a final examination — both morning and late afternoon (bein hashmashot). This final day examination is the most critical.
↓
Immersion (Tevilah): After completing all seven clean days without any finding, she immerses in a mikveh and is permitted to her husband.
↓
Interruption: Any blood found — whether by examination or as a kesem — restarts the entire count from day one.
