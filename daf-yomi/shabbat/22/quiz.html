Shabbat 22 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava's explanation, what is the core disagreement about blood found inside discharged tissue?
A. Whether tissue discharge is a normal occurrence for women
B. Whether the woman herself is pure even though the uterine location may be impure
C. Whether the blood must meet a minimum volume to cause impurity
D. Whether dried blood retains its impurity when rewetted
Correct: B — Rava explains that everyone agrees tissue discharge is not normal. The dispute is whether the woman herself is rendered impure (Rabbis) or whether only the uterine location is considered impure while the woman remains pure (R. Eliezer).
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Rav Huna rule that a man who extracts semen with a wood sliver is ritually pure?
A. Semen extracted artificially has a different legal category than naturally emitted semen
B. Impurity requires the penis to touch emitted semen, which a sliver prevents
C. Seminal emission impurity requires a quantity sufficient to block the tip of the penis — too large an amount to extract with a sliver
D. Wood objects cannot become vectors of impurity transmission
Correct: C — Rav Huna derives from the minimum measure required for seminal emission impurity (enough to cause blockage at the penile tip) that any smaller amount — such as what a wood sliver could extract — does not trigger impurity.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How is an ambiguous discharge resembling a shell, hair, or soil tested to determine ritual impurity?
A. The item is burned; residual ash indicates organic (blood) origin
B. The item is placed in lukewarm water — if it dissolves, it is blood and the woman is impure
C. A physician examines it to determine its physical composition
D. The item is soaked in cold water for a full twenty-four hours
Correct: B — Reish Lakish rules and a baraita confirms: the discharged item is placed in lukewarm water. Dissolution indicates blood, rendering the woman impure; failure to dissolve means she is pure.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Shmuel's explanation of Rabbi Meir, why does discharging an animal-shaped fetus render the mother impure with childbirth impurity?
A. Any uterine discharge is treated as a birth under Rabbi Meir's stringent ruling
B. The word 'formation' (yetzira) is stated for both man and animals, creating a valid verbal analogy equating their halakhic status
C. Animal forms are treated as human because they share anatomical features
D. Rabbi Meir rules stringently in all cases of doubtful fetal status
Correct: B — Shmuel explains: the Torah uses yetzira (formation) for man (Gen 2:7) and for animals (Gen 2:19). This gezera shava — free on both sides — equates animal-shaped fetuses with human births for purposes of childbirth impurity.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why can a gezera shava NOT be drawn to include sea-monster-shaped fetuses in childbirth impurity?
A. Sea creatures are explicitly excluded from purity laws by a special Torah verse
B. The word 'creation' (beria) for sea monsters is needed to teach their own creation and is therefore not superfluous — making the analogy invalid per Rabbi Yishmael
C. Rabbi Yishmael forbids any verbal analogy involving marine creatures categorically
D. Sea monsters are classified as creeping animals already covered by a separate ruling
Correct: B — Per Rabbi Yishmael, a gezera shava must be 'free' (superfluous) to be valid. The word beria applied to sea monsters teaches their actual creation and is therefore not free — unlike the yetzira analogy for animals, which is superfluous on both sides.
