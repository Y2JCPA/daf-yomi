📖 Overview

נידה כב
🩸 Niddah 22 — Edge Cases of Ritual Impurity This daf probes three major boundary questions: (1) Is blood found inside discharged tissue impure — and is the dispute about normalcy of discharge or about whether the woman herself vs. the uterine location is impure? (2) Does extracting semen artificially with a wood sliver create seminal-emission impurity? (3) Does discharging a fetus shaped like an animal trigger full childbirth impurity? The daf also introduces the lukewarm water test for ambiguous dry discharges and closes with an extended debate about the limits of verbal analogy (gezera shava) — free on both sides vs. one side — as applied to sea monsters.

🪵 Semen Extracted with a Wood Sliver — Pure or Impure?

Rabba
If a man extracts semen with a wood sliver, does he become impure like one who had a natural seminal emission?
Rav Huna
He is pure. Impurity requires semen sufficient to block the tip of the penis — far more than a sliver can extract. Derive his purity from that minimum measure.
Rabba
But if impurity comes from the penis touching emitted semen, the measure is irrelevant — even a tiny amount would suffice! And a seminal emission negates a zav's count, implying it resembles ziva…
Rav Huna
A seminal emission negates the count only because it contains traces of ziva. Torah decrees: pure ziva negates the full seven-day count; semen mixed with ziva negates only one day — these are distinct categories.
Ruling Artificial extraction with a sliver cannot yield the halakhic minimum for impurity — the man remains pure. Impurity of seminal emission is bound to the natural emission process, not mere contact.

🧵 Two Explanations of the Tissue-Blood Dispute

First Explanation

  • Core dispute: is it normal for a woman to discharge blood inside tissue?
  • First tanna (following R. Eliezer): Normal → blood is genuine niddah blood → impure
  • Rabbis: Not normal → blood did not originate as a flow → pure
  • The tissue itself is the trigger of the debate

Rava's Explanation

  • All agree: discharging blood in tissue is not a normal occurrence
  • Core dispute: can the woman be pure while the uterine location is impure?
  • R. Eliezer: woman is pure (no halakhic niddah-flow occurred to her person)
  • Rabbis: the uterus is also pure — no valid impurity at all

📜 Verbal Analogy Chain — Why Animals but Not Sea Monsters

Rabbi Meir's rule: A woman who discharges an animal-shaped fetus has full childbirth impurity — because yetzira (formation) is stated for both man (Gen 2:7) and animals (Gen 2:19), creating a valid gezera shava
↓
Challenge: Sea monsters also have beria (creation) stated (Gen 1:21) — shouldn't the same logic apply to a sea-monster-shaped fetus?
↓
Response: The analogy is drawn via yetzira–yetzira (formation), not beria–beria (creation). Sea monsters do not share the formation link.
↓
Counter: The word yetzira also appears for creeping animals — can't sea monsters enter via that route? And beria appears for man too (Gen 1:27)!
↓
Resolution (per Rabbi Yishmael): A gezera shava must be free on both sides (superfluous in both verses). Yetzira is free for both man and animals → valid. Beria for sea monsters is needed to teach their own creation → not free → cannot override received tradition.
