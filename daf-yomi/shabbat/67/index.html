🔥 R. Yoḥanan's Iron Knife Ritual for Fever

Take a knife made entirely of iron, including the handle
↓
Go to a bush; tie a string of hair to it
↓
Day 1: Carve the bush slightly; recite Exodus 3:2 — the burning bush verse
↓
Day 2: Carve more of the bush; recite Exodus 3:3
↓
Day 3: Carve more; recite Exodus 3:4 — completing the three-day cycle
↓
Lower yourself, cut close to the ground, and recite: 'The bush, the bush — not because you are higher than all trees did the Holy One rest His Presence upon you, but because you are lower than all trees'

🔮 Carrying Amulets on Shabbat: The Mishna Debate

Rabbi Meir
One may go out on Shabbat with a locust egg, a fox tooth, and a nail from the crucified — all for the purpose of healing.
The Rabbis
We prohibit these remedies even during the week, due to the ways of the Amorite.
Gemara
The locust egg is for earache. The fox tooth: from a live fox promotes sleep; from a dead fox keeps one awake. The nail from the crucified is for swelling.
Rav Aḥa b. Rava
For the fever ritual, should one not recite Exodus 3:5 — 'Remove your shoes, for the ground is holy'? That verse seems more suited to an incantation!
Core Dispute Rabbi Meir holds these items have genuine therapeutic value; the Rabbis regard them as Amorite superstition forbidden in all circumstances.

✅ Permitted Practices vs. 🚫 Ways of the Amorite

✅ Permitted — Genuinely Effective

  • Chip of mulberry wood in pot → cooks faster
  • Lump of salt in candle → burns more brightly
  • Mud or clay daubed on candle → prevents it from blowing out
  • Iron knife + bush incantation (R. Yoḥanan) → treats fever
  • Reciting fish-bone formula: 'Go down, go down'

🚫 Darkhei Emori — Forbidden Superstition

  • Saying 'Gad gaddi' for good fortune day and night
  • Switching names with one's wife for luck
  • Woman dancing over kutaḥ so it will succeed
  • Saying 'I drink and leave over' to increase wine supply
  • Telling a raven to call for a good omen
  • Woman urinating in front of her pot to cook faster

💡 Abaye & Rava's Unanimous Principle

כָּל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם רְפוּאָה — אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם דַּרְכֵי הָאֱמוֹרִי
Halakhic Principle Abaye and Rava both said: Anything that contains an element of healing and appears to be effective does not contain an element of the prohibition against following the ways of the Amorite.

Possible inference questioned: Does the absence of healing automatically make something Amorite? — No. Beating a fruit tree that sheds fruit is permitted because it causes passersby to pray for mercy on it — demonstrating that beneficial intent and purpose, not just medical efficacy, can also justify a practice.
