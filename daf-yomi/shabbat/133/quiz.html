QUESTION 1 OF 5
Rava disagrees with Abaye about the necessity of the verse 'basar' (flesh) regarding a tzara'at lesion. What is Rava's reason for requiring the verse even according to Rabbi Shimon?
A. Rabbi Shimon only permits unintentional acts on weekdays, not Shabbat
B. Excising the tzara'at lesion during milah is an inevitable consequence (psik reisha), which Rabbi Shimon still prohibits
C. The verse functions only as an asmakhta (support), not a derivation — Rava agrees it is unnecessary
D. Rabbi Shimon agrees with Rabbi Yehuda specifically in cases involving biblical prohibitions
Correct: B — Rava holds that even Rabbi Shimon, who permits unintentional acts, would prohibit an inevitable unintended consequence (psik reisha). When circumcising over a bright spot, excision of the lesion is unavoidable — thus a biblical verse is required to permit it.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does Abba Shaul uniquely derive from the word ve'anveihu in Exodus 15:2?
A. One must adorn the Torah scroll with a beautiful cover and crown
B. The circumcision should be performed with a specially crafted ceremonial knife
C. Reading it as 'Ani vaHu' (me and Him), one must emulate God's compassion and mercy
D. One glorifies God by performing mitzvot in the most aesthetically beautiful manner possible
Correct: C — Abba Shaul splits ve'anveihu into 'Ani vaHu' — me and Him. This teaches imitatio Dei: be similar to God. Just as He is compassionate and merciful, so too must you be. The other tannaim read ve'anveihu as hiddur mitzva (beautifying commandments with fine objects).
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why did Rav need to rule that the halakha follows Rabbi Akiva with respect to BOTH circumcision AND the Paschal lamb separately?
A. Because the two halakhot appear in different tractates and Rav only quoted each once
B. Each ruling prevented a mistaken inference: the circumcision ruling might have been unique to its subject; the Paschal ruling might have been unique to Festival labor, not Shabbat
C. Because Rabbi Akiva's principle applies to Shabbat but not to Festivals, making two separate rulings necessary
D. Because Abaye and Rava disagreed about circumcision while Rav Pappa disagreed about the Paschal lamb
Correct: B — The Gemara explains: if Rav had ruled only for circumcision, one might think the rule applies only there; if only for the Paschal lamb, one might think the reason is unique to Festivals vs. weekdays. Both rulings together establish that the principle is universal — across Shabbat and Festivals, and across milah and korban Pesach.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the halakha if a circumcisor withdraws from performing the milah before completing removal of the shreds of skin (tzitzin ha-me'akvin)?
A. He may return and remove them at any point during the same day
B. He may not return — once he has withdrawn, he cannot resume; someone else must complete it
C. He must wait until the wound is healed and then remove them at a later date
D. The milah is still valid; removal of shreds is only a rabbinic requirement, not essential
Correct: B — The baraita and Gemara rule that as long as the circumcisor is still engaged in the milah, he may return to remove improperly cut shreds. But once he has withdrawn from the mitzva, he may not return. This ruling is attributed to Rabbi Yosei (or per Neharde'a, the Rabbis) based on the principle that one who withdraws from a mitzva in progress loses the right to re-engage.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rav Pappa rules that a circumcisor who omits metzitzah (sucking the blood) should be removed from practice. The Gemara says this is 'obvious' — why?
A. Because metzitzah is listed explicitly in the Torah as part of milah
B. Because Shabbat is desecrated to perform metzitzah, proving it is life-threatening to omit it; what overrides Shabbat is clearly life-saving
C. Because the Talmud records a specific incident where a child died from the omission of metzitzah
D. Because metzitzah is a positive commandment and omitting a positive commandment always incurs court removal
Correct: B — The Gemara argues: since we permit desecration of Shabbat to perform metzitzah, this establishes that omitting it poses mortal danger to the child. Any act permitted on Shabbat due to pikuach nefesh (life-danger) self-evidently proves that failure to perform it is dangerous — making Rav Pappa's ruling logically obvious.
