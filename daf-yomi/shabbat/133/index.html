✂️ When Does Milah Override Shabbat?

Is circumcision performed on the 8th day — its proper time?
↓
✅ Yes — proper time
Milah overrides Shabbat; all direct necessities (peri'ah, metzitzah, cumin, bandage) are permitted
❌ No — delayed
Milah does not override Shabbat or Festivals; must wait until after Shabbat
↓
Rabbi Akiva's limiting rule: Any preparatory act that could have been done before Shabbat does NOT override Shabbat — even for a timely milah

🔴 Bright White Spot (Tzara'at) During Circumcision — Four Voices

Rabbi Yoshiya
The word basar (flesh) in the circumcision verse explicitly teaches: even if there is a bright white tzara'at spot there, one still circumcises. A verse is required to permit this.
Rabbi Yonatan
No special verse is needed — the act is unintentional. The circumcisor intends to cut the foreskin, not to excise a lesion. Unintentional acts are already permitted.
Abaye
The derivation from basar is necessary only according to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that an unintentional act is prohibited. For Rabbi Shimon (unintentional = permitted), no verse is needed.
Rava
Even according to Rabbi Shimon, the verse is necessary — because excising the lesion during milah is inevitable (psik reisha). Rabbi Shimon only permits truly unforeseeable acts, not inevitable consequences.
Key Legal Concept Psik reisha (lit. 'cut off the head') — an inevitable unintended consequence is treated as intentional even by Rabbi Shimon, who otherwise permits unintentional acts.

📚 Four Amoraim, Four Proofs: Why Only the 8th Day Overrides

Question: What is the source that milah overrides Shabbat/Festivals only when performed on the proper 8th day?
↓
Ḥizkiya: Analogy to the Paschal lamb — just as the Paschal offering overrides Shabbat only at its appointed time (14 Nisan), so too milah overrides only at its appointed time.
↓
Abaye: From the Shabbat burnt-offering verse: "the burnt-offering of each Shabbat on its Shabbat" — each mitzva is fulfilled on its own designated day, not displacing others.
↓
Rava: From the Festival verse: "save that which every person shall eat" — only food-related labor overrides Festivals; other mitzvot override only at their fixed time.
↓
Rav Ashi: Festivals carry both a negative commandment (no labor) and a positive commandment (Shabbaton — solemn rest). A positive mitzva can only override another positive mitzva when performed at its own proper time.

🩹 What May Be Done for Milah on Shabbat — Step by Step

Milah — cut the foreskin (orla) with a knife
↓
Peri'ah — tear back and remove the thin inner membrane to uncover the corona
↓
Metzitzah — suck the blood from the wound. Rav Pappa: failure to do so endangers the child; a circumcisor who skips this is removed from practice.
↓
Cumin salve — if not ground before Shabbat, the circumcisor may chew it with his teeth and apply directly to the wound
↓
Wine and oil — if not mixed before Shabbat, apply separately: wine to one limb, oil to another
↓
Bandage — may wrap a cloth rag as a dressing; may NOT make a new bandage pouch ab initio on Shabbat. If no bandage was brought to the location before Shabbat, may carry it differently (shinui).
↓
Bandage recipes: Abaye's nurse: 7 parts fat + 1 part wax. Rava: ⅔ wax + ⅓ resin, heated together with a fig, applied to the wound. (Rava's recipe was so effective that the doctors of Meḥoza tore their garments in distress when he taught it publicly.)

✨ Ve'anveihu — Glorifying God Through Mitzvot

זֶה אֵלִי וְאַנְוֵהוּ — אֲנִי וָהוּ: הֱוֵי דּוֹמֶה לוֹ, מַה הוּא חַנּוּן וְרַחוּם, אַף אַתָּה הֱיֵה חַנּוּן וְרַחוּם
Exodus 15:2 — שִׁירַת הַיָּם "This is my God and I will glorify Him [ve'anveihu]"

Tannaitic interpretation (main view): Beautify the mitzvot before God — make a beautiful sukkah, a fine lulav, a graceful shofar, a dignified Torah scroll. This is the source for hiddur mitzva.

Abba Shaul's interpretation: Read ve'anveihu as two words — Ani vaHu, me and Him. Emulate God: "Just as He is compassionate and merciful, so too should you be compassionate and merciful."

Abba Shaul's reading is the classic rabbinic source for imitatio Dei (tzelem Elohim ethics) — moral resemblance to God as a religious obligation.
