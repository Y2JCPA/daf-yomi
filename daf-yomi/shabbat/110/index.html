↓
Descend into water
Place a basket on your head
↓

Then flee while it is occupied

If it wraps in garment → flee (directed at garment)
If not → it is directed at her
💊 Her remedy to repel it
Take dirt from beneath the snake's tracks
Throw the dirt over the snake

↓
The community slaughtered all 13 white donkeys in Pumbedita to use as a remedy

🍺 What Is 'Water That Stabs'? (Egyptian Zitom)

Rav Yosef

  • ⅓ Barley (שְׂעוֹרָה)
  • ⅓ Saffron (כַּרְכֹּם)
  • ⅓ Salt (מֶלַח)
  • Mishna term: mei dekalim
  • Tosefta term: mei dekarim — 'water that stabs'

Rav Pappa

  • ⅓ Wheat (חִטָּה)
  • ⅓ Saffron (כַּרְכֹּם)
  • ⅓ Salt + Cumin (מֶלַח וְכַמּוֹן)
  • A mnemonic device is given for the ingredients
  • Both agree it is medicinal and forbidden on Shabbat

⚗️ Is Causing Sterility Permitted?

The Gemara's Question
The kos ikarin remedy and the jaundice beer both cause sterility in the drinker. But is it permitted to cause oneself to become sterile?
Baraita
One who is wounded in the genitals shall not enter the congregation (Deut. 23:2). From here we learn: a man may not castrate himself, nor have himself sterilized. This is a Torah prohibition for men.
Resolution
Women are NOT commanded in פְּרוּ וּרְבוּ (be fruitful and multiply). Therefore the prohibition against sterilization does NOT apply to them. A woman may drink a cup of roots even knowing it will render her sterile.
Rav Ḥiyya bar Abba citing Rabbi Yoḥanan
This is confirmed by Rabbi Yoḥanan's ruling: women may take measures to prevent further pregnancies. The sterility prohibition is gender-specific — it falls on men alone.
Legal Principle Men: forbidden to cause sterility (Torah prohibition). Women: permitted — they bear no obligation in pru u'rvu.

🌿 The Arab and the Leeks — A Jaundice Folk Cure

צַהֶבֶת — jaundice | כַּרְתֵּי — leeks | טַל — dew
Aggadic Story A certain Arab was suffering from jaundice (צַהֶבֶת). He said to the gardener: 'Take my cloak and give me a row of leeks (כַּרְתֵּי) in exchange.'

The gardener agreed. The Arab ate the leeks raw. Then he said: 'Now lend me your cloak — I will sleep in the field.' He slept there and the night dew fell upon him.

In the morning he arose cured of jaundice, returned the cloak, and went his way.

The story illustrates how desperate patients turn to folk remedies — raw leeks plus exposure to night dew — that fall outside the formal medical treatments discussed in the Gemara.
