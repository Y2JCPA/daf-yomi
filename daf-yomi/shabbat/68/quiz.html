Shabbat 68 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
If one performs many prohibited labors all falling under a single category of labor, how many sin-offerings is he liable for?
A. One sin-offering per labor performed
B. One sin-offering for all the labors combined
C. No sin-offering — only lashes
D. A sin-offering for each Shabbat on which he worked
Correct: B — The mishna's rule is that multiple labors under a single category incur only one sin-offering — liability is per category, not per individual act.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the Gemara say the mishna uses the term 'significant principle' (כלל גדול) regarding Shabbat?
A. Because Shabbat is mentioned more times in the Torah than any other commandment
B. Because the scope of punishable Shabbat violations is greater than the scope for Pe'a or tithes
C. Because the rabbis wished to emphasize Shabbat's emotional importance
D. Because violating Shabbat carries the death penalty
Correct: B — The Gemara explains: 'significant' means significant relative to other principles — more materials fall under Shabbat liability than under Pe'a or tithes obligations.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav and Shmuel, whom does the mishna refer to when it speaks of 'one who forgot the very essence of Shabbat'?
A. Only a person who once observed Shabbat and later forgot
B. Only a child taken captive among gentiles
C. Both a child taken captive among gentiles and a convert who converted among gentiles
D. Any Jew who was never taught the laws of Shabbat
Correct: C — Rav and Shmuel hold the mishna covers both the child captive and the convert — both are examples of someone who grew up with no exposure to Shabbat whatsoever.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish, is a convert who converted among gentiles liable for a sin-offering for violating Shabbat?
A. Yes, liable for each Shabbat violated
B. Yes, but only one sin-offering for all violations
C. No — he never knew of Shabbat and is therefore exempt
D. No — converts are never liable for Shabbat violations
Correct: C — R' Yochanan and Reish Lakish require prior knowledge: since the convert who converted among gentiles never knew of Shabbat, he cannot be held liable — he has nothing to have 'forgotten.'
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What requirement does Munbaz derive from the juxtaposition of intentional and unintentional sinners in the Torah?
A. Unintentional sinners must pay a monetary fine in addition to a sin-offering
B. Prior awareness of the prohibition is necessary for a sin-offering obligation
C. Unintentional sinners are fully exempt from all punishment
D. The sin-offering must be brought within thirty days of the transgression
Correct: B — Munbaz reasons: just as intentional sinners are defined by awareness, unintentional sinners must have had prior knowledge of the prohibition — without it, there is nothing to be 'unintentionally' forgetting.
