⚔️ Two Camps: Who Is Liable for Forgetting Shabbat?

Rav & Shmuel
The mishna refers to both a child taken captive among gentiles and a convert who converted among gentiles — neither was ever exposed to Shabbat observance, yet both are included.
R' Yochanan & Reish Lakish
One is only liable to bring a sin-offering if he once knew the essence of Shabbat and later forgot. A child captive or gentile convert who never knew at all is exempt entirely.
The Central Question How can a Jew 'forget the very existence of Shabbat'? This dispute defines the scope of liability for unintentional Shabbat transgression.

📊 Pe'a vs. Tithes — Scope of the Significant Principles

Pe'a (Corner of the Field)

  • Must be food
  • Must be protected (storable)
  • Must grow from the ground
  • Must be harvested
  • Must be brought in as one harvest
  • Woad & madder excluded — used for dye, not food

Tithes (Maaser)

  • Must be food
  • Must be protected (storable)
  • Must grow from the ground
  • No harvest requirement
  • No single-harvest requirement
  • Broader scope than Pe'a — hence its own כלל גדול

🤝 Munbaz and Rabbi Akiva — The Nature of Unintentional Sin

Munbaz
Since the Torah calls both intentional and unintentional transgressors 'sinners,' just as intentional sinners require awareness at the time of the act, so too unintentional sinners require prior knowledge of the prohibition.
Rabbi Akiva
If we learn from intentional sinners — then just as intentional sinners are not liable for a sin-offering, unintentional sinners should be exempt too! Your own logic undermines the institution of the חטאת!
Munbaz
Correct — and now that you've elaborated, my argument is even stronger. Prior awareness is the criterion, not intentionality itself. The child captive had no prior awareness and is therefore exempt from bringing a sin-offering.
Scriptural Source Munbaz derives from: 'One law for you, for one who acts in error' (Numbers 15) — native and stranger treated equally, requiring the same level of prior knowledge.

📣 The 'Significant Principle' Unpacked

כלל גדול אמרו בשבת — כל המלאכות שעשה בהעלם אחת חייב אחת
כלל גדול — A Significant Principle The mishna opens: 'A significant principle was stated regarding Shabbat.'

Why significant? The scope of materials for which one is punished for violating Shabbat is greater than the scope for Pe'a and tithes.

Bar Kappara applies the same phrase to tithes — because tithes also have a broader scope than Pe'a. Significance is always relative: each principle is 'great' compared to a narrower one below it.

Rabbi Yosei bar Avin disagrees: the term does not depend on comparison at all — it reflects the intrinsic weight of the halakhot of Shabbat and the Sabbatical year.
