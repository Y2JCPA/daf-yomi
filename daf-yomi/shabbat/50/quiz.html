QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Meir, why can a blind judge NOT adjudicate monetary cases?
A. Blind people are disqualified by rabbinic decree to protect court integrity
B. Disputes are compared by gezeira shava to leprous marks, which require visual inspection by sighted priests
C. The Torah explicitly states that judges must be physically whole
D. Blind judges cannot read written documents presented as evidence
Correct: B — Rabbi Meir derives from Deuteronomy 21:5 that 'every dispute' is linked to 'every leprous mark.' Just as leprous marks require 'as far as appears to the priest' — sighted inspection — so too disputes require sighted judges.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does the mishna add by specifically listing meat, fish, and eggs in the context of ritual impurity of food?
A. They are subject to tithes unlike other animal products
B. They require pe'a designation from the owner
C. They are susceptible to food ritual impurity but are NOT subject to the obligation of tithes
D. They must be inspected by a priest before consumption
Correct: C — The mishna's final clause adds meat, fish, and eggs as the 'something extra' — items that can become ritually impure as food yet fall entirely outside the tithe system, since tithes apply only to produce that grows from the earth.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yochanan, when must intent occur for endives (originally planted for animals) to become susceptible to ritual impurity as food?
A. When the endives are first planted in the ground
B. At any point while the endives are still growing in the ground
C. Only after the endives have been detached from the ground
D. Intent is never required — all plants are automatically food
Correct: C — Rabbi Yochanan holds that intent while produce is still attached to the ground is legally meaningless, since attached produce cannot acquire impurity. Intent must occur after detachment to designate the item as food and make it susceptible.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rav Pappa explain why the 'chicken of Yavne' required post-mortem intent despite being present before the Sages while alive?
A. The Sages of Yavne were deliberately lenient in their ruling as a special dispensation
B. It was a marsh hen (de'agma) — no one lives in marshes, so no one could have formed intent while it was alive
C. The chicken had fallen into a winepress and become disgusting, removing its food status
D. It was a non-kosher bird and therefore subject to different impurity rules
Correct: B — Rav Pappa identifies the Yavne chicken as a marsh hen (עוף דאגמא). Since no human population lives in marshes, there was literally no opportunity for anyone to form intent designating it as food while it was still alive — explaining why post-mortem intent was both necessary and sufficient.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the Rabbis' core refutation of Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's kal va'chomer argument?
A. A kal va'chomer argument cannot be used to derive laws of ritual impurity
B. The severe impurity of a bird carcass requires no intent because it does not assume that status through intent — unlike food impurity, which does assume its status through intent and therefore requires it
C. The carcass of a kosher bird does not in fact transmit severe impurity to garments
D. Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri misquoted the verse about bird carcass impurity
Correct: B — The Rabbis argue that the kal va'chomer is defeated by a pircha: severe impurity needs no intent precisely because it doesn't acquire its status through intent at all. Food impurity, by contrast, acquires its status through intent (machshava) — so it logically requires intent. The 'leniency' (no intent required) flows from a characteristic that the severe case has and the lenient case lacks.
