🔑 Two Worlds, One Daf

כָּל הָרָאוּי לָדוּן רָאוּי לְהָעִיד — וְיֵשׁ רָאוּי לְהָעִיד וְאֵינוֹ רָאוּי לָדוּן
Overview Niddah 50 bridges two major halakhic worlds: court procedure (can a blind judge adjudicate?) and ritual purity (when does food become susceptible to impurity?). The connecting thread is analogical reasoning — whether through a gezeira shava linking disputes to leprous marks, or a kal va'chomer about bird carcass impurity. Along the way, the mishna maps the overlapping obligations of pe'a, tithes, and food susceptibility.

🦯 Can a Blind Judge Adjudicate?

Rabbi Meir
A blind judge CANNOT adjudicate. Deuteronomy 21:5 links 'every dispute' to 'every leprous mark.' Just as leprous marks require visual inspection by sighted priests — 'as far as appears to the priest' — so too disputes must be judged by those who can see.
Gemara Challenge
But Rabbi Yochanan permitted a blind neighbor to render judgments and said nothing! And the mishna here teaches that anyone fit to judge is fit to testify — implying blind people CAN judge (since they can testify about things they heard).
Resolution
Rabbi Yochanan relied on the unattributed mishna in Sanhedrin: monetary cases are judged by day, concluded by day — implying visual perception is central to adjudication. When two stam mishnayot conflict, he accepted Sanhedrin's as stronger because it aligns with the logic that daytime judgment requires sight.
Key Principle When unattributed mishnayot conflict, the Gemara must determine which takes precedence — here, Sanhedrin's daytime-judgment rule outweighs the Niddah mishna's apparent inclusion of blind judges.

🌾 Pe'a, Tithes & Food Impurity — Three Overlapping Systems

Pe'a Obligated

  • Grain crops: wheat, barley, spelt
  • Legumes: beans, lentils
  • Must be: food + stored long-term + grows from earth + can be harvested all at once
  • Garlic and onions in garden beds (stored = obligated)

Tithes but NOT Pe'a

  • Figs (short-term storage)
  • Vegetables: not harvested all at once
  • Pe'a conditions not met but tithes still apply
  • Must be: food + protected + grows from earth

Food Impurity, NOT Tithes

  • Meat
  • Fish
  • Eggs
  • Does not grow from the earth → no tithes, but IS susceptible to ritual impurity as food

🐓 When Does Produce Become Susceptible to Ritual Impurity?

Endives planted to feed animals — not designated as human food
↓
Owner reconsiders — wants them for human consumption
Intent while still attached to ground?
Insufficient — produce attached to the ground cannot acquire impurity; intent at that stage is legally meaningless
Intent after detachment from ground?
Required AND effective — once detached, intent designates it as human food and makes it susceptible to ritual impurity
↓
Rabbi Yochanan's ruling: Intent must occur after detachment — because intent while attached cannot take legal effect

🍗 The Chicken of Yavne — Untangling a Famous Precedent

The Incident: A chicken in Yavne died in the presence of the Sages, who then deemed it impure — relying on post-mortem intent
↓
Rabbi Zeira's Original Claim: A young bird that fell from a height and died was never 'before us' while alive. Therefore, intent could only be formed after death — the Yavne case is no proof for birds present while alive.
↓
Abaye's Challenge: The Yavne chicken WAS present before the Sages while alive — yet intent was still only formed afterward! This undermines Rabbi Zeira's distinction entirely.
↓
Rav Pappa's Resolution: The Yavne chicken was a marsh hen (עוף דאגמא). Since no one lives in a marsh, there was literally no opportunity for anyone to designate it as food while alive — so post-mortem intent was the only option.
↓
Alternative Resolution (Rabbi Zeira bar Ḥanina): The bird fell into a winepress, making it disgusting. This removed its food status entirely, so fresh intent was needed even in a city — regardless of population.
↓
Mnemonic: Like the rule 'an Ammonite man (not woman) may not enter the assembly' — the marsh hen (the 'female' permitted case) is remembered by contrast with the restricted case.

⚖️ Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's Kal Va'chomer

R. Yochanan ben Nuri
A fortiori: If a kosher bird carcass transmits SEVERE impurity (renders garments impure when an olive-bulk is swallowed) WITHOUT requiring intent — surely it should transmit the LENIENT impurity of food without intent as well!
The Rabbis
Invalid kal va'chomer! Severe impurity needs no intent precisely because it does NOT assume that status through intent. But lenient food impurity DOES assume its status through intent — so it logically also requires intent to take effect.
R. Yochanan ben Nuri
The Yavne chicken case refutes you! There, intent WAS required even though the chicken was present while alive — proving that 'assuming status through thought' does NOT determine whether intent is needed.
The Rabbis
Yavne proves nothing — it was a special case: either a marsh hen (no opportunity for prior intent) or a bird rendered disgusting by the winepress. Neither represents the standard rule. Your kal va'chomer remains invalid.
The Core Dispute Does the mechanism by which an impurity status is acquired (with or without thought) determine whether intent is needed for it to apply in a given instance?
