🔗 The Mishna's Triple Asymmetric Principle

Entry → Exit (Earthenware Vessel)

  • Any hole allowing liquid IN also allows liquid OUT
  • But: some holes allow EXIT without allowing ENTRY
  • Larger holes = exit only; smaller = neither

Nail → Bone (Limbs of Body)

  • Any limb with a nail certainly has a bone beneath it
  • But: some limbs have bone without a nail
  • Extra fingers may have bone but lack a nail entirely

Zav Treading → Corpse Impurity (Vessels)

  • Any vessel susceptible to zav treading is susceptible to corpse impurity
  • But: some vessels receive corpse impurity without zav treading impurity
  • Boards, shelves, ladders = corpse impurity only

🏺 Methods for Testing an Earthenware Vessel's Hole

First Tanna — Tub Method: Fill a tub with water. Place the broken vessel inside. Observe whether water flows into the vessel — if so, the hole is entry-sized.
↓
Rabbi Yehuda — Inverted Tub Method: Grasp the pot's handles and place it upside down in an empty tub, then pour water over it from above. If water enters through the hole, it is entry-sized.
↓
Fire Test (alternative): Place the pot containing liquid onto a fire. If the fire holds the liquid back and it does not exit, the hole is small — exit-size only, not entry-size.
↓
Rabbi Yosei objects: Do not use the fire test! Heat can seal even a large hole temporarily, causing a false result. A hole that is truly entry-sized may appear small.
↓
Ulla explains the dispute between the first tanna and Rabbi Yehuda: their methods differ in cases of a crack that opens at the vessel's rim versus a hole pierced through its body.

🔥 Is the Fire Test Reliable?

First Tanna
Place the vessel with liquid ON THE FIRE. If the liquid does not exit, the hole is small — only exit-sized. This is a valid practical test.
Rabbi Yosei
Do NOT rely on this test! The fire itself can temporarily close the hole. Even a hole large enough for liquid to enter may be held shut by the heat — giving a completely false result.
First Tanna
The fire test is useful when no tub is available and one needs a quick determination in the field.
Rabbi Yosei
Only the tub method gives a definitive answer — use that exclusively. The risk of error in the fire test is too great for a halakhic ruling.
Core Dispute Does fire artificially shrink a hole, making a large (entry-sized) hole appear small? Rabbi Yosei says yes — the fire test must be rejected.

💅 The Extra Finger: Bone Without Nail

אצבע יתירה — עצם ללא צפורן
Rav Ḥisda citing Rav — with Rabbi Yoḥanan's limit Rav Ḥisda (citing Rav): An extra finger with a bone but no nail transmits ritual impurity through contact and movement — even if its size is less than an olive-bulk — because the bone qualifies it as a full limb. Rabba bar bar Ḥana (citing Rabbi Yoḥanan): This applies only when the extra finger cannot be counted along the back of the hand (i.e., it grows inward). If it aligns naturally with the other fingers and can be counted, it does not transmit impurity unless it reaches olive-bulk size. Practical rule: inward-growing extra bone-finger = full limb regardless of size; outward-countable extra finger = needs olive-bulk.

🪑 Zav Treading Impurity: Which Vessels Qualify?

Is the vessel designated for sitting or lying upon?
YES — a seat, bed, or saddle
Susceptible to zav treading impurity → Also susceptible to corpse impurity
NO — a board, shelf, or ladder
Not susceptible to zav treading impurity → But may still receive corpse impurity
↓
Verse (Leviticus 15:6): "He who sits on any object whereon the zav sits" — only objects designated for sitting qualify for zav treading; boards and ladders are excluded
