⚖️ Can a Rope Become Ritually Impure?

Baraita (initial)
I might have thought that even ropes and thin cords become ritually impure — they are textile-like and associated with sacks and garments.
Logical Counter
The verse only deems impure an object that has a minimum size. A rope has no minimum size and therefore cannot be in the same category.
Challenge
If the Torah was lenient about creeping-animal impurity (a less severe form) and excluded ropes, perhaps stricter corpse-impurity should include them — a kal va-chomer!
Rejection
Reversed: if even the lenient creeping-animal impurity excluded ropes, the more severe corpse impurity certainly excludes them. The kal va-chomer goes the other way.
Conclusion Ropes and thin cords do not become ritually impure under either category. The exclusion is confirmed by logic in both directions.

💎 The Kumaz: War Spoils and Communal Atonement

כָּאן מְקוֹם זִמָּה — K-M-Z
Numbers 31:50 — The Officers' Offering The officers brought gold ornaments — chains, bracelets, rings, agil, and kumaz — as an offering to atone before God. Moses rebuked the officers (Rav Naḥman citing Rabba bar Avuh): 'Did you spare all the women? They are the very ones who caused Israel to sin at Peor!' What is kumaz? • Rav Yosef: We translate it as maḥokh (Aramaic) — 'that which leads to foolishness.' • Rabba: The meaning is in the word itself — an acronym: כָּאן מְקוֹם זִמָּה — 'Here is the place of lewdness,' referring to its provocative placement on the body. School of Rabbi Yishmael: Why did Israel need atonement after the war? Because they nourished their eyes from nakedness. Rav Sheshet: One who gazes upon a woman's little finger is considered as if he gazed upon her most intimate place.

👁️ Marit Ayin: Does It Apply in Private?

Rav
Any action the Sages prohibited because of marit ayin — the appearance of prohibition — is forbidden even in the innermost private chambers where no one will see. The Sages made no distinction between public and private.
R. Anani bar Sason (R. Yishmael)
All ornaments have the same status as a woolen cap: permitted in the courtyard. The prohibition is only in the public domain — there is no general extension to private spaces.
Ulla (explaining Rav)
The mishna permits woolen cap and wig only in the courtyard for a specific reason: so she will not become unappealing to her husband. This is a unique dispensation, not a general rule about ornaments.
Objection from Baraita
A plugged bell prohibited due to marit ayin — but a Tanna'itic baraita disputes whether this applies in private. If Rav's rule were absolute, there would be no dispute!
Status: Tanna'itic Dispute Whether marit ayin restrictions apply in private is left as a dispute between Tanna'im — the Gemara does not resolve it here.
