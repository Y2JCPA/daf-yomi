Shabbat 99 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Gemara on 99a, the clasps in the loops connecting the Tabernacle curtains resembled what?
A. Golden knots tied in sequence
B. Stars in the sky
C. Silver rings on a chain
D. Olive branches in the wind
Correct: B — The Gemara teaches that the clasps in the loops appeared like stars in the sky — a vivid image of the Tabernacle's celestial beauty that made the seams nearly invisible.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What are the minimum dimensions for an object in the public domain to constitute a private domain (רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד)?
A. 10 cubits high and 4 cubits wide
B. 4 handbreadths high and 10 handbreadths wide
C. 10 handbreadths high and 4 handbreadths wide
D. 16 handbreadths high and 4 handbreadths wide
Correct: C — The rule requires exactly 10 טְפָחִים (handbreadths) in height and 4 טְפָחִים in width. Both thresholds must be met to confer private domain status on an object standing in public space.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What logical argument does Ulla use to prove that a wall enclosing a karmelit is itself a private domain?
A. Gezerah shavah — verbal analogy between two verses
B. Binyan av — a paradigm case extending to others
C. Kal va-ḥomer — a fortiori inference
D. Hekesh — a textual comparison between passages
Correct: C — Ulla applies a קַל וָחֹמֶר (a fortiori): if this wall creates private domain status for the area it encloses, then all the more so must the wall itself — which provides the enclosure — be private domain.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Rava rules regarding water poured onto water from one domain to another. What is his ruling?
A. Liquids cannot constitute a halachic 'placement' (הֲנָחָה)
B. Pouring water onto existing water is considered placement — one is liable
C. Only the first droplet counts; the rest are exempt
D. Water poured into a karmelit is always exempt
Correct: B — Rava rules that pouring water onto water of the same type constitutes hanaḥah (placement). Therefore, transferring it between a private and public domain on Shabbat makes one liable.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What was the width of each wagon used to transport the Tabernacle beams, from which the Gemara attempts to derive the 16-cubit public domain?
A. One cubit
B. Two cubits
C. Two and a half cubits
D. Four cubits
Correct: C — Each wagon was two and a half cubits wide. Rava explains that the sides of the wagon (between body and wheel plus wheel thickness) equaled another full wagon-width, and combining two wagons with passage space yields the 16-cubit standard.
