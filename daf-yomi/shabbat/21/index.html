⚖️ The Central Dispute: Tissue Without Blood

Rabbis
A woman who discharges amorphous tissue without blood is pure. The womb can open without a blood discharge.
Rabbi Yehuda
She is impure — the womb cannot open without blood. If no blood is visible, it was absorbed into the tissue.
Shmuel (via Rav Yehuda)
Rabbi Yehuda ruled impure only when the tissue has one of the four impure blood colors; for other colors he agrees she is pure.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
The dispute is only when the woman does not know the tissue's appearance (e.g., it was lost). When the color is known, all agree on the ruling.
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
The true dispute: can פתיחת הקבר (womb-opening) occur without blood? This underlies both the Mishna and the tannaitic debate about offerings.
Resolution Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak: Rabbis hold the womb CAN open without blood → tissue alone does not make her impure. Rabbi Yehuda holds it CANNOT → absent blood was absorbed by the tissue.

🍷 Blood Examination: Wine & Color Standards

Sharon Wine (Standard)

  • Used as reference for blood color comparison
  • Equivalent to undiluted Carmelite wine
  • Equivalent to new (not old) Carmelite wine
  • Specific to the Sharon region of Eretz Yisrael

Tiberian Cup

  • Used for blood with the color of diluted wine
  • Ruled by Rav Yitzḥak bar Avudimi
  • Abaye: cups follow a universal standard
  • Ensures consistent color comparison everywhere

Four Impure Blood Colors

  • Red — standard menstrual blood
  • Black — darkened blood
  • Crocus color — bright yellow-red
  • Earthy/muddy water — murky reddish
  • Only these four render a woman niddah

🔬 How to Examine Amorphous Tissue — Four Tannaitic Views

Sumakhos / R. Shimon ben Menasya
A Sage presented with tissue must examine it carefully. The ruling follows what the Sage finds — more stringent than the Rabbis of the Mishna.
Rabbi Aḥa
One tears open the tissue. If the interior is red — even without visible blood — the woman is impure. Focuses on the interior color.
Rabbi Binyamin
One tears it open: if there is a bone → treated as a fetus, mother has yoledet impurity. Only blood → impure as niddah. Neither → pure.
R. Shimon ben Yoḥai (via R. Yoḥanan)
One tears it open. If it contains accumulated blood (כמין מוגלא) → impure. If not → pure. This pooled blood counts; blood via a tube does not.
Key Distinction All opinions require physically opening the tissue. The dispute is what finding triggers impurity: red color, a bone, any blood, or specifically accumulated/pooled blood.

🩺 The Tube Question — Rabbi Yirmeya & Rabbi Zeira

בְּבְשָׂרָהּ — "in her flesh" (ויקרא טו:יט)
הלכה Question (Rabbi Yirmeya): A woman inserts a tube into her vagina and finds blood in it — is she impure?

Verse: "And if a woman has an issue, and her issue in her flesh be blood" (Lev. 15:19)

Answer (Rabbi Zeira): She is pure. The Torah requires discharge through her own flesh directly — a tube is an intervening channel and is excluded by בְּבְשָׂרָהּ.

But contrast: Blood found inside tissue DOES make her impure — it is the normal way for a woman's blood to appear within gestational tissue, so it satisfies 'in her flesh.' A tube is unnatural.

Abaye's conclusion: Regarding a tube, everyone agrees the woman is pure — it is derived unanimously from 'in her flesh.'
