⚖️ Rabbi Shimon vs. Rabbi Yehuda: Unattributed Mishnayot and Muktzeh

Rabbi Yehuda
One may not chop wood from building beams or a beam that broke on a Festival — these are muktzeh (set-aside) and forbidden to move or use on Shabbat.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
That unattributed mishna follows Rabbi Yehuda — but I found a different unattributed mishna following Rabbi Shimon: Beit Hillel say one may lift bones and peels from the table on Shabbat.
Rabbi Yehuda
Non-domesticated desert animals may not receive water or be slaughtered on a Festival. Since people do not generally tend to them, they are set-aside and forbidden.
Rabbi Shimon
Beit Shammai permit lifting set-aside shells from the table; Beit Hillel are more lenient still. Items that were food do not truly become muktzeh.
Rabbi Yoḥanan's Resolution Some unattributed mishnayot follow Rabbi Yehuda; others follow Rabbi Shimon. Each mishna must be examined individually to determine which opinion it reflects.

⚖️ Two Approaches to Muktzeh

Rabbi Yehuda — Strict

  • Muktzeh is broadly prohibited on Shabbat
  • Broken Festival beam: forbidden to chop
  • Desert animals: may not water or slaughter
  • Items mentally set aside are restricted
  • Most unattributed mishnayot reflect his view

Rabbi Shimon — Lenient

  • Muktzeh is minimally restricted on Shabbat
  • Bones and peels: permitted to lift from table
  • Beit Hillel: even set-aside items may be cleared
  • Items with residual food use are never truly muktzeh
  • Halakha generally follows his view on Shabbat

🗣️ Rav Aḥa and Ravina: Where Does the Exception Lie?

Rav Aḥa
In all Shabbat disputes where Rabbi Shimon disagrees with Rabbi Yehuda, halakha follows Rabbi Shimon — except for the entire category of muktzeh, where we rule like Rabbi Yehuda.
Ravina
Halakha follows Rabbi Shimon even in muktzeh — except for one narrow case: an item set aside due to prohibition (muktzeh machmat issur), where we rule like Rabbi Yehuda.
Practical Difference Rav Aḥa restricts all muktzeh by Rabbi Yehuda's standard. Ravina is more lenient — only the strictest muktzeh category (e.g., a candle, money) is restricted; ordinary muktzeh follows Rabbi Shimon's leniency.

📜 May One Nullify Vows on Shabbat?

Mishna: A father or husband may nullify his daughter's or wife's vow on Shabbat
↓
Dilemma raised: Is nullification always permitted on Shabbat, or only when it serves Shabbat's needs?
↓
Position A
Nullification is always permitted on Shabbat — it involves no forbidden labor (melakha)
Position B
Nullification is permitted only for the purpose of Shabbat — e.g., to let her prepare food she vowed not to eat
↓
Zutei of Rav Pappa's school: "One may nullify vows on Shabbat for the purpose of Shabbat" — implying purpose matters
↓
Rav Ashi: The mishna's rule — nullification applies for the entire day — involves a leniency and stringency about timing, not a restriction to Shabbat purposes only

🪟 The Alleyway, the Cracked Roofing, and the Sealed Window

Setting: A small alleyway (heilketei) stood between two houses, with a corpse inside the alleyway
↓
The Problem: A cracked roofing covered both houses. If intact it would function as a "tent," spreading corpse-impurity through the windows into both houses
↓
Action 1: They sealed a window using an earthenware vessel — blocking the pathway of impurity from alleyway to house
↓
Action 2: They tied an earthenware shard with a long reed-grass — an additional form of sealing to contain the impurity
↓
Action 3: They measured to determine the precise dimensions involved — measurement permitted on Shabbat for purity needs
↓
Triple Ruling Derived: One may seal a window, measure, and tie a knot on Shabbat when required for ritual purity — completing Tractate Shabbat
