Shabbat 43 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava, why does a priest holding his penis with a soft cloth NOT increase the seminal emission when his limbs are quaking?
A. A soft cloth absorbs emission before it exits the body
B. When limbs are quaking, the emission will be expelled regardless — holding cannot increase it
C. Quaking indicates the emission already completed internally
D. A soft cloth is halachically equivalent to a coarse one in all cases
Correct: B — Rava distinguishes two scenarios: limbs quaking (emission inevitable, holding irrelevant) vs. dripping without quaking (holding might increase flow). His ruling that soft cloth works applies specifically to the quaking case.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the practical halakhic difference between Shmuel's two versions regarding when semen renders a man impure?
A. Version 1 applies to priests; version 2 applies to Israelites only
B. Semen uprooted with sensation but emitted without sensation: impure by version 1 but not by version 2
C. Version 2 is more lenient, requiring only minimal sensation
D. The Gemara concludes there is no practical difference between them
Correct: B — Version 1 (felt by whole body) focuses on sensation at uprooting. Version 2 (shot like arrow) focuses on force at emission. Semen that begins with full sensation but exits slowly or weakly fulfills version 1 but not version 2.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yishmael (as ruled by Shmuel), what minimum amount of discharge is required for a zav to become impure?
A. Any visible discharge, however small
B. A lentil-bulk of discharge
C. An amount large enough to block the tip of the penis
D. Three separate emissions of any amount
Correct: C — Shmuel rules in accordance with Rabbi Yishmael that zav discharge must be substantial enough to 'dam up' (block) the tip of the penis. The mishna's 'any amount' means any amount above this threshold — not truly any amount.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In Rava's dilemma about a gentile whose semen was uprooted before his immersion-conversion, what outcome follows from following the moment of emission?
A. He is not impure, because he was a gentile when the semen was uprooted
B. He is impure, because at the moment of emission he was already a Jew
C. He must immerse again to remove the impurity
D. The question is irrelevant because gentiles can never become impure
Correct: B — Following emission is the stringent position: at the moment semen exited his body, he had already converted and was subject to Jewish law on semen-impurity. Rava leaves this dilemma unresolved.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to the new mishna (43b), from what age can a baby boy become impure with ziva impurity?
A. From age three years
B. From the age of bar mitzvah (13 years)
C. From one day old
D. Only when he shows signs of physical maturity
Correct: C — The mishna rules that a baby boy, even one day old, becomes impure with ziva, tzaraat, and corpse impurity, and creates a levirate bond for his widow — full legal capacity applies from the very first day of life.
