⚖️ Muktzeh: Two Great Schools Clash at the Shabbat Table

Beit Shammai
Clear bones and shells by hand only — one may not move the board itself
Beit Hillel
Remove the entire board (tavla) and shake — bones and shells fall off together
Rav Naḥman
Reverse the opinions! Beit Shammai follows Rabbi Yehuda (muktzeh prohibited); Beit Hillel follows Rabbi Shimon (muktzeh permitted)
The Underlying Debate The table-clearing dispute maps onto the fundamental muktzeh controversy: Rabbi Yehuda prohibits moving set-aside items; Rabbi Shimon permits unintentional acts. Rav Naḥman realigns each school with its true Tannaitic position.

🏠 Table Clearing: Two Schools, Two Methods

Beit Shammai

  • Clear bones and shells by hand only
  • Cannot lift or shake the entire board
  • Follows Rabbi Yehuda — muktzeh is prohibited
  • Stringent: no indirect board movement
  • Pea and lentil pods: forbidden (set-aside)

Beit Hillel

  • Remove entire board (tavla) and shake
  • Bones and shells fall off indirectly
  • Follows Rabbi Shimon — muktzeh is permitted
  • Lenient: board movement fully allowed
  • Pea and lentil pods: permitted (animal fodder)

🌴 How Seven Sages Disposed of Date Pits on Shabbat

Aramean date pits — low quality, occasionally fed to animals
Challenge: muktzeh concern prevents ordinary removal on Shabbat
↓
Shmuel
Carried pits along with bread — piggybacking on permitted item
Rabba
Moved them with a pitcher of water — alongside permitted vessel
Rav Huna b. R. Yehoshua
Rendered them a chamber pot — repulsive vessels may be moved
↓
Rav Sheshet
Disposed of pits with his tongue — no hand movement at all
Rav Pappa
Left them behind the divan — avoided handling entirely
R. Zekharya b. Avkolas
Would turn his couch over onto them to cover the pits

✂️ Psik Reisha — When the Outcome Is Inevitable

ר' שמעון מודה בפסיק רישיה ולא ימות — אביי ורבא
פסיק רישא — קטל רישא ולא ימות? Rabbi Shimon generally permits unintentional Shabbat violations (מלאכה שאינה צריכה לגופה) — acts where the prohibited result is incidental to one's purpose.

However, Abaye and Rava both rule: even Rabbi Shimon agrees when the forbidden outcome is completely inevitable.

Classic case: wiping a table with a handleless sponge — squeezing will occur. There is no way to avoid it.

"Cut off its head — will it not die?" When the consequence is certain, intent is irrelevant.

This is why the sponge with a leather handle is permitted — the handle allows wiping without inevitably squeezing.

🛢️ Rescuing Wine/Oil from a Broken Barrel on Shabbat

Step 1: A barrel of wine or oil breaks on Shabbat — immediate action is possible
↓
Step 2: One may rescue food sufficient for three meals for oneself — a normal Shabbat amount
↓
Step 3: One may say to others: "Come and rescue food for yourselves" — each person takes their own Shabbat portion
↓
PROHIBITED: Soaking up wine with a sponge or cloth — resembles weekday commercial salvage
↓
PROHIBITED: Deliberately collecting oil into a vessel — conduct unbecoming of Shabbat (tirḥa d'chol)
↓
Tosefta principle: One must not act on Shabbat in the manner one acts during the week, even when rescuing permitted quantities
