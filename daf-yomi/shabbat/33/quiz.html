QUESTION 1 OF 5
What level of impurity does bedding above a zav carry, according to the Gemara's conclusion?
A. Severe impurity — defiles people and their garments
B. Lesser impurity — defiles food and drink only
C. No impurity at all — the verse excludes it entirely
D. Identical impurity to bedding beneath a zav
Correct: B — The Gemara concludes that the verse (Lev. 15:10) removes bedding above a zav from severe impurity, giving it only lesser impurity: it defiles food and drink but not people or garments.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Abaye argue that the verses about a man who engages in intercourse with a niddah do NOT constitute a standard generalization and detail (כלל ופרט)?
A. Because the two verses appear in different books of the Torah
B. Because "every bed upon which he lies" is expansive (ריבוי) language, not a limiting detail
C. Because the phrase "he shall be impure seven days" interrupts between the generalization and the detail
D. Because Rav Aḥai's prior ruling already settled the matter definitively
Correct: C — Abaye argues that "he shall be impure seven days" intervenes between "her impurity be upon him" (the generalization) and "every bed upon which he lies" (the detail), making them too distant to function as a standard כלל ופרט derivation.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What practice of Samaritan women causes their husbands to be treated as men who engage in intercourse with menstruating women?
A. They do not immerse in a mikveh after their niddah period ends
B. They count seven days of impurity beginning from any blood emission — including green, non-impure blood
C. They show their blood to Sadducee authorities rather than to rabbinic Sages
D. They treat all uterine discharges as ziva regardless of timing in the cycle
Correct: B — Samaritan women begin counting seven days from any blood — even green blood, which is not halakhically impure. If red blood appears during those seven days they do not restart the count, creating invalid counting periods that leave their husbands in a state of ba'al niddah.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rava resolve Rami bar Ḥama's dilemma about whether a zava who discharges semen negates her clean-day count?
A. A zava's seminal discharge is equivalent to ziva and always negates the full seven-day count requiring her to restart
B. A zava's seminal discharge never negates her count because women cannot be compared to a zav in this regard
C. Even granting it negates something, she cannot negate just one day and cannot restart the full count — so the dilemma has no valid resolution
D. The Gemara leaves the question unresolved (תיקו)
Correct: C — Rava shows the dilemma is ill-formed: even if seminal discharge negated her count, she cannot negate just one day (since Lev. 15:28 requires seven fully clean days, implying an unbroken seven), nor does the discharge constitute ziva requiring a restart — leaving no coherent outcome, the dilemma dissolves.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rav Shimi bar Ashi, why does the mishna rule leniently about a Samaritan ḥaver's garments — not requiring the teruma that touched them to be burned?
A. Because a Samaritan ḥaver is presumed pure by a specific rabbinic enactment
B. Because multiple overlapping uncertainties compound: did he recently have intercourse? Did his wife count from green blood? Did red blood appear during that count?
C. Because his immersion in a mikveh removes all concerns about ba'al niddah impurity
D. Because the garments of any ḥaver can never be treated as having contact with a ba'al niddah
Correct: B — Rav Shimi bar Ashi explains that a ספק ספיקא (compounded doubt) applies: we are uncertain whether he recently had intercourse at all; if yes, uncertain whether his wife counted from green blood; if yes, uncertain whether she saw red blood during that period — multiple layers of doubt prevent the stringent ruling.
