↓
Forgotten there
Not a true basis — permitted item may be moved indirectly (tilt, shake, walk past)
Intentionally placed
Object becomes a basis l'davar ha'assur — the permitted item is now forbidden to move

🛢️ Stone on a Barrel — Navigating the Prohibition

Stone (muktzeh) rests on mouth of a barrel
↓
Stone was forgotten there
Tilt barrel sideways — stone falls off. Permitted.
Stone was intentionally placed
Barrel becomes a basis — may not be moved at all
↓
Barrel is surrounded by other barrels that might break?
↓
No obstacles
Simply tilt in place; stone falls
Breakable vessels nearby
Lift barrel first, carry it to an open area, then tilt. Also permitted.
Glass vessels beneath it
Per Rabbi Yosei: may not be moved at all — too risky

💰 Coins on a Cushion — Three Opinions on the Limit

Rav Huna (citing Rav)
The ruling that one shakes the cushion and the coins fall applies only when the coins were forgotten on the cushion before Shabbat. If placed intentionally, the cushion is a basis — forbidden.
Rav Yoḥanan
Even when forgotten, one may only shake if he needs the cushion itself. If he needs the place where the cushion lies, he may not shake it, as he has no use for the cushion per se.
Rabbi Oshaya
If one forgot a purse of money in the courtyard: have multiple people walk toward it — it will be moved incidentally as they pass by. This indirect movement is permitted.
Mar Zutra
All these leniencies apply only to forgetfulness. If one intentionally left an item before Shabbat, he may not employ any artifice or workaround whatsoever to move it on Shabbat.
Final Halakha Mar Zutra's strict ruling stands: intentional placement forfeits all indirect-movement workarounds.

🌾 Moving Impure Teruma on Shabbat — Three Views

Rav Ḥisda

  • Only permitted when pure teruma is on the bottom
  • Moving the impure on top is justified — one needs to reach the pure beneath
  • If pure is on top, there is no need; just take it directly without touching the impure
  • Proof: the next mishna about coins implies one cannot move things without need

Baraita Objection

  • Permits moving impure teruma with pure teruma in either arrangement
  • Whether pure is on top or pure is on bottom — both are permitted
  • Seems to directly contradict Rav Ḥisda's restriction

Resolution

  • No contradiction: the cases differ by what the person needs
  • Baraita: needs the place the objects occupy — both arrangements valid
  • Mishna (Rav Ḥisda): needs the basket itself — only bottom-pure arrangement justified
  • Same law, different factual scenarios

🍴 Abaye & Rava — The Art of the Permitted Workaround

The Problem: Important sages need to move items that are muktzeh (set-aside) on Shabbat — direct handling is forbidden.
↓
Abaye's Method: Places a spoon (a permitted, useful object) on top of bundles of produce before moving them. The spoon renders the whole assembly moveable.
↓
Rava's Method: Places a knife (permitted) on a slaughtered young dove. The knife's presence permits moving the dove along with it.
↓
Rav Yosef's Reaction: 'How sharp is this halakha!' — a sarcastic remark implying the workarounds seem contrived or overly clever.
↓
Their Justification: Abaye: bundles are normally fit to lean on — only his dignity prevents him from doing so directly. Rava: the dove is fit to be eaten raw — it has intrinsic non-muktzeh status.
↓
Broader Principle (from Rava): Even without the knife, the dove may be moved to feed a cat — anything fit for any living creature's use is not muktzeh. The knife was only needed for an important person's dignity.
