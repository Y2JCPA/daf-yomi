📐 Carrying Measures: Three Material Shiurim

🦴 Bone

  • Rabbis: enough to make a spoon
  • R. Yehuda: enough to make a key
  • Ulla resolves: R. Yehuda meant the teeth of a key, not the full key
  • Conclusion: both opinions reflect the same measure

🫙 Glass

  • Mishna: enough to scrape the top of a bobbin
  • Tosefta (variant): enough to fill a crack in a small furnace
  • Also cited: fill a hole in a small earthenware pot
  • All represent a very small minimum

🪨 Pebble / Stone

  • Rabbis: enough to throw at a bird to chase it away
  • R. Elazar b. Ya'akov: enough to throw at a person (larger)
  • Bathroom use: minimum 3 stones may be moved on Shabbat
  • Size dispute: nut-bulk (R. Meir) vs. egg-bulk (R. Yehuda)

🪨 What Is the Measure for Bathroom Stones?

Zunin (to the Sages)
My teachers — how many bathroom stones may a person move on Shabbat for wiping purposes, and what is their required measure?
The Sages
Only three stones of appropriate size may be moved on Shabbat for bathroom use — no more.
Rabbi Meir
Each stone must be at least the size of a nut-bulk (כאגוז) — that is sufficient for bathroom use.
Rabbi Yehuda
Each stone must be at least the size of an egg-bulk (כביצה) — a larger quantity is required.
Rafram bar Pappa (Rav Ḥisda)
This follows the standard pattern: wherever Rabbi Meir rules nut-bulk, Rabbi Yehuda rules egg-bulk — a consistent halakhic difference between them.
Baraita Three sharpened stones (שלשה אבנים משופות) may be taken into the bathroom on Shabbat to fulfill one's needs with dignity.

🚫 Wiping Materials: Permitted vs. Prohibited

✅ Smooth stones — the standard permitted material; three may be moved on Shabbat as a set
↓
✅ Rain-cleansed used stones — Abaye to Rav Yosef: if rain fell and obscured prior use, it is treated like a fresh stone and is permitted
↓
⚠️ Stones with grass growing on them — Reish Lakish: wiping is permitted, but one who detaches the grass unwittingly is liable to bring a sin-offering
↓
❌ Earthenware shards — Rabbi Yoḥanan: prohibited on Shabbat due to (1) danger of sharp edges, (2) removal of hairs as a forbidden melacha, and (3) risk of witchcraft
↓
❌ Payis (soft Babylonian earthen clods) — Rav Yehuda / Rava: prohibited; manipulating these clods on Shabbat is forbidden

🏺 Why Is Wiping with a Shard Prohibited?

Rabbi Yoḥanan: Prohibited to wipe with an earthenware shard (חרש) on Shabbat — but what is the reason?
↓
Reason 1 — Rejected
Danger from sharp edges
Rejected: if so, it should be forbidden even on weekdays — but it is not
Reason 2 — Accepted
Removal of hairs
Scraping = forbidden melacha on Shabbat
Rava: this is Rabbi Yoḥanan's true reason
Reason 3 — Story
Witchcraft
A matron attempted a spell via a shard against Rav Ḥisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna on a boat
↓
Halakha: Prohibited — primarily for hair removal (melacha) and the danger of sorcery; the sharp-edges reason was rejected

👑 Kavod HaBriot — Human Dignity as a Halakhic Override

גָּדוֹל כְּבוֹד הַבְּרִיּוֹת שֶׁדּוֹחֶה לֹא תַּעֲשֶׂה שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה
Great Principle Rabba bar Rav Sheila raised a dilemma before Rav Ḥisda: What is the halakha regarding carrying bathroom stones up to a roof-bathroom on Shabbat — is it permitted despite the effort and carrying involved? Rav Ḥisda ruled: It is permitted. The reason: גָּדוֹל כְּבוֹד הַבְּרִיּוֹת שֶׁדּוֹחֶה לֹא תַּעֲשֶׂה שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה — great is human dignity, for it overrides a negative rabbinic commandment. Though muktzeh and carrying restrictions normally apply, the Sages did not intend their decrees to compromise basic human dignity. This principle recurs throughout the Talmud as one of the most powerful leniencies in rabbinic law.
