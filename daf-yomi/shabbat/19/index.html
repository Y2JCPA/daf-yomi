🩸 Niddah 19 — Colors, Controversies, and the Source of Purity

חֲמִשָּׁה דָמִים טְמֵאִים בָּאִשָּׁה — אָדֹם, שָׁחוֹר, כְּקֶרֶן כַּרְכּוֹם, כְּמַיִם שֶׁנִּתְמַזְּגוּ בְּאֶרֶץ אֲדֻמָּה, כְּיַיִן מָזוּג
MISHNA FOUNDATION The mishna opens a new chapter: five colors of blood render a woman ritually impure. The gemara immediately asks — from where do we know that any blood in a woman can be pure? Rabbi Abbahu derives it from a doubled plural in the Torah. Debates follow over green blood, Beit Shammai's additions, and exactly which shade of red qualifies.

🎨 The Five Colors — And the Disputes Around Them

The Mishna's Five (Impure)

  • 🔴 Red — like blood from a wound
  • ⚫ Black — like ḥeret (deep black pitch)
  • 🌼 Karkom — bright crocus/saffron color
  • 🟤 Earth-water — Beit Kerem valley red soil pooling
  • 🍷 Diluted wine — two parts water, one part wine

Beit Shammai Add (Impure)

  • 🌿 Fenugreek-soaked water — impure
  • 🥩 Liquid dripping from roast meat — impure
  • Total: 7 types impure
  • They may reject Rabbi Abbahu's derivation — or apply it differently

Beit Hillel Rule Pure

  • 🌿 Fenugreek-soaked water — pure
  • 🥩 Liquid from roast meat — pure
  • Only the mishna's 5 are impure
  • Practical difference from first tanna: whether to leave in abeyance

🌿 Is Green Blood Impure? — Four-Way Dispute

Akavya ben Mahalalel
Green blood is impure as niddah blood. It fully transmits the impurity of a menstruating woman — I either reject Rabbi Abbahu's derivation or count green among the four.
The Rabbis
Green blood is pure regarding niddah. Impure fluids must resemble saliva — gathered then dispersed. Green blood does not share that character.
Rabbi Meir
Even if not impure as niddah blood, green blood should render food susceptible to ritual impurity as one of the seven liquids — derived by verbal analogy from Song of Songs: 'like a scarlet thread are your lips… your mouth is lovely.'
(yeraq = greenish liquid mentioned in both contexts)
Rabbi Yosei
Neither applies. Green blood is not niddah blood nor does it render food susceptible. The verbal analogy Rabbi Meir uses cannot be applied without a received tradition.
HALAKHIC PRINCIPLE A gezerah shavah (verbal analogy) cannot be derived independently — it requires a received tradition. A kal va-ḥomer (a fortiori), by contrast, may be derived on one's own.

📜 From Where Do We Know Pure Blood Exists?

Question: Perhaps all blood that exits a woman is impure — why does the mishna imply some is pure?
↓
Deut. 17:8 — 'If a matter is too hard… between blood and blood' → implies pure and impure blood exist side by side
↓
Objection: That verse is about the rebellious elder (zaken mamre) — blood there means different legal contexts (menstrual, birth, Zavah), not purity vs. impurity!
↓
Answer: All three categories (blood/plea/leprous mark) must contain real distinctions a judge can dispute — hence pure blood must exist for the elder to rule on
↓
Rabbi Abbahu's Derivation
'Dameha' (Lev. 12:7) + 'Dameha' (Lev. 20:18) — plural × 2 = 4 types impure, all others pure
But the mishna lists 5!
Rabbi Ḥanina: Black is darkened red — not a separate color type. Still only 4 distinct categories. Confirmed in baraita.

⚫ Black Blood: A Transformation, Not a Separate Type

שָׁחוֹר כְּחֵרֶת — אִם שָׁחוֹר מִמֶּנּוּ טָמֵא, אִם מְרֻקָּע מִמֶּנּוּ טָמֵא
BARAITA TEACHING The mishna lists black as its own color, yet Rabbi Ḥanina (confirmed in a baraita) teaches it is simply red blood that has darkened. The halakhic scale: if blacker than ḥeret → impure; if lighter than ḥeret (trending red) → impure; the name 'black' covers the entire dark-red spectrum. This preserves Rabbi Abbahu's count of only four distinct impure colors derived from the doubled plural 'dameha.'

Practical rule: when in doubt whether blood is too dark or too light, it remains impure — the threshold of ḥeret is the anchor point.
