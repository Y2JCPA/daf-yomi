🪨 Stone vs. Grasses — Which to Wipe With on Shabbat?

Rav Ḥisda
Wipe with the stone, not with grasses. Grasses must be detached from the ground to use — this risks an act of uprooting (תּוֹלֵשׁ), a Shabbat violation. The stone poses no such risk.
Rav Hamnuna
Wipe with the grasses, not with the stone. The stone may injure him and cause bleeding. Bodily harm overrides the concern about uprooting, which may not apply here.
Undisputed Rule Both agree: an earthenware shard is always avoided — it will certainly injure and cause bleeding. Rav Huna states this explicitly (82a).

🚶 Remedies for Difficulty Relieving Oneself

Spiritual warning: One who needs to defecate and does not — Rav Ḥisda says an evil spirit dominates him; Ravina says an odor of filth dominates him.
↓
Rav Ḥisda's remedy: Stand and sit, stand and sit repeatedly — physical movement stimulates the bowels.
↓
Rav Ḥanan of Neharde'a: Move to the sides — attempt to relieve oneself in a different direction or body position.
↓
Baraita — before a long meal: Pace four cubits ten times, or alternatively ten cubits four times, to prepare and stimulate the digestive system.
↓
After the meal: Walk four cubits before lying down — aids digestion and prevents harmful retention.

🏺 Three Opinions: Minimum Measure of an Earthenware Shard (Shabbat Liability)

Rabbi Yehuda

  • Measure: enough to place between one pillar and another when stacked on the ground
  • Function: separating/spacing building materials
  • Gemara implies: the smallest of the three measures

Rabbi Meir

  • Measure: enough to rake hot coals (לַחְתּוֹת אֶת הָאֵשׁ)
  • Function: stoking or moving fire — requires a sturdy, cupped shard
  • Linked to verse: to scoop fire from the hearth (Isaiah 30:14)

Rabbi Yosei

  • Measure: enough to hold a quarter-log (רְבִיעִית) of liquid
  • Derived from Ezekiel: potsherds used to draw water
  • Gemara concludes: the greatest measure of the three

🛐 Idolatry Transmits Impurity by Carrying — Like a Niddah

תִּזְרֵם כְּמוֹ דָוָה — צֵא תֹּאמַר לוֹ (ישעיהו ל:כב)
Rabbi Akiva's Derivation (Mishna, 82a) From where is it derived that an idol transmits impurity to one who carries it — even without contact — just as a niddah (menstruating woman) does? From the verse: 'You shall cast them away as a niddah' (Isaiah 30:22). The Torah juxtaposes idolatry to a niddah: just as a niddah transmits carrying impurity (טֻמְאַת מַשָּׂא) to whoever lifts her, so too does an idol transmit impurity to whoever carries it — even without direct skin contact.

⚖️ Rabba vs. Rabbi Elazar: The Nature of the Dispute on Idolatry's Impurity

Rabba
Everyone agrees an idol transmits carrying impurity (נְשִׂיאָה) — the niddah juxtaposition is unambiguous. The dispute between Rabbi Akiva and the Sages concerns tent impurity (טֻמְאַת אֹהֶל): does an idol render impure those beneath the same roof? Rabbi Akiva: yes. Sages: no.
Rabbi Elazar
Everyone agrees that a very heavy idol that cannot be lifted does not transmit impurity — carrying requires actual movement. The dispute is about carrying impurity itself. Rabbi Akiva: the idol transmits it. Sages: it does not — the niddah verse is only a metaphor for rejection.
The Creeping Animal (שֶׁרֶץ) Juxtaposition According to Rabbi Akiva, the idol is also juxtaposed to a sheretz (creeping animal). Since carrying impurity is already derived from the niddah verse, the sheretz juxtaposition teaches a separate law — possibly regarding the severity or scope of defilement.
