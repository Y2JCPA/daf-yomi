🌿 Niddah 47 — From Dough to Womanhood

פגה, בוחל, צנומה — Song of Songs 2:13
Two Worlds in One Daf The daf opens with a halakhic debate on whether ḥalla and teruma apply today by Torah or rabbinic law — resolved by Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua, who proves the two obligations have independent conditions. The daf then pivots to the famous Mishna comparing the three stages of female development to figs: paga (unripe), bohal (ripening), tzenuma (ripe). An extended discussion covers the signs of grown womanhood (bogeret) and concludes with the laws of the saris and aylonit, plus a dispute about how to calculate legal years.

🫓 Ḥalla Today: Torah Law or Rabbinic?

First View
Teruma today is by Torah law, and ḥalla today is likewise Torah law — the two obligations rise and fall together.
Rav Huna b. Rav Yehoshua
On the contrary — even if teruma is Torah law, ḥalla is only rabbinic. Proof: during the 7 years of conquering and 7 years of dividing Eretz Yisrael, ḥalla applied but teruma did not. The obligations are independent.
Baraita
ḥalla applies by Torah law only when the entire Jewish people dwells in Eretz Yisrael. Since not all Jews returned with Ezra, Second Temple ḥalla is rabbinic only.
Resolution ḥalla in the present era is rabbinic even for those who hold teruma is biblical — they have different conditions for full Torah obligation and cannot be compared.

🌿 The Fig Tree Parable — Three Stages of Development

פגה — Paga (Unripe Fig)

  • Stage: Minor (Ketana)
  • Under 12 years old
  • Father controls: earnings, lost objects, vows
  • Source: 'The fig tree puts forth her green fruits' — Song of Songs 2:13

בוחל — Bohal (Ripening Fig)

  • Stage: Young Woman (Na'ara)
  • 12 years + one day through 12½ years
  • Father retains control during this period
  • Transition: rights begin shifting toward independence

צנומה — Tzenuma (Ripe Fig)

  • Stage: Grown Woman (Bogeret)
  • 12½ years + physical signs of maturity
  • Father loses all control
  • She controls earnings, vows, and her own marriage

👤 Saris & Aylonit — How Status Is Determined

Person reaches age 20
without developing two pubic hairs
↓
Male
Bring proof of age → status of saris (sexually underdeveloped man)
Exempt from levirate marriage and ḥalitza
Female
Bring proof of age → status of aylonit (sexually underdeveloped woman)
Her kiddushin is rabbinic; different levirate rules
↓
Rav (via Rav Shmuel bar Yitzḥak): If physical signs of saris appear before age 20, he is classified as saris from that point — age 20 is not required
↓
Rabbi Ḥiyya: If no signs appear at all, he remains a minor until most of his years pass — i.e., until age 35 (majority of a 70-year lifespan)

📅 Calculating Years — Rav vs. Ulla

The Question: When the Torah specifies a number of years (sacrificial animal, Hebrew slave, walled city house, ages of maturity), are these counted as whole years (date to date) or calendar years (any day in the final year counts)?
↓
Rav: All years in this chapter are calculated from the exact date (e.g., birth) until that same date in the specified year — whole years required.
↓
Ulla: Years are calendar years — entering the relevant year suffices. This explains why earlier Mishnayot (44b, 45a) add 'and one day': a day into the new year is needed to push into it.
↓
Scriptural proof (Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov): 'A lamb in its first year' (Lev. 12:6) — implies a complete year from birth, not merely entering a calendar year.
↓
Baraita (Rabbi Yosei ben Keifar): 'The twentieth year of which thirty days have passed' — demonstrates that a partial year is insufficient; whole years are required, supporting Rav.
