Shabbat 29 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yochanan, what is the legal significance of the head emerging among the limbs of a piecemeal fetus?
A. It has no special significance — a numerical majority of limbs is always required
B. The head's emergence exempts the remaining limbs entirely from further counting
C. The head counts as equivalent to most of the limbs, thereby establishing birth
D. The head only counts if the temples have fully cleared
Correct: C — Rabbi Yochanan rules that if the head is among the emerged limbs, it is equivalent to most limbs — the head alone establishes birth even without a numeric majority of the body.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What anatomical landmark does Rabbi Yosei identify as 'most of the head' for a normal headfirst birth?
A. The forehead
B. The cheekbones
C. The crown of the skull
D. The temples
Correct: D — Rabbi Yosei defines 'most of the head' as when the temples emerge — this is the point at which the widest part of the head has passed, and birth is legally established.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the core disagreement between Rav Pappa and Rav Zevid about the baraita's language?
A. Rav Pappa reads 'piecemeal OR reversed' as two separate cases; Rav Zevid reads it as 'piecemeal AND reversed' — one combined scenario
B. Rav Pappa follows Beit Hillel and Rav Zevid follows Beit Shammai
C. Rav Pappa discusses animals; Rav Zevid discusses human births
D. Rav Pappa requires temple emergence; Rav Zevid requires cheekbone emergence
Correct: A — Rav Pappa reads 'or' as two distinct cases, implying normal delivery only needs head emergence (supporting Rabbi Yochanan). Rav Zevid insists both conditions must occur together, so normal delivery always requires most of the head.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does the Gemara distinguish Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's 'river discharge' ruling from the mishna's unknown-fetus case?
A. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's ruling applies only to kohanim
B. The mishna discusses an animal; Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi discusses a human
C. The mishna involves a woman not previously presumed pregnant; Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's case involves a confirmed pregnancy where majority applies
D. The mishna requires two witnesses; Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi relies on one
Correct: C — The mishna's woman was not previously known to be pregnant, so no presumption of viability can be applied. In Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's case, pregnancy was confirmed, allowing the majority rule (most fetuses are fully formed) to operate.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why is the 'forgetful woman' prohibited from intercourse during the fourth week even though she sees blood during that time?
A. All blood seen in this period is definitely niddah blood
B. Beit Shammai require an extra seven days of waiting after any visible blood
C. Each day's blood might be the end of her purity period, making the following day potentially impure — the uncertainty cannot be resolved day by day
D. The fourth week falls within the forty-day period where all blood is forbidden
Correct: C — During the fourth week there is daily uncertainty: any given day's blood could mark the final day of her purity period, rendering the next day forbidden. Since the specific day cannot be identified, she remains prohibited throughout the entire week.
