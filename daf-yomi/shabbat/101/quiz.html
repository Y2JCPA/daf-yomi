QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yehuda, if a boat is 10 handbreadths deep but its rim does not rise 10 handbreadths above the water surface, what carrying rule applies?
A. Carrying in both directions — into and out of the boat — is fully permitted
B. Carrying from the boat out to the sea is permitted, but not from the sea into the boat
C. Carrying in both directions is forbidden because the boat lacks proper walls
D. Carrying from the sea into the boat is permitted, but not from the boat to the sea
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda rules asymmetrically: one may carry from the boat (a private domain) out to the sea, but not from the sea (a karmelit) into the boat. The private-domain-to-karmelit direction is more lenient than bringing something from a karmelit into a private domain.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why did Rav Huna rule that carrying in the small Meishan boats is limited to only four cubits?
A. The boats are too short lengthwise to qualify as a domain
B. Their bottoms are narrower than four handbreadths, so they lack the floor area required for private domain status
C. Meishan boats have no partition walls whatsoever
D. They sit too high above the water to be considered enclosed
Correct: B — Meishan boats are wide on top but narrow at the bottom — less than four handbreadths wide at the base. A private domain requires a floor area of at least four by four handbreadths. Without this, the boat cannot be classified as a private domain, so the default rule of four cubits applies.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the Gemara's conclusion about whether fish passing through a water partition invalidate it?
A. Fish passage fully invalidates the partition, just like a breach animals walk through
B. Fish passage invalidates the partition unless it is closed with a net
C. Fish passage does not invalidate a water partition — the Sages instituted a special leniency for water
D. Fish passage only matters for impurity law, not for Shabbat domain law
Correct: C — The Gemara concludes that the passage of fish does not constitute a breach (פירצה) in a water partition. This is a Rabbinic leniency specific to water domains. From this ruling we derive that 'passage of fish is not considered [a breach of] a partition.'
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What important correction did Rav Safra make to the mishna's ruling about tied boats?
A. He ruled that tied boats always require two separate eiruvim, not one
B. He clarified that the mishna permits carrying only directly from one boat to the other — not by way of a small intermediate boat
C. He said that mats between boats are never needed when boats are tied
D. He ruled that the connection must be with a rope, not a string
Correct: B — Rav Safra corrected Rava's interpretation, noting that the mishna permits carrying only directly between the two tied boats. A small boat situated between them retains its own separate domain status and may not serve as a stepping-stone for carrying.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the key distinction Shmuel draws between a cloak-string tying two boats (for Shabbat) and a rope connecting a boat to the public domain (for tumah)?
A. A cloak-string is valid for neither Shabbat domains nor tumah — only a proper rope counts
B. Any string works for both Shabbat domains and tumah equally
C. For Shabbat domains, even a minimal cloak-string suffices; but for transmitting tumah to the public domain, only a rope capable of holding the boat and extending into that domain creates the connection
D. Tumah connection requires the rope to be tied by a scholar; Shabbat domains do not
Correct: C — Shmuel teaches that the threshold for merging Shabbat domains is very low — a string strong enough to hold the boats is sufficient. However, the tumah connection to the public domain (based on Numbers 19:16) requires a functional rope that actually extends into that domain, representing a higher standard.
