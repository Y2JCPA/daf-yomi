QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Yehuda's version of the mishna, what is the ruling when one circumcised the baby designated for after Shabbat, on Shabbat?
A. Liable — the act itself triggers the sin-offering obligation
B. Exempt — he was preoccupied with performing a mitzva
C. Exempt — circumcision always overrides Shabbat regardless of which baby
D. Liable only if he knew it was the wrong baby
Correct: B — Rav Yehuda taught the first clause as 'Exempt,' based on Rabbi Meir's baraita: since the circumcisor was preoccupied with a mitzva (even the wrong one), Rabbi Yehoshua holds him exempt — unlike idolatry where no mitzva preoccupation exists.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
A baby is born at twilight on Friday night (Shabbat eve). On which day is he circumcised?
A. Day 8 — circumcision overrides Shabbat in all cases
B. Day 9 — doubtful birth day pushes it off one day
C. Day 10 — Day 9 would fall on Shabbat; this is not a definite Shabbat baby
D. Day 11 — all doubtful cases wait until after the Festival
Correct: C — Born at twilight before Shabbat: Day 8 may be Shabbat or Sunday (uncertain). Day 9 would be Shabbat — but since this baby's Shabbat circumcision is in doubt, it cannot override Shabbat. Therefore circumcision is pushed to Day 10.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is required for peri'ah, and what happens if it is omitted?
A. Peri'ah is optional; the circumcision is valid without it
B. Peri'ah means removing extra flesh; omitting it requires a blessing but no redo
C. Peri'ah means folding back the inner membrane; without it the circumcision is void
D. Peri'ah applies only to adult converts, not infant circumcision
Correct: C — The mishna states explicitly: if one circumcised but did not perform peri'ah (fold back the thin inner membrane beneath the foreskin), it is as if he did not circumcise at all. The act must be redone.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does the Gemara rule on the required waiting period after a baby recovers from fever?
A. Three days — matching the standard recovery principle
B. Seven calendar days from the day fever broke
C. Seven complete 24-hour periods, time-to-time
D. No waiting required — once the fever breaks, circumcision may proceed
Correct: C — The Gemara rejects the analogy to birth (where only 8 calendar days are needed) and concludes that recovery from illness is stricter: one must wait seven complete periods, from the exact time of recovery to the same time seven days later.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the Gemara's explanation for why Rabbi Eliezer permits suspending a strainer on a Festival?
A. Rabbi Eliezer holds that suspending a strainer is not a prohibited labor at all
B. Rabbi Eliezer agrees it is tent-building but follows a lenient view on food-preparation labors on Festivals, without distinguishing whether it could have been done before
C. Rabbi Eliezer rules only that adding to an existing tent is permitted, not constructing a new one
D. Rabbi Eliezer permits all rabbinic prohibitions to be waived on Festivals for any need
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that Rabbi Eliezer acknowledges the tent-building issue but adopts Rabbi Yehuda's principle that food-preparation labors are permitted on Festivals — and extends it further than Rabbi Yehuda by not requiring that the act was impossible to perform before the Festival.
