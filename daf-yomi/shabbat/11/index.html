🩸 Rav vs. Levi: One Source or Two?

Rav
One source: post-birth blood and blood of purity both originate from the same place. The Torah divided them by decree — impure first, then pure. Therefore a woman in her purity period need not examine herself.
Levi
Two distinct sources: impure post-birth blood flows from one channel, blood of purity from another. The impure source could reactivate — she should examine herself at the transition.
Gemara
The mishna exempts a woman observing the purity period from examination. This fits Rav well. Per Levi, she should examine herself — unless we say she is already within the purity period, so the impure source has definitively ceased.
Gemara
The one-source view aligns with Beit Shammai (35b) — but can an unattributed mishna follow Beit Shammai? The Gemara resolves: the mishna says 'who is observing,' not 'anticipating' — she is already inside the purity period.
Halakhic Impact Whether a post-birth woman in her dam tohar period needs daily self-examination hinges entirely on this dispute about anatomical sources.

⚠️ Combined Day + Jump Cycle
If jumps always occur on the same weekday, she may have a combined cycle (days + jumping). Rav Ashi: lest we say the day alone caused it.

🍼 Post-Birth Blood: Sources and Exemptions

Rav
Blood after birth and blood of purity flow from one source. Torah deems the first impure and the second pure. Once she enters the purity period, the impure source is sealed — no examination needed.
Levi
Two separate sources: the impure channel and the pure channel are anatomically distinct. The impure source could reopen — examination should be required at the end of the post-birth period.
Beit Shammai
Only one source exists for both blood types (see 35b) — consistent with the mishna's blanket exemption from examination during purity period.
Beit Hillel
Two sources — aligning with Levi. Yet the Gemara resolves that even per Levi, once she is already in the purity period (not merely anticipating it), the impure source has ceased.
Gemara's Resolution The mishna's phrasing — 'who is observing' (not 'about to observe') the purity period — avoids the problem for both Rav and Levi.

📋 Daily Examination: Who, When, and Why

All women with a fixed menstrual cycle must examine themselves twice daily: in the morning (to detect nighttime bleeding) and at twilight (to detect daytime bleeding)
↓
Exception 1 — Niddah: A menstruating woman's impure status is already established. Examination is unnecessary — it is designed to detect impurity, not confirm it.
↓
Exception 2 — Dam Tohar period: A post-birth woman in her purity period is in a known-pure state. Per the mishna (supported by Rav), no examination is required.
↓
Exception 3 — Virgin: Per Beit Hillel, her blood is attributed to the torn hymen while she continues bleeding from intercourse. Examination would be inconclusive.
↓
Before intercourse: Shmuel rules that even a woman without a fixed cycle must examine herself before relations — she may not engage in intercourse without prior bedikah.

🛏️ Examination Cloths: Required vs. Exempt

Uses Examination Cloths

  • Woman with a fixed cycle — uses bedikah cloths during every instance of intercourse
  • Woman without a fixed cycle — must examine herself before relations; intercourse without prior examination is forbidden (Shmuel)
  • Woman approaching her expected veset — heightened caution required near anticipated onset

Exempt from Cloths / Examination

  • Menstruating woman (niddah) — intercourse is entirely forbidden; cloths are irrelevant
  • Dam tohar period — known pure state; no concern about niddah onset per the mishna
  • Virgin (Beit Hillel) — blood attributed to hymen wound while she consistently bleeds from intercourse; examination would be inconclusive
