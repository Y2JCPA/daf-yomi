QUESTION 1 OF 5
How does Rava prove that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi views Rabbi Yosei's twilight as halakhically uncertain (not definitively night)?
A. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi explicitly states it in a separate ruling
B. From both clauses of his ruling: one requires examination during Rabbi Yosei's period to confirm clean status; the other makes her concern dependent on whether she examined during it
C. Rava found a contradicting baraita that proves this
D. Rabbi Yosei himself stated that his twilight has uncertain status
Correct: B — Rava analyzes both clauses of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's ruling. In the 'examined' clause, she must have checked during Rabbi Yosei's twilight — proving it is not definitively night (otherwise why check?). In the 'did not examine' clause, lack of examination during it creates concern — proving it is not definitively day either.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the corrected position of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar regarding a stain on a garment and retroactive impurity?
A. She renders herself and all touched items impure retroactively from laundering
B. She renders consecrated items impure from laundering, but not herself
C. She does not even render consecrated items impure retroactively from laundering — only items touched from the moment of discovery
D. She renders nothing impure — a stain alone is not sufficient
Correct: C — The Gemara corrects an initial misreading. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's true position is that a stain does NOT retroactively render consecrated items impure from the time of laundering. Only items touched from the very moment the stain was discovered are rendered impure — a more lenient position than the Rabbis.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, when may a woman attribute her stain to subsequent bleeding?
A. Any time within 24 hours of finding the stain
B. Only if she bled within 12 hours of the stain
C. Only if the blood appeared on the same calendar day she discovered the stain
D. Only within 7 days of the stain
Correct: C — Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar permits attribution only when the blood appears on the same calendar day as the stain discovery. The Rabbis are more lenient, allowing attribution within a full 24-hour period. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi notes that Rabbi Shimon's ruling actually benefits her ziva count while the Rabbis' 24-hour rule can cause harm.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What does Rav Hisda teach about a woman who experiences bleeding during twilight on the eleventh day of her zava days?
A. She is definitively a niddah and begins counting seven days
B. She is definitively a zava and must count seven clean days
C. She is tahor since the doubt cannot be resolved
D. She must be concerned both that it is the beginning of her seven niddah days AND the end of her eleven zava days
Correct: D — Rav Hisda clarifies the mishna: because twilight is uncertain, she must simultaneously hold both possibilities. She cannot begin counting clean days for either status and cannot immerse until both doubts are resolved — an essentially impossible situation requiring maximum stringency.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What was Rabbi Yehoshua's critique of the Rabbis' approach to twilight-bleeding uncertainties?
A. Their rulings were too lenient and should be made stricter
B. They should focus on remedying the difficult uncertainties facing careful, knowledgeable women rather than addressing careless ones
C. They ignored the case of post-birth bleeding entirely
D. Rabbi Yehoshua held that twilight bleeding is always treated as daytime
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehoshua challenged the Rabbis with rhetorical force: rather than expending effort on provisions for 'misguided' women who don't track their cycles, they should address the complex halakhic traps created for careful, competent women who know their cycle but face insoluble doubts from bleeding during bein hashmashot.
