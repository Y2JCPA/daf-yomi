🔍 What Do 'Volitional' and 'Unvolitional' Actually Mean?

First Reading
Volitional = one is actively pleased with the liquid emerging.
Unvolitional = indeterminate — one neither seeks nor opposes it.
Gemara's Rejection
No! Volitional includes even indeterminate preference.
Unvolitional = only where one explicitly stated: "I do not want this liquid."
⚖️ Stakes of the Debate The stricter reading extends Shabbat prohibition to ambiguous intent. The lenient reading restricts prohibition only to cases of clear desire — silence alone does not make liquid forbidden.

🍋 Pomegranates and the Role of Local Custom

Is squeezing pomegranates a prohibited melacha on Shabbat?
↓
Key evidence: House of Menashya bar Menaḥem routinely squeezed pomegranates on weekdays
↓
Does one individual's practice establish a fruit as a 'juice fruit'?
↓
Rav Naḥman's defense
Yes — regional customs (e.g., Arabia for diverse kinds) ground halakha in actual practice
Gemara's rebuttal
Arabia is a whole region; one household is not comparable! Individual practice cannot determine halakha alone.
↓
Rav Naḥman's true basis: Rav Ḥisda's principle — pomegranate juice is a significant liquid → squeezing prohibited

🛁 Liquids That Invalidate a Ritual Bath (Mikveh)

Wine & Vinegar

  • Universally agreed to invalidate mikveh
  • Must change the mikveh's appearance
  • Classic case in Tractate Mikvaot
  • Treated as significant liquids by all

Olive Discharge (מי גרעינים)

  • Sap from olive bale after pressing
  • Changes mikveh color → invalidates
  • Rabbis: treated as a liquid (mashkeh)
  • Rabbi Shimon: not inherently a liquid — Sages enacted a special decree

Beet Juice (Rav Ḥisda)

  • Squeezed beets placed into mikveh
  • Invalidates if mikveh changes color
  • Cannot constitute mikveh ab initio
  • Rav Pappa: core rule — any such liquid invalidates

🌿 Rabbi Shimon vs. Rabbis: The Nature of Olive Discharge

Rabbis
Olive discharge is a liquid (mashkeh). Once it emerges from the bale of the olive press, it renders foods susceptible to ritual impurity and can invalidate a mikveh.
Rabbi Shimon
Olive discharge is not truly a liquid. When the Sages said bale-derived olive sap renders food susceptible, it was a special gezeira — a rabbinic decree — not because it is inherently a mashkeh.
💡 Where They Agree Both agree: olive sap still inside the olive cannot render food susceptible. Olive sap that has emerged from the bale can. The dispute is about the theoretical underpinning — and thus affects edge cases not explicitly covered.
