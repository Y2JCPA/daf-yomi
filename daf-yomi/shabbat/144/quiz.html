QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Shmuel, what is the ruling when one squeezes a cluster of grapes on Shabbat into a pot that already contains food?
A. Prohibited — squeezing any fruit is forbidden regardless of destination
B. Permitted — it is treated as adding one food to another, not extracting liquid
C. Permitted only if the grapes were already cut before Shabbat
D. Depends on whether the juice alters the food's flavor
Correct: B — Shmuel rules this is permitted because squeezing juice onto food in a pot is viewed as combining two foods — the classic prohibition of squeezing (סחיטה) applies only when pure liquid is extracted into an empty vessel (Amud B).
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What ultimately justifies the halakhic prohibition of squeezing pomegranates on Shabbat, according to Rav Naḥman's final reasoning?
A. The widespread Arabian custom of squeezing pomegranates
B. Rabbi Yehuda's general ruling that all fruit juice is prohibited
C. Rav Ḥisda's principle that pomegranate juice is a significant liquid
D. The practice of the house of Menashya bar Menaḥem alone
Correct: C — After the Gemara rejects the comparison to Arabia (an entire region vs. one household), Rav Naḥman's ruling is grounded in Rav Ḥisda's principle: pomegranate juice is a significant liquid that invalidates a mikveh, confirming its status as a prohibited juice-fruit (Amud B).
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Rav Ḥisda's ruling regarding beet juice placed in a ritual bath (mikveh)?
A. It always invalidates the mikveh, even without changing its color
B. It never invalidates the mikveh since beet juice is considered food
C. It invalidates the mikveh only if it changes the mikveh's appearance
D. It is only prohibited if more than three logs of juice were added
Correct: C — Rav Ḥisda rules that beet juice invalidates a mikveh if it changes the mikveh's color, because beet juice is a liquid with which one cannot construct a mikveh ab initio — and Rav Pappa identifies this as the operative principle (Amud B).
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does the Gemara ultimately define 'unvolitionally' (שלא לדעת) with regard to liquid seeping from fruit?
A. The liquid emerged while the person was asleep or unaware
B. The person's preference was entirely indeterminate — neither desired nor opposed
C. The person explicitly stated that he does not want the liquid
D. The liquid was expressed by a Gentile on the Jew's behalf
Correct: C — The Gemara's final ruling is that 'unvolitionally' refers specifically to a case where the person openly declared he does not want the liquid. Mere indifference or unstated preference does not qualify as unvolitional — it counts as volitional (Amud A).
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the core disagreement between Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis regarding olive discharge (מי גרעינים)?
A. The Rabbis say olive discharge is not a liquid; Rabbi Shimon says it is
B. Rabbi Shimon holds it is not truly a liquid; the Rabbis treat it as a liquid
C. Both treat it as a liquid but disagree on whether it invalidates a mikveh
D. They disagree only about whether it may be consumed on Shabbat
Correct: B — Rabbi Shimon maintains that olive discharge is not inherently a liquid — the Sages' decree that bale-derived olive sap renders food susceptible to impurity is a special rabbinic enactment, not a recognition of its liquid nature. The Rabbis treat it as a full liquid (Amud B).
