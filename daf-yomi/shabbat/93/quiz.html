QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yehuda, what is the ruling if two people who are each individually capable of carrying alone carry a heavy beam together on Shabbat?
A. Both are liable, since each had full capability
B. Both are exempt, since neither fully performed the prohibited act alone
C. Only the stronger of the two is liable
D. They are liable only if they intended to share the load equally
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda derives from the third exclusionary term in the verse that two capable people acting jointly are exempt — neither alone 'performed it,' and joint performance by capable individuals is explicitly excluded by the singular language.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
When one person is capable of carrying an object alone and the other is incapable, and they carry it together — who does Rav Ḥisda say is liable?
A. Both are liable
B. Neither is liable, since it was a joint act
C. Only the capable person is liable
D. Only the incapable person is liable, since they caused the capable one to act
Correct: C — Rav Ḥisda rules that the capable person is liable. He could have performed the forbidden act alone — the incapable partner's contribution is insubstantial and does not dilute the capable person's responsibility.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What does מְסַיֵּעַ אֵין בּוֹ מַמָּשׁ (an assisting factor is insubstantial) imply about garments placed under an animal while a zav rides it?
A. All garments beneath the animal become impure by zav-riding
B. Garments under the front legs are impure; garments under the rear legs, which merely assist, remain pure
C. No garments become impure unless the zav directly touches them
D. Only garments under the animal's belly are impure
Correct: B — Rabbi Yosei rules that the front legs bear the primary weight and transmit zav-impurity; the rear legs merely assist and are therefore legally insubstantial. This is the application of מְסַיֵּעַ אֵין בּוֹ מַמָּשׁ in purity law, cited by Rav Zevid and Rav Pappi in the name of Rava.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rabbi Meir counter the argument that the verse contains three exclusionary terms, which would leave no term available to hold two capable people liable?
A. He agrees there are three terms but interprets the third as referring to intent, not capability
B. He argues the verse contains only two exclusionary terms, so no third term exists to exempt joint capable carriers
C. He derives liability for two capable people from a separate verse in Deuteronomy
D. He rules that two capable people are liable only in the public domain, not the private
Correct: B — Rabbi Meir challenges the premise itself: the verse does not repeat singular language three times. With only two exclusionary terms, both are 'used up' excluding (1) this lifts/that places and (2) capable/incapable pairs — leaving two capable people fully liable.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to the Mishna at the end of Daf 93, if someone carries food below the minimum Shabbat measure in a vessel, what is the halakha regarding the vessel?
A. One is liable for carrying the vessel but not the food
B. One is liable for both, since the vessel was carried regardless
C. One is exempt even for the vessel, because it is secondary to the food
D. One is exempt only if the vessel is made of wood; metal vessels are always liable
Correct: C — The vessel is טְפֵלָה — secondary to the food. Since the food is below the minimum and carries no liability, the vessel cannot create independent liability. The Gemara adds: if the vessel was independently needed by the carrier, it can be liable on its own terms.
