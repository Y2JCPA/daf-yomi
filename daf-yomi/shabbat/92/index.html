🍈 Abaye vs. Rava — Carrying Fruit in Hand

Abaye
If one carries fruit out in his hand, he is liable — even if the rest of his body remained in the private domain. The hand's act of carrying is sufficient to create liability.
Rava
The mishna taught exemption for a basket of cucumbers and gourds because it is an abnormal method of carrying. An abnormal carry cannot create liability regardless of where the hand is.
Gemara
These opinions contradict their known positions elsewhere in the Talmud. Therefore — reverse the attributions: it is Rava who says carrying in the hand alone renders one liable.
📌 Redaction Note The Gemara explicitly reverses the names Abaye and Rava to maintain consistency with their established halakhic stances throughout the tractate.

🏛️ Tabernacle Precedent — Carrying Above 10 Handbreadths

וְעָשִׂיתָ אֶת-הַקְּרָשִׁים לַמִּשְׁכָּן עֲצֵי שִׁטִּים עֹמְדִים — שמות כ״ו:ט״ו
Rabbi Elazar's Ruling One who carries a burden from private to public domain, even above ten handbreadths (normally beyond the legal airspace of the public domain), is liable. The Levites carried the Tabernacle boards on their shoulders — boards that were 10 cubits (≈20 handbreadths) tall — necessarily raising them far above 10 handbreadths as they walked through the wilderness.

The Ark itself was 9 handbreadths + 1 handbreadth for the Ark-cover = 10 handbreadths, confirming the Levites carried above that threshold.

👥 Two Who Carried Together — Tanna'im Dispute

Rabbi Meir

  • Two who jointly carry a large mass are liable
  • Applies even when each person could carry it alone
  • Each individual participates in a complete prohibited labor
  • Joint performance does not reduce individual culpability

Rabbi Yehuda

  • Two who carry together are generally exempt
  • Exception: when neither could carry it alone
  • If each could carry it alone → each did only partial labor
  • If neither could do it alone → together they complete the melacha → liable

🪙 Coins in Money Belt — Baraita Analysis

Case: A person carries coins in a money belt with the opening facing upward, and the belt's opening faces behind him as he exits to the public domain
↓
Question: Is this considered intentional carrying-behind, or is the orientation incidental to how he walks?
↓
Rabbi Yehuda reports: I offered my argument to the Rabbis; they offered theirs. Neither side could refute the other's reasoning.
↓
Gemara resolves: Behind → behind (intention fulfilled) = everyone agrees he is liable. The tanna'im only dispute the cases where intention diverged from outcome.
↓
Principle: When action precisely matches intention, liability is unambiguous — the debate centers only on mismatched intention/outcome scenarios.
