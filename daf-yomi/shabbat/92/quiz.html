Shabbat 92 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the mishna on Amud A, which combination of carrying methods renders a person liable on Shabbat?
A. Only right hand or left hand
B. Only on one's shoulders or back
C. Right hand, left hand, lap, or shoulders
D. Any method, including unusual ones, as long as the object moves
Correct: C — The mishna explicitly lists right hand, left hand, lap (חֵיקוֹ), and shoulders as typical carrying methods that all render one liable, because these are standard human ways of transporting objects.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Rabbi Elazar rules one is liable for carrying above ten handbreadths. What is his primary proof?
A. A direct verse prohibiting carrying at any height
B. The Levites carrying the Tabernacle boards on their shoulders, raising them above 10 handbreadths
C. The height of the Ark of the Covenant which was exactly 10 handbreadths
D. A tradition from Moshe Rabbeinu about the wilderness camp
Correct: B — Rabbi Elazar derives this from the Levites, whose Tabernacle boards were 10 cubits tall — necessarily carried above 10 handbreadths — establishing that the Torah's paradigm of carrying includes above that height.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
In the mishna about intention (Amud B), a person intends to carry an object before him but it ends up behind him. What is the ruling and why?
A. Liable — the object still crossed into the public domain
B. Liable — intention is irrelevant once the act occurs
C. Exempt — his intention was not realized in the act performed
D. Exempt — carrying behind is never a prohibited act
Correct: C — He is exempt because his intention was not realized. Shabbat liability requires that the prohibited act be performed in the manner one intended — when the outcome diverges from the intent, the act is flawed and he is exempt.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yehuda's opinion in the final mishna, when do two people who jointly carried an object become liable?
A. Always, whenever two people carry something together
B. Never — shared labor always creates exemption
C. Only when neither person could have carried the object alone
D. Only when they explicitly agreed to carry together beforehand
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehuda holds that two are liable only when neither one could carry the object alone. In that case, their combined effort constitutes a complete prohibited labor. If each could carry it alone, both are exempt because neither individually completed the melacha.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is special about the case of a woman wearing a sinar that makes her liable regardless of which side the object ends up on?
A. Women have a stricter standard for all Shabbat laws
B. The sinar is classified as a public domain garment by default
C. The sinar wraps her body on both sides, so the object is covered by her garment either way
D. She is liable only because she acted intentionally in all cases
Correct: C — The Tosefta teaches that a sinar (a pants-like girdle) wraps the entire body. Therefore her garment covers both her front and back — the object is considered intentionally carried in her garment regardless of which side it rests on, rendering her liable in all cases.
