🚪 Open or Closed? The Alley Debate

Ben Beteira
One may rescue objects from fire into an open alley — even one without a post or lechi.
Rabbis
One may only rescue into a closed alley — defined as three walls plus one post.
Rabba
An alley with three walls and a post is already closed — it cannot be called 'open' at all!
Rav Ashi
They disagree on definitions: three walls + post = closed; three walls alone with no post = open.
Core Dispute What constitutes an 'open' alley for Shabbat carrying purposes — and does Rabbi Eliezer's leniency apply there?

⚖️ פסיק רישא — Rabbi Shimon's Concession

Rabbi Shimon
I generally permit actions that cause an unintended melachah (דבר שאינו מתכוין) — intention determines prohibition.
Abaye & Rava
Rabbi Shimon concedes in cases of psik reisha: cut off its head and it will not die? An inevitable result cannot be called unintentional.
Gemara
Application here: moving the hide of the Paschal lamb inevitably tans it — a forbidden melachah — so even Rabbi Shimon forbids it.
פסיק רישא When the forbidden outcome of an action is 100% inevitable, it is treated as fully intentional and prohibited by all opinions — even Rabbi Shimon.

🏛️ Base for Permitted vs. Forbidden Object

Torah Scroll Casing

  • Casing is a base for a permitted object
  • The Torah scroll itself may be moved on Shabbat
  • Therefore its casing may also be carried
  • ✅ Permitted — even if casing contains money, may be carried out to save the scroll
  • The incidental money does not override the permitted primary object

Hide of Paschal Lamb

  • Hide is a base for a forbidden object
  • The lamb's flesh is forbidden on Shabbat
  • Moving the hide serves only the forbidden meat
  • ❌ Prohibited — a base for a forbidden object is muktze
  • Plus: moving it inevitably causes tanning (psik reisha)

🍞 Shabbat Bread Customs — Rav Hisda & Rav Kahana

Rav Hisda's instruction: Rise early on Friday to prepare all Shabbat expenses and needs — as Scripture says: "On the sixth day they shall prepare that which they bring in" (Exodus 16:5)
↓
לחם משנה — Two Loaves: At each Shabbat meal one holds two complete loaves, commemorating the double portion of manna that fell on Friday in the wilderness.
↓
Rav Kahana's practice (reported by Rav Ashi): He took two loaves and broke only one — he did not break both simultaneously.
↓
Source verse: "They collected double the bread" — the obligation is to hold two loaves together, but one breaks only one for the HaMotzi blessing.
↓
Bread rescue hierarchy: If fine bread (פת נקייה) was already rescued, one may not then rescue coarse bread (הדרא). But if coarse bread was rescued first, one may add fine bread — upgrading is permitted.
