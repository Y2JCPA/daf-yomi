QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava, why did the Sages limit rescue from fire to food for only three meals?
A. Three meals is all that is halachically required on Shabbat, so no more is needed
B. The fire will naturally extinguish after consuming three meals' worth of fuel
C. A person agitated about property loss might extinguish the fire if permitted to save more
D. The Torah only permits carrying in a public domain a maximum of three times per Shabbat
Correct: C — Rava teaches that since a person is distraught about property loss, allowing rescue of more than three meals' worth might lead him to actively extinguish the fire — a Torah-level prohibition. The limit preemptively prevents this.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the disagreement between Ben Beteira and the Rabbis regarding rescue on Shabbat?
A. Whether Torah scrolls may be rescued at all on Shabbat
B. Ben Beteira permits rescuing into an open alley; the Rabbis restrict rescue to a closed alley
C. Whether food for animals may be rescued alongside food for humans
D. How many meals' worth of food may be rescued before Shabbat morning
Correct: B — The mishna records that Ben Beteira permits rescuing into an open alley, while the Rabbis limit rescue to a closed alley (defined as three walls with a post). Rav Ashi clarifies: three walls + post = closed; three walls alone = open.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why may one carry the Torah scroll casing (even with money inside) to save it, but NOT carry the hide of the Paschal lamb?
A. A Torah scroll is more sacred than any korban and therefore overrides all restrictions
B. The casing is a base for a permitted object (the scroll); the hide is a base for a forbidden object (the meat)
C. Money is not muktze when placed inside a permitted vessel before Shabbat
D. The Paschal lamb's hide is inherently forbidden as a reminder of the Temple service
Correct: B — The Gemara distinguishes: the casing serves the Torah scroll, which may be moved on Shabbat — making it a base for a permitted object (permitted). The hide serves the Paschal lamb's flesh, which is forbidden on Shabbat — making it a base for a forbidden object (prohibited).
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is פסיק רישא (psik reisha), and which authority concedes to it?
A. A rabbinic penalty for performing melachah carelessly; conceded by Rabbi Akiva
B. An action whose forbidden result is inevitable — even Rabbi Shimon concedes it is prohibited
C. A leniency permitting side effects when the primary action is for a mitzvah, taught by Rav Ashi
D. The rule that one must intend a melachah for it to be punishable; debated by Abaye and Rava
Correct: B — Abaye and Rava rule: Rabbi Shimon generally permits unintended melachah (davar she-eino mitkavein), but concedes that when the forbidden result is inevitable — 'can you cut off its head and it will not die?' — even he considers the action prohibited.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What did Rav Kahana demonstrate about the practice of לחם משנה (two Shabbat loaves)?
A. Both loaves must be broken simultaneously before reciting HaMotzi
B. The two loaves must come from the same batch of dough to be valid
C. He held two loaves but broke only one — 'collecting double' means holding two, not breaking two
D. One should first break the smaller loaf, then the larger, to honor Shabbat
Correct: C — Rav Ashi reports that Rav Kahana held two loaves and broke only one. The verse 'they collected double the bread' teaches that the obligation of lechem mishneh is fulfilled by holding/presenting two loaves — breaking both is not required.
