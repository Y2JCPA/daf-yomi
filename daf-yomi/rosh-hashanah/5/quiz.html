QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does Rav Ḥisda say the Paschal lamb is mentioned in the baraita listing offerings subject to bal te'acher?
A. Because one may delay the Paschal lamb to the Second Passover (Pesach Sheni)
B. It was listed kedi — incidentally, alongside firstborns and tithes — with no real application
C. Because a Paschal lamb can be replaced by a peace-offering that is then subject to delay
D. Because the Paschal lamb is subject to the three-Festival delay rule
Correct: B — Rav Ḥisda says the Paschal lamb is mentioned kedi — incidentally, only because it was grouped with firstborn offerings and animal tithes. The prohibition of bal te'acher does not actually apply to it.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rav Sheshet, what does 'Paschal lamb' refer to in the baraita about delayed offerings?
A. The standard Paschal lamb slaughtered on 14 Nisan
B. A peace-offering brought as a replacement for a Paschal lamb that was lost
C. A burnt-offering substituted for the Paschal lamb
D. The additional Paschal offering brought on the Second Passover
Correct: B — Rav Sheshet explains that the baraita refers to a peace-offering that replaced a lost Paschal lamb. Once the original is found and sacrificed, the replacement becomes a peace-offering — and this type of offering CAN be subject to the delay prohibition.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What does the phrase 'And it would be sin in you' (Deuteronomy 23:22) teach about transgressing bal te'acher?
A. The delayed offering becomes disqualified and must not be sacrificed
B. The transgressor must bring a separate sin-offering as atonement
C. The sin is upon the person, but the offering itself remains valid
D. Bal te'acher is only a rabbinic prohibition, not biblical
Correct: C — The Gemara derives from 'sin in you' that the transgression rests with the person — not with the offering. Even after violating bal te'acher, the offering retains its sanctity and must still be brought.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why is a substitute for a sin-offering excluded from the prohibition against delaying?
A. Substitutes for sin-offerings are entirely invalid as sacrifices
B. The verse says 'you shall not delay paying it' — 'it' refers to the original, not the substitute
C. Rabbi Meir holds that all substitutes are categorically exempt
D. The sin-offering substitute is automatically redeemed with money
Correct: B — The verse says 'you shall not delay paying it' — 'it' refers specifically to the original vowed offering. When the original sin-offering is found and sacrificed, the substitute (which goes to pasture) is excluded from the prohibition.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rabba bar Shmuel's teaching cited on this daf, what is derived from the phrase 'a month of days' (Numbers 11:20)?
A. The Passover seder must last at least one full month of preparation
B. One counts days to determine the new month, and counts days to sanctify offerings — teaching the seven-day redress period
C. The Shavuot offering may only be redressed for one day
D. Festival offerings remain valid for thirty days after the Festival
Correct: B — Rabba bar Shmuel teaches that just as the Torah says to count days for the new month, so too one counts days for sanctifying offerings. This parallel supports the principle that offerings have a multi-day window for redress (generally seven days, like a week).
