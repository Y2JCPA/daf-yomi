5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabba bar Rav Huna, even though Rabban Gamliel says the etrog follows picking time for tithes, what is its halakhic new year?
A. Rosh HaShana (1 Tishrei)
B. The fifteenth of Shevat (Tu B'Shvat)
C. The first of Nisan
D. The first of Adar in a leap year
Correct: B — Even though Rabban Gamliel treats the etrog like a vegetable for determining which tithe year's rules apply, Rabba bar Rav Huna rules that its new year is still the fifteenth of Shevat — Tu B'Shvat — like all other trees.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In a leap year, does the new year for trees (Tu B'Shvat) move to the First Adar?
A. Yes, it moves to the fifteenth of First Adar
B. Yes, but only for single-brood trees like carobs
C. No — it remains in Shevat regardless
D. It depends on when the Sanhedrin declared the leap year
Correct: C — Rav Naḥman (clarifying Rabbi Yannai's ruling) states that the new year for trees does not shift in a leap year — it stays fixed in Shevat, the lunar month, not a solar season.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Rav Hamnuna's ruling on a sixth-year etrog that was picked during the Sabbatical Year?
A. It is always considered Sabbatical produce — exempt from tithes
B. It is always considered sixth-year produce — subject to tithes
C. It follows whichever standard is more stringent
D. It is exempt from both tithes and biur
Correct: B — Rav Hamnuna holds that a sixth-year etrog picked in the Sabbatical Year retains its sixth-year identity in all respects: it is subject to tithes and exempt from the Sabbatical mitzva of biur (elimination).
QUESTION 4 OF 5
For single-brood trees (palm, olive, carob), what happens to fruit that formed before the fifteenth of Shevat?
A. It is tithed with the prior year, like multi-brood trees
B. It is tithed with the coming year, despite forming before Tu B'Shvat
C. It is entirely exempt from tithes
D. It depends on when the fruit was actually picked
Correct: B — Unlike multi-brood trees where formation before Tu B'Shvat means the prior year's tithe, single-brood trees cannot split their one annual crop between two years — so even fruit formed before Tu B'Shvat is tithed with the coming year.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Abba the priest wonder whether Reish Lakish actually challenged Rabbi Yochanan about carob tithing?
A. Rabbi Yochanan had already answered the same question in another context
B. A clear refutation was available but never used — suggesting the exchange may not have occurred
C. Reish Lakish privately agreed with Rabbi Yochanan about carobs
D. The white fig baraita cited by Reish Lakish was not actually relevant
Correct: B — Rabbi Abba notes that Rabbi Yochanan could have easily refuted Reish Lakish by pointing out that carob tithe is only rabbinic (by Torah law only grapes and olives require tithe). Since this clear answer was never used, Rabbi Abba doubts the challenge was actually raised.
