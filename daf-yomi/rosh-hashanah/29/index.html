🎯 Must the Shofar-Sounder Have Intent?

Rabbi Zeira
The sounder must have explicit intent to discharge the hearer's obligation — both parties require kavanah.
Baraita (Strict View)
If only one party has intent — whether sounder or hearer — the obligation is not fulfilled. Both must intend.
Tanna Kamma
The hearer fulfills for himself; the sounder sounds in the usual way. No specific intent required from the sounder — shome'a k'oneh.
Gemara
This is a dispute between tannaim — the halakha is not definitively resolved, reflecting two legitimate traditions.
📜 Key Distinction The community shofar-sounder has everyone in mind by definition. The debate applies specifically to one sounding on behalf of a particular individual.

🙌 Moses's Hands and the Serpent on the Pole

Do Moses's raised hands win the battle?
"When Moses raised his hand, Israel prevailed; when he lowered it, Amalek prevailed" (Ex. 17:11)
↓
❌ Literal Reading
Moses's hands had direct magical power over battle outcomes
✅ Mishna's Answer
When Israel looked upward and directed their hearts to God, they prevailed — the hands are purely symbolic
↓
Similarly: Did the bronze serpent heal the bitten?
"Everyone who looks at it shall live" (Num. 21:8)
↓
No — looking directed hearts to God. God heals; the symbol elevates.
Application: The shofar sound alone does not fulfill the mitzvah — kavanah (intent directed toward Heaven) is the true operative force.

⛓️ The Half-Slave: Can He Even Fulfill His Own Obligation?

Rav Huna
He cannot discharge others' obligations, but he can discharge his own. His free component fulfills the mitzvah for the free part of his identity.
Rav Naḥman
Even for himself he cannot discharge the obligation! His slave component cannot discharge his free component — these statuses are fundamentally incompatible.
Rav Naḥman (challenge)
What is your distinction, Rav Huna? If his slave-self cannot discharge his free-self, how can you call it self-fulfillment at all?
Baraita
"A half-slave, half-freeman cannot discharge the obligation even for himself" — confirming Rav Naḥman's position.
⚖️ Ruling Rav Naḥman is confirmed by the baraita: the split status is so fundamentally contradictory that no valid discharge of obligation can occur — not even for himself.

🔔 Fulfilling Blessings: For Yourself vs. On Behalf of Others

Ahava bar Rabbi Zeira's Rule

  • Even after one has recited a blessing and fulfilled his own obligation…
  • He may still recite it again on behalf of others who have not yet fulfilled theirs
  • Basis: Kol Yisrael arevin zeh bazeh — all Israel are surety for one another
  • Applies broadly: blessings over mitzvot, Havdalah, Kiddush, etc.

Rava's Open Dilemma (end of 29a)

  • Rava raises a new question building on Ahava's rule
  • Can one who has already fulfilled his obligation serve as motzi others with a blessing in all cases?
  • Or is there a meaningful distinction between different types of blessings?
  • The dilemma remains unresolved at the close of Amud A — continued on Amud B…

🎺 The Logic of Who Discharges Whom

Foundational Rule (Mishna): Whoever is not themselves obligated in a matter cannot discharge the obligation of others who are.
↓
Clear cases — Cannot discharge: Deaf-mute, imbecile, minor (no cognition); women (not time-bound obligated); slaves (not fully obligated).
↓
Clear cases — Can discharge: Priests, Levites, Israelites, converts, freed slaves — all fully obligated and may act for others.
↓
Gray areas: Tumtum and androginos are of uncertain sex; half-slave is of uncertain freedom — all therefore cannot discharge others (or themselves).
↓
Positive extension (Ahava bar R. Zeira): One who is obligated remains capable of discharging others even after personal fulfillment, because communal responsibility persists.
