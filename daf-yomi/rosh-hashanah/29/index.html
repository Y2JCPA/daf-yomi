🎯 Must Both Sounder and Hearer Have Intent?

Rabbi Zeira
The sounder must intend to discharge the hearer's obligation — derived from: 'Have intent to sound the shofar on my behalf and sound it for me.'
Baraita Objection
If the hearer had intent but sounder did not, or vice versa — the obligation is not fulfilled. Both must intend!
Resolution
This is a tannaitic dispute: One tanna requires only the hearer's intent; another requires both. The community representative (shaliach tzibbur) sounds for everyone at once — no individual intent needed.
Key Principle One who has already fulfilled his own obligation may still discharge it on behalf of others — because all Jews are responsible for one another (kol Yisrael areivim zeh bazeh).

🎯 Does Kavvanah Discharge the Shofar Obligation?

Someone hears the shofar — is the obligation fulfilled?
↓
Community Shaliach
Sounds for entire congregation — all are in mind collectively; no individual intent required
Individual Sounder
Must have specific intent to discharge this hearer's obligation (per Rabbi Zeira)
↓
✅ Fulfilled
Congregation discharged even without naming each person individually
⚖️ Tannaitic Dispute
Does the hearer also need intent? Some say hearer alone suffices; others require both sounder and hearer to intend

🙌 Moses' Hands & the Copper Serpent

וְהָיָה כָּל הַנָּשׁוּךְ וְרָאָה אֹתוֹ וָחָי — Numbers 21:8
It Is Not the Action — It Is the Heart Moses' raised hands (Exodus 17:11): Did Moses' hands make Israel win or lose? No — when Israel looked upward and directed their hearts to their Father in Heaven, they prevailed. When they did not, Amalek prevailed.

Copper Serpent (Numbers 21:8): Did the serpent kill or heal? No — when Israel looked upward and subjected their hearts to Heaven, they were healed from snakebite.

The principle: Physical objects and rituals are catalysts only. The inner intention directed heavenward is the true source of efficacy — and this principle directly informs why kavvanah matters when hearing the shofar.

⚖️ Who Can Discharge the Shofar Obligation?

✅ Can Discharge for Others

  • Adult Jewish male — obligated himself
  • Priests, Levites, and Israelites
  • Converts and freed slaves
  • One who already fulfilled his own obligation (per Ahava b. R. Zeira — can also recite blessings for others)

❌ Cannot Discharge for Others

  • Deaf-mute, imbecile, minor — not themselves obligated
  • Tumtum — uncertain male/female status; cannot discharge a definite male
  • Androgynos — cannot discharge one of definite status
  • Half-slave half-freeman — Rav Naḥman: cannot even discharge for himself

⚠️ Disputed Cases

  • Half-slave half-freeman for himself: Rav Huna says yes; Rav Naḥman says no (supported by baraita)
  • Women: obligated by some opinions; debate whether they discharge men's obligation
  • Tumtum for another tumtum: cannot — one may be female, other male

📅 Why Is Shofar Prohibited on Shabbat Outside the Temple?

R. Levi bar Laḥma
Torah law distinguishes the two: Lev. 23:24 says 'zikron teruah' (memorial of sounding) for Shabbat; Num. 29:1 says 'yom teruah' (day of actual sounding) for weekday. Shabbat = remembrance only.
Rava — Objection
If it is a Torah prohibition, how can the Temple override it? The Temple overrides only rabbinic prohibitions. A Torah prohibition cannot be suspended even in the Temple!
Rava — Conclusion
By Torah law, shofar is permitted even on Shabbat. The Sages decreed against it — following Rabba: one might carry the shofar four cubits in a public domain to find an expert teacher. Same rabbinic logic applies to lulav and Megilla on Shabbat.
Unified Principle The single rabbinic decree covers shofar, lulav, and Megilla on Shabbat — all suspended lest one carry in a public domain (reshut harabim).

🏛️ Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai's Decree — Story & Scope

Before the decree: Shofar was sounded on Shabbat only in the Temple (Jerusalem). After the Temple's destruction — it was sounded nowhere on Shabbat.
↓
The encounter at Yavne: Rosh HaShana fell on Shabbat. The sons of Beteira were uncertain — should they sound the shofar? They asked Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai.
↓
His bold move: 'First let us sound — then we discuss!' After sounding, he declared: 'The horn has already been sounded in Yavne — the deed is done!'
↓
The decree: In any place where there is a permanent beit din (court of 23 judges), the shofar may be sounded on Shabbat — transferring the Temple's privilege to Torah courts.
↓
Scope dispute: R. Elazar: decree was only for Yavne. Rabbis: any permanent court. Further dispute: does the decree apply to a temporary (non-fixed) court? Practical difference between tanna'im.
