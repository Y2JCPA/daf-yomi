QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Ḥisda, how does the flood 'fit the crime' even according to Rabbi Eliezer, who holds it began in Marcheshvan — a normal rainy month?
A. The flood happened to be unusually heavy, which itself was miraculous
B. They sinned with boiling heat (illicit passion), so the floodwaters were supernaturally scalding hot
C. The flood came from underground springs, not rain, which was unnatural
D. God caused the flood to last an unusually long time as punishment
Correct: B — Rav Ḥisda teaches that even in Marcheshvan a change occurred: the Generation of the Flood sinned through unrestrained passion ('boiling heat'), so measure-for-measure the floodwaters were made supernaturally hot. The change was in the water's temperature, not the season.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the baraita list vegetable tithes BEFORE grain tithes, even though grain tithes are stricter (d'oraita vs. d'rabbanan)?
A. Vegetable tithes are actually a Torah-level obligation in rabbinic interpretation
B. The tanna listed shorter words before longer words for stylistic reasons
C. The tanna held this halakha dear and therefore placed it first
D. Grain tithes do not have a Rosh Hashana cutoff and are therefore mentioned second
Correct: C — The Gemara explicitly states that the tanna was particularly fond of the halakha of vegetable tithes and therefore mentioned it first — even though it is only a rabbinic obligation, while grain tithes are from the Torah.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
In the third year of the shemita cycle, which tithes are given?
A. Only poor man's tithe — first tithe to Levites is suspended
B. Only second tithe — the poor man's tithe replaces first tithe
C. First tithe to Levites is given as always; second tithe is replaced by poor man's tithe
D. No tithes at all — the third year is treated like a mini-shemita
Correct: C — Numbers 18 proves the Levites continue receiving first tithe in year 3 (since terumas ma'aser from it must still be separated). The verse 'year of the tithe' means the year when the second tithe — normally taken by the owner to Jerusalem — is instead replaced by the poor man's tithe.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
If someone picks vegetables just before sunset on Rosh Hashana eve, then picks more after sunset, what is the law regarding tithing?
A. Both batches may be combined freely since they were picked on the same day
B. One may not tithe from the pre-sunset batch to exempt the post-sunset batch, or vice versa
C. The post-sunset batch requires double tithe since it straddles two years
D. The pre-sunset batch is exempt from tithe since it was picked before Rosh Hashana began
Correct: B — Since 1 Tishrei marks the new year for vegetables, produce picked before sunset belongs to the old year and produce after sunset to the new year. One may never tithe from new-year produce to exempt old-year produce, or vice versa — the two batches are legally distinct.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What triggers a crop's tithe obligation, and why does this matter for the Sabbatical Year?
A. When the crop is harvested; Shemita produce harvested in year 7 has sanctity
B. When the crop is planted; seeds planted in Shemita retain sanctity forever
C. When the crop reaches one-third of its growth; produce at one-third in Shemita retains sanctity even if harvested in year 8
D. When the crop flowers; blossoms in Shemita determine the year even after harvest
Correct: C — The Gemara establishes that tithe obligation (and Shemita sanctity) attaches when produce reaches one-third growth. Therefore, grain or olives that reached one-third during the Sabbatical Year carry Shemita sanctity even if they are not harvested until after Sukkot of the following year.
