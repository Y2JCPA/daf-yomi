  • New Year for Kings — regnal years counted from here
  • New Year for Pilgrimage Festivals — determines their order
  • Basis for dating legal documents

  • New Year for Animal Tithes — animals born before/after separated
  • Tanna Kamma's position
  • R. Elazar & R. Shimon: 1 Tishrei instead

  • New Year for Tree-Fruit Tithes

👑 Why Does a King's Year Start on 1 Nisan?

ר"ה ב׳ — רב חסדא: מפני שמשתמשים במלכים לשטרות
Rav Ḥisda — משום שטרות Legal documents were dated by the regnal year of the king. Without a fixed annual reset date, two documents from different kings' first years could be mistakenly treated as contemporaneous, enabling fraud — antedating or postdating loans. Fixing 1 Nisan as the universal start of every king's year allows courts to determine precisely when each year began and whether a document is valid.

⏱️ One Day Before Nisan Counts as a Full Regnal Year

King is crowned on 29 Adar
Only one day before the new year
↓
1 Nisan arrives — the royal New Year resets
Does that 1 day count?
YES — the Baraita rules it is Year 1 of his reign. Nisan 1 closes the year regardless of how little of it he reigned.
Why not count from coronation?
Nisan 1 is the fixed New Year for kings — just as Tishrei 1 resets the calendar year for everyone.
↓
Two principles established:
(1) Nisan is the royal New Year
(2) One day in a year = a full year (יום אחד בשנה חשוב שנה)

📜 Is the Nisan Succession Case Even Necessary to Teach?

Questioner
The baraita says: if both kings reigned in Nisan, each gets the Nisan year counted. But isn't this obvious? Why does the baraita need to state it explicitly?
Gemara (Talmud's Answer)
It is not obvious at all! You might have thought: since both kings share the same Nisan month, perhaps only one of them gets that year counted — perhaps the month can only belong to a single king. The baraita teaches that each independently receives full credit for the Nisan year.
Further Question
The baraita also says that if princes agreed to appoint the new king already in Adar, his year begins from Nisan anyway — why not from the decision in Adar?
Gemara (Final Answer)
Lest you say that since the decision was made in Adar, the new king's first year starts then. The baraita teaches: no — only actual coronation and accession to the throne triggers the count, and the year resets at Nisan.
Core Principle A single month (Nisan) can simultaneously be the final year of one king and the first year of another.

📖 R. Yoḥanan's Chain of Proof — Nisan as the Royal New Year

Source Verse (1 Kings 6:1): "In the 480th year after Israel left Egypt… in the 4th year of Solomon's reign… in the month of Ziv [= Iyyar]… he built the Temple." — This links Solomon's regnal years to the Exodus count.
↓
Question: We now know Solomon's 4th year = Exodus year 480. But when does the Exodus year start — Nisan or Tishrei? If Tishrei, kings' years would start in Tishrei.
↓
Proof from Aaron's Death (Num. 33:38): "Aaron the priest went up to Mount Hor… and died there, in the 40th year after Israel left Egypt, on the 1st of Av." — The Exodus year count is tied to an event in Av (the 5th month).
↓
Sequence Evidence: Aaron died before the defeat of Sihon (Deut. 1:4 says Moses spoke "after he had slain Sihon"; Sihon was still alive when Aaron died). Moses' speech in the 40th year came after Sihon's defeat, hence in a later month. So the 40th year must have begun in Nisan (before Av), not Tishrei (which would be after Av).
↓
Verbal Analogy (Gezerah Shavah): The phrase "the twentieth year" appears both in Solomon's reign and in the Exodus count, linking them. Since the Exodus count begins in Nisan, Solomon's regnal count — and all kings' years — begins in Nisan.
