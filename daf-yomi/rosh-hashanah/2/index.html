ראש השנה למלכים — לענין שטרות (רב חסדא, ר"ה ב.)
👑 Why Does Nisan Matter for Kings? The Gemara asks: why set a fixed New Year for kings instead of counting from coronation day?

Rav Ḥisda answers: Legal documents were dated by the king's regnal year. Without a fixed New Year, one could not determine whether a document was antedated or postdated — enabling fraud. With 1 Nisan as a universal reset, courts can verify any document's authenticity.

📖 From Where Do We Know? — R. Yochanan's Proof

Rabbi Yochanan
We derive that kings' years begin in Nisan from 1 Kings 6:1: Solomon began the Temple 'in the 480th year after the Exodus, in Iyar, in the 4th year of his reign.' The two counts (from the Exodus and from Solomon) align — both must start in Nisan.
Gemara (Challenge)
Perhaps the years from the Exodus are counted from Tishrei? Then the proof collapses — Solomon's 4th year could start in Nisan while the 480 years from the Exodus are counted from Tishrei independently.
Gemara (Resolution)
Aaron died on 1 Av in 'the 40th year' (Num. 33:38). Moses gave his speech on 1 Shevat in 'the 40th year' (Deut. 1:3). Since Av comes before Shevat, Aaron's death came first — so the 40th year began before Av, pointing to Nisan, not Tishrei.
Conclusion Both the Exodus years and kings' regnal years are counted from Nisan — proven by cross-referencing Aaron's death and Moses' speech, both placed in 'the 40th year' from the Exodus.
