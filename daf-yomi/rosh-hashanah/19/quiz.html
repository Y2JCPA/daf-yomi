QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Gemara, why is fasting forbidden on the 3rd of Tishrei in the time of the Temple?
A. It is the day Rosh HaShana was originally celebrated
B. It is listed in Megillat Ta'anit as a great military victory
C. It is the day Gedaliah ben Ahikam was assassinated
D. It is the day before Yom Kippur, which requires extra preparation
Correct: C — The Gemara derives the prohibition from the assassination of Gedaliah ben Ahikam on the 3rd of Tishrei. His death ended the last remnant of Jewish autonomy after the Temple's destruction, making this a day of national mourning — hence fasting is commemoratively forbidden.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the Gemara's final dual ruling about Megillat Ta'anit after the Temple's destruction?
A. It was completely nullified; none of its fast-prohibition days apply anymore
B. It was never nullified; all its days remain fully in force forever
C. Both — it was nullified AND not nullified, each applying in different eras or contexts
D. It was nullified only for fast days, but festive-day prohibitions remain
Correct: C — The Gemara issues a striking dual ruling: 'the halakha is that they were nullified AND the halakha is that they were not nullified.' This is resolved: when the Temple stood — not nullified; after destruction — nullified. Both rulings stand, each in its proper context.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does the Gemara say messengers must go out in Tishrei even though they already went in Elul?
A. Because Tishrei has more festivals than any other month and requires extra announcements
B. Because Elul messengers only announced Rosh HaShana and could not inform about Sukkot dates
C. Because the court might have added a day to Elul, shifting all Tishrei festival dates by one day
D. Because distant communities always lose their calendar reckoning after the High Holidays
Correct: C — If the court added a 30th day to Elul, Rosh HaShana would fall on the 31st day after the previous New Moon — communities that counted only 30 days would observe all Tishrei festivals (Sukkot, Shemini Atzeret) one day too early. Tishrei messengers protected against this error.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What did Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi testify in the name of the holy community of Jerusalem regarding the two months of Adar in a leap year?
A. Both months of Adar are full months of 30 days in a leap year
B. The first Adar is full (30 days) and the second Adar is short (29 days)
C. Both months of Adar are short months of 29 days — never made into full months
D. The length of each Adar is determined anew by the court each leap year
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi testified that both Adars are sanctified as short months (29 days). They are declared on the 30th day (which could theoretically have been added to make them full), but neither Adar is ever extended to 30 days.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What ruling was sent from Eretz Yisrael to Mar Ukva (the Exilarch in Babylonia) about Adar?
A. Both Adars in a leap year are always short months of 29 days
B. The Adar before Nisan is always short; the first Adar in a leap year may be full (30 days)
C. The first Adar in a leap year is always short; only the second Adar may be full
D. Both Adars are always full months of 30 days in a leap year
Correct: B — The ruling states: the Adar immediately preceding Nisan is always short (29 days) in both regular and leap years. However, the first Adar in a leap year — which does not immediately precede Nisan — may sometimes be made into a full month of 30 days.
