⚖️ How Much Partial Time Counts as a Full Year?

Rabbi Meir
One day at the start of a new year counts as a full year. The flood verse proves it — even one day into year 601 is called 'the year.'
Rabbi Elazar
Thirty days are required to count as a year. The same flood verse proves it — 'year' refers back to the final 30 days of year 600.
Rav Naḥman (citing Rabba bar Avuh)
Per the 30-day view: 30 days to take root plus 30 days to count as a year = 60 days total must precede Rosh HaShana.
Gemara (Resolution)
The baraita follows Rabbi Meir throughout. The '30 days' means rooting time only. One additional day then counts as a full year — confirmed.
📌 Practical Result A tree planted more than 30 days before Rosh HaShana: that Rosh HaShana counts as Year 1 of orla. The three-year orla period and fourth-year restrictions run until the 15th of Shevat.

📜 The Flood Verse: One Verse, Two Readings

Rabbi Meir
"In the one and six hundredth year" — 'year' refers to year 601. It was only the 1st of Nisan, the very first day of that year. Since one day is called 'the year,' one day = a full year.
Rabbi Elazar
"In the one and six hundredth year" — if the Torah meant year 601, it would simply say 'the six hundred and first year.' The unusual phrasing means 'year' refers back to year 600 — specifically its last 30 days. Thirty days = a year.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Remarkably, both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Elazar expounded the very same verse yet arrived at opposite conclusions — a classic case of a single biblical phrase supporting two conflicting halachic positions.
📖 The Verse — Bereishit 8:13 "And it came to pass in the one and six hundredth year [שנת שש מאות ושנה], in the first month, on the first day of the month, the waters dried from upon the earth."

⏳ The 30-Day Rooting Requirement: How It Works

Step 1 — Planting: A tree, layer, or graft is placed in the ground. The orla clock cannot start until the tree establishes itself.
↓
Step 2 — 30 Days of Rooting (חזקת השרשה): The tree must remain in the ground for 30 days. This is biological, not halachic — only after rooting can the tree count toward any orla year.
↓
Step 3 — One Day (Rabbi Meir): Even a single day at the start of a new year — after rooting — counts as a complete first year of orla.
↓
Step 4 — Rosh HaShana as Year Boundary: If 30+ days have passed before Rosh HaShana, that Rosh HaShana becomes Year 1. The count continues through subsequent Rosh HaShanas.
↓
Step 5 — Three Full Years: After three Rosh HaShanas, the orla period ends — but fourth-year produce (נטע רבעי) remains restricted.
↓
Step 6 — Tu BiShvat (15 Shevat): The New Year for Trees marks the final boundary. All fourth-year restrictions lift; fruit becomes fully permitted from this date forward.
