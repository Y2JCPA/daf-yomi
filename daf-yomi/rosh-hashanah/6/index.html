⏳ The Law of Bal Te'acher (You Shall Not Delay)

Source: Deuteronomy 23:22 — "You shall not delay paying it; for the Lord your God will surely require it from you" — the core prohibition
↓
Positive Mitzvah: "That which is gone out of your lips you shall keep and do" — a separate affirmative obligation to fulfill your vow
↓
Warning to Court: "He shall offer it" (Lev. 1:3) — Beit Din is commanded to compel the vower to fulfill his pledge
↓
Violation Threshold: Three Festivals (Pesach → Shavuot → Sukkot) must pass unfulfilled — or one full year — before transgression begins
↓
Ongoing Violation (Rava): Once the threshold is crossed, the transgression renews every single day

🔍 Deriving Bal Te'acher — The Gemara's Analysis

Ben Azzai
From 'it shall not be accepted' (Leviticus 19) we derive that an offering brought too late is invalid — and a prohibition against delay follows.
Gemara
The verse 'and it would be a sin IN YOU' limits the prohibition: the sin is imputed to you — but NOT to your wife or other household members.
Objection
But Rabbi Yoḥanan taught: a person's wife may die because of HIS sins! If so, why would household members not be implicated in his delay?
Gemara (Resolution)
That principle applies to monetary sins between people. Here the verse explicitly singles out 'in you' — only the vower transgresses, never the household.
Key Verse לֹא תְאַחֵר לְשַׁלְּמוֹ — You shall not delay paying it (Deut. 23:22)

🐂 Vow-Offering vs. Gift-Offering

נֶדֶר — Vow-Offering

  • "I hereby vow to bring a burnt-offering"
  • No specific animal yet designated
  • If animal dies or is stolen: owner MUST bring a replacement
  • The obligation rests on the PERSON
  • Court compels fulfillment even before Festivals pass
  • Transgresses Bal Te'acher after three Festivals

נְדָבָה — Gift-Offering

  • "This animal shall be a burnt-offering"
  • A specific animal IS designated
  • If animal dies or is stolen: owner is EXEMPT
  • The obligation rests on the ANIMAL
  • Rava: Can exist without specific animal — e.g., conditional pledge
  • Also subject to Bal Te'acher once set aside

📅 When Does Bal Te'acher Trigger?

Person makes a vow or solemn pledge
↓
Vow of Charity (צְדָקָה)
Rava: Transgresses immediately if a poor person is present and needy — no Festivals needed
Vow of Offerings (קָרְבָּן)
Must bring the offering before three Festivals (Pesach → Shavuot → Sukkot) have passed
↓
Three Festivals elapse without fulfillment → Bal Te'acher transgressed
↓
Rava (6b): Every subsequent day is a new, independent transgression of the prohibition

👤 Rabbi Zeira's Two Dilemmas Dilemma 1 — The Heir: Does a son who inherits his father's estate transgress Bal Te'acher for his father's unfulfilled vow?

Answer: No. R. Ḥiyya's baraita derives from "from you" (מֵעִמָּךְ) that the prohibition is personal — an heir is excluded. However, he must still fulfill the vow itself.

Dilemma 2 — The Woman: Does a woman transgress Bal Te'acher? She is not obligated in the Temple pilgrimage (עֲלִיָּה לָרֶגֶל) — so do the Festivals even count for her?

Answer (Abaye): She IS obligated in simchat yom tov (rejoicing on the Festival) — therefore the Festivals count against her, and she can transgress Bal Te'acher.
