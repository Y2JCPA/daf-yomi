Rosh Hashanah 14 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why did Shmuel need to state all three of his rulings about tithes rather than just two?
A. Each ruling applies to a different category of produce with no overlap
B. Each ruling alone (or any two together) leaves an ambiguity that only all three together resolve
C. The three rulings were stated on three separate occasions years apart
D. One of the three rulings directly contradicts another, and all three are needed to explain the contradiction
Correct: B — The Gemara shows: Statement 1 alone implies he agrees with the Sages; Statement 2 alone implies another error; Statements 1+2 together appear contradictory (R. Zeira's difficulty). Only all three together unambiguously clarify Shmuel's full position.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the practical halakhic difference between Rabbi Yosei HaGelili and Rabbi Akiva in their exposition of the threshing floor verse?
A. Whether it is permitted to build a sukkah in a field during harvest season
B. Whether seedless onions and cowpeas fall under the same tithing rules as grain and wine
C. Whether one may tithe vegetables using grain as a substitute measure
D. Whether the verse applies to Sukkot at all or only to Shavuot
Correct: B — Rabbi Abbahu identifies the test case as seedless onions and cowpeas. R. Yosei HaGelili's criterion (sun/wind exposure during processing) would exclude them; R. Akiva's criterion (edible food, not waste) would include them.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to the Gemara's resolution, why did Rabbi Akiva set aside two tithes from a single etrog?
A. He ruled that an etrog always requires double tithing as a rabbinic stringency
B. He deliberately adopted the stringencies of both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel simultaneously
C. He was genuinely uncertain whether Beit Hillel placed the new year for trees on the 1st or the 15th of Shevat
D. He forgot which tithe year he was in and doubled to be safe
Correct: C — The Gemara explicitly rejects the idea that one may adopt the stringencies of two conflicting authorities. R. Akiva wanted to follow Beit Hillel but had a tradition-doubt (safek) about whether Beit Hillel said 1st or 15th of Shevat — so both tithes reflect genuine uncertainty.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In how many ways is the etrog like an ordinary fruit tree, and in how many ways is it like a vegetable?
A. Two ways like a tree; two ways like a vegetable
B. Four ways like a tree; one way like a vegetable
C. Three ways like a tree; one way like a vegetable
D. One way like a tree; three ways like a vegetable
Correct: C — The mishna states: The etrog is like an ordinary tree in three ways — orla, revai, and tithe year following fruit formation (R. Eliezer). It is like a vegetable in one way — tithe year following picking (Rabban Gamliel).
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rabba bar Rav Huna, if we rule like Rabban Gamliel that the etrog's tithe year follows picking (like a vegetable), what would its Rosh Hashana for tithing be?
A. 1st of Shevat — Beit Shammai's date for trees
B. 15th of Shevat — Tu B'Shevat
C. 1st of Tishrei — the new year for vegetables
D. 1st of Nisan — the new year for kings
Correct: C — Rabba bar Rav Huna reasons: since Rabban Gamliel treats the etrog like a vegetable for tithing purposes (following time of picking), its Rosh Hashana should logically be 1 Tishrei — the new year governing all vegetables and produce not specifically assigned to another date.
