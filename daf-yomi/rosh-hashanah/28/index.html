🎺 Does Fulfilling a Mitzva Require Intent (Kavanah)?

Questioner
One who heard the shofar unintentionally — did he fulfill his obligation? The verse says "a memorial of blasts" (Lev. 23:24), implying conscious intent is required.
Attempted Answer
Perhaps mitzvot do not require intent. The shofar verse is needed only to exclude someone who acted completely unawares — not to require full directed intent.
Objection (Baraita)
A baraita states: If the hearer had intent but the sounder did not, or vice versa — neither has fulfilled the obligation!
Rava's Resolution
Fulfilling a mitzva does NOT require intent. But transgressing Bal Tosif (do not add) or Bal Tigra (do not diminish) does require intent.
Core Principle Mitzvot are fulfilled without kavanah, but one cannot inadvertently violate the prohibition against adding to or subtracting from mitzvot.

🔄 The Shofar Kavanah Debate — Objections & Refutations

Claim: Mitzvot do not require intent — hearing shofar unintentionally fulfills the obligation
↓
Objection 1
Shema reader: One reading Torah when Shema-time arrived — only fulfills if he focused his heart. Implies intent needed!
Refutation 1
Mishna means he intended to read as a mitzva, not just casually. The intent distinguishes sacred reading from mere study.
↓
Objection 2
Passing synagogue: Mishna says one who heard shofar — fulfilled only if he directed his heart. Intent explicitly required!
Refutation 2
Mishna means he intended to hear purposefully, not to merely be present. Directed listening vs. passive hearing.
↓
Objection 3 (Baraita)
If hearer had intent but sounder did not, or vice versa — obligation not fulfilled. Both sides must have intent!
Refutation 3
Baraita deals with bark-like blasts — improper shofar sounds. Without intent in such a case, there is no valid sound at all.

🛖 Abaye vs. Rava — Sukka on Shemini Atzeret

Abaye
If mitzvot require no intent, then one who sleeps in a sukka on Shemini Atzeret should receive lashes for Bal Tosif! He is adding to the mitzva of sukka even though its time has passed.
Rava
No. Bal Tosif is violated only when one adds to a mitzva during its prescribed time. Adding outside the time of obligation — like sitting in a sukka on the 9th of Tishrei or Shemini Atzeret — is not a violation.
Rav Shemen bar Abba
Objection from a baraita: A priest should not add personal blessings after the Priestly Blessing, lest it appear he is adding to the Torah. This applies even after the mitzva is complete!
Gemara (Answer)
The baraita refers to adding blessings in the middle of the Priestly Blessing — still within the time of obligation. Or: he completed one verse but the others remain — the mitzva period has not yet ended.
Key Distinction Bal Tosif applies only within the prescribed time of a mitzva, not when the obligation period has expired.

🩸 Mixed Blood Sprinklings — R. Eliezer vs. R. Yehoshua

Rabbi Eliezer

  • Blood of 4-sprinkling offering mixed with blood of 1-sprinkling offering
  • Rule: Sprinkle the mixture four times
  • Reason: If only once, violates Bal Tigra (do not diminish) — the 4-sprinkling blood did not receive its full rite
  • Bal Tosif for the 1-sprinkling blood: permitted, since it is part of a mixture — the prohibition applies only when blood stands alone

Rabbi Yehoshua

  • Same case: mixed bloods
  • Rule: Sprinkle the mixture once only
  • Reason: Bal Tosif applies even to mixtures. Sprinkling four times violates Bal Tosif.
  • Bal Tigra for the 4-sprinkling blood: permitted — any omission is passive, not an active transgression. "You do not perform it with your own hand."
  • Active transgression (Bal Tosif) is worse than passive omission (Bal Tigra)

✅ Rava's Final Resolution

מצוות אינן צריכות כוונה — אך עבירת בל תוסיף ובל תגרע צריכה כוונה
The Decisive Principle (סוגיא כרבא) Two separate rules govern the same act:

1. Fulfilling a mitzva — does NOT require intent (kavanah). One who hears the shofar, reads Shema, or sleeps in the sukka without conscious intent has nonetheless fulfilled the obligation.

2. Transgressing Bal Tosif or Bal Tigra — DOES require intent. Without deliberate intent to add to or subtract from a mitzva, there is no punishable violation.

These two rules operate independently. A person can fulfill a mitzva passively, but cannot accidentally become liable for Bal Tosif.
