🎯 The Great Kavanah Debate

Rava (initial)
One who sounds a shofar merely for music — without mitzva intent — has fulfilled his obligation. The proof: one compelled to eat matza on Passover also fulfills the mitzva.
Baraita
If the hearer had intent but the sounder did not — or vice versa — he has not fulfilled his obligation!
Rava (reply)
The baraita refers to bark-like blasts — improper sounding done entirely without intent. Normal hearing without specific mitzva intent is still valid.
Rava (conclusion)
Fulfillment of a mitzva does not require intent. But transgressing bal tosif (do not add) or bal tigra (do not diminish) does require intent.
Core Principle — מצוות אין צריכות כוונה Mitzvot do not require intent (Rava's final position) — but actively transgressing the prohibitions of adding or diminishing does require intent.

🕳️ Partial Shofar Blasts: When Is Obligation Fulfilled?

One hears a shofar blast in two parts — different locations or different times
↓
Part in pit, part at rim
Both locations are valid places of obligation. A pit is obligatory for those standing in it. ✅ Fulfilled
Part before dawn, part after dawn
Night is not a time of obligation at all — the pre-dawn sound has no halakhic weight. ❌ Not fulfilled
↓
Key rule: A place can be valid even if inconvenient. But a time outside the mitzva period has zero validity.
↓
Rabba's edge case: One who sounds for himself in a pit — head sometimes inside, sometimes outside — still fulfilled, since he is not listening to himself as an outside hearer.

🐂 Shofar from Consecrated Animals — Rav Yehuda vs. Rava

Burnt-Offering (עולה)

  • Should not blow l'chatchila (ab initio)
  • Subject to me'ila (misuse of consecrated objects)
  • Rav Yehuda: If he blew anyway, he fulfilled the obligation — misuse renders it non-sacred, so he retroactively blew with a valid shofar
  • Rava objects: When does misuse occur? Only after blowing — so at the moment of blowing it was still sacred!
  • Rava's final ruling: Did NOT fulfill the obligation
  • Shofar = body of the animal; remains prohibited throughout

Peace-Offering (שלמים)

  • Should not blow l'chatchila
  • Not subject to me'ila after slaughter
  • Rav Yehuda: Did NOT fulfill — shofar is the body of the offering itself
  • Rava: Initially agreed with Rav Yehuda's distinction...
  • ...then retracted: both types are treated equally
  • Rava's final ruling: Did NOT fulfill with either type — both are bodies of sacrifices

⚖️ Bal Tosif — When Does 'Do Not Add' Apply?

Abaye
If mitzvot need no intent, one who accidentally sleeps in a sukkah on Shemini Atzeret should receive lashes for bal tosif — he added to the mitzva without intending to!
Rava
Bal tosif is only violated when adding during the prescribed time of the mitzva. Shemini Atzeret is outside Sukkot's time — no violation whatsoever.
Rav Shemen bar Abba
A baraita about the Priestly Blessing suggests that adding even after completing the blessing violates bal tosif — the priest's entire day is the mitzva's time!
Gemara
Priestly Blessing is different: another Jew may always appear requiring blessing, so the whole day remains the prescribed time. This is a special case, not a refutation of Rava's rule.
Rava's Final Resolution Bal tosif requires (1) intent AND (2) the addition must occur during the mitzva's prescribed time. Outside that window — no prohibition.

🫓 From Compelled Matza to Shofar Kavanah — The Chain of Reasoning

Ruling sent from Eretz Yisrael: One forcibly compelled by Persians to eat matza on Passover has fulfilled the mitzva of matza — even with zero personal intent.
↓
Rav Ashi's application: Therefore one who sounds shofar for music — with no mitzva intent — should similarly fulfill the shofar obligation.
↓
Possible objection: The shofar verse says זִכְרוֹן תְּרוּעָה — 'a memorial of blasts' — suggesting conscious awareness is uniquely required for shofar.
↓
Response: Matza is equally described with awareness-language. If compulsion fulfills matza despite that, the same logic applies to shofar.
↓
Challenge from Shema: One reading Torah when Shema-time arrives fulfills Shema only if he focused his heart — doesn't this prove intent is needed? The Gemara resolves: he must intend to fulfill the mitzva, not merely to read.
↓
Rava's conclusion: מצוות אין צריכות כוונה — Mitzvot do not require intent. The halakhic act itself is sufficient for fulfillment.
