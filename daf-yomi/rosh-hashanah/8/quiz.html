QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Ashi, why does the Mishna list only four New Years rather than including all New Years (e.g., Tu B'Shvat)?
A. Because there are only four seasons in the year, one New Year per season
B. Because the Mishna counts only New Years that fall on Rosh Chodesh (the New Moon / first of the month)
C. Because the Sages of Yavneh abolished the other New Years after the Temple's destruction
D. Because Tu B'Shvat and the other New Years were later rabbinic enactments, not biblical
Correct: B — Rav Ashi explains that the Mishna enumerates only New Years coinciding with Rosh Chodesh (the first of the month). New Years falling on other dates — like Tu B'Shvat (15th of Shevat) — are excluded from the count of four.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is Rava's resolution of the dispute between Rabbi Meir (1 Elul) and Rabbi Elazar (1 Tishrei) for the animal tithe New Year?
A. They disagree about when sheep are born — Elul (5 months after Adar) vs. Tishrei (6 months after Adar)
B. They disagree about which biblical verse serves as the source for animal tithe
C. Both agree flocks primarily conceive in Adar; they disagree whether animal tithe is juxtaposed to grain tithe (Elul) or set aside at the new year's start (Tishrei)
D. Rabbi Meir holds the calendar year begins in Nisan while Rabbi Elazar holds it begins in Tishrei
Correct: C — Rava resolves the dispute: both agree the flocks primarily conceive in Adar when 'the valleys are wrapped in grain.' The disagreement is about when tithe is separated: Rabbi Meir juxtaposes animal tithe to grain tithe (set aside after completion, i.e., after birth = Elul); Rabbi Elazar holds it is set aside at the beginning of the new year (Tishrei).
QUESTION 3 OF 5
From which verse does the Gemara derive that Rosh Hashana is the day of Divine judgment?
A. "You shall afflict your souls on the tenth day of the seventh month" (Leviticus 16:29)
B. "Blow a shofar at the New Moon, at the covered time for our Festival day... a judgment of the God of Jacob" (Psalms 81:4–5)
C. "A land that the Lord your God cares for; the eyes of the Lord are always upon it, from the beginning of the year" (Deuteronomy 11:12)
D. "And you shall hallow the fiftieth year and proclaim liberty throughout the land" (Leviticus 25:10)
Correct: B — Psalms 81:4–5 is the primary proof: 'Blow a shofar at the New Moon, at the covered time (keseh) for our Festival.' The moon is hidden only on Rosh Hashana (all other Festivals are mid-month). The next verse calls it 'a judgment of the God of Jacob,' proving Rosh Hashana is the annual day of judgment.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yishmael son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, what is the unique status of Hebrew slaves between 1 Tishrei and 10 Tishrei of the Jubilee Year?
A. They are fully free and may return home immediately upon Rosh Hashana
B. They continue working as slaves until Yom Kippur, when they are formally released
C. They are neither released to their homes nor enslaved to their masters — they eat, drink, and rejoice in a liminal state
D. They must remain in their master's household but receive double wages until the shofar is blown
Correct: C — Rabbi Yishmael holds that the Jubilee is sanctified from Rosh Hashana (1 Tishrei), but formal liberation requires the Yom Kippur shofar (10 Tishrei). In the interim 10-day period, slaves occupy a unique liminal state: not yet free to go home, but no longer obligated to their masters — they eat, drink, and rejoice.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does the Gemara resolve the apparent contradiction between Rav Ḥisda (New Year for kings is 1 Nisan) and Rav Pappa (New Year for years/documents is 1 Tishrei)?
A. Rav Ḥisda's ruling was later retracted in favor of Rav Pappa
B. Nisan applies to Jewish kings' documents; Tishrei applies to documents dated by gentile kings' reigns
C. Nisan is used for monetary documents; Tishrei is used for land and property documents
D. The Tishrei rule applies only in the Land of Israel; Nisan applies in the Diaspora
Correct: B — The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction by distinguishing between types of kingdoms: documents dated according to Jewish kings use Nisan (the first biblical month); documents dated according to gentile kings use Tishrei as the start of the new year. Both rulings stand simultaneously for different contexts.
