🔔 What Makes a Jubilee Year Valid?

Rabbi Yehuda
Freeing slaves is the indispensable criterion. The verse's use of deror (liberty) points most directly to slave liberation — without it, the year is not a Jubilee at all.
Rabbi Yosei
Sounding the shofar is the indispensable criterion. It is entrusted to the court, which can always fulfill it. Even without freeing slaves or returning land, if the shofar is blown, it is a Jubilee Year.
The Rabbis
All three are equally indispensable: releasing property to its owners, sounding the shofar, AND liberating slaves. All must be performed for a valid Jubilee.
Core Question The Jubilee (Yovel) has three components. Which, if any, is the sine qua non — the act whose omission nullifies the entire Jubilee Year?

🔔 Rabbi Yosei's Two Arguments for Shofar as Criterion

Argument 1 — Court vs. Individual
Inclusion vs. Exclusion logic: One verse includes all Jubilee acts; another excludes to name an essential one. The shofar is entrusted to the court (a reliable body), while slave-freeing is left to each individual owner — so the court's duty is more indispensable.
Argument 2 — The Alternatively
Edge case: It is conceivable that no Hebrew slaves exist at the onset of Jubilee — making slave-freeing impossible. The court, however, can always blow the shofar. What can never be performed cannot be the indispensable criterion.
Why Two Arguments? Argument 1 assumes slave owners always exist. The second argument addresses: what if there are truly no slaves? Only then is the alternative needed — ensuring the shofar remains the criterion in all conceivable scenarios.

📊 Three Opinions: What Defines the Jubilee Year?

Rabbi Yehuda

  • Essential act: Freeing Slaves
  • Deror (liberty) refers primarily to slaves
  • Without liberation → not a Jubilee
  • Shofar alone does not sanctify the year

Rabbi Yosei

  • Essential act: Shofar Blowing
  • Court-based — always performable
  • Even without slaves freed or land returned, the year is Yovel
  • Individual duties cannot be the sine qua non

The Rabbis (via R. Yoḥanan)

  • Essential acts: All Three
  • 1. Returning property to owners
  • 2. Sounding the shofar
  • 3. Liberating Hebrew slaves
  • Omitting any one nullifies the Jubilee

🌱 Orla Counting: Why Tishrei, Not Nisan?

Question: From where do we derive that 1 Tishrei marks the new year for Orla (three-year fruit prohibition)?
↓
Rejected Approach
Derive via gezera shava (verbal analogy) from Nisan: 'It shall be the first of months' → year begins in Nisan
Accepted Approach
Derive by analogy to Shemita: just as the Sabbatical year begins at Tishrei, so too the Orla year begins at Tishrei
↓
Conclusion: Orla years are counted from 1 Tishrei — Rosh Hashana is the new year for trees

📅 The Thirty-Day Rule for Planting

Case: One plants a tree, layers a vine, or grafts a branch 30 days before Rosh Hashana on the eve of the Shemita year
↓
Rule: Upon arrival of Rosh Hashana, the 30-day period is counted retroactively as the tree's entire first year of Orla
↓
Result: The tree enters its second Orla year at the next Rosh Hashana — saving nearly a full calendar year in the three-year wait
↓
Threshold: If planted fewer than 30 days before Rosh Hashana, the partial period does NOT count as a full year — the Orla count restarts from Rosh Hashana
↓
Principle: Thirty days grants sufficient rootedness for the tree to be considered legally established before the new year begins
