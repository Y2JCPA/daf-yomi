⚔️ Source of Tosefet: Adding to Sacred Time

Rabbi Akiva
The verse 'In plowing and in harvest you shall rest' (Ex. 34:21) teaches tosefet — one must add from the profane to the sacred by refraining from Sabbatical-year plowing both before and after the Shemitah year itself.
Rabbi Yishmael
That verse refers to ordinary Shabbat restrictions, not Shemitah. I derive tosefet from: 'You shall afflict your souls on the ninth of the month in the evening' — fasting begins before the 10th, adding from the 9th.
Rabbi Akiva
Then what does the ninth-of-the-month verse teach me? It cannot mean literally fasting on the 9th — rather, one who eats and drinks on the 9th and then fasts on the 10th is credited as if he fasted both days!
Shared Conclusion Both Tannaim agree: one must extend Yom Kippur at both ends — they dispute only the scriptural source for the principle of tosefet.

📋 How Tosefet Works: Extending Sacred Time

Core Principle (תוספת קדושה): One must add time from the חול (profane) to the קודש (sacred) — at both the beginning and the end of every sacred period.
↓
Before: Even while forbidden labor is technically still permitted, one must already accept the holiness of the incoming Shabbat or Yom Tov and cease work.
↓
After: When the sacred time ends, one waits briefly before resuming forbidden activities — extending honor to the departing sanctity.
↓
Yom Kippur Proof: 'From evening to evening shall you rest on your Shabbat' (Lev. 23:32) — the fast begins on the evening of the 9th and extends through the evening of the 10th.
↓
Extension to All Shabbatot: The word תִּשְׁבְּתוּ (tishbetu — you shall rest) alludes that the same extension applies to every regular Shabbat throughout the year.

🔔 Three Requirements of the Jubilee Year — Who Is Indispensable?

🔊 Shofar Blowing

  • Blown on Yom Kippur of the 50th year
  • Obligation of the court (communal)
  • If court blows, all of Israel is affected
  • R. Yosei: This alone is indispensable
  • Given over to the court — cannot be avoided

🏡 Property Return

  • Land reverts to ancestral owners
  • Obligation of each seller (private)
  • Civil/economic dimension of Jubilee
  • Rabbis: One of three required
  • 'Each man shall return to his holding' (Lev. 25:10)

⛓️ Slave Liberation

  • Hebrew slaves go free at Jubilee
  • Obligation of each slave owner (private)
  • Defined by word deror — liberty
  • R. Yehuda: This alone is indispensable
  • 'Proclaim deror throughout all the land'

⚖️ Rabbi Yehuda vs. Rabbi Yosei: The Jubilee Debate

Rabbi Yehuda
Slave liberation is the indispensable criterion. The verse says 'proclaim deror' — and deror means liberty, specifically freedom of the enslaved. Without releasing slaves, there is no Jubilee at all.
Rabbi Yosei
The shofar is indispensable. It is given over to the court — a communal obligation they can always fulfill. Slave liberation is given to each individual owner; you cannot penalize the entire nation because one master refuses to comply.
Rabbi Yosei
Alternatively: Even if you say there may not be slaves in every generation — the shofar can always be blown. The public, court-mandated act is the anchor of the entire institution of Jubilee.
Rabbis
All three are indispensable together: shofar, property return, and slave liberation. The verse 'Jubilee Year' is a term of inclusion that counters each exclusionary reading — all three must be present.
Key Verse "And you shall hallow the fiftieth year, and proclaim deror throughout all the land to all its inhabitants; it shall be a Jubilee for you" (Lev. 25:10)

🕊️ What Is Deror? — The Meaning of Liberty

דְּרוֹר — כְּאָדָם הַמְּדַיֵּר בְּכָל מָקוֹם שֶׁרוֹצֶה וּמַעֲבִיר חֲפָצָיו מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם
דְּרוֹר — Deror A baraita teaches: Deror is a term meaning only liberty. Rabbi Yehuda elaborates: it is like a person who medayyer (dwells, from דּוּר) wherever he pleases and carries his possessions from place to place freely — like a bird unconfined. A slave who receives deror can return home, settle anywhere, and live without restriction. This freedom of movement and residence is the essence of what the Jubilee proclaims.

🌱 Orla Years: Why 1 Tishrei Is the New Year for Planting

IssueRule / ReasoningSource
What is orla?Fruit of a tree's first 3 years is forbidden for useLev. 19:23 — 'three years it shall be orlah to you'
Which Rosh HaShana counts?1 Tishrei — the New Year for trees/planting yearsMishna RH 1:1; baraita below
Why not 1 Nisan?1 Nisan is the New Year for kings and festivals, not agricultural year-countingVerbal analogy (gezera shava) on 'year' would lead here — but is rejected
30-day ruleIf planted ≥30 days before 1 Tishrei, Rosh HaShana counts as the end of Year 1 of orlaBaraita: 'thirty days before Rosh HaShana is considered a year'
Fewer than 30 days before?The partial year does NOT count — Year 1 begins only at the following Rosh HaShanaSame baraita; 30 days is the minimum for a planting to be considered established
