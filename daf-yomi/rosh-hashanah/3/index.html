☁️ Aaron Dies — The Clouds Depart When Aaron died, the Clouds of Glory that had shielded Israel in the desert were removed. Rabbi Abbahu teaches: do not read vayiru — 'they (the Canaanites) saw' — but rather vayeira'u — 'they (Israel) were seen.' The protective covering was gone, and suddenly Israel was exposed. The Canaanite king of Arad heard of Aaron's death and seized on Israel's vulnerability to attack. The word ki ('that') in the verse signals a cause: because the clouds had been removed.

🔍 Ruling Out the Months — Only Nisan Survives

The Challenge: Could counting years of kings begin in any month — Iyar, Sivan, Tammuz, Av, or Adar?
↓
Not Iyar: The Tabernacle was erected on 1 Nisan of Year 2 (Exodus 40:17). If Iyar were the New Year, Nisan would fall in Year 1 — contradicting the verse.
↓
Not Sivan: Israel arrived at Sinai 'in the third month' of Year 1 (Exodus 19:1). Sivan is already anchored as the third month of a Nisan-based year.
↓
Not Tammuz, Av, or Adar: No biblical event or chronological marker establishes any of these as the start of a new year in the Exodus count.
↓
Conclusion: By systematic elimination, Nisan alone can be the New Year for counting kings' years — all other candidates are ruled out by the Torah's own chronology.

🏛️ Rabbi Elazar's Proof vs. Ravina's Challenges

R. Elazar
Proof from II Chronicles 3:2: Solomon began building 'in the second month, in the second, in the fourth year of his reign.' The redundant 'the second' refers to Iyar — the second month. Since Iyar is Month 2, the fourth year must have begun in Nisan!
Ravina
Objection! Perhaps 'the second' simply means the second day of the month — not the second month.
Gemara
If so, Scripture would have written 'on the second of the month' — that is the standard biblical formulation. The unusual phrasing proves 'the second' refers to the second month, i.e., Iyar.
Ravina
Then perhaps 'the second' means the second day of the week — Monday?
Gemara
Two refutations: (1) Day of the week is never mentioned this way anywhere in Scripture. (2) The parallel verse in I Kings 6:1 calls this month 'Ziv' — an ancient name for Iyar, the second month. Case closed.
Ruling Rabbi Elazar's proof stands: Iyar is the second month from Nisan — confirming that Nisan opens the royal year.

⚖️ Rav Ḥisda's Distinction: Two Different New Years for Kings

Jewish Kings 🇮🇱

  • New Year counted from 1 Nisan
  • Source: Baraita derived from Exodus chronology
  • Confirmed by Solomon's Temple (II Chronicles 3:2)
  • Even one day of Nisan counts as a full regnal year
  • Used for dating royal documents, legal calculations

Gentile Kings 🌍

  • New Year counted from 1 Tishrei
  • Source: Nehemiah 1:1 and 2:1 — both say 'the twentieth year' of Artaxerxes
  • Kislev (9th month) and Nisan (1st month) share the same year number
  • This is only possible if Tishrei — not Nisan — starts the year
  • Applies to Persian and other foreign monarchies
