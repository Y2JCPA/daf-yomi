👑 When Does a King's Regnal Year Begin?

Rav Ḥisda
Jewish kings count regnal years from 1 Nisan. Gentile kings count from 1 Tishrei.
Gemara
Proof for gentiles: Nehemiah 1:1 calls Kislev 'the 20th year' of Artaxerxes; Nehemiah 2:1 calls the following Nisan also the 20th year — so the new year for gentile kings falls in Tishrei.
Rav Yosef
Objection! Haggai 1:1 dates the 1st of Elul in the 2nd year of Darius, and Haggai 2:10 places the 24th of Kislev also in year 2 — this looks like a Nisan reckoning, not Tishrei!
Rabbi Abbahu
Answer: Cyrus was a righteous king (melech kasher), so his years were counted like those of a Jewish king — from Nisan.
Rav Yosef
Two objections: (1) The verse discusses Darius, not Cyrus! (2) Darius was never called righteous — Rabbi Abbahu's answer doesn't apply here!
Rabbi Yitzḥak
Resolution: While Ezra was in Babylonia, Darius behaved righteously → counted from Nisan. After Ezra ascended to Israel, Darius became corrupt → counted from Tishrei.
⚠️ Still Difficult Rav Kahana objects: Did Darius really become corrupt after Ezra left? The Gemara leaves this difficulty unresolved at the end of the daf.

📅 Two New Years — Two Types of Kings

🕍 Jewish Kings of Israel

  • New Year begins: 1 Nisan (Rosh HaShanah l'melachim)
  • Even a king crowned in Adar enters 'Year 2' on the very next Nisan
  • Proof from Solomon: Temple construction began in Iyar (2nd month), '4th year' of Solomon = 480th year from Exodus
  • The 480th year from the Exodus counts from Nisan → Solomon's reign counts from Nisan
  • Baraita: 'We count the years of kings only from Nisan'
  • Source: I Kings 6:1; II Chronicles 3:2

🏛️ Gentile Kings of the World

  • New Year begins: 1 Tishrei
  • Even a king crowned mid-year, his year 2 starts next Tishrei
  • Proof from Nehemiah: Kislev = '20th year' of Artaxerxes AND Nisan = still '20th year'
  • If Nisan were the start, Nisan would be year 21 — but it is not
  • Verbal analogy (gezeirah shavah) links both '20th year' mentions to Artaxerxes
  • Source: Nehemiah 1:1 and 2:1

📜 Nehemiah's Timeline — Step-by-Step Proof for Tishrei New Year

Month of Kislev, '20th year' of Artaxerxes: Hanani arrives in Shushan and reports to Nehemiah that Jerusalem's walls are broken down and its gates burned (Nehemiah 1:1–3)
↓
Nehemiah fasts, mourns, and prays for days — he has not yet spoken to the king
↓
Month of Nisan, '20th year' of Artaxerxes: Nehemiah serves wine to King Artaxerxes; the king notices Nehemiah's sadness and asks what he wants (Nehemiah 2:1)
↓
Nehemiah prays silently to God, then requests permission to travel to Judea to rebuild Jerusalem — the king grants it (Nehemiah 2:4–6)
↓
Critical observation: The baraita confirms — what Hanani told Nehemiah in Kislev, Nehemiah reported to the king in Nisan. Kislev precedes Nisan, yet both are year 20.
↓
Conclusion: The year did not change between Kislev and Nisan → the new year for Artaxerxes (a gentile king) must fall in Tishrei, which lies between the previous Nisan and this Kislev.

⚖️ The Darius Contradiction

Rav Yosef
Challenge to Rav Ḥisda: Haggai 1:1 — '24th of Elul, second year of Darius.' Haggai 2:10 — '24th of Kislev, second year.' Elul and Kislev are both year 2 → this is a Nisan reckoning!
Rabbi Abbahu
Cyrus was a righteous king — so Haggai counted his years from Nisan, like a Jewish king.
Rav Yosef
Objection 1: Ezra 6:15 says the Temple was completed in the 6th year of Darius (Adar) — yet Haggai 2:10 also has a '6th year.' If you say Haggai uses Nisan reckoning for Cyrus, and Ezra uses Tishrei for Darius — they contradict each other!
Rav Yosef
Objection 2: More fundamentally — Rabbi Abbahu spoke of Cyrus being virtuous, but Haggai 1:1 names Darius. Darius was never called righteous! The answer doesn't fit the question.
Rabbi Yitzḥak
Resolution: When Ezra was still in Babylonia, Darius behaved righteously → counted from Nisan. After Ezra ascended to Eretz Yisrael, Darius became corrupt → counted from Tishrei.
Rav Kahana
Strong objection: Did Darius really become corrupt right after Ezra left? But it is written… [the Gemara ends with this difficulty unresolved]
תיקו The daf ends with Rav Kahana's objection unresolved — a rare open-ended conclusion (teku) suggesting the question awaits future resolution.
