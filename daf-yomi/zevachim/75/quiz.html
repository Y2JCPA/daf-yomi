QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava, what does the baraita's distinction between 'alive' and 'slaughtered' offerings actually refer to?
A. Whether the blood is in separate cups vs. mixed in one cup
B. Whether the animals can still be identified by their owners
C. Whether semicha (laying of hands) was already performed
D. Whether the animals were consecrated before or after intermingling
Correct: A — Rava reinterprets 'alive' as meaning separate cups (each animal's blood distinct, like living animals) and 'slaughtered mixed' as blood combined in a single cup — the physical state of the blood, not the animals, is what matters.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does the Gemara resolve the contradiction between R' Yehuda HaNasi requiring a minimum blood measure for altar placement but saying water of purification needs no minimum?
A. R' Yehuda HaNasi changed his mind over time
B. Either he cited R' Eliezer without agreeing, or they are two discrete halakhot
C. Water of purification is d'Rabbanan while blood is d'Oraisa
D. The minimum only applies to communal offerings, not individual ones
Correct: B — The Gemara offers two resolutions: (1) R' Yehuda HaNasi merely reported R' Eliezer's view without endorsing it, or (2) sprinkling purification water and placing blood on the altar are separate, independent halakhot with different rules.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why do offerings intermingled with a bechor need to graze until they develop a blemish?
A. Because a bechor can only be eaten after developing a mum, and the animals cannot be distinguished
B. Because the Torah requires all mixed offerings to be treated as bechor
C. Because the animals need time for their sanctity to expire
D. Because the kohen must wait for a beit din ruling before slaughtering
Correct: A — Since a bechor cannot be sacrificed as another korban and the intermingled animals cannot be identified, all must wait for a blemish (mum) and then be eaten under bechor restrictions — preserving the dignity of kodashim.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the halakha regarding the substitute (temura) of a bechor, according to the baraita cited by Rava?
A. The substitute has no sanctity and may be used freely
B. The substitute has the same restrictions as the bechor itself
C. The substitute may be redeemed but not weighed by litra
D. The substitute is offered as a shelamim instead
Correct: B — The baraita teaches that substitutes of bechor and ma'aser have the same status as the original — all restrictions (no feeding to niddah per Beit Shammai, no redemption, no weighing by litra) apply equally to the temura.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why do Rav Huna and R' Chizkiyya distinguish between the mishna's case and a bechor dedicated to bedek habayis regarding weighing by litra?
A. Because bedek habayis has greater sanctity than a regular offering
B. Because the mishna involves two sanctities in two bodies, while dedication involves two sanctities in one body
C. Because Temple maintenance overrides all rabbinic prohibitions
D. Because the kohen loses ownership when he dedicates to bedek habayis
Correct: B — They argue the cases differ: the mishna has two separate animals (two bodies, two sanctities) so the bechor's dignity restricts the other animal. But when a kohen dedicates his own bechor to bedek habayis, two sanctities exist in one body — potentially allowing Temple profit considerations to override the litra prohibition.
