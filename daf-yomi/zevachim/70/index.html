Chelev Purity: Which Fats Are Pure?

Leviticus 7:24
"The fat of a neveila and the fat of a tereifa may be used for any service"
↓
Which animals does this purity apply to?
Kosher Domesticated
Chelev is pure — fat forbidden, meat permitted
Kosher Undomesticated
Chelev is impure — "you shall not eat of it" excludes these
Non-Kosher Animals
Chelev is impure — tereifa doesn't apply to their species
Key Principle The word tereifa in the verse limits the purity of chelev to species where the concept of tereifa is meaningful — i.e., kosher domesticated animals.

Why Does the Verse Say 'Tereifa'?

Gemara
The impurity of a non-kosher animal's chelev can already be derived from "you shall not eat of it" — it excludes fats not forbidden as chelev. So tereifa in the verse seems superfluous!
Initial Answer
Perhaps tereifa includes the chelev of a kosher undomesticated animal (chaya) carcass, teaching it is pure.
Gemara
But if a chaya's chelev is pure, why would a non-kosher animal's chelev be impure? In both cases fat is not distinct from meat!
Abaye
Tereifa teaches that the chelev of a tereifa of a kosher domesticated animal is pure — don't compare it to a non-kosher animal just because both are forbidden while alive.
Gemara
But can you really derive tereifa from non-kosher? A tereifa once had a kosher period — a non-kosher animal never did. They are incomparable!
Challenge A kal va-chomer from non-kosher to tereifa fails because the two categories are fundamentally different — tereifa had a prior kosher status.

Rava: Double Liability for Chelev

Chelev of a Neveila

  • Prohibition of chelev already exists
  • Prohibition of neveila takes effect on top
  • Liable for two prohibitions
  • Needed because: neveila imparts tumah

Chelev of a Tereifa

  • Prohibition of chelev already exists
  • Prohibition of tereifa takes effect on top
  • Liable for two prohibitions
  • Needed because: tereifa is forbidden while alive
Rava's Reading Both terms in the verse teach issur chal al issur — a new prohibition takes effect even where another already applies. Each case is needed because neither could be derived from the other.

R' Meir: Pinching a Bird Found to Be Tereifa

Mishna (R' Meir)
A bird offering whose nape was pinched (melika) and was found to be a tereifa does not impart impurity as a carcass (neveila).
R' Yochanan
R' Meir deemed pure only unblemished birds — those actually fit for sacrifice. Blemished birds are not purified by melika.
R' Elazar
R' Meir deemed even blemished birds pure. He did not distinguish between them.
Rav Beivai / R' Elazar
R' Meir would even deem geese and chickens pure — species never brought as offerings and never subject to melika!
Dispute R' Yochanan limits R' Meir's leniency to Temple-eligible birds; R' Elazar extends it to all birds, even species that are never sacrificed.

R' Yirmeya's Dilemma: Breaking the Neck of a Goat

R' Meir (per R' Elazar): Melika purifies even geese & chickens, because they are species of birds
↓
Does breaking the neck of a goat similarly prevent neveila impurity?
Not Pure
A goat is small livestock; a heifer is large livestock. They are different species — no analogy.
Pure
A goat is still a domesticated animal like a heifer — close enough to extend the analogy.
Underlying Assumption This dilemma assumes the eglah arufah (heifer whose neck is broken) is itself pure — a point Abaye immediately questions.
