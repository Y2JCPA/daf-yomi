Impurity, Invalid Service & the Status of Sacrificial Blood

Reish Lakish
An impure person who eats sacrificial meat before the sprinkling of blood is flogged — "Every consecrated item she shall not touch" applies at all times.
R' Yochanan
He is not flogged — the gezeira shava of "his impurity" links the prohibition only to meat after the sprinkling of blood.
Reish Lakish
If it meant only after sprinkling, the verse should say "a consecrated item." The word "every" includes before sprinkling too.
R' Yochanan
An invalid blood presentation does not render the remaining blood a remainder — only a fit priest's intentional piggul-creating act does.
Core Theme This daf explores the boundaries of sacrificial prohibitions — when does impurity trigger punishment, and when does an invalid act still carry halachic consequences?

Impure Person Eating Meat Before Sprinkling

Reish Lakish
He is flogged. The verse says "Every consecrated item she shall not touch" — no time limitation is specified.
R' Yochanan
He is not flogged. The gezeira shava of "his impurity" (טֻמְאָתוֹ) links this prohibition to after the sprinkling of blood.
Reish Lakish
If only post-sprinkling, the verse should say "a consecrated item." The extra word "כָּל" (every) teaches two conclusions — including before sprinkling.
Key Verse "כׇּל קֹדֶשׁ לֹא תִגָּע" — Every consecrated item she shall not touch (Leviticus 12:4)

Impurity of the Body vs. Impurity of the Meat

Abaye's View

  • The Reish Lakish / R' Yochanan dispute is about impurity of the body (the person eating)
  • If the meat itself is impure — all agree he is flogged
  • Proof: "And the flesh" includes even wood and frankincense
  • If non-food items are included, certainly edible pre-sprinkling meat is included

Rava's View

  • The dispute is about impurity of the body
  • If the meat itself is impure — all agree he is NOT flogged
  • Since karet doesn't apply to impure meat before sprinkling, the prohibition also doesn't apply
  • Wood & frankincense are different: they were sanctified in a vessel
Abaye vs. Rava They agree the Reish Lakish / R' Yochanan debate concerns impurity of the person — but disagree about what happens when the meat itself is impure before sprinkling.

When Is One Liable for Eating Impure Items?

Does the item have permitting factors
(מַתִּירִין)?
Yes (e.g. sacrificial meat)
Liable only after the permitting factors are sacrificed (e.g. blood sprinkled)
No (e.g. frankincense)
Liable from the moment it is sanctified in a vessel (קִדְּשׁוּ בִּכְלִי)
↓
This resolves Rava's position: wood & frankincense sanctified in a vessel are like items whose permitting factors were completed
Mishna Me'ila 10a Items with permitting factors — liable after those factors are sacrificed. Items without — liable from sanctification in a vessel.

R' YochananHe is not floggedA prohibition derived from a positive mitzva (לאו הבא מכלל עשה) does not carry the punishment of lashes

Principle לָאו הַבָּא מִכְּלַל עֲשֵׂה — A prohibition implied by a positive commandment: does it carry the force of a full negative prohibition?

Does Invalid Blood Presentation Create a 'Remainder'?

Reish Lakish
If an unfit person presented the blood, what is the status of the remaining blood? Is it now a שִׁיָּרִים (remainder)?
R' Yochanan
An invalid presenting does not render the rest a remainder — unless a fit priest presented with piggul intent (חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ / חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ).
Rav Zevid
Alternative version: Reish Lakish asked about a cup of disqualified blood that was presented — does it create a remainder?
R' Yochanan
The ruling on disqualified blood follows the ruling on an unfit person — they stand or fall together.
Three Versions Rav Zevid and Rav Yirmeya of Difti each transmit a different version of this exchange, reflecting the complexity of the tradition.
