Zevachim 108 — Fitness, Purity & Liability Outside the Courtyard

DAF OVERVIEW This daf explores the boundaries of liability for acts performed outside the Temple courtyard. It examines whether salt completes an olive-bulk for pigeon offerings, how fitness of a sacrifice determines liability for offering outside, the rules for impure persons eating kodshim, whether two people acting jointly are liable, and how multiple lapses of awareness affect sin-offering obligations.

Does Salt Complete the Olive-Bulk?

Head of pigeon burnt offering has less than a kezayit of flesh
↓
Salt adhering to it completes the measure to a kezayit
↓
Is one liable for offering it up outside the courtyard?
R' Yochanan's Logic
Bone completes the measure because it is the same kind as flesh — but salt is not the same kind as pigeon
Reish Lakish's Logic
Bone doesn't complete because if separated there's no mitzvah to offer it — but salt, if separated, has a mitzvah to be offered
TEIKU The dilemma stands unresolved — it cannot be determined from either position whether salt completes the measure.

Slaughter Inside, Offer Outside — R' Yosei HaGelili vs. the Rabbis

R' Yosei HaGelili
Slaughtered inside then offered outside → liable. Slaughtered outside then offered outside → exempt, because he offered up something already unfit.
Rabbis
By your logic, even slaughtering inside and offering outside should be exempt — the moment he took it outside, it became unfit!
R' Yosei HaGelili
No — that case is different because the offering had a period of fitness before it was taken out. Slaughtered outside? It was never fit.
KEY PRINCIPLE Liability for offering up outside depends on whether the sacrifice ever had a period of fitness (sha'at ha-kosher).

Two Defenses of R' Yosei HaGelili

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi

  • The offering had a period of fitness (sha'at ha-kosher)
  • Slaughtered outside → never had fitness → exempt
  • Slaughtered at night inside → exempt (disqualified from outset)
  • Blood collected in non-sacred vessel → exempt (disqualified from outset)

R' Elazar b'R' Shimon

  • The disqualification occurred in sanctity (b'kodesh)
  • If placed on altar, sanctity makes it acceptable — not removed
  • Slaughtered at night inside → liable (disqualification in sanctity)
  • Blood in non-sacred vessel → liable (disqualification in sanctity)
PRACTICAL DIFFERENCES Ze'eiri: Slaughtered at night inside, offered outside. Rabba: Blood collected in non-sacred vessel, offered outside. These cases distinguish the two defenses.

Impure Person Eating Impure Sacrificial Food

R' Yosei HaGelili
An impure person who eats pure sacrificial food → liable. But one who eats impure sacrificial food → exempt, as he merely ate an impure item.
Rabbis
By your logic, even eating pure food should be exempt — the moment he touches it, he renders it impure!
Rava
The key is: where the person is first rendered impure and the food was already impure — the prohibition applies only to pure sacrificial food.
PARALLEL STRUCTURE This dispute mirrors the slaughtering debate: R' Yosei HaGelili consistently requires the object to be fit/pure for liability to apply.
