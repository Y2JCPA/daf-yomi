QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why is the question about salt completing the olive-bulk measure NOT simply resolved by the R' Yochanan/Reish Lakish dispute about bone?
A. Salt differs from bone — it is not 'the same kind' as flesh, AND if separated it still has a mitzvah to be offered
B. Salt is always considered part of the offering regardless of the dispute
C. R' Yochanan and Reish Lakish never discussed the pigeon offering
D. The dispute about bone was already resolved in favor of Reish Lakish
Correct: A — Salt differs from bone in two ways: (1) unlike bone, it's not the same kind as flesh (relevant to R' Yochanan), and (2) unlike bone, if separated it still has a mitzvah to be offered (relevant to Reish Lakish). So neither position clearly resolves the salt question.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the practical difference between R' Yehuda HaNasi's and R' Elazar b'R' Shimon's defenses of R' Yosei HaGelili?
A. Whether slaughtering at night inside or collecting blood in a non-sacred vessel creates liability when the animal is then offered outside
B. Whether the Torah requires intent for liability
C. Whether two people acting together are liable
D. Whether an impure person can eat sacrificial food
Correct: A — R' Yehuda HaNasi focuses on 'period of fitness' (slaughter at night = never fit = exempt), while R' Elazar focuses on 'disqualification in sanctity' (slaughter at night inside = sanctity context = liable). These cases reveal the difference.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to R' Yosei, why are two people who offer up a limb together outside the courtyard exempt?
A. He derives from 'that man' (hahu) that only one who acts alone is liable
B. He derives from 'ish ish' that two acting together are exempt
C. The Torah requires a complete animal to be offered
D. Two people cannot form proper intent simultaneously
Correct: A — R' Yosei holds 'ish ish' is merely normal speech (the Torah spoke in the language of people), so he derives exemption for joint actors from 'that (hahu) man' — the singular form indicates only an individual is liable.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does the Rabbis' challenge to R' Yosei HaGelili about an impure person eating pure food parallel their challenge about slaughtering inside?
A. In both cases, the act of the person itself creates the disqualification — taking the offering outside disqualifies it, and touching pure food renders it impure
B. Both cases involve the same Torah verse
C. Both cases deal with karet penalties exclusively
D. In both cases R' Yosei HaGelili agrees with the Rabbis' conclusion
Correct: A — The Rabbis use the same logical structure: just as taking an offering outside disqualifies it yet the person is still liable, so too when an impure person touches pure food and renders it impure, he should still be liable for eating it.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In the dispute about multiple limbs offered outside in separate lapses of awareness, what does R' Yosei hold 'to sacrifice it' (Leviticus 17:9) refers to?
A. The whole animal — liability is incurred only once the entire animal has been offered
B. Each individual limb — liability is incurred per limb
C. The altar itself — liability depends on where it was offered
D. The intent of the person — liability depends on awareness
Correct: A — R' Yosei holds 'to sacrifice it' refers to the whole animal. Therefore, even if limbs were offered in separate lapses of awareness, there is only one sin offering obligation — the unit of liability is the complete animal, not each limb.
