What Does 'A Man's Olah' Exclude?

R' Yehuda
It excludes the olah of consecrated property (עולת הקדש) — offerings from leftover funds.
R' Yosei b'R' Yehuda
It excludes the olah of converts (עולת גרים) who died without heirs.
R' Chiyya bar Yosef
R' Yehuda means: a leftover Asham animal sold and used as a communal gift offering (קיץ המזבח) — not 'a man's' offering.
R' Aivu / R' Yannai
R' Yehuda means: one who consecrates an Olah for Temple maintenance (בדק הבית) — shifting ownership away from the individual.
Key Dispute The phrase עולת אדם ('a man's olah') limits which hides Kohanim receive — but the Tannaim disagree on what it excludes.

Did R' Yehuda Retract?

Rav Nachman / Rabba bar Avuh
"A man's olah" excludes an olah from leftover property — Kohanim don't get that hide.
Rav Hamnuna
That follows R' Yehuda — but didn't he retract?
Rav Hamnuna
R' Nechemya challenged him: If Kohanim don't get hides from the 6 shofar-boxes (קופות), you've nullified Yehoyada HaKohen's teaching!
R' Yehuda
Yehoyada taught: surplus Asham money goes to Olos. If Kohanim get no hide from these, the entire institution is undermined.
Narrator
R' Yehuda accepted the challenge and retracted his position.
Yehoyada HaKohen His teaching (Shekalim 6:6): surplus Asham money buys Olos — the flesh goes to Hashem, the hides to Kohanim. Denying hides would undermine this.

Three Approaches: Hides of Kodshei Kodashim

First Tanna

  • Derives from verse: "עור העולה אשר הקריב"
  • Includes all Kodshei Kodashim
  • "Olah" limits to most sacred only
  • Excludes Kodashim Kalim

R' Yishmael

  • Derives via Kal Va-Chomer logic
  • Olah: no meat → yes hide
  • Kodshei Kodashim: yes meat → certainly hide!
  • Mizbe'ach doesn't disprove: it never gets hides

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi

  • Verse needed only for Olah itself
  • Other offerings: hide follows flesh naturally
  • Parim/se'irim burned → hides burned too
  • Chatat/Asham to Kohen → hide included
Rabbi's Insight Without the verse, we'd think the Olah hide is burned with the flesh. The verse is needed to separate the hide from the altar — not to extend to other offerings.
