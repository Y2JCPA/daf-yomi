Daf 11: Blood in the Sanctuary & the Sanctity Hierarchy

Does blood entering the Sanctuary disqualify an offering?
↓
Sin Offering
Disqualified per Lev. 6:23
Other Kodshei Kodashim
Not disqualified — derived from "whose blood"

Separate question: Slaughtering for the sake of a different offering

Shimon brother of Azarya: For greater sanctity → fit; for lesser sanctity → unfit
Key Theme The Gemara explores two distinct mechanisms of disqualification: blood entering the Sanctuary, and sacrificing an offering for the wrong purpose — and whether sanctity hierarchy determines the outcome.

Kal Va'chomer: Does Guilt Offering Blood Survive Entering the Sanctuary?

Burnt offering (Olah) — entirely consumed on altar, yet blood entering Sanctuary doesn't disqualify
↓
Kal va'chomer: Guilt offering (Asham) — not entirely consumed — should certainly not be disqualified!
↓
Refutation: Olah is unique — it doesn't atone for sin
↓
Meal offering of a sinner proves the point — atones for sin yet not disqualified
↓
Refutation: Meal offering is not a slaughtered offering
↓
Back to Olah (which is slaughtered) → Tzad HaShaveh from both together
Talmudic Method The Gemara uses a classic revolving kal va'chomer (a fortiori) that fails individually but succeeds as a tzad hashaveh (common element argument) combining burnt offering and sinner's meal offering.

Rava of Barnish Challenges the Tzad HaShaveh

Gemara
The common element of Olah and sinner's meal offering: both are Kodshei Kodashim and blood entering the Sanctuary doesn't disqualify → same should apply to Asham
Rava of Barnish
But both Olah and sinner's meal offering have no fixed monetary value! An Asham does have a fixed value — you can't derive from them!
Gemara
Rather, the Rabbis' true source is the verse: "Any sin offering whose blood is brought into the Tent of Meeting" (Lev. 6:23)
Gemara
"Whose blood" — the blood of this offering (sin offering) is disqualified, not the blood of other offerings
Pirka The kal va'chomer was only an initial attempt. The actual halakhic source is a gezeirat hakatuv — a Scriptural derivation from the word "דָּמָהּ" (whose blood).

Rabbi Eliezer vs. Rabbis: Interpreting "Whose Blood" (דָּמָהּ)

Rabbis (Chachamim)

  • "Whose blood" excludes TWO things:
  • 1) Blood of OTHER offerings — not disqualified by entering Sanctuary
  • 2) FLESH of a sin offering — not disqualified by entering Sanctuary
  • Derived from redundancy: could have said just "blood" instead of "whose blood"

Rabbi Eliezer

  • "Whose blood" teaches only ONE thing:
  • Only the BLOOD is disqualified — not the FLESH
  • Does NOT exclude other offerings' blood
  • Does not derive extra meaning from "whose blood" vs. "blood"
Practical Difference For the Rabbis, a guilt offering whose blood enters the Sanctuary remains fit. For Rabbi Eliezer, it is disqualified — just like a sin offering.

Rabbi Yehoshua Refutes Rabbi Eliezer on Pesach/Olah

R' Eliezer
A Paschal offering slaughtered for the sake of another offering should render that other offering unfit — kal va'chomer from sin offering
R' Yehoshua
Your logic backfires! A leftover sin offering becomes a burnt offering, but a leftover burnt offering does NOT become a sin offering
R' Yehoshua
By your reasoning: if a sin offering slaughtered as an Olah is unfit — then an Olah slaughtered as a sin offering should certainly be unfit! Yet it's fit!
R' Eliezer
A sin offering is fit for its sake all year. A Paschal offering is fit for its sake only at its designated time — this distinction matters!
Key Distinction Rabbi Eliezer argues the Paschal offering's time-limited validity makes it uniquely susceptible to disqualification when another offering is sacrificed for its sake.
