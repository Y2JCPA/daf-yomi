Zevachim 72 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why are both the mishna in Zevachim and the mishna in Avoda Zara needed to teach that a prohibited animal prohibits the mixture?
A. Each teaches a different number of animals required for nullification
B. Each covers a scenario where one might have thought to be lenient — offerings vs. ordinary benefit
C. One deals with biblical prohibitions and one with rabbinic prohibitions
D. One applies to kohanim and one applies to Israelites
Correct: B — Without Zevachim, one might think offerings should be treated leniently to avoid Temple loss. Without Avoda Zara, one might think ordinary benefit (not being 'repulsive') allows nullification.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the core dispute between R' Yochanan and Reish Lakish regarding R' Meir's opinion?
A. Whether bitul applies to liquids or only solids
B. Whether the threshold for nullification is 1:60 or 1:200
C. Whether 'davar shebeminyan' means exclusively counted or also counted
D. Whether animals can be offered on the altar after being mixed
Correct: C — R' Yochanan holds R' Meir considers only items exclusively sold by unit as significant; Reish Lakish holds even items also (but not exclusively) sold by unit are significant.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to the Rabbis (Chachamim), how many specific items are significant enough to always prohibit their mixture?
A. Three
B. Six
C. Ten
D. Twelve
Correct: B — The Rabbis list six items that are sufficiently significant to prohibit a mixture. R' Akiva adds a seventh — loaves of a homeowner.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why is R' Yochanan's position problematic for explaining our mishna's ruling about prohibited animals?
A. Because R' Yochanan holds animals are too holy to be nullified
B. Because animals are not exclusively sold by unit (sometimes sold in herds), so they shouldn't be 'significant'
C. Because R' Yochanan disagrees with nullification entirely
D. Because R' Yochanan holds the mishna is only rabbinic in origin
Correct: B — Since animals are sometimes sold as part of a herd rather than exclusively by unit, R' Yochanan's standard ('exclusively counted') would not classify them as significant — yet our mishna says they cannot be nullified.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Rav Pappa resolve the difficulty with R' Yochanan's position?
A. He says R' Yochanan retracted his opinion
B. He identifies our mishna's tanna as the tanna of the litra of dried figs, who has a broader standard
C. He says animals are always an exception to the rule of bitul
D. He says the mishna is only discussing a case of equal numbers
Correct: B — Rav Pappa explains that according to R' Yochanan, the tanna of our mishna follows the opinion of the tanna of the litra of dried figs, who applies a different, broader standard for when items prohibit their mixture.
