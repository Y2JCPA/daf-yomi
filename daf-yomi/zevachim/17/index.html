Three Categories of Disqualified Priests

Who disqualifies Temple service?
↓
Impure Priest (Tamei)
Derived from Lev. 22:2 — 'They shall separate from sacred items'
Tevul Yom (Immersed Today)
Derived from Lev. 21:6 — 'They shall not profane the name of their God'
Mechusar Kippurim
One who hasn't yet brought atonement offering
↓
All three are necessary — each has a unique stringency (tzrikhuta)
Key Theme The Gemara demonstrates why the Torah cannot derive any one disqualification from the other two, requiring all three to be stated explicitly.

Source for Tevul Yom Disqualifying Service

קדושים יהיו לאלהיהם ולא יחללו שם אלהיהם
R' Simai's Derivation Leviticus 21:6 — 'They shall be sacred to their God and they shall not desecrate the name of their God'

This verse is not needed for an impure priest (already derived from Lev. 22:2).
➜ Apply it instead to a tevul yom (טבול יום) — one who immersed but awaits nightfall.

He is no longer fully impure, yet remains prohibited from teruma, sacrificial food, and entering the Temple.

Death Penalty for Tevul Yom — Gezera Shava

Lev. 21:6 — 'And not profane the name of their God'
↓
Verbal analogy (gezera shava) on the word חילול (profanation)
↓
Lev. 22:9 — 'Lest they bear sin and die therein, if they profane it' (regarding teruma)
↓
One who disqualifies teruma (tevul yom) → profanes service → liable to death at the hand of Heaven
Implication One who does NOT disqualify teruma (e.g., one who shaves a bald spot or destroys his beard) does NOT profane the service — the gezera shava limits the verse's application.

Rabba's Tzrikhuta — Why All Three Cases Are Needed

CategoryUnique StringencyWhy can't derive from others?
Impure Priest (Tamei)Imparts impurity to othersTevul yom and mechusar kippurim don't transmit impurity
Tevul YomUnfit to eat terumaMechusar kippurim MAY eat teruma
Mechusar KippurimHas not yet performed a required action (bringing offering)Tevul yom need only wait for nightfall — no action required
Rabba's Principle Each case has a חומרא (stringency) not found in the other two, so no case can be derived from the combination of the other two.

Can We Derive Any One from the Other Two?

Gemara
Granted, from ONE case we can't derive the other two. But derive one from the OTHER TWO combined!
Rabba
Try dropping mechusar kippurim — derive it from tamei + tevul yom?
Gemara
Refuted! Both tamei and tevul yom are unfit for teruma. Mechusar kippurim IS fit for teruma — maybe he doesn't disqualify!
Rabba
Try dropping tevul yom — derive from tamei + mechusar kippurim?
Gemara
You might say: both tamei and mechusar kippurim haven't performed a necessary action. But mechusar kippurim's impurity is weaker — he's not truly considered 'lacking an action.'
Rabba
This works only if a zav who hasn't brought atonement is still considered a full zav — making his impurity stronger than tevul yom.
Resolution Rabba holds a zav who hasn't brought atonement IS still considered a zav, making mechusar kippurim's status stronger than tevul yom's, so tevul yom cannot be derived.

Red Heifer Dispute — Who Is Fit to Participate?

First Tanna

  • 'The pure person shall sprinkle' (Num. 19:19) — emphasizes purity specific to the red heifer
  • Tevul yom of ANY impurity is fit for the red heifer
  • Mechusar kippurim (even a zav) is ALSO fit
  • A zav who hasn't brought atonement is NOT considered a full zav

Yosef the Babylonian

  • 'Pure' refers only to the impurity discussed in THAT passage — corpse impurity
  • Tevul yom from corpse impurity → fit for red heifer
  • Acute mourner and sheretz impurity → fit (derived a fortiori from corpse)
  • Zav's mechusar kippurim → NOT fit (impurity emerges from his own body — more severe)
Key Disagreement Both agree a tevul yom is fit for the red heifer. They disagree whether this leniency extends to ALL types of impurity or only corpse-related impurity.
