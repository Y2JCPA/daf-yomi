Intent, Disqualification & Piggul: When Does Thought Equal Action?

Mishnah Context
The Mishnah listed cases where improper intent does not disqualify an offering — but Rabbi Yehuda disagrees regarding intent to leave over (notar).
R' Elazar
Rabbi Yehuda derives his view from two verses about notar: one in Exodus 12:10 and one in Leviticus 7:15 — the extra verse teaches that intent to leave over also disqualifies.
Gemara
But Leviticus 7:15 is needed to teach many halakhot about time-limits for eating offerings! And a baraita shows Rabbi Yehuda uses logical reasoning, not a verse.
R' Abba
Even if slaughter had notar intent, subsequent piggul intent during sprinkling can still establish the offering as piggul.
Rav Huna
A baraita refutes R' Abba — notar intent does block subsequent piggul, and the refutation is conclusive (teyuvta).
Key Principle Rabbi Yehuda equates intent with action: just as actually leaving an offering overnight disqualifies it, so does the intent to do so at the time of slaughter.

Rabbi Yehuda vs. the Rabbis: Does Notar Intent Disqualify?

R' Yehuda
Do you not concede that if one actually left the offering overnight, it is disqualified? Then intent to leave it over should also disqualify!
Rabbanan
Intent only disqualifies in cases of piggul (beyond time / outside area) and for Paschal/sin offerings done not for their sake.
R' Elazar
R' Yehuda has a scriptural source: two verses about notar — the extra one in Leviticus 7:15 teaches about notar intent.
Gemara
But that verse is needed for many other halakhot! And a baraita shows R' Yehuda relies on logic, not the verse.
Resolution The phrase "He shall not leave" (lo yaniach) is the extra wording — even after the verse teaches other halakhot, this phrase remains available for notar intent.

Why Doesn't R' Yehuda Disqualify ALL Improper Intent?

Intent That Disqualifies (R' Yehuda)

  • Intent to leave over until morning (notar)
  • Intent to remove from designated area
  • Reason: Even if DONE, the offering itself is disqualified

Intent That Does NOT Disqualify

  • Intent to break bones of Paschal offering
  • Intent to eat partially roasted meat
  • Intent for impure/uncircumcised to partake or sacrifice
  • Reason: Even if DONE, the blood presentation still effects acceptance — the offering itself is not disqualified
Logic R' Yehuda only equates intent with action where the action itself disqualifies the offering. Where the action only creates a personal prohibition (like impurity), intent cannot disqualify.

R' Abba vs. Rav Huna: Does Notar Intent Block Piggul?

R' Abba
Even if one slaughtered with intent to leave blood overnight, if he then sprinkled with piggul intent (beyond time), the offering becomes piggul.
Rava
Proof: Intent to sprinkle tomorrow inherently includes leaving blood overnight — yet it still creates piggul once the blood is sprinkled!
Gemara
That's one intent fulfilled at sprinkling. Here there are two separate intents — notar intent already exists before piggul intent is formed.
Rav Huna
A baraita proves: blood placed above/below the line the next day (notar intent per R' Yehuda) — subsequent piggul intent does not create piggul. Conclusive refutation!
Teyuvta R' Abba's position is conclusively refuted (teyuvta d'Rabbi Abba teyuvta). Notar intent does block subsequent piggul.
