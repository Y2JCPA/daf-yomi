Frequent vs. Holy & Contributed Oil

Gemara
The תדיר (frequent) offering always precedes — but does it also precede one of greater sanctity (מקודש)?
Challenges
Multiple proofs attempted from daily vs. mussaf, kiddush blessings, and prayer order — all rejected!
Rava
Peace offerings are merely common (מצוי), not frequent (תדיר) — the dilemma stands unresolved.
Shmuel
On Amud B: One who contributes oil — a priest removes a handful (קומץ) and the remainder is eaten.
Abaye vs. R' Zeira
They dispute what the mishna implies: is contributed oil entirely burned or does only the handful go on the altar?
Core Tension The daf wrestles with two unresolved questions: the precedence of frequency vs. sanctity, and how to treat voluntary oil offerings.

More Attempted Proofs — All Rejected

Gemara
Beit Hillel say: Wine blessing before kiddush because wine is more frequent — even though kiddush is holier!
Rejection
Shabbat sanctity affects both the wine blessing and kiddush equally.
R' Yochanan
If mussaf prayer was delayed, one prays mincha first because it's more frequent — even though mussaf is holier!
Rejection
Again: Shabbat sanctity elevates both prayers.
עדיין עומדת The dilemma of תדיר ומקודש — which takes precedence — remains unresolved through all these attempts.

Common (מצוי) vs. Frequent (תדיר)

תדיר — Frequent

  • Refers to an offering brought at a fixed, regular interval
  • Example: Daily tamid, Shabbat mussaf
  • Creates a genuine precedence question vs. holier offerings

מצוי — Common

  • Merely happens to occur more often — no fixed obligation
  • Example: Peace offerings (שלמים) vs. sin offerings
  • Rava: Does NOT create a precedence question
Rav Huna bar Yehuda's Challenge Circumcision is called 'frequent' relative to the Paschal offering — yet it has no fixed schedule! Rava answers: it's frequent in its numerous associated mitzvot, or its overwhelming frequency makes it like תדיר.

Contributed Oil: Handful or Entirely Burned?

Shmuel
Per R' Tarfon: one who contributes oil — priest removes a קומץ (handful), burns it; remainder eaten by priests. Derived from the word קרבן in Lev. 2:1.
R' Zeira
The mishna supports this: oil distributed in the courtyard must be leftover — implying contributed oil is also distributed (not fully burned).
Abaye
But the last clause says oil on the altar flames is leftover — implying contributed oil would be entirely burned!
Gemara
R' Zeira's reading works: first clause = remainder (eaten), last clause = handful (burned). Abaye's reading is problematic.
Resolution
The tanna taught the first clause due to the last clause — matching its style even if the wording is misleading.
Key Principle A tanna may formulate an earlier clause to stylistically match a later clause, even if this creates a potentially misleading inference.
