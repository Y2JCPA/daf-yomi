Piggul: Mixed Intentions, Retroactive Disqualification & The Comprehensive Principle

Core Issue
When improper intentions are mixed together, does the offering become piggul or merely disqualified?
R' Eliezer
Leviticus 7:18 speaks of one who intends to eat on the third day — not one who actually eats then
R' Akiva
But retroactive disqualification is possible — just as a zav who sees a new discharge retroactively negates purity!
Mishna
The principle: intent beyond time = piggul + karet; intent outside area = disqualified only — provided the permitting factor was offered properly
Key Principle Piggul requires that the permitting factor (blood service) be sacrificed in accordance with its mitzva — mixed intentions of time and place prevent piggul liability.

Half Olive-Bulk Combinations

פיגול יהיה — חצי זית חוץ לזמנו וחצי זית חוץ למקומו מצטרפין
Leviticus 7:18 — 'It Shall Be' The singular 'it shall be' (not 'they shall be') teaches that half a k'zayit of intent beyond time and half a k'zayit of intent outside area combine to disqualify the offering — but this combined intent does not create piggul or karet liability.

Does Eating on the Third Day Retroactively Disqualify?

R' Eliezer
The verse speaks of one who intends to eat on the third day — would you say an offering fit for two days becomes disqualified retroactively?
R' Akiva
Yes! A zav or zava who immerses has presumptive purity — yet a new discharge retroactively negates everything they touched!
R' Eliezer
But the verse says 'he who offers it' — disqualification occurs at the time of offering, not on the third day!
R' Eliezer
If it meant the priest is disqualified, it would say 'he who offers shall not be credited.' Instead, 'neither shall it be credited to he who offers it' — the offering is disqualified.
Resolution R' Eliezer proves from the verse's structure that disqualification is about intent at the time of sacrifice, not actual consumption on the third day.

Interpretations of Key Phrases in Leviticus 7:18

Ben Azzai

  • "It" excludes offerings brought late from a delayed vow
  • Late fulfillment does not disqualify the offering
  • Derives offering vs. priest from "he who offers it"

Aḥerim

  • "Lo yeḥashev" = disqualified by maḥashava (intention)
  • Not disqualified by actual consumption on the third day
  • The verb form itself implies improper thought

R' Eliezer

  • "He who offers it" — disqualified at time of offering
  • Uses "lo yeḥashev" for R' Yannai's rule: intentions displace each other
  • Also for Rav Mari: improper intent violates a prohibition
Rav Ashi's Challenge Rav Ashi objects to Rav Mari: having improper intent is a prohibition without action — one is not normally flogged for it. Rav Mari: follows R' Yehuda, who holds one is flogged.

Rava: 'Outside Its Designated Area' Requires Three Dimensions

DimensionMeaning
Outside the blood's limitsBlood sprinkled in a location not designated for it
Outside the meat's limitsMeat consumed outside the permitted area
Outside the sacrificial portions' limitsEimurim burned outside the proper location
Why Two Verses? Rav Mattana explains: If derived only from Lev. 7:18, the detail-generalization rule (prat u'khlal) would include all areas. The verse in 'You shall be holy' (Lev. 19) restricts 'outside' to places that are outside in all three ways.
