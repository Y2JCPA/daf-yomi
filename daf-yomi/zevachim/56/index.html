Temple Courtyard
187 × 135 cubits

Not walls/windows
Mishna: walls & windows count as inside
Chambers

Rava: Torah specifically includes chambers for eating kodshei kodashim, but not for slaughter or tumah liability

Temple Courtyard: 187 × 135 Cubits

Included (Sacred)

  • The 187 × 135 cubit courtyard area
  • Windows in the wall
  • Thickness of the wall
  • Roofs of chambers built in sacred area

Excluded (Limited Sanctity)

  • Chambers built in non-sacred area
  • Interior of chambers opening outward
  • Roofs of chambers built in non-sacred area
  • Dimensions stated to exclude these areas
Rav Naḥman In this area priests eat kodshei kodashim, slaughter kodashim kalim, and are liable for entering in a state of tumah.

Chambers Built Outside, Opening Inward — What Sanctity?

Gemara
A Mishna in Ma'aser Sheni says chambers built in non-sacred area opening to sacred — their interior is sacred, roofs are not.
Gemara
But a baraita derives from "In the courtyard of the Tent of Meeting" (Lev. 6:9) that priests eat kodshei kodashim there — implying Torah-level sanctity!
Rava
The Torah specifically amplified these chambers only for eating offerings. For slaughter and tumah liability, they are not sacred by Torah law.
Gemara
But a baraita says one is liable for karet for entering these chambers while tamei!
Rava
Emend the baraita: since it already says you cannot slaughter there, teach also that there's no karet for tumah.
Key Principle The verse "in a holy place; in the courtyard of the Tent of Meeting" uses a double expression to amplify — but only for eating, not for all sanctity purposes.

Must Eating Be "Before the Entrance"?

R' Yosei b'R' Yehuda: Two wickets in the Chamber of Knives rendered the courtyard fit for eating kodshei kodashim and slaughter of kodashim kalim
↓
Does eating require being before the entrance of the courtyard?
Ravina
Remove the clause about eating — eating does NOT require being before the entrance
Challenge
But Lev. 8:31 says "eat it there" at the entrance!
↓
Answer: Inauguration offerings were a one-time command (hora'at sha'ah) — no general halakhot can be derived from them
Resolution Slaughtering kodashim kalim requires before the entrance. Eating kodshei kodashim does not.

Blood Disqualified at Sunset

בְּיוֹם זִבְחוֹ יֵאָכֵל — ביום שאתה זובח אתה מקריב, ביום שאי אתה זובח אי אתה מקריב
Rav Yitzḥak bar Avudimi From where do we derive that blood becomes disqualified at sunset?

The verse states: "On the day that he sacrifices his offering (zivḥo)" (Lev. 7:16).

The Torah could have simply said "on the day he slaughters it." The extra word "that he sacrifices it" teaches: on the day you slaughter — you sacrifice the blood. On a day you do not slaughter — you do not sacrifice the blood.

Therefore, blood that was not presented by sunset is disqualified and may no longer be placed on the altar.

Peace Offering on the Eve of the Third Day

Ḥizkiyya
One who intends to eat a peace offering on the evening before the third day — the offering is valid. It has not yet been designated for burning until the third morning.
R' Yoḥanan
It is disqualified as piggul. Once the second day ends, the meat is rejected from eating — even though burning hasn't yet begun.
Ḥizkiyya
Similarly, one who eats a peace offering that evening is exempt from karet — it hasn't yet become notar.
R' Yoḥanan
He is liable for karet — it has already been rejected from eating after sunset of the second day.
Baraita
A baraita supports R' Yoḥanan: intending to eat two-day offerings after sunset of the second day renders them piggul.
Core Dispute Does the evening before the third day count as within or beyond the offering's time? Ḥizkiyya: burning defines the cutoff. R' Yoḥanan: rejection from eating defines it.
