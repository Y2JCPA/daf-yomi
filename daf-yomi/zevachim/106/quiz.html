Zevachim 106 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Rabbis in the baraita, where outside Jerusalem are the bulls and goats of Yom Kippur burned?
A. East of Jerusalem
B. North of Jerusalem, outside the three camps
C. On the Mount of Olives
D. South of Jerusalem near the Kidron Valley
Correct: B — The baraita states that the bulls and goats are burned north of Jerusalem, outside the three camps. R' Yosei HaGelili holds they are burned at the place of the ashes.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the practical difference between the Tanna Kamma and Rabbi Shimon regarding garment impurity from burning the Yom Kippur offerings?
A. Whether the one who kindles the fire becomes impure
B. Whether a charred mass that has not yet become ash still transmits impurity
C. Whether the wood-arranger becomes impure
D. Whether impurity applies before burning begins
Correct: B — Rava explains the difference: when the offering becomes a charred mass (form distorted but not yet ash), the Tanna Kamma holds it still transmits impurity while R' Shimon holds it does not.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does R' Yosei HaGelili exempt someone who slaughters outside and then offers up outside?
A. Slaughter outside is not a real prohibition
B. The offering up was done by a different person
C. Slaughtering outside rendered the offering unfit, and one is only liable for offering up a fit item
D. Offering up outside requires witnesses
Correct: C — R' Yosei HaGelili reasons that slaughtering outside renders the offering unfit, and the prohibition of offering up outside applies only to an item that is fit to be offered inside the Temple.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does R' Avin derive the prohibition against slaughtering an offering outside the Temple?
A. From a direct verse in Deuteronomy
B. Through a kal va-chomer: if offerings from the permission period are prohibited without karet, then offerings with karet must certainly be prohibited
C. From a gezerah shavah with the Yom Kippur service
D. From a rabbinic enactment
Correct: B — R' Avin uses an a fortiori inference: since the Torah prohibits sacrificing outside even offerings from the permission period (which carry no karet), certainly offerings that carry karet must also be prohibited.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why can't the prohibition against eating forbidden fat (chelev) be derived through a kal va-chomer from the prohibition against eating a carcass (neveilah)?
A. Chelev carries a more severe punishment than neveilah
B. Neveilah has a unique stringency — it transmits impurity through contact — which chelev does not share
C. There is no punishment stated for eating neveilah
D. Chelev and neveilah are from different categories of animals
Correct: B — Rava refutes the inference because neveilah has a unique stringency (transmitting impurity through contact) that chelev lacks. This 'pirka' breaks the a fortiori reasoning.
