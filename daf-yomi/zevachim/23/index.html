Zevachim 23: Frontplate Acceptance & Standing for Service

Core Question: Can an impure owner send offerings?
↓
Elders of the South
Corpse-impure owner may send Paschal offering
Sages
Tzitz does not bear iniquity of those who consecrate
↓
Second Topic: Must a priest stand to perform service?
↓
Yes — sitting disqualifies the rite (Deut. 18:5)
Key Daf Two major discussions: the scope of the Tzitz's power to accept impure offerings, and the standing requirement for priestly service.

Can the Paschal Offering Be Slaughtered for One Who Cannot Eat?

Gemara
The verse says 'according to his eating' — one may bring the Paschal offering only if able to eat it.
Challenger
Perhaps this is only a mitzvah ab initio, not indispensable?
Baraita
The verse repeats 'you shall make your count' — the counting is indispensable. Slaughtering for an unregistered person disqualifies.
Gemara
Those who eat are juxtaposed to those registered — slaughtering for one who cannot eat also disqualifies.
Elders of the South
We do not juxtapose the phrases in the verse — the two requirements are separate.
Principle The Elders of the South hold that juxtaposition of phrases (heikesh) within the verse about the Paschal offering is not a valid source of derivation.

Two Types of Impurity: Creeping Animal vs. Corpse

Tum'at Sheretz (Creeping Animal)

  • Lighter level of impurity
  • Owner may send offerings ab initio (per Elders of the South)
  • NOT permitted in cases involving the public
  • Immersion same day suffices

Tum'at Met (Corpse)

  • Severe level of impurity
  • General prohibition was permitted for the public (tzibbur)
  • Requires 7-day purification process
  • Elders of the South: owner may still send offerings
Key Distinction Corpse impurity is more severe, yet its general prohibition was 'permitted in cases involving the public' — a critical factor in the Tzitz discussion.

What Sin Does the Tzitz (Frontplate) Bear?

Exodus 28:38: 'Aaron shall bear the sin of the sacred items'
↓
Piggul?
❌ Already stated: 'it shall not be accepted' (Lev. 7:18)
Notar?
❌ Same verse: 'it shall not be accepted'
Impurity?
✅ The Tzitz bears the sin of impurity
↓
Which impurity? Must be corpse impurity — the type 'permitted in cases involving the public'
↓
One Reading
Refers to owner's impurity → supports Elders of the South
Opposite Reading
Bears iniquity of sacred items, NOT of those who consecrate → refutes Elders
Two Readings The same verse about the Tzitz is read in opposite ways — one supporting and one refuting the Elders of the South.

Tumat HaTehom — Impurity Imparted in the Depths

AspectDetail
DefinitionA source of impurity that was unknown at the time of the offering
R' Ḥiyya's RuleApplies ONLY to impurity due to a corpse
What it excludesDebate: excludes ziva (discharge) impurity — NOT creeping animal
Rami bar Ḥama's QuestionWas tumat hatehom permitted for the officiating priest?
Per Elders of the SouthShould be permitted, since they read the mishna as referring to an impure priest
Gemara's ResolutionRami bar Ḥama disagrees with the Elders of the South on this point
Note Tumat hatehom is a unique leniency — retroactive discovery of corpse impurity does not disqualify the offering, per the Tzitz's acceptance.

Standing to Minister

לַעֲמֹד לְשָׁרֵת — לַעֲמִידָה בְּחַרְתִּיו וְלֹא לִישִׁיבָה
Source Rav Naḥman: 'For the Lord your God has chosen him... to stand to minister' (Deut. 18:5) — I have chosen him for standing, not for sitting.

The verse repeats this in Deut. 18:7: 'as all his brethren the Levites do, who stand there' — the repetition makes this indispensable, disqualifying rites performed while sitting.
