Daf 6 — Atonement, Heirs & Substitution

Core Question:
Do heirs acquire a deceased father's offering?
↓
Evidence: Yes
One heir can effect substitution — showing the offering atones for him
Evidence: No
Two heirs of a minḥa can bring it together — it's not truly 'theirs'
↓
Rav Ashi's Resolution:
The offering atones for heirs only incidentally (מכופיא), not by its essence
↓
→ Leads to broader question: Does any offering atone beyond its original designation?
KEY THEME This daf explores the boundaries of atonement — who an offering can atone for, and whether atonement extends beyond original intent or designation.

Can Two Heirs Effect Substitution?

Gemara
The verse "הָמֵר יָמִיר" (Lev. 27:10) — the extra word hamer includes an heir as one who can effect substitution.
Gemara
But the singular form of the verb teaches: one heir can effect substitution, but two heirs cannot.
Rav Ya'akov of Nehar Pekod
Objection: The same logic should apply to second tithe — "גָּאֹל יִגְאַל" (Lev. 27:31). The singular should mean two heirs don't add the fifth! But they do.
Gemara
Answer: Tithe redemption is different — the father could redeem it in partnership while alive. Substitution cannot be done by partners.
PRINCIPLE The heir's power to substitute is derived from the doubled verb, but the singular form limits it to a single heir — unlike tithe redemption where partnership was possible in the father's lifetime.

Rav Asi's Proof: Do Heirs Acquire the Offering?

Given: One heir can effect substitution of a deceased father's offering
↓
Who can effect substitution?
R' Abbahu in R' Yoḥanan's name:
Only the one for whom the offering atones
↓
If heir acquires it ✓
The offering atones for the heir → he can substitute. This works.
If heir doesn't acquire ✗
The offering doesn't atone for him → how can he substitute at all?
↓
Rav Ashi: The offering atones for the heir incidentally (מכופיא) — enough for substitution, but heirs don't truly acquire it
R' YOḤANAN'S RULE If one consecrates his own animal for another's atonement: the owner adds the fifth when redeeming, but only the one it atones for can effect substitution.

Offerings Slaughtered Not for Their Sake (שלא לשמן)

Status of the Offering

  • The offering is fit (כשר) to be sacrificed
  • It is brought on the altar
  • It does not satisfy the owner's obligation
  • Owner must bring a second offering

Did It Atone?

  • Rav Sheshet b. Rav Idi: It did NOT atone
  • If it atoned — why bring a second offering?
  • It is sacrificed based on its prior consecration
  • The second offering is what actually atones for the sin
RAV ASHI'S CLARIFICATION The first offering is brought because it was already consecrated. The second offering provides actual atonement. The offering שלא לשמן does not atone.

Does a Burnt Offering Atone for Post-Designation Sins?

Gemara
Dilemma: An עולה (burnt offering) atones for violating positive mitzvot. Does it atone for violations committed after the animal was designated?
Side A
Perhaps it's like a חטאת (sin offering) — one offering per sin, and only for sins before designation.
Side B
An עולה is different — it atones for multiple violations of positive mitzvot. Perhaps it also covers post-designation violations.
Baraita
Lev. 1:4 — "וְסָמַךְ...וְנִרְצָה לוֹ לְכַפֵּר" — Placing hands doesn't atone; blood sprinkling does. So what does hand-placing achieve?
Baraita (cont.)
It means: if he placed hands — it atoned. If not — it's as if it didn't atone, though it still atones...
PARALLEL The concept of 'atoned but not before Heaven' parallels R' Yoḥanan ben Nuri's ruling about oil placement on the leper — the act is non-essential, but its omission is ascribed as blame.
