How Many Blood Placements Make a Sin Offering Valid?

Beit Shammai
A sin offering requires at least two placements on the altar corners to facilitate atonement.
Beit Hillel
Even one placement suffices for atonement after the fact (b'di'avad).
Tanna (Baraita)
Three repetitions of "V'khiper alav haKohen" (Lev. 4:26, 31, 35) teach that atonement is achieved with 3, 2, or even 1 placement.
Rava
Bar Adda Mari explained: Each verse says both "atonement" and "forgiveness" — the duplication frees the phrase for this derivation.
Core Issue The Torah prescribes four blood placements on the corners for a sin offering (chatat). The dispute is whether all four are indispensable (me'akev) or only the first one.

The Logical Inference That Failed

Can we derive the law of outer altar sin offerings from a simpler case?
↓
Approach A
Derive from bird sin offering (also external altar) → one placement suffices
Approach B
Derive from inner sin offering (also four-corner placement) → all placements indispensable
↓
Both inferences are equally reasonable — neither can prevail
↓
Therefore we need the three verses of "V'khiper" to resolve the question
Key Logic The Gemara pits two analogies against each other: same altar type (external) vs. same offering type (four-corner placement). Since neither wins, Scripture must provide the answer.

Three Verses, Three Reductions

📖 Torah prescribes four blood placements on the corners of the outer altar for a chatat
↓
1️⃣ First "V'khiper" (Lev. 4:26 — king's sin offering): Atonement even with three placements
↓
2️⃣ Second "V'khiper" (Lev. 4:31 — goat offering): Atonement even with two placements
↓
3️⃣ Third "V'khiper" (Lev. 4:35 — sheep offering): Atonement even with one placement
↓
✅ This is the source for Beit Hillel: one placement facilitates atonement
Objection & Answer But aren't these verses needed for their own offerings? Rava (citing Bar Adda Mari): Each verse already says "v'nislach" (and he shall be forgiven) — "v'khiper" is therefore superfluous and available for derivation.

What Does "Tohoro" of the Altar Mean?

Initial Reading
Tohoro means the middle of the altar — like tihara (clear noon) means midday. Blood sprinkled 7 times would land above and below the midpoint.
Rava bar Sheila
No — tohoro means the revealed/clear top of the altar, as in "latohar hashamayim" (Exodus 24:10) — the pure gold surface after ashes are cleared.
Why It Matters The Gemara is testing whether any blood is ever placed both above and below the red line on the same altar. Rava bar Sheila's reading avoids this problem.

Piggul and Placements: Beit Shammai vs. Beit Hillel

Beit Shammai

  • Two placements of a sin offering render it permitted for eating
  • One placement for all other offerings renders them permitted
  • Piggul intent during these placements makes the offering piggul
  • More STRINGENT on permission (requires 2 for chatat)

Beit Hillel

  • One placement of a sin offering renders it permitted for eating
  • One placement for all other offerings renders them permitted
  • Piggul intent during even one placement makes the offering piggul
  • More STRINGENT on piggul (1 placement is enough to create piggul)
Rav Oshaya's Question Why isn't this listed in Eduyyot Ch. 4 as a case where Beit Shammai are lenient? Rava answers: The dispute was originally framed about permission to eat — where Beit Shammai are stricter — not about piggul.

R' Yochanan: Status of the Last Three Placements

HalakhaLast 3 Placements
Nighttime performance❌ Prohibited — blood is invalidated at sunset
After owner's death✅ Permitted — atonement already achieved by first placement
Offering blood outside Temple⚠️ Liable for karet — blood still has sanctity
Are they indispensable?No — but it is a mitzvah to perform them
Source of obligationThey remain a valid mitzvah even though not me'akev
R' Yochanan's Principle According to Beit Hillel, the last three placements are a mitzvah but not indispensable. Despite this reduced status, they retain significant halakhic weight.
