Why Each Disqualification Needs a Separate Verse

Question: Why can't we simply say these animals are unfit for the Tent of Meeting?
↓
Because each animal was initially consecrated and fit
↓
A separate verse is needed to teach exemption from karet for slaughtering outside
PRINCIPLE The Torah must explicitly exclude each category because mere unfitness after initial consecration doesn't automatically exempt from liability.

Can Consecrated Animals Be Disqualified by Idol Worship?

Gemara (Challenge)
An already-consecrated animal can't be disqualified by idol worship — a person cannot render forbidden an item that is not his!
Gemara (Resolution)
It works with kodashim kalim (lesser sanctity offerings) like a shelamim
R' Yosei HaGelili
Kodashim kalim are the property of the owner (mamon be'alim). One who swears falsely about another's korban kalim violates trespass against God (Lev. 5:21)
R' YOSEI HAGELILI Since lesser sanctity offerings belong to the owner, the owner can render them forbidden through idol worship — unlike most sacred items.

Why All Three Cases Are Necessary (Rabbis vs. R' Shimon)

Temporarily Blemished Animals

  • Were fit, then became blemished
  • Unique factor: They are repulsive (me'usin)
  • Without this case: Maybe Rabbis exempt only because of repulsiveness

Underage Doves

  • Time of fitness has not yet arrived
  • Unique factor: Not repulsive, not 'rejected'
  • Without this case: Maybe R' Shimon would concede since blemished animals were 'rejected'

Oso V'Es B'no (Animal & Offspring)

  • Disqualification is external (parent slaughtered today)
  • Unique factor: External cause, not inherent
  • Without this case: Maybe Rabbis would concede since disqualification is not inherent
CONCLUSION All three cases are needed — each has a unique feature that might have led one side to change their position.

Does One Receive Lashes for a Premature Offering Inside the Temple?

R' Yirmeya
One who slaughters a premature offering should receive lashes for violating 'You shall not do' (lo ta'asun)!
R' Zeira
No lashes — the verse 'from the eighth day it may be accepted' (Lev. 22:27) converts the negative precept into a positive mitzva, and one doesn't receive lashes for that
Gemara
But what about the separate prohibition of 'lo ta'asun'?
Gemara (Resolution)
R' Zeira follows the Rabbis who say 'lo ta'asun' doesn't apply to premature offerings. R' Shimon would indeed give lashes.
Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak
Even R' Shimon agrees — no lashes inside the Temple, because 'lo ta'asun' pertains only to outside (derived from Gilgal, where inside = outside)
LASHES RULING According to Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, all agree that slaughtering a premature offering inside the Temple does not incur lashes.

Rabba: R' Shimon's True Source

לא תוכל לזבח את הפסח באחד שעריך
ALTERNATIVE DERIVATION Rabba says R' Shimon's prohibition is not from 'lo ta'asun' but from:

"Lo tukhal lizbo'ach et ha-Pesach be'achad she'arecha"
(Deut. 16:5–6)

The word be'achad — 'within one of your gates' — teaches that the prohibition applies only when all of Israel enters through one gate (i.e., when a central altar exists).

Rabba's inference: Since a Paschal offering slaughtered on a private altar violates only a prohibition (not karet), it must be discussing before midday on the 14th — when its time hasn't arrived. This proves R' Shimon holds a premature offering violates a prohibition.
