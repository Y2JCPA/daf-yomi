QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to R' Yosei HaGelili, why can an owner disqualify a consecrated animal through idol worship?
A. Because offerings of lesser sanctity (kodashim kalim) are the property of the owner
B. Because all consecrated animals remain the owner's property until sacrificed
C. Because idol worship overrides all forms of sanctity
D. Because the Torah explicitly states that idol worship disqualifies consecrated animals
Correct: A — R' Yosei HaGelili holds that kodashim kalim (lesser sanctity offerings like shelamim) are mamon be'alim — the owner's property. Since it's 'his,' he can render it forbidden through idol worship, unlike kodshei kodashim.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the mishna need to list all three cases — temporarily blemished animals, underage doves, and oso v'es b'no?
A. Each has a unique feature that might lead one side of the debate to change position
B. Each derives from a different verse in the Torah
C. The three cases represent different levels of sanctity
D. Each applies to a different type of sacrifice
Correct: A — Blemished animals are uniquely 'repulsive'; underage doves were never 'rejected'; oso v'es b'no has an external disqualification. Each unique feature might have caused the Rabbis or R' Shimon to rule differently, so all three are necessary.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, why doesn't R' Shimon's 'lo ta'asun' prohibition apply inside the Temple?
A. Because the prohibition is derived from Gilgal, where 'inside' was considered like 'outside'
B. Because lashes cannot be administered for Temple violations
C. Because the positive mitzva of 'from the eighth day' overrides the prohibition
D. Because R' Shimon's prohibition only applies to private altars
Correct: A — Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak explains that 'lo ta'asun' is derived from the Tabernacle at Gilgal, where inside was equivalent to outside. Therefore the prohibition applies only to sacrificing outside, not inside the Temple.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rabba derive R' Shimon's source for the prohibition against slaughtering a premature offering outside?
A. From the verse about the Paschal offering — one who slaughters it on a private altar before midday violates a prohibition
B. From the verse 'lo ta'asun' in Deuteronomy about the Tabernacle at Gilgal
C. From the general prohibition of 'it shall not be accepted' in Leviticus
D. From the principle that all negative precepts carry lashes
Correct: A — Rabba says R' Shimon derives his position from the Paschal offering verse (Deut. 16:5-6). Since slaughtering it on a private altar yields only a prohibition (not karet), it must refer to before midday — when its time hasn't arrived. This proves a premature offering violates a prohibition.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What correction did Ze'eiri make to the mishna, and why?
A. Added 'leper' (metzora) because the mishna mentions guilt offerings, which a zav/zava don't bring
B. Added 'nazirite' because the mishna mentions peace offerings, which a zav/zava don't bring
C. Removed 'zav' because a zav doesn't sacrifice outside the Temple
D. Added 'woman after childbirth' because she brings a sin offering
Correct: A — Ze'eiri noted that the mishna mentions guilt offerings (asham) in connection with zav, zava, and yoledet — but these individuals don't bring guilt offerings. A metzora (leper) does bring an asham as part of purification, so Ze'eiri added metzora to the list.
