Time-Intent vs. Area-Intent: Which Comes First?

R' Yehuda
There is a principle: If intent regarding time preceded intent regarding area, the offering is piggul and one is liable for karet.
R' Yehuda
But if area-intent came first, it is merely disqualified — no karet.
Rabbanan
In both cases — whether time came first or area came first — the offering is disqualified with no karet.
Rabbanan
The two intents cancel each other; neither alone can establish piggul.
Halacha The Rabbis hold that conflicting improper intents prevent piggul status regardless of order.

Deriving Melikah Rules from Leviticus 1:15

📖 "The priest shall bring it to the altar" — even one bird suffices (not two)
↓
👨‍🦱 "The priest shall bring it" — only a kohen may perform melikah
↓
✋ R' Akiva: "The priest" is extra — teaches pinching is with the body of the priest (thumbnail), not a knife
↓
📍 Juxtaposition of pinching and burning — melikah occurs above the red line on the altar
↓
🔗 Gezeirah shavah: "pinch off its head" — at the nape (oreif), derived from bird chatat
Kal Va-Chomer Reversed Slaughter needs a knife but not a priest; pinching needs a priest but not a knife — the Torah overrides the expected logical inference.

Rending the Bird (Amud B)

וְשִׁסַּע אֹתוֹ בִּכְנָפָיו לֹא יַבְדִּיל
Biblical Source The verse states "And he shall rend it" (Lev. 1:17) — rending is performed only by hand. Proof: regarding Samson, "And he rent it as one would have rent a kid, and he had nothing in his hand" (Judges 14:6).

Separating the Head of a Bird Chatat

Tanna Kamma (Mishna)

  • If priest separated the head → disqualified
  • Squeezing blood after separation = performing bird olah rite on a chatat
  • This constitutes shinui (change in procedure)

R' Elazar b'R' Shimon

  • "I heard that one may separate the head"
  • Separation does not constitute performing olah rite
  • The offering remains valid
Note The mishna follows the Tanna Kamma, not R' Elazar b'R' Shimon.
