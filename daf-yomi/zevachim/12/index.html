Daf 12: Paschal Timing, Permanent Disqualification & Ben Azzai's Tradition

Ben Beteira's View: Can the Korban Pesach be sacrificed all day on the 14th?
↓
R' Yochanan: No — slaughtered in the morning = pasul, whether for its sake or not
↓
R' Abbahu's Challenge: If so, when can it ever be valid?
↓
R' Abbahu
Designate after noon on the 14th
Abaye
Even morning — same-day arrival is not 'time not yet arrived'
Rav Pappa
Even previous night — nighttime designation is valid
Core Theme This daf explores the precise timing rules for the Korban Pesach, the concept of permanent disqualification (dichuy), and Ben Azzai's addition of the Olah to offerings invalidated when sacrificed not for their sake.

R' Yochanan vs. R' Oshaya on Ben Beteira

R' Oshaya
Ben Beteira holds bein ha'arbayim means the entire day — a Pesach slaughtered in the morning for its sake is valid.
Gemara
But R' Yehuda HaNasi argued on Ben Beteira's behalf that only part of the 14th is fit for the Pesach — implying not the whole day!
R' Yochanan
Rather, Ben Beteira holds: slaughtered in the morning on the 14th = pasul, whether for its sake or not for its sake.
R' Yochanan
It is unfit even shelo lishmah because part of the day (the afternoon) is fit for it as a Pesach.
Key Rejection R' Oshaya's interpretation is rejected because it contradicts R' Yehuda HaNasi's own argument on behalf of Ben Beteira — that only part of the 14th is fit.

R' Abbahu's Challenge: When Is a Pesach Ever Valid?

Per R' Yochanan: Pesach slaughtered in the morning of the 14th = pasul
↓
R' Abbahu asks: If designated in the morning, it can't be sacrificed — so it's disqualified from the start!
If designated the previous day, it was fit shelo lishmah but becomes disqualified the next morning.
Once disqualified, it can never become fit again!
↓
R' Abbahu's Answer
Designate it on the 14th after noon — when it can already be sacrificed
Abaye's Answer
Even morning is fine — an offering whose time arrives that same day is not considered 'time not yet arrived'
Rav Pappa's Answer
Even the previous night — citing the school of R' Yishmael: an animal on the night before its 8th day can enter the pen for tithing
Proof Text R' Appetoriki resolves Leviticus 22:27 — at night an animal is fit for consecration; in the day it is fit for sacrifice. Nighttime designation does not create 'dichuy.'

Permanent Disqualification: Three Cases

Apostate (Ulla / R' Yochanan)

  • Ate forbidden fat, designated a Chatat
  • Then apostatized — unfit to bring offerings
  • Later recanted his apostasy
  • Ruling: Offering is PERMANENTLY disqualified
  • Needed: because he disqualified himself by choice

Imbecile (R' Yirmeya / R' Abbahu)

  • Ate forbidden fat, designated a Chatat
  • Then became a shoteh (imbecile)
  • Later regained competence
  • Ruling: Offering is PERMANENTLY disqualified
  • Needed: even involuntary disqualification is permanent

Court Ruling (R' Yirmeya's Dilemma)

  • Ate forbidden fat, designated a Chatat
  • Court ruled that fat is actually permitted
  • Court later retracted its ruling
  • A certain elder: THIS is the primary case
  • Here the offering itself was disqualified — even more obvious
Why Both Cases? If only apostate: maybe involuntary (imbecile) is different. If only imbecile: maybe when it's in his power to return (apostate), it's not permanent. Both are needed.

72 Elders The Gemara notes the singular zaken (elder) rather than plural zekenim — because they all held one unified opinion on this halakha.

Deriving Ben Azzai's Rule: Why Is the Olah Disqualified?

Rav Huna
The verse says 'It is (הוא) a burnt offering' (Lev. 1:13) — 'it' teaches: for its sake = fit; not for its sake = unfit.
Gemara
But 'הוא' is also written regarding an Asham (Lev. 7:5), yet an Asham is not disqualified!
Rav Huna
That 'הוא' is written after the burning of the portions — since the offering is valid even without burning, it can't disqualify.
Gemara
The Olah's 'הוא' is also after the burning! And 'הוא' appears twice for the Asham too!
Gemara
Rather, Ben Azzai derives it by kal vachomer: If a Chatat (partially eaten) is disqualified not lishmah, then an Olah (entirely burned) — all the more so!
Attempted Refutation The Gemara challenges: A Chatat is unique because it atones for sin. A Korban Pesach can prove the point — but it too is unique because it has a fixed time. The kal vachomer remains under scrutiny.
