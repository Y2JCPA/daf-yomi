Deriving Lishma for All Four Sacrificial Rites

Core Question: Where do we learn that sacrificial rites must be performed for the offering's own sake (לשמה)?
↓
Slaughter (שחיטה)
"A sacrifice (זבח) of peace offerings" (Lev 3:1)
The extra word זבח teaches slaughter must be lishma
↓
Can we derive all other rites from slaughter alone?
No — Unique Stringency
Slaughter uniquely disqualifies a Paschal offering if done for non-eaters
So each rite needs its own source
Collection, Sprinkling, and Conveying each derived from separate verses
Key Principle No single sacrificial rite can serve as the universal source for the lishma requirement because each has a unique stringency (צד חמור) that prevents derivation from it to the others.

Where Does the Lishma Requirement Come From?

Gemara
From where do we derive that slaughter must be performed lishma (for the offering's own sake)?
Source
"A sacrifice (זבח) of peace offerings" (Lev 3:1) — the word זבח teaches that zeviḥa (slaughter) must be for the sake of a peace offering.
Gemara
But maybe "sacrifice of peace offerings" is simply the name of this offering type, not about intent?
Source
Other verses say "blood of the peace offering" and "sprinkles the blood of the peace offering" without the word זבח. Here it says זבח — so it must teach something extra: slaughter must be lishma.
Method The Gemara identifies a seemingly redundant word (זבח) by comparing it to parallel verses that omit it, proving it carries independent legal meaning.

Why Each Rite Needs Its Own Verse

Rite (עבודה)Verse SourceUnique Stringency (צד חמור)Why You Can't Derive Others From It
Slaughter (שחיטה)"A sacrifice (זבח) of peace offerings" (Lev 3:1)Disqualifies Paschal if slaughtered for non-eatersOther rites don't have this disqualification
Collection (קבלה)"Who sacrifices (המקריב) the blood" (Lev 7:33)Invalid if done by a non-priest or womanSlaughter is valid by any competent Jew
Sprinkling (זריקה)"Who sprinkles the blood of the peace offering" (Lev 7:14)Non-priest liable to death at Heaven's handOther rites don't carry this penalty
Conveying (הולכה)"And the priest shall sacrifice (והקריב)" (Lev 1:13)Dispensable — not needed if slaughtered next to altarAll other rites are indispensable
Pattern Each rite has a unique feature that prevents a kal va'chomer or binyan av derivation from any other rite, forcing the Torah to provide a separate source for each.

The Derivation Chain: Rite by Rite

Step 1: Slaughter must be lishma
"זבח שלמים" (Lev 3:1)
↓
Can we derive collection from slaughter?
❌ No
Slaughter is unique: disqualifies Paschal for non-eaters
✅ Separate verse needed
"המקריב את דם השלמים" (Lev 7:33) — collection requires lishma
↓
Can we derive sprinkling from slaughter + collection?
❌ No
Both require the north side for kodshei kodashim; sprinkling does not
✅ Separate verse needed
"הזורק את דם השלמים" (Lev 7:14) — sprinkling requires lishma
↓
Step 4: Conveying
Dispensable rite — can't derive from indispensable ones
Derived from shared language: והקריב = הקרבה (Lev 1:5 & 1:13)
Linguistic Link The Torah uses the same root ק-ר-ב for both collection (והקריבו — Lev 1:5) and conveying (והקריב — Lev 1:13), teaching that the same lishma laws apply to both.

Shinui Kodesh vs. Shinui Ba'alim

Deviation from Type of Offering (שינוי קודש)

  • Slaughtering a shelamim as an olah
  • Derived from verses in Leviticus 3 & 7
  • A change of thought regarding the offering itself
  • Applies to all four sacrificial rites

Deviation Regarding the Owner (שינוי בעלים)

  • Performing rites for a person other than the owner
  • Derived from "it shall be accepted for him" (Lev 1:4)
  • A change of thought regarding the person
  • Rav Ashi: "for him" — sprinkling that atones must be for the owner
Gemara's Point The Gemara initially tries to distinguish the two types of deviation but concludes that both are ultimately "changes of thought" — requiring separate verses for each.

Rav Ashi's Derivation for Shinui Ba'alim

Rav Ashi
Sprinkling must be for the owner's sake: "It shall be accepted for him (לו) to atone for him" (Lev 1:4) — the rite that atones must be specifically for him.
Gemara
But doesn't that verse teach R' Shimon's halakha — that one who says "it is incumbent upon me" (עלי) bears responsibility to replace a lost offering?
Rav Ashi
R' Shimon derives from "for him" (alav). I derive from the phrase "it shall be accepted for him" (ונרצה לו) — a different part of the same verse.
R' Yitzḥak bar Avdimi
R' Shimon's logic: once a person says "it is upon me," it is as though the offering is loaded upon his shoulders — he must bring a replacement.
Two Halakhot, One Verse Leviticus 1:4 teaches both (1) that sprinkling must be for the correct owner and (2) R' Shimon's rule about personal obligation — from different phrases in the same verse.
