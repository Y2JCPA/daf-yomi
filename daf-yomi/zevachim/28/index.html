The Skin of the Tail & Sources of Piggul

Question
The skin of the tail is burned on the altar — so intent to eat it beyond its time is shifting consumption from altar to person. How can this be piggul?
Shmuel
The mishna follows Rabbi Eliezer, who holds shifting intent between altar and person still renders piggul.
Rav Huna
The skin of the tail is not considered like the tail — it is eaten, not burned. Both mishnayot follow the Rabbis.
Rav Ḥisda
The skin is like the tail, but our mishna discusses a kid (goat), whose tail is eaten, not burned.
Rava
On Amud B: The doubled verb 'he'akhol ye'akhel' teaches that piggul applies to two consumptions — by person and by altar.
Core Issue Three Amoraim offer three different solutions to reconcile two seemingly contradictory mishnayot about piggul intent regarding items not normally eaten.

The Problem: Skin of the Tail

עור של אליה
Setup The tail of a sheep peace offering (אַלְיָה) is burned on the altar, not eaten. Its skin is considered part of the tail.

If someone slaughters with intent to eat the skin of the tail tomorrow, they are shifting intent from altar consumption to human consumption.

The mishna rules this is piggul — but how? Piggul normally applies only when the item is meant for the same type of consumption!
4 / 9

Three Amoraic Solutions

Shmuel
The mishna follows Rabbi Eliezer: shifting intent from altar to person (or vice versa) still renders piggul.
Objection
But the next mishna implies only normal-manner intent creates piggul — that's the Rabbis' view! First clause R' Eliezer, second clause Rabbis?
Shmuel
Yes. The two clauses indeed follow different opinions.
Rav Huna
No need! The skin of the tail is not like the tail — it is eaten by people. Both mishnayot follow the Rabbis.
Rav Ḥisda
The skin is like the tail, but the mishna discusses a kid (young goat) whose tail is eaten, not burned.
Key Verse (Rav Huna) "The fat thereof, the fat tail" (Lev. 3:9) — the fat of the tail is offered, but not its skin.

Why Each Amora Rejects the Others

Shmuel

  • Accepts: skin = tail (burned)
  • Solution: R' Eliezer's view
  • Rejects Rav Huna: tradition says skin IS like tail
  • Rejects Rav Ḥisda: the mishna clearly discusses a sheep

Rav Huna

  • Accepts: both mishnayot = Rabbis
  • Solution: skin ≠ tail (skin is eaten)
  • Rejects Shmuel: won't split mishnayot between tannaim
  • Source: Lev. 3:9 — 'fat of tail,' not skin of tail

Rav Ḥisda

  • Accepts: skin = tail + both mishnayot = Rabbis
  • Solution: mishna discusses a kid (goat)
  • Rejects Shmuel: won't split mishnayot
  • Rejects Rav Huna: tradition says skin IS like tail
Objection to Rav Ḥisda If referring to a kid, what novelty does 'skin of the tail' teach? We already know from Ḥullin 122a that skin beneath the tail has the status of meat!

Rav Ḥisda's Response: Why the Mishna Is Needed

Ḥullin 122a teaches: skin of the tail = meat (soft & edible)
↓
You might think this applies only to ritual impurity (tumah)
↓
Possible Limitation
For Temple service: priestly portions must be eaten 'in greatness' (כדרך שהמלכים אוכלין) — kings don't eat skin of the tail!
Mishna's Teaching
Even for kodashim (sacred offerings), the skin of the tail counts as an edible portion — piggul intent applies
Key Phrase "As a consecrated portion" (Num. 18:8) — priestly gifts must be eaten 'in greatness' (בגדולה), as kings eat.

Amud B: Deriving Karet for Piggul & Notar

ConceptVerse SourcePenalty
Notar (leftover offering)"He has profaned the sacred item" (Lev. 19:8)Karet
Piggul (improper time intent)"It shall be piggul" (Lev. 7:18)Karet (derived via gezera shava)
Intent — outside designated areaAlso from Lev. 7:18 (Shmuel)Disqualified — NO karet
Gezera shava: 'sacred'–'sacred'Lev. 19:8 ↔ Exodus 29:34Links karet to notar specifically
Doubled verb: he'akhol ye'akhelLev. 7:18Two consumptions: person + altar
Rava's Conclusion The doubled verb he'akhol ye'akhel (הֵאָכֹל יֵאָכֵל) teaches that piggul applies to two types of consumption: eating by a person AND burning on the altar.
