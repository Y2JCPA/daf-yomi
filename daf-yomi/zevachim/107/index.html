Source of the Prohibition for Slaughtering Outside

Rava
The prohibition is derived per R' Yona: from the juxtaposition of 'there you shall offer up' and 'there you shall do' (Deut. 12:14).
R' Yona
Just as offering up (ha'ala'ah) has both a prohibition and a punishment, so too slaughtering — the Torah would not prescribe punishment without first prohibiting it.
Rava
Even though no explicit prohibition is stated for slaughtering outside, the juxtaposition makes clear it is prohibited.
PRINCIPLE The Torah does not prescribe punishment (karet) unless the act is also independently prohibited — אין עונשין אלא אם כן מזהירין.

Liability for Offering Up Unfit Offerings Outside

Rav Kahana

  • 'Va'alehem' (ואלהם) with alef sounds like 'alehem' (עליהם) with ayin
  • Read as: 'About that which is written in the adjacent passage'
  • Links offering up outside to offerings already slaughtered outside

Rava / School of R' Yishmael

  • Rejects Rav Kahana: 'to them' is plainly written, not 'about them'
  • Instead: the vav ('and') in 'And to them you shall say' mixes the two passages
  • Combines slaughtering outside and offering up outside

R' Yochanan

  • Verbal analogy (gezeirah shavah): 'bringing' in slaughtering passage ↔ 'bringing' in offering up passage
  • Just as slaughtering liability covers offerings burned outside, so does offering up
OBJECTION Rav Beivai: If these are two independent prohibitions (offering up after inside slaughter vs. after outside slaughter), the mishna in Karetot should list 37 cases of karet, not 36! The Gemara concedes: קשיא.

Source for Liability for Sprinkling Blood Outside

R' Yishmael
It is derived from the slaughtering passage: 'Blood shall be imputed to that man, he has shed blood' (Lev. 17:4) — this includes sprinkling (zerikah).
R' Akiva
It is derived from the offering up passage: 'a burnt offering or sacrifice' (Lev. 17:8) — the word 'or' (או) includes sprinkling.
Gemara
For R' Yishmael, 'or' divides burnt offering and sacrifice into two independent cases. For R' Akiva, the word 'it' (singular) in the next verse teaches they are separate cases.
NOTE R' Yishmael uses 'it' (אותו) for a different purpose: teaching liability for offering up a single limb outside.

Why Can't We Derive Sprinkling from a Common Element?

Can we derive liability for sprinkling from the common element of slaughtering + offering up?
↓
From Slaughtering?
Refuted: Slaughtering is unique — Paschal offering disqualified if slaughtered for those who can't eat
From Offering Up?
Refuted: Offering up is unique — liability also exists for meal offerings
↓
Common element should overcome individual refutations?
↓
Answer: The Torah explicitly wrote a verse for sprinkling — to teach that we cannot derive rites from a common element. Each rite needs its own verse.
IMPLICATION One who takes a handful from a meal offering (kemitzah) or collects blood (kabbalah) outside the Temple is NOT liable — no verse teaches these.
