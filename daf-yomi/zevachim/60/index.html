The Altar's Dimensions, Sanctity & Aftermath

R' Yehuda
The copper altar was 3 cubits high and 10×10 cubits wide. Solomon consecrated the courtyard floor to serve as an altar.
R' Yosei
The copper altar was 10 cubits high with a 5×5 cubit surface. Solomon only consecrated the courtyard to stand the altar in it.
Rava
Even R' Yehuda concedes that blood must be presented on the altar — the Passover eve cup of mixed blood proves this.
R' Elazar
If the altar is damaged, one may not eat the remainder of a meal offering — the verse says 'beside the altar.'
R' Yishmael
Second-tithe produce may not be eaten in Jerusalem after the Temple's destruction — it is juxtaposed with the firstborn offering.
Core Theme This daf explores how the altar's physical dimensions, sanctity, and functional presence define the validity of sacrificial service — from the Tabernacle era through the Temple's destruction.

The Copper Altar: R' Yehuda vs. R' Yosei

R' Yehuda

  • Altar height: 3 cubits (literal reading)
  • Surface: 10 × 10 cubits
  • Gezerah shavah of "square" teaches width from Ezekiel's altar
  • Derives external altar from external altar in Ezekiel
  • Priest is visible above courtyard curtains but items in hand are not

R' Yosei

  • Altar height: 10 cubits (from gezerah shavah)
  • Surface: 5 × 5 cubits
  • Gezerah shavah of "square" teaches height from golden incense altar
  • Derives portable vessel from portable vessel
  • Curtains were 5 cubits above the altar's upper edge
Key Principle R' Yehuda derives the copper altar's dimensions from the permanent altar in Ezekiel (external from external), while R' Yosei derives portable vessel from portable vessel (copper altar from golden incense altar).

The Gezerah Shavah of 'Square' (רָבוּעַ)

Both the copper altar and the incense altar are called "square" (רָבוּעַ)
↓
What does this verbal analogy teach?
R' Yehuda
Teaches the width = 10 cubits
(derived from Ezekiel's altar)
R' Yosei
Teaches the height = 10 cubits
(derived from incense altar)
↓
Underlying disagreement: Do we derive from an edifice (Temple altar) or from a portable vessel (incense altar)?
Logic R' Yosei holds we derive a portable vessel from another portable vessel; R' Yehuda holds we derive the external altar from the external altar in Ezekiel, even though it is a permanent structure.

Did Solomon Consecrate the Courtyard Floor?

R' Yehuda
"The king sanctified the middle of the court" — Solomon consecrated the floor itself to function as an altar for burning offerings.
R' Yosei
"The king sanctified" means he consecrated the courtyard merely to stand the altar in it, not that the floor itself had altar status.
Gemara
According to R' Yosei, what does "too small to receive" mean? It refers to the 5×5 altar being too small for all offerings.
R' Yehuda
"Too small" refers to Solomon's stone altar — it replaced the copper altar but couldn't accommodate the large quantity of offerings.
I Kings 8:64 "The king sanctified the middle of the court that was before the house of the Lord, because the copper altar that was before the Lord was too small to receive the burnt offerings."

Rava's Proof: Blood Must Be on the Altar

🩸 R' Yehuda says the entire Temple courtyard was consecrated as an altar for burning sacrificial portions
↓
🏺 Yet R' Yehuda also says: On Passover eve, a priest fills a cup with mixed blood from the floor
↓
⬆️ The priest then pours this cup on the altar — in case any offering's blood was never properly presented
↓
❓ But if the courtyard floor has altar status, the blood reaching the floor should already fulfill the mitzva!
↓
💡 Rava concludes: Even R' Yehuda agrees that blood must be presented on the altar itself
↓
🔄 Gemara rejects: Perhaps it's only for optimal performance (mitzva min ha-muvchar), not a strict requirement
Rejection The Gemara offers two counter-arguments: (1) R' Yehuda may require human-directed pouring (כח גברא), or (2) pouring on the altar is merely the preferred method, not strictly required.

R' Yishmael: Second Tithe After the Destruction

CategoryBrought to Jerusalem?Requires Altar?Eaten Without Temple?
Firstborn (בכור)YesYes — blood & fats on altarNo
First Fruits (ביכורים)YesNo blood — but placed beside altarNo
Second Tithe (מעשר שני)YesNo placement at all❓ Perhaps yes?
R' Yishmael's Ruling—Deut. 12:6 juxtaposes tithe with firstbornNo
Why a Verse Is Needed A kal va-chomer from firstborn and first fruits fails because each has a unique stringency (altar involvement). The Torah therefore juxtaposes second tithe with firstborn in Deuteronomy 12:6 to teach that it too requires the Temple's presence.
