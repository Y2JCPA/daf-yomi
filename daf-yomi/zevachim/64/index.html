Why the Southeast Corner for Bird Burnt Offerings?

Gemara
What is the reason the bird burnt offering (olat ha-of) is ideally at the southeast corner?
R' Yoḥanan
Because it is closest to the place of the ashes (beit ha-deshen), where the crop and feathers are placed.
R' Yoḥanan
Come and see the strength of the priests! The crop and feathers are the lightest parts, yet priests could toss them over 30 cubits from the southwest corner to the ash-place!
Gemara
But the distance is actually over 31 cubits!
R' Yoḥanan
I did not factor in the space occupied by the priest himself standing on the altar.
Insight R' Yoḥanan marvels at priestly athleticism — tossing tiny bird parts over 30 cubits (~15 meters) across the altar.

Does the High Priest Circle with Wine?

Gemara
But a Mishna in Tamid says the High Priest circles the altar and then is given wine to pour! This contradicts R' Yoḥanan's smoke concern!
R' Yoḥanan
The circling is done empty-handed (raglit). He receives the wine only after arriving at the southwest corner.
Rava
The Mishna's language confirms this: it says he 'is given wine to pour' — not that he is 'told to pour wine.' This implies he didn't have it yet.
Proof Rava's precise reading (diyuk) of the Mishna supports R' Yoḥanan: the passive "is given" shows the wine was handed to the Kohen Gadol only at the destination.

Bird Sin Offering Blood: Above or Below the Red Line?

Logical Argument (Rejected)

  • Animal offerings: burnt = below, sin = above
  • Bird offerings: burnt = above the red line
  • So bird sin offering should also be above?
  • This kal va-ḥomer would place blood above the line

Verse Overrides (Accepted)

  • Leviticus 5:9 — remaining blood "yimmatze" at the base
  • Blood must drain naturally to the altar base
  • The ledge blocks blood sprinkled above it
  • Therefore: sprinkling must be BELOW the red line
Rava's Key Insight The verse uses yimmatze (passive — 'it shall be drained') not yematze (active — 'he shall drain'). The blood must trickle down on its own to the base, requiring placement below the ledge.
