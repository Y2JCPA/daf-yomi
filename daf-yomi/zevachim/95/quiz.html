QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does an earthenware vessel need to be broken AFTER being punctured with a small root-sized hole?
A. The puncture purifies from tumah but the vessel retains vessel status for other purposes, so the Torah obligation to break it still applies
B. The puncture is only a rabbinic enactment and does not actually purify the vessel
C. The vessel must be broken to remove the absorbed flavor of the sin offering
D. Breaking is only a precautionary measure in case the puncture was not done correctly
Correct: A — A small root-sized hole removes the vessel's susceptibility to tumah, but it still functions as a vessel (e.g., for holding fruit). Since it's still technically a 'vessel,' the Torah's command to break it applies once it's brought back into the courtyard.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does Reish Lakish solve the problem of bringing the High Priest's impure robe into the courtyard?
A. He allows tearing it because the prohibition only applies during Temple service
B. He says to bring it in gradually, in portions less than 3×3 fingerbreadths, laundering each section at the threshold
C. He rules that the robe is not susceptible to tumah at all
D. He says to immerse it in a mikveh outside the courtyard instead
Correct: B — Since the Torah prohibits tearing the High Priest's robe (Exodus 28:32), Reish Lakish devises an alternative: bring it across the threshold gradually in portions smaller than the minimum size for tumah susceptibility, laundering section by section.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the Gemara's solution for using urine (one of the 7 laundering substances) in the Temple?
A. Apply all seven substances simultaneously to hide the urine
B. Substitute water for urine since they are similar
C. Absorb the urine in tasteless saliva (רוק תפל) from one who hasn't eaten since waking
D. Perform the laundering outside the Temple courtyard
Correct: C — Reish Lakish teaches that tasteless saliva (from someone who hasn't yet eaten) accompanies each substance, serving as the vehicle for the urine. This maintains Temple dignity while fulfilling the laundering requirement. Applying all 7 simultaneously is explicitly rejected by the Mishna.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is Rami bar Ḥama's unresolved dilemma regarding an earthenware oven?
A. Whether a cracked oven can still be used for offerings
B. Whether meat suspended in the oven's airspace (cooking without absorption) requires breaking the oven
C. Whether an oven used for meal offerings can also be used for sin offerings
D. Whether a metal oven requires scouring and rinsing like copper vessels
Correct: B — The dilemma is whether cooking alone — without the meat's flavor being absorbed into the vessel walls — triggers the Torah obligation to break an earthenware vessel. The Gemara attempts two proofs but rejects both, leaving the question unresolved.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does Rabba bar Ahilai prohibit eating bread from a fat-smeared oven even with salt alone?
A. Because the fat makes the bread taste bad
B. Because salt intensifies the flavor of the absorbed fat
C. Because one might come to eat the bread with kutaḥ (a dairy dish), violating meat-and-milk prohibitions
D. Because the oven becomes permanently tamei from the animal fat
Correct: C — The concern is a protective decree: even eating the bread plain might lead someone to eat it with kutaḥ (a dairy condiment), which would violate the prohibition of meat and milk — since the bread has absorbed meat fat from the oven walls.
