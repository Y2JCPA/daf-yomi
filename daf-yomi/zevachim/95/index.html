Earthenware Vessel: Puncture Then Break

Earthenware vessel used for sin offering becomes tamei outside the courtyard
↓
Puncture with hole the size of a small root (שורש קטן)
↓
Is it still a vessel?
For Tumah
❌ No — purified from impurity
For Other Uses
✅ Yes — still holds fruit
↓
Bring back into courtyard and break it to fulfill: "The earthenware vessel…shall be broken" (Lev. 6:21)
Insight The small puncture resolves the tumah problem, but the vessel retains enough identity to require the Torah-mandated breaking inside the azarah.

Earthenware vs. Copper: Vessel Procedures

Earthenware (כלי חרס)

  • Puncture small root-sized hole outside
  • Purified from tumah but still a 'vessel'
  • Bring inside the courtyard
  • Break the vessel (Torah obligation)
  • Cannot be repaired — earthenware absorbs permanently

Copper (כלי נחושת)

  • Bore a large hole outside
  • Purified from tumah and no longer a vessel
  • Bring inside the courtyard
  • Hammer and refashion into a new vessel
  • Scour (מריקה) and rinse (שטיפה) the refashioned vessel
Why Scour After Refashioning? Since copper can be repaired, the Torah obligation of scouring and rinsing applies to the refashioned vessel — unlike earthenware which is simply destroyed.

The High Priest's Robe: How to Bring It In?

General Rule
A garment sprayed with sin offering blood that became tamei outside must be torn before being brought back in for laundering.
Reish Lakish
The High Priest's robe (מעיל) is different — the Torah says "It shall not be torn" (Ex. 28:32). Instead, bring it in gradually, in sections less than 3×3 fingerbreadths, laundering each section as it crosses the threshold.
Rav Adda bar Ahava
Objection! Thick garments (בגדים עבים) are not subject to the standard 3×3 measure — they need to be larger to become tamei. The robe is thick, so why worry about 3×3?
Gemara
The whole robe is a garment of special significance. Even small portions are significant due to their source garment, so they are susceptible to impurity at 3×3 fingerbreadths.
Resolution The robe's special status as a priestly garment makes even small portions significant — requiring the careful, gradual entry method of Reish Lakish.

The Seven Abrasive Substances & the Urine Problem

🩸 Blood of sin offering sprayed on a garment requires laundering with 7 abrasive substances
↓
🧪 One of the 7 substances is urine (מי רגלים)
↓
🚫 Problem: Urine may not be brought into the Temple — it is inappropriate
↓
❌ Can't apply all 7 simultaneously — the Mishna says this is invalid
↓
❌ Can't absorb urine with just one substance — each must be rubbed 3 times independently
↓
✅ Solution (Reish Lakish): Absorb the urine in tasteless saliva (רוק תפל) — saliva from one who hasn't eaten since waking
↓
💡 Tasteless saliva accompanies each substance applied to the garment
Reish Lakish's Principle Tasteless saliva (רוק תפל) must accompany each of the seven substances — it serves as the vehicle for the urine, maintaining Temple dignity while fulfilling the laundering requirement.

Mishna: Scouring & Rinsing — Cooking vs. Pouring

FactorHalakha
Cooked offering in copper vesselRequires scouring and rinsing
Poured boiling offering into vesselAlso requires scouring and rinsing
Most sacred offerings (קדשי קדשים)Requires scouring and rinsing
Lesser sanctity offerings (קדשים קלים)Requires scouring and rinsing
R' Shimon's view on lesser sanctityDoes NOT require scouring and rinsing
Source verse"In which it is…shall be broken" — if hot meat is IN the vessel, whether cooked or poured
Derivation The phrase "in which it is" (Lev. 6:21) expands beyond cooking — any vessel containing hot sacrificial meat, even by pouring, is subject to these laws.
