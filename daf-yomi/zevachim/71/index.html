When Offerings Become Intermingled with Forbidden Animals

An offering becomes intermingled with forbidden animals
↓
What kind of forbidden animal?
Sin offerings left to die / Ox sentenced to stoning
All must die — even 1 in 10,000
Animals unfit for altar (e.g. kilayim, tereifa)
Graze until blemished, then sold; owner brings replacement
Key Principle Animals from which benefit is forbidden (issur hana'ah) render the entire mixture prohibited in any ratio — they are never nullified.

Additional Disqualified Animals (Amud B)

Biological Defects

  • כלאים — Born of diverse kinds (e.g. ram + goat)
  • טריפה — Animal with fatal wound (dies within 12 months)
  • יוצא דופן — Born by caesarean section

Resolution

  • Animals graze until they develop a blemish
  • Then sold at market value
  • Owner brings replacement equal to highest-quality animal in the mixture
Financial Note The owner always bears the cost — replacement is based on the most expensive animal in the mixture.

Rules for Intermingled Sacrificial Animals

Sacrificial animal intermingled with another sacrificial animal
↓
Are they the same type of offering?
✅ Same Type
Each is sacrificed for the sake of whoever is its owner — both fulfill their obligation
❌ Different Types
Graze until blemished → sold → owner brings two replacements (one of each type) at value of the best animal
Exception If intermingled with bechor (firstborn) or ma'aser (animal tithe): graze until blemished, then eaten with the restrictions of bechor/ma'aser.

Critical Rule

הכל יכולין להתערב זה בזה חוץ מן החטאת ומן האשם
Mishna All offerings can become intermingled with each other — except for a חטאת (sin offering) and an אשם (guilt offering), as the Gemara will explain separately.

Why Are Both Mishnayot Needed? (Zevachim & Temura)

Gemara
We already learned in Temura 28a that prohibited animals render a mixture forbidden in any amount. Why repeat it here?
Rav Shimi
Both are necessary! Each teaches a novelty (חידוש) the other does not.
Rav Ashi
If we only had Temura, I'd say the stringency applies only to sacrificing to the Most High (גבוה) — but an ordinary person (הדיוט) could still benefit.
Rav Ashi
So our mishna teaches: even for benefit to a hedyot, all must die — no redemption possible.
Rav Ashi
And if we only had our mishna, I'd limit it to items where benefit is already forbidden (sin offerings to die, ox to be stoned).
Rav Ashi
So Temura teaches: even animals like רובע/נרבע, where benefit is not forbidden, still render a mixture unfit for the altar in any ratio.
Conclusion Each mishna covers a different scope of prohibition: Temura addresses fitness for sacrifice; Zevachim addresses prohibition of benefit.
