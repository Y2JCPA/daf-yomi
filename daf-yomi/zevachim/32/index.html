Daf 32: Unfit Slaughter & Partial Entry into the Azarah

Mishna: Unfit persons who slaughtered — valid after the fact
↓
Gemara asks: Why only after the fact? Torah permits non-priests ab initio!
↓
Answer: Mishna uses past tense because of impure person — rabbinically barred lest he touch the flesh
↓
But how can an impure person slaughter at all? He can't enter the Azarah!
↓
Solution
He fashions a long knife and stands outside the courtyard
New Question
Is partial entry (miktzat bi'ah) considered entry?
Key Issue The daf pivots from the validity of slaughter by unfit persons to a major debate about whether inserting part of one's body into the Temple courtyard constitutes forbidden entry.

Ab Initio or Only After the Fact?

Mishna
All those who are unfit who slaughtered — the slaughter is valid.
Gemara
The past tense implies: after the fact yes, but ab initio no. But the Torat Kohanim teaches non-priests may slaughter ab initio!
Gemara
"Veshachat" — "And he shall slaughter" — does not specify a priest. From collection of blood onward is priestly duty.
Resolution
Ab initio is indeed permitted. The Mishna uses past tense only because of the ritually impure person, who is rabbinically barred lest he touch the flesh.
Leviticus 1:5 "And Aaron's sons, the priests, shall present the blood" — from kabbalah (collection) onward is the mitzva of priesthood, but shechitah is valid by anyone.

How Can an Impure Person Slaughter?

An impure person is prohibited from entering the Azarah (Temple courtyard)
↓
Slaughter must be performed "before the Lord" — i.e., inside the Azarah
↓
Solution: He fashions a long knife (סכין ארוכה), stands outside, and slaughters the animal inside
↓
But for semicha (placing hands), could he also insert hands from outside?
↓
First Tanna
Miktzat bi'ah = bi'ah — partial entry counts as entry. He may not insert his hands.
Shimon HaTimni
The slaughterer himself must stand inside the Azarah — no long knife solution!
Key Dispute The first tanna allows the slaughterer to stand outside with a long knife, while Shimon HaTimni requires the slaughterer's hands to be farther inside than the animal.

Rav Ḥisda vs. First Tanna: Semicha & Shechita

First Tanna's Reading

  • Just as semicha requires ritual purity → so does shechita
  • Semicha is the known requirement ("before the Lord")
  • Shechita purity is derived from semicha
  • Long knife solves shechita; partial entry bars semicha

Rav Ḥisda's Reading

  • Just as shechita requires purity → so does semicha
  • Shechita purity is the known requirement
  • Semicha purity is derived from shechita
  • Follows Shimon HaTimni: slaughterer must be inside
Common Ground Both agree the juxtaposition of Leviticus 1:4–5 links semicha and shechita. They disagree on which is the source and which is the derivation.

Reish Lakish: Partial Entry Means Flogging

Ulla / Reish Lakish
A ritually impure person who inserted his hand into the Azarah is flogged — partial entry is entry!
Ulla / Reish Lakish
Proof: Leviticus 12:4 juxtaposes touching (נגיעה) and coming (ביאה). Just as touching with part of the body counts, so does entry.
Rav Hoshaya
Objection! A leper on Erev Pesach who had a seminal emission — he must insert his thumb into the Azarah for purification. If partial entry = entry, he'd face karet!
Ulla
A leper is different — the Torah permitted him to enter for purification. Since he's permitted for leprosy, he's also permitted regarding the emission. הותרה מכללה.
Principle מקצת ביאה כביאה — Partial entry into the Temple courtyard is legally equivalent to full entry, carrying the same prohibitions and penalties.

The Leper on Erev Pesach: Competing Obligations

FactorDetails
ScenarioLeper's 8th day falls on Erev Pesach; he experiences a seminal emission
ProblemHe is a tevul yom (immersed but awaiting nightfall) — normally barred from Temple Mount
Sages' RulingHe may enter the Temple Mount despite being tevul yom
ReasoningKorban Pesach (positive mitzva + karet) overrides the sending-away of impure (positive mitzva, no karet)
R' YoḥananTevul yom's prohibition from the Azarah is only rabbinic — derived from II Chronicles 20:5, "the new courtyard"
Ulla's ChallengeIf partial entry = entry, this leper faces two karet-level mitzvot — neither overrides the other!
Ulla's AnswerThe leper is a special case: Torah permitted his partial entry for purification — this extends to his emission too
R' Yoḥanan on II Chronicles 20:5 "The new courtyard" — the court that innovated (חידשו) the rule that a tevul yom may not enter the Levite camp. This proves the prohibition is rabbinic, not Torah law.
