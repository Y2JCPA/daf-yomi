QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the Mishna use past tense ("who slaughtered") implying only after-the-fact validity, when non-priests may actually slaughter ab initio?
A. Because the Mishna follows a stricter opinion that non-priests may never slaughter
B. Because of the ritually impure person, who is rabbinically barred from slaughtering ab initio lest he touch the flesh
C. Because the Torah requires priestly slaughter ab initio
D. Because women and slaves may only slaughter after the fact
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that the Mishna's past-tense language is driven by the case of the ritually impure person, who is rabbinically prohibited from slaughtering ab initio lest he touch and contaminate the flesh. All other unfit persons (non-priests, women, slaves) may in fact slaughter ab initio.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does the Gemara explain that an impure person can perform slaughter if he cannot enter the Temple courtyard?
A. He is temporarily purified for the duration of the slaughter
B. He fashions a long knife, stands outside the courtyard, and slaughters the animal inside
C. Another priest holds the animal outside the courtyard for him
D. The impure person may enter the courtyard for slaughter purposes only
Correct: B — The Gemara proposes that the impure person uses a long knife (סכין ארוכה), standing outside the Azarah while reaching the knife inside to slaughter the animal. This avoids any physical entry into the courtyard.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Reish Lakish's position on miktzat bi'ah (partial entry into the Azarah)?
A. Partial entry is permitted as long as most of the body remains outside
B. Partial entry is only prohibited rabbinically
C. Partial entry is considered full entry and the impure person is flogged
D. Partial entry is permitted for hands only, not for feet
Correct: C — Reish Lakish (transmitted by Ulla) rules that miktzat bi'ah k'vi'ah — partial entry is legally equivalent to full entry. He derives this from Leviticus 12:4, which juxtaposes 'touching' and 'coming': just as touching with part of the body counts, so does entry with part of the body. The violator receives lashes.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Ulla resolve the objection from the leper on Erev Pesach who must insert his thumb into the Azarah?
A. The leper's partial entry is not considered entry because it is for a mitzva
B. The Torah specifically permitted the leper to enter for purification, and this permission extends to his other impurities
C. R' Yoḥanan holds that tevul yom's prohibition is only rabbinic, so there is no problem
D. The leper does not actually need to insert his thumb — the priest can do it outside
Correct: B — Ulla responds with the principle הותרה מכללה — the leper is a special case where the Torah explicitly permitted his partial entry for the purification ritual. Since he was permitted entry regarding his leprosy, this permission extends to cover his impurity from the seminal emission as well.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does Abaye reject Rav Yosef's extension of Ulla's principle to zavim who became corpse-impure?
A. Because zavim cannot bring a Paschal offering under any circumstances
B. Because the leper case involves one body with two impurities permitted together, while zavim and corpse-impurity are separate categories that cannot be linked
C. Because Abaye holds that miktzat bi'ah is not considered bi'ah
D. Because corpse impurity is more severe than ziva
Correct: B — Abaye distinguishes: in the leper's case, the same person has two impurities and the Torah's permission for one extends to the other on the same body. But Rav Yosef's case involves two entirely separate categories of impurity (ziva and corpse-impurity) — permission granted for one category does not automatically extend to a different category.
