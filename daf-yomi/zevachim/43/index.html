Daf 43 — Can the Altar Remove Disqualification?

Mishna lists items exempt from piggul:
Handful, incense, frankincense, priestly meal offerings, blood
↓
Ulla: A piggul handful offered on the altar —
its piggul status leaves it
↓
Novelty
Even a handful (separate pieces) — if fire took hold of any part, the entire handful returns to the altar
Extension
R' Yoḥanan: Piggul, notar, and impure flesh brought up to the altar — prohibition leaves them
Core Question Does the altar's fire have the power to remove disqualifications from sacrificial items?

Items Exempt from Piggul (Mishna)

ItemWhy Exempt
Handful (Kometz)It is itself the mattir (permitting factor)
Incense (Ketoret)It is itself the permitting factor
Frankincense (Levonah)It is itself the permitting factor
Meal offering of KohanimNo handful removed — burned entirely
Meal offering of anointed KohenHalf morning, half evening — burned entirely
BloodIt is the permitting factor for all offerings
Independent libations (R' Meir)Not permitted by another item
Principle An item that is itself the mattir (permitting factor) cannot become piggul — piggul only applies to items permitted by something else.

Libations & the Leper's Log of Oil

R' Meir
Libations brought independently are exempt from piggul. But the log of oil of a leper's guilt offering is subject to piggul — the blood of the guilt offering permits it.
Rabbis
Even libations brought with an animal offering are exempt from piggul.
R' Shimon
The leper's log of oil is not subject to piggul — it is not permitted by any other item.
R' Meir
It is permitted — the blood must be sacrificed before the oil is sprinkled and given to the Kohanim.
Key Dispute Does the blood of the leper's guilt offering serve as a mattir for the log of oil? R' Meir says yes; R' Shimon says no.

Ulla's Teaching: Piggul Handful on the Altar

Ulla: A piggul handful offered on the altar — its piggul status has left it
↓
Gemara challenges: What is the novelty?
We already know the handful is exempt from piggul!
↓
Perhaps: If piggul items ascend the altar, they don't descend?
But we know that too from a mishna (84a)!
↓
Perhaps: If they descended, they re-ascend?
But the mishna (84b) says they do not re-ascend!
↓
Answer: Ulla's novelty is the case where fire already took hold of the handful before it descended — then it returns to the altar
Still Not Done The Gemara presses further: Ulla already taught elsewhere that fire taking hold means items re-ascend! The final novelty: this applies even to a handful of separate pieces, not just a connected limb.

Limb vs. Handful — Fire Takes Hold

Limb of an Offering (אֵבֶר)

  • All parts are connected together as one unit
  • If fire took hold of part, the whole limb is considered 'food of the altar'
  • If it descended — it re-ascends in its entirety
  • This is the expected case

Handful of Meal Offering (קוֹמֶץ)

  • Consists of separate pieces — not one unit
  • You might think only the burned portion returns
  • Ulla's novelty: The ENTIRE handful re-ascends
  • Rav Aḥai adds: Even if half is on the ground — bring it all up ab initio
Rav Aḥai Since the handful is considered one unit, if half is on the ground and half is on the altar with fire taking hold — bring all of it up to the altar, even lekhatchila.

Does the Altar Remove Impurity?

R' Yoḥanan
Piggul, notar (leftover), and ritually impure flesh — if brought up to the altar, their prohibition has left them.
Rav Ḥisda
Is the altar a mikveh (ritual bath) that can purify an impure item?!
R' Zeira
R' Yoḥanan refers to a case where fire took hold — then the item belongs to the altar and the prohibition lapses.
R' Yitzḥak bar Bisna
But a baraita says impure flesh's impurity cannot depart — unlike a person who can immerse in a mikveh!
Rava
The baraita means impurity cannot depart via a mikveh — but it can depart via the altar's fire.
Rav Pappa
The verse discusses peace offering meat, which is eaten, not burned — so the altar cannot help it.
Ravina
The verse says 'upon him' — referring to one whose impurity departs while whole. Flesh only loses impurity when deficient (burned), so the verse excludes it either way.
Resolution Multiple answers reconcile R' Yoḥanan with the baraita: the verse specifically excludes purification via mikveh, or refers to peace offering meat (not altar-bound), or requires departure while 'whole.'
