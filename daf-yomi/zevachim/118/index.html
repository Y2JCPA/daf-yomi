Private Altars: Who May Sacrifice What?

R' Yehuda
On a great public altar, even an individual's compulsory offerings may be sacrificed. The verse 'whatsoever is fitting in his own eyes' refers only to private altars.
Rabbis
On a private altar, an individual may sacrifice only burnt offerings and peace offerings — voluntary offerings alone.
Gemara
But isn't 'man' written in the verse — implying an individual may only bring voluntary offerings?
R' Yehuda
The word 'man' teaches that a non-priest (zar) may serve on a private altar — not a limitation on offering types.
Gemara
But non-priest eligibility is already derived from Leviticus 17:6 — priest required only at the Tent of Meeting!
Gemara
'Man' excludes even the firstborn requirement — on a private altar, any person may sacrifice, not just firstborns as before the Tabernacle.
Key Point Before the Tabernacle was built, the firstborn served as priests. The verse 'every man' teaches that on private altars, this restriction no longer applies.

First Tanna vs. Rabbis: Libations in the Wilderness

First Tanna

  • Private altar: only burnt & peace offerings
  • Libations were NOT offered in the wilderness
  • Libations were NOT offered in Gilgal
  • Libation obligation began only later

Rabbis (Latter Section)

  • Private altar: only burnt & peace offerings
  • Libations WERE offered in the wilderness
  • Libations WERE offered in Gilgal
  • Same offering types — different view on libations
Rav Pappa Rav Pappa identifies the sole practical difference between the first tanna and the Rabbis: whether libations (nesakhim) accompanied offerings during the wilderness period and Gilgal.

R' Shimon: Gilgal Was Limited to Time-Bound Offerings

Joshua 5:10 — 'They kept the Passover in Gilgal'
↓
Why state the obvious that they brought the Paschal offering?
↓
It teaches: In Gilgal, only offerings similar to Pesach were brought
↓
Permitted in Gilgal
Paschal offerings and compulsory public offerings with a set time (zman kavu'a)
Not Permitted
Offerings that do not have a set time were not sacrificed in Gilgal
Rabbis' View The Rabbis use the same verse for R' Yochanan's teaching: an uncircumcised Jew who became impure from a corpse may receive sprinkling of the red heifer waters — as occurred when Joshua circumcised the people after purification.

Rav Adda's Clarification When the baraita mentions 'compulsory offerings with a set time' not sacrificed on private altars, it refers to the olas re'iyah (burnt offering of appearance on Festivals) — since there is no individual compulsory sin offering with a set time.

Eating Kodashim Kalim: Overlooking Shiloh

R' Abbahu
Genesis 49:22 — Joseph is a 'fruitful vine by a fountain (ayin).' The eye that refused to benefit from Potiphar's wife merited that offerings be eaten wherever Shiloh can be seen.
R' Yosei b'R Chanina
Deuteronomy 33:16 — 'The goodwill of Him who dwelt in the bush (seneh).' Read seneh as senu'in (haters): offerings may be eaten even in other tribes' portions overlooking Shiloh.
Tanna
'Overlooks' means one sees Shiloh in its entirety with nothing obstructing the view.
Rav Pappa
Even partial sight of Shiloh suffices. But what if one must stand to see it — does sitting disqualify the location?
R' Yirmeyah
What if one can see Shiloh from the bank of a stream but not from within it? Teiku — the dilemma stands unresolved.
R' Shimon ben Elyakum He illustrated 'overlooking' with a practical example: the synagogue of Maon, from which the city of Tiberias was fully visible — this is the type of unobstructed line-of-sight required.
