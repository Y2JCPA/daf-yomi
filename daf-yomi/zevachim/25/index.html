Why Does R' Shimon Need Rava's Verbal Analogy?

Gemara
According to R' Shimon, who doesn't require sanctification of the handful (or deems left hand valid for sanctification) — why is Rava's gezeira shava of 'hand'/'hand' needed?
Rav Yehuda b. R' Chiyya
R' Shimon compares the meal offering to a sin offering and guilt offering (Lev. 6:10). By hand → right hand (like sin offering). With vessel → left hand permitted (like guilt offering).
Gemara
So the right-hand rule for kemitzah is already known! What does the gezeira shava add?
Answer
It teaches that even a sinner's meal offering (minchat chotet) requires the right hand. You might think: since it has no oil or frankincense (to avoid superior quality), left hand should also be valid.
Conclusion
The gezeira shava teaches: kemitzah always requires the right hand, even for a sinner's offering.
Principle R' Shimon's logic that a sinner's offering should be inferior does not extend to using the left hand for kemitzah.

Mishna: Blood That Spills Before Collection

🐂 Animal is slaughtered
↓
Does the blood flow directly into a service vessel?
Yes
✅ Blood is fit for sprinkling
No — Falls on Floor
❌ Even if collected afterward, blood is disqualified (pasul)
Source Derived from "middam happar" (Lev. 4:5) — read as dam mehappar: the blood must come from the bull, not from the floor.

Types of Blood: What Counts as 'Dam HaNefesh'?

Type of BloodDescriptionStatus
Dam HaNefesh (Blood of the Soul)Blood that flows at the moment of slaughter as the animal dies✅ Fit
Dam HaOr (Blood of the Skin)Blood that seeps when the skin is cut before slaughter❌ Unfit
Dam HaTamtzit (Blood of Exudate)Blood that oozes after the initial spurt of slaughter ends❌ Unfit
Blood from the FloorDam HaNefesh that spilled before collection❌ Unfit
Blood from the KnifeBlood remaining on the slaughter knife❌ Must not enter vessel
Rav via Shmuel The slaughterer must hold the knife upward after slaughter so knife-blood doesn't contaminate the collection vessel. He wipes it on the rim of the bowl (keforei — Ezra 1:10).

Proper Blood Collection: Step by Step

🔪 Slaughter the animal at the neck
↓
⬆️ Hold the knife UP so blood doesn't drip into the vessel (Shmuel)
↓
🧹 Wipe knife blood on the RIM of the bowl — keforei zahav (Abaye)
↓
🫙 Place the service vessel directly under to catch the blood flow
↓
🩸 Ensure ALL blood is collected — "v'et kol dam hapar" (Rav)
↓
🫀 Place the VEINS into the vessel so all blood empties out (R' Yirmeyah bar Abba)
↓
✅ Veins must 'see' the airspace of the vessel (R' Yochanan)
Key Requirement Rav derives from "and all the blood of the bull he shall pour" (Lev. 4:7) — since the pouring requires all the blood, the collection must include all the blood too.

The Broken Bowl Dilemma: Airspace of a Vessel

R' Asi
If the priest collects blood and the bottom of the bowl breaks before the blood settles — does entering the airspace (avir) count as collection?
R' Yochanan
A baraita about a barrel under a pipe: water in its airspace is considered collected (unfit as mei chatat). So yes — airspace counts!
Gemara
But that barrel will ultimately hold the water! R' Asi asks about a vessel that won't hold it (the bowl is broken).
Gemara (Resolves)
R' Asi actually asked two dilemmas: (1) Airspace when it won't settle; (2) If not — what about when it will settle? R' Yochanan answered the second.
Rav Yosef / Rav Kahana / Rabba
Three versions of this exchange are recorded, each framing the question and answer slightly differently.
Core Question Is the airspace (avir kli) of a vessel considered part of the vessel? This principle affects both Temple service and mei chatat (water of purification).
