QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to R' Yehuda, why is it permitted to collect and use blood that spilled on the Temple floor despite it being mixed with blood of exudate?
A. Blood of exudate is valid for atonement
B. Blood does not nullify blood — the valid blood retains its status
C. The blood of exudate evaporates quickly
D. A majority of the blood is blood of the soul
Correct: B — R' Yehuda holds 'ein dam mevatel dam' — blood does not nullify blood. Even though blood of exudate is mixed in, the valid blood (blood of the soul) is not nullified and the mixture may be sprinkled on the altar.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does moist blood on the Temple floor NOT constitute an interposition (chatzitza) between the priests' feet and the floor?
A. The blood was sanctified and therefore cannot interpose
B. Moist substances (blood, ink, honey, milk) do not interpose — only dry ones do
C. The priests wore special sandals
D. Interposition only applies to the hands, not the feet
Correct: B — A baraita teaches that blood, ink, honey, and milk constitute an interposition when dry, but not when moist. Since the pooled blood was moist, it did not create a chatzitza problem.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
If a person slaughters an offering intending to eat an olive-bulk of the altar portions (emurim) outside the designated time, what is the ruling according to the Sages?
A. The offering is piggul
B. The offering is fit — intent regarding wrong-category items doesn't disqualify
C. The offering must be burned immediately
D. The offering is valid but the person receives lashes
Correct: B — The Mishna rules that intent to eat items normally burned on the altar (or burn items normally eaten) does not disqualify the offering. This is a 'wrong category' intent. R' Eliezer disagrees and deems it unfit.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why did the priests walk in blood only while carrying wood to the altar, not during actual rites?
A. Wood carrying was done by non-priests
B. The wood absorbed the blood
C. Carrying wood is not classified as an actual rite, so soiled garments wouldn't invalidate it
D. The wood was carried before the blood accumulated
Correct: C — Soiled priestly garments invalidate Temple rites. Since carrying wood to the altar arrangement is not classified as an actual avodah (rite), walking through blood during this task does not create a disqualification problem.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Rava prove that piggul of the main offering extends to the fetus and placenta (supporting R' Elazar)?
A. A verse explicitly includes fetus and placenta in piggul laws
B. The Mishna exempts milk and eggs from piggul karet but omits fetus and placenta — implying they ARE subject to it
C. R' Yehuda explicitly states that fetus and placenta become piggul
D. The baraita lists fetus and placenta as items that render piggul
Correct: B — Rava notes that the Mishna specifically exempts milk of animals and eggs of doves from karet for piggul, but does NOT mention fetus and placenta. This omission implies that fetus and placenta are indeed subject to piggul — through the disqualification of the main offering.
