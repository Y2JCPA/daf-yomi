Zevachim 47 — Intent, Ownership & Sacred Locations

DAF OVERVIEW This daf explores the requirement of conscious intent during slaughter of offerings, debates whether an owner's improper thought can invalidate a sacrifice, maps the logical relationships between rulings on cross-person intent in Temple service, Shabbat, and idolatry, and then transitions to the Mishna detailing where and how the most sacred offerings are processed — including the unique dual blood-collection of the leper's guilt offering.

Slaughter Without Awareness (Mitasek)

Shmuel
From where do we derive that slaughtering without intent (מתעסק) disqualifies a korban?
Rav Huna
"And he shall slaughter the bull" (Lev. 1:5) — the slaughter must be for the sake of a bull, meaning with awareness of the act.
Shmuel
We already know it's a mitzvah — but from where do we know it's indispensable (מעכב)?
Rav Huna
"לִרְצֹנְכֶם תִּזְבָּחֻהוּ" — With your will you shall slaughter it (Lev. 19:5). Full conscious awareness is required.
KEY VERSE Leviticus 19:5 — "לִרְצֹנְכֶם תִּזְבָּחֻהוּ" serves double duty: requiring both conscious intent during slaughter and establishing that willful awareness is indispensable.

Can the Owner Render an Offering Piggul?

Mishna's Rule: Intent follows only the one performing the rite (the priest)
↓
R' Elazar b"R Yosei disagrees: The owner too can render it piggul
↓
Rava's Reason
Numbers 15:4 — "he who sacrifices shall sacrifice his offering" — the owner is called 'the one who sacrifices'
Mishna's View
Only the priest performing the service can affect the offering's status through intent
DISPUTE The Mishna holds intent follows the kohen alone. R' Elazar b"R Yosei holds the owner's improper thought can also create piggul.

Abaye's Triad: Cross-Person Intent

SageContextRuling
R' Elazar b"R YoseiTemple offeringsOwner's intent renders the priest's service piggul
R' EliezerSlaughter for a gentile (Chullin 38b)Gentile's presumed idolatrous intent invalidates the Jew's slaughter
R' Shimon ben ElazarCarrying on ShabbatOne who stores an atypical item — another who carries it out is liable based on the storer's intent
ABAYE'S PRINCIPLE All three hold: Even with two people — one intending and one acting — the intention of one is effective for the action of the other.

Logical Relationships: Who Accepts Whom?

R' Shimon ben Elazar

  • Accepts R' Eliezer: If cross-person intent works for Shabbat, certainly for idol worship (similar to Temple)
  • Accepts R' Elazar b"R Yosei: Kal va-chomer from outside Temple to inside

R' Eliezer

  • Accepts R' Elazar b"R Yosei: Temple intent is explicit in Torah
  • Does NOT necessarily accept R' Shimon ben Elazar: Shabbat requires planned constructive labor by the doer

R' Elazar b"R Yosei

  • Does NOT necessarily accept either sage
  • Perhaps cross-person intent works only inside the Temple where intent is explicit
ASYMMETRY The acceptance flows upward (Shabbat → Idolatry → Temple) but not downward. Broader rulings encompass narrower ones, but not vice versa.

Yom Kippur Bull & Goat — Blood Service

🔪 Slaughter in the north of the Temple courtyard
↓
🏺 Collect blood in a service vessel in the north
↓
🩸 Sprinkle between the staves of the Ark (Holy of Holies)
↓
🩸 Sprinkle on the Parochet (Curtain)
↓
🩸 Sprinkle on the Golden Altar (מזבח הזהב)
↓
💧 Pour remainder on western base of external altar (not indispensable)
↓
🔥 Burn carcass at the place of ashes outside Jerusalem
CRITICAL RULE Failure to perform even one of the three sprinklings (Ark, Curtain, Golden Altar) disqualifies the offering. But failure to pour the remainder does not disqualify.
