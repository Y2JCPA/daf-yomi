Zevachim 79 — The Laws of Nullification (Bitul)

CORE THEME Rava summarizes three principles of nullification: by taste (min b'she'eino mino), by majority (min b'mino), and by appearance. The Gemara then applies these principles to ritual purity, Temple offerings, and even the Pesach seder.
3 / 9

Rava's Three Rules of Nullification (Bitul)

A prohibited substance is mixed with a permitted one
↓
Min b'She'eino Mino (different types)
Nullified by taste (ta'am) — if the taste is undetectable, the mixture is permitted
Min b'Mino (same type)
Nullified by majority (rov) — the larger quantity determines the status
Appearance is distinguishable
Nullified by appearance (mar'it) — if visually recognizable, it is not nullified
KEY PRINCIPLE Rava organizes all nullification law into three categories based on the nature of the mixture.

Rabbi Yehuda & Rabban Gamliel: Min b'Mino

R' Yehuda (Mishna)
Water of purification (mei chatat) is nullified in a majority of mikveh water.
R' Yehuda (Baraita)
White wine mixed with water is not nullified — it remains wine!
Gemara
Isn't this a contradiction? Both are min b'mino cases!
Resolution
The mishna is R' Yehuda's own lenient view; the baraita reflects his teacher Rabban Gamliel's stricter ruling.
R' Yehuda citing Rabban Gamliel
Blood doesn't nullify blood, spittle doesn't nullify spittle, urine doesn't nullify urine — min b'mino is never nullified.
DISTINCTION R' Yehuda personally holds min b'mino can be nullified by majority, but transmits Rabban Gamliel's stricter view that it cannot.

Rava's Alternative: The Bucket Explanation

Torah Law

  • Bucket: inside is pure, outside is impure
  • Any amount of mikveh water touching the outside suffices
  • The mei chatat inside is irrelevant to the purification

Rabbinic Decree

  • Sages required immersing the entire vessel (inside too)
  • Concern: owner might try to spare the mei chatat
  • He might immerse improperly to avoid water entering
  • Once majority is mikveh water inside — Torah law is satisfied
RAVA Rava reframes the mishna: it's not about nullification at all, but about a rabbinic precaution regarding proper immersion of the vessel.

Hillel's Korech — Mitzvot Don't Nullify Each Other

עַל מַצּוֹת וּמְרֹרִים יֹאכְלֻהוּ
FAMOUS HALAKHA Hillel the Elder would wrap the Paschal offering, matza, and maror together and eat them as one, based on the verse: 'They shall eat it with matzot and bitter herbs' (Bamidbar 9:11).

R' Elazar derives from Hillel: Just as mitzvah items do not nullify one another, so too prohibited items do not nullify one another — disagreeing with Reish Lakish who says prohibited items (piggul, notar, tamei) do nullify each other.

Purifying a Flask That Absorbed a Zav's Urine

🏺 Broken earthenware flask absorbed urine of a zav/zava (primary impurity source)
↓
💧 Pour WATER into it — 1st wash: still impure
↓
💧 2nd wash with water: still impure
↓
💧 3rd wash with water: NOW PURE ✓
↓
⚠️ But if washed with URINE of a pure person instead of water — even 10 times remains impure!
↓
🔄 R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov disagrees: even with urine, 3 washes suffice
MIN B'MINO The first tanna (identified as R' Yehuda) holds that urine cannot nullify urine — a classic case of min b'mino. Water, being a different substance, can wash away the impurity.
