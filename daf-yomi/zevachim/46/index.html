Why Is Blood Exempt from Me'ilah?

Ulla
The verse says "to you" (לכם) — this teaches that blood is yours. It is not the property of the Temple, so me'ilah does not apply.
School of R' Yishmael
"To make atonement" — God says: I gave blood for atonement, not for the prohibition of me'ilah.
R' Yochanan
"For it is the blood that makes atonement" — the word hu (הוא) teaches that blood's status remains the same before and after atonement.
Gemara
R' Yochanan relies on the principle: once a mitzva is performed, the item is no longer subject to me'ilah. Since blood is exempt after, "it is" teaches it's also exempt before.
Source All three opinions derive from Leviticus 17:11, each focusing on a different phrase in the same verse.

Challenge: Items Subject to Me'ilah After Their Mitzva

Principle: No item whose mitzva has been performed is still subject to me'ilah
↓
But what about removal of ashes (תרומת הדשן)? Me'ilah applies even after removal!
↓
Ashes + priestly vestments of Yom Kippur = two verses that come as one (שני כתובים הבאים כאחד)
↓
Two verses that come as one do not teach their common element to other cases
Principle שני כתובים הבאים כאחד אין מלמדין — When two cases share a unique halakha found nowhere else, they are considered exceptions that cannot serve as models.

Rabbis vs. R' Dosa: Priestly Vestments After Yom Kippur

Rabbis

  • White garments of Yom Kippur require interment (גניזה)
  • "He shall leave them there" (Lev. 16:23) — not fit for further use
  • Me'ilah applies after mitzva → pairs with ashes as two verses as one

R' Dosa

  • Vestments are fit for an ordinary priest to wear
  • Only a High Priest may not reuse them on a different Yom Kippur
  • No me'ilah after mitzva → need different second case: the eglah arufah (heifer whose neck is broken)
Resolution According to R' Dosa, the two cases are ashes and the eglah arufah — both prohibit benefit after their mitzva, forming two verses as one.

Three Mentions of Karet — What Each Includes

Verse PhraseHermeneutical RoleWhat It Includes
"That approaches the sacred items"Generalization (כלל)Broad category of sacred items
"Of the flesh of the sacrifice of peace offerings"Detail (פרט)All items consecrated to the altar (via כלל ופרט)
"When anyone shall touch any impure item"Additional inclusionItems not typically eaten: wood, frankincense (Rabbis) or inner sin offerings (R' Shimon)
Dispute R' Shimon uses the third mention to include inner sin offerings (whose blood enters the Sanctuary), since he exempts inedible items from impurity liability.

Wood & Frankincense: Impurity of Meat vs. Impurity of Body

Opinion A
The dispute between Rabbis and R' Shimon is only about impurity of the meat (טומאת בשר) — whether impure wood/frankincense carries a flogging penalty. But if the person is impure, all agree he is exempt.
Opinion B
They dispute both cases — impurity of the meat and impurity of the body. Since "the flesh that touches any impure item" includes wood/frankincense, so does the next verse about impurity of the body.
Rava
The correct reading is that R' Shimon exempts in both cases. Since one does not read karet for impure persons eating wood/frankincense, one likewise does not read the impurity-of-meat verse for them.
Note Two pairs of Amoraim debated this: R' Yochanan & Reish Lakish, and R' Elazar & R' Yosei b"R Chanina. Rav Tavyumei and Rav Kahana transmit different versions of who said what.
