The Core Rule of Zevachim: Shelo Lishmah

An offering is slaughtered
shelo lishmah (not for its own sake)
↓
Most Offerings
Fit (kasher) — but does not satisfy owner's obligation
Pesach (at its time) & Chatat
Disqualified (pasul) — completely unfit
Key Principle All slaughtered offerings that were slaughtered not for their own sake are fit — except the Paschal offering and the sin offering.

The Mishna's Rule: Shelo Lishmah

Offering TypeSlaughtered Shelo LishmahOwner's Obligation
Most Offerings (e.g. Olah, Shelamim)✅ Fit — may continue rites❌ Not satisfied — must bring another
Korban Pesach (at its appointed time)❌ Disqualified (pasul)❌ Not satisfied
Korban Pesach (not at its time)✅ Fit❌ Not satisfied
Chatat (sin offering) — any time❌ Disqualified (pasul)❌ Not satisfied
Mishna The Paschal offering is disqualified only at its appointed time (14th of Nisan after noon), while the sin offering is disqualified at all times.

Tannaitic Dispute: Which Offerings Are Disqualified?

Tanna Kamma
Only Pesach and Chatat are disqualified when slaughtered shelo lishmah. All other offerings remain fit.
R' Eliezer
The Asham (guilt offering) is also disqualified! The Chatat is brought for a transgression and the Asham is brought for a transgression — the same rule should apply.
Yosei ben Ḥoni
Even other offerings slaughtered for the sake of a Pesach or Chatat are disqualified — not just Pesach and Chatat themselves.
Shimon brother of Azarya
It depends on the hierarchy of sanctity: slaughtering for a higher-level offering is fine; for a lower-level offering, it's disqualified.
R' Eliezer's Reasoning A kal va-chomer from shared characteristics: since both Chatat and Asham atone for transgressions, the same disqualification applies.

Shimon Brother of Azarya: Hierarchy of Sanctity

An offering is slaughtered for the sake of a different offering
↓
Compare the sanctity level of the original offering vs. the intended offering
↓
For Higher Sanctity
Fit
e.g. Shelamim → for sake of Olah
For Lower Sanctity
Disqualified
e.g. Olah → for sake of Shelamim
Examples Kodshei kodashim (e.g. Chatat, Olah) slaughtered for kodashim kalim (e.g. Shelamim) = unfit. Kodashim kalim slaughtered for kodshei kodashim = fit. Bechor/Ma'aser for Shelamim = fit; Shelamim for Bechor/Ma'aser = unfit.

Rava's Rule: No Further Deviations

אמר רבא: עולה ששחטה שלא לשמה — אסור לזרוק דמה שלא לשמה
Ella Shelo The Gemara explains the Mishna's language "ella shelo" (but they did not) teaches that even after improper slaughter, the remaining rites must still be performed with proper intent. A burnt offering slaughtered shelo lishmah — it is still prohibited to sprinkle its blood shelo lishmah.

Logical argument: Just because one deviated once in slaughter, should one continue to deviate in all remaining rites?
Verse: "That which has gone out of your lips you shall observe and do" (Devarim 23:24) — even a nedavah (voluntary offering) must follow proper protocol.

Rava's Contradiction: Offerings vs. Gittin

Korbanot (Offerings)

  • Slaughtered shelo lishmah → fit but doesn't satisfy obligation
  • Slaughtered stam (without specification) → satisfies obligation
  • Reason: offerings stand designated for their own sake (עומדים לשמן)
  • Default presumption = proper intent

Gittin (Divorce Documents)

  • Written not for the woman → not valid
  • Written stam (without specification) → also not valid
  • Reason: a wife does not stand designated for divorce
  • No default presumption of intent
Rava's Resolution Ordinary slaughtered offerings stand designated for their own sake (עומדים לשמן) from the moment of consecration. Therefore unspecified intent = proper intent. But an ordinary wife does not stand designated for divorce, so a get requires explicit specification.
