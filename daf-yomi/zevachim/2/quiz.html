QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Mishna, what happens to most offerings slaughtered shelo lishmah (not for their sake)?
A. They are completely disqualified and cannot be offered
B. They are fit but do not satisfy the owner's obligation
C. They are fit and fully satisfy the owner's obligation
D. They must be burned entirely and a new offering brought
Correct: B — The Mishna's core rule is that most offerings slaughtered shelo lishmah remain fit (kasher), and one may continue their sacrificial rites, but the owner must bring another offering to fulfill their obligation.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Rava say that unspecified intent (stam) counts as lishmah for offerings but NOT for a get (bill of divorce)?
A. Because offerings are more important than divorce documents
B. Because offerings stand designated for their own sake from consecration, but a wife does not stand designated for divorce
C. Because the Torah explicitly states that offerings have default intent
D. Because a Rabbinic decree distinguishes between the two cases
Correct: B — Rava resolves the contradiction by explaining that from the moment of consecration, an offering is presumed to be destined for its designated purpose (עומדים לשמן). A wife, however, has no such presumption of being designated for divorce, so explicit intent is required.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Shimon brother of Azarya, what determines whether an offering slaughtered for the sake of a different offering is fit or unfit?
A. Whether the slaughterer is a Kohen or a non-Kohen
B. Whether the offering was slaughtered in the correct location
C. Whether it was slaughtered for an offering of higher or lower sanctity
D. Whether the owner was present during the slaughter
Correct: C — Shimon brother of Azarya establishes a hierarchy: slaughtering for a higher-sanctity offering = fit; slaughtering for a lower-sanctity offering = unfit. For example, Shelamim (lesser sanctity) slaughtered for Olah (higher sanctity) is fit, but the reverse is not.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What does the Gemara derive from the Mishna's use of 'ella shelo' (but they did not) instead of simply 'velo' (and they did not)?
A. That the offerings must be completely destroyed
B. That the owner receives partial credit for the offering
C. That the only deficiency is the owner's obligation — the remaining rites must still be performed with proper intent
D. That a second slaughter is required to rectify the first
Correct: C — The word 'ella' (but) teaches that these offerings retain their sanctity and it is prohibited to deviate from proper protocol in the remaining sacrificial rites. This supports Rava's ruling that a burnt offering slaughtered shelo lishmah still may not have its blood sprinkled shelo lishmah.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does R' Eliezer argue that the guilt offering (Asham) should also be disqualified when slaughtered shelo lishmah?
A. Because the Torah lists it alongside the Chatat
B. Because both Chatat and Asham are brought for transgressions, so the same rule should apply
C. Because the Asham has the same monetary value as the Chatat
D. Because the Asham is slaughtered in the same location as the Chatat
Correct: B — R' Eliezer draws an analogy: the Chatat is brought for a transgression and the Asham is brought for a transgression. Just as the Chatat is disqualified shelo lishmah, so too should the Asham be disqualified shelo lishmah.
