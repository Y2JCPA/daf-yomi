Can an Acute Mourner Bring the Paschal Offering?

Gemara
Rabbi Shimon seems to contradict himself: in one baraita he says an onen may NOT send a Paschal offering, but a mishna in Pesaḥim says he immerses and eats it!
Rav Mari
Death on the 14th = Torah-level aninut at night → overrides Pesach. Death on the 13th, burial on 14th = rabbinic → Pesach overrides it.
Rav Ashi
But Rabbi Shimon himself cites the mishna as proof! Rabbi Yehuda could just dismiss it as a different case. Difficulty stands.
Abaye
Death before midday = never fit for Pesach → aninut applies. Death after midday = already obligated → aninut doesn't block Pesach.
Rava
Both after midday! Death before slaughter → may not send. Death after slaughter → since eating is me'akev, Sages suspend rabbinic aninut.
Core Issue Multiple Amoraim propose different ways to resolve Rabbi Shimon's apparent contradiction about whether an acute mourner may participate in the Paschal offering.

Rav Mari's Resolution: Day of Death vs. Day of Burial

Died on 14th Nisan

  • Aninut on day of death = Torah law
  • Nighttime aninut is also Torah law
  • Paschal offering does NOT override
  • Onen may NOT send the offering

Died on 13th, Buried on 14th

  • 14th is only day of burial
  • Aninut is rabbinic only
  • Nighttime aninut is rabbinic
  • Paschal offering OVERRIDES it
Refuted Rav Ashi challenges: if so, Rabbi Yehuda could dismiss Rabbi Shimon's proof from the mishna, since the mishna discusses burial day (rabbinic) while the dispute concerns death day (Torah). This difficulty stands against Rav Mari.

Abaye's Resolution: Before vs. After Midday

Relative dies on 14th of Nisan
↓
Before Midday
Never became obligated in Pesach → aninut applies → may NOT send offering
After Midday
Already obligated in Pesach → aninut does not apply to this mitzva → immerses and eats
Proof Abaye cites the incident of Yosef the priest whose wife died on Erev Pesach — his fellow priests forcibly rendered him impure. This supports a distinction based on midday timing.

Challenge to Abaye: The Yosef/Nazirite Contradiction

Baraita 1
Yosef the priest's wife died on Erev Pesach. He didn't want to become tamei. His fellow priests forced him to become impure — burying relatives is obligatory.
Baraita 2
A nazirite going to slaughter his Pesach or circumcise his son who hears a relative died — he shall NOT become impure. The Pesach/milah override.
Abaye
Baraita 1 (priest) = death before midday. Baraita 2 (nazirite) = death after midday.
Gemara
Perhaps both are after midday! One follows R' Yishmael, the other R' Akiva — a tannaitic dispute, not a timing difference.
Result The Gemara undermines Abaye's proof by suggesting the contradiction can be resolved through a tannaitic dispute rather than a midday distinction.

Rava's Resolution: Before vs. After Slaughter

🕐 Relative dies after midday on 14th Nisan
↓
🔪 Has the Pesach already been slaughtered and its blood sprinkled?
↓
❌ If BEFORE slaughter — onen may NOT send the offering
↓
✅ If AFTER slaughter — since nighttime aninut is only rabbinic...
↓
🍖 And eating the Pesach is INDISPENSABLE (me'akev)...
↓
📜 The Sages suspend rabbinic aninut to allow him to eat the Pesach
Key Principle Ravina explains: partaking of the Paschal offering is me'akev (essential to the mitzva), so the Sages did not apply their decree of aninut to prevent consumption — unlike other sacrificial meat.
