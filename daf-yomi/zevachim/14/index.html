External vs. Inner Sin Offerings: Finger-Dipping

External Sin Offerings

  • Verse says: 'the priest shall take of the blood with his finger' (Lev 4:34)
  • Dipping finger is NOT an essential rite (avodah)
  • Piggul intent during dipping has no effect
  • If a monkey places blood on priest's hand — must take again from vessel

Inner Sin Offerings

  • Verse says: 'the priest shall dip his finger in the blood' (Lev 4:6)
  • Dipping IS an essential rite for these offerings
  • Piggul intent during dipping CAN disqualify
  • Blood must be sprinkled inside the Sanctuary
LANGUAGE MATTERS 'Take' (וְלָקַח) vs. 'Dip' (וְטָבַל) — the Torah's word choice signals whether finger-dipping is an essential sacrificial rite.

R' Shimon's Concession on Inner Sin Offerings

Reish Lakish
R' Shimon concedes that conveying blood (holakhah) of inner sin offerings with prohibited intent disqualifies them — because conveying is indispensable for those offerings.
Gemara
But R' Shimon says piggul never applies to inner sin offerings — their blood isn't sprinkled on the outer altar like a shelamim!
R' Yosei b"R Ḥanina
R' Shimon concedes it disqualifies (pasul) even if it doesn't create full piggul — via kal vachomer: if shelo lishmah (valid for shelamim) disqualifies a chatat, then chutz lizmano (which disqualifies shelamim) certainly disqualifies a chatat!
Gemara
What about intent to eat outside its designated area (chutz limkomo)?
Gemara
Derive it from the juxtaposition in Leviticus 7:18 — 'on the third day' (chutz lizmano) and 'it shall be piggul' (chutz limkomo) are linked in one verse.
KAL VACHOMER If shelo lishmah — which doesn't even disqualify a shelamim — disqualifies a chatat, then surely chutz lizmano, which does disqualify a shelamim, should disqualify a chatat.

Non-Priest Conveying Blood: Is It Valid?

A non-priest (zar) conveys blood to the altar
↓
Is conveying (holakhah) classified as a full avodah?
↓
Rabbanan: Yes
Dispensable rite = still a rite
→ Non-priest disqualifies
R' Shimon: No
Dispensable rite ≠ true rite
→ Non-priest is valid
↓
Ulla citing R' Elazar: Even R' Shimon agrees non-priest conveying disqualifies
REFUTATION Rav Ḥisda's proof from the verse (non-priests passed blood to priests) is rejected: the non-priests merely served as an 'itztaba' (bench) — holding the blood, not conveying it.

The Red Heifer Challenge

Abaye
You say slaughter isn't an avodah, so a non-priest can do it. But Rav says slaughter of the red heifer by a non-priest is pasul — derived from 'Elazar the priest' and 'statute'!
Rabba / Rav Yosef
The red heifer is different — it's not a korban. It has the sanctity of bedek habayit (Temple maintenance), not altar offerings.
Abaye
That makes the argument stronger! If slaughter of a mere maintenance-sanctity animal requires a priest, surely altar offerings should too!
Rav Sheisha b. Rav Idi
No — the red heifer's priestly requirement is like diagnosing nega'im (leprous marks). A priest is needed, but it's not because it's a sacrificial rite.
ANALOGY Just as a priest must pronounce 'tamei' or 'tahor' on leprous marks — a priestly function, not a sacrificial rite — so too the red heifer requires a priest without classifying its slaughter as avodah.
