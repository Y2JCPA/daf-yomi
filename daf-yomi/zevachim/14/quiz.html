QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the Gemara distinguish between 'dipping' (וְטָבַל) and 'taking' (וְלָקַח) the blood with one's finger?
A. To show that 'dipping' makes finger-contact an essential rite for inner sin offerings, while 'taking' does not for external ones
B. To prove that both external and internal sin offerings require dipping
C. To demonstrate that the priest must use his right hand
D. To distinguish between different types of altars
Correct: A — The Torah uses 'dip' (טבל) for inner sin offerings, making it an essential rite where piggul intent matters. For external sin offerings, the word 'take' (לקח) indicates dipping is not essential — it merely requires the priest to actively take the blood.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to R' Yosei son of R' Ḥanina, why does R' Shimon agree that intent beyond the designated time disqualifies an inner sin offering?
A. Because the Torah explicitly states piggul applies to all offerings
B. Via kal vachomer: if shelo lishmah (valid for shelamim) disqualifies a chatat, then chutz lizmano (which disqualifies shelamim) certainly disqualifies a chatat
C. Because Reish Lakish rules that all inner offerings follow stricter rules
D. Because the blood must be sprinkled inside the Sanctuary
Correct: B — The kal vachomer reasons: shelo lishmah doesn't even disqualify a shelamim, yet it disqualifies a chatat. So chutz lizmano, which DOES disqualify a shelamim, must certainly disqualify a chatat — even if it doesn't create full piggul status per R' Shimon.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does the Gemara explain the verse where non-priests passed blood to priests, refuting the idea that non-priest conveying is valid?
A. The verse is discussing a temporary emergency ruling
B. The non-priests served as an 'itztaba' (bench) — holding blood, not conveying it
C. The verse refers to Levites, not ordinary non-priests
D. The verse describes events before the Temple was built
Correct: B — The Gemara reinterprets the verse: the non-priests merely held the blood like a bench (itztaba) while priests took it back and sprinkled it. They were not actually performing the rite of conveying (holakhah) to the altar.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why doesn't the red heifer's requirement for priestly slaughter prove that slaughter is a sacrificial rite (avodah)?
A. Because the red heifer is not an offering — its priestly requirement is like diagnosing leprous marks, a priestly function but not a sacrificial rite
B. Because the red heifer was only slaughtered once in history
C. Because the requirement only applies to the High Priest
D. Because slaughter of the red heifer occurs outside the Temple
Correct: A — Rav Sheisha b. Rav Idi explains: just as a priest must diagnose nega'im (not a sacrificial rite but a priestly function), the red heifer requires a priest without classifying its slaughter as avodah. It has bedek habayit sanctity, not altar sanctity.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the unresolved difficulty regarding conveying limbs vs. conveying blood?
A. Whether conveying limbs can be done by a non-priest
B. Whether conveying blood requires walking or can be done by passing hand-to-hand
C. If dispensable limb-conveying explicitly requires a priest, then indispensable blood-conveying should certainly require one — yet the Torah never states this explicitly
D. Whether the limbs must be carried on the priest's right side
Correct: C — The kal vachomer argues: conveying limbs is dispensable yet requires a priest (Lev 1:13). Blood-conveying is MORE essential (prevents atonement without it), so it should certainly require a priest. The Gemara's answer — 'where the Torah revealed, it revealed' — doesn't fully resolve this logical argument.
