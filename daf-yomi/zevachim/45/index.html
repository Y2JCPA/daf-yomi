Gentile Offerings: Status & Liability

Rava
Why issue a practical ruling for laws that apply only in the messianic era?
Abaye
By that logic, don't teach all of Zevachim! We learn for the principle: דרוש וקבל שכר — study Torah and receive reward.
R' Shimon
Gentile offerings are exempt from piggul, notar, impurity, and slaughtering outside the courtyard.
R' Yosei
I see logic in being stringent — the verse says 'any man… or of the strangers' (Lev. 22:18), equating gentile and Jewish offerings.
Key Tension Do gentile offerings carry the full sanctity and prohibitions of Jewish offerings, or are they treated with reduced status?

Study Torah & Receive Reward

Rava
Why do we need a practical halakhic ruling for laws that won't apply until the Temple is rebuilt?
Abaye
Should we then not study all of Masechet Zevachim? It's entirely about Temple service!
Abaye
דרוש וקבל שכר — One is rewarded for Torah study regardless of practical applicability.
Rava
I agree we study — my question is specifically about issuing a formal ruling (פסק הלכה) for the messianic era.
Principle Torah study has intrinsic value beyond immediate practical application. The reward exists even for laws not currently in effect.

Gentile Offerings: R' Shimon vs R' Yosei

R' Shimon (Lenient)

  • No piggul liability
  • No notar liability
  • No impurity liability
  • Exempt for slaughter outside courtyard
  • Cannot make substitution (temurah)
  • Cannot bring independent libations
  • No me'ilah liability after the fact

R' Yosei (Stringent)

  • Sees logic to apply ALL stringencies
  • Cites Lev. 22:18 — 'any man… or strangers'
  • Equates gentile offerings with Jewish offerings
  • Liable for slaughter outside courtyard
  • Full sanctity applies to gentile korbanot
  • Agrees: items for Temple maintenance → full me'ilah
  • Frontplate (tzitz) does NOT atone for gentiles
Source Leviticus 22:18 — 'Any man of the house of Israel, or of the strangers in Israel that will sacrifice his offering… to the Lord' — R' Yosei's proof that gentile offerings carry full sanctity.

The Tzitz and Gentile Offerings

Rav Pappa
R' Yosei can agree that if impure blood was sprinkled for a gentile's offering, it is not accepted — because the tzitz (frontplate) only atones 'for them' (Exodus 28:38), meaning Jews.
Rav Huna b. Rav Natan
But the impurity verse also says 'children of Israel' — yet R' Yosei still applies impurity liability to gentile offerings! How can you use the same logic selectively?
Rav Ashi
It's not about the words 'for them' — rather, gentiles are simply not subject to acceptance (רצוי) of offerings at all. The tzitz mechanism doesn't extend to them.
Resolution Rav Ashi distinguishes: the tzitz's atoning power is structurally limited to those within the system of 'acceptance' — gentiles stand outside this framework entirely.
