QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does Abaye argue that halakhot of the messianic era should still be taught?
A. Because we need to be ready when Mashiach comes
B. Because of the principle 'study Torah and receive reward'
C. Because the Sanhedrin requires this knowledge
D. Because these laws have allegorical meaning
Correct: B — Abaye invokes the principle of 'drosh v'kabel s'char' — one is rewarded for Torah study regardless of its practical applicability. He points out that by Rava's logic, all of Zevachim would be unnecessary.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does the Gemara derive that gentile offerings are exempt from me'ilah?
A. From a direct verse excluding gentiles from me'ilah
B. From a gezeirah shavah linking 'sin' in me'ilah to 'sin' in terumah, which specifies 'children of Israel'
C. From a kal va-chomer argument based on piggul
D. From an oral tradition received at Sinai
Correct: B — The exemption derives from a verbal analogy (gezeirah shavah) between 'sin' (חטא) in the me'ilah verse (Lev. 5:15) and 'sin' in the terumah verse (Lev. 22:9). Since terumah specifies 'sacred items of the children of Israel' (Lev. 22:15), excluding gentiles, this exclusion carries over to me'ilah.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Ashi, why doesn't the tzitz (High Priest's frontplate) atone for gentile offerings?
A. Because the verse says 'for them' meaning only priests
B. Because gentiles are not subject to the framework of 'acceptance' (ritzui) of offerings
C. Because gentile offerings cannot become impure
D. Because the tzitz only works inside the Temple
Correct: B — Rav Ashi explains that it's not simply about the word 'for them' — rather, gentiles fundamentally stand outside the system of 'acceptance' (ritzui) that the tzitz operates within. The mechanism of atonement through the frontplate doesn't extend to them structurally.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the key distinction between items consecrated by gentiles for the altar versus for Temple maintenance?
A. Altar items require libations but maintenance items do not
B. Altar items are exempt from me'ilah after the fact, but Temple maintenance items carry full me'ilah liability
C. Altar items can be eaten but maintenance items cannot
D. There is no distinction — both are treated identically
Correct: B — The baraita explicitly distinguishes: for items consecrated for the altar, one who derives benefit is not liable for me'ilah after the fact. But for items consecrated for Temple maintenance (bedek ha-bayit), full me'ilah liability applies even to gentile consecrations.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does the Gemara say that the kal va-chomer argument should have limited impurity liability to items with permitting factors — and what overrides it?
A. Because impurity is more stringent than piggul; a Rabbinic decree overrides it
B. Because impurity is more lenient than piggul (sliding-scale offering, two awareness changes, communal exceptions); the verse 'that they separate from sacred items' overrides the logic
C. Because impurity and piggul are equal in severity; a tradition from Moses overrides it
D. Because impurity only applies to blood; the verse about the tzitz overrides it
Correct: B — Piggul is stricter (fixed offering, one awareness, no exceptions) yet only covers items with permitting factors. Impurity, being more lenient, should logically be at least as limited. But Leviticus 22:2 — 'that they separate from the sacred items… which they consecrate to Me' — expands impurity liability to ALL consecrated items, overriding the kal va-chomer.
