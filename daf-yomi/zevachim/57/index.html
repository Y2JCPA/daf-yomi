Zevachim 57 — Altar Blood, Eating Durations & Midnight Limits

DAF OVERVIEW This daf explores where blood is placed on the altar (at the base), then presents a vivid debate in the academy at Yavne over how long a firstborn offering may be eaten — R' Tarfon argues for two days and one night (like peace offerings), while R' Yosei HaGelili argues for one day and one night (like sin/guilt offerings). R' Akiva resolves via a verse. The daf then discusses hekkesh (juxtaposition) principles for flour measures, and concludes with the dispute over the Paschal offering's midnight deadline.

The Vineyard at Yavne: How Long Is a Firstborn Eaten?

R' Tarfon
A firstborn offering is eaten for two days and one night. Peace offerings are lesser sanctity, and so is a firstborn — so we learn from peace offerings.
R' Yosei HaGelili
My teacher, from where do you derive this? A firstborn is a gift to the priest (מתנת כהונה), just like sin and guilt offerings — which are eaten for one day and one night!
R' Tarfon
We judge from the most similar matter: peace offerings don't come as atonement, and neither does a firstborn. So: two days and one night.
R' Yosei HaGelili
I can match you: sin/guilt offerings don't come as a vow or gift, and neither does a firstborn. So: one day and one night.
R' Akiva
The verse says explicitly: "their flesh shall be yours, as the breast of waving and the right thigh" (Numbers 18:18) — juxtaposing firstborn with peace offerings. Two days and one night.
RESOLUTION R' Akiva settles the debate with a direct scriptural juxtaposition (hekkesh) — the firstborn is compared to the breast and thigh of peace offerings.

R' Yosei HaGelili's Two Competing Analogies

Like Peace Offerings (R' Tarfon)

  • Both are offerings of lesser sanctity (קדשים קלים)
  • Neither comes as atonement for sin
  • Eaten for two days and one night

Like Sin/Guilt Offerings (R' Yosei)

  • All are gifts to the priest (מתנת כהונה)
  • None come as a vow or gift offering
  • Eaten for one day and one night
METHODOLOGICAL NOTE Both sides use the same method — learn from the most similar — but select different criteria for similarity. The verse in Numbers 18:18 breaks the tie.

HEKKESH DEBATE If a matter derived via juxtaposition + another principle is itself a juxtaposition, it cannot teach further via juxtaposition. The Gemara resolves: 'תביאו' is a superfluous word, not a hekkesh — so the chain of derivation holds.

Paschal Offering: Until Midnight or Until Dawn?

R' Elazar b. Azarya
The verse says "on that night" (Exodus 12:8). The same phrase appears regarding the plague of the firstborn, which occurred at midnight. So the Paschal offering may be eaten only until midnight.
R' Akiva
The verse says "you will eat it in haste" (Exodus 12:11) — the time of haste is dawn, when Israel left Egypt. The Paschal offering may be eaten until dawn.
Gemara
Then why does the verse say "on that night"? To teach that unlike other offerings, the Paschal offering is eaten at night, not during the day.
Rav Yosef
Our mishna — which says the Paschal offering is eaten only until midnight — follows R' Elazar ben Azarya.
Abaye
How do you know the mishna is R' Elazar ben Azarya? Perhaps it's R' Akiva, and midnight is only a rabbinic safeguard!
HALACHIC IMPACT This dispute echoes in the Haggadah: the obligation to eat the afikoman (representing the Paschal offering) by midnight reflects R' Elazar ben Azarya's position.
