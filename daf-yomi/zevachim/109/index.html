Offering Outside the Courtyard: Sources & Scope of Liability

Leviticus 17:8–9
"That offers up a burnt offering or sacrifice…"
↓
"Burnt Offering"
Liability for offering a burnt offering outside
"Sacrifice"
Includes sacrificial portions of all offerings (guilt, sin, etc.)
"To the Tent of Meeting"
Anything fit to be brought to the altar — including unfit offerings disqualified in sanctity
Core Principle The Torah expands liability step by step: from burnt offerings, to all sacrificial portions, to anything the altar would accept — even offerings disqualified during the Temple service.

Expanding Liability: Three Derivations from One Verse

📖 "Burnt offering" — Liability for offering a complete olah outside
↓
📖 "Or sacrifice" — Includes sacrificial portions (eimurim) of guilt, sin, and all other offerings
↓
📖 "To the Tent of Meeting" — Includes handful of meal offering, frankincense, incense, priestly meal offerings, and libations
↓
📖 "To sacrifice it to the Lord" — Includes unfit offerings whose disqualification occurred in sanctity (nitan, yotzei, tamei, pigul, etc.)
↓
🔑 Any item the altar would accept if placed upon it creates liability if offered outside
Method Each phrase in the verse progressively widens the scope of liability, from the most obvious case (burnt offering) to the least intuitive (disqualified offerings).

Unfit Offerings Disqualified in Sanctity

DisqualificationDescription
Linah (left overnight)Sacrificial meat not offered before dawn
Yotzei (went outside)Offering left the Temple courtyard
Tamei (impure)Offering became ritually impure
Pigul (wrong intent — time)Slaughtered with intent to eat beyond designated time
Chutz limkomo (wrong intent — place)Intent to eat outside designated area
Blood misplacedBlood placed above the line instead of below, or vice versa
Pesach/Chatat shelo lishmoPaschal or sin offering whose blood was applied not for its sake
Key Rule All these share one trait: if placed on the altar, the altar renders them accepted (lo yerdu). Therefore offering them outside still creates liability.

Does Flesh of a Peace Offering Combine with Its Eimurim?

Mishna
An olive-bulk of burnt offering flesh + its eimurim offered outside → liable
Gemara Inference
By implication: olive-bulk of peace offering meat + its eimurim → not liable
Reason
Burnt offering: entirely consumed on the altar — flesh and fat have the same destination, so they combine
Reason
Peace offering: meat is eaten, fat is burned — different destinations, so they don't combine
Difficulty This logic works for offering outside, but the baraita also says they don't combine for pigul and notar — and another mishna (Me'ila 15a) says all pigul items combine!

Combining Half Olive-Bulk of Meat + Half Olive-Bulk of Fat

Burnt Offering (Olah)

  • ½ olive-bulk flesh + ½ olive-bulk fat = combine
  • One sprinkles the blood
  • Reason: entire offering goes on the altar
  • All parts share the same destination

Eaten Offerings (Shelamim etc.)

  • ½ olive-bulk meat + ½ olive-bulk fat = do NOT combine
  • One does not sprinkle the blood
  • Reason: meat is eaten, fat is burned
  • Different destinations prevent combining
R' Yehoshua This ruling follows Rabbi Yehoshua's opinion: for eaten offerings, if only half an olive-bulk of meat remains, even with fat remaining, one may not sprinkle the blood.

Rabbi Eliezer vs. the Rabbis: Partial Offerings Outside

Rabbis
One who burns even an olive-bulk of incense, frankincense, or the handful outside is liable
R' Eliezer
One is exempt unless he sacrifices the entire amount of any of these items outside
Rabbis
An olive-bulk constitutes a significant act of burning (haktarah)
R' Eliezer
A partial offering is not a complete act of haktarah
R' Eliezer concedes
If one offered part inside and left over an olive-bulk, then offered that olive-bulk outside → liable
Agreement R' Eliezer agrees: if the service began inside and leftover was taken outside, even a partial amount creates liability, because the haktarah was already initiated inside.
