Camps, Refuge & Private Altars: From Wilderness to Gilgal

Abaye
If the Levite camp didn't exist in Shiloh, zavim and temei meitim would share one camp — contradicting the Torah's plural 'camps' (Numbers 5:3)
Rava
And if the Israelite camp didn't exist, zavim and metzora'im would share one place — contradicting 'he shall dwell alone' (Leviticus 13:46)
Gemara
Resolution: All three camps existed in Shiloh. The teaching that 'only two camps' refers to the Levite camp not providing refuge to unintentional killers
R' Meir
In Gilgal, vow and gift offerings — including meal offerings and nazirite offerings — could be sacrificed on private altars
Rabbis
Only burnt offerings and peace offerings on private altars — meal offerings are never on a private altar, and nazirite offerings are compulsory
Key Verse "Every man whatsoever is fitting in his own eyes" (Deut. 12:8) — The word hayashar (fitting) is the basis for limiting private altars to voluntary offerings

Were All Three Camps Present in Shiloh?

Abaye
The Levite camp must have existed in Shiloh — otherwise zavim and temei meitim share one camp, but the Torah says 'camps' (plural), requiring distinct zones for each!
Rava
And the Israelite camp must have existed too — otherwise zavim and metzora'im share one place, but the Torah says the leper 'shall dwell alone'!
Gemara
Conclusion: All three camps existed in Shiloh. The Mishnah's 'two camps' means the Levite camp did not serve as a city of refuge there
Proof Text Numbers 5:3 — "Outside the camp you shall put them; that they will not defile their camps" — the plural demands separate camps for different categories of impurity

Three Categories of Impurity & the Three Camps

Metzora (Leper)

  • Sent outside ALL three camps
  • "He shall dwell alone" (Lev. 13:46)
  • May not dwell even with other impure people
  • Most restrictive status

Zav / Zavah

  • Sent out of Camp of the Divine Presence
  • Sent out of the Levite Camp
  • Permitted in the Israelite Camp
  • Intermediate restriction

Tamei Meit (Corpse Impurity)

  • Sent out of Camp of the Divine Presence only
  • Permitted in the Levite Camp
  • Permitted in the Israelite Camp
  • Least restrictive status
Logic Without all three camps, these distinctions collapse — proving all three camps must have existed in Shiloh

The Levite Camp as City of Refuge in the Wilderness

An unintentional killer in the wilderness — where does he flee?
↓
Exodus 21:13 — "I will appoint for you a place where he may flee"
↓
"A place" = from your place — the Levite camp of Moshe provided refuge
↓
In the Wilderness
Levite camp provides refuge for accidental killers
In Shiloh
Levite camp does NOT provide refuge — this is the meaning of 'only two camps'
Halakhah A Levite who kills unintentionally is exiled from one Levite city to another. If exiled within his own city, that city provides him refuge (Numbers 35:28)

Are Nazirite Offerings Voluntary or Compulsory?

R' Meir
Nazirite offerings are vow offerings — no one is forced to become a nazirite! They qualify as yesharot (fitting/voluntary) and may be sacrificed on a private altar
Rabbis
Once one becomes a nazirite, the offerings are compulsory. The voluntary element is only in taking the vow — the obligation itself is binding
Shmuel
The dispute is only about the nazirite's burnt offering and peace offering. All agree the sin offering and guilt offering are compulsory and not on a private altar
Rabba
Objection! A baraita omits the nazirite's cooked foreleg from private altar rules — this only works if the Rabbis hold even the peace offering wasn't on a private altar!
Gemara
Correction of Shmuel: The dispute is actually about the burnt offering and peace offering. All agree the sin and guilt offerings are compulsory even per R' Meir
Rabba's Proof The baraita lists breast & thigh (peace offering) and terumah of loaves (thanks offering) as applying only to the great altar — but omits the nazirite's foreleg, proving his peace offering wasn't on a private altar per the Rabbis
