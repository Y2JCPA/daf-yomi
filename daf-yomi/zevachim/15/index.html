Can Improperly Conveyed Blood Be Corrected?

Ulla (R' Yochanan)
Conveying blood not by foot renders the offering permanently unfit — it cannot be corrected.
Rav Nachman
But the mishna (32a) says: if blood spilled from the vessel onto the floor and was recollected, it is fit! Spilling is also improper conveying!
Ulla (Defense)
The blood spilled outward, away from the altar — on an inclined plane, into a hole, or because it was thick. Not real conveying.
Rav Nachman
Did the Tanna go to all that trouble (ikhpal) just to teach such unlikely cases? And why doesn't the mishna distinguish inward vs. outward spilling?
Gemara
Teyuvta — conclusive refutation! Improperly conveyed blood can be corrected.
CONCLUSION The Gemara rejects Ulla's position: improper conveying does not permanently disqualify the blood.

Greater vs. Lesser Conveying — Intent Dispute

R' Shimon vs. Rabbis: Does improper intent (machshava) during conveying disqualify?
↓
Greater Conveying (by foot)
All agree: prohibited intent disqualifies
Lesser Conveying (by hand)
Dispute: Rabbis say disqualifies; R' Shimon says does not
↓
Scholars of Eretz Yisrael laughed at this distinction
CONTEXT Lesser conveying occurs when the animal is slaughtered right next to the altar, so the priest only moves blood by hand, not by walking.

The Bird Sin Offering Challenge

Scholars of Eretz Yisrael
A bird sin offering (chatat ha'of) is pinched on the altar and its blood sprinkled directly from the neck — all conveying is by hand!
Scholars of Eretz Yisrael
Per R' Shimon: intent before blood exits = nothing (just hand conveying). Intent after blood exits = mitzva already done. When can intent disqualify?
Gemara
Between blood leaving the bird and reaching the altar — that window allows disqualifying intent.
R' Yirmeya → R' Zeira
Proof: if the sprinkling priest's hand was severed mid-flight, the sprinkling is invalid — blood must reach the altar while conditions hold.
R' Zeira
We require: "and sprinkle" (Lev. 4:6) = "and place upon the corners" (Lev. 4:7). The blood must actually arrive at the altar.
PRINCIPLE Sprinkling is not complete until blood reaches the altar — intent or disqualification can occur during transit.

R' Eliezer vs. Rabbis — Necessary vs. Unnecessary Conveying

Rabbis (First Tanna)

  • Collected outside → brought inside = necessary conveying
  • Collected inside → taken outside = unnecessary
  • Brought in, taken out, brought back = necessary (must return to altar)
  • Prohibited intent during return disqualifies

R' Eliezer

  • Collected outside → brought inside = necessary conveying
  • Collected inside → taken outside = unnecessary
  • Brought in, taken out, brought back = unnecessary (already conveyed once)
  • Prohibited intent during return does not disqualify
KEY DISPUTE Does a second conveying to the altar count as 'necessary' conveying? Rabbis: yes, since blood must reach altar. R' Eliezer: no, since original conveying was completed.
