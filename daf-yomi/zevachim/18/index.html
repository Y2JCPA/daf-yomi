Deriving Disqualification of Improper Priestly Vestments

The Torah uses the word "statute" (חוקה) in three contexts
↓
Wine Drinking
Leviticus 10:9
Priestly Vestments
Exodus 28:43
Washing Hands/Feet
Exodus 30:21
↓
Gezeirah shavah links all three — violation of any disqualifies service
↓
Multiple verses needed to expand scope to all rites, not just death-penalty ones
KEY PRINCIPLE The word "statute" (חוקה) creates a verbal analogy uniting wine, vestments, and washing — a deficiency in any one disqualifies the Temple service.

Why Multiple Sources Are Needed

Gezeirah shavah of "חוקה" links wine-drinkers to vestment-lackers
↓
But wine passage discusses only rites carrying death penalty for non-priests
Without Exodus 29:9
Only death-penalty rites disqualified
With Exodus 29:9
All rites are disqualified
↓
The gezeirah shavah then circles back to expand wine-drinking disqualification to all rites too
LOGIC Each source alone is limited in scope. The verbal analogy cross-pollinates the expansions so all three disqualifications apply universally.

What About "Between Holy and Common"?

Gemara
If the disqualification of wine-drinkers comes from the gezeirah shavah, why do we need "that you may put difference between the holy and the common" (Lev. 10:10)?
Gemara
It is needed for the practice of Rav — who would not lecture publicly from one Festival day until the next after drinking wine.
Rav
The verse "that you may teach the children of Israel" (Lev. 10:11) teaches that one who drank wine may not issue halakhic rulings.
Gemara
So the verse applies beyond Temple service — it prohibits ruling while intoxicated.
PRACTICAL HALAKHA Leviticus 10:10–11 is not redundant — it extends the wine prohibition beyond Temple rites to include issuing halakhic rulings.

VerseTeachesWithout It, I'd Think…

Leviticus 1:7 — "sons of Aaron the priest"High Priest wearing ordinary priest's vestments is disqualifiedOnly atonement-essential rites are affected
Leviticus 1:8 — "sons of Aaron, the priests"Ordinary priest wearing High Priest's vestments is disqualifiedOnly lacking vestments disqualifies, not excess
PRINCIPLE Both lacking and excess vestments disqualify. A High Priest in 4 garments or an ordinary priest in 8 garments — both are invalid.

Dragging vs. Raised Vestments

Shmuel

  • Dragging on the ground → Fit
  • Raised above the ground → Unfit
  • The extra length does not invalidate

Rav

  • Dragging on the ground → Unfit
  • Raised above the ground → Unfit
  • Any deviation from proper fit invalidates

Rami bar Ḥama (Resolving the Baraita)

  • Baraita says raised is fit — refers to initially proper vestments raised by belt
  • Shmuel says raised is unfit — refers to vestments that never reached the feet
  • No contradiction between sources
RESOLUTION According to R' Zeira, Rav reads the baraita as one case: dragging vestments raised by a belt to proper height are fit. Any other deviation is unfit.

The Baraita: When Are Vestments Valid?

👘 Vestments dragging on the ground — service is valid
↓
📏 Vestments raised up (מסולקין) from the ground — service is valid
↓
🧵 Vestments frayed — service is valid
↓
👖 Wore two pairs of trousers — service is disqualified
↓
💧 Vestments soiled — service is disqualified
↓
✂️ Vestments ripped — service is disqualified
KEY DISTINCTION Fit issues (length) are tolerated; condition issues (soiled/ripped) and duplication (extra garments) are not.
