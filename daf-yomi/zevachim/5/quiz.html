Zevachim 5 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
What was Reish Lakish's fundamental challenge regarding offerings slaughtered shelo lishmah?
A. If they are fit and propitiate, what is the novelty of the halakha?
B. If they are fit but don't propitiate, why bring them at all — and if they propitiate, why call them shelo lishmah?
C. Why does the Torah need to state that they are disqualified?
D. How can one slaughter an offering for the wrong purpose accidentally?
Correct: B — Reish Lakish challenged that the status of shelo lishmah offerings seems contradictory: if fit, they should fulfill the obligation; if they don't fulfill it, why are they brought as offerings at all?
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why did Reish Lakish reject R' Elazar's first answer (offerings after death) for the case of a guilt offering?
A. Because guilt offerings are never brought on the altar
B. Because a guilt offering is not brought after the owner's death, unlike an olah
C. Because guilt offerings cannot be slaughtered shelo lishmah
D. Because the mishna explicitly states guilt offerings are disqualified
Correct: B — Reish Lakish accepted the precedent for an olah (which IS brought after death), but challenged that a guilt offering is NOT brought after death — so the precedent doesn't apply to it.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rava ultimately derive that a guilt offering slaughtered shelo lishmah is fit but does not propitiate?
A. From a kal va-chomer (a fortiori) from the burnt offering
B. From the common element of olah, shelamim, and todah
C. From the juxtaposition (hekesh) of guilt offering with peace offering in Leviticus 7:37
D. From the word 'it' in the verse about the sin offering
Correct: C — Rava uses the verse 'This is the law of the burnt offering… and of the guilt offering… and of the peace offerings' (Lev. 7:37). The juxtaposition teaches that just as shelamim shelo lishmah are fit but don't propitiate, so too the asham.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rav Huna citing Rav, when can a guilt offering whose owner died be slaughtered as a burnt offering?
A. Immediately upon the owner's death
B. Only after it develops a blemish and is sold
C. Only after the court consigns it to grazing
D. It can never be offered as a burnt offering
Correct: C — Rav Huna says that if the court consigned the asham to grazing (to develop a blemish for sale), and it was then slaughtered without specification, it is fit as an olah. But if NOT consigned, 'it is a guilt offering' — it retains its identity.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why was the Gemara's attempt to derive the asham rule through a 'common element' (tzad ha-shaveh) of olah, shelamim, and todah ultimately rejected?
A. Because all three have different numbers of required loaves
B. Because all three can be brought as vow or gift offerings, unlike a guilt offering which is obligatory only
C. Because a guilt offering is more severe than all three combined
D. Because the derivation would also include the sin offering, which contradicts the halakha
Correct: B — The common element was rejected because olah, shelamim, and todah share the trait that they can be brought voluntarily (as a vow or gift), whereas a guilt offering is brought only to fulfill an obligation — making them fundamentally different.
