If It Doesn't Propitiate, Why Bring It?

Reish Lakish
If offerings sacrificed shelo lishmah (not for their sake) are fit, they should propitiate (fulfill the owner's obligation). If they don't propitiate, why bring them at all?
R' Elazar
We have a precedent: offerings brought after the owner's death are fit but don't propitiate — because there is no owner needing atonement.
Reish Lakish
I concede for an olah (burnt offering). But a guilt offering is not brought after death — so where is the source that it's still brought shelo lishmah?
Reish Lakish
I will resolve my own difficulty: "That which has gone out of your lips" teaches that a shelo lishmah offering is brought as a gift offering, even without propitiation.
Key Principle An offering slaughtered not for its sake is fit (kasher) but does not satisfy its owner's obligation — it becomes like a voluntary gift to God.

R' Elazar's Precedent: Offerings After Owner's Death

Woman After Childbirth — Died After Sin Offering

  • She brought her sin offering, then died
  • Heirs MUST bring her burnt offering
  • The olah is fit — but no owner to propitiate
  • Proves: fit ≠ must propitiate

Woman After Childbirth — Died After Burnt Offering

  • She brought her burnt offering, then died
  • Heirs do NOT bring her sin offering
  • Sin offerings are not brought for the dead
  • Sin offering has stricter rules than olah
Mishna Kinnim 2:5 This mishna is the source for the principle that an olah can be brought even when it cannot fulfill its owner's obligation.

Extending the Rule to Guilt Offerings (Asham)

Reish Lakish
I accept the rule for an olah — it's brought after death. But an asham (guilt offering) is NOT brought after the owner's death. Why would it be brought shelo lishmah?
R' Elazar
R' Eliezer in the mishna says the asham IS disqualified shelo lishmah — so your difficulty is answered by his view!
Reish Lakish
"Is he the one people call a great man?" I'm asking about the first tanna's accepted view — and you answer me with R' Eliezer's minority opinion?!
Reish Lakish
I'll resolve it myself: "That which has gone out of your lips" — the offering is brought as a nedavah (gift), even without propitiation.
Sharp Exchange Reish Lakish's sharp rebuke of R' Elazar — "Is this the great man?" — reflects the Talmudic norm of rigorous intellectual honesty over politeness.

Abaye's Two-Verse Framework

VerseWhat It TeachesApplies To
"And slaughter it for a sin offering" (Lev. 4:33)Only a sin offering is disqualified shelo lishmah — all others are fitAll offerings including asham
"That which has gone out of your lips" (Deut. 23:24)Though fit, such offerings do not propitiate — they are treated as gift offeringsVow and gift offerings
Kal va-chomer (a fortiori) from olahIf an olah (not for sin) doesn't propitiate shelo lishmah, certainly an asham (for sin) doesn'tGuilt offerings
Abaye's Insight The verse "it" excludes the sin offering from fitness shelo lishmah; the verse about lips excludes propitiation — two separate derivations for two separate rules.

The Gemara's A Fortiori Chain (and Challenges)

🔥 Olah (burnt offering) — doesn't propitiate shelo lishmah, though it's a voluntary offering
↓
⚖️ Kal va-chomer: If olah doesn't propitiate, surely asham (which atones for sin) doesn't!
↓
❌ Challenge: Olah is unique — it's entirely consumed on the altar!
↓
🕊️ Shelamim (peace offering) proves it — not entirely consumed, yet doesn't propitiate shelo lishmah
↓
❌ Challenge: Shelamim is unique — it can be a voluntary offering!
↓
🍞 Todah (thanks offering) proves it — brought for obligation, not propitiation shelo lishmah
↓
🔄 Common element: All are offerings; all fit shelo lishmah; none propitiate — so too asham
Rejected! The common-element argument is rejected because olah, shelamim, and todah can all be brought as vow/gift offerings, while an asham is obligatory only.
