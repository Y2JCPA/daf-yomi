Eating Sacrificial Meat Without the Tabernacle

R' Yishmael
Meat of a firstborn offering (lesser sanctity) cannot be consumed if the altar is damaged or absent — derived from the halakha of the blood of the firstborn.
Sages
We disagree. The meat may still be consumed even without a functioning altar.
Gemara
Alternatively, both baraitot refer to offerings of the most sacred order. The second baraita's 'two locations' means: arriving at a new camp before the Tabernacle is erected, or leaving after it is dismantled but before the altar is removed.
Resolution As long as the altar remains in place, the sacrificial food is permitted — the verse 'The Tent of Meeting shall travel' teaches that sanctity persists even during travel.

🏕️ Israelites arrive at new camp
↓
⛺ Levites have NOT yet erected the Tabernacle
↓

↓
📦 Levites begin dismantling the Tabernacle
↓
🧱 Partitions are removed but altar still stands
↓
✅ Food is STILL permitted — altar's presence is what matters
↓
🚚 Altar is finally removed — consumption no longer permitted
Why Not Disqualified? One might think food is disqualified once the partitions fall. The Torah teaches: even while traveling, the site retains its status as the Tent of Meeting.

The Altar in Shiloh: Stone or Copper?

Rav Huna / Rav

  • The altar in Shiloh was fashioned of stones
  • R' Eliezer ben Yaakov: Three mentions of 'stones' allude to three stone altars
  • One in Shiloh, one in Nov/Gibeon, one in the Temple

R' Natan (Reconciliation)

  • The Shiloh altar was copper, hollow, and filled with stones
  • This preserves the tradition that heavenly fire stayed on the copper altar until Solomon
  • Rav Huna follows this view — copper exterior, stones inside
The Problem If Shiloh's altar was pure stone, then the heavenly fire would have left the copper altar at Shiloh — not in Solomon's time as the baraita states.

The Heavenly Fire: Copper to Stone

Rav Aḥa bar Ami
Objection! The baraita says the heavenly fire from Moses' time did not leave the copper altar until Solomon replaced it with stone. If Shiloh had a stone altar, the fire would have departed earlier!
R' Natan
The Shiloh altar was copper, hollow, and full of stones — so the fire remained on copper throughout.
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
Alternative answer: 'Did not depart' means it was not nullified — it was still somewhat present on the copper altar even at Shiloh.
Rabbis
The copper altar would emit sparks toward the stone altar when offerings were sacrificed on it.
Rav Pappa
The fire was like a guest — sometimes here on the copper altar, sometimes there on the stone altar.
Beautiful Image Rav Pappa's metaphor: the heavenly fire was like a guest, visiting both the copper and stone altars — never fully departing from either.
