Building the Framework: Sources for Lishmah Requirements

Core Question: Where do we derive that a chatat (sin offering) must be sacrificed lishmah (for its sake)?
↓
Slaughter & Collection:
"The priest shall take of the blood of the sin offering" (Lev. 4:25)
↓
Sprinkling:
"The priest shall make atonement for him concerning his sin" (Lev. 4:26)
↓
Type of Offering
"Sin offering" — must be for sake of a chatat
Owner
"For him" — must be for the specific owner
KEY THEME The Gemara systematically builds a complete framework of lishmah requirements, deriving each rite and each type of deviation from specific verses.

Step-by-Step Derivation of Lishmah for the Chatat

📖 Slaughter & Collection: "Of the blood of the sin offering" (Lev. 4:25) — must collect blood as a chatat
↓
💉 Sprinkling: "Shall make atonement concerning his sin" (Lev. 4:26) — atonement = sprinkling, must be lishmah
↓
👤 Owner (ab initio): "For him" — not for another person
↓
🔒 Owner (indispensable): Rav Huna b. Rav Yehoshua — "His sin offering" (Num. 6:16) — the extra "his" teaches indispensability
↓
⚡ All Rites: R' Yona — "ve'asa (shall offer) his sin offering" — all asiyyotav (actions) must be lishmah
METHOD Each requirement is derived from a distinct word or phrase in the Torah, building from the specific to the comprehensive.

Which Sin Offering Teaches the General Rule?

R' Yona
Derive from the nazirite's sin offering: "Ve'asa et chatato" (Num. 6:16) — all actions must be lishmah
Ravina
Objection: The same verse says "his burnt offering" — shall we say a burnt offering not-lishmah is also invalid?
Gemara
Further problem: Can we even compare a standard chatat to a nazirite's? Each has a unique stringency the other lacks!
Rava
Rather, derive from the leper's sin offering: "Ve'asa haKohen et ha-chatat" (Lev. 14:19) — all actions of the chatat must be lishmah
Gemara
Owner requirement for leper: "Ve'khiper alav" — "for him," not for another — and this is indispensable
RESOLUTION Rava shifts the source from the nazirite to the leper's sin offering, avoiding Ravina's objection about burnt offerings and the problem of incomparable stringencies.

Why Can't We Compare These Sin Offerings Directly?

Standard Chatat (e.g., Eating Chelev)

  • Atones for a sin punishable by karet
  • Brought alone — no companion offerings required
  • This stringency is absent from the nazirite's chatat

Nazirite's Chatat

  • Brought alongside other offerings (olah, shelamim)
  • "Other blood" is brought with it — unique stringency
  • Does NOT atone for a karet-level transgression

Leper's Chatat (Rava's Source)

  • Has explicit verse: "ve'asa haKohen et ha-chatat"
  • Teaches all rites must be lishmah
  • Combined with standard chatat and nazirite to cover all cases
PRINCIPLE When two cases each have a unique stringency (pirka), we cannot derive one from the other. We need a third source or a tzad ha-shaveh (common element).

Paschal Offering: For Its Sake vs. Not For Its Sake

ScenarioOn Passover EveRest of the Year
Sacrificed for its sake (lishmah)✅ Fit❌ Unfit (no longer its time)
Sacrificed not for its sake (shelo lishmah)❌ Unfit✅ Fit (becomes a shelamim)
SourceMishna (Zevachim 2a)Shmuel's father: Lev. 3:6
KEY VERSE "If his offering for a sacrifice of peace offerings unto the Lord is of the flock" (Lev. 3:6) — an offering brought only from the flock (like the Paschal) becomes a shelamim when sacrificed not for its sake outside its appointed time.

The Paschal Offering: What Does It Become?

A Paschal offering is sacrificed outside Passover eve, not for its own sake
↓
Shmuel's father: Lev. 3:6 — an offering "of the flock" → becomes a shelamim
↓
Challenge: Maybe only if slaughtered as a shelamim specifically?
↓
R' Ila / R' Yochanan: "For a sacrifice" — extra term includes any type of sacrifice
↓
Result
Whatever offering it's slaughtered for → it becomes a shelamim, not that other offering
Reason
"For a sacrifice of peace offerings" — the shelamim status is fixed
NOTE The word order matters: "sacrifice OF peace offerings" means it always defaults to shelamim status, regardless of the intent at slaughter.
