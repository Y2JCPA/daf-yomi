QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does Rava derive the lishmah requirement from the leper's sin offering rather than the nazirite's?
A. The nazirite's offering is too rare to serve as a general source
B. Ravina objected that 'his burnt offering' would also imply invalidity, and nazirite/standard chatat have incomparable stringencies
C. The leper's offering is mentioned earlier in the Torah
D. The nazirite's sin offering does not require blood sprinkling
Correct: B — Ravina pointed out that the same logic would make a burnt offering invalid if not lishmah (from 'his burnt offering'), and the Gemara noted that each sin offering has a unique stringency the other lacks, preventing direct comparison. The leper's chatat avoids these problems.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What happens to a Paschal offering sacrificed not for its sake during the rest of the year?
A. It is completely disqualified
B. It becomes whatever offering it was slaughtered for
C. It retains Paschal status but may be eaten for two days
D. It becomes a shelamim (peace offering)
Correct: D — Shmuel's father derives from Leviticus 3:6 that an offering 'of the flock' (like the Paschal) defaults to shelamim status. The verse's structure — 'for a sacrifice OF peace offerings' — means it always becomes a shelamim regardless of the slaughterer's intent.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does the Gemara derive that the sin offering's lishmah requirement for the OWNER is indispensable (me'akev), not just ab initio?
A. From the word 'atonement' which implies absolute requirements
B. From Rav Huna b. Rav Yehoshua: 'his sin offering' — the extra 'his' teaches indispensability
C. From a gezeirah shavah between chatat and asham
D. From the principle that all sacrificial requirements are automatically indispensable
Correct: B — Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua notes that the verse could have simply said 'sin offering' but instead says 'HIS sin offering' (Numbers 6:16). The seemingly superfluous 'his' teaches that owner-specific intent is not merely preferred but indispensable.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does the Gemara ultimately prefer the hermeneutical method of ribbuy u-mi'ut over klal u-frat u-klal for Leviticus 3:6?
A. Klal u-frat u-klal would limit the Paschal to becoming only vow/gift offerings (like shelamim), excluding chatat and asham
B. Ribbuy u-mi'ut is always preferred over klal u-frat
C. The verse has four terms, which is too many for klal u-frat
D. R' Yishmael's school rejected klal u-frat entirely
Correct: A — Using klal u-frat u-klal, we would include only items 'similar to the detail' — shelamim are vow/gift offerings, so only other vow/gift offerings would be included. This would exclude chatat and asham. Ribbuy u-mi'ut produces a broader inclusion, covering all offering types.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does the Gemara derive lishmah requirements for sin offerings where the Torah doesn't explicitly say 'for a sin offering' (e.g., sin offering for idol worship)?
A. Through an oral tradition (halakhah l'Moshe mi-Sinai)
B. Idol worship chatat is derived from standard chatat since both involve karet; others from the common element of three sources
C. All sin offerings automatically have the same rules by definition
D. From the general principle that all offerings must be lishmah
Correct: B — The idol worship chatat shares the karet-level severity of the standard chatat, so it is derived from there directly. The remaining sin offerings (shemi'at kol, bituy sefatayim, tum'at Mikdash) are derived from the tzad ha-shaveh (common element) of three sources: standard, nazirite, and leper sin offerings.
