QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the standard principle, why can't the goat of Yom Kippur's laws be derived from the idol worship goats via kal vachomer?
A. Because a law derived by hekkesh cannot subsequently teach via kal vachomer in consecrated matters
B. Because goats and bulls cannot be compared to each other
C. Because Yom Kippur offerings have entirely unique rules
D. Because the idol worship goats are not brought inside the Sanctuary
Correct: A — The Gemara states the principle that a halakha derived by juxtaposition (hekkesh) with regard to consecrated matters cannot subsequently teach another halakha via a fortiori inference (kal vachomer).
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the purpose of the extra phrase 'with the bull' in Leviticus 4:20, according to Rav Pappa?
A. To include the bull of Yom Kippur for all its laws
B. To re-establish the diaphragm/kidney law as if explicitly written for the communal sin bull, avoiding hekkesh-from-hekkesh
C. To exclude the anointed priest's bull from these laws
D. To teach that only bulls, not goats, require burning the diaphragm
Correct: B — Rav Pappa explains that 'with the bull' anchors the derived law as though it were originally written for the communal sin bull, so that the subsequent derivation to the idol worship goats is not a prohibited hekkesh-from-hekkesh.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
In the parable of the king and his beloved servant, why does the Torah omit details of the communal sin bull's offering?
A. Because the communal sin bull is less important than the priest's bull
B. Because God abbreviated Israel's sin offering details out of affection for them
C. Because the details can be inferred from logical reasoning alone
D. Because communal offerings don't require burning the diaphragm and kidneys
Correct: B — The school of R' Yishmael teaches that like a king who speaks little of his beloved servant's offense, God doesn't elaborate on Israel's sin offering details because of His love for them.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Reish Lakish, what is Rabbi Meir's actual reason for ruling piggul when there was improper intent only during the handful but not the frankincense?
A. Half a permitting factor is sufficient for piggul
B. The handful is more important than the frankincense
C. One who performs an action silently is considered to act with his initial intent
D. The frankincense is not actually a permitting factor
Correct: C — Reish Lakish explains that R' Meir's principle is 'anyone who performs an action performs it with his initial intent' — so the silent burning of frankincense carries forward the piggul intent from the handful.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does the mishna's distinction between placing the LAST placement improperly vs. the FIRST placement improperly support Reish Lakish's interpretation?
A. It shows that only the last placement matters for piggul
B. If R' Meir held half-factor piggul in general, this distinction would be unnecessary — the structure proves his rule is about intent continuity
C. It proves that the Rabbis agree with R' Meir in this case
D. It demonstrates that all placements must be improper for piggul
Correct: B — The mishna rules differently based on whether the improper intent came first or last. If R' Meir simply held that half a permitting factor creates piggul regardless, this distinction would be meaningless. The fact that it matters proves his reasoning is about silent actions carrying forward initial intent.
