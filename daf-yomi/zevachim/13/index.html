ScenarioStatus

Mishna Example Lishmah then shelo lishmah: Slaughtering a Korban Pesach for the sake of a Pesach and then for the sake of a Shelamim — pasul.

Is Conveyance (Holakhah) Indispensable?

R' Shimon
Conveyance is not indispensable. One can slaughter beside the altar and sprinkle directly — no conveyance needed. Therefore improper intent during holakhah does not disqualify.
Tanna Kamma
Conveyance is one of the four avodos. Improper intent during any of them disqualifies the offering.
R' Eliezer
It depends: where conveyance is required, intent disqualifies. Where it is not required, intent does not disqualify.
R' Shimon's Logic If one slaughters the animal alongside the mizbe'ach and sprinkles immediately, no holakhah occurs — proving it is not an essential rite.

Kabbalah vs. Zerikah: R' Akiva's Distinction

Kabbalah (Collection)

  • Torah does NOT render intent like action
  • Prohibited intent does NOT disqualify
  • Collecting blood outside the Temple — NO karet
  • If unfit persons collected — NOT liable

Zerikah (Sprinkling)

  • Torah DOES render intent like action
  • Prohibited intent DOES disqualify
  • Sprinkling blood outside the Temple — karet
  • If unfit persons sprinkled — liable to death
R' Tarfon's Declaration "I will bury my sons if I did not hear a distinction between collection and sprinkling from my teachers — but I cannot explicate it." R' Akiva then provided the explanation.

Rava: When Does Intent During Collection Create Piggul?

One slaughters with intent regarding collection tomorrow
↓
Is collection considered consumption?
No
Collection is NOT consumption → Not piggul
Yes — via remainder
Collecting with intent to pour remainder tomorrow → Piggul
Rava's Resolution Intent during kabbalah to pour the shirayim (remainder) tomorrow creates piggul — because pouring the remainder is a form of consumption on the altar.

Two Types of 'Eating' — Leviticus 7:18

ואם האכל יאכל מבשר זבח שלמיו ביום השלישי לא ירצה (ויקרא ז:יח)
Scriptural Source The doubled verb "he'akhol ye'akhel" (אם האכל יאכל) teaches two consumptions:

1️⃣ Consumption by man — priests or owner eating the meat
2️⃣ Consumption by the altar — pouring remainder of blood and burning sacrificial portions (emurim)

Both are subject to the laws of piggul when performed with intent beyond the designated time.
