5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Mishna, if one slaughters a Paschal offering for the sake of a Paschal offering AND for the sake of a peace offering, what is the status?
A. Fit — the primary intent was correct
B. Unfit — mixed intent disqualifies
C. Piggul — it must be burned
D. Valid but does not fulfill the owner's obligation
Correct: B — The Mishna explicitly states that performing a rite 'for its sake and not for its sake' (lishmah v'shelo lishmah) renders the offering pasul. Even though one intent was correct, the presence of improper intent disqualifies it.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does R' Shimon hold that improper intent during conveyance (holakhah) does NOT disqualify the offering?
A. Because conveyance is only a rabbinic requirement
B. Because the Torah never mentions conveyance
C. Because one can slaughter beside the altar and sprinkle directly without conveyance
D. Because conveyance is performed by non-priests
Correct: C — R' Shimon's reasoning is that conveyance is not indispensable — if one slaughters alongside the mizbe'ach and sprinkles immediately, no conveyance is needed. Since it can be dispensed with, it is not significant enough for intent to disqualify.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to R' Akiva, what is the key difference between collection (kabbalah) and sprinkling (zerikah) regarding prohibited intent?
A. Collection requires a priest but sprinkling does not
B. For collection the Torah does NOT render intent like action, but for sprinkling it does
C. Collection can be done by a non-priest but sprinkling cannot
D. Sprinkling is only required for sin offerings whereas collection applies to all
Correct: B — R' Akiva distinguishes: for kabbalah, the Torah does not equate intent with action (so prohibited intent doesn't disqualify), while for zerikah the Torah does render intent like action. Additionally, collecting outside the Temple incurs no karet, while sprinkling outside does.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In Rava's resolution, when does intent during blood collection create piggul?
A. When one collects intending to sprinkle the next day
B. When one collects intending to eat the meat the next day
C. When one collects intending to pour the remainder (shirayim) the next day
D. Intent during collection can never create piggul
Correct: C — Rava distinguishes: if one says during slaughter 'I intend to collect tomorrow' — no piggul, since collection is not consumption. But if during collection one intends to pour the shirayim tomorrow — piggul, because pouring the remainder is considered consumption on the altar.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is R' Yosei b'R' Chanina's argument for why dipping one's finger in blood of an inner sin offering does NOT create piggul?
A. Dipping is not one of the four essential avodos
B. The Torah explicitly excludes inner sin offerings from piggul
C. All piggul laws derive from shelamim, and shelamim has no finger-dipping requirement — so there is no parallel
D. Only Kohanim Gedolim perform inner sin offerings so piggul doesn't apply
Correct: C — R' Yosei b'R' Chanina maintains that ALL piggul halakhos are derived from shelamim. Since a shelamim has no requirement of dipping one's finger in blood, there is no basis to say that dipping creates piggul for a sin offering. The disqualification of shelo lishmah in a chatas parallels chutz limkomo in shelamim — but dipping has no such parallel.
