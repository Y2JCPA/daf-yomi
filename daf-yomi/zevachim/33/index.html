Is Partial Entry Considered Entry?

Ulla (Reish Lakish)
An impure person who inserted his hand into the Temple courtyard is flogged — partial entry is considered entry
Gemara
Can we prove this from the leper's semicha? If partial entry isn't entry, let him insert his hands and do semicha inside!
Rav Yosef
No proof — this baraita follows R' Yosei b. R' Yehuda, who limits the slaughter zone to north of the altar, far from the entrance
Ravina
Reish Lakish's rule applies to malkot (lashes) only — he agrees there is no karet for partial entry
Key Inference Both Abaye and Rava hold that tumah (impurity) is overridden (דחויה) in public offerings — not entirely permitted (הותרה)

Why Can't the Leper Do Semicha Inside the Courtyard?

A metzora (leper) must do semicha on his asham (guilt offering)
↓
Normally: semicha must immediately precede slaughter, in the same location
↓
But the metzora cannot enter the azarah until blood is sprinkled on his behalf
↓
So semicha is done at the threshold of the Nicanor Gate — outside the courtyard
↓
Why not just insert hands through the gate?
↓
Answer 1 (Rav Yosef)
Slaughter zone is too far north per R' Yosei b. R' Yehuda
Answer 2
Semicha requires full force — needs head & majority of body
Tosefta Source: Tosefta Nega'im 8:10 — the leper's semicha is the sole exception to the rule that semicha must occur in the place of slaughter

The Temple Cannot Be Modified

הַכֹּל בִּכְתָב מִיַּד ה׳ עָלַי הִשְׂכִּיל כֹּל מַלְאֲכוֹת הַתַּבְנִית — דברי הימים א כח:יט
Abaye & Rava The Gemara suggests: Make an opening (pishpash) in the northern courtyard wall facing the altar so the leper can insert his hands!

Both Abaye and Rava reject this — the Temple's structure is fixed by divine plan:

"All in writing, as the Lord has made me wise by His hand upon me, even all the works of this pattern"

Amud B: Impure Person Touching Kodashim

Reish Lakish

  • An impure person who touches sacrificial food is flogged
  • Source: "Every consecrated item she shall not touch" (Lev. 12:4)
  • The word touch = prohibition against touching
  • The juxtaposition to Temple entry = prohibition against eating
  • Both prohibitions derived from the same verse

Rabbi Yochanan

  • An impure person who touches sacrificial food is NOT flogged
  • That verse refers to terumah, not kodashim
  • Prohibition for eating kodashim while impure: from Bardela's gezeirah shavah
  • Verbal analogy: tumato / tumato — Lev. 7:20 ↔ Num. 19:13
  • Just as Temple entry has karet + prohibition, so does eating kodashim
Resolution Reish Lakish derives two prohibitions from Leviticus 12:4 — touching (from the word "touch") and eating (from juxtaposition to Temple entry)
