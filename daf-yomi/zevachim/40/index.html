Indispensability of Blood Placements

Baraita
The seven placements on the Curtain are indispensable. From where do we derive that the four placements on the inner altar are also indispensable?
Rav Pappa
The seven indispensable placements are found in the red heifer and the leper purification.
R' Yirmeya
The derivation of 'so shall he do' for the four placements is needed only according to R' Shimon, who derives only two corners explicitly from the verse.
Gemara
R' Shimon and R' Yehuda each use different verses to derive the four corners — and each repurposes the other's verse for a different halakha.
Core Issue How do we know that all blood placements — both the seven on the Curtain and the four on the inner altar — are indispensable for atonement?

Two Sets of Indispensable Placements

Seven Placements (Curtain)

  • Sprinkled on the Parochet (Curtain)
  • Source: Red heifer (Bamidbar 19)
  • Source: Leper purification (Vayikra 14)
  • Written and repeated — clearly indispensable

Four Placements (Inner Altar)

  • Placed on four corners of the golden altar
  • Derived from 'כֵּן יַעֲשֶׂה' — 'so shall he do'
  • Dispute: how are four corners derived?
  • Also indispensable for atonement
Rav Pappa The seven indispensable placements are those of the parah adumah (red heifer) and the metzora (leper) purification.

How Are Four Corners Derived?

R' Shimon
The verse says קַרְנוֹת (corners, plural) for the anointed priest's bull = 2 corners. It says קַרְנוֹת again for the communal bull = 2 more. Total: 4 corners by juxtaposition.
R' Yehuda
No need for that derivation! The phrase 'בְּאֹהֶל מוֹעֵד' ('in the Tent of Meeting') is superfluous — it teaches that blood goes on all corners of the altar in the Tent of Meeting, i.e., all four.
Gemara
So what does R' Yehuda do with 'כֵּן יַעֲשֶׂה' ('so shall he do')?
R' Yehuda
I use it to teach that the bull of Yom Kippur requires semicha (placing hands) and pouring the remainder of blood — rites not stated explicitly for it.
Key Dispute R' Shimon derives four corners from plural forms; R' Yehuda derives them from 'in the Tent of Meeting.' Each then uses the other's verse for a different law.

Extending Rites to the Bull of Yom Kippur

Bull for Unwitting Communal Sin
Explicit rites: semicha, blood sprinkling, pouring remainder
↓
Verse: 'כֵּן יַעֲשֶׂה לַפָּר'
'So shall he do with the bull'
↓
Bull of Yom Kippur
Now also requires semicha and pouring remainder
↓
Why is this needed if 'with the bull' already includes it?
Without 'so shall he do'
Might limit comparison to services indispensable for atonement only (e.g., sprinkling)
With 'so shall he do'
Extends even to services not indispensable for atonement (semicha, pouring remainder)
Principle 'So shall he do' ensures that even non-essential rites like semicha and pouring the blood remainder apply to the Yom Kippur bull.

Blood Requirements for Sprinkling (Amud B)

🤚 אֶת (et) — Priest sprinkles with his right hand specifically
↓
☝️ אֶצְבָּעוֹ (his finger) — his actual finger, not with a wart or blister interposing
↓
🩸 בַּדָּם (in the blood) — must have sufficient blood for ALL sprinklings from the outset
↓
💧 וְטָבַל (and immerse) — enough blood remains to immerse the finger, not just wipe vessel walls
↓
🔥 קְטֹרֶת (sweet incense) — altar must have been inaugurated with incense before sprinkling blood on it
Why Both Terms? 'In the blood' teaches sufficient measure from the outset. 'And immerse' teaches there must be enough blood for actual immersion even at the end. Both are necessary.
