Blood Entering the Sanctuary: How Far Does the Disqualification Reach?

Rabbi Akiva
Any blood meant for the external altar that entered the Sanctuary to atone is disqualified — this applies to all offerings.
Rabbi Yosei HaGelili
Even if you expound 'any' (kol) all day, I won't accept it. The verse speaks of sin offerings alone — individual, communal, male, and female.
Shmuel
A parable: A student mixed wine with hot water. When told 'mix another,' he asked 'hot or cold?' The teacher said: since we were already using hot water, the new instruction must mean to expand the options.
Rav Huna b. R' Yehoshua
The parable is flawed! Since the passage already applies to all offerings (re: scouring/rinsing), mentioning 'sin offering' should limit, not expand. Rather, Rabbi Akiva derives it from 'any' (kol).
Core Dispute Does Leviticus 6:23 disqualify only sin offering blood brought into the Sanctuary, or does it extend to all offerings?

The Source Verse

וְכׇל חַטָּאת אֲשֶׁר יוּבָא מִדָּמָהּ אֶל אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד לְכַפֵּר בַּקֹּדֶשׁ לֹא תֵאָכֵל בָּאֵשׁ תִּשָּׂרֵף
Leviticus 6:23 "And any sin offering, whereof any of the blood is brought into the Tent of Meeting to atone in the Sanctuary, shall not be eaten; it shall be burned with fire."

This verse is the basis for the dispute: does kol chatat (any sin offering) extend beyond sin offerings, or does it merely include all types of sin offerings?

Shmuel's Wine Parable vs Rav Huna's Objection

Shmuel
A student was already mixing wine with hot water. The teacher then said: 'Mix me another.' The student asked: 'Hot or cold?' The teacher replied: 'We were already dealing with hot — I must mean expand to include cold too.'
Shmuel
Similarly, the Torah passage already discusses sin offerings. Why repeat 'sin offering'? To teach: not sin offering alone — all offerings are disqualified if their blood enters the Sanctuary.
Rav Huna b. R' Yehoshua
Objection! Since the passage's rules (scouring/rinsing) already apply to all offerings, the context is already broad. Mentioning 'sin offering' again should narrow the scope.
Rav Huna b. R' Yehoshua
The correct parable: a student mixed wine with both hot and cold. The teacher then said: 'Mix with hot only.' — This limits, not expands.
Rav Huna b. R' Yehoshua
Rather, Rabbi Akiva derives it from the word kol (any/all): 'Any sin offering' → includes kodshei kodashim. 'And any' → includes kodashim kalim.
Resolution Rav Huna rejects Shmuel's parable and says Rabbi Akiva's true source is the expansive word kol in the verse.

Rabbi Akiva vs Rabbi Yosei HaGelili: Reading 'Kol Chatat'

Rabbi Akiva

  • "Chatat" (sin offering) → baseline category
  • "Kol chatat" (any sin offering) → includes kodshei kodashim (most sacred offerings)
  • "V'khol chatat" (AND any) → includes kodashim kalim (lesser sanctity offerings)
  • Result: ALL offerings disqualified if blood enters Sanctuary

Rabbi Yosei HaGelili

  • "Chatat" → individual sin offering only
  • "Kol chatat" → includes communal sin offerings
  • "V'khol chatat" → includes male sin offerings (goats, not just female)
  • Result: ONLY sin offerings are disqualified — verse speaks of bulls/goats that are burned
Key Distinction Both agree kol expands scope — they disagree on what it expands to: other offering types, or other categories of sin offering.

Blood vs Meat Entering the Sanctuary

Offering's blood or meat leaves its designated area
↓
Blood goes OUTSIDE courtyard
Does not disqualify the remaining blood inside
Blood goes INSIDE Sanctuary
Disqualifies the blood that remains outside
Meat goes OUTSIDE boundary
Disqualified — 'flesh torn in the field' (Exodus 22:30)
Meat goes INSIDE Sanctuary
Not disqualified — verse says 'blood,' not meat
Asymmetry Blood brought inside is stricter than blood going outside. But for meat, the reverse applies — going outside disqualifies, entering inside does not.
