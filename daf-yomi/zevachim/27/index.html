Misapplied Blood: Status & Consequences

Blood sprinkled in the wrong location on the altar
↓
Is it considered as if sprinkled in the proper place?
Shmuel / Reish Lakish: Yes
Owner achieves atonement, but meat may not be consumed
R' Yochanan: No
Offering is completely disqualified; no atonement without remaining blood
Core Dispute The Amoraim debate whether misapplied blood retains legal efficacy — affecting piggul status, atonement, and whether blood can be gathered and reapplied.

Intent to Sprinkle Blood Improperly the Next Day

Slaughter with intent to sprinkle blood in wrong place AND beyond its time (next day)
↓
Is the offering piggul?
↓
❌ Disqualified but NOT piggul — no karet liability
↓
Why? The improper-place intent already disqualified the offering — piggul intent alone didn't cause the disqualification
Mar Zutra's Principle Only sprinkling intent that permits the meat for consumption can cause piggul. Sprinkling in the wrong place never permits meat — so it cannot generate piggul.

Does Misapplied Blood Count as Properly Applied?

Shmuel
Blood applied not in its proper place is considered as though applied in its proper place — the owner achieves atonement.
Mar Zutra
Even Shmuel concedes: although the owner atones, the meat may not be consumed. So this sprinkling cannot cause piggul.
Rav Ashi
From where do you derive this distinction?
Mar Zutra
From Leviticus 7:18 — 'it shall be piggul' implies only when piggul intent alone caused the disqualification.
Rav Nachman b. Yitzchak
Intent beyond designated time still disqualifies even when it doesn't create piggul — just like intent to leave portions overnight (per R' Yehuda, 35b).
Key Verse Leviticus 7:18 — Only an offering whose piggul intent alone caused disqualification is rendered piggul.

Three Amoraic Views on Misapplied Blood

Shmuel

  • Misapplied blood = as if properly applied
  • Owner achieves atonement
  • Meat still forbidden to eat
  • Applies to both silence and intent cases

Reish Lakish

  • Misapplied blood = as if properly applied
  • Silence: owner achieves atonement
  • With piggul intent: offering disqualified
  • Distinguishes silence vs. stated intent

R' Yochanan

  • Misapplied blood ≠ properly applied
  • Offering completely disqualified
  • If blood of the soul remains → re-sprinkle
  • If no blood remains → no remedy
Practical Difference R' Yochanan holds the mishna speaks of silence (no improper intent) yet the offering is still disqualified — because misapplication itself invalidates.

Disqualified Offerings That Ascend the Altar

R' Yehuda
Leviticus 6:2 — 'This is the law of the burnt offering: it is that which goes up.' Three exclusionary terms: zot, hi, ha — excluding three cases that descend.
R' Yehuda
These three descend: (1) Slaughtered at night, (2) Blood spilled, (3) Blood left the courtyard.
R' Shimon
From 'ha'ola' — everything that ascends! All disqualified offerings that went up do not descend, as long as their disqualification occurred in sanctity.
R' Shimon
This includes: slaughtered at night, blood spilled, blood left courtyard, left overnight, became impure, piggul intent, wrong-place intent, and misapplied blood.
R' Shimon
But 'zot' excludes offerings disqualified before sanctity: animal that copulated, object of bestiality, set aside for idolatry, tereifa, etc.
Key Principle Disqualification in sanctity (during Temple service) — offering does not descend. Disqualification before sanctity (inherent defect) — offering descends even if placed on altar.
