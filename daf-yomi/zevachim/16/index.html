Who Desecrates the Temple Service?

Core Question:
Does a zar (non-priest) or onen (acute mourner) who performs sacrificial rites desecrate the service?
↓
Attempt kal va'chomer from blemished priest (ba'al mum)
↓
Each single-source derivation is refuted — unique stringencies exist
↓
Use tzad hashaveh (common element) of multiple cases to derive the law
Key Method The Gemara employs a revolving kal va'chomer: when each source is refuted, the common element of two or more sources establishes the derivation.

Kal VaChomer: Non-Priest from Blemished Priest

Gemara
A blemished priest (ba'al mum) may eat sacrificial meat, yet if he serves — he desecrates the service.
Gemara
A non-priest (zar) may not even eat most-sacred meat. Kal va'chomer — he should certainly desecrate!
Refutation
A blemished priest has a unique stringency: the Torah equates blemished animals with blemished priests — both are disqualified.
Gemara
An impure priest (kohen tamei) will prove it — no animal–priest equivalence for impurity, yet he desecrates!
Refutation
An impure priest has his own stringency: he imparts impurity to others.
Gemara
A blemished priest proves that doesn't matter — he cannot impart his blemish. The derivation revolves back!
Pirka Pattern Each source is refuted by a unique stringency, then proved by the other source — a classic revolving kal va'chomer.

Tzad HaShaveh — Common Element for Non-Priest

Blemished Priest (Ba'al Mum)

  • Unique: animal–priest equivalence for blemishes
  • Does NOT impart blemish to others
  • May eat sacrificial meat
  • Prohibited from serving → desecrates

Impure Priest (Kohen Tamei)

  • No animal–priest equivalence for impurity
  • Unique: DOES impart impurity to others
  • May eat sacrificial meat (when pure)
  • Prohibited from serving → desecrates

Non-Priest (Zar) — Derived

  • No animal–priest equivalence
  • Does NOT impart status to others
  • Common element applies: prohibited from serving
  • CONCLUSION: desecrates the service
Result Their common element: both are prohibited from serving and desecrate if they do. This applies equally to a non-priest.

The Bamot (Private Altars) Refutation

Refutation: Blemished & impure priests were never permitted on bamot (private altars)
↓
But a non-priest WAS permitted to serve on bamot!
↓
This shared stringency of blemished + impure is not shared by the non-priest
↓
Solution: Replace impure priest with acute mourner (onen) in the derivation
↓
But then…
An onen is prohibited from ma'aser sheni (second tithe) — a non-priest is not!
Response
A blemished priest may eat ma'aser sheni — proves this isn't decisive
Bamot Before the Temple was built permanently, private altars were permitted. Non-priests could serve on them — this created a key distinction from disqualified priests.

Amud B: Deriving That an Onen Desecrates the Service

Source CaseRefutation (Pirka)Counter-Proof
Blemished Priest → OnenAnimal–priest equivalence unique to blemishesNon-priest proves (no such equivalence, yet desecrates)
Non-Priest → OnenNon-priest has NO remedy — can never serveBlemished priest proves (has remedy if healed, yet desecrates)
Common element: Ba'al Mum + ZarTheir prohibition was NEVER permitted (but High Priest onen may serve!)Add impure priest — impurity IS permitted for communal offerings
Final tzad hashaveh: 3 sourcesBa'al mum + zar + tamei kohen → common elementAll prohibited from serving, all desecrate → onen desecrates too
Source for Onen The onen's prohibition derives from "This day have they offered their sin offering" (Lev. 10:19) — Aaron's offering was burned because of his acute mourning.

Rav Mesharshiyya's Alternative: The Sitting Priest

Rav Mesharshiyya
A sitting priest may eat offerings, yet if he serves while sitting he desecrates. A non-priest/onen who can't even eat — kal va'chomer!
Gemara
Pirka: A sitting person is also disqualified from bearing witness — an added stringency a non-priest doesn't share!
Rav Mesharshiyya
A sitting Torah scholar (talmid chakham) IS permitted to sit while testifying — so sitting isn't inherently disqualifying!
Gemara
The general category of sitting still disqualifies witnesses, even with exceptions.
Response
One refutes derivations only from concrete cases, not abstract categories (milta).
Principle Ein meshivin mi-milta — One cannot refute an a fortiori inference using a general conceptual category; only concrete parallel cases are valid refutations.
