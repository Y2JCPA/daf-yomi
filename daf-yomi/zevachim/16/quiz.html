QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why can't the Gemara simply derive that a non-priest desecrates the service from the blemished priest alone?
A. Because a blemished priest has a unique stringency: the Torah equates blemished animals with blemished priests
B. Because a blemished priest can eat sacrificial meat
C. Because a blemished priest was permitted on private altars
D. Because a blemished priest imparts impurity to others
Correct: A — The Torah drew an equivalence between blemished animals and blemished priests — both are disqualified. This added stringency doesn't apply to a non-priest, so the kal va'chomer from blemished priest alone fails.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the 'bamot' refutation against deriving a non-priest's law from the common element of blemished and impure priests?
A. Non-priests could never serve on bamot
B. Bamot were never permitted under any circumstances
C. Blemished and impure priests were never permitted on bamot, but non-priests were — so they differ fundamentally
D. Bamot were only permitted for impure priests
Correct: C — The Gemara argues that blemished and impure priests share a stringency not found in non-priests: they were never allowed to serve even on private altars (bamot), whereas non-priests could serve on bamot when they were permitted.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Rav Mesharshiyya's alternative source for deriving that a non-priest desecrates the service?
A. The case of an impure priest
B. The case of a sitting priest who performs rites while seated
C. The verse 'a common man shall not draw close'
D. The case of Aaron on the day his sons died
Correct: B — Rav Mesharshiyya proposes a kal va'chomer from a sitting priest: he may eat offerings yet desecrates the service when sitting. A non-priest who cannot even eat most-sacred meat should certainly desecrate.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What principle does the Gemara invoke to defend Rav Mesharshiyya's derivation from the sitting-priest refutation about witnesses?
A. One may derive halakha from prophetic statements
B. One cannot refute a derivation from a general conceptual category (milta) — only from concrete cases
C. Torah scholars are always exempt from testimony rules
D. The laws of witnesses have no bearing on Temple service
Correct: B — The Gemara states 'ein meshivin mi-milta' — a kal va'chomer can only be refuted by pointing to a concrete parallel case, not by invoking an abstract category like 'sitting disqualifies witnesses generally.'
QUESTION 5 OF 5
On Amud B, what unique refutation is raised against deriving an acute mourner's law from a non-priest?
A. A non-priest can eat second tithe but an onen cannot
B. A non-priest has no remedy — he can never become a priest — unlike an onen whose mourning ends
C. A non-priest imparts his status to his children
D. A non-priest was never permitted on bamot
Correct: B — The Gemara argues that a non-priest's disqualification is permanent (he has 'no remedy'), whereas an acute mourner's prohibition is temporary and will end. This difference potentially undermines the derivation.
