Where Did the Altar Stand? Altar Placement & Dimensions

R' Yosei HaGelili
The entire altar stood in the north section of the Temple courtyard. The Basin was placed to the south, between the Entrance Hall and the altar.
R' Eliezer ben Yaakov
"Northward before the Lord" — the north must be vacant of everything, even the altar. The entire altar stood in the south.
Rav
A damaged altar disqualifies all sacrifices slaughtered there. The source is: "An altar of earth you shall make for Me."
R' Yosei
Moses' altar was 5×5 cubits as written. The copper altar was disqualified when the Temple was built — "too small" is a euphemism.
R' Yehuda
Moses' altar was 10×10 cubits — measured from its center like Ezekiel's altar, via a gezera shava of "square."
Core Issue The Gemara works through the spatial logic of altar placement to determine whose view the Mishna in Tamid follows, then pivots to the halakha of a damaged altar and the dimensions of Moses' altar.

Where Did the Altar Stand? R' Yosei HaGelili's Reasoning

Exodus 40:29 — Altar set at the entrance to the Tent of Meeting
↓
Only the altar — not the Basin — stood at the entrance
↓
Basin placed between Entrance Hall and altar, extended toward the south
↓
Why must the Basin go south specifically?
If altar is in the south
Basin could go north of the entrance — no need to go south!
If altar is in the north
North must be vacant (Lev. 1:11) — Basin must go south ✓
Conclusion R' Yosei HaGelili holds the entire altar stood in the north. The Mishna in Tamid follows his view.

Altar Location: North vs. South

R' Yosei HaGelili — North

  • Entire altar in the north section
  • Basin placed south, between Entrance Hall and altar
  • North side partly occupied by the altar
  • Leviticus 1:11 — north must be vacant of other vessels (but altar remains)
  • Mishna in Tamid follows this view

R' Eliezer ben Yaakov — South

  • Entire altar in the south section
  • "Northward before the Lord" — north vacant of everything
  • Even the altar may not stand in the north
  • North section completely open for slaughter
  • Derives from Leviticus 1:11
Key Verse Both Tannaim derive their views from Leviticus 1:11 — "He shall slaughter it on the side of the altar northward" — but disagree on whether "northward" excludes even the altar itself.

Damaged Altar Disqualifies Sacrifices

מזבח אדמה תעשה לי וזבחת עליו
Rav's Ruling Rav states: If the altar is damaged (shenifdema), all sacrificial animals slaughtered there are disqualified.

Rav admitted: "We have a verse as the source but we have forgotten which one it is."

Rav Kahana later found the source in Eretz Yisrael from R' Shimon b. R' Yehuda HaNasi in the name of R' Yishmael b. R' Yosei:

"An altar of earth you shall make for Me, and you shall slaughter upon it..." (Exodus 20:21) — the altar must be whole for valid sacrifice.

Was Moses' Copper Altar Disqualified or Just Too Small?

R' Yehuda
Solomon consecrated the Temple courtyard floor as an altar because the copper altar was too small to hold all his offerings (I Kings 8:64).
R' Yosei
Solomon sacrificed more per cubit on Moses' altar than on the Temple altar! The verse isn't about size — it's a euphemism: the copper altar was disqualified.
Gemara
Like calling someone a nanas (dwarf) — really meaning he is disqualified from Temple service. A gentle way of saying it.
R' Yehuda
My view works because Moses' altar was actually 10×10 cubits (measured from center), so per-cubit the Temple altar was more burdened.
Core Dispute R' Yosei reads "too small" as a euphemism for disqualification. R' Yehuda takes it literally, arguing Moses' altar was larger than its plain reading suggests.
