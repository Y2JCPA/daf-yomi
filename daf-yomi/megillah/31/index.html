⚡ Why Must the Tochecha Be Read by One Person?

Anonymous Baraita
One should not interrupt the reading of the curses by having two different people read them. Rather, one person reads all of them — both in Leviticus and in Deuteronomy.
Rabbi Yochanan
So that it should not appear as if one person is cursing his fellow. If a new reader begins mid-section of curses, it looks as though he is pronouncing those curses upon the previous reader.
Reish Lakish
A different reason: one does not recite a blessing over a calamity. If a second reader begins in the middle of the curses, the blessing he recites before his reading would be a blessing over something terrible.
Abaye
This applies only to Leviticus curses (Bechukotai, ch. 26). The Deuteronomy curses (Ki Tavo, ch. 28) may be split between readers — they are stated in the singular and are Moses's own rebuke, not direct divine curses.
Practical Story Levi bar Buti was reading the curses before Rav Huna and began to stammer — it was too painful for him to utter. Rav Huna permitted him to stop and let another continue, as a special individual dispensation.

🌟 Divine Might Is Always Paired with Humility

כָּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה מוֹצֵא גְּדֻלָּתוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, שָׁם אַתָּה מוֹצֵא עֲנוָותָנוּתוֹ
Rabbi Yochanan's Principle Wherever you find mention of God's might (גְּדֻלָּה) in Scripture, you find His humility (עֲנָוָה) right next to it.

📖 Torah (Deuteronomy 10:17–18):
"God of gods and Lord of lords" → immediately followed by → "He executes judgment for the fatherless and widow"

📖 Prophets (Isaiah 57:15):
"The High and Lofty One who inhabits eternity" → immediately followed by → "to revive the spirit of the humble"

📖 Writings (Psalms 68:5–6):
"Extol Him who rides upon the skies" → immediately followed by → "Father of the orphans and judge of the widows"

🏛️ Non-Priestly Watches, Creation & Abraham's Covenant

Question: Why do the non-priestly watches (מַעֲמָדוֹת) read the account of Creation?
↓
Rabbi Ami: Were it not for the ma'amadot, heaven and earth could not endure — their prayers and Torah reading sustain the world while the priests offer sacrifices
↓
Abraham's Anxiety: "By what shall I know that I shall inherit the land?" (Genesis 15:8) — What if Israel sins and loses the inheritance?
↓
God's Answer
"Take Me a heifer of three years old" — the sacrificial system will atone for Israel's sins
Abraham's Follow-up
But what when the Temple is destroyed and sacrifices are impossible?
↓
God's Promise: I have already arranged the order of sacrifices (סֵדֶר קָרְבָּנוֹת) in the Torah — whenever Israel reads the order of sacrifices, I will account it as if they had offered them

📜 Ezra's Enactment: Ending Each Year with Its Curses

Ezra's Principle: The Torah portion containing the curses should be read immediately before the new year begins — so the old year 'ends together with its curses'
↓
Leviticus 26 (Bechukotai) — read on the Shabbat before Shavuot, because Shavuot marks the new year for fruit of the trees
↓
Deuteronomy 28 (Ki Tavo) — read on the Shabbat before Rosh HaShana, because Rosh HaShana is unambiguously the start of the new year
↓
Objection: Why read Leviticus curses before Shavuot? Shavuot is only a new year for fruit — hardly the primary new year!
↓
Answer (from Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's parable): "If elders say 'demolish' and children say 'build' — demolish and do not build." Ezra's wisdom, like that of elders, appears destructive (reading curses) but is ultimately constructive (clearing the slate for a new year)
↓
Practical continuity rule (Sages): Where the Shabbat morning reading concludes → the afternoon reading continues from there → Monday and Thursday also continue from that same place
