🗣️ The Language Debate: Resolving the Contradiction

Mishna: Torah scrolls written in any language may be rescued on Shabbat — they have scroll status
↓
Baraita contradicts: Scrolls in other languages/scripts do not have sacred scroll status
↓
Rava's Attempt (rejected by Abaye)
Mishna = other language, Hebrew script
Baraita = other language, foreign script
Final Resolution
Mishna = Rabbis — any language valid
Baraita = R. Shimon b. Gamliel — Greek only
↓
Conclusion: Only Greek is permitted beyond Hebrew — established by the miracle of King Ptolemy's translation

🤝 Abaye Challenges Rava's Resolution

Rava
The mishna speaks of Torah scrolls written in another language but in our Hebrew script. The baraita speaks of scrolls in another language written in their foreign script — that is why they differ.
Abaye
If so, why does the baraita specifically discuss a Hebrew verse written amid foreign text? Your distinction cannot explain that teaching — your framework must be wrong.
Gemara
Rather: Mishna = the Rabbis who permit any language for Torah scrolls. Baraita = Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel who permits only Greek. And even Greek is restricted to Torah scrolls — not tefillin or mezuzot.
Rav Ashi
Alternatively: The baraita concerns the other books of the Bible (not Torah), per Rabbi Yehuda — our Rabbis permitted writing them only in Greek.
Why Tefillin Must Be Hebrew Derived from 'And these words shall be' (Deut. 6:6) — their words may not be altered. Tefillin and mezuzot are forever Hebrew-only.

🏛️ Why Specifically Greek? The Japheth Connection

יַפְתְּ אֱלֹהִים לְיֶפֶת וְיִשְׁכֹּן בְּאָהֳלֵי שֵׁם — בראשית ט:כז
The Scriptural Basis for Greek's Honor Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: Among all languages, only Greek may be used for Torah scrolls beyond Hebrew. Challenge: Why not the languages of Gomer or Magog — they too descend from Japheth (Gen. 10:2)? Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba answers: It is written — "God shall enlarge Japheth, and he shall dwell in the tents of Shem" (Genesis 9:27). The beauty of Japheth — the Greek language — is specially designated to dwell within the sacred space of Shem, the domain of Torah. The miracle of the 72 Elders confirms this: each scribe, working in isolation, made the exact same 13 changes — demonstrating divine sanction for the Greek translation.

⚗️ High Priest Categories: Duties and Distinctions

Anointed High Priest

  • Consecrated with special anointing oil (First Temple era)
  • ✓ Brings bull on Yom Kippur for personal sins
  • ✓ Brings tenth of ephah meal-offering daily
  • Rabbi Meir: verse 'if the anointed priest shall sin' (Lev. 4:3) extends to garment-consecrated HP too
  • Fully incumbent High Priest — no temporary status

Garment-Consecrated High Priest

  • Consecrated by donning 8 priestly garments (Second Temple era)
  • ✓ Brings bull on Yom Kippur — same as anointed
  • ✓ Brings tenth of ephah meal-offering daily
  • Rabbis: no halakhic difference from anointed HP
  • Rabbi Meir: does NOT bring the bull — anointing is required

Former / Temporary High Priest

  • Filled role temporarily when regular HP was disqualified
  • ✗ Does NOT bring the Yom Kippur bull
  • ✗ Does NOT bring the daily meal-offering
  • Cannot return as High Priest — causes jealousy
  • Cannot return as common priest — 'we elevate, not lower'
  • Incident: Rabbi Yosef ben Elem of Tzippori

🕍 Sacred Sites: A Hierarchy of Sanctity

Personal Altar (bamah ketanah): An individual could sacrifice only their own obligatory offerings — e.g., a Paschal lamb. No voluntary communal offerings permitted.
↓
Great Public Altar — Nob & Gibeon era: The public sacrificed Paschal lambs and compulsory offerings. Per Rabbi Shimon: not voluntary offerings. Bamot were permitted during this period.
↓
Tabernacle at Shiloh: Offerings of lesser sanctity eaten throughout the region; offerings of highest sanctity eaten only within the courtyard hangings; firstborn, tithe eaten in any city of Israel.
↓
Temple in Jerusalem: Offerings of lesser sanctity eaten within Jerusalem's walls; highest sanctity only within the Temple courtyard; firstborn, tithe, and Paschal lamb within the city.
↓
Key Shiloh vs. Jerusalem distinction: Shiloh had no permanent roof — just hanging curtains. Jerusalem had permanent stone walls. Once Jerusalem was chosen, bamot became permanently forbidden.
