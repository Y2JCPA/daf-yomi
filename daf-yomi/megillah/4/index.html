🏙️ Cities Walled Since Joshua — But Didn't Elpaal Build Them?

בנויות מימות יהושע בן נון (מגילה ד.)
Historical Puzzle Resolved The Rule: Lod, Ono, and Gei HaCharashim read the Megilla on the 15th of Adar, as cities walled since Joshua's time.

The Problem: I Chronicles 8:12 says Elpaal built Ono and Lod — implying they were founded centuries later!

Rabbi Elazar's Resolution: These cities were walled from Joshua's time. They were destroyed during the War of the Concubine of Gibeah, when all of Benjamin's cities were razed. Elpaal then rebuilt them — hence the verse credits him with 'building.'

The verse about King Asa (II Chr. 14:6) — 'Let us build these cities' — also supports this: 'these cities' implies they had existed before.

🌙 Reading the Megilla at Night and by Day — The Lishnota Dispute

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: A person is obligated to read the Megilla at night and then lishnota during the day — as it is written: 'I call by day… and at night' (Psalms 22:3).
↓
Student Misunderstanding: Some heard 'lishnota' and thought it meant: read at night, then study the relevant tractate of Mishna by day (lishna = Mishna).
↓
Rabbi Yirmeya's Correction: No — 'lishnota' means to repeat (liShNoT) the Megilla reading. One reads the full Megilla both at night and again during the day.
↓
Corroboration — Rabbi Ḥelbo citing Ulla Bira'a: Confirmed independently: read at night and repeat during the day, as it is written, 'So that my glory may sing praise to You and not be silent' (Psalms 30:13) — both night and day.
↓
Also from Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: Women are obligated in Megilla reading — 'they too were significant partners in that miracle' (the salvation came through Esther).

🚫 Why Is the Megilla Not Read on Shabbat?

Rabba
Everyone is equally obligated in Megilla reading. If Purim were on Shabbat, someone who cannot read might pick up the Megilla and carry it four cubits in a public domain to bring it to an expert — a Torah-level Shabbat violation. This same concern explains why the shofar is not blown when Rosh HaShana falls on Shabbat, and why the lulav is not taken on Shabbat-Sukkot.
Rav Yosef
The reason is entirely different: the eyes of the poor are raised to the Megilla reading. The poor come to the reading because that is when matanot l'evyonim (gifts to the poor) are distributed. If the reading were on Shabbat, gifts could not be given then — the poor would gather in vain. Therefore the reading must occur on a weekday when gifts can be distributed.
Practical Consequence Both agree on the ruling — no Megilla on Shabbat — but their different rationales could affect edge cases. Rav Yosef's reason implies the Megilla reading and gift-giving are linked: collecting and distributing gifts must happen on the same day the Megilla is read.

⚖️ When the 14th Falls on Friday — Who Reads When?

Tanna Kamma

  • Villages → advance to Thursday (day of assembly)
  • Large towns → read on Friday, the 14th
  • Walled cities → read on Friday, the 14th
  • Logic: 'in every year' — large towns are never deferred from the 14th

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi

  • Villages → advance to Thursday
  • Large towns → advance to Thursday
  • Walled cities → read on Friday, the 14th
  • Logic: large towns are not deferred — but they read on Thursday, their advance day, not Friday

Rabbi Yosei

  • Villages → advance to Thursday
  • Large towns → read on Friday, the 14th
  • Walled cities → advance to Thursday
  • Logic: walled cities must never precede large towns — and 15th is Shabbat, so they go to Thursday
