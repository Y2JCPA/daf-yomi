QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why did Rabbi Yitzchak retract his ruling that sacrifices may be offered at the Temple of Onias in Egypt?
A. Rava proved the Temple of Onias was actually a house of idol worship
B. Rav Mari raised an objection from the Mishna about Jerusalem's permanent sanctity
C. The Temple of Onias was built only after the Jerusalem Temple was destroyed
D. Rabbi Yehoshua disagreed with his interpretation of the relevant verse
Correct: B — Rav Mari objected from the Mishna: since Jerusalem's sanctity is permanent and bamot were never re-permitted after the Temple's destruction, the same logic undermines the Temple of Onias as a legitimate alternative.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the key halakhic difference between the sanctity of Shiloh and the sanctity of Jerusalem?
A. Shiloh's sanctity was eternal; Jerusalem's was only for its time
B. Both Shiloh and Jerusalem have permanent sanctity that survives destruction
C. Shiloh's sanctity was temporary; after its destruction bamot were permitted — Jerusalem's sanctity is permanent
D. Jerusalem permitted bamot after destruction; Shiloh's sanctity was permanent
Correct: C — This is the fundamental distinction derived from Deuteronomy 12:9: 'Rest' = Shiloh (temporary) and 'Inheritance' = Jerusalem (permanent). The verse's juxtaposition implies different levels of lasting sanctity.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Ashi's conclusion, what distinguishes 'vayhi' alone from the phrase 'vayhi bimei'?
A. Both always indicate grief with no exceptions
B. 'Vayhi' alone can indicate either joy or grief; 'vayhi bimei' always indicates grief
C. 'Vayhi bimei' can indicate joy or grief; 'vayhi' alone always indicates grief
D. Neither phrase is inherently an omen — context determines the meaning
Correct: B — Rav Ashi carefully distinguishes: 'vayhi' alone has instances of both joy and sorrow, so no rule can be drawn. But 'vayhi bimei' in all five Biblical occurrences introduces a period of grief and tribulation.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani's prologue, why is Mordecai identified as the 'cypress [berosh]' of Isaiah 55:13?
A. Because Mordecai was tall and straight like a cypress tree
B. Because berosh contains the word rosh (chief) and Mordecai was chief of the spices — mor deror echoes his name
C. Because Mordecai planted cypress trees in the royal garden
D. Because berosh is an acronym for his full Hebrew name
Correct: B — The Gemara offers a wordplay: berosh contains rosh (head/chief). Mordecai was the chief of the spices — the Aramaic translation of 'pure myrrh' (mor deror) is mira dachya, phonetically echoing Mordecai.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
When Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi asks whether God truly rejoices over the destruction of the wicked, what is Rabbi Elazar's resolution?
A. God does rejoice over the wicked's downfall because justice demands it
B. God never rejoices and the verse is to be read metaphorically as human joy only
C. God Himself does not rejoice, but He causes others to rejoice — the verse uses causative language
D. God rejoices only when the wicked repent before their destruction
Correct: C — Rabbi Elazar resolves the contradiction: God does not personally rejoice over the downfall of the wicked, but He causes others to rejoice. The proof: the angels were silenced as Egypt drowned — 'My creations are drowning and you wish to sing?'
