🏛️ Bar Kappara: What Were the 'Great Houses'?

Opinion A
Torah study halls — places where Torah is made great. Proof: 'The Lord was pleased, for His righteousness' sake, to make the Torah great and glorious' (Isaiah 42:21). Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's ruling (synagogue → study hall is permitted) supports this view.
Opinion B
Synagogues — places where prayer is made great. Proof: 'My house shall be called a house of prayer for all peoples' (Isaiah 56:7). The 'greatness' of the house lies in communal prayer.
Conclusion The Gemara identifies Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi with Opinion A (Torah study halls), since he explicitly rules that a synagogue may be converted into a study hall — an elevation, not a downgrade.

📊 Levels of Sanctity — What May Be Sold or Converted?

Torah Scroll

  • Highest level of sanctity
  • Sale permitted only: to study Torah or to marry
  • Proceeds cannot purchase Prophets or Writings
  • Cannot sell old scroll merely to buy a new one (risk of negligence)
  • May not be rolled in wrappings of lesser scrolls

Prophets & Writings

  • Lesser sanctity than Torah scroll
  • Torah scroll proceeds may NOT fund these
  • May be wrapped in synagogue curtains
  • Proceeds cannot fund synagogue appurtenances

Synagogue

  • Community object — not sold to individuals (Rabbis)
  • R. Meir: sale only with right-of-return stipulation
  • Rabbis: permanent sale allowed — not for degrading use
  • May be converted to a study hall (elevation permitted)

Study Hall (Beit Midrash)

  • Torah is made 'great and glorious' here
  • Synagogue may become a study hall
  • Considered an upgrade in sanctity
  • Site of the 'great houses' per Opinion A

💰 Surplus Funds After Sacred Sale — Rava vs. Abaye

Community sells sacred object; uses proceeds to purchase a new sacred object
↓
Money is left over — does it retain sanctity?
Rava: Depends on stipulation
No stipulation → surplus retains sanctity.
Prior stipulation made → surplus is ordinary money.
Abaye objects
Even with a stipulation, once sanctity attached to the money, how can a prior statement remove it? The stipulation seems meaningless!
↓
Rava replies: The stipulation was made before the sanctity fully took hold — therefore it is halakhically effective in limiting the sanctity that attaches

🏛️ Selling a Synagogue — Conditions and Limits

Rabbi Meir
A synagogue may be sold only with a stipulation allowing sellers to reclaim it. The community retains ultimate title; no permanent alienation of sacred property.
The Rabbis
A synagogue may be sold permanently for any use — except a bathhouse, tannery, ritual bath, or latrine. These four uses are affronts to its former sanctity.
Gemara Challenge
According to Rabbi Meir, if the buyer lives in the sold synagogue during the conditional period, isn't this ribbis (interest)? The buyer uses the space; if the sale is undone, the money is returned — like a loan with benefit!
Rabbi Yohanan
Rabbi Meir rules like Rabbi Yehuda, who permits uncertain interest — since the sale might never be undone, any 'interest' is merely hypothetical and therefore permitted.
Rava's Alternative Explanation The real dispute is about interest paid on condition it will be returned (ribbis al menat lehachzir) — Rabbi Yehuda permits it; the Rabbis prohibit it. Both agree uncertain interest itself is forbidden.
