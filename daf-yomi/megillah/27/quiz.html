Megillah 27 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
Bar Kappara interprets 'great houses' (II Kings 25:9) as referring to two types of places. What are the two opinions?
A. Homes of wealthy nobles and palaces of kings
B. Houses where Torah is made great (study halls) and houses where prayer is made great (synagogues)
C. The Temple and the Sanhedrin's chamber
D. Houses of charity collection and houses of Torah scroll storage
Correct: B — Bar Kappara says 'great houses' means houses where greatness occurs — one opinion cites Isaiah 42:21 for Torah study, the other cites Isaiah 56:7 for prayer. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's ruling supports the Torah-study opinion.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to the baraita cited in the Gemara (Rabbi Yohanan in the name of Rabbi Meir), for what purposes may a Torah scroll lawfully be sold?
A. To repair a synagogue or fund a charity collection
B. To purchase scrolls of the Prophets or Writings
C. To study Torah or to marry a woman
D. To buy a new, better-quality Torah scroll
Correct: C — The baraita rules that a Torah scroll may be sold only if the seller needs the money to study Torah or to marry a woman — both mitzvot of the highest personal importance.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Rava's ruling about surplus funds remaining after a community uses sacred-sale proceeds to buy a new sacred object?
A. Surplus always retains full sanctity and must be used for sacred purposes
B. Surplus never retains sanctity and may be used freely
C. Surplus retains sanctity only if the community made no prior stipulation about the surplus
D. Surplus must be returned to the original buyers of the sacred object
Correct: C — Rava holds that surplus funds carry sanctity only absent a prior stipulation. If the community stipulated beforehand how to treat surplus funds — before sanctity fully attached — that stipulation is effective.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Yohanan say Rabbi Meir permits a conditional sale of a synagogue even though buyers may use it (raising an interest concern)?
A. Rabbi Meir holds that synagogues have no monetary sanctity
B. Rabbi Meir follows Rabbi Yehuda, who permits uncertain interest since the sale may never be reversed
C. Rabbi Meir holds that living in a synagogue is not a monetary benefit
D. Rabbi Meir agrees with the Rabbis and actually bans the sale entirely
Correct: B — Rabbi Yohanan explains that Rabbi Meir rules like Rabbi Yehuda: since it is uncertain whether the sale will ever be undone, the potential interest is only uncertain — and Rabbi Yehuda permits uncertain interest.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In what merit did Rabbi Zakkai attribute his long life?
A. He always arrived first to the study hall before any disciple
B. He never made a shortcut through a synagogue
C. He never urinated within four cubits of a place used for prayer
D. He never missed kiddush on Shabbat even in poverty
Correct: C — Rabbi Zakkai credited his longevity to scrupulously avoiding urinating within four cubits of any place that had been used for prayer — showing that the sanctity of a prayer site demands reverence even in mundane bodily conduct.
