Megillah 25 Quiz

QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why is the prayer leader silenced for saying "We give thanks, we give thanks"?
A. Repetition invalidates the blessing and must be restarted
B. It sounds as though he is praying to and acknowledging two divine authorities
C. It is not a proper Hebrew grammatical formulation
D. The congregation cannot respond Amen to a repeated phrase
Correct: B — Repeating "we give thanks" makes it appear the leader is acknowledging two authorities — a form of dualist heresy. By contrast, repeating Shema is merely reprehensible, not silenced, unless done in a way that seems like two authorities.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What was Rabbi Ḥanina's criticism of the prayer leader who listed many divine epithets?
A. Only the High Priest may add epithets during the Temple service
B. Long prayers tire the congregation and reduce kavvanah
C. Even the three standard epithets are only permitted because Moses and the Great Assembly established them — adding more is theologically presumptuous
D. Epithets not found in the Torah are considered foreign influence
Correct: C — Rabbi Ḥanina teaches that we can barely justify the three standard epithets (great, mighty, awesome) because they were specifically sanctioned by Moses and the Great Assembly. Adding finite praises beyond that implicitly diminishes God — like praising a king of gold for having silver.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why is the Priestly Benediction (birkat kohanim) read but NOT translated publicly?
A. It is too sacred and may only be heard by kohanim
B. "May God lift His countenance upon you" could be misunderstood as God showing favoritism toward the wealthy
C. The translation would alter the blessing's legal efficacy
D. Kohanim must recite it exclusively in Hebrew
Correct: B — The phrase "May God lift His countenance" (יִשָּׂא ה׳ פָּנָיו אֵלֶיךָ) could be misread as God showing personal favoritism — especially favoring the rich over the poor. Withholding the translation prevents this misunderstanding.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Rabbi Ḥanina taught: "Everything is in the hands of Heaven, except for _____."
A. Health and illness
B. Wealth and poverty
C. Fear of Heaven (yirat shamayim)
D. The length of one's life
Correct: C — Rabbi Ḥanina teaches that free will to fear God — yirat shamayim — belongs entirely to man. God decrees many things, but the choice to serve Him cannot be imposed from Heaven. The Gemara notes this seems to make yirat shamayim a "minor matter," but responds: for the average person, it is not minor at all.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
When the Torah contains a coarse or crude word, what is the rule for public Torah reading?
A. The verse is skipped and the reader jumps to the next one
B. The word is read aloud using a refined substitute
C. The verse is read as written but the translator must use refined language
D. The Rabbi alone whispers the verse to avoid public embarrassment
Correct: B — The rule is that coarse words are read aloud using refined substitutes — e.g., yishgalena is replaced with yishkavena. The exception is contemptuous language for idols, which may be read as written, since mockery of idolatry is permitted (and even modeled by the prophets).
