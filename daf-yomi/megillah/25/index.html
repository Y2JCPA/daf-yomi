🐦 Bird's Nest Prayer — Why Is It Silenced?

Prayer Leader
"Just as Your mercy extends to the bird's nest — commanding us to send away the mother — so too may Your mercy extend to us!"
Abaye
Silence him! The mitzvot are the King's decrees — not acts of divine compassion. Framing them as mercy implies God's commands arise from emotion, not sovereign will.
Rabba
Silence him! (Privately to Abaye: I said this only to sharpen your thinking. But your answer — that it makes mitzvot look like acts of mercy — is the correct reason.)
R. Yosei bar Avin vs. R. Yosei bar Zevida
These two Amoraim in Eretz Yisrael debated the reason: one said it engenders jealousy among God's creatures (implying only some merit mercy); the other said it makes mitzvot seem like mere compassion rather than divine commands.
Both Reasons Agree The prayer is silenced regardless — the debate concerns which theological problem is primary.

🌟 Rabbi Ḥanina: The Danger of Excessive Praise

כלום שיבחת את רבך? — Have you exhausted all the praises of your Master? (Berakhot 33b)
Restraint in Prayer A prayer leader recited an expanded list of divine praises: "the great, the mighty, the awesome, the powerful, the strong, the fearless..." Rabbi Ḥanina said: "Have you concluded all the praises of your Master?" Even the three standard epithets — great, mighty, awesome — we only recite because Moses wrote them in the Torah and the Men of the Great Assembly instituted them. Without that authorization, we could not say even those. It is like a king who possesses mountains of gold, being praised for owning silver. Listing finite praises actually diminishes God by implying His greatness can be enumerated.

🗣️ Coarse Language: The Rule of Refined Public Reading

Torah and Prophets sometimes contain coarse or crude language
↓
General Rule: All verses written coarsely are read aloud using refined substitutes
↓
Example: yishgalena — "shall violate her" (Deut 28:30) → read as yishkavena, "shall lie with her"
↓
Example: lemoḥra'ot — "latrines" (II Kings 10:27) → read as lemotza'ot, a refined equivalent
↓
Exception — Idolatry: Contemptuous or crude terms for idols are read as written
↓
Rav Naḥman: All mockery is forbidden except mockery of idol worship — prophets themselves mocked idols (Isaiah 46:1: "Bel bows down, Nevo stoops")
