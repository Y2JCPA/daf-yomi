⚔️ Rav vs Shmuel: Dividing a 5-Verse Paragraph

Rav
When two readers must split a 5-verse paragraph, the second reader repeats the final verse read by the first. Both readers thus complete the 3-verse minimum.
Shmuel
The first reader splits the third verse — reading 2½ verses — so that the second reader begins cleanly and also reads 2½ verses.
Objector
Can we even divide a verse? Rabbi Ḥananya Kara said he needed special permission from Rabbi Ḥanina each time he divided a verse for schoolchildren!
Gemara
For schoolchildren it is permitted because there is no other option. Here too — when a 5-verse paragraph must be split between readers — it is equally unavoidable, and therefore permitted.
Baraita
Objection: A 5-verse paragraph should be read by a single reader. If the first read 3, the second has only 2 — and the solution is to continue reading more verses from the next paragraph.
Gemara
The baraita is different — there, adding verses is possible. On the New Moon the next paragraph covers a completely different topic, making continuation impossible. Rav and Shmuel address only that case.
Ruling (Rav Yosef) The halakha follows Rav: the second reader repeats a verse. And the verse-division solution applies only where no alternative exists.

🏛️ Rav's Incident on a Public Fast in Babylonia

Rav arrives in Babylonia on a public fast day and is called to read from the Torah
↓
He recites a blessing before reading — standard practice
↓
He does not recite a blessing after reading — an unusual departure
↓
Attempted proof: Since Rav read the 3rd portion (Israelite), the fast had only 3 readers
↓
Rejected: Rav read first — the leading authority of his generation precedes even priests in the Torah reading order
↓
No closing blessing: Rav read first; no one left early out of deference to him, so latecomers heard everything and needed no separate conclusion
↓
During prayer, Rav did not prostrate himself — the stone floor directly before him prohibited it (Lev. 26:1)

🙇 Three Forms of Bowing in Halakha

קִידָּה · Kidda

  • Falling on one's face toward the ground
  • Source: Bathsheba before King David — "וַתִּקֹּד בַּת־שֶׁבַע" (1 Kings 1:31)
  • Performed by the High Priest on Yom Kippur
  • Levi demonstrated it for Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi — and became lame
  • Extremely difficult: bending at the waist until forehead touches ground while balancing on thumbs

כְּפִיפָה · Kefifah

  • Bending / inclining the head
  • A lighter, more common form of bowing
  • Source: "כָּל־בֶּרֶךְ תִּכְרַע" — every knee shall bow (Isa. 45:23)
  • Associated with kneeling in the prayer service
  • Used for everyday acts of reverence and honor

הִשְׁתַּחֲוָיָה · Hishtachavayah

  • Full prostration — arms and legs outstretched on the ground
  • Source: "בֹּאוּ נִשְׁתַּחֲוֶה" — Come, let us bow down (Ps. 95:6)
  • Rav's personal custom — he would not perform a lesser form
  • Prohibited on a stone floor outside the Temple (Lev. 26:1)
  • An important person (אדם חשוב) may not do this unless God will answer him (R. Elazar)

🪨 Why Rav Did Not Prostrate — Four Answers

אָדָם חָשׁוּב שָׁאנֵי — An important person is different (22b)
Halakhic Principle Answer 1: The stone section of the floor was directly in front of Rav. The Torah (Lev. 26:1) prohibits bowing on a figured stone floor outside the Temple. The rest of the congregation stood on regular flooring.

Answer 2: He did not want to trouble the congregation to make room so he could move to a non-stone area.

Answer 3: He refused to perform a partial prostration. His custom was full hishtachavayah with arms and legs extended; he would not alter it, and where he stood he could not do so fully.

Answer 4 (Rabbi Elazar): An important person may not fall on his face in public unless he is as confident God will answer him as Yehoshua was (Josh. 7:6) — a standard Rav did not presume to meet.
