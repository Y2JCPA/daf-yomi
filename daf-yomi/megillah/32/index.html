📜 How to Read from the Torah Scroll

Open the scroll and see the place from which you will read
↓
Rabbi Meir: Close the scroll before blessing — lest onlookers think the blessing is written in the Torah (Ulla's reason)
↓
Recite the blessing over the Torah reading
↓
Rabbi Yehuda: Bless over an open scroll — no concern about confusion with blessings, only with the Aramaic Targum
↓
Rav Mattana (final halakha): Open → see place → bless immediately → read. No need to close and reopen.

🗣️ Reciting the Blessing: Open or Closed Scroll?

Rabbi Meir
Close the scroll before reciting the blessing. Reason (Ulla): if blessed over an open scroll, onlookers may think the blessing text is actually written in the Torah itself.
Rabbi Yehuda
Bless over an open scroll — no problem. People confuse Aramaic translation with Torah text, but they will not confuse a blessing with Torah text. There is no concern here.
Rav Mattana (via Rabbi Zeira)
Halakha: Open the scroll, see the place, recite the blessing right then — no closing required. The earlier statement didn't need repeating, but it was stated explicitly to prevent confusion about which sage holds which view.
Why Rabbi Zeira Restated It Some had reversed the opinions, attributing Rabbi Meir's view to Rabbi Yehuda and vice versa. Rabbi Zeira stated the halakha explicitly to eliminate ambiguity.

🌀 Proper Technique for Rolling a Torah Scroll (Rabbi Yoḥanan)

Close on the seam: Furl the scroll so it closes on the seam between two sheets of parchment — the seam lands between the two rolls for structural integrity
↓
Roll from the outside: Position rollers parallel to you; rotate the far roller toward you to advance to the desired section
↓
Tighten from the inside: Once the section is found, tighten using the near roller — not the far one
↓
Why? Do not extend your arm past the Torah scroll — reaching beyond it shows disrespect for the sacred text
↓
The greatest person present furls the scroll after the reading — this is the most distinguished honor of all

🎵 Torah Reading — With Melody vs. Without

With Melody ✓

  • Torah read aloud with traditional cantillation (ta'amei ha-mikra)
  • Mishna studied with a sing-song tone (niggun)
  • Demonstrates love and vitality in Torah engagement
  • The melody aids memory and signals the text's sacred character

Without Melody ✗

  • Rabbi Yoḥanan: Verse applies — 'statutes not good, judgments whereby they should not live' (Ezekiel 20:25)
  • Abaye objects: Punish someone who simply cannot carry a tune?!
  • Rav Mesharshiya reinterprets: Verse targets one who does not explain the reasons behind the laws — not about musical ability
  • Depth of engagement matters more than vocal performance

🤲 Holding an Uncovered Torah Scroll — Consequence

Person holds Torah scroll directly on the parchment — without a cloth cover or any intervening material between hand and scroll
↓
Rabbi Parnakh said Rabbi Yoḥanan said:
Will be buried uncovered
↓
Gemara challenges: Surely you don't mean literally buried without a shroud — every person has that right! What does 'buried uncovered' really mean?
↓
First reading
Buried without the merit of his mitzvot protecting him — he squandered the Torah's protection
Abaye's explanation
Buried without the Torah advocating for him — the scroll he disrespected cannot speak in his defense before the Heavenly Court
