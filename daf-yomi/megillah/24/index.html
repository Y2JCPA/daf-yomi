📖 Torah vs. Prophets: Reading and Translation Rules

Torah

  • One verse at a time to the translator
  • Cannot skip from section to section
  • Minimum: three verses (correspond to Torah, Prophets, Writings)
  • Error could cause practical halachic mistakes
  • Exception: High Priest on Yom Kippur — two sections, same topic

Prophets (Haftara)

  • Three verses at a time to the translator
  • May skip from place to place within a book
  • Cannot skip between different prophetic books
  • May skip within the Twelve Minor Prophets
  • Condition: translator must not finish before reader resumes

🎗️ Why Does the Haftara Reader Receive Extra Honors?

Rav Pappa
It is due to honor — since even minors can read the haftara, the role was considered insufficient, so the rabbis granted additional honors to make it feel dignified.
Rabba bar Shimi
It is to prevent quarreling — the congregation might dispute who gets the honor of leading prayers or passing before the ark, so these roles are automatically given to the haftara reader.
Gemara Challenge
If the reason is honor — why does a minor's father or teacher pass before the ark in his place? Does a minor require honor to be preserved?
Rabba bar Shimi
Even for a minor: it prevents his father or teacher from quarreling with others who might want those same honors.
Practical Difference They disagree when the reader passes before the ark for free (no payment). According to Rav Pappa there is still a need to honor him; according to Rabba bar Shimi, with no payment there is nothing to quarrel over.

🕯️ The Blind Man with a Torch

כָּל יָמַי הָיִיתִי מִצְטַעֵר עַל מִקְרָא זֶה עַד שֶׁבָּא מַעֲשֶׂה לְיָדִי — רַבִּי יוֹסֵי
Aggadic Story — Rabbi Yosei Rabbi Yosei said: All my life I was troubled by a verse — until one dark night I saw a blind man walking with a torch. I asked: 'Why do you need a torch if you cannot see?' He replied: 'As long as I carry the torch, others can see me and will save me from pits and thorns.'

From this, Rabbi Yosei understood: even a blind person benefits from light — not directly, but because others see him and guide him to safety. Therefore, the Rabbis ruled that a blind person may recite blessings over the luminaries and lead the prayer service.

✋ Priestly Benediction: Grounds for Disqualification

Visible blemishes on face, hands, or feet — the congregation will stare at the priest and be distracted from prayer
↓
Colored or stained hands (e.g., dyer) — draws undue attention; permitted if most of the town shares the same occupation and is accustomed to it
↓
Speech impairment — Priests from Haifa or Beit She'an who cannot pronounce guttural letters (aleph, ayin, chet, heh) may not duchen
↓
Chronically watery eyes — Rav Huna disqualifies; local custom may override if congregation is already accustomed to the priest
↓
Blind in one eye — Rabbi Yochanan disqualifies because people will gaze; again, local familiarity creates an exception
↓
General rule: Anything causing the congregation to gaze at the priest during Duchening is disqualifying — unless they are already accustomed to him
