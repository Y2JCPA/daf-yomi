QUESTION 1 OF 5
What is the key difference between a vow offering (נֶדֶר) and a gift offering (נְדָבָה) if the animal dies before it is sacrificed?
A. Both require the owner to bring a replacement animal
B. The vow offering must be replaced; the gift offering need not be
C. The gift offering must be replaced; the vow offering need not be
D. Neither requires replacement if the animal died of natural causes
Correct: B — A vow offering binds the person — 'it is incumbent upon me' — so if the animal dies, the person must bring another. A gift offering consecrates a specific animal, so once that animal is gone, the obligation lapses with it.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does Rabbi Simai derive from Leviticus 15 that two emissions are sufficient to establish full zav status and impurity?
A. From the word 'all' in 'all the days of his emission'
B. From a gezeira shava between zav and niddah
C. From the double mention of the word 'emission' in two consecutive verses
D. From the phrase 'from his emission' (מִזּוֹבוֹ) appearing twice
Correct: C — Rabbi Simai counts the word 'emission' twice in Leviticus 15:2–3: 'due to his emission' and 'when his flesh runs with his emission.' Each mention corresponds to one discharge, establishing two emissions as the threshold for full zav impurity.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What does the phrase 'from his emission' (מִזּוֹבוֹ) in Leviticus 15:13 teach, according to the Gemara's conclusion?
A. That a three-emission zav must bring a korban
B. That a zav counts his seven days once he is clean from his emission, not from concurrent leprosy
C. That a two-emission zav is exempt from counting seven clean days
D. That the seven-day count begins from the first emission
Correct: B — Abaye explains that the phrase 'from his emission' is superfluous if it excludes the two-emission zav, since the Torah could simply stay silent. Therefore it teaches a positive inclusion: the zav counts his seven days when clean from his emission, not from any co-occurring leprosy.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In what way are a quarantined leper and a confirmed leper treated identically?
A. Both must let their hair grow and rend their garments
B. Both are expelled from the camp and are ritually impure
C. Both must bring two birds as part of their purification
D. Both must shave all body hair when they are purified
Correct: B — The mishna teaches that both a quarantined and a confirmed leper are expelled from all three camps and both are fully ritually impure. The differences only emerge in the obligations of hair, garments, shaving, and birds — which apply only to the confirmed leper.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What does Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel hold regarding which languages are permitted for writing Torah scrolls?
A. Any language is permitted, exactly like the Tanna Kamma rules
B. Only Hebrew (Ashurit) is permitted; no other language is valid
C. Only Greek is permitted besides Hebrew; all other languages are invalid
D. Aramaic and Greek are both permitted alongside Hebrew
Correct: C — Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel restricts the broader ruling: while the Tanna Kamma theoretically permits any language, in practice the Sages only permitted Torah scrolls to be written in Greek (besides Hebrew), likely because of Greek's precision and the tradition of the Septuagint translation.
