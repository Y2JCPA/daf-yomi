Megillah 26 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
If a community sells their Torah scroll, what may they do with the proceeds?
A. Purchase scrolls of Prophets and Writings
B. Purchase a new synagogue building
C. The proceeds may not fund any lesser-sanctity purchase
D. Purchase an ark cover or Torah wrapping
Correct: C — A Torah scroll holds the highest sanctity. Its proceeds may not be used to purchase anything of lesser sanctity — not Prophets/Writings, not a synagogue, not ark covers. The principle is absolute: one may never demote in sanctity.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani, which type of synagogue may be sold?
A. Only a city synagogue, since it has defined communal ownership
B. Only a village synagogue, since it belongs to its villagers alone
C. Any synagogue, provided the local rabbi approves the sale
D. Only synagogues in Jerusalem, which belong to all Israel
Correct: B — Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani restricts the ability to sell to village synagogues only. Village synagogues belong solely to the villagers who built them. City synagogues, by contrast, belong to all of Israel and cannot be sold.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Menaḥem bar Yosei hold that the town square has sanctity?
A. It is adjacent to the synagogue and shares its holiness by proximity
B. It was formally donated to the community for sacred use
C. People pray there on communal fast days and on non-priestly watch days
D. It is municipal property and thus consecrated to all Israel
Correct: C — Rabbi Menaḥem bar Yosei reasons that since the town square is used for prayer on fast days and on the days of the non-priestly watch (ma'amad), it qualifies as a designated place of prayer and therefore bears sanctity — making it subject to the rules governing the sale of sacred spaces.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Rava rules that a synagogue may be sold or exchanged — but what does he prohibit?
A. Selling it to a non-Jew without rabbinic approval
B. Renting it out or using it as collateral for a mortgage
C. Using the proceeds to purchase mundane household items
D. Demolishing the structure before completing the sale
Correct: B — Rava prohibits renting or mortgaging because under those arrangements the building continues to exist as a synagogue while simultaneously being used for secular purposes — its sacred status never fully departs. A sale or exchange, by contrast, fully and finally transfers the item out of the sacred domain.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What does Rava rule regarding the materials of an ark (aron kodesh) that has fallen apart?
A. The materials may be freely used for any sacred or secular purpose
B. They must be buried immediately alongside a Torah scroll
C. They may be made into a smaller ark, but not into a lectern
D. They may be made into a lectern, but not into a smaller ark
Correct: C — Rava rules that the materials of a disassembled ark may be used to construct a smaller ark — the same level of sanctity — but may not be fashioned into a lectern, which has a lesser degree of sanctity. This follows the universal principle that one may not demote sacred objects to a lower sanctity level.
