📖 Four Opinions: Where Must Megilla Reading Begin?

Rabbi Meir

  • Read the entire Megilla
  • From 'In the days of Ahasuerus'
  • 'Saw' = Ahasuerus used Temple vessels

Rabbi Yehuda

  • From 'There was a certain Jew'
  • Esther 2:5 — Mordecai introduced
  • 'Saw' = Mordecai's zealousness vs. Haman

Rabbi Yosei

  • From 'After these things'
  • Esther 3:1 — Haman elevated
  • 'Saw' = Haman's fury at non-prostration

R. Shimon bar Yoḥai

  • From 'On that night'
  • Esther 6:1 — the turning point
  • 'Saw' = Ahasuerus read the chronicles

🏘️ Rava vs. Abaye: The Traveling Megilla Reader

Rava
A traveler reads by his home city's custom only if he is destined to return home on the night of the fourteenth. A villager who went to a town reads with the townspeople — even if he already read in his village — out of respect for them.
Abaye
Objection from a baraita: A walled-city resident in an unwalled town reads by his permanent home's custom in all circumstances — whether or not he will return! This contradicts your ruling.
Rava
But you yourself admitted that baraita requires emendation. Since it needs revision anyway, emend it further: he reads with the residents of the town.
Halakha If returning home on the night of the 14th → read by home custom. If not returning → read with the local community.

📚 The Megilla as Both 'Book' and 'Letter'

Rav Ḥelbo citing Rav Ḥama bar Gurya citing Rav: The halakha follows reading the entire Megilla from beginning to end.
↓
The Megilla is called a sefer (book) — like a Torah scroll, this implies it must be written on parchment with ink in Ashurit script.
↓
The Megilla is also called an iggeret (letter) — a letter can be written on any material, suggesting lower requirements.
↓
Resolution: Both names apply. Written on parchment is required l'khatchila; but sections not on parchment are valid b'dieved after the fact.
↓
Shmuel: If one reads from a Megilla written together with the rest of the Writings — obligation is not fulfilled. The Megilla must stand alone as a distinct scroll.

🔍 Two Amoraic Approaches to Deriving the Starting Point

Rabbi Yoḥanan's Method

  • All four Tannaim expound one verse: Esther 9:26
  • 'Acts of power' (כֹּחַ) — each finds a different subject
  • R. Meir: power of Ahasuerus (Temple vessels)
  • R. Yehuda: power of Mordecai (zealousness)
  • R. Yosei: power of Haman (elevation & fury)
  • R. Shimon: power of the miracle night itself

Rav Huna's Method

  • Expounds Esther 9:26: 'that which they saw'
  • R. Meir: Ahasuerus 'saw' — used Temple vessels; calculated exile wrongly
  • R. Yehuda: Mordecai 'saw' — his zealousness; Haman's self-elevation
  • R. Yosei: Haman 'saw' — fury at Mordecai's non-bow
  • R. Shimon: Ahasuerus 'saw' — commanded chronicles to be read; Esther's invitation
