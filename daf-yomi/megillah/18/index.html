🌍 Which Languages May the Megilla Be Read In?

Rav
The Megilla may be read in Greek — but not in other foreign languages. Greek alone is permitted as an alternate.
Shmuel
I agree with Rav. Greek has a special status — other languages do not.
Baraita Objection
A baraita teaches: one may read in Coptic to Copts, in Hebrew to Hebrews, in Elamite to Elamites, in Median to Medes — any language works for native speakers!
Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel
Among all languages, only Greek was singled out as suitable — the Torah was legitimately translated into Greek (LXX). Other translations lack this status.
Gemara Resolution
Rav and Shmuel follow Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. They did not need to state 'the halakha follows him' because they issued it as their own ruling — which amounts to the same thing.
Key Ruling A foreign-language speaker who hears the Megilla read in Hebrew still fulfills their obligation — even without understanding, since comprehension is not required (Ravina).

🔗 Why Are These Blessings Adjacent? — The Logic of Adjacency

Why does Birkat David follow Birkat Yerushalayim?
↓
Hosea 3:5 — "the children of Israel shall return and seek the Lord their God and David their king"
King and city are linked in redemption
↓
Why does Birkat Kohanim follow Temple Service, not precede it?
↓
Leviticus 9:22 — Aaron blessed the people and then descended from the altar
The verse means: he blessed them BECAUSE he had just sacrificed
↓
Why does Shalom follow Birkat Kohanim?
↓
Numbers 6:27 — "they shall put My name upon the children of Israel"
After the priests invoke God's name, peace (God's name = shalom) follows naturally

📖 Valid vs. Invalid Megilla Reading

✅ Obligation Fulfilled

  • Read from a written scroll with intent
  • Read in Hebrew (Ashurit) to anyone
  • Read in Greek to Greek speakers
  • Read at intervals (seirugin) — pausing briefly
  • Read while drowsy (not fully asleep)
  • Read while copying from a complete scroll
  • Heard in Hebrew even without understanding

❌ Obligation NOT Fulfilled

  • Read entirely by heart (no text)
  • Read in Aramaic translation
  • Read in other foreign languages (per Rav & Shmuel)
  • Read out of order (words or verses)
  • Paused long enough to finish entire Megilla (per Rabbi Mona / Rav)
  • Scribe omitted verses — reader filled in from memory
  • Read from blurred letters with no visible imprint

🤫 The Paradox of Praising God

לְךָ דֻמִיָּה תְהִלָּה (תהלים סה:ב) — "For You, silence is praise"
Rabbi Yehuda of Kefar Gibboraya "For You, silence is praise" (Psalms 65:2) — The best remedy is silence. Like one who praises a priceless gem with too many words and only diminishes it — listing God's praises exhaustively implies you have finished them. Rabba bar bar Ḥana said in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan: One who excessively praises the Holy One, Blessed be He, is liable to be uprooted from the world. The 19 blessings of the Amida are a fixed ceiling — no additional blessings may be added.

⏸️ Seirugin — Reading the Megilla at Intervals

Word Origin Unknown: The Sages didn't know what seirugin meant — they learned it from Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's maidservant, who scolded a visitor entering the house intermittently: "How long will you come in seirugin seirugin?"
↓
Basic Rule (Mishna): One who reads the Megilla at intervals — pausing and resuming — has fulfilled the obligation.
↓
Out of Order: If one changes the sequence of words or verses, the obligation is not fulfilled — order is essential.
↓
Rabbi Mona's Stringency: Even when reading at intervals, if the pause is long enough to have finished reading the entire Megilla, the obligation is not fulfilled — the reading loses its continuity.
↓
Rav vs. Shmuel: Rav rules in accordance with Rabbi Mona (strict). Shmuel rules the halakha does NOT follow Rabbi Mona (lenient). The Gemara ultimately accepts Rav's stricter version, as supported by Rav Beivai's tradition.
