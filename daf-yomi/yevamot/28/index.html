📖 Overview

יבמות כ״ח — וְלֹא תִקַּח אִשָּׁה אֶל אֲחֹתָהּ
📖 Daf Overview The Gemara relentlessly challenges Rabbi Yoḥanan's claim that he cannot identify who authored the 'sisters' ruling — proposing five explanations in succession, each rejected. Rav Pappa then resolves the mother-in-law case through careful sequencing. Three Amoraim (Rav Yehuda, Abaye, Rav Safra) debate how broadly the sister principle applies, culminating in Rav Safra's intricate six-brother scenario. The daf closes with a new mishna on three brothers where two married sisters, and Rabbi Shimon's sweeping exemption derived from Leviticus 18:18.

🤔 Five Challenges to Rabbi Yoḥanan — All Rejected

Challenger
Let Rabbi Yoḥanan say the ruling is a rabbinic decree lest the second brother die — then it would be forbidden to negate levirate marriage!
Gemara
Rejected: that reason only justifies ḥalitza retroactively. But the mishna implies ḥalitza is the first course of action — ab initio.
Challenger
Let him say the mishna follows Rabbi Elazar: once she stood before him as forbidden, she is always forbidden — even later!
Gemara
Rejected: Rabbi Yoḥanan does not rule in accordance with Rabbi Elazar on this matter.
Challenger
Let him say the brothers died simultaneously — both women fell before the yevamin at the same moment!
Gemara
Rejected: the mishna's latter clause — 'if they married, they must divorce' — becomes inexplicable. Why would the marriages need undoing in this scenario?
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Conclusion: 'I do not know who taught: Sisters' — I cannot properly resolve the ruling of this mishna.
Key Insight The Gemara's inability to resolve the mishna is itself the point — Rabbi Yoḥanan's admission of uncertainty becomes a talmudic teaching.

👩‍👩‍👧 Rav Pappa: How Both Sisters Become Forbidden

Two brothers each married a sister
Sister A = not the mother-in-law | Sister B = the mother-in-law
↓
Both brothers die → both sisters fall before the surviving yevamin for levirate marriage
↓
✅ Sister A (non-mother-in-law) falls FIRST
Permitted to both yevamin
→ Either may consummate levirate marriage with her
❌ Sister B (mother-in-law) falls SECOND
Forbidden to both yevamin as mother-in-law
→ Now her rival wife (Sister A) is also forbidden
↓
Both women are exempt from levirate marriage — ḥalitza only
The mother-in-law's forbidden status retroactively closes the door on her sister

📚 Three Amoraim: How Broadly Does the Sister Principle Apply?

Rav Yehuda

  • Applies from mother-in-law and onward in the list only
  • Excludes the first 6 cases: his daughter, wife's daughter, and their descendants
  • Reason: two sisters who are also daughters of both yevamin cannot occur in those 6 cases via marriage
  • Cases of rape excluded entirely

Abaye

  • Applies even to daughter born from a raped woman
  • The scenario is findable even in cases of rape
  • Principle: if the statement can encompass rape cases, it should — prefer the broader rule
  • More expansive than Rav Yehuda

Rav Safra

  • Applies even to wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist
  • Findable via the six-brothers scenario (Reuven, Shimon, Levi, Yehuda, Yissakhar…)
  • Requires the opinion of Rabbi Shimon
  • Most expansive application of all three

👨‍👩‍👧 New Mishna: Three Brothers, Two Married Sisters

Setup: Three brothers — two married two sisters (or a woman and her daughter), the third married an unrelated woman
↓
Both husbands die: The two sisters fall before the third brother for levirate marriage
↓
Default ruling: Both sisters are forbidden to the third brother — each is a rival wife's sister. Both perform ḥalitza and may not enter levirate marriage
↓
Exception — forbidden relative: If one sister is forbidden due to a prohibited relative (e.g., she is his own relative), the third brother is forbidden to her but permitted to marry her sister via levirate
↓
Rabbi Shimon disagrees: Derives from "You shall not take a woman to her sister" (Lev. 18:18) — even where one sister is only rabbinically forbidden, he treats her as a full rival wife, exempting both sisters from ḥalitza and levirate entirely
↓
Practical ruling (Sages): The permitted sister performs ḥalitza or enters levirate marriage; Rabbi Shimon: she is fully exempt
