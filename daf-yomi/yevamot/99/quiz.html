QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why must the mixed-up sons perform ḥalitza with BOTH women rather than just one?
A. Because the Torah requires double ḥalitza in all cases
B. Because it is unknown which woman is their mother and which is their yevama
C. Because both women requested ḥalitza from the beit din
D. Because the sons are mamzerim and need extra ḥalitza
Correct: B — Since the babies were mixed at birth, each son doesn't know which woman is his biological mother (forbidden for yibum) and which is his yevama (requiring ḥalitza), so he must perform ḥalitza with both to cover the uncertainty.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to R' Meir, what makes the fourth child a mamzer?
A. The child was born before the parents' conversion
B. The maidservant was freed (making her a Jewess) but the father is still a slave
C. The child was born from two freed slaves
D. The master did not properly immerse the parents
Correct: B — R' Meir holds that when a slave has relations with a Jewish woman (the freed maidservant now has the status of a Jewess), the offspring is a mamzer — similar to a child born from a forbidden relationship.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why can the certain sons of the ELDER woman (mother-in-law) perform yibum, while the daughter-in-law's certain sons cannot?
A. The elder woman's sons are kohanim and have priority
B. For the elder woman's sons, the widow is either a brother's wife (yibum valid) or nephew's wife (permitted) — neither is forbidden
C. The daughter-in-law's sons are too young for yibum
D. The elder woman gave special permission for yibum
Correct: B — For the elder woman's certain sons, each widow is either a brother's wife (yibum applies) or a brother's son's wife (permitted after ḥalitza) — neither option involves a forbidden relative. But for the daughter-in-law's sons, the widow might be a father's brother's wife, which is forbidden.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the core dispute between R' Yehuda and R' Yosei regarding teruma distribution to a slave?
A. Whether slaves are permitted to eat teruma at all
B. Whether receiving teruma publicly creates a presumption of priestly status in that locale
C. Whether the master must physically hand teruma to the slave
D. Whether teruma from the granary is different from teruma from the field
Correct: B — In R' Yehuda's locale, seeing someone receive teruma created a presumption (ḥazakah) of priesthood, so a slave alone might be mistakenly elevated to priestly status. In R' Yosei's locale, no such presumption existed, so a slave could receive teruma independently.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does a firstborn animal belonging to a mixed priest/slave offspring 'graze until it gets a blemish'?
A. Because blemished animals are more valuable at market
B. Because sacrificing it would give it to priests, but once blemished it cannot be taken from him since he might be a priest himself
C. Because the animal needs time to grow before sacrifice
D. Because only blemished animals can be eaten by non-priests
Correct: B — If sacrificed unblemished, the animal goes to the priests — against the owner's interest if he IS a priest. Once blemished, it can't be sacrificed and can't be appropriated from him (since he might be a priest), so it becomes his private property to eat.
