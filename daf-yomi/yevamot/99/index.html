Daf 99: Identity Uncertainty & Its Halakhic Consequences

Babies born in hiding — identities mixed up
↓
Which relatives are they? Mother? Sister? Daughter?
↓
Ḥalitza Cases
Perform ḥalitza with both women due to safek
Yibum Blocked
No levirate marriage — might be a forbidden relative
Key Theme When identities are uncertain, we apply stringencies of all possible statuses — ḥalitza resolves the bond without risking forbidden unions.

Ḥalitza with One's Mother Due to Uncertainty

Two mothers each have a known son, then each gives birth in hiding — babies get mixed
↓
Known son of Woman A marries Woman B (mother of known son B), and vice versa
↓
Both known sons die childless
↓
Which widow is the mixed son's mother vs. his yevama?
↓
Resolution
Each mixed son performs ḥalitza with both women
Result
Each one ends up performing ḥalitza with his own mother due to safek
Principle The same pattern applies to ḥalitza with one's sister (mixed female babies, paternal half-brothers marry them) and one's daughter (mixed female babies, brothers marry them).

R' Meir: Five Nations from One Couple

1️⃣ Gentile — slave & maidservant have a child before immersion
↓
2️⃣ Convert — one of those children converts to Judaism
↓
3️⃣ Slave — master immerses both parents; they have a child with slave status
↓
4️⃣ Mamzer — master frees maidservant (now a Jewess); slave has relations with her → child is mamzer (per R' Meir)
↓
5️⃣ Regular Jew — master frees both, marries them; their new child is fully Jewish
R' Meir's Principle A gentile or slave who has relations with a Jewish woman — their offspring is a mamzer. This view underlies the entire teaching.

A Son Sells His Own Father

מוכר אדם את אביו לגבות כתובת אמו
Unusual Case A Jew buys a slave and maidservant. They have a son. The master frees the maidservant and marries her. He then writes all his property — including the slave — to her son.

The son now owns his own father (the slave). If the mother demands her ketubah, the son may need to sell his father to pay it.

This teaches that according to R' Meir, movable property (including slaves) can be mortgaged for a ketubah.

Mixed Offspring: Mother-in-Law vs. Daughter-in-Law's Sons

Certain Sons of Daughter-in-Law

  • Perform ḥalitza only with mixed sons' widows
  • Cannot do yibum — widow might be father's brother's wife
  • Each widow is safek sister-in-law or uncle's wife

Certain Sons of Elder Woman

  • Can perform ḥalitza OR yibum
  • Widow is either brother's wife (yibum valid) or nephew's wife (permitted)
  • More lenient because neither possibility is a forbidden relative
Rava Pappa's Correction The mixed-up status does not render the sons unfit — the text should read 'if the certain sons died,' not implying any disqualification.
