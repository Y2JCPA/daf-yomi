🏛️ Does the Father's Presence Override Yibbum Inheritance?

Sages
One who performs yibbum inherits his deceased brother's entire estate. The Torah commands: "He shall succeed in the name of his dead brother" — the yavam steps fully into his brother's place, regardless of whether the father lives.
Rabbi Yehuda
Whether he performs yibbum or ḥalitza, if the father is still alive — the father inherits. The yavam is like a firstborn son: a firstborn takes his double portion only after the father's death, not while the father lives.
Ulla / R. Yitzḥak Nappaḥa
The verse reads "in the name of his dead brother" — he succeeds his brother, not his father. This teaches: when the father is alive, normal inheritance rules apply and the father comes first.
Sages (rebuttal)
The mitzva of levirate marriage is never made dependent on inheritance. The yavam must always perform yibbum — and then the inheritance is resolved. The mitzva cannot be withheld because the father is present.
Halakhic Ruling Rabbi Yannai dismissed Rabbi Ḥanina Kara's citation of Rabbi Yehuda: 'Leave the study hall and recite your verses outside!' The halakha follows the Sages — yibbum grants full inheritance regardless of the father's status.

🔍 Do Secondary Rabbinic Prohibitions (Shniyot) Apply to Ḥalutza?

The Dilemma: Sages decreed shniyot (secondary forbidden relationships) to guard Torah-level prohibitions. But ḥalitza is only a rabbinic quasi-marriage — do shniyot extend beyond a rabbinically-created status?
↓
Position A: Shniyot apply only when the underlying relationship is Torah-forbidden. A ḥalutza's status is entirely rabbinic — so shniyot should not apply to her relatives.
↓
Position B: Once the Sages enacted the decree treating ḥalitza like divorce, all associated prohibitions fully follow — including secondary forbidden relatives (shniyot).
↓
Proof attempts: The Gemara examines each relative in the mishna and baraita (grandfather, grandson, mother's father, daughter's son). Each time: is the prohibition due to the ḥalitza-decree, or due to the original marriage with the deceased?
↓
Result: Every case is explained as deriving from the original marriage — the Gemara cannot find a clean proof that shniyot independently stem from ḥalitza status. The question remains technically unresolved (teiku-adjacent).

⚔️ Rival Wife (Tzara) of Ḥalutza — Permitted or Forbidden?

✅ Relative of Rival Wife — PERMITTED

  • The rival wife was never the yavam's own ḥalutza
  • She has no direct connection to his ḥalitza ceremony
  • Her relatives are therefore not restricted
  • Rav Yosef: Infers from mishna — rival wife is treated as an outsider
  • The mishna explicitly states: "permitted to a relative of her rival wife"

❌ Rival Wife of Ḥalutza's Relative — FORBIDDEN

  • The ḥalutza's relative is forbidden to the yavam
  • The rival wife of that relative shares her status
  • Rav Tovi bar Rav Kisna ruled this forbidden before Rava
  • The rival wife is drawn into the prohibition through the relative
  • Mishna contrast: rival wife ≠ stranger when her co-wife is already forbidden
