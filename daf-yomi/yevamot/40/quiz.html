Yevamot 40 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Sages (the accepted halakha), when does a yavam inherit his deceased brother's full estate?
A. Always — whether he performs yibbum or ḥalitza
B. When he performs yibbum, regardless of whether the father is still alive
C. Only when he performs yibbum AND the father is already deceased
D. Never — the estate is always divided equally among all the brothers
Correct: B — The Sages hold that performing yibbum grants the yavam full inheritance of his brother's estate, period. Rabbi Yehuda added the condition that the father must be deceased — but the Sages reject this. The father's presence does not affect the yavam's inheritance rights when yibbum is performed.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What analogy do Ulla and Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa use to support Rabbi Yehuda's opinion that the father inherits when alive?
A. A hired laborer who steps aside when the property owner returns
B. A court guardian who transfers authority once the heir comes of age
C. A firstborn son who receives his double portion only after the father's death
D. A slave whose freedom is contingent on his master having no living heirs
Correct: C — The verse compares the yavam to a firstborn: 'He shall succeed in the name of his dead brother.' Just as a firstborn receives his special double portion only after the father dies, so too the yavam's inheritance rights activate only when there is no living father. The Sages counter that this comparison doesn't override the mitzva itself.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
After a yavam performs ḥalitza, he is forbidden to marry the ḥalutza's mother. By what authority does this prohibition apply?
A. Torah law — she is a direct relative of his former wife
B. Rabbinic decree — ḥalitza is treated as equivalent to actual divorce
C. Both Torah and rabbinic law simultaneously
D. It is actually permitted, since ḥalitza never created a real marital bond
Correct: B — The mishna establishes that by rabbinic decree, ḥalitza is treated 'as though she had been married to him and then he divorced her.' All the resulting forbidden relationships — her mother, daughter, his father, his son — derive from this rabbinic enactment, not from Torah law.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
The Gemara repeatedly tries to prove from the mishna and baraita that secondary prohibitions (shniyot) apply to ḥalutza. What happens to each proof?
A. Each proof is accepted, confirming that shniyot fully apply to ḥalutza
B. Each proof is rejected — the prohibition is explained as stemming from the original marriage with the deceased, not from the ḥalitza
C. Each proof is accepted for women but rejected for men
D. The proofs are inconclusive because the mishna predates the shniyot decree
Correct: B — Every time the Gemara tries to prove shniyot apply to ḥalutza, the proof is rejected: the forbidden relative (grandfather, grandson, etc.) is explained as being forbidden due to the ḥalutza's original Torah-level relationship with the deceased — not due to any independent application of shniyot to the ḥalitza status.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What does the mishna rule about a yavam's relationship with a rival wife (tzara) of a relative of his ḥalutza?
A. Permitted — the rival wife has no direct connection to the ḥalitza ceremony
B. Forbidden — she is drawn into the prohibition through her co-wife who is the ḥalutza's relative
C. Permitted only if the rival wife was married before the ḥalitza took place
D. Forbidden only if the yavam also performed ḥalitza with that rival wife
Correct: B — The mishna distinguishes two cases: a yavam is permitted to relatives of a rival wife of his ḥalutza (the rival wife is an outsider), but forbidden to a rival wife of a relative of his ḥalutza (she is drawn in through her forbidden co-wife). Rav Tovi bar Rav Kisna ruled this before Rava, and Rav Yosef confirmed it by inference from the mishna.
