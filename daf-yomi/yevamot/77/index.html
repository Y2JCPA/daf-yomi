David's Lineage Under Fire

Doeg
David descends from Ruth the Moabite — he is unfit to enter the congregation!
Amasa (Yithra)
I received a tradition from Shmuel's court in Rama: An Ammonite man is prohibited — not an Ammonite woman. Same for Moab.
Gemara
But Amasa is an interested party — his own status depends on this ruling! Can he be trusted?
Gemara
Shmuel and his court were still alive — the tradition could be verified independently.
KEY PRINCIPLE The Torah's exclusion of Ammon and Moab applies only to males, not females — saving David's royal lineage through Ruth the Moabite.

Why Are Ammonite/Moabite Women Permitted?

Babylonia
"The king's daughter is all glorious within" (Psalms 45:14) — women don't venture outside, so they couldn't have been expected to greet Israel.
Eretz Yisrael / R' Yitzchak
"Where is Sarah your wife? Behold, in the tent" (Genesis 18:9) — it is praiseworthy for a woman to remain at home.
R' Yehuda (Tanna)
The Torah says "Amoni" / "Moavi" — masculine form excludes women by grammatical derivation.
R' Shimon (Tanna)
"Because they did not meet you with bread and water" — it is the way of a man to go out and meet, not a woman.
TWO APPROACHES R' Yehuda derives the exclusion from the masculine grammar of the verse; R' Shimon derives it from the reason behind the prohibition — women wouldn't have gone out to greet travelers.
5 / 9

R' Yehuda vs. R' Shimon — Source of the Exemption

R' Yehuda — Textual

  • Derives from masculine form: עַמּוֹנִי / מוֹאָבִי
  • Grammatical exclusion of women
  • No need to explain the reason behind the prohibition
  • Aligns with Babylonian approach

R' Shimon — Logical

  • Derives from the reason: "they did not meet you"
  • It is the way of men to go out — not women
  • Women were not expected to provide hospitality
  • Aligns with Eretz Yisrael approach
PRACTICAL DIFFERENCE Both agree on the outcome: Ammonite and Moabite women may enter the congregation. They differ on the derivation — text vs. reason.

Rava: David's Psalms of Gratitude

פִּתַּחְתָּ לְמוֹסֵרָי — רות המואביה ונעמה העמונית
RAVA'S TEACHING "You have loosened my bands" (Psalms 116:16) — David praises Hashem for removing the two 'bands' that threatened his lineage:

🔹 Ruth the Moabite — David's great-grandmother
🔹 Na'ama the Ammonite — mother of Rechavam (Rehoboam), Solomon's son

"Your wonders and thoughts are upon us" (Psalms 40:6) — not 'upon me' but 'upon us' — David said this with Rechavam on his lap, as both were affected.

"In the scroll of a book it is written about me" (Psalms 40:8) — David's existence was hinted at from the daughters of Lot (Genesis 19:15): "your two daughters that are found" → "I have found David, My servant" (Psalms 89:21).

Can a Scholar's Tradition Be Trusted?

A Torah scholar issues a ruling based on a tradition that benefits himself
↓
Stated BEFORE the incident
He stated the ruling before it became personally relevant
Stated AFTER the incident
He stated it only after it was personally relevant
↓
✅ Trusted
We listen to him
❌ Not Trusted
We do not listen — he is a נוגע בדבר (interested party)
↓
Exception: Amasa was trusted because Shmuel's court was alive to verify independently
R' ABBA / RAV A scholar who reports a self-serving tradition is believed only if he stated it before it became relevant — unless it can be independently verified.
