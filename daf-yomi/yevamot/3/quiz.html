QUESTION 1 OF 5
What verbal analogy does Rava cite in the name of Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi to derive an exemption for certain relatives from levirate marriage?
A. The word 'zimmah' (wickedness) linking Lev. 18:17 and Lev. 20:14
B. The word 'hena' in the verse about a son's or daughter's daughter creating a link to the levirate context
C. The phrase 'erva' (nakedness) connecting the two passages about forbidden relations
D. The word 'brother' appearing in both Leviticus and Deuteronomy
Correct: B — Rava cites Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi: the word 'hena' in Lev. 18:10 (son's/daughter's daughter) creates a verbal analogy (gezeirah shavah) linking that forbidden-relations passage to the levirate context.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the Mishnah use 'exempt' (פְּטוּרוֹת) rather than 'prohibited' (אֲסוּרוֹת) to describe rival wives?
A. The rival wives are never actually forbidden and may freely enter levirate marriage
B. 'Prohibited' would wrongly imply they are also forbidden from performing ḥalitza, which is never truly prohibited
C. The Tanna used softer language to avoid stigmatizing the rival wives
D. Because the prohibition only takes effect after ḥalitza has already been performed
Correct: B — Using 'prohibited' would imply she is forbidden even from ḥalitza. But ḥalitza cannot be truly prohibited — if performed, nothing is violated. 'Exempt' more precisely captures that her obligation (and restriction) is simply non-existent.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What case does Rav add that he believes should be exempt from levirate marriage, which the Mishnah's enumeration of 'fifteen' serves to exclude?
A. The rival wife of a woman who performed refusal (mi'un)
B. The rival wife of an aylonit (infertile woman)
C. The rival wife of a sota (woman suspected of adultery)
D. The rival wife of a convert who married the deceased brother
Correct: C — Rav holds that the rival wife of a sota should also be exempt from levirate marriage. However, the Mishnah's specific count of fifteen excludes this position.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to the baraita, what does the phrase 'to be a rival to her' (לִצְרֹר) in Leviticus 18:18 specifically teach?
A. That the wife's sister prohibition applies only when there is genuine rivalry between the women
B. That the rival wife of a wife's sister is also exempt from levirate marriage
C. That levirate marriage creates rivalry between women and is therefore morally discouraged
D. That a woman may refuse levirate marriage if she is already the rival wife of a forbidden relative
Correct: B — The baraita derives from 'to be a rival to her' that not only is the wife's sister herself exempt from levirate marriage, but her rival wife is also exempt — the verse explicitly includes the rival.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why can the positive mitzva of levirate marriage NOT override the prohibition against marrying a wife's sister?
A. Because the prohibition against a wife's sister is of rabbinic origin and cannot be overridden even by a Torah mitzva
B. Because a positive mitzva can only override a prohibition punishable by lashes, not one punishable by karet
C. Because levirate marriage is itself only a rabbinic enactment and therefore carries less halakhic weight
D. Because the wife's sister prohibition was enacted specifically to prevent the levirate mitzva from applying
Correct: B — The Gemara (Amud B) establishes that a positive mitzva overrides a prohibition only when the prohibition is punishable by lashes alone. The wife's sister prohibition carries karet — a far more severe punishment — so the levirate mitzva cannot override it.
