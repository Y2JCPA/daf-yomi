⚔️ Why Does the Mishnah Say 'Exempt' and Not 'Prohibited'?

Gemara
Why does the Mishnah say rival wives are exempt? Let it say they are prohibited — they are certainly not permitted to enter into levirate marriage!
Gemara
If we wrote 'prohibited,' that would imply she is also prohibited from performing ḥalitza. But ḥalitza is never truly forbidden — even if performed, nothing is actually violated!
Gemara
Then write: she is prohibited from levirate marriage and exempt from ḥalitza — two separate clauses!
Gemara
The prohibition only arises where levirate marriage is relevant. Since a rival wife may sometimes be permitted (where no forbidden relation exists), 'exempt' more precisely conveys the conditional nature of the rule.
Conclusion 'Exempt' (פְּטוּרוֹת) captures both the absence of a levirate obligation AND the absence of a ḥalitza requirement — without falsely implying an absolute prohibition in every context.

🔗 The Gezeirah Shavah Chain: Two Verbal Analogies

Verbal Analogy #1 — hena: Rava cites Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi: the word hena in Lev. 18:10 (son's/daughter's daughter) creates a link to the levirate context
↓
Verbal Analogy #2 — zimmah (wickedness): Lev. 18:17 ↔ Lev. 20:14 — both use 'zimmah,' deriving the mother-in-law prohibition into the levirate framework
↓
Result: Levirate marriage applies only where a mitzva exists — 'uncover her nakedness with her' (Lev. 18:18) includes only cases of mitzva, not forbidden unions

🔢 What Does 'Fifteen Women' Exclude?

Rav's Addition

  • Rival wife of a sota (woman suspected of adultery)
  • The sota is forbidden to her husband by rabbinic/biblical rule
  • Rav held her rival wife should also be exempt from levirate marriage
  • Excluded by the Mishnah's enumeration of exactly fifteen

Rav Asi's Addition

  • Rival wife of an aylonit (infertile woman, signs of which appear at puberty)
  • An aylonit cannot fulfill levirate marriage's purpose of continuing the brother's name
  • Rav Asi held her rival should similarly be exempt
  • Excluded by the Mishnah's precise count of fifteen

The Mi'un Case

  • Rival wife of a woman who performed refusal (mi'un)
  • A minor bride may retroactively annul her marriage through refusal
  • Her rival wife is then not covered by the fifteen's exemption
  • One enumeration excludes this case if Rav and Rav Asi agree with each other

📋 Why Did the Tanna List Cases in This Specific Order?

Organizing Principle: The Tanna arranges cases by closeness of relationship to the yavam, moving from most intimate to more distant
↓
Group 1 — His own offspring (3 generations below): daughter → son's daughter → daughter's daughter
↓
Group 2 — Wife's offspring (parallel 3 generations): wife's daughter → wife's daughter's daughter → wife's son's daughter
↓
Group 3 — Wife's ascending relatives: mother-in-law → mother-in-law's mother → father-in-law's mother
↓
Group 4 — His own blood relatives: sister → mother's sister → father's sister (more distant than daughter)
↓
Group 5 — Sister-adjacent cases: wife's sister grouped here because the Tanna was already discussing sisters; brother's wife follows as the final case

📖 Baraita: Biblical Derivation Step by Step

StepBiblical SourceWhat Is Derived
1Lev. 18:18 — 'you shall not take a woman to her sister'Wife's sister is herself exempt from levirate marriage
2Lev. 18:18 — 'to be a rival to her' (לִצְרֹר)Wife's sister's rival wife is also exempt
3Verbal analogy: just as wife's sister (whose violation = karet)…All 15 forbidden relatives in the same karet-category are exempt
4Extension: 'just as wife's sister's rival is exempt…'All rival wives of the 15 forbidden relatives are also exempt
5Excluding the 6 more severe cases (e.g., mother)They are forbidden to ALL brothers — a different legal category entirely
6Positive mitzva vs. karet prohibition (Amud B)Levirate mitzva cannot override a karet-level prohibition — exemption is absolute
