🏠 Yevama's Property Rights — Mishna (38a)

Both Agree — She Is Alive

  • Yevama may sell or give away her property
  • Levirate bond alone does not strip her rights
  • If yavam consummates — she becomes his full wife

Beit Shammai — If She Died

  • Husband's heirs AND father's heirs divide all property
  • Marriage contract (כתובה) funds split between both families
  • Melog (נכסי מלוג) property also divided equally

Beit Hillel — If She Died

  • Husband's heirs take the marriage contract
  • Father's heirs take the melog property
  • Each party takes what is theirs by prior right

🔗 Ulla vs Rabba: The Two Clauses of the Mishna

Ulla
The two clauses differ in timing. First clause: she came before the yavam while still betrothed, then later received property. A levirate bond (זיקה) with a betrothed woman = status of uncertain betrothal. So yavam's claim is only uncertain — she may sell freely.
Ulla
Second clause: she was already married when zikah formed = status of uncertain marriage. A married woman's property is controlled by her husband, so on her death Beit Shammai require division between both sets of heirs.
Rabba
If your explanation is correct, the second clause should have debated whether her sale while alive was valid — not who inherits on her death! Your reading is inconsistent with the actual structure of the Beit Shammai / Hillel dispute.
Rabba
Rather: both clauses deal with a married woman. In the first clause (alive), even though yavam has a definite claim, her living body's possession is decisive — Beit Shammai concede she may act. On her death, no living body remains, so both heirs contest equally.
Core Disagreement Ulla: the zikah's legal weight depends on whether she was betrothed or married. Rabba: both clauses involve married women — the alive/dead distinction is what drives the halakhic difference.

📜 Abaye's Objection — Bill of Debt as Definite Claim

Rabba's principle: Beit Shammai hold that an uncertain claimant cannot extract property from a definite claimant — explaining why on death the yavam's definite claim would prevail.
↓
Abaye objects from Bava Batra 157a: A son died, then the father died. The son's creditor seeks to collect from the father's estate (via inheritance). Father's heirs = definite claimants. Creditor = uncertain (did son actually inherit before dying?).
↓
Beit Shammai there rule: DIVIDE — apparently allowing an uncertain claimant (creditor) to extract from a definite claimant (father's heirs). This contradicts Rabba's principle!
↓
Rabba answers: Beit Shammai hold שטר העומד לגבות כגבוי דמי — a bill of debt awaiting collection is treated as already collected. The creditor therefore has a definite claim, not an uncertain one. No contradiction.
↓
Proof from Sota: Beit Shammai rule that a suspected adulteress who loses her ketubah — even an uncertain claim against it — confirms they treat a debt as collected; her ketubah was already 'taken' by the husband's uncertainty.

🧩 Final Resolution: What Does the Mishna Actually Record?

נכסי מלוג — property entering marriage with the wife; husband holds only usufructuary interest; principal returns to wife (or her heirs)
Gemara's Conclusion (Rav Ashi) Abaye asks: why not object to Rabba from the present mishna (38a) itself, where Beit Shammai appear to say heirs divide the marriage contract — an uncertain claim extracting from a definite one? The Gemara answers: Beit Shammai do not actually dispute the marriage contract at all. The Tanna left that question unanswered and moved on. The real dispute concerns only the melog property (נכסי מלוג). Rav Ashi confirms: the mishna's precise wording — 'father's heirs divide together with husband's heirs' — implies the father's heirs are merely joining to claim what was already conceded to them, not extracting anything contested.
