Yevamot 98 — Converts, Lineage & Mixed Offspring

גר שנתגייר כקטן שנולד דמי
KEY THEME This daf explores the legal erasure of gentile family ties upon conversion, examining which marriage prohibitions carry over and which do not. It concludes with the mishna's intricate procedure for resolving ḥalitza when the identity of brothers is uncertain due to mixed offspring.
3 / 9

Does a Gentile Have Legal Patrilineage?

Rava
A gentile has no patrilineage — not because of uncertainty about the father's identity, but as a legal principle.
Rava
Even with identical twins — one drop divided into two — they still do not perform ḥalitza or levirate marriage for each other.
Proof Text
"Whose flesh is the flesh of donkeys, and whose semen is the semen of horses" (Ezekiel 23:20) — the Torah dispossesses the gentile father of his offspring.
PRINCIPLE The Merciful One severs the legal paternal bond for gentiles — this is a gezerat ha-katuv (scriptural decree), not a factual claim about paternity.

Niftayim the Convert — Can He Marry His Brother's Wife?

Niftayim the convert married his maternal half-brother's wife
↓
The Sages ruled: No valid marriage prohibition exists for a convert regarding a brother's wife
↓
Did the brother marry her before or after conversion?
While Gentile
Marriage dissolved upon conversion — obvious that she's permitted
After Converting
Would prove converts have no brother's-wife prohibition at all
RESOLUTION The Gemara deflects each proof: the brother married her while still a gentile. The chiddush is that we do not decree a prohibition even as a safeguard.

Jonah's Prophecy"The word of Hashem came to Jonah a second time" — only twice, never a thirdJonah 3:1
ChallengeBut Jonah prophesied about Jeroboam ben Joash restoring Israel's borders! (II Kings 14:25)Gemara's objection
RELIABILITY The convert's personal stake in the ruling raises concerns. The Gemara offers three defenses: he taught it before acting on it; he cited a prior case; and he included an unrelated teaching, lending credibility.

Convert's Permitted & Forbidden Relatives After Conversion

May Maintain (מותר לקיים)

  • Father's paternal half-sister
  • Mother's paternal half-sister (Rabbis)
  • Maternal brother's wife
  • Father's brother's wife
  • Father's wife

Must Divorce (חייב לגרש)

  • Mother's maternal half-sister
  • Mother's paternal half-sister (R' Meir)
  • Any maternal relative forbidden due to kinship with the mother (R' Meir)
R' MEIR'S RULE Any forbidden relative connected through the mother — whether paternal or maternal — must be divorced. Paternal-side prohibitions are permitted because gentile patrilineage is not recognized.

Mother-in-Law After Wife's Death — For a Convert

R' Yishmael
A mother-in-law remains fully forbidden even after the wife dies. Therefore, a convert must divorce his mother-in-law — we decree lest a born-Jew do the same.
R' Akiva
The prohibition is weakened after the wife's death — no death penalty applies. So for a convert, the Sages did not decree a prohibition.
CONTEXT Both views agree that a convert who married a woman and her daughter must divorce one. The dispute is only about what happens after the daughter/wife dies.
