Yevamot 95 — Forbidden Relations & Their Effect on the Wife

Core Question If a man has relations with his wife's relatives — her sister or her mother — does this render his wife forbidden to him? The Gemara explores the derivation from "אותה" (her), disputes between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, and the meaning of R' Yosei's principle: "Whoever disqualifies others, disqualifies himself."
3 / 9

Beit Shammai vs Beit Hillel: What Do They Dispute?

R' Yehuda
All agree that relations with one's mother-in-law disqualifies the wife. They disagree about the wife's sister.
Beit Shammai (per R' Yehuda)
Relations with the wife's sister renders the wife disqualified.
Beit Hillel (per R' Yehuda)
Relations with the wife's sister does not render the wife disqualified.
R' Yosei
No! All agree the wife's sister does NOT disqualify. They disagree about the mother-in-law.
Beit Shammai (per R' Yosei)
Relations with the mother-in-law disqualifies the wife.
Beit Hillel (per R' Yosei)
Even the mother-in-law does not disqualify the wife.
Key Dispute R' Yehuda and R' Yosei disagree about which case Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel debate — the wife's sister or the mother-in-law.

R' Yosei's Kal Va-Ḥomer: Why the Wife's Sister Doesn't Disqualify

Through betrothal, he renders her forbidden to ALL men
↓
She renders him forbidden only to her relatives
↓
If she (with the greater prohibition) sinned unwittingly with another man — she is not forbidden to her husband
↓
Then he (with the lesser prohibition), if he unwittingly sinned with her sister — kal va-ḥomer he should not be forbidden to his wife!
The Verse "אותה" — Her intercourse renders her forbidden, but her sister's intercourse does not render her forbidden (Numbers 5:12-13). This covers the intentional case; the kal va-ḥomer covers the unwitting case.
5 / 9

What is the "Light Prohibition" in the Kal Va-Ḥomer?

Rav Ḥisda

  • The light prohibition = remarrying one's divorcée after she married another
  • Second husband's relations forbid her to the first husband
  • REJECTED: This is actually stringent — the body is 'defiled' and the prohibition is permanent

Rava / R' Yoḥanan

  • The light prohibition = a married woman (אשת איש)
  • Why 'light'? Her husband can negate the prohibition by giving a get
  • ACCEPTED: Abba Ḥanan in the name of R' Elazar agrees
The Logic If even with a married woman (light — husband can divorce), adultery forbids her to her husband permanently, then surely a wife's sister (severe — prohibition lasts her whole life) should forbid. The verse "אותה" teaches otherwise.

Reish Lakish: R' Yehuda's Source for Mother-in-Law

באש ישרפו אותו ואתהן
Burning Verse R' Ami said in the name of Reish Lakish: R' Yehuda's source that relations with one's mother-in-law forbids the wife is from "They shall be burned in fire, he and they" (Leviticus 20:14). Shall the entire household be burned? Rather, "if it does not refer to burning, refer it to prohibition" — both women (wife and mother-in-law) are forbidden to him.

👤 A man has relations with his mother-in-law

📢 "If not for Shmuel's ruling that the halakha is NOT like R' Yehuda..."
