⚖️ Abaye vs. Rava: Why Can She Eat Teruma?

Abaye
A woman betrothed to a priest with crushed testicles may eat teruma because his priestly status entitles his wife even before consummation — derived from the laws of acquisition of marriage.
Rava
She may eat teruma because his priestly status entitles his Canaanite slaves — and that power to enable others extends to his betrothed wife — derived from the laws of acquisition of property.
The Practical Difference If he was validly married and his testicles were then crushed: Abaye's reason fails (she had already been eating teruma), but Rava's reason still holds — his slave-entitlement power persists.
3 / 9

⚖️ Why Each Sage Rejects the Other's Reasoning

Abaye
Rejects Rava: We derive marriage-acquisition laws from marriage-acquisition laws — not from property acquisition. Slave entitlement cannot extend to a wife.
A betrothed woman had not previously partaken of teruma, so Rava's 'continuation' logic doesn't apply either.
Rava
Rejects Abaye: It is different for the wife who was already eating teruma before her husband's testicles were crushed — she may simply continue.
Abaye's logic based on prior entitlement-of-wife would not help a woman who had never yet begun eating teruma.
Key Principle Both agree she may eat — they disagree on the legal mechanism, which affects edge cases like a woman whose valid marriage preceded the husband's injury.

🤔 R' Yochanan's Dilemma: Daughter of Converts Married to Impaired Priest

A priest with crushed testicles marries the daughter of converts whose mother was Jewish from birth — per R' Eliezer b. Ya'akov's rule
↓
She is permitted to marry a priest — but on what basis?
↓
If: fitness was ADDED to her
She gained priestly marriage rights but remains in 'congregation of converts' → a priest with crushed testicles is forbidden from this congregation → she may NOT eat teruma
If: she LEFT the congregation of converts
She is now considered fully Jewish → the 'crushed testicles' prohibition does not apply → she MAY eat teruma
↓
R' Yochanan left this as an unresolved dilemma before R' Oshaya

📜 Three Tannaitic Views: Who Is the 'Congregation of the Lord'?

Rabbi Yehuda

  • Converts ARE called 'congregation of the Lord'
  • A man with crushed testicles may NOT enter this congregation
  • Whether or not he retains priestly sanctity, she may NOT eat teruma
  • R' Yochanan's dilemma makes NO SENSE under this view — outcome is always forbidden

Rabbi Yosei

  • Converts are NOT called 'congregation of the Lord'
  • A man with crushed testicles CAN marry a convert's daughter
  • Whether or not he retains priestly sanctity, she MAY eat teruma
  • R' Yochanan's dilemma makes NO SENSE under this view — outcome is always permitted

R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov

  • A convert's daughter may marry a priest only if her mother was Jewish
  • This creates a meaningful intermediate category
  • Did marrying a Jew make her fully part of the congregation, or only add fitness?
  • R' Yochanan's dilemma is based on THIS view — the answer is genuinely unclear

🚪 The Wedding Canopy (Chuppah) and Disqualification from Teruma

Rav's Rule: If a priest's daughter who is unfit to marry a priest enters the wedding canopy with a priest, she is disqualified from eating teruma — even without any act of intercourse.
↓
Shmuel's Concession from Rav: Even Rav agrees that for a girl under three years and one day old, entering the canopy does NOT disqualify her — because at that age, intercourse has no legal significance, so the canopy has none either.
↓
Rava's Proof: A baraita states: 'A girl three years and one day old can be betrothed via intercourse... if one who renders women unfit had intercourse with her, he has disqualified her from teruma.'
↓
Rava's Inference: Only a girl of three years and one day is disqualified via intercourse — and therefore also via the wedding canopy. Below that age: neither intercourse nor canopy disqualifies.
↓
Rami bar Chama: This dispute about the legal significance of chuppah may correspond to the very mishna dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Eliezer.

R' Acha bar Chinnana's Contribution

פְּצוּעַ דַּכָּא וּכְרוּת שׁוֹפְכָה לֹא יָבֹא בִּקְהַל ה׳ (דברים כג:ב)
📜 The Baraita from the South — Resolution Attempt When R' Acha bar Chinnana came from the south, he brought a baraita: The verse that restricts a man with crushed testicles from 'entering the congregation' implies that his wife may eat teruma when the marriage is otherwise valid — the very act of restricting his congregation-entry presupposes she has teruma rights.

The Gemara notes: this baraita was stated according to R' Eliezer ben Ya'akov's view and helps resolve one horn of R' Yochanan's dilemma — pointing toward the conclusion that she may eat teruma.

📋 Yevamot 57 — Summary Map

Core Question (Amud A): Can a woman betrothed to a priest with crushed testicles eat teruma?
↓
Abaye: Yes — his priestly status entitles his wife (derives from marriage-acquisition laws)
Rava: Yes — his priestly status entitles his slaves, which extends to his wife (derives from property-acquisition laws)
↓
R' Yochanan's Dilemma: What if she is a convert's daughter? Is she in the 'congregation of the Lord' that such a priest is barred from entering?
↓
Tannaitic Framework: R' Yehuda (converts = congregation → forbidden) | R' Yosei (converts ≠ congregation → permitted) | R' Eliezer b. Ya'akov (intermediate — dilemma genuine)
↓
Amud B — New Question: Does entering the wedding canopy with an unfit man disqualify a priest's daughter from teruma? Rav: Yes. Shmuel/Rava: Not if the girl is under three years and one day old.
