⚖️ Rabba vs. Abaye: Must Doubtful Betrothal Require Ḥalitza?

Rabba
When betrothal is uncertain, we do NOT require ḥalitza. Everyone will see it as a mere stringency and will not be confused — they'll know the betrothal was not definitively valid.
Abaye
But if you say she must perform ḥalitza, people will infer she may also perform yibum — a stringency that generates a leniency! This is why the fallen house case is difficult for your view.
Rabba
The fallen house differs: there, the co-wife's presumptive status is already forbidden (the deceased was a forbidden relative), so requiring ḥalitza there creates no risk of permitting yibum.
Rava
Rabba's distinction is rejected. The mishna is written in the style of 'His fellow speaks of him' — cases grouped together share the same ruling. All betrothal uncertainties also apply to divorce, but divorce has additional unique cases.
Core Tension חומרא דאתי לידי קולא — a stringency that produces a leniency is to be avoided. Requiring ḥalitza in a doubtful case might signal that yibum is also permitted from that same doubt.

🏠 The Fallen House Case — Abaye's Challenge to Rabba

Mishna (67b)
A house fell on a man and on his brother's daughter (his own wife). We do not know who died first. The rival wife requires ḥalitza but may not perform yibum.
Abaye
This is a case of pure doubt — yet ḥalitza IS required! This contradicts Rabba: if we fear that requiring ḥalitza will lead people to permit yibum, why require it here?
Rabba
Here the rival wife is already presumed forbidden — the deceased co-wife was a forbidden relative, so yibum was never an option. Requiring ḥalitza here cannot open the door to permitting yibum.
Gemara
Rabba's distinction is ultimately rejected. The mishna's structure follows 'His fellow speaks of him' (Job 36:33) — related cases are grouped together, not because they have fundamentally different logic.
Abaye's Conclusion The mishna is not structured by differing legal reasoning — Rabba's attempt to distinguish the cases fails, and the ruling is uniform across doubt scenarios.

📜 Uncertain Get — Halakhic Consequences

Get thrown between husband and wife
Uncertain whether it landed closer to her (valid) or to him (invalid)
↓
Gemara: This doubt does NOT arise from the facts themselves — only from conflicting sets of witnesses (one set says closer to her, one says closer to him)
↓
✅ Witnesses: Closer to Her
Fully divorced — document entered her legal domain
❓ Witnesses Conflict
מגורשת ואינה מגורשת
Divorced AND not divorced
❌ Witnesses: Closer to Him
Not divorced — document never left his domain
↓
Two practical consequences of 'divorced and not divorced' status:
↓
If husband is a Kohen
Wife is forbidden to him — we apply the stringency that she may be divorced
If she remarries
Child may be a safek mamzer — she may still be a married woman

📅 Betrothal Deed vs. Bill of Divorce — Date Requirements

📜 Bill of Divorce (Get)

  • Date MUST be written in the document
  • Reason 1: Prevents husband retroactively claiming wife's produce earnings
  • Reason 2: Prevents cover-up if he committed adultery with his daughter's husband's wife
  • Woman cannot erase the date — it protects her rights
  • Date is integral: without it the get may be invalid
  • Rava: divorce has unique uncertainty scenarios betrothal lacks

💍 Betrothal Document (Shtar Kiddushin)

  • Date is NOT required by rabbinic enactment
  • Betrothal can be done by money, deed, or intercourse — no deed needed
  • If some betroth by money (no deed), a date rule for deeds is inconsistent
  • Unlike a get, a betrothal deed cannot be used to claim retroactive produce rights
  • Signed witnesses can testify from memory if the date is forgotten
  • Contrast: slave acquisition always uses a deed → date IS required there

👬 Three Brothers Mishna — How the Exemption Works

Setup: Brothers A, B, and C are each married to an unrelated woman (W-A, W-B, W-C respectively)
↓
Step 1: Brother A dies childless → W-A falls before brothers B and C for yibum
↓
Step 2: Brother B performs levirate betrothal (eirusin) with W-A but dies before consummating the marriage
↓
The Double Bond: W-A now falls before C twice simultaneously — once as A's widow and once as B's mekudeshet. She is the rival wife of herself!
↓
W-A's Status: Because her double bond creates a co-wife relationship with herself, she is exempt from yibum and ḥalitza by Torah law
↓
W-B's Status: As the rival wife (צרה) of W-A — who is herself exempt — W-B is also exempt. The rival wife of a forbidden/exempt woman is herself exempt.
↓
Torah Source (Deut. 25:5): 'If brothers dwell together' — when two simultaneous yibum bonds exist, the Torah's levirate framework does not apply. Yet the Sages require ḥalitza by rabbinic decree.
