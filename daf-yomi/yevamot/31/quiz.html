QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does Rabba argue we should NOT require ḥalitza when a betrothal is of uncertain validity?
A. Because ḥalitza is only valid when the original marriage was fully consummated
B. Because requiring ḥalitza signals some validity to the betrothal, leading people to also permit yibum from that same doubt
C. Because uncertain betrothals are treated as completely non-existent by Torah law
D. Because only a Beit Din of 23 may rule on uncertain betrothal cases
Correct: B — Rabba's concern is the principle of חומרא דאתי לידי קולא — a stringency that produces a leniency. If we require ḥalitza, people will infer the betrothal had some validity and also permit levirate marriage, which is worse than not requiring ḥalitza at all.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In the fallen house case, why can the rival wife not perform yibum even though she must perform ḥalitza?
A. Because rabbinic decree forbids yibum in all cases where the death order is uncertain
B. Because the uncertainty about who died first means the yibum bond may not exist — she requires ḥalitza for the doubt but cannot perform yibum
C. Because the rival wife was herself a forbidden relative of the surviving brother
D. Because the Sages ruled that a fallen house nullifies all marital bonds retroactively
Correct: B — The uncertainty about who died first creates a doubt as to whether a yibum bond ever arose. She requires ḥalitza out of stringency (in case the husband died first and the bond did arise), but may not perform yibum since the bond may not exist.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Rava's final ruling regarding cases of uncertainty in betrothal vs. cases of uncertainty in divorce?
A. The two domains are completely identical in every type of uncertainty
B. Divorce cases encompass all betrothal uncertainties, plus additional cases unique to divorce
C. Betrothal uncertainties are more numerous because betrothal can be done in multiple ways
D. Neither domain can generate legally significant uncertainty cases
Correct: B — Rava teaches that every type of doubtful betrothal has a parallel in doubtful divorce, but divorce has additional uncertainty scenarios — such as those involving the physical delivery of the get — that have no betrothal equivalent.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why did the Sages NOT institute a requirement to include a date in a betrothal document?
A. Because the Torah explicitly forbids adding dates to betrothal documents
B. Because betrothal can be effected through money alone without any document, making a universal date rule unworkable
C. Because betrothal witnesses are exempt from remembering the date of the ceremony
D. Because the Sages feared that including a date would reveal private family information
Correct: B — Since betrothal is legally valid through the giving of money (kesef kiddushin) without any document, instituting a date requirement for betrothal deeds would be unenforceable — people would simply betroth without a deed, bypassing the rule entirely.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In the Three Brothers Mishna, both W-A and W-B are exempt from yibum with Brother C. Why is W-B (Brother B's original wife) exempt?
A. Because W-B performed ḥalitza with Brother A before he died
B. Because W-A — the rival wife of herself due to her double bond — is exempt, and the rival wife of an exempt woman is also exempt
C. Because W-B is too closely related to Brother C by blood
D. Because Brother C already consummated yibum with W-A, which releases all remaining bonds
Correct: B — W-A's double yibum bond (she fell before C simultaneously as A's widow and B's mekudeshet) makes her the rival wife of herself — exempt by Torah law. As W-A's rival wife, W-B is then exempt by the rule that the rival wife of a forbidden/exempt woman is herself exempt (צרת ערוה).
