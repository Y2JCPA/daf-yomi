Yevamot 25 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Abaye (citing his mother), how long does a persistent rumor (shemu'ah) last in a city?
A. Half a day
B. One full day
C. One and a half days
D. Three days
Correct: C — Abaye said his mother taught him: a rumor in the city lasts yom u'meḥatzah — a day and a half — provided it does not cease and then resume, which would make it a new rumor.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why specifically may an agent who brings a get FROM OVERSEAS not marry the divorcée (while a local agent may)?
A. He is presumed to be a close relative of the husband
B. The court relies on his testimony ('written and signed in my presence') to validate the get, raising suspicion he engineered the divorce
C. Overseas agents are assumed to be of questionable character
D. The Rabbis decreed this as a universal stringency for all messengers
Correct: B — Specifically because the court relies on his declaration to establish the get's validity. Having personally enabled the divorce, suspicion arises that he did so with ulterior motive to marry the woman himself.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yehuda, if a man testifies 'I killed the husband,' may the widow remarry based on his testimony?
A. Yes — his testimony is accepted like any other witness for agunah leniency
B. No — by admitting to murder he declares himself a rasha, disqualifying his own testimony
C. Yes — but only if a second witness corroborates the killing
D. No — but she may remarry after waiting ninety days
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda holds that 'I killed him' is not accepted because the witness thereby renders himself wicked (rasha), disqualifying himself. He contrasts this with 'we killed him' — where others involved can testify — which is accepted.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
A Sage successfully annulled a woman's vow (permitting her to remain with her husband). She was later divorced for an unrelated reason. May the Sage now marry her?
A. No — any involvement in her case permanently prohibits him from marrying her
B. Yes — he acted to preserve her marriage, not end it, so there is no suspicion of ulterior motive
C. Only if three years have passed since the annulment ruling
D. No — unless the original husband gives explicit written consent
Correct: B — The Gemara derives: the prohibition applies only when the Sage caused the divorce (by refusing to annul). When he permitted her — acting to save the marriage — no suspicion attaches, and he may marry her if she later becomes available.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
If one of three judges on a beit din presided over a ḥalitza ceremony, may he later marry the ḥalutza?
A. No — any judicial involvement creates the same suspicion as an agent bringing a get
B. Yes — functioning as part of a three-member court removes individual suspicion
C. Only if he was not the presiding (lead) judge
D. No — ḥalitza is treated more strictly than a get because it involves physical contact
Correct: B — The mishna explicitly permits this: he functioned as one of three judges — a court, not an individual actor. The suspicion of ulterior motive that applies to solo enablers does not apply to a member of a judicial panel.
