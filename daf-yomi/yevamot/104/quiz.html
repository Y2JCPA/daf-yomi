QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why is the court's ḥalitza sandal valid even though it was never actually used for walking?
A. Because the court has special authority to validate any sandal
B. Because it is capable of being walked in, even if it is not used that way
C. Because it was once walked in during a previous ḥalitza
D. Because court property is exempt from normal sandal requirements
Correct: B — Rav Ashi explains that if a court messenger walked in it, no one would reprimand him — the sandal is capable of being used for walking, unlike the Elder's burial sandal which is forbidden for any other use.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What was the specific problem Shmuel identified with Rabba bar Ḥiyya Ketosfa'a's ḥalitza?
A. Using a non-leather slipper, which has no basis in halakha
B. Performing ḥalitza at night, which contradicts the mishna
C. Conducting ḥalitza in private as the sole judge, relying on an individual opinion
D. All three of his leniencies equally troubled Shmuel
Correct: C — The Gemara identifies that the slipper and nighttime practices have support from unattributed (majority) sources. Only the private setting relies on the individual opinion of Rabbi Akiva, which is what Shmuel criticized.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rabbi Zeira's principle about meal-offerings apply to ḥalitza?
A. Just as meal-offerings require mixing, ḥalitza requires spitting
B. If one is capable of reciting the verses, recitation is not indispensable; if one cannot recite, it invalidates the ḥalitza
C. Meal-offerings and ḥalitza share the same Torah source
D. Both meal-offerings and ḥalitza must be performed during daytime
Correct: B — Rabbi Zeira's principle — 'what is fit to be mixed, mixing is not indispensable' — teaches that capability determines indispensability. A normal person can recite, so skipping recitation doesn't invalidate. A mute person cannot recite, so the inability becomes a fatal flaw.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to the Gemara's final understanding, why is a deaf-mute's ḥalitza invalid?
A. Only because they cannot physically remove the shoe
B. Because they lack intellectual capacity (da'at)
C. Because the Torah explicitly excludes deaf-mutes from ḥalitza
D. Because they cannot fulfill 'he says' and 'she shall say' as required by the verse
Correct: D — The scholars from the house of Rabbi Yannai explain that deaf-mutes are disqualified because they cannot fulfill the verse's requirements of 'he says' and 'she shall say' — not merely due to lacking intellectual capacity.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the core dispute between the first Tanna and Rabbi Elazar regarding ḥalitza at night?
A. Whether monetary law can be compared to leprosy law at all
B. Whether ḥalitza is analogous to the commencement of a court case or to the delivery of a verdict
C. Whether ḥalitza is a Torah obligation or rabbinic
D. Whether nighttime invalidates the shoe or only the recitation
Correct: B — Both agree that monetary verdicts may be delivered at night. They disagree on the analogy: Rabbi Elazar views ḥalitza as the commencement of judgment (requiring daytime), while the first Tanna views it as the verdict (permitted at night).
