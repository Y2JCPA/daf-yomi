Invalid Shoes for Ḥalitza

Invalid Even After the Fact

  • 🏚️ Sandal of an idolatrous city — destined for destruction with all city property
  • ⚰️ Elder's burial sandal — made for dignity, not for walking
  • Both lack the essential quality of being a functional walking shoe

Valid: Court's Sandal

  • 👞 Kept by the court specifically for ḥalitza ceremonies
  • Ravina challenges: This sandal also isn't used for walking!
  • Rav Ashi: If a court messenger walked in it, would anyone object? No!
  • Key distinction: capable of being walked in vs. forbidden to use
Key Distinction The Elder's sandal is forbidden for any other use — it is exclusively for the dead. The court's sandal could be walked in; it simply happens not to be. Capability, not actual use, determines validity.

Ḥalitza at Night: Valid or Invalid?

First Tanna
Ḥalitza performed at night is valid.
R' Elazar
Ḥalitza at night is invalid.
Gemara
Do they disagree about comparing monetary law to leprosy (which requires daytime)?
Gemara
No. All agree we don't fully compare them — verdicts can be delivered at night.
Gemara
R' Elazar: Ḥalitza = commencement of judgment → must be by day.
First Tanna: Ḥalitza = verdict → may be at night.
Analogy Monetary cases must begin during the day but the verdict may be delivered at night. The dispute is whether ḥalitza resembles the start or the conclusion of a court proceeding.

Left Foot vs. Right Foot for Ḥalitza

Mishna: Ḥalitza on left foot — what is the law?
↓
Rabbis (Majority)
❌ Invalid
Verbal analogy (gezera shava): "foot" here = "foot" of the leper
Leper requires right foot (Lev. 14:14)
So too ḥalitza requires the right foot
R' Elazar
✅ Valid
"So shall it be done" (Deut. 25:9) — "so" limits indispensable elements
Only actions directly on the man are indispensable
Ulla's Source Ulla derives the right-foot requirement through a gezera shava (verbal analogy) linking the word "regel" (foot) in ḥalitza to the purification ritual of the metzora (leper).

What Makes 'So Shall It Be Done' Indispensable?

R' Elazar
"So shall it be done" (Deut. 25:9) — the word kakha (so) means any action required for ḥalitza is indispensable.
R' Akiva
"So shall it be done to the man" — only actions performed on the man's body (shoe removal) are indispensable.
R' Akiva
Therefore spitting, which occurs in his presence but not on him, is not indispensable.
R' Elazar
Spitting is indispensable — it is one of the defined actions of the ḥalitza ritual.
Practical Difference If spitting is indispensable (R' Elazar), a ḥalitza without spitting is completely invalid. If not (R' Akiva), the shoe removal alone suffices after the fact.
