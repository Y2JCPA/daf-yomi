Why Is Impure Sacred Meat Nullified but Pure Sacred Meat Is Not?

Rav Sheisha b. Rav Idi
The first clause deals with impurity from liquids — which is only rabbinic. Since the prohibition is rabbinic, it can be nullified.
Rav Sheisha b. Rav Idi
The latter clause involves sacred food mixed with non-sacred — forbidden to non-priests by Torah law, so it is not nullified.
Rabba
First clause: a standard prohibition (eating impure kodesh) → nullified. Latter clause: a prohibition carrying karet (non-priest eating kodesh) → not nullified.
Gemara
But Rabba himself said there is no difference between a standard prohibition and karet for bitul! This is difficult (kashya).
Rav Ashi
The sacred meat is a davar she'yesh lo matirin — a temporarily prohibited item — because a priest could eat it. Such items are never nullified.
Gemara
This is a beduta (mistake)! For a priest it was never forbidden; for an Israelite it is always forbidden. There is no 'temporary' prohibition here.
Key Outcome Rav Ashi's explanation is rejected. Rav Sheisha's distinction between rabbinic and Torah-level impurity remains the strongest answer.

Reish Lakish: The Crushed Meat Distinction

A piece of impure sin-offering falls into pieces of pure sin-offering
↓
Was the impure piece crushed (broken into small parts)?
Yes — Crushed
The impure piece is nullified in 100 pieces — it has lost its distinct identity
No — Intact
The impure piece is not nullified — it retains its significance as a distinct piece (chatikha ha'reuya lehitkabeid)
R' Yehuda's Principle Min b'mino — when a forbidden food is mixed with the same type of food, it can never be nullified according to R' Yehuda.

Teruma Today: R' Yochanan vs. Reish Lakish

R' Yochanan

  • Teruma nowadays applies by Torah law
  • Follows R' Yosei (author of Seder Olam)
  • The second sanctification of Eretz Yisrael was permanent
  • A hermaphrodite priest enables his wife to eat breast and thigh of shelamim

Reish Lakish

  • Teruma nowadays applies only by rabbinic law
  • The sanctity of the land lapsed after the Second Temple's destruction
  • Implications for bitul: rabbinic teruma can be nullified more easily
  • A hermaphrodite is of uncertain status — does not enable eating kodshim
Seder Olam "Your fathers inherited… and you shall inherit it" (Deut. 30:5) — a first inheritance (Joshua) and a second inheritance (return from Babylon), but no third will ever be needed.

The Two Baskets Case — Teruma & Non-Sacred Produce

🧺 Two large baskets: one of non-sacred produce, one of teruma
↓
📏 Two one-se'a vessels: one of non-sacred, one of teruma
↓
⬇️ Each vessel's contents falls into a basket — but we don't know which fell where
↓
🤔 Possibility: teruma fell into non-sacred produce — creating a forbidden mixture?
↓
✅ R' Yochanan: We can say the teruma fell into teruma and the non-sacred into non-sacred — so no nullification is needed
Legal Principle When we can assume each item returned to its own type (kol d'parish), we follow that presumption — even when dealing with Torah-level teruma.
