QUESTION 1 OF 5
For a yevama (levirate widow), when does the 3-month waiting period begin according to Rava?
A. From the time of ḥalitza with the yavam
B. From the time she declares intent to remarry
C. From the time of her husband's death
D. From the time of betrothal to the yavam
Correct: C — Rava derives via an a fortiori argument: even the karet-level prohibition of levirate is resolved from the husband's death, so the 3-month count begins from his death, not from ḥalitza.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is Rava's reason for the rabbinic 3-month waiting requirement?
A. To allow the woman time to properly mourn her deceased husband
B. To distinguish seed sown in sanctity from seed not sown in sanctity
C. To prevent a child from being misidentified as the son of the second husband, leading to incest or levirate errors
D. To give the yavam time to decide whether to perform yibum or ḥalitza
Correct: C — Rava says it is a rabbinic decree against paternity misidentification. Option B is Rav Naḥman/Shmuel's separate reason. Both reasons are raised and each defended in the Gemara.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does the Gemara reject the idea of waiting only 2.5 months before remarrying?
A. Because a baby born after 7 months is always nonviable
B. Because Mar Zutra teaches that a 9-month pregnancy can extend a few extra days, derived from Hannah and Samuel
C. Because Rav Safra rules that physical examinations are unreliable before 3 months
D. Because the Torah explicitly requires 3 full months of mourning
Correct: B — Mar Zutra derives from the verse about Hannah ('when the seasons of the days had come') that a 9-month pregnancy can run slightly beyond the ninth month. This means a birth just over 6 months after remarriage could still be a 9-month pregnancy from the first husband.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why can't we resolve the waiting period question by examining the woman's body or gait at the 3-month mark?
A. Ancient medical knowledge was insufficient to detect early pregnancy
B. Examining married women's bodies is shameful, and a woman could deliberately hide her gait to conceal pregnancy
C. The Torah forbids physical examination of widows
D. Rav Safra ruled that gait examination is halakhically invalid evidence
Correct: B — Rav Safra objects that examining married women shames them before their husbands. Rami bar Ḥama adds that a pregnant woman would alter her walk intentionally so the child is attributed to her new husband — rendering gait examination unreliable.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rabbi Abbahu, when an unattributed ruling appears in the Mishna and a dispute on it appears in a baraita, the halakha follows:
A. The majority opinion among the disputants in the baraita
B. The more stringent position in the baraita
C. The unattributed ruling in the Mishna
D. The ruling of whichever Tanna is named first in the baraita
Correct: C — Rabbi Abbahu teaches that an unattributed Mishna ruling takes precedence even when disputed in a baraita. He sharply criticized the questioner for not knowing this rule, saying he was 'not concerned for his flour' (i.e., wasting effort on a non-question).
