QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Ashi, what is the key distinction that resolves the baraita's contradiction?
A. Whether ḥalitza or levirate marriage was ultimately performed
B. Whether the surviving brothers were born before or after the first brother's betrothal/death
C. Whether the yevama was a sota or a regular wife
D. Whether the yavam was a priest or an Israelite
Correct: B — Rav Ashi (following Reish Lakish + Rabbi Shimon) holds that the baraita's two clauses speak of different brothers: those born after the betrothal have no levirate bond, while those born before the first brother's death do — this timing distinction dissolves the contradiction.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does Rav derive that the rival wife of a sota is forbidden from levirate marriage?
A. From a direct verse in Deuteronomy about levirate marriage
B. From a rabbinic decree protecting the family's honor
C. From a gezeira shava: 'defilement' in the sota passage (Num 5:13) links to 'defilement' in the forbidden-relations passage
D. From the principle that any doubt about permitted status renders her forbidden
Correct: C — Rav uses a verbal analogy (gezeira shava): the word 'defilement' appears both in the sota passage (Num 5:13) and in the verse listing forbidden sexual relations, thereby subjecting the sota to all the exemptions of those forbidden women — including their rival wives being released from levirate.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rav respond to Rav Ḥisda's objection from a woman who remarried thinking her husband had died?
A. Rav concedes that his ruling about the sota's rival wife does not hold
B. Rav distinguishes: his ruling applies to a definite sota (Torah law); the objection concerns a rabbinic sota (inadvertent/unavoidable)
C. Rav argues her second marriage is void so she was never a sota
D. Rav says the levirate bond does not apply to any woman who remarried
Correct: B — Rav draws a sharp line between Torah-level sota (intentional infidelity — subject to his gezeira shava) and rabbinic sota (a woman who remarried in error without knowing her husband was alive). These are categorically different, and his ruling governs only the former.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What moral principle does Rav Yosef derive from Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's ruling about ḥalitza with a disqualified yevama?
A. One should always prefer levirate marriage over ḥalitza to honor the deceased
B. A yavam must give priority to the fit yevama when two fall before him
C. A person should not pour water from his well when others need it — avoid causing unnecessary loss to others
D. One should never perform ḥalitza if levirate marriage is halakhically possible
Correct: C — Rav Yosef reads Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's ruling (that ḥalitza must still be performed even for a forbidden yevama) as a moral teaching: just as one should not waste water that others need, a yavam should not refuse to perform ḥalitza and thereby leave the rival wife permanently tied as a yevama.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What question remains formally unresolved at the end of Daf 11 regarding the remarried divorcée?
A. Whether the Torah prohibition on remarrying a divorcée applies to all Jews or only priests
B. Whether the rival wife of a remarried divorcée must perform ḥalitza or may remarry freely if the husband dies childless
C. Whether children born from the forbidden remarriage are considered mamzerim
D. Whether the prohibition takes effect from the moment of betrothal or only from full marriage
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan formally poses the dilemma: the remarried divorcée is herself forbidden from levirate marriage — but does this exempt her rival wife entirely, or must the rival wife still perform ḥalitza to be released? Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi dispute this, and the sugya ends without a definitive resolution.
