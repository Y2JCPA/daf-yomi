⚖️ Two Amoraic Resolutions of the Baraita Contradiction

Rav Ashi
Follows Reish Lakish + resolves via Rabbi Shimon: The ḥalitza clause speaks of newborn brothers (born after the betrothal); the betrothal clause speaks of pre-existing brothers. The baraita's two clauses address different birth-timing scenarios.
Ravina
Follows Rabbi Yoḥanan + resolves via the Rabbis: The ḥalitza clause speaks of pre-existing brothers. Brothers born only after the first brother's death have no levirate bond at all — she is forbidden to them as a brother's wife.
Root of the Dispute Reish Lakish: ḥalitza creates a new prohibition binding on surviving brothers. Rabbi Yoḥanan: brothers born after the deceased never shared a levirate connection with the yevama in the first place.

Brother A dies childless — yevama falls before surviving brothers for levirate
↓
Brothers born before Brother A's death
Coexisted with deceased → full levirate bond applies; levirate or ḥalitza required
Brothers born after Brother A's death
No levirate bond — she was already his brother's wife before they existed; now she is forbidden to them
↓
Per Rav Ashi: distinguishing these two groups resolves the baraita's apparent contradiction between its clauses

💀 Is There Karet for Relations with a Rival Wife After Levirate Performed?

Opinion: Liable (karet)
Follows Reish Lakish: once levirate marriage is performed with one yevama, the surviving brother has now strengthened the prohibition — the rival wife becomes fully forbidden as a brother's wife, and relations incur karet.
Opinion: Exempt (no karet)
Follows Rabbi Yoḥanan: the mitzva having been performed dissolves the levirate bond — rival wives are now exempt from both levirate and its attendant prohibitions. No karet applies.
Same Dispute, New Application This karet question is the direct consequence of the Reish Lakish / Rabbi Yoḥanan disagreement that structured the entire baraita resolution above.

🚫 Three Categories of 'Defilement' (טֻמְאָה) and Levirate Exemption

Sota — Definite (Torah)

  • Wife intentionally unfaithful to husband
  • "Defilement" written: Num 5:13
  • Gezeira shava links to forbidden-relations passage
  • Forbidden to husband AND to yavam
  • Rival wife also exempt from levirate (Rav's ruling)

Sota — Uncertain (Rabbinic)

  • Wife secluded long enough for relations — no witnesses
  • Defilement language also appears in relevant baraita
  • Rav: this is rabbinic sota — a different category
  • Unintentional / unavoidable situation
  • Rival wife's status not equated to definite sota

Remarried Divorcée

  • Husband remarries ex-wife after she wed another
  • "Defilement" written: Deut 24:4 (to'evah passage)
  • She is forbidden; does the levirate bond still apply?
  • Does her rival wife go free or need ḥalitza?
  • Formal unresolved dilemma (Rabbi Yoḥanan)

🔄 Step-by-Step: The Remarried Divorcée Dilemma

Step 1: Reuven divorces his wife Leah. Leah then marries Shimon.
↓
Step 2: Leah and Shimon divorce (or Shimon dies). Reuven remarries Leah — Torah forbids this: "that is an abomination" (Deut 24:4).
↓
Step 3: Reuven dies childless. He also had a second wife, Rachel. Leah is now forbidden — does the levirate bond even apply to her?
↓
Step 4: If Leah has no levirate bond → does Rachel (rival wife) automatically go free? Or must Rachel still perform ḥalitza to be released?
↓
Step 5: Rabbi Yoḥanan raises this formally; Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi dispute it. The a fortiori argument: if she is forbidden to one previously permitted (her ex-husband), certainly to the yavam — but does that exempt the rival wife?
