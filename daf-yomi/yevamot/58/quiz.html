QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Sheshet (as transmitted by Rav Amram), what is the legal effect of a priest entering the wedding canopy with a woman unfit to marry him?
A. It has no independent effect — only completed intercourse matters
B. It disqualifies her from partaking of teruma
C. It entitles her to partake of teruma just like valid intercourse
D. It only matters if the priest subsequently divorces her
Correct: B — Rav Sheshet holds that chuppah with an unfit woman disqualifies her from teruma, demonstrating that chuppah entry has independent legal significance — parallel to how betrothal can disqualify according to Rabbi Meir.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the Gemara question whether Rabbi Meir's betrothal ruling can be extended to chuppah?
A. Because Rabbi Meir explicitly excluded chuppah from his ruling in another tractate
B. Because betrothal involves formal acquisition while chuppah, being proximate to intercourse, may operate under different rules
C. Because chuppah was only instituted after Rabbi Meir's time
D. Because Rabbi Meir only ruled about daughters of priests, not other unfit women
Correct: B — The Gemara raises the possibility that Rabbi Meir's stringency applies specifically to betrothal's formal acquisition, while chuppah — being close to intercourse — might be treated differently, either more strictly (like intercourse) or more leniently by various authorities.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rami bar Ḥama, what scenario makes the sota mechanism work for a betrothed woman?
A. Her husband warned her during betrothal and she drank while still betrothed
B. Her betrothed had licentious intercourse with her in her father's house before the canopy
C. She entered the canopy and then committed adultery before intercourse with her husband
D. She was warned while married but had not yet entered the canopy
Correct: B — Rami bar Ḥama explains that the betrothed man had intercourse with her licentiously (not for acquisition purposes) in her father's house. Since this was not canopy-acquiring intercourse, she remains legally 'betrothed' for sota purposes, satisfying the verse's requirement.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is Rabbi Yohanan's reason for permitting a yevama to eat teruma after levirate betrothal when the yavam has a priestly brother?
A. The yibbum bond is too tenuous to create any teruma disqualification
B. Rabbi Meir's restriction only applies to intercourse invalid at the Torah level; yibbum with a priestly brother is only rabbinically restricted
C. The priestly brother has no halakhic connection to the yevama
D. A yevama always retains full teruma rights regardless of who her yavam's brothers are
Correct: B — Rabbi Yohanan distinguishes the levels of invalidity: Rabbi Meir forbids teruma when a woman awaits Torah-level invalid intercourse. Here, the yavam's intercourse with the yevama is only rabbinically problematic (due to his priestly brother's existence), not a Torah prohibition, so even Rabbi Meir would permit it.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does Reish Lakish prohibit the yevama from eating teruma when the yavam has a priestly brother, even though Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon are generally lenient?
A. Because yibbum betrothal is a stronger form of acquisition than regular betrothal
B. Because Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon's permissive ruling only applies when the priest himself can entitle the woman, not when it is a different priestly brother
C. Because the existence of any brother automatically triggers the full disqualification
D. Because a yevama never has teruma rights until the actual yibbum is performed
Correct: B — Reish Lakish argues that Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon only permit teruma in betrothal cases where the priest himself has the power to entitle the woman through marriage. Here, the entitlement would have to come from the priestly brother — who has no such power in relation to this yevama — so even their lenient ruling does not apply.
