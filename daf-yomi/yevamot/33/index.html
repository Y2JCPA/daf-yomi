⚖️ Rabbi Ḥiyya vs. Bar Kappara: What Did Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi Teach?

Bar Kappara
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi taught me 3 cases of exemption — non-priest on Shabbat, blemished priest while impure, and non-priest eating a bird killed by pinching. All are cases of simultaneous prohibitions (isur chal al isur).
Rabbi Ḥiyya
He taught you only 2 cases. You added the bird case yourself! The first two involve sequential prohibitions — they are cases of isur mosif, not simultaneous ones.
Bar Kappara
The bird case can only occur with simultaneous prohibitions, so I inferred the other two must be simultaneous as well — all following Rabbi Shimon's rule that one prohibition cannot take effect atop another.
Rabbi Ḥiyya
Bar Kappara erred! Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi taught those two cases under isur mosif. The bird analogy was faulty — it does not belong to the same category at all.
Core Dispute Did Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi teach 2 cases (isur mosif / sequential) or 3 cases (isur chal al isur / simultaneous)? Rabbi Ḥiyya and bar Kappara sharply disagree about what was transmitted and how the cases should be categorized.

🔑 Two Types of Overlapping Prohibitions

Isur Mosif (More Inclusive)

  • A new prohibition adds scope to an existing one
  • Ex: Non-priest always forbidden on Shabbat; now also forbidden from Temple service every day
  • The new prohibition expands the original restriction to more persons or times
  • Rabbi Yosei: such a prohibition can take effect atop an existing one
  • Result: person is liable for both violations separately

Isur Chal Al Isur (Simultaneous)

  • Two prohibitions take effect at exactly the same moment
  • Ex: A minor grows two hairs and simultaneously becomes ritually impure
  • Both 'blemished' and 'impure' prohibitions begin at once — neither precedes the other
  • Rabbi Shimon: second prohibition cannot take effect atop the first
  • Result: liable for only one of the two violations

⚗️ Bar Kappara's Reasoning — and Where It Went Wrong

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi teaches bar Kappara two exemption cases: non-priest on Shabbat, and blemished priest while impure
↓
Bar Kappara also encounters the case of a non-priest eating a bird killed by pinching — where both prohibitions clearly arise simultaneously
↓
Bar Kappara's inference: Since the bird case involves simultaneous prohibitions, all three cases must involve simultaneous prohibitions (isur chal al isur)
↓
Rabbi Ḥiyya's Rebuttal
The first two cases are sequential (isur mosif), not simultaneous. Bar Kappara was wrong to analogize from the bird case — the categories are different!
What Really Happened
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi taught the first two cases as isur mosif per Rabbi Shimon. Bar Kappara added the third himself and miscategorized all three as simultaneous.

💒 Mishna (33b): The Switched Wives

Setup: Two men betroth two women. At the wedding canopy, they switch — Man A enters with Woman B's chamber, Man B with Woman A's.
↓
Liability: Both men are liable for bi'at zenut (forbidden sexual relations) and must bring a sin-offering.
↓
Separation: The women are separated from their husbands for three months before reuniting with their proper spouses.
↓
Why three months? To determine paternity — a child born after return would otherwise be of uncertain lineage (from proper husband or forbidden union).
↓
Gemara's challenge: Could they really be so wicked to do this intentionally? Rabbi Ḥiyya taught 16 such cases — implying these accidents happen in darkness or confusion.
↓
Proof of accidental nature: If it were intentional, would they be permitted to return to their wives after three months? The mishna's permission to return implies the act was unintentional.

🔥 What Service Did the Non-Priest Perform on Shabbat?

מַאי עֲבוֹדָה עָבַד? — In what form of Temple service did he serve?
Gemara's Sharp Analysis Problem: If the non-priest burned sacrificial portions (haktarah), this follows Rabbi Yosei — but Rabbi Yosei holds kindling on Shabbat is singled out to impose only a negative commandment, not the death penalty. That changes the whole case! Rav Aḥa bar Yaakov: The case involves slaughtering the High Priest's bull on Yom Kippur — disqualified if performed by a non-priest, making it a genuine Temple service violation on Shabbat. Rav Ashi objects strongly: The earlier rejection was groundless! The tanna is not counting how many sin-offerings are brought — he is counting which prohibitions were violated. Burning is perfectly valid as the case.
