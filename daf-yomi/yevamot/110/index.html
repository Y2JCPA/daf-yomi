Yevamot 110: Marriage Validity & Levirate Status of Minors and Deaf-Mutes

Core Question: When is a marriage to a minor fully realized?
↓
Rav
Marriage validated once she reaches majority — intercourse confirms it
Shmuel
Intercourse is within the framework of the original betrothal, not a new act of acquisition
↓
Mishna: Levirate rules for orphaned minors and deaf-mutes — equal rabbinic status can exempt rival wives
↓
Amud B: Can ḥalitza work for a deaf-mute whose levirate bond is only rabbinic?
Key Theme This daf explores how marriages of different legal force (Torah vs. rabbinic) interact in the levirate system, and whether rabbinic tools like ḥalitza can address bonds of varying strength.

Does a Minor's Marriage Require Intercourse After Majority?

Rav
Once she reaches majority, her marriage is fully realized — intercourse after majority confirms it definitively.
Shmuel
When anyone has intercourse with a woman he married as a minor, his intention is that it is within the framework of the initial betrothal — not a new act of acquisition (kinyan).
Gemara
But Rav and Shmuel already disagreed about this in the case of conditional betrothal (kiddushin al t'nai)!
Gemara
Both cases are necessary: In conditional betrothal, Rav might say he retracted the condition. In a minor's marriage, Shmuel might concede the intercourse was a new kinyan. Neither can be derived from the other.
Parallel Dispute Conditional betrothal: Rav says she needs a get (he retracted the condition by marrying her). Shmuel says she doesn't (intercourse follows the original framework, condition still stands).

The Neresh Incident: The Bride Snatcher

A woman was betrothed as a minor and reached majority
↓
Her husband seated her in the bridal chair under the chuppah — but had not yet had intercourse
↓
Another man seized her and married her!
↓
Rav Bruna & Rav Ḥananel (Rav's students): No get needed from the second man
↓
But wait — if Rav requires intercourse after majority, why no get?
Rav Pappa
In Neresh, the custom was to have intercourse before the bridal chair — he already consummated!
Rav Ashi
The snatcher acted improperly, so the Sages annulled his betrothal — afke'inhu rabbanan l'kiddushin
Rabbinic Power The Sages can retroactively annul a betrothal: if done with money, they declare it ownerless; if done via intercourse, they render it a licentious act (be'ilat zenut).

Mishna: Levirate Rules — Equal vs. Unequal Rabbinic Status

Two Orphaned Minors

  • Both married by rabbinic law
  • Equal legal status
  • Levirate marriage or ḥalitza with one EXEMPTS the rival wife
  • Same rule applies to two deaf-mutes

Minor + Deaf-Mute

  • Both married by rabbinic law
  • But statuses are NOT the same
  • Levirate marriage with one does NOT exempt the other
  • Different categories of rabbinic marriage cannot interact

Competent + Deaf-Mute

  • Competent wife: married by Torah law
  • Deaf-mute: married by rabbinic law
  • Levirate with competent wife EXEMPTS deaf-mute
  • Levirate with deaf-mute does NOT exempt competent wife
Principle A wife whose marriage has greater legal force (Torah law) can exempt a wife of lesser force (rabbinic law), but not vice versa. Equal rabbinic statuses can exempt each other only if they are the same category.

The Gemara's Chain of Proofs on Deaf-Mute Ḥalitza

🔎 Proof attempt: Baraita says competent man + deaf-mute yevama → levirate yes, ḥalitza no
↓
❌ Rejected: Maybe she was competent and BECAME deaf-mute — ḥalitza can't undo a Torah-level bond
↓
🔎 New proof: Mishna with deaf-mute brothers married to sisters — surviving brother's wife is 'forbidden forever'
↓
❓ If he was competent and became deaf-mute, can he divorce? Mishna says NO — he may never divorce her
↓
✅ Therefore: He must have been deaf-mute from the outset — and still no ḥalitza is available
↓
⚡ Rav Yosef to Abaye: The sisters could be deaf-mute from the outset while unrelated women became deaf-mute later — challenge is flawed
↓
🔄 Better proof from Mishna 112b: Two deaf-mute brothers — consummate levirate marriage yes, ḥalitza no
Analytical Method The Gemara distinguishes between deaf-mute from the outset (cheresh me'ikara) and one who became deaf-mute (nit'charesh), as the legal force of their marriages differs significantly.
