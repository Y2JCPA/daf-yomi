Ḥalitza: What Makes It Valid?

Ḥalitza Ceremony
Three elements: shoe removal, spitting, recitation
↓
R' Eliezer
Any action (shoe + spit) is indispensable
'So shall it be done' — actions matter
R' Akiva
Only actions on the yavam's body (shoe removal) are indispensable
↓
Secondary question: Can a minor perform ḥalitza?
↓
R' Meir
Minor's ḥalitza disqualifies levirate marriage but doesn't fully permit remarriage
R' Yosei / Rabbis
Female minor may perform valid ḥalitza — 'yevama' not 'woman' is written
Key Theme This daf dissects ḥalitza into its component parts — asking which elements are biblically required, in what order, and who may perform them.

Three Elements of Ḥalitza — What If One Is Missing?

ScenarioShoe Removed?Spat?Recited?Status
Full ḥalitza✅✅✅✅ Valid
No recitation✅✅❌✅ Valid
No spit, no recitation✅❌❌⚠️ Depends on opinion
No shoe removal❌✅❌❌ Disqualified (from brothers)
No shoe, no spit❌❌✅❌ No significance at all
Core Rule Shoe removal (חליצה) is the essential act. Without it, nothing of halakhic significance occurs. Spitting and recitation are secondary but may have disqualifying effects.

Why Does Spitting Alone Disqualify from the Brothers?

Gemara
According to R' Akiva, spitting and recitation are both non-essential. Why does spitting disqualify her from the brothers, but recitation has no effect?
R' Akiva's Reasoning
Recitation happens both before and after shoe removal. A witness seeing her recite won't assume the shoe was removed.
R' Akiva's Reasoning
But spitting happens only after shoe removal. A witness seeing her spit may assume she already removed the shoe — leading to confusion.
Gemara
So the Rabbis rabbinically disqualify her from the brothers after spitting, to prevent people from permitting a true ḥalutza to marry the brothers.
מראית עין The concern is appearance: onlookers who see the spit may conclude a full ḥalitza occurred and mistakenly permit a ḥalutza to marry the yavam's brothers.

R' Ami's Ruling: Spitting Before Shoe Removal

A woman comes before R' Ami for ḥalitza.
She spits before removing the shoe.
↓
R' Ami: Tell her to remove the shoe.
She does not need to spit again.
↓
R' Abba bar Memel challenges:
Don't we need proper order?
↓
R' Ami Responds
Proper order is not indispensable.
And requiring a second spit could cause a disaster — people will think spit alone is meaningless!
Supporting Baraita
Whether shoe removal preceded spitting or spitting preceded shoe removal — what is done is done.
Practical Halakha R' Abba bar Memel initially thought R' Ami was making weak arguments, but upon investigation found a baraita confirming that the order of ḥalitza steps is not indispensable.

Minor's Ḥalitza: Male vs Female

Male Minor (Yavam)

  • R' Meir: Ḥalitza disqualifies her from brothers but doesn't permit remarriage
  • Rabbis: Minor's ḥalitza has NO significance at all
  • She remains fully permitted to brothers in levirate marriage

Female Minor (Yevama)

  • R' Meir: Must perform ḥalitza again as adult; first ḥalitza is invalid if she doesn't
  • R' Yosei: Torah says 'man' (adult male required) but 'yevama' — not 'woman'
  • Therefore a female minor CAN validly perform ḥalitza
Textual Basis "And if the man does not wish" (Deut. 25:7) — 'Man' requires adult male. But "his yevama" (Deut. 25:9) uses a general term, not 'woman,' so the female may be a minor.

The Story of R' Yishmael, Abdon, and Rebbi

🗣️ R' Ḥiyya and R' Shimon debate prayer posture — eyes up or eyes down?
↓
🙏 R' Yishmael b. R' Yosei arrives: 'My father said — eyes DOWN, heart UP'
↓
🏃 Rebbi arrives at the study hall — everyone rushes to sit
↓
🚶 R' Yishmael, being heavy, walks slowly over seated students
↓
😤 Abdon (Rebbi's attendant) rebukes him: 'Who steps over a sacred people?'
↓
⚡ R' Yishmael retorts sharply — 'Is your teacher God?' — Rebbi stays silent
↓
👧 A minor yevama comes for ḥalitza — R' Yishmael cites his father R' Yosei that a minor may perform ḥalitza
↓
💥 Abdon is punished with leprosy, two sons drown, two daughters-in-law refuse their marriages
Moral Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak: Blessed is the Merciful One who punishes Abdon in this world — suffering in this world atones and prevents punishment in the World to Come.
