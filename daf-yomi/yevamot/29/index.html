👨‍👨‍👦 The Three Brothers Case

Three brothers: Two married to two sisters; one single
↓
First brother dies childless — his wife requires levirate marriage
↓
The single brother performs levirate betrothal (not full marriage) with the widow
↓
Then the second brother dies — his wife (sister of the betrothed) now also requires levirate from the single brother
↓
Beit Shammai
Betrothed wife stays with him; sister is exempt as sister of his wife
Beit Hillel
He divorces both; both are exempt from levirate marriage and ḥalitza

⚖️ What Does Levirate Betrothal Accomplish?

Beit Shammai
His betrothed wife remains with him. The betrothal has the force of marriage — she is legally his wife, making her sister forbidden to him.
Beit Hillel
He must divorce both women. Levirate betrothal is not a full acquisition; neither woman may remain with him.
Rabbi Elazar
Do not say even Beit Shammai hold that betrothal fully acquires the yevama. It only precludes the rival wife from entering levirate marriage — nothing more.
Rabbi Avin
The mishna itself proves this: Beit Shammai say 'they may maintain them' — after the fact (b'diavad) yes, but not permitted ab initio. Proof that betrothal is not a full acquisition.
Core Dispute Does קידושי יבמין (levirate betrothal) create a full marriage bond, or merely preclude the rival wife from levirate marriage?

💍 Two Readings of Beit Shammai on Levirate Betrothal

Full Marriage Bond

  • Betrothal = complete nisu'in (marriage)
  • Yavam inherits her estate if she dies
  • Kohen-yavam becomes impure for her death
  • Yavam can nullify her vows alone
  • Rival wife fully and completely exempt
  • Supported by: Rabbi Ami bar Ahava (from Nedarim)

Partial / Preclusion Only

  • Betrothal only precludes rival wife's levirate
  • Does NOT create full marital rights
  • Yavam still must bring her under ḥuppa
  • Beit Shammai permit it b'diavad — not ab initio
  • No automatic inheritance or impurity rights
  • Supported by: Rabbi Elazar, Rabbi Avin

🔮 Rabba's Questions on Levirate Betrothal's Legal Force

Rabba's Core Question: Does levirate betrothal create full nisu'in, or only betrothal (eirusin)-level status?
↓
Sub-question A: Does the yavam inherit the yevama's estate upon her death after betrothal?
↓
Sub-question B: Does a kohen yavam incur ritual impurity for her death (as for a full wife)?
↓
Sub-question C: Can the yavam nullify her vows alone, as a husband can — or only jointly with her father?
↓
Abaye's objection: Even without betrothal, the Torah commands the yavam to 'have intercourse with her' — implying he must bring her to his home. Betrothal doesn't alter this requirement.
↓
Rabba's response: Once levirate betrothal is performed, the yavam is removed from the category of yavam entirely — he becomes like a regular arus (betrothed man) and is subject to ordinary betrothal law.

📜 Resolving Rabba's Question via Nedarim 74a

Mishna in Nedarim: Who nullifies vows of a widow waiting for her yavam?
↓
Rabbi Akiva — 1 yavam
Levirate bond not substantial → neither father nor yavam can nullify alone
Rabbi Akiva — 2 yevamin
Weak levirate bond → both together may nullify her vows
↓
Rabbi Ami bar Ahava: Mishna refers to case where one yavam performed levirate betrothal — per Beit Shammai he nullifies her vows alone
↓
Conclusion: Per Beit Shammai, levirate betrothal = full marriage bond — the yavam acquires all a husband's rights, including unilateral vow nullification
