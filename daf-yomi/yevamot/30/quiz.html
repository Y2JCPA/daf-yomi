5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav, what is the critical moment for determining a yevama's levirate obligation?
A. When the court formally rules on her status after her husband's death
B. When the yavam performs ḥalitza or yibbum
C. At the moment she first 'happens before' the surviving brothers for levirate marriage
D. When she chooses to pursue yibbum or ḥalitza
Correct: C — Rav Yehuda teaches: any yevama to whom 'her brother-in-law will have intercourse with her' cannot be applied at the time she first happens before the brothers — she is forever exempt. The moment the levirate obligation crystallizes (the brother's death) is determinative, not later developments.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the core dispute between Rav Ashi and Rav Naḥman about the levirate bond (zikah)?
A. Rav Ashi says ḥalitza is preferred; Rav Naḥman says yibbum is preferred
B. Rav Ashi says the zikah IS substantial (like a full acquisition); Rav Naḥman says it is NOT substantial
C. Rav Ashi limits the bond to full brothers; Rav Naḥman extends it to half-brothers
D. Rav Ashi requires biblical ḥalitza; Rav Naḥman accepts rabbinic ḥalitza
Correct: B — Rav Ashi holds the zikah is substantial — nearly equivalent to full acquisition, with real legal effect on rival wife status. Rav Naḥman holds it is weak, so a rival wife of a forbidden relative is fully exempt even from ḥalitza. Rabbi Yirmeya resolves their dispute by noting the mishnayot they cite reflect different tannaitic opinions.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does Rabba say the mishna (30b) omits the case of 'uncertain whether the get landed closer to her or him'?
A. Because it is a hypothetical scenario that never occurs in practice
B. Because in that case no ḥalitza is required — the rival wife retains her chazakah of being permitted
C. Because that case is already covered by the rule of a get written without witnesses
D. Because the tannaim disagreed so strongly that no consensus could be formulated
Correct: B — Rabba explains: when the get's proximity is uncertain, the rival wife has a chazakah (presumptive status) of being permitted — she was permitted before the uncertain get was ever thrown. The mishna omits this case because ḥalitza is not required; she remains in her presumptive permitted state.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Abaye challenge Rabba's chazakah argument in the case of uncertain get?
A. He argues chazakah is broken the moment any get is thrown, even if proximity is uncertain
B. He points out that the same chazakah logic should exempt the rival wife from ḥalitza in uncertain kiddushin too — but the mishna requires ḥalitza there, proving chazakah does not suffice
C. He cites a baraita that directly contradicts Rabba's ruling on uncertain proximity
D. He argues that once a get is thrown at all, we assume it was received validly
Correct: B — Abaye challenges: by Rabba's logic, the rival wife also has a chazakah of being permitted before any betrothal occurred — so uncertain kiddushin should also exempt her from ḥalitza. But the mishna explicitly requires ḥalitza for uncertain kiddushin. This inconsistency undermines Rabba's chazakah argument.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What does Rabbi Yirmeya mean when he says the mishnayot in this chapter are 'disjointed' (מקוטעות)?
A. The mishnayot were edited by different scribes and contain textual corruptions
B. They were taught by different tannaim who hold fundamentally different halakhic positions, so rulings cannot be derived by comparing them
C. Each mishna is self-contained and may not serve as the basis for a kal va-chomer argument
D. The mishnayot contradict each other irreconcilably and neither ruling is accepted as law
Correct: B — Rabbi Yirmeya resolves the apparent contradiction between this mishna and an earlier one (13a) by saying they were taught by different tannaim with different fundamental positions — e.g., on whether death or the moment of 'happening before' determines the yevama's status. Since they reflect different authorities, one cannot derive halakha by cross-referencing them.
