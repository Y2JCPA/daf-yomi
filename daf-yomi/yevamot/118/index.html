Testimony, Rivals & Uncertain Obligations

Mishna
One rival wife says 'He died', the other says 'He did not die' — the denier may not marry.
Gemara
If she were silent, could she rely on the rival's testimony? But a rival wife cannot testify for another!
Gemara
The Mishna states 'He did not die' to prevent us from thinking she's lying to ruin her rival — 'Let me die with the Philistines.'
R' Tarfon
A wife says her husband died — she may remarry, but her rival wife may still eat teruma.
R' Akiva
That's not the way to spare from transgression! The rival must be forbidden both marriage and teruma.
⚖️ Core Tension When testimony is suspect due to rivalry, how far do we extend stringency — and does 'sparing from transgression' require double restrictions?

↓
Other wife says: 'He did NOT die'
She may not marry
(explicit contradiction)
Other wife is SILENT
Still may not marry
(rival wife cannot testify for another)
💡 Key Insight The Mishna specifies 'He did not die' to teach that we do NOT assume she's lying out of spite. A rival wife's testimony is simply inadmissible regardless of the other's response.
4 / 9

'Let Me Die with the Philistines'

תמות נפשי עם פלשתים
📖 Judges 16:30 The Gemara considers that a rival wife might falsely deny her husband's death to ruin her rival, even though she herself suffers the same consequence — remaining unable to remarry.

This is called tamut nafshi im Pelishtim — willingness to harm oneself in order to harm one's enemy.

The Mishna rejects this reasoning: we do not assume such spite. The restriction is based on the legal inadmissibility of rival wife testimony, not on assumed malice.

R' Elazar vs. R' Yochanan: Who Authored the First Clause?

Mishna
One witness says 'He died,' another says 'He did not die' — she may not marry.
R' Meir
One says 'died,' one says 'killed' — they contradict each other, so she may not marry.
R' Elazar
R' Meir also disagrees with the first clause. The unnamed Mishna follows R' Yehuda and R' Shimon.
R' Yochanan
Even R' Meir agrees: a claim of 'He did not die' is not true contradiction — it's a denial, not conflicting testimony.
Gemara
But the Mishna says 'He did not die' and she may not marry — this is difficult for R' Yochanan!
⚡ Unresolved The Gemara concludes 'kashya' — indeed difficult for R' Yochanan's position, but does not retract it entirely.

R' Tarfon vs. R' Akiva: Teruma & Sparing from Sin

R' Tarfon

  • Wife says husband died → SHE may remarry
  • Rival wife / mother-in-law may NOT remarry
  • But rival wife / mother-in-law MAY eat teruma
  • Reasoning: her testimony is rejected entirely regarding the other woman
  • Halakha follows R' Tarfon (per Shmuel)

R' Akiva

  • Wife says husband died → SHE may remarry
  • Rival wife / mother-in-law may NOT remarry
  • AND rival wife / mother-in-law may NOT eat teruma
  • 'This is not the way to spare from transgression'
  • Must be stringent on BOTH counts
⚖️ Halakha Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: The halakha follows R' Tarfon — the suspect testimony is entirely rejected regarding the other woman, including for teruma restrictions.

Why Both Cases? Rival Wife AND Mother-in-Law

CaseNature of AnimosityWithout This Case...
Rival wife (tzara)Physical suffering — shares husbandWe'd think only HERE does R' Tarfon suspect lying, but not with a mother-in-law
Mother-in-lawVerbal suffering — quarrels onlyWe'd think only HERE does R' Akiva forbid teruma, but with a rival wife she's certainly lying
📐 Talmudic Method Both cases are necessary (tzrikha): each case alone would lead to an incorrect inference about the other. The Mishna teaches that the dispute applies equally regardless of the degree of animosity.
