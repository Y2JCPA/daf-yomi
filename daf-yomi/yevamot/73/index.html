🌾 Three Agricultural Gifts & Their Disputed Laws

Opening Question: Can an uncircumcised man eat maaser sheni (second tithe)? The dilemma is raised before Rav Sheshet.
↓
Rav Sheshet's Proof: Cites mishna (Ḥalla 1:9) — uncircumcised men are not listed among those forbidden from maaser sheni, implying they are permitted.
↓
Rejection: The tanna taught only some differences between categories; the mishna's silence on uncircumcised men proves nothing.
↓
Further Debate: Burning impure maaser sheni and bikkurim for benefit is forbidden — but not so for teruma. What is the biblical source?
↓
Flogging: One who eats impure maaser sheni or bikkurim is flogged. The Gemara seeks and debates the scriptural derivation.

📋 Three Agricultural Categories — Key Differences

Teruma

  • Priestly gift — property of the priest
  • Acute mourner (onen) MAY eat
  • No eradication before Passover required
  • MAY burn when impure — benefit permitted
  • NOT flogged for eating impure teruma
  • Nullified in mixture of 101 parts

Bikkurim (First Fruits)

  • Priestly gift — property of the priest
  • Onen: DISPUTE — Rabbis forbid; R. Shimon permits
  • Eradication: DISPUTE — Rabbis require; R. Shimon exempts
  • May NOT burn when impure — benefit forbidden
  • FLOGGED for eating while produce is impure
  • Torah calls them both 'teruma' AND 'minḥa'

Maaser Sheni (Second Tithe)

  • Owner's property — eaten in Jerusalem
  • Acute mourner (onen) may NOT eat
  • Eradication required before Passover (4th & 7th years)
  • May NOT burn when impure — benefit forbidden
  • FLOGGED for eating while produce is impure
  • Nullified in mixture of 101 parts

✂️ Can an Uncircumcised Man Eat Maaser Sheni?

Questioners
The Paschal lamb forbids an uncircumcised man from eating it. Is maaser sheni derived from the Paschal lamb (and thus also forbidden)? Or is the lenient case of maaser sheni independent?
Rav Sheshet
You have already learned the answer in the mishna! Ḥalla 1:9 lists what applies to teruma and bikkurim but NOT to maaser sheni — and an uncircumcised man's prohibition is not mentioned. Therefore he MAY eat maaser sheni.
Gemara
Rejected! The tanna taught only SOME differences and omitted others. He also omitted the fact that eating impure maaser sheni incurs flogging. An argument from silence cannot stand.
Rav Ashi
Even from the FIRST clause of the mishna no proof can be adduced. The question of the uncircumcised man and maaser sheni remains unresolved.
Halakhic Principle An argument from a tanna's silence (omission) fails when the same tanna demonstrably omits other known halakhot in the same passage.

😢 Acute Mourner & Bikkurim — Rabbi Shimon vs. the Rabbis

Rabbis
An acute mourner (onen) is forbidden from eating both maaser sheni AND bikkurim (first fruits). Both require joy and intentional eating in holiness; a mourner on the day of bereavement is precluded from this.
Rabbi Shimon
An onen is PERMITTED to eat bikkurim! The Torah calls bikkurim 'teruma' — so the laws of eating follow teruma. Just as an onen may eat teruma, so too may he eat bikkurim.
Rabbis
The Torah ALSO calls bikkurim a 'minḥa' (Exodus 23:19). A mourner who eats a minḥa violates a prohibition. Therefore the 'teruma' analogy cannot apply to permit an onen to eat bikkurim.
Rabbi Shimon
The 'minḥa' derivation governs the BRINGING of bikkurim to the Temple, not the eating. The 'teruma' designation governs the eating — and therefore the onen may eat.
Also Disputed R. Shimon likewise exempts bikkurim from eradication (bi'ur) before Passover in the 4th and 7th years of the Sabbatical cycle. The Rabbis require eradication for both maaser sheni and bikkurim.

🔥 Burning Impure Produce for Benefit — Permitted or Forbidden?

Sacred agricultural produce became ritually impure and cannot be eaten. May you burn it and benefit from the heat or fire?
↓
Teruma
YES — Permitted
The verse 'of it' (Deuteronomy context) excludes teruma from the prohibition. Impure teruma may be burned and the fire's heat may be enjoyed.
Maaser Sheni & Bikkurim
NO — Forbidden
Burning impure second tithe or first fruits for benefit is prohibited. These sacred categories are more protected than teruma in this regard.
Consecrated Items (Kodashim)
NO — Forbidden
Also prohibited. Kodashim carry the most stringencies of all (mem, ḥet, peh, zayin), so they are certainly included in the prohibition against burning for benefit.
↓
Gemara's Logic: 'Of it you may not eat' implies 'of it you may not burn for benefit.' Teruma is excluded by the verse; consecrated items are included because their accumulated stringencies are greater than those of teruma.
