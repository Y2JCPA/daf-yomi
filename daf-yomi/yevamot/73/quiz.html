Yevamot 73 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
What was the basis of Rav Sheshet's argument that an uncircumcised man may eat maaser sheni?
A. A direct verse in the Torah explicitly permits it
B. The mishna in Ḥalla lists prohibitions for teruma and bikkurim but does not mention uncircumcised men regarding maaser sheni
C. He derived it from a kal va-chomer from the laws of the Paschal lamb
D. An acute mourner is permitted to eat it, so an uncircumcised man certainly can
Correct: B — Rav Sheshet cited the mishna in Ḥalla (1:9) which lists what applies to teruma and bikkurim but not to maaser sheni, arguing that since uncircumcised men are not listed as forbidden from maaser sheni, they must be permitted. The Gemara ultimately rejected this argument.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why did the Gemara reject Rav Sheshet's proof from the mishna's silence?
A. The mishna was discussing a different topic and was not relevant
B. The tanna of that mishna taught only some differences and demonstrably omitted others, so silence proves nothing
C. The mishna in fact explicitly mentions that uncircumcised men are forbidden
D. Rav Sheshet was citing the wrong mishna
Correct: B — The Gemara pointed out that the tanna omitted other known differences — such as the prohibition on burning impure tithe and the flogging for eating impure tithe. Since the mishna is known to be incomplete, the omission of the uncircumcised man proves nothing.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
On what basis did Rabbi Shimon permit an acute mourner to eat bikkurim (first fruits)?
A. Bikkurim become ownerless once delivered to the Temple, so anyone may eat them
B. The Torah calls bikkurim 'teruma,' so their eating laws follow teruma, which an onen may eat
C. An onen prohibition is purely rabbinic and bikkurim are exempt from rabbinic restrictions
D. Rabbi Shimon held that the onen prohibition applies only in the Temple, not outside it
Correct: B — Rabbi Shimon reasoned that since the Merciful One calls bikkurim 'teruma,' their eating laws should follow teruma. Just as an onen is permitted to eat teruma, so too may he eat bikkurim. The Rabbis countered that the Torah also calls bikkurim 'minḥa,' which is forbidden to an onen.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Regarding produce that became ritually impure: for which category is one permitted to burn it and benefit from the fire?
A. Maaser sheni only, since it is the owner's property
B. Bikkurim only, since they are compared to a minḥa offering
C. Teruma only — the verse 'of it' excludes teruma from the prohibition against burning for benefit
D. All three categories — teruma, bikkurim, and maaser sheni
Correct: C — The Gemara derives from the verse 'of it' that impure teruma may be burned and the fire's benefit enjoyed. By contrast, impure maaser sheni and bikkurim may NOT be burned for benefit — this stringency applies to them but not to teruma.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Who receives malkot (flogging) in connection with impure sacred produce?
A. One who eats impure teruma while he himself is ritually pure
B. One who eats maaser sheni or bikkurim that are themselves ritually impure, even if the person eating is pure
C. One who burns teruma when it is impure and uses the fire for heat
D. An acute mourner who eats maaser sheni in violation of the prohibition
Correct: B — The Gemara derives from Deuteronomy 12:17 that one who eats maaser sheni (and by extension bikkurim) that is itself ritually impure is subject to flogging — even if the person eating is ritually pure. This stringency does NOT apply to teruma.
