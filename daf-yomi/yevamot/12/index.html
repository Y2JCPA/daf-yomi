⚖️ Which Rival Wives Are Forbidden in Levirate Marriage?

Rav Asi
The rival wife of an aylonit is forbidden — the aylonit's marriage was valid, so her rival wife falls under the levirate bond.
Rav Sheshet
Objection from mishna: The rival wife of a fully fit yevama who underwent levirate betrothal became forbidden — implying only a fit yevama creates such a prohibition, not an aylonit!
Rav Yosef
'This is' refers to the rival wife of a fit yevama — the emphatic phrase actually excludes the aylonit's rival wife into the forbidden category, not the permitted one.
Gemara (Mishna)
The mishna says 'were found to be aylonit' — discovered after marriage. That was a mistaken betrothal, so rival wife is permitted. Even if known beforehand, levirate never applied to her — rival wife still permitted.
Final Ruling The rival wife of an aylonit is always permitted — either because of mistaken betrothal (unknown at marriage) or because the levirate obligation never applied to her in the first place (known at marriage).

🗣️ 'Fit' and 'Disqualified' — What Do These Terms Mean?

Rabbi Ami
The baraita says he performs ḥalitza with the disqualified woman. 'Disqualified' must mean disqualified for him specifically — like a woman who performed refusal (me'un), who is forbidden only to this yavam.
Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba
No! 'Disqualified' means disqualified for all men — e.g., a divorcée forbidden to marry any priest. Her rival wife is still fit to others; he performs ḥalitza with the disqualified one to release both.
New Baraita
One who remarries his divorcée after she wed another — she and her rival wife must perform ḥalitza. The case of rival-wife ḥalitza exists even when one woman is disqualified through a prior marriage.
Key Principle A woman disqualified only for the specific yavam (through divorce or refusal) still creates a levirate bond requiring ḥalitza to release her rival wife.

🚫 Why Is the Rival Wife of a Me'un Girl Forbidden?

Shmuel: The rival wife of a girl who performed refusal (me'un) is forbidden to the yavam
↓
She refused the husband (before death)
This equals divorce — but mishna says rival wife of a divorcée is permitted! Contradiction.
She refused the yavam (after death)
She was never married to the yavam — her refusal retroactively annuls the first marriage, unlike actual divorce.
↓
Rabbinic decree: If the girl who refused was the yavam's own daughter, her rival wife would be forbidden as rival of a forbidden relative. To prevent confusion, ALL such rival wives are forbidden.
↓
From the moment the rival wife came before the yavam, she appears as his father's daughter-in-law — even before refusal occurs. Appearance alone triggers the rabbinic prohibition.

🔍 Rival Wife Status: Forbidden vs. Permitted

Forbidden to Yavam

  • Rival wife of his daughter who refused — she was a forbidden relative
  • Rival wife of me'un girl — rabbinic decree due to above case
  • Rival wife of yevama who underwent levirate betrothal before dying
  • Rival wife when one wife is any forbidden relative (e.g., sister)

Permitted to Yavam

  • Rival wife of aylonit unknown at marriage — mistaken betrothal, bond void
  • Rival wife of aylonit known at marriage — levirate obligation never applied
  • Rival wife after forbidden wife died, divorced, or refused
  • Rival wife of divorcée — not a forbidden relative, no rabbinic decree

📅 Rabba bar Livai: Three Stages of a Minor Girl

בנים הרי הן כסימנים — Rav Safra (12b)
Age-Based Pregnancy Limits Under age 11: Cannot become pregnant at all — body is not yet capable.

Ages 11–12 (from one day past 11 to one day past 12): Can become pregnant but both she and her fetus will die — she uses the contraceptive resorbent.

Over age 12: Pregnancy proceeds normally like any adult woman — no special precautions needed.

Implication (Rav Safra): A girl who gives birth has adult legal status — children are equivalent to signs of puberty, removing her minor status retroactively.
