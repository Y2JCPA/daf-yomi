What Makes a Valid Shoe for Ḥalitza?

Yevama must remove shoe from yavam's foot
(Deuteronomy 25:9)
↓
Does the footwear protect the foot?
Yes ✅
Valid: Leather shoe, wooden prosthetic, wooden sandal, another's shoe
No ❌
Invalid: Cloth anpileya, torn shoe exposing foot, oversized/undersized shoe
Key Principle The shoe must be protective footwear fitting for walking — not merely a covering. The yavam's posture (standing, sitting, leaning) does not matter, but his foot must press the ground.

What Makes the Prosthetic Valid?

Abaye
The entire baraita follows the Rabbis (R' Yosei). The prosthetic is valid only because it's covered in leather.
Rava
If leather covering is the key, why doesn't the baraita distinguish within the prosthetic case itself? It should say: 'When covered in leather — valid; without — invalid.'
Rava
Rather, the whole baraita follows Rabbi Meir. The prosthetic is valid because it protects the foot, while the cloth anpileya is invalid because it lacks a hard sole.
Abaye
(Silence — Rava's logical argument from the baraita's structure prevails)
Resolution R' Meir doesn't require leather — he requires protective function. A wooden prosthetic protects; a cloth shoe without a hard sole does not.

Ameimar's Foot Requirements

Yavam performs ḥalitza
↓
Must he press his foot to the ground?
Ameimar: Yes
Even if sitting or leaning, foot must be pressed down
Rav Ashi objects
Baraita says leaning is valid — hard to press foot while leaning!
↓
Ameimar answers: All positions valid — as long as foot is pressed down (even if difficult while leaning)
↓
Is the yavam clubfooted (walks on backs of feet)?
No
✅ Can perform ḥalitza
Yes
❌ Cannot perform ḥalitza — his foot cannot press normally
Practical Rav Ashi noted that bar Uva and bar Kipof — famous eulogizers with crooked feet — could never perform ḥalitza.

"His Shoe" — Whose Shoe May Be Used?

Derash: "His Shoe"

  • Verse says "his shoe" — implies it must belong to him
  • But the word "shoe" appears twice in the passage
  • Double mention teaches: ANY person's shoe is acceptable
  • "His" = fitting for HIM, not necessarily owned by him

Exclusions from "Fitting"

  • Shoe so large he cannot walk in it
  • Shoe so small it doesn't cover most of his foot
  • Sandal hamesulyam — heelless sandal unfit for walking
Derivation "Na'alo" (his shoe) teaches fitness, not ownership. The shoe must fit the yavam properly to count as his footwear.

Abaye's Left Sandal — A Practical Lesson

Rav Yosef
Abaye, give the yavam your sandal so he can perform ḥalitza.
Abaye
(Gives his left sandal)
Rav Yosef
The left shoe is only valid after the fact (bedi'avad). Should we really use it ab initio (l'khatchila)?
Abaye
But using another's shoe is also only valid after the fact — so aren't we already in bedi'avad territory?
Rav Yosef
My intention was: give him your sandal as a gift, transferring ownership so it becomes his shoe — l'khatchila!
Halacha To perform ḥalitza in the ideal manner, the sandal should belong to the yavam. A temporary gift transfer solves the ownership issue.
