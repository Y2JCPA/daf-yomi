Yevamot 91: Penalties & the Duress Defense

Woman remarried on false testimony of husband's death
↓
Full penalties applied: loses ketubah, disqualified from teruma/tithe
↓
Dispute: How far do penalties extend?
Rav Huna
Later Sages concede to earlier — penalties expand outward
R' Yoḥanan
Earlier Sages concede to later — penalties contract inward
Core Theme The Gemara systematically tests when a woman's lack of fault excuses her from penalties — and consistently holds she must verify before acting.

Ḥalitza: Brother of First vs. Second Husband

Brother of First Husband

  • Performs ḥalitza — by Torah law
  • She was legally his brother's wife
  • Does NOT consummate yibum
  • Reason: Sages penalized her from returning to first husband

Brother of Second Husband

  • Performs ḥalitza — by rabbinic law
  • So people won't say she can leave without ḥalitza
  • Does NOT consummate yibum
  • Reason: Marriage to second was an error — no real bond
Result Both brothers perform ḥalitza but neither consummates levirate marriage — she is stuck between two families with no full resolution.

The Concession Hierarchy: Who Agrees with Whom?

Rav Huna
The later Sages concede to earlier ones and expand their rulings. R' Shimon concedes to R' Elazar: if he doesn't penalize sexual relations, he certainly doesn't penalize earnings.
Rav Huna
R' Elazar and R' Shimon both concede to R' Yosei on the ketubah: if they don't penalize her during marriage, certainly not at its end.
R' Yoḥanan
No — the earlier Sages concede to later ones. The first opinions are more inclusive; later Sages restrict.
R' Yoḥanan
R' Yosei concedes to R' Elazar: if she keeps her ketubah, all the more so she keeps found objects and earnings.
R' Yoḥanan
R' Elazar concedes to R' Shimon: if the husband retains her earnings, certainly the yavam acquires her through relations.
Dispute Rav Huna and R' Yoḥanan read the kal va-ḥomer in opposite directions — producing completely different pictures of which Sages agree with each other.

Ravina's Distinction: Court vs. Witnesses

Woman remarried — first husband returns
↓
On what basis did she remarry?
Court Permission
Treated as willful act (ones)
→ No offering required
Individual who follows court is exempt
Witness Testimony
Treated as unwitting act (shogeg)
→ Must bring an offering
Ravina The baraita discusses offerings, not divorce requirements. Court-sanctioned remarriage = ones (exempt); witness-based = shogeg (liable for offering).

Ulla's Challenge: 'What Could She Have Done?'

🔍 Ulla objects to Rav Sheshet: shouldn't duress excuse her from penalties?
↓
📜 Case 1: Get dated to wrong kingdom — she should have had a scholar read it
↓
👩 Case 2: Rival wife of aylonit remarried — she should have waited to confirm
↓
📋 Case 3: Scribe swapped get and receipt — she should have had the document read by expert
↓
✏️ Case 4: Wrong names on get — again, should have had it read by scholar
↓
📄 Case 5: Bare get missing signatures — should have had it verified
↓
⚖️ Consistent answer: She bears responsibility to verify before acting
Principle The Gemara rejects the 'what could she have done?' defense in every case — a woman must exercise due diligence before remarrying, whether checking a get or confirming testimony.
