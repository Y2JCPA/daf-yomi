Yevamot 102 — The Ḥalitza Shoe

וְחָלְצָה נַעֲלוֹ מֵעַל רַגְלוֹ — She shall remove his shoe from upon his foot (Devarim 25:9)
DAF OVERVIEW This daf explores who may serve as judges for ḥalitza, and then dives deep into the physical requirements of the ḥalitza shoe — debating whether a soft leather shoe (min'al) or only a hard leather sandal (sandal) is valid, what materials are permitted, and linguistic analysis of the word ḥalitza itself as meaning removal.

If Elijah Comes: Min'al vs. Sandal

Rabba (in name of Rav)
If Elijah says a soft leather shoe (min'al) is valid — we listen.
If he says a hard sandal is invalid — we do not listen, for the people already practice with a sandal.
Rav Yosef (in name of Rav)
If Elijah says a soft leather shoe is invalid — we listen.
If he says a hard sandal is invalid — we do not listen, for the people already practice with a sandal.
Gemara
Both agree on the sandal. The difference is: may one use a min'al ab initio (לכתחילה)?
Rabba's View
No — only if Elijah comes and permits it. Until then, min'al is not used ab initio.
Rav Yosef's View
Yes — min'al is valid ab initio until Elijah comes and says otherwise.
KEY PRINCIPLE Established communal practice (minhag) is so powerful that even Elijah the Prophet cannot override it.

Why Not Use a Soft Shoe (Min'al) Ab Initio?

Reason 1: Torn Shoe

  • A soft leather shoe can still be worn when torn
  • A torn shoe doesn't properly cover the foot
  • People might accidentally use a torn min'al for ḥalitza
  • A torn sandal would never be worn — no such concern

Reason 2: Half Shoe

  • The ḥalitza shoe must cover most of the foot
  • Some people wear soft shoes that cover only half the foot
  • One might mistakenly use a half-shoe for ḥalitza
  • A half sandal is never worn — no such concern
NOTE Since the prohibition is only rabbinic (a decree), the min'al is valid after the fact (בדיעבד) — it is merely not used ab initio.

Underage Yevama Growing Up Among Brothers

👧 An underage yevama grows up in the household of her husband's brothers
↓
👟 Concern: perhaps she casually removed a sandal from one of the brothers?
↓
⚖️ Rav rules: she may still perform levirate marriage — we are not concerned
↓
🤔 But didn't a baraita teach that ḥalitza requires mutual intention?
↓
📋 First version: Even if she was seen removing a shoe, no concern — intention was lacking
↓
📋 Second version: If seen removing a shoe — she is disqualified for the brothers, even without intention
↓
💡 Distinction: Intention needed to permit marrying a stranger, but even unintentional ḥalitza disqualifies levirate marriage
TWO VERSIONS The two versions of Rav's statement disagree on whether unintentional shoe removal is sufficient to disqualify her for levirate marriage with the brothers.
