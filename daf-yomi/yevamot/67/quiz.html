QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Gemara's conclusion, why does Rabbi Yosei rule that a pregnant widow's slaves may not eat teruma?
A. Because the fetus in a non-priest's womb is itself a non-priest — it takes on the mother's status
B. Because the fetus is a potential male heir with a claim to the slaves
C. Because the widow has lost her priestly status by marrying an Israelite
D. Because the slaves are considered consecrated property during pregnancy
Correct: B — The Gemara concludes that Rabbi Yosei's reason is the inheritance rationale (יורש) — the fetus may be a male heir with ownership over the slaves. This is proven by Rabbi Yosei's own words: 'This case I heard; this case I did not hear,' which only makes a logical distinction under the inheritance theory.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai, if a priest left male children and his widow is pregnant, may the slaves eat teruma?
A. No — the fetus might be a male heir who would displace the children
B. Yes — we follow the majority; most fetuses do not become male heirs
C. Only usufruct slaves may eat teruma, not guaranteed-investment slaves
D. Only if the court determines the widow is in her third trimester
Correct: B — Rabbi Shimon rules that the slaves may eat teruma because we follow the majority: roughly half of fetuses are female and some are stillborn, so most fetuses will not become male inheritors. The court also appoints a steward (apotropos) for the fetus's potential share.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Abaye explain the baraita's case where a son does not enable the slaves to eat teruma?
A. A minor son lacks legal standing to confer teruma rights until he reaches bar mitzvah
B. The son's priestly status is in question until he performs his first Temple service
C. The estate is insufficient property — daughters receive sustenance, so the son effectively cannot inherit and cannot enable the slaves
D. The son must first perform ḥalitza before his priestly rights activate
Correct: C — Abaye explains that the case involves an estate only large enough to sustain the daughters. The Sages enacted that daughters receive sustenance from such an estate, so the son has no effective inheritance — and the fetus (if male) would be in the same position, unable to enable the slaves.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
The new Mishna teaches that the fetus, yavam, and betrothal all share the same effect on a daughter of a priest's teruma rights. What is that shared effect?
A. They all enable her to eat teruma if her husband was a kohen
B. They all disqualify her from eating teruma but do not enable her
C. They suspend her teruma rights temporarily until the matter is resolved
D. They enable teruma rights only if there is no levirate bond
Correct: B — The Mishna teaches that the fetus, the yavam (levirate husband), betrothal to an Israelite, and other cases all disqualify the daughter of a priest from eating teruma — but none of them enable her. She cannot be 'returned to her father's house' as long as any of these bonds persist.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Shmuel, what is the difference between Rabbi Yosei's view and the Rabbis' view regarding the deceased priest's slaves?
A. Rabbi Yosei says both types of slaves may eat teruma; the Rabbis say neither may
B. Rabbi Yosei's ruling is only for Israelite widows; the Rabbis extend it to priestly widows too
C. Rabbi Yosei's ruling applies where there are no children; the Rabbis distinguish based on whether children exist
D. Rabbi Yosei and the Rabbis fully agree — the dispute is only about the reason
Correct: C — Shmuel explains that Rabbi Yosei's ruling (slaves may not eat) applies specifically when the priest died without children and the widow is pregnant. The Rabbis hold: if the dead husband has children, slaves eat teruma due to the children; if he has no children, they eat due to the widow — and if she is pregnant, she herself still eats while the slaves may not.
