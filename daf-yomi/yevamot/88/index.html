Why Is One Witness Believed About a Husband's Death?

Core Question: What is the source for accepting ed echad (one witness) that a husband died?
↓
Attempt 1: Because silence = admission? Rejected — no proof witness is actually believed
↓
Attempt 2: Like uncertain meat — one witness resolves doubt? Rejected — married woman has chezkat issur
↓
Attempt 3: Analogy to tevel, hekdesh, konamot? Rejected — each case is due to personal power, not witness credibility
↓
R' Zeira's Answer: The stringency imposed if testimony is wrong justifies the leniency of accepting one witness
KEY PRINCIPLE The Gemara tests multiple logical analogies for why one witness suffices, rejecting each before arriving at R' Zeira's principle: the severe consequences of error justify the initial leniency.

Why Does Silence Obligate an Offering?

Gemara
The mishna says: if he remains silent, the Rabbis obligate him to bring an offering based on one witness's testimony. Why?
Attempt
Perhaps the witness is deemed credible?
Gemara
Rejected! Even with two witnesses, if he contradicts them he is exempt. One witness should certainly not obligate him!
Conclusion
Rather, silence is like admission (shtikah k'hoda'ah). The offering stems from his silence, not from the witness's credibility.
IMPLICATION If the reason is silence = admission, this proves nothing about a single witness being independently believed.

The Uncertain Meat Analogy — And Its Rejection

Proposed analogy: Like uncertain meat (safek chelev, safek shuman) — one witness says 'it's permitted fat' and is believed
↓
Uncertain Meat
No chezkat issur established — never proven forbidden
Married Woman
Chezkat issur IS established — she was definitely married
↓
Counter: Unlike definite chelev (where even 100 witnesses can't help), here 2 witnesses could permit — so maybe 1 can too?
↓
This leads to the analogy of tevel, hekdesh, and konamot...
DISTINCTION The key difference: forbidden fat with established status can never be permitted by witnesses, but a married woman's status CAN change through testimony — opening the door for one witness.

Three Failed Analogies: Tevel, Hekdesh & Konamot

CategoryCase ProposedWhy Rejected
Tevel (untithed produce)Witness says tithes were separated from another's produceIf he can tithe it himself → believed because of b'yado (in his power). If not → this very case needs its own source!
Hekdesh (consecrated property)Witness says the item was redeemed or the vow dissolvedIf it's his → b'yado to redeem. If another's → from where do we derive he's believed?
Konamot (vow-prohibitions)Witness says the owner asked a Sage to dissolve the vowSame problem: if his own → b'yado. If another's → this itself needs a source
PATTERN Each analogy collapses because the witness is believed only when the matter is 'in his power' (b'yado) — which doesn't apply to testifying about another's husband.

R' Zeira's Resolution

מתוך חומרא שהחמרת עליה בסופה — הקלת עליה בתחילתה
BREAKTHROUGH R' Zeira said: Due to the stringency that the Sages imposed on her at the end — if the testimony proves false and the husband is alive, the consequences are devastating — therefore, they were lenient at the beginning to accept one witness.

The severity of the consequences deters false testimony and justifies the leniency of relying on a single witness.

Contradicting Pairs of Witnesses (Amud B)

First Tanna (Majority View)

  • 2 say dead + 2 say alive → she may NOT marry
  • But if she married anyway → she need NOT leave
  • Applies whether witnesses came before or after marriage

R' Menaḥem b. R' Yosei

  • If contradicting witnesses came BEFORE marriage → she must leave
  • If she married FIRST and then witnesses came → she need not leave
  • Timing of contradiction is decisive
RAV'S RULING Rav rules she need not leave — either following the first tanna entirely, or (per 'some say') following R' Menaḥem b. R' Yosei's timing distinction.
