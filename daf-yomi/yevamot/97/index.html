Yevamot 97 — Identity, Lineage & Kinship After Transformation

גר שנתגייר כקטן שנולד דמי
Core Theme This daf explores how identity and family bonds are redefined — from the eternal voice of Torah scholars beyond the grave, to distinguishing eunuchs from late bloomers, to the dramatic erasure of family ties upon conversion. The Gemara uses vivid riddles to illustrate how incestuous unions create tangled relationships, contrasting this with the halakhic principle that a convert is like a newborn child.

Torah Scholars Live On Through Their Words

שפתותיו דובבות בקבר
R' Shimon ben Yoḥai Any Torah scholar in whose name a halakha is stated in this world — his lips mouth the words in the grave.

Derived from Shir HaShirim 7:10: "Like the best wine that glides down smoothly… moving gently the lips of those who are asleep."

Just as pressing a finger on a pile of grapes (כומר) causes wine to burst forth and the whole pile to move — so too, when a teaching is said in a scholar's name, his lips move in the grave.

Rape/Seduction vs. Marriage: Relatives' Status

Raped or Seduced a Woman

  • May marry her daughter (Torah level)
  • May marry her mother or sister
  • Rabbinic prohibition only if she is still alive
  • Key: 'Lying' (שכיבה) is the Torah's term

Married a Woman

  • Prohibited from marrying her daughter
  • Prohibited from her mother or sister
  • Full Torah prohibition — capital punishment or karet
  • Key: 'Taking' (קיחה) is the Torah's term
Key Distinction The Torah uses "taking" (לקיחה) for wife's relatives — only through marriage are these relationships forbidden by Torah law, not through non-marital intercourse.

May a Man Marry a Woman His Father Raped?

Rabbanan
A man may marry a woman raped or seduced by his father. Non-marital intercourse does not create the forbidden relationship.
R' Yehuda
A man may not marry a woman raped or seduced by his father. Even non-marital intercourse creates a prohibition.
Rami bar Ḥama
The riddle — 'My half-brother is my mother's husband, and I am the daughter of his wife' — is only valid according to the Rabbanan, not R' Yehuda.
Practical Result According to the Rabbanan, a man can marry a woman his father had relations with (outside marriage), creating the strange family dynamics the Gemara explores.

The Family Riddles (Solved with Gentile Cases)

Riddle StatementSolution
"He is my brother and my son"Gentile had relations with his daughter — their son is both her brother and son
"Peace, my son; I am the daughter of your sister"Gentile had relations with his granddaughter (daughter's daughter) — she bore him a son
"This boy I carry is my son; I am the daughter of his brother"Gentile had relations with his son's daughter — their child is also her uncle
"Woe! My brother is my father, my husband, and son of my husband"Gentile had relations with his mother, then with their daughter; then his father had relations with her too
"You and I are siblings; your father and I are siblings; your mother and I are siblings"Gentile had relations with his mother, producing two daughters, then with one daughter producing a son
Why Gentiles? The Gemara uses gentile examples because it does not wish to entertain the idea that a Jew would commit such acts. These riddles illustrate how incest tangles lineage.
