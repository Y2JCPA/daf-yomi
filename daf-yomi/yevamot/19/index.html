⚖️ Does the Levirate Bond Make Her Like a Married Woman?

Rav Oshaya
Rabbi Shimon holds the levirate bond (zikah) IS substantial — the yevama is 'like a married woman,' exempting her rival wife from further obligation.
Abaye
You cannot prove this from that baraita! Perhaps Rabbi Shimon holds the bond is strong only when there is ONE yavam — not when two yevamin face him simultaneously.
Rav Oshaya
But the mishna shows Rabbi Shimon exempts BOTH women — this implies the bond creates full marital-like status regardless of the number of yevamin!
Rav Ashi
Resolution: If yevamot appear for yibbum one after the other, the first IS like a married woman and exempts the second. If both fall simultaneously, neither exempts the other.
Core Question Does the levirate bond alone grant the yevama the status of a married woman, automatically exempting her rival wife — or must actual yibbum or ḥalitza occur?

👶 What Happens When Brother C Is Born After Levirate Betrothal?

Brother A dies childless.
His widow falls for yibbum to surviving brothers B and C.
↓
Brother B performs ma'amar (levirate betrothal) with Widow A.
↓
Brother C born BEFORE ma'amar
Normal rules apply. B must complete yibbum or perform ḥalitza. No 'coexistence' problem.
Brother C born AFTER ma'amar
C never coexisted with A's marriage — she is 'wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist,' creating an exemption problem.
↓
If Brother B then dies: Widow A goes free (exempt). But what of Brother B's original wife? She too may be exempt — or not — depending on the strength of the zikah.

📜 Rabbi Shimon vs. The Rabbis — Rival Wives and the Levirate Bond

Rabbi Shimon

  • Zikah IS substantial — yevama is 'like a married woman'
  • First woman bound exempts her rival wife
  • ḥalitza or yibbum with one exempts the other
  • But: ḥalitza alone with the betrothed does NOT exempt rival
  • Derives exemption from Lev. 18:18: 'You shall not take a woman to her sister'

The Rabbis

  • Zikah alone is NOT sufficient for full marital status
  • Each yevama remains bound independently
  • Each requires her own ḥalitza or yibbum
  • Rival wife is NOT automatically exempted by bond
  • Two mishnayot repeat the same ruling for different factual scenarios

🤝 Non-Consensual Levirate Betrothal: Valid or Not?

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
Non-consensual levirate betrothal IS valid! Just as non-consensual intercourse (yibbum) makes her his wife, so too forcible ma'amar. We derive yibbum laws from yibbum laws — they form one halakhic category.
The Rabbis
Non-consensual ma'amar is NOT valid. Levirate betrothal requires consent. We do NOT derive laws of ma'amar from laws of yibbum — they are distinct legal categories that cannot be conflated.
The Principle at Stake Can halakhic rules governing yibbum (levirate intercourse) be analogically applied to derive rules of ma'amar (levirate betrothal)? Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: yes. The Rabbis: no — they are separate domains.

🔍 Rava's Interpretation — and the Gemara's Correction

וְאִשָּׁה אֶל-אֲחֹתָהּ לֹא תִקָּח לִצְרֹר (ויקרא יח:יח) — 'You shall not take a woman to her sister, to be a rival'
Rava's Proposal — Then Retracted Rava proposed: The baraita where Rabbi Shimon exempts 'both' involved four pairs of related wives across two deceased brothers. He thought this complex arrangement explained how exemption cascades from one woman to another.

The Gemara corrects him: Rava was mistaken — the mishna only describes two brothers who each married two sisters (not four pairs). Two proofs: (1) the mishna's plain language describes only two pairs, (2) an explicit baraita quotes Rabbi Shimon's reasoning from a Torah verse, making Rava's construction unnecessary.

The explicit baraita states: Rabbi Shimon exempts both women from ḥalitza and yibbum based on the verse — no complicated four-pair reading required.
