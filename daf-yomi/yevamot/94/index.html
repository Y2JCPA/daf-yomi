Rav Sheshet's Proof from the Mishna

Rav Sheshet
The mishna says: If told 'your husband died, then your child died,' she remarried, then learned the matters were reversed — she must leave, and both children are mamzerim.
Rav Sheshet
If this were two vs. two witnesses, why rely on the second pair over the first? The child should only be a safek mamzer!
Rav Sheshet
It must be one witness contradicted by two — proving a single witness is normally credible for such testimony!
Gemara
No — as Rav Aḥa bar Manyumi explained: It's two vs. two, but the second pair are edei hazamah (conspiring witnesses).
Gemara
They didn't just contradict the story — they said the first witnesses were elsewhere at the time. This fully disqualifies them.
Hazamah Edei hazamah don't merely offer an alternate account — they testify the first witnesses couldn't have seen what they claimed, completely invalidating their testimony.

Rav Mordekhai's Attempted Proof

Mishna (118b): A woman is not credible to say:
'My yavam died' (to permit herself to marry)
↓
Inference: It's specifically she who isn't credible —
implying one witness would be credible?
↓
Problem: The same mishna says a man isn't credible to say 'my brother died'
↓
Woman's Case
Sages were lenient due to iguna (deserted wife) concern
Man's Case
No iguna concern — a man can marry multiple wives
↓
Conclusion: The mishna's exclusion of the woman herself doesn't imply one witness is credible — it addresses Rabbi Akiva's opinion specifically
Why Mention Her? Under Rabbi Akiva, a yevama's child from an unlawful marriage is a mamzer. You might think she'd be extra careful — so the mishna teaches she fears her own ruin but not her children's.

Rava's Kal Va-Chomer & Its Refutation

Rava
One witness should be credible for a yevama by kal va-chomer: If for karet (married woman/adultery) one witness suffices, then for a regular prohibition (yevama to another man), certainly!
Challenger
The woman herself disproves this: For karet she is believed, but for a regular prohibition (yavam's death) she is not!
Challenger
Why isn't she believed? Because sometimes she hates the yavam and won't investigate carefully.
Challenger
The same applies to one witness — the court only trusts him because they assume she verified. But if she hates the yavam, she won't verify his report either!
Gemara
The kal va-chomer collapses. The question of one witness for yevama remains unresolved.
Core Logic One witness is trusted for a married woman because the court assumes she independently verifies before remarrying. If she hates the yavam, this assumption fails — so the witness's credibility depends on her diligence, not merely the severity of the prohibition.

Sister's relationship with his relativesPermitted (e.g., his son)
If first wife later diesHe may now marry the sister
Child born while wife aliveMamzer (born from union with sister-in-law)

Principle An erroneous marriage to a wife's sister is treated as mere licentious relations — it invalidates the sister for her own husband but does not affect the man's original marriage.

Rabbi Akiva's Get Requirement: Betrothal vs. Marriage

Rabbi Akiva's Rule

  • Wife's sister who was erroneously married requires a get
  • Brother's wife erroneously taken in yibum requires a get
  • These women could become permitted (sister after wife dies; brother's wife via yibum)

Rav Giddel's Limitation (in Rav's name)

  • Rabbi Akiva's rule applies only to betrothal (kiddushin), not full marriage
  • Reason: Onlookers might think there was a condition in the betrothal that was unfulfilled
  • They'd conclude the second marriage was valid — hence a get is needed to dispel this
  • But with full marriage (nisuin): no one assumes a condition — so no get needed
Practical Result Our mishna involves actual marriage, not mere betrothal — so even Rabbi Akiva agrees no get is needed, and the wife remains permitted to her husband.
