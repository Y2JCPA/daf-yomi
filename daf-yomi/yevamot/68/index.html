⚖️ Abaye vs. Rava: The Nine-Year-Old Yavam

Abaye
The mishna refers to a nine-year-old yavam who already had intercourse with his yevama — she was acquired by Torah law. The Rabbis decreed she cannot eat teruma lest people conclude that a minor's betrothal is fully valid.
Rava
If so, why does the mishna also discuss uncertainty whether he is nine years and one day old? For a case already acquired, there would be no such uncertainty! Rather, the mishna's nine-year-old is among the unfit males who disqualify women by intercourse.
Gemara
Rava's position is accepted: the nine-year-old is an Ammonite, Moabite, Egyptian, Edomite convert, or similar unfit male — his intercourse disqualifies a woman from marrying into the priesthood, even though he cannot legally marry her.
Halakhic Result Rava's view prevails: the mishna's nine-year-old is an unfit male whose intercourse disqualifies, not a yavam acquiring a yevama.

📖 Deriving Disqualification from Leviticus 22

Lev. 22:12: 'If a priest's daughter be married to a common man [זָר], she may not eat of that which is set apart from the sacred'
↓
Rav (via Rav Yehuda): The extra phrase 'set apart from the sacred' teaches that intercourse with an unfit man renders a woman unfit for teruma and priesthood marriage
↓
Objection
Isn't that verse needed to prohibit the priest's daughter (who married a non-priest) from eating teruma?
Answer
Lev. 22:13 ('widow/divorced returns to father's house') already covers the basic disqualification from teruma — freeing Lev. 22:12 for this new teaching

🔗 Which Women Are Disqualified by Unfit Intercourse?

Priest's Daughter

  • Explicitly stated in Lev. 22:12
  • Disqualified from eating teruma
  • Disqualified from marrying into priesthood
  • Primary source of the entire halakha

Levite / Israelite Woman

  • Derived via vav-prefix ('and if a priest's daughter') — Rabbi Akiva's method
  • Or: derived from inclusive reading of the verse
  • Also disqualified from teruma if she had a son from a priest
  • Disqualified from priesthood marriage equally

The Rejected A Fortiori

  • Proposed: if a priestess (sacred by birth) is disqualified, certainly a Levite/Israelite woman
  • Rejected: non-sacred women may be MORE susceptible — logic can go either way
  • Conclusion: a verse, not logic, must provide the source
  • Final derivation stands on textual grounds alone

🔍 The 'Common Man' Verses — Counting the Sources

Gemara Q
The phrase 'no common man [זָר] shall eat of the sacred' appears twice in Leviticus. One verse teaches the basic prohibition — non-priests may not eat teruma. What does the second teach?
R. Yosei b. R. Ḥanina
The phrase וְזָר לֹא יֹאכַל קֹדֶשׁ teaches: I, God, declared that common men shall not eat — but a common man who is a priest's husband enables his wife to eat teruma by virtue of their marriage bond.
Gemara
If one verse goes to R. Yosei b. R. Ḥanina and one to the basic prohibition — there is still a third 'common man' context available. The extra phrase 'that which is set apart from the sacred' (תְּרוּמַת הַקֳּדָשִׁים) in Lev. 22:12 teaches disqualification by unfit intercourse.
Key Hebrew Phrase תְּרוּמַת הַקֳּדָשִׁים — 'that which is set apart from the sacred' — the seemingly superfluous expression is the textual hook for teaching disqualification by unfit intercourse

⚙️ The Full Chain of Derivation — Step by Step

Step 1: Lev. 22:12 — 'If a priest's daughter marries a common man, she may not eat teruma' → seemingly teaches the basic prohibition
↓
Step 2: Lev. 22:13 already covers that basic case (widow/divorced returns home) → so Lev. 22:12 is free to teach something new
↓
Step 3: Two 'common man' prohibitions exist → one for basic rule, one for R. Yosei b. R. Ḥanina's teaching → the extra phrase in Lev. 22:12 teaches disqualification by unfit intercourse
↓
Step 4: Extension to Levite/Israelite women — derived via vav-prefix inclusion (Rabbi Akiva's method) or inclusive reading of 'and if a priest's daughter'
↓
Step 5: Disqualification from priesthood marriage derives from the same verse — the inclusion of Levite/Israelite also covers marriage disqualification, not just teruma
↓
Step 6: Gentile/slave intercourse also disqualifies — though they cannot marry her, their intercourse still renders her unfit, as derived separately by Rabbi Yitzḥak
