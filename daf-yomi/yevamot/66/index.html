📦 Melog vs. Tzon Barzel — Two Types of Marital Property

Melog (Usufruct) Slaves

  • If slaves die → her loss
  • If value increases → her gain
  • Husband enjoys their labor only
  • Considered 'her property' throughout
  • Do NOT eat teruma if wife is a forbidden wife
  • Partake if wife is a permitted Israelite

Tzon Barzel (Guaranteed) Slaves

  • Husband bears full financial responsibility
  • Returned at fixed appraised value
  • Husband is treated as 'owner' during marriage
  • Rav Yehuda: she gets actual items back
  • Rabbi Ami: he returns monetary value only
  • Rav Naḥman (final ruling): halakha follows Rav Yehuda

🗣️ Why Can't the Forbidden Wife's Slaves Eat Teruma?

Gemara Q
Let her slaves eat teruma! They are 'his acquisition who acquired an acquisition' — the verse (Lev. 22:11) explicitly includes them!
Ravina
The principle is: whoever partakes of teruma enables others; whoever does not partake, cannot enable others. Since the forbidden wife cannot eat teruma herself, her slaves cannot eat either.
Rava
By Torah law they CAN eat teruma — they are indeed included in 'his acquisition who acquired an acquisition.' The Sages decreed a penalty prohibiting it as punishment upon the priest who transgressed.
Rav Ashi
It is a rabbinic decree — lest she enable the slaves to eat teruma even after the priest's death. To prevent this, the Sages treated her throughout as a non-partaker.
Key Insight All three agree on the practical ruling — her slaves do not eat. The debate is whether this is Torah-level (Ravina) or a rabbinic decree (Rava, Rav Ashi), and if a decree, what motivated it.

🔍 Rav Ashi's Refined Decree — The Widowed Priestess Case

Objection to Rav Ashi: If the decree is 'lest she feed them teruma after her husband's death,' then any Israelite woman married to a priest should also be restricted from enabling her slaves to eat teruma!
↓
Rav Ashi's Refinement: The decree targets specifically a widowed daughter of a priest (בת כהן) who then married the High Priest.
↓
After the High Priest dies, she might reason: 'I am a daughter of a priest — I myself may eat teruma in my father's house — therefore my slaves may also partake.' This logic is flawed but dangerously plausible.
↓
An ordinary Israelite woman has no such reasoning — after her husband's death she clearly loses all priestly privilege for herself and her slaves with no ambiguity.
↓
Remaining Question: What if an Israelite woman (not a priest's daughter) married the High Priest? The Gemara accepts the gap — the Sages decreed broadly to cover the priestess case and the Israelite case follows automatically.

💰 Guaranteed Property at Dissolution — Rav Yehuda vs. Rabbi Ami

Rav Yehuda
The halakha favors her — she may reclaim the actual items upon divorce or death. They are assets of her paternal family, and their family prestige suffers if the items are not returned in kind.
Rabbi Ami
The halakha favors him — he may retain the actual items and return only their monetary value. Since he bore full financial responsibility throughout, the items effectively became his.
Baraita (supports Rav Yehuda)
He may not sell guaranteed property — it belongs to her. Nor may she sell it — for he bears responsibility for its loss. Both sides protect the item itself.
Rav Naḥman (via Rava)
Final ruling: Halakha follows Rav Yehuda — the wife reclaims the actual items. Though a baraita supports Rabbi Ami, it does not override the established ruling.
Legal Principle Tzon barzel property: the husband bears financial risk but the wife retains ultimate ownership — she gets the items themselves returned upon dissolution of the marriage.

קנייה ליה מיתה — הקדש וחמץ ושחרור מפקיעין מידי שעבוד
Case Study — The Buried Robe A woman brought a fine wool robe (itztela de-meileta) into her marriage as guaranteed (tzon barzel) property. Her husband died and the orphans (heirs) buried him in the robe. She then came to collect her guaranteed property back. Rava ruled: The dead has acquired it (קנייה ליה מיתה). Deriving benefit from items consecrated for the dead is prohibited — she cannot take it back. Apparent contradiction: But Rav Naḥman ruled (per Rava himself!) that halakha follows Rav Yehuda — she gets the actual items! Resolution: Even Rav Yehuda agrees when the item has been consecrated for burial. Consecration, prohibition of chametz on Pesach, and hekdesh (Temple dedication) all take effect immediately upon designation — overriding prior ownership claims.
