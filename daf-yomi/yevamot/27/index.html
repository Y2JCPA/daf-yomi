⚔️ Rav vs. Rabbi Yoḥanan: Two Sisters Before One Yavam

Rav
If he performed ḥalitza with the first sister, the second is now permitted to him for levirate marriage.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
If the second sister dies, he may take the first. But if the first sister dies, he is permanently prohibited from taking the second.
Rav
If the first sister dies before any ḥalitza, he is permitted to take the second — just as one may marry the sister of a deceased wife.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Any yevama to whom the verse 'her brother-in-law will have intercourse with her' cannot be applied at the moment she falls before him — is forever prohibited, even if the disqualification later disappears.
Core Dispute Rav: death of a co-yevama dissolves the prohibition retroactively. Rabbi Yoḥanan: the status at the moment of zikka (falling before the yavam) is determinative and permanent.

🧩 Shmuel's Three Brothers Case: Ḥalitza and Rival Wives

Setup: Three brothers. Two married wives including two sisters. Both married brothers die. All widows fall before the third brother as yavam.
↓
Yavam performs ḥalitza with the TWO SISTERS
Rival wives are NOT exempt — must themselves perform ḥalitza
Yavam performs ḥalitza with the RIVAL WIVES
Sisters ARE exempt from the levirate bond — but still require ḥalitza (not levirate marriage)
↓
Why? The sisters are forbidden as rivals of his zikka-wife who is her sister. Ḥalitza with a defectively-bound woman cannot release her rival wives.

🔗 Is the Levirate Bond (Zikka) Substantial? — Shmuel's Disputed Ruling

Shmuel
The levirate bond IS substantial — it creates a genuine quasi-marital tie making the yevama like an actual wife.
Gemara
If the bond is substantial, the sister IS related to him as sister of his wife — so why isn't she exempt entirely rather than needing ḥalitza?
First answer
Shmuel stated this ruling according to those who say the bond is NOT substantial — even though he himself disagrees.
Rav Ashi
No — Shmuel follows his own view that the bond IS substantial. The sisters must perform ḥalitza because they are rivals of a woman forbidden to him, not because the bond is weak.
Baraita
Supports Rav Ashi: 'If he performed ḥalitza with the sisters, the rival wives are NOT exempt — but if he performed levirate marriage with the sisters [hypothetically], their rivals ARE exempt.'
Conclusion Rav Ashi's reading of Shmuel prevails: zikka IS substantial; the sisters' defective status (as rivals of a forbidden co-wife) is what disqualifies their ḥalitza from releasing their own rivals.

📜 Which Has Precedence for Ḥalitza — Get or Ma'amar?

📄 Bill of Divorce (Get)

  • Yavam gave a get to one sister-in-law
  • A get partially severs the levirate bond
  • Argument: stronger dissolution → higher priority
  • If get were superior, subsequent ma'amar would be void — but it is NOT

💍 Levirate Betrothal (Ma'amar)

  • Yavam performed ma'amar with one sister-in-law
  • Ma'amar intensifies the levirate bond
  • Argument: stronger bond → more urgent release needed
  • If ma'amar were superior, subsequent get would be void — but it is NOT

⚖️ Rav Ashi's Resolution

  • Neither clearly precedes the other
  • The Sages dispute both cases — showing equivalence
  • Each can follow the other without being nullified
  • Question remains formally unresolved (תיקו)

👥 Rav Huna citing Rav: Two Sisters, One Yavam — Step by Step

Setup: Two brothers each married one of two sisters. Both brothers die. Both sisters fall before the surviving brother as yavam — each is the other's rival.
↓
Option A — Ḥalitza with the first sister: First sister is released and permitted to others. The second sister is now permitted to the yavam for levirate marriage.
↓
Option B — First sister dies before any action: The yavam is now permitted to take the second sister in levirate marriage.
↓
Reason for Option B (Rav): The situation is now equivalent to one yevama falling before him alone. Just as a man may marry the sister of his deceased wife, the death dissolves the mutual prohibition.
↓
Rabbi Yoḥanan disagrees with Option B: The second sister is permanently prohibited because at the moment she fell before him, the verse could not apply to her — and that initial status is determinative.
