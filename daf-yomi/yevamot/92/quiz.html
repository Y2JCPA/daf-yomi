QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does Ze'eiri compare the court's permission to remarry to the court declaring the sun has set?
A. Both involve factual errors, not mistaken legal reasoning
B. Both require the court to bring a communal offering
C. Both are valid rulings that exempt individuals from offerings
D. Both involve reliance on multiple witnesses
Correct: A — Ze'eiri's point is that just as declaring the sun has set is a factual observation (not a legal ruling), so too the court's permission based on witness testimony is a factual determination — making it a ta'ut (error), not a hora'ah (ruling).
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is Rav Nachman's proof that the court's permission constitutes a legal ruling (hora'ah)?
A. The Torah explicitly calls it a ruling
B. Normally one witness is not credible, yet here he is — because the court's authority backs the decision
C. The court performs a formal vote before permitting her
D. The woman is exempt from a sin-offering, which only applies to rulings
Correct: B — Rav Nachman argues that the fact that one witness is believed — contrary to the normal rule — shows that the woman relies on the court's authoritative decision, not merely on the witness, making it a hora'ah.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to R' Yochanan, what does 'ruined herself' (קלקלה) mean in the mishna?
A. She engaged in licentious sexual relations
B. She deliberately deceived the court
C. She married in a prohibited manner, such as a widow to a Kohen Gadol
D. She refused to leave her second husband
Correct: C — R' Yochanan interprets 'ruined herself' as marrying in a prohibited manner (e.g., widow to Kohen Gadol, divorcée to a kohen). For mere licentious relations, she could claim 'you deemed me unattached' and would not be liable.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rav, what is the status of betrothal (kiddushin) performed with a yevama by a man other than the yavam?
A. It is fully valid
B. It is valid but she needs ḥalitza first
C. It does not take effect at all
D. It is valid only if the yavam consents
Correct: C — Rav derives from Devarim 25:5 ('shall not be married outside the family') that betrothal by another man simply does not take effect with a yevama. Shmuel, however, is uncertain whether the verse prohibits or nullifies, and therefore requires a get.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does the Gemara refute Reish Lakish's claim that the mishna about yevama betrothal follows only R' Akiva?
A. By showing that R' Akiva agrees betrothal works for a yevama
B. By citing another mishna that explicitly follows the Rabbanan
C. By noting that R' Akiva holds one can transfer something not yet in existence — so 'after ḥalitza' should work for him
D. By proving that the Rabbanan also hold betrothal is ineffective for prohibitions
Correct: C — R' Akiva holds that one can acquire something not yet in existence. Therefore, 'you are betrothed after your yavam performs ḥalitza' should be valid according to R' Akiva — yet the mishna says it's not. This means the mishna's reasoning must be that betrothal inherently doesn't apply to a yevama, not just R' Akiva's prohibition-based rule.
