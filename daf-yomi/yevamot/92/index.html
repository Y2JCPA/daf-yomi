Daf 92: Court Errors, Offerings & Yevama Betrothal

Woman remarries with court permission based on one witness
↓
Husband turns out to be alive — she must leave
↓
Is the court's permission a ruling (hora'ah) or a factual error (ta'ut)?
Hora'ah (Ruling)
Rav Nachman: Court brings communal sin-offering
Ta'ut (Error)
Ze'eiri, Rava, R' Eliezer: She brings individual sin-offering
Core Theme This daf explores the legal nature of court permission — whether it constitutes an authoritative ruling or merely a factual determination — and examines whether betrothal (kiddushin) can take effect with a yevama.

Rumors After Marriage

אין חוששין לקול לאחר נישואין
Rav Ashi's Principle Even when a woman's case was discussed in court before she was permitted to remarry, we do not give credence to rumors that arise after her marriage.

One might think that since her case required court deliberation, her status is uncertain — but Rav Ashi teaches that once married, unsubstantiated rumors are disregarded.

Is Court Permission a Ruling or an Error?

Ze'eiri
The mishna's exemption from a sin-offering is not accepted. Just as when the court declares the sun has set and then it shines again — that is an error (ta'ut), not a ruling. Each individual must bring their own offering.
Rav Nachman
The court's permission is a ruling (hora'ah). Proof: normally one witness is not credible, yet here he is. Why? Because she relies on the court's decision, not on the witness alone.
Rava
It is an error. With forbidden fat, once the court reverses a ruling, a new argument cannot reinstate permission. But here — one witness permits, two forbid, one more permits again. The court merely follows facts from witnesses, not its own reasoning.
R' Eliezer
יקוב הדין את ההר — 'Let the law pierce the mountain!' If the husband is alive, she brings a sin-offering. If it were a ruling, the court should bring the offering — proving it's an error.
Key Argument Rava's proof: Unlike standard rulings on forbidden substances, the court here changes its mind based on new witnesses, not new legal reasoning — indicating factual error, not mistaken ruling.

What Does 'Ruined Herself' Mean?

R' Eliezer

  • She engaged in licentious relations (not for marriage)
  • If liable for this, she is all the more so liable for marrying a Kohen Gadol as a widow
  • Broader scope of liability

R' Yochanan

  • She married in a prohibited manner (e.g., widow to Kohen Gadol, divorcée to a kohen)
  • For mere licentious relations she is NOT liable
  • She can claim: 'You deemed me unattached — I may live with whom I choose'
Baraita A baraita supports R' Yochanan: 'If the court ruled she may marry and she ruined herself — for example, a widow with a Kohen Gadol, or a divorcée/ḥalutza with a kohen.'

Rav Ashi's Practical Ruling (per Ameimar/Shmuel)

A man betroths a yevama before ḥalitza
↓
Per Shmuel: she needs a get from the second man
↓
What is the yavam's status?
Yavam is a Kohen
He cannot marry her (she's a divorcée). He performs ḥalitza → she may marry the second man.
Yavam is a Yisrael
Second man gives her a get → she is permitted to the yavam (non-priest may marry a divorcée).
Principle A sinner must not benefit from his transgression — so the case where the yavam is a kohen is structured to prevent the second man from gaining through his illegal betrothal.
