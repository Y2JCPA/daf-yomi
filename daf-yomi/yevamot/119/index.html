↓

Core Dilemma The Mishna rules she is stuck: she cannot marry a stranger (maybe yibum is required) and cannot do yibum (maybe the tzara is pregnant, exempting her). The Gemara explores why we don't simply follow the majority of women who give birth.

Evident vs. Non-Evident Majority (Rov)

Ruba D'Ita Kaman

  • Evident majority — physically present and countable
  • Example: Meat found near 9 kosher stores and 1 non-kosher
  • Example: Sanhedrin majority vote
  • Rabbis agree: we follow this majority

Ruba D'Leta Kaman

  • Non-evident majority — based on general statistics
  • Example: Most women become pregnant and give birth
  • Example: Most minors develop normally
  • Dispute: Do Rabbis follow this type of rov?
Key Distinction The Gemara initially suggests that only R' Meir is concerned about minorities in non-evident cases, but proves from the case of minor boys and girls that the Rabbis also follow non-evident majorities — forcing us to attribute our Mishna to R' Meir.

R' Meir vs. The Rabbis on Minors & Yibum

R' Meir
A minor boy may not do chalitza or yibum. A minor girl likewise — neither chalitza nor yibum.
Rabbanan
Agreed on chalitza — the Torah says ish (man), implying an adult. But why not yibum?
R' Meir
I am concerned the boy may turn out to be a saris (underdeveloped male) or the girl an aylonit — then the yibum was with a forbidden relative!
Rabbanan
Follow the majority — most minors develop normally. This is not a real concern.
Gemara
This proves the Rabbis do follow a non-evident majority! So our Mishna must follow R' Meir who is concerned about the minority.
Proof The case of minors refutes the claim that Rabbis ignore non-evident majorities, establishing our Mishna as following R' Meir.

Chazaka + Minority vs. Majority: Why She Can't Marry

First clause: Husband had a brother (yavam exists)
↓
Chazaka: She is bound to yibum
↓
Rov: Most women give birth → tzara likely had a child → no yibum needed
↓
But add the minority who miscarry to the chazaka…
↓
Minority + Chazaka ≈ equal balance against the majority
↓
Result: Cannot marry stranger, cannot do yibum — stuck
Latter Clause When the mother-in-law is overseas and husband had no brothers, the chazaka supports her freedom to marry. The possibility of a new yavam is a minority of a minority — even R' Meir is not concerned about that.

How Long Must She Wait? Ze'eiri vs. R' Chanina

Ze'eiri
She waits 9 months (for the tzara's possible pregnancy to resolve), then performs chalitza as a safeguard, then waits 3 months before remarrying.
Ze'eiri
If the tzara gave birth — she's free anyway. If not — the chalitza exempts her. Either way she's covered.
R' Chanina
For herself: 3 months. But for the tzara's pregnancy — she must wait indefinitely until it is verified whether the tzara gave birth.
Gemara
But R' Chanina — why not just do chalitza after 9 months like Ze'eiri says?
Abaye b. Avin & R' Chanina b. Avin
It's a rabbinic decree: if the tzara had a viable child, the chalitza was unnecessary — and then people at the chalitza ceremony who missed the later announcement (hakrazah) might think a chalutza may marry a Kohen!
The Hakrazah Problem If chalitza turns out to be superfluous (because a child was born), an announcement is needed that she may still marry a Kohen. But people who saw the chalitza but missed the announcement would be misled.
