👤 Offspring Status: Rava vs. Abaye

Rava
My proof: the mishna lets a child of a priest and Israelite woman enable teruma — even in an equally prohibited union. This shows we attribute the child to the rumored priest, even when other men are also rumored.
Abaye
The mishna involves only a rumor about the priest — no competing rumors! Rav's rule stands: if she is rumored with others, the offspring is a mamzer regardless of the priest rumor.
Rava
Even if she is rumored with others, so long as she is also rumored with him — we cast the child after the priest. The mishna supports this reading.
Central Dispute When a woman is rumored with a priest AND with non-priests, does the child follow the priest (Rava) or is the child a mamzer (Rav / Abaye)?

🤔 Rava's Proof from the Mishna — Contested

Rava
A priest committed intercourse with an Israelite woman — both acts equally prohibited. Yet her child enables her to eat teruma. This proves we follow the priest-rumor even amid other rumors.
Abaye
No — the mishna speaks of a case where only the priest is rumored. That's precisely why the child enables teruma. Your inference collapses if the mishna lacks competing rumors.
Rava
Why else state 'extramarital'? The very term implies competing possibilities — and still the child follows the priest. My reading is correct.
Stakes The outcome determines whether a child born in ambiguous circumstances is a mamzer (carrying severe restrictions on marriage) or a legitimate son of the priest.

⚠️ The Mamzer's Dual Halakhic Role

Mamzer Disqualifies from Teruma

  • A priest's daughter who has an unfit child
  • Even an unfit (mamzer) child counts halakhically
  • Derived: 'ein la' — examine her (Lev. 22:13)
  • Her child's disqualified status blocks her return
  • Child of child also disqualifies — same principle

Mamzer Enables Teruma

  • A priest's daughter whose child is from a priest
  • Even if the child is a mamzer — priestly lineage counts
  • Derived: 'ayyein ala' — inspect her child carefully
  • Unfit child can still carry teruma-enabling status
  • The verse's ambiguity reveals both outcomes

📜 Gezera Shava: Deriving the Uncircumcised Priest's Exclusion

Question: Where is it derived that an uncircumcised priest (ערל) may not eat teruma?
↓
Rabbi Elazar
Gezera shava: 'sojourner and hired servant' (Exodus 12:45 ↔ teruma law) — links the same exclusion to both
Rabbi Akiva
Doubled word 'ish ish' (Lev. 22:4) includes uncircumcised among those barred from holy things
↓
Gemara's refinement: Only the Paschal lamb phrase is superfluous — not the teruma phrase — so the analogy is valid from one available side
↓
Conclusion: Uncircumcised priest is excluded from teruma — bodily condition parallels Paschal lamb exclusion

😔 Can the Verbal Analogy Exclude an Acute Mourner?

Challenge raised: If the gezera shava bars the uncircumcised from teruma, why not also bar an acute mourner (onen)? Acute mourning has many stringencies.
↓
Argument for including mourner: Acute mourning applies any time a close relative dies, unlike circumcision which is resolved by a single act. It is arguably stricter.
↓
Counter-argument: Lack of circumcision is a personal bodily deficiency — like leprosy and zav, listed in Lev. 22:4. Acute mourning is a temporary external circumstance.
↓
Rava's ruling: Leviticus 22:10 — 'No stranger (zar) shall eat holy things' — only foreignness as a status disqualifies, not a passing emotional/ritual state.
↓
Resolution: The verbal analogy extends only to bodily personal conditions (uncircumcision). Acute mourning is excluded — the mourner may eat teruma once burial is complete.
