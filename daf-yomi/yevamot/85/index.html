Yevamot 85 — Penalties, Prohibitions & Priestly Daughters

DAF OVERVIEW This daf examines whether daughters of kohanim are warned against marrying disqualified men, the ketubah rights of sheniyot (secondary forbidden relatives) in levirate marriage, and a major dispute about who bears the penalty when a couple violates priestly marriage prohibitions — with practical ramifications tested across multiple edge cases.
3 / 9

Are Daughters of Kohanim Warned Against Marrying Disqualified Men?

Rav Pappa
The Mishna in Kiddushin 69a lists who may marry whom — it never says priestesses may marry a ḥalal. This implies they are forbidden!
Rav Huna b. R' Yehoshua
No proof! The Mishna teaches where men may marry women and vice versa. Since a male kohen can't marry a ḥalala, the Mishna skips the entire category.
Rav Idi bar Avin
This is what Rav Yehuda said in Rav's name: Daughters of kohanim who are fit to marry kohanim are not warned against marrying disqualified men.
RULING The prohibition of marrying disqualified men applies to male kohanim — daughters of kohanim are not independently warned.

Priestly Impurity: Why Only Male Kohanim?

Torah states: "Speak to the kohanim, sons of Aaron" (Lev. 21:1)
↓
"Sons of Aaron" — not daughters of Aaron
Impurity prohibition excludes women
↓
Why did the Torah need to exclude them? Wouldn't we have assumed they're included?
Gemara's Initial Thought
Because Rav Yehuda/Rav: Women are equated to men for all punishments
Gemara's Rejection
No — inclusion is from "lo yikḥu" (marital laws), not from a general equation
KEY POINT The verse "they may not take" includes women in marital prohibitions of kehuna, but not necessarily in all priestly laws like impurity.

Ketubah of a Sheni'ya in Yibum

ScenarioKetubah from Husband?Ketubah from Yavam?
Normal levirate widowYes — from first husband's estateOnly if first husband left no property
Sheni'ya of the husband (not of yavam)No — she was a secondary forbidden relativeNo — even from the yavam
Woman whose husband died without propertyNo property availableYes — Sages instituted from yavam
RAV SHESHET'S RULING A sheni'ya of the husband does not receive a ketubah even from the yavam, because the primary obligation stems from the first husband, and she had no ketubah rights from him.

Marriage Contract Penalties: Who Gets Penalized?

Both Disqualified (e.g., Widow + Kohen Gadol)

  • She becomes a ḥalala; he is disqualified until divorce
  • HE encouraged HER — he bears the penalty
  • She KEEPS her ketubah
  • Torah prohibition — marriage cannot last

Both Fit (Secondary Relatives)

  • Neither is disqualified from priesthood
  • SHE encouraged HIM — she bears the penalty
  • She FORFEITS her ketubah
  • Rabbinic prohibition — penalty needed to speed divorce
LOGIC The one who benefits more from the prohibited marriage is presumed to be the encourager, and the Sages penalize that party through the ketubah.

R' Yehuda HaNasi vs. R' Shimon ben Elazar: Why the Different Penalties?

R' Shimon ben Elazar
The key factor is who encourages whom. When he disqualifies her → he encourages, so he pays. When neither is disqualified → she encourages, so she forfeits.
R' Yehuda HaNasi
Torah prohibitions don't need strengthening — she keeps the ketubah. Rabbinic prohibitions need strengthening — she forfeits it to incentivize divorce.
Gemara
But what about a ḥalutza married to a kohen? That's rabbinic, yet she keeps her ketubah! R' Yehuda HaNasi must adopt the 'encourager' logic for that case too.
CHALLENGE The case of a ḥalutza forces R' Yehuda HaNasi to partially adopt R' Shimon ben Elazar's reasoning — since he disqualifies her, he is the encourager and must pay.
