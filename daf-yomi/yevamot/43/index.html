🪮 Comb Mishna: Challenging the Principle of Unattributed Authority

Rabbi Naḥum
The halakha does NOT follow the comb mishna in Kelim — so how can you say halakha always follows an unattributed mishna? This is a direct counterexample!
Rabbi Ḥiyya
That mishna is not truly authoritative because it contradicts itself internally — the first clause conflicts with the latter clause.
Rav Huna bar Manoaḥ
Furthermore, those exact in their rendition of the tradition append 'This is the statement of Rabbi Meir' — so it is not truly anonymous at all!
Principle An unattributed mishna is authoritative only when self-consistent and genuinely anonymous — the comb mishna fails both tests

📆 How to Count the Three-Month Period (Practical Halakha)

Day of death or divorce: Does not count — excluded from the three months
↓
Month 1: Count only the majority of days (more than half of the month suffices)
↓
Month 2: Count the entire middle month in full
↓
Month 3: Count only the majority of days (more than half suffices)
↓
Day of betrothal: Does not count — must be after the three-month period has elapsed
↓
Nursing mother: Special case — must wait 24 months, also excluding the first and last days

💍 Emending Rabbi Yosei's Ruling — Betrothal vs. Marriage

Rav Ḥisda
A fortiori: If betrothal is prohibited even in the period before Tisha B'Av (a national mourning), how can Rabbi Yosei permit betrothal during a widow's personal mourning? The widow's mourning should be at least as stringent!
Gemara
The prohibition before Tisha B'Av applies to the week of the fast — but Rabbi Yosei's ruling addresses a period only prior to that week, which is less severe.
Rava
Even from that earlier period the a fortiori holds! Therefore: do not read Rabbi Yosei as permitting betrothal — emend his ruling to say all women may be married within three months.
Rav Ashi
Alternatively: new mourning (for a recently deceased relative) is fundamentally different from old mourning (Tisha B'Av, historical tragedy) — the comparison never applied to begin with.
Emendation (Rava) Rabbi Yosei: All women who were only betrothed may marry within three months — a widow alone may not, due to the mourning period she must observe

👨‍👨‍👧 New Mishna (43b): Levirate Cases — Four Brothers & Two Wives

Four Brothers, Four Wives

  • Two brothers die childless; two brothers survive
  • Four wives become yevamot (levirate-bound)
  • The eldest surviving brother cannot marry all four simultaneously
  • He must perform ḥalitza with some to release them from the bond
  • Marriage to one does not automatically free the others

One Husband, Two Wives

  • Husband dies childless; both wives become yevamot
  • Yavam performs levirate marriage OR ḥalitza with one wife
  • This act automatically releases the rival wife
  • The rival wife need not undergo levirate marriage or ḥalitza separately
  • One act covers both women — they are bound as a unit
