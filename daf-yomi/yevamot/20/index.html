🏛️ Two Categories of Rabbinic Prohibition — Sages vs. Rabbi Yehuda

Sages' View

  • Prohibition from Mitzva: Secondary forbidden relationships (rabbinic fences around Torah law)
  • Why 'mitzva'? Abaye: They derive from a mitzva — fencing the Torah's intent
  • Prohibition from Sanctity: Widow to High Priest; divorcée or ḥalutza to common priest
  • Why 'sanctity'? Written: 'He shall be holy' (Lev. 21:6) — priestly holiness demands it
  • Both categories: ḥalitza required, levirate forbidden

Rabbi Yehuda's View (Reversed!)

  • Prohibition from Mitzva: Widow to High Priest; divorcée/ḥalutza to common priest
  • Why 'mitzva'? These priests are commanded by a positive mitzva to sanctify themselves
  • Prohibition from Sanctity: Secondary rabbinic forbidden relationships
  • Why 'sanctity'? Abaye: Whoever fulfills the Sages' words is called sacred
  • Rava objects: Is someone who violates Sages' words thereby unsanctified?

👑 Why Can't the High Priest Take a Widow in Levirate Marriage?

Rav (via Rav Giddel)
The verse states: 'His yevama shall ascend to the gate to the Elders and say: my brother-in-law refused…' — only a yevama who could consummate levirate marriage is required to do ḥalitza. A widow forbidden to the High Priest must do ḥalitza but cannot do levirate.
Rava (objection)
A baraita says: if a prohibited yavam engages in intercourse with his yevama or performs ḥalitza, both are effective. If Rav is right that only 'eligible' women need ḥalitza, why would ḥalitza be needed after intercourse already acquired her?
Rava (resolves)
The baraita speaks disjunctively: 'intercourse' refers to prohibition-from-mitzva cases; 'ḥalitza' refers to prohibition-from-sanctity cases. They are two separate rulings, not one combined case.
Rava (rejects Rav entirely)
Actually Rav's principle is wrong. The real reason: a High Priest cannot take a widow because this violates both a positive mitzva — 'take a wife in her virginity' (Lev. 21:13) — AND a prohibition. Any Torah ban backed by both blocks levirate marriage.
Conclusion Rav's derivation from the verse is rejected. Rava's 'double violation' principle — positive mitzva + negative prohibition — explains why the High Priest cannot take a widow, leaving ḥalitza as the only valid option.

🔄 Rava's Rabbinic Decree Against the First Act of Intercourse

Scenario: A yavam prohibited from levirate marriage (e.g., High Priest with a widow) performs the first act of intercourse with his yevama.
↓
Legal puzzle: The first act technically fulfills the levirate mitzva — but the man is forbidden from marrying her. Does the mitzva 'override' the prohibition for that first act?
↓
Rava's initial answer: The first act is forbidden by rabbinic decree, because permitting it would inevitably lead to a second act — which has no mitzva justification and would be a clear Torah violation.
↓
Baraita confirms: If he violated the decree and cohabited, he acquires her with the first act — but he is immediately prohibited from retaining her and must divorce her.
↓
Rava retracts: That reason isn't fully correct — there is a simpler basis for the rabbinic prohibition. The decree stands on its own grounds independent of 'slippery slope' logic.

⚔️ Rabbi Yoḥanan vs. Rabbi Elazar — High Priest Who Cohabited

Rabbi Yoḥanan
If a High Priest engaged in intercourse with his widow-yevama: she is fully acquired as his wife. The first act of intercourse, despite being rabbinically prohibited, still accomplishes the levirate acquisition.
Rabbi Elazar
Even after such intercourse, the legal situation is more complex — the interaction between the priestly prohibition and the levirate obligation affects what was accomplished and what still remains required.
Core Question When a prohibited yavam violates the rabbinic decree and cohabits with his yevama, does the first act fully discharge the levirate obligation and acquire her as a wife — or does the prohibition undermine the legal effect of the act? This dispute between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Elazar closes the daf.
