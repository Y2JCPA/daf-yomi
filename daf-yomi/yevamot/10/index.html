⚖️ Does Ḥalitza Fully Dissolve All Levirate Bonds?

Reish Lakish
The ḥalitza performer is not liable for karet with his ḥalutza — his karet was replaced by the lesser 'does not build up' prohibition. But the other brothers remain liable for karet with the ḥalutza, and all brothers remain liable for karet with her rival wife.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
No brother is liable for karet — not for the ḥalutza and not for the rival wife. Before the ḥalitza, any brother could have performed it; one brother acts as agent for all. Similarly, one wife's ḥalitza releases her rival wife too.
The Core Verse לֹא יִבְנֶה — "Does not build up" (Devarim 25:9): the prohibition against marrying one's ḥalutza. Reish Lakish: this replaces karet only for the performer. Rabbi Yoḥanan: ḥalitza dissolves all bonds entirely.

🧠 Why Rabbi Yoḥanan Rejects Partial Release

Reish Lakish
The performer of ḥalitza personally dissolved his own karet bond — so for him, only 'does not build up' remains. The other brothers never performed ḥalitza, so the ḥalutza retains her status as a forbidden brother's wife relative to them — full karet applies.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Is there anything like this in all of halakha? Before the ḥalitza, any brother could perform it. They all stood in the same position! How can one act create different legal statuses for different brothers? The performer acts as agent for all — his ḥalitza releases everyone equally.
Rabbi Yoḥanan's Parallel Just as the ḥalitza performer acts as agent for his brothers, the ḥalutza performs ḥalitza as agent for her rival wife — releasing the rival wife from the levirate bond entirely.

📜 The Baraita Challenge and Resolution (Reish Lakish ↔ Rabbi Yoḥanan)

The Case: A brother performs ḥalitza with his yevama, then goes back and betroths her
↓
Baraita — First Clause: If a brother arose and betrothed her after the ḥalitza, she requires ḥalitza from all the remaining brothers before remarrying — implying the betrothal took effect
↓
Rabbi Yoḥanan's Challenge to Reish Lakish: If karet applies, why does she need ḥalitza? Karet means the betrothal never took effect at all — yet the baraita says it did!
↓
Baraita — Second Clause: 'If a brother arose and betrothed her, she has no claims on him' — implying betrothal did not take effect
↓
Resolution (Rav Sheshet): The two clauses reflect two opinions. The latter clause follows Rabbi Akiva: betrothal with a woman forbidden by a mere prohibition (not karet) is void

🤝 Two Theories of Ḥalitza's Legal Effect

Reish Lakish — Personal Act, Partial Release

  • Ḥalitza is a personal act by the performing brother
  • It replaces his karet with 'does not build up'
  • Other brothers retain their karet prohibition with ḥalutza
  • Rival wife: all brothers remain liable for karet (she wasn't released)
  • Betrothal with ḥalutza by a brother takes legal effect

Rabbi Yoḥanan — Communal Agency, Full Release

  • All brothers stood equally before the yevama — any could have acted
  • One brother's ḥalitza = agency discharge for all brothers
  • Similarly: one wife's ḥalitza releases her rival wife
  • After ḥalitza: no karet for any brother re: ḥalutza or rival wife
  • Betrothal with ḥalutza is void (per Rabbi Akiva's principle)
