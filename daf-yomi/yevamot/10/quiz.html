QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Reish Lakish, what is the karet status of the brother who performed ḥalitza if he later has relations with his ḥalutza?
A. He is not liable for karet — his karet was replaced by the lesser prohibition 'does not build up'
B. He is fully liable for karet, just like the other brothers
C. He is exempt entirely — ḥalitza releases him from all prohibitions
D. The question is unresolved between Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yoḥanan
Correct: A — Reish Lakish holds that for the performer himself, the karet-level prohibition of 'brother's wife' has been dissolved and replaced by the lesser prohibition 'does not build up' (lo yivneh). He is still prohibited from marrying her, but the penalty is no longer karet.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Reish Lakish, what is the liability of the brothers who did NOT perform ḥalitza if they have relations with the ḥalutza?
A. They are fully exempt — the one brother's ḥalitza releases all of them
B. They are liable for the prohibition of 'does not build up' only
C. They are liable for karet — she remains a forbidden brother's wife to them
D. They must each perform their own ḥalitza but face no karet
Correct: C — According to Reish Lakish, the ḥalitza only personally dissolved the performing brother's karet bond. The other brothers never performed ḥalitza, so the ḥalutza retains her status as a forbidden 'brother's wife' relative to them — full karet liability remains.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Rabbi Yoḥanan's logical argument for why one brother's ḥalitza releases all brothers from karet liability?
A. The Torah explicitly states that one brother's ḥalitza suffices for all
B. Before the ḥalitza, any brother could have performed it — they all stood equally, so one acts as agent for all
C. Ḥalitza is always a communal act even when performed by one person
D. The ḥalutza herself releases the brothers through her act of removing the sandal
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan argues from the equal standing of all brothers: before the ḥalitza, any brother could have performed it — 'if this one wanted he could perform ḥalitza, and if that one wanted he could.' This equality means one brother's performance serves as agency for all, releasing all equally.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, what happens to the rival wife of the ḥalutza after ḥalitza is performed?
A. She remains fully bound — ḥalitza only releases the specific woman who performed it
B. She is also released — just as one brother acts for all brothers, the ḥalutza acts as agent for her rival wife
C. She must perform her own separate ḥalitza with each remaining brother
D. Her status depends on whether she was the first or second wife
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan extends the agency logic: just as the ḥalitza performer acts as agent for his brothers, the ḥalutza performs ḥalitza as agent for her rival wife. The rival wife is also fully released from the levirate bond, and no brother faces karet for having relations with her.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Rav Sheshet resolve the contradiction between the two clauses of the baraita about a brother who betroths his ḥalutza?
A. He explains that the baraita discusses two different types of ḥalitza
B. He rules that only the first clause is authoritative and the second is a scribal error
C. He explains that the two clauses reflect two different tannaitic opinions — the latter follows Rabbi Akiva, who holds betrothal with a prohibited woman is void
D. He distinguishes between betrothal before and after she has remarried someone else
Correct: C — Rav Sheshet resolves the apparent contradiction by noting the baraita contains two opinions. The latter clause — 'she has no claims on him' (betrothal is void) — reflects Rabbi Akiva's ruling that betrothal with a woman who is forbidden by a mere prohibition (not karet) has no legal effect whatsoever.
