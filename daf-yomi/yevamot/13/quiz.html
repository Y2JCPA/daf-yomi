QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Beit Shammai, what is the primary textual source for permitting rival wives of forbidden relatives in levirate marriage?
A. The phrase 'to one not of his kin' (Deut. 25:5) — kiddushin is ineffective
B. The word 'haḥutza' (outside) — she who goes outside is forbidden, implying the rival wife who stays is permitted
C. A kal v'chomer from the rival wife of a permitted yevama
D. The general principle that a doubt in capital matters follows leniency
Correct: B — Beit Shammai reads 'haḥutza' (the outside one) as describing the forbidden relative who goes outside the family — implying the rival wife, who does not go outside, may enter levirate marriage. Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi articulates this derivation in the Gemara.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does Beit Hillel use the word 'haḥutza' (outside) to derive, leaving them needing a different source for rival wives?
A. That rival wives are forbidden
B. That ḥalitza must be performed in public
C. That kiddushin (betrothal) is not valid with a yevama
D. That the forbidden relative herself is exempt from levirate
Correct: C — Beit Hillel uses 'haḥutza' to teach Rav Yehuda's ruling in the name of Rav: kiddushin is not valid with a yevama — she is 'outside' ordinary marriage law. This exhausts the verse's teaching for them, requiring another source for rival wives.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rava explain Beit Shammai's position on rival wives WITHOUT relying on a specific verse?
A. He argues that levirate marriage is optional, so rival wives need not be bound
B. He applies ein issur chal al issur — since erva preceded the levirate bond, she was never truly obligated in levirate, so her rival wife has no special status
C. He rules that Beit Shammai follows Rabbi Akiva's expansive interpretation of yibbum
D. He cites a tradition from Sinai that rival wives are always permitted
Correct: B — Rava argues that the prohibition of 'brother's wife' (creating levirate obligation) never took effect on the forbidden relative because the erva prohibition already applied — ein issur chal al issur. Without a levirate bond, her rival wife is simply an unrelated woman.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rabbi Yoḥanan answer Reish Lakish's challenge that Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel violated 'lo titgodedu' by maintaining different practices?
A. He says lo titgodedu only applies to mourning practices, not halakhic disputes
B. He argues different courts in different cities may rule differently; factionalism is only forbidden within a single court in a single city
C. He concedes that they did technically violate it but were forgiven due to the sanctity of their dispute
D. He says the prohibition was suspended during the Second Temple era
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan distinguishes: lo titgodedu forbids contradictory halakhic factions within a single city or court. Two legitimate courts in different locations ruling differently is not factionalism — it reflects the nature of the oral Torah's ongoing development.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What makes the six women listed in the second mishna (13b) more severe than the forbidden relatives in the first mishna?
A. They require a different form of ḥalitza involving special witnesses
B. Violation of their prohibition carries karet rather than lashes
C. They may never enter levirate marriage with any surviving brother — permanently forbidden to marry anyone in that family
D. Their rival wives are not exempt but must perform ḥalitza
Correct: C — The six relatives (mother, father's wife, father's sister, paternal half-sister, wife of father's brother, wife of paternal half-brother) are called 'more severe' because they are forbidden not only to this yavam but to ALL brothers — they can never enter levirate marriage with anyone in the family, unlike erva relatives who are only forbidden to specific individuals.
