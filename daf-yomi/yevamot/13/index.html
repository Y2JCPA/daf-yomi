⚖️ May Rival Wives of Forbidden Relatives Enter Levirate Marriage?

Beit Shammai
Rival wives ARE permitted. 'The wife of the dead man shall not be married outside' (Deut. 25:5) — 'outside' implies: she who goes outside is forbidden. The rival wife, who does not go outside, is therefore permitted for levirate marriage.
Beit Hillel
Rival wives are FORBIDDEN. The word 'outside' teaches only that betrothal (kiddushin) is invalid with a yevama — not that rival wives are permitted. The rival wife stands in place of the forbidden relative and is equally forbidden.
Beit Shammai
We derive betrothal-invalidity from 'to one not of his kin' — so 'outside' must teach something else: that rival wives ARE permitted. Also: ein issur chal al issur — the erva prohibition precedes the levirate bond, so she was never truly a yevama.
Beit Hillel
We also derive betrothal-invalidity from 'to one not of his kin.' Then 'outside' teaches a further ruling: the rival wife of a rival wife is also exempt from levirate marriage. The chain of exemption extends.
Peace Amid Dispute Despite this fundamental disagreement, Beit Shammai did not refrain from marrying women of Beit Hillel, nor did Beit Hillel refrain from Beit Shammai — a model of machloket l'shem shamayim.

⚖️ Beit Shammai vs. Beit Hillel: Rival Wives in Detail

Beit Shammai — Rival Wives Permitted

  • Rival wife of forbidden relative MAY perform yibbum
  • Source: 'haḥutza' — she who does not go outside may enter
  • Also: ein issur chal al issur — levirate bond never applied to her
  • If she performed ḥalitza → DISQUALIFIED from priesthood (ḥalitza was fully valid)
  • If she entered levirate marriage → FIT for priesthood (marriage was valid)

Beit Hillel — Rival Wives Forbidden

  • Rival wife of forbidden relative is EXEMPT — no yibbum, no ḥalitza required
  • Source: rival wife stands in place of the erva — same prohibition applies
  • If she performed ḥalitza → FIT for priesthood (ḥalitza was not fully valid)
  • If she entered levirate marriage → DISQUALIFIED from priesthood
  • Chain extends: rival wife of rival wife is also exempt

🔗 Rava's Principle: Ein Issur Chal al Issur

Two brothers: Brother A married to Sister 1 — levirate bond exists between Sister 2 and Brother B (wife's sister prohibition also applies to Brother B)
↓
Brother A married Sister 1 FIRST, then Brother B married Sister 2
When Brother A dies: Sister 2 was bound to Brother B as yevama BEFORE erva applied. The prohibition of wife's sister seeks to take effect — but can it?
Brother B married Sister 2 FIRST, then Brother A married Sister 1
When Brother A dies: erva (wife's sister) prohibition existed from the start. The levirate bond of 'brother's wife' never came to apply — she was always erva.
↓
Rava's ruling: Since the prohibition of brother's wife never took effect on her (blocked by erva), she has NO levirate obligation — and therefore her rival wife needs no exemption either. Ein issur chal al issur min haTorah.

🤝 Lo Titgodedu — Do Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel Violate It?

Reish Lakish
How could Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel maintain different practices simultaneously? 'Lo titgodedu' (Deut. 14:1) — do not become numerous factions (agudot agudot). The Jewish people must present unified halakhic practice!
Rabbi Yoḥanan
The prohibition applies to a single court in a single city. Two different courts in two different cities may issue different rulings — that is legitimate halakhic diversity, not forbidden factionalism.
Reish Lakish
But Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel were in the SAME city! Members of the same community followed different opinions on rival wives, Megilla reading dates, and other matters. Is that not splitting into factions?
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Different dates for Megilla reading: villagers read on the 11th-13th, townspeople on the 14th, walled cities on the 15th. This is not factionalism — it is legitimate variation rooted in the original Purim enactment, not contradictory rulings.
לֹא תִתְגֹּדְדוּ — Deuteronomy 14:1 Literally 'do not cut yourselves' (mourning rite). Reinterpreted: do not become separate agudot (factions) — different groups following contradictory halakhot in the same locale, creating the appearance of two Torot.

📋 When Does a Rival Wife Become Permitted? (Mishna 13a)

Initial status: Brother dies leaving a forbidden relative (e.g., wife's sister) and her rival wife. The rival wife is forbidden — she may not perform yibbum nor is ḥalitza required.
↓
Event A — Forbidden relative dies: She is no longer anyone's wife. The rival wife's status changes — she is now an ordinary yevama, obligated in ḥalitza or yibbum.
↓
Event B — Forbidden relative performs mi'un (refusal): A minor who was married by her mother/brother can refuse. This retroactively nullifies the marriage — as if she were never married. Rival wife becomes permitted.
↓
Event C — Forbidden relative is divorced: From the moment of divorce she is no longer his wife. The rival wife transitions from forbidden to obligated for levirate marriage.
↓
Gemara's challenge: The mishna seems contradicted by another mishna (30a) involving three brothers. The resolution: one mishna holds that the levirate bond is established at time of marriage; the other holds it is established at time of death.
↓
Rava's harmonization: Both mishnayot reflect the same opinion, using the literary style of 'this goes without saying, and it is unnecessary to say that' — presenting the obvious case first, then the non-obvious.
