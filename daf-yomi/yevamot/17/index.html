⚖️ Is the Levirate Bond (Zikah) Substantial?

Rav Huna (citing Rav)
If a waiting yevama dies before ḥalitza, her yavam's son may marry her sister — implying the zikah bond is not substantial enough to make her like a full wife
Rav Yehuda
If a waiting yevama dies, her yavam is forbidden to marry her mother — implying the zikah bond is substantial, creating a mother-in-law prohibition
Gemara (challenge to Rav Huna)
The mishna (49a) says 'if his yevama dies, he may marry her sister' — precise reading implies only the sister is permitted, not that her mother is also permitted!
Gemara (resolution)
The mishna contrasts with 'if his wife dies' to show that a wife's mother stays forbidden even after death, but a yevama's mother does not — no contradiction with Rav Huna
Key Term Zikah (זִיקָה) — the levirate bond tying a waiting widow to her brother-in-law. Whether it carries the force of full marriage determines which relatives become forbidden and whether his son may marry her kin.

📜 Verbal Analogy: Why Levirate Applies Only to Paternal Brothers

Rav Yehuda
Gezerah shavah on 'brotherhood': the word in levirate law parallels 'brotherhood' among Jacob's sons — who were paternal brothers with different mothers. So paternal brotherhood alone suffices for levirate.
Rabba
Same conclusion via the same gezerah shavah to Jacob's sons — 'brothers' means paternal brotherhood, and 'together' means they need not share a mother.
Challenge
Why learn from Jacob's sons? Derive instead from forbidden relations (arayot) — those 'brothers' share a mother! This would mean only maternal brotherhood suffices for levirate!
Response
The gezerah shavah to Jacob's sons is stronger and more precise — the word there is unqualified. School of Rabbi Yishmael: minor differences in words do not invalidate a verbal analogy.
Further Challenge
Derive instead from Abraham and Lot: 'For we are brothers' (Gen. 13:8) — yet they shared neither father nor mother! This would make levirate apply even between non-relatives!
Response
Abraham called Lot 'brother' only colloquially (they were uncle and nephew). That verse cannot serve as a legal source — Jacob's sons remain the correct analogy.
Both Words Are Needed The Torah writes both 'brothers' and 'together' in levirate law: 'brothers' teaches paternal brotherhood suffices; 'together' teaches maternal brotherhood also suffices independently.

⚖️ The Levirate Bond (Zikah) — Step-by-Step Analysis

The Setup: A widow (yevama) is bound to her surviving brother-in-law by the zikah bond, waiting for levirate marriage or ḥalitza. She is not yet his wife — but not fully free either.
↓
Rav Huna (citing Rav): If the yevama dies before ḥalitza, the yavam's son from another woman may marry her sister. If zikah were substantial, the sister would be forbidden as a co-wife's relative.
↓
Why not state the rule simply? If Rav only said 'zikah is not substantial between two brothers,' we might think this applies only when multiple brothers share the bond — making it weaker. By ruling on a single yavam, Rav Huna extends the ruling.
↓
Rav Yehuda's ruling: A yevama who dies while waiting — the yavam is forbidden to marry her mother. Here zikah IS substantial enough to create a mother-in-law prohibition (ḥamat status).
↓
Apparent tension, not contradiction: Zikah may be partially substantial — strong enough to forbid the mother (a lighter-weight prohibition), but not strong enough to forbid the sister (which requires full wife status). Both Rav Huna and Rav Yehuda can be correct.
