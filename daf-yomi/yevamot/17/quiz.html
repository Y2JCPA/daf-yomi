QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Shmuel (as explained by Ravina), what is the status of a child born to a Jewish woman who was taken captive by a gentile?
A. The child's status is uncertain (safek) and requires investigation
B. The child is considered fully Jewish via matrilineal descent
C. The child is a gentile and must convert to enter the Jewish people
D. The child is a mamzer due to the forbidden union
Correct: B — Ravina derived from Shmuel's teaching ('your son from a Jewish woman is called your son') that the inverse is also true: 'the son of your daughter from a gentile is called your son' — establishing matrilineal descent for Jewish identity.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does the folk etymology of 'Harpanya' mean according to Rabbi Zeira, and why is this significant?
A. A city built on a high mountain; it was geographically isolated
B. A mountain to which all turn (har shehakol ponin bo); it attracted those who could find no wife elsewhere
C. A house of refuge (har panya); it sheltered exiles from Babylon
D. A crossroads city (har paniyot); it was a major trade hub
Correct: B — Rabbi Zeira explained Harpanya as 'the mountain to which all turn' — it became the refuge for those unable to find wives in their own communities, leading to widespread intermarriage with disqualified families like Meishon, and ultimately contaminating the lineage of Harpanya's residents.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
In the Mishna on 17a, if the second brother performed only levirate betrothal (not consummation) and then died childless, what happens to his rival wife?
A. She is completely exempt because the betrothal was never consummated
B. She requires ḥalitza or levirate marriage from the third surviving brother
C. She falls back to the first brother's estate for levirate marriage
D. She may remarry freely since the second brother's betrothal was incomplete
Correct: B — The Mishna teaches that both the betrothed yevama AND the rival wife of the second brother require ḥalitza or levirate marriage from the surviving third brother. Levirate betrothal creates a bond sufficient to bind the rival wife as well.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does the Gemara reject deriving the 'paternal brothers only' rule for levirate via a verbal analogy to the word 'brotherhood' in Abraham's words to Lot (Genesis 13:8)?
A. Abraham and Lot were from different tribes, making their relationship legally irrelevant
B. The verse about Abraham and Lot is in Genesis, before the Torah was given at Sinai
C. Abraham called Lot 'brother' colloquially; they were actually uncle and nephew, not brothers
D. The word used by Abraham is a different Hebrew root than the word used in levirate law
Correct: C — Abraham and Lot were uncle and nephew, not actual brothers. Abraham used the term 'brother' in a casual, colloquial sense to avoid conflict. Such informal usage cannot serve as a legal source for a verbal analogy (gezerah shavah).
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rav Huna citing Rav, if a waiting yevama dies before ḥalitza, the yavam's son from another woman may marry her sister. What does this ruling reveal about the nature of the zikah bond?
A. The zikah bond is as strong as full marriage, but the sister prohibition is an exception
B. The zikah bond is not substantial — it does not create the same restrictions as full marital status
C. The zikah bond is only relevant for the yavam himself, not for his relatives
D. The zikah bond dissolves entirely upon the yevama's death, as if she were never married
Correct: B — If zikah were a full marital bond, the yevama's sister would be forbidden to the yavam's son as the sister of a close relative's wife. Since Rav permits this marriage, it demonstrates that zikah does not have the legal weight of full marriage — the bond is 'not substantial' (ein zikah).
