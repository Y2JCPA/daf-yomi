Mi'un: The Minor Girl's Right of Refusal

Mishna
A minor girl whose father died may be married off by her mother or brothers — but she retains the right of mi'un (refusal) to annul the marriage.
Beit Shammai
Refusal applies only to betrothal, not marriage. Only before the husband. Only in court. Only once.
Beit Hillel
Refusal applies to betrothal or marriage. In his presence or absence. In court or not. Even four or five times.
Core Dispute How broadly does a minor girl's power of refusal extend — and what are the consequences for marriage, levirate bonds, and property?

Beit Shammai vs Beit Hillel: Five Dimensions of Mi'un

Beit Shammai

  • Only betrothed girls may refuse
  • Refusal directed only at husband, not yavam
  • Must be in the husband's presence
  • Must be in court
  • May refuse only once, then wait for majority

Beit Hillel

  • Both betrothed and married girls may refuse
  • Refusal may be directed at husband or yavam
  • In his presence or absence
  • In court or not in court
  • May refuse four or five times if remarried each time
Beit Shammai's Concern "The daughters of Israel are not to be treated with disregard" — repeated refusals degrade the dignity of Jewish women.

Why Can't a Married Minor Refuse? (Beit Shammai's Reason)

Shmuel
Because there are no conditions with regard to marriage. If she could refuse after marriage, people might think adult marriages can also be conditional.
Gemara
What about chuppah without consummation? Or delivery to the husband's agents?
Shmuel
The Sages did not distinguish between circumstances — no marriage is conditional.
Rabba & Rav Yosef
Different reason: A man would not readily render his act licentious. If she later refuses, his intimacy retroactively becomes outside marriage.
Beit Hillel
Since a minor's marriage is only rabbinic, no one would confuse it with adult marriage. And since there is ketubbah and kiddushin, it is not licentious.
Key Concept Bi'ato bi'at zenut — Beit Shammai fear that retroactive annulment turns the husband's prior intimacy into a forbidden act.

Mi'un Directed at the Yavam: Can It Dissolve the Levirate Bond?

Minor girl's husband dies → she falls to yavam for levirate marriage
↓
Can her mi'un dissolve the levirate bond itself?
R' Oshaya
She can refuse consummation and levirate betrothal, but cannot nullify the levirate bond (imposed by Heaven)
Ulla
She can refuse even his levirate bond — because mi'un nullifies the original marriage, not the bond itself
Practical Difference A minor who refuses a yavam: according to R' Oshaya, she is still bound; according to Ulla, she is completely free.
7 / 9

If She Refuses One Yavam — Is She Forbidden to His Brothers?

Rav
She is forbidden to the other brothers — just like a yevama who receives a get from one yavam is forbidden to all.
Shmuel
She is permitted to the other brothers. With a get, he acts on her. With mi'un, she acts — saying 'I don't want you, but I may want your brother.'
Rav Asi
She is permitted even to the same yavam she refused! With two yevamin, there is no refusal of half a levirate bond.
R' Yochanan
She is permitted to the brothers — but they did not agree with him.
Who Disagreed? Abaye says it was Rav. Rava says it was R' Oshaya. Some say it was Rav Asi (who went even further, permitting her to the same yavam).
