QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Reish Lakish, what must happen if ḥalitza was performed with a pregnant yevama and she subsequently miscarried?
A. The ḥalitza is fully valid; she is free to remarry immediately
B. She requires another ḥalitza or yibbum, as the first was not effective
C. She is permanently forbidden from remarrying
D. The miscarriage retroactively validates the original ḥalitza with no further action
Correct: B — Reish Lakish holds that ḥalitza with a pregnant woman is not effective, because whoever is eligible for yibbum is eligible for ḥalitza and vice versa — and since the majority of pregnancies produce viable offspring, she may not have had a yibbum obligation. A baraita confirms: if she performed ḥalitza and then miscarried, she needs another ḥalitza or yibbum.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In which three disputes does Rava declare that halakha follows Reish Lakish?
A. Ḥalitza with a pregnant woman, rival wives' status, and 30-day infant viability
B. Ḥalitza with a pregnant woman, verbal property division, and usufruct vs. title
C. Yibbum validity, ḥalitza timing, and divorce penalties for prohibited marriages
D. Rival wives, property inheritance language, and nursing woman marriage
Correct: B — Rava explicitly rules following Reish Lakish in: (1) ḥalitza with a pregnant woman is not effective; (2) in verbal property division, stating 'gift' for one recipient suffices for all; and (3) usufruct rights are not equivalent to ownership of the title itself.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
A father writes all his possessions to his son while retaining usufruct. The son sells the property and then dies before the father. According to Reish Lakish (and Rava's ruling), what is the law?
A. The purchaser acquires nothing — the father's usufruct equals full ownership and the son had no title to sell
B. The sale is void and the property reverts to the father automatically upon the son's death
C. The purchaser acquires the property — usufruct rights are not tantamount to ownership of the title
D. The property is split between the purchaser and the father's estate
Correct: C — Reish Lakish holds שיעבוד פירות לאו כשיעבוד הגוף — rights to produce are not equivalent to ownership of the item. Therefore the son held full title and his sale was valid. Rava rules halakha follows Reish Lakish, so the purchaser acquires the property.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Rava argued that Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Eliezer share the same principle. What is that shared principle?
A. Both require a public announcement before any prohibited marriage can take effect
B. Both hold that one who enters a prohibited marriage must divorce, even if the prohibition is only rabbinic
C. Both exempt a yavam from penalties when performing yibbum with a pregnant yevama
D. Both permit remarriage after divorce if the original prohibition was rabbinic rather than Torah-level
Correct: B — Rava said Rabbi Meir (who penalizes one who marries a pregnant woman — a rabbinic prohibition) and Rabbi Eliezer (who penalizes a yavam who consummates yibbum with a pregnant yevama — also a rabbinic violation) both hold that one is penalized with divorce even for violating a rabbinic prohibition.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
A widow whose baby died within 30 days remarried, thinking she was exempt from yibbum. According to Ravina citing Rava, what must she do if she was betrothed to a Kohen?
A. Nothing — since the baby's death within 30 days proves it was never viable, she had no levirate obligation
B. She must perform ḥalitza due to the doubt about viability, which renders her forbidden to the Kohen as a ḥalutzah
C. She performs yibbum with the yavam to resolve the doubt, then returns to the Kohen
D. She waits 90 days to clarify her status, after which the Kohen may keep her
Correct: B — Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules that a baby surviving 30 days is presumed viable. A baby dying before 30 days creates halakhic doubt. Therefore the widow may have had a levirate obligation, requiring ḥalitza. But performing ḥalitza makes her a ḥalutzah — forbidden to a Kohen. This tragic outcome arises from the doubt created by the 30-day threshold.
