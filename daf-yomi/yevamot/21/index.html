⚖️ Which Is More Severe — Dishonest Measures or Forbidden Relations?

Rabbi Levi
The punishment for dishonest measures is MORE severe than for forbidden relations — because one who commits forbidden relations CAN repent, but one who uses false weights cannot find all his anonymous victims to return their money!
Gemara
But Rava used 'ha'el' in the forbidden-relations section to signal special harshness — implying sheniyot. Does Rabbi Levi contradict this by saying measures are harsher?
Resolution
No contradiction. 'eleh' also appears in the forbidden-relations section itself (Lev. 18:29). The word 'ha'el' in 18:27 adds something extra — it hints at the rabbinic sheniyot beyond the Torah prohibitions.
Rabbi Levi
Agreed — both can be true. Measures are harder to repent for (victims untraceable), while 'ha'el' still marks the forbidden-relations section as the locus for sheniyot.
Core Distinction Forbidden relations: victim is known → restitution/repentance possible. Dishonest measures: victims anonymous → full repentance structurally blocked → therefore uniquely severe.

🌳 Rav Ashi's Orchard Parable — Why Distance Matters

סוּר מֵעָלֶיהָ וַעֲבֹר — 'Avoid it, pass not by it' (Proverbs 4:15)
The Orchard Guard A man guarding an orchard:

From the OUTSIDE → all of it is protected, since no one can enter without passing him
From the INSIDE → only the plot directly in front of him is protected

So too with sheniyot: by keeping distance from acts near the prohibition (secondary relationships), one protects against ever reaching the primary Torah violation itself.

This is why Rabbi Oshaya's verse (Proverbs 4:15) — 'Avoid it, pass not by it' — is the ideal source: it commands preemptive distancing.

🔚 Rav's Tradition: Four Women Who Have a Conclusion

What is 'a conclusion' (yesh lahen sof)? The prohibition applies to that specific woman but does not extend further up the generational chain or to parallel cases — the decree has a defined limit.
↓
His daughter-in-law is forbidden by Torah (Lev. 18:15) — so Rav must mean his son's daughter-in-law. Rav Ḥisda explains: she was decreed rabbinic to prevent confusion with one's own daughter-in-law (the Torah prohibition). The decree stops there — not son's son's daughter-in-law.
↓
His father's father's wife — extends one generation beyond the Torah prohibition (father's wife), but has a conclusion: does not extend to great-grandfather's wife.
↓
His mother's father's wife — parallel to above on the maternal side; also has a conclusion.
↓
Ze'eiri adds a fourth: Wife of father's maternal half-brother. Why does Rav omit it? It might be confused with the wife of a father's paternal half-brother, which is a Torah prohibition — dangerous to mention without distinction.
↓
Ze'eiri's counter: He is not concerned about confusion because one frequently visits one's father's father's home (where the paternal uncle lives) but rarely visits one's mother's household (where the maternal uncle lives) — so the two cases feel distinct in practice.

🔍 Is the Wife of One's Mother's Maternal Half-Brother Forbidden?

Wife of father's paternal half-brother = forbidden by Torah (Lev. 18:16 — uncle's wife)
↓
Sages decreed: wife of father's maternal half-brother = rabbinic sheniyah
↓
Question: Does the same logic extend to wife of mother's maternal half-brother?
↓
Rav Safra objects
She herself (mother's paternal half-brother's wife) is already only rabbinic. Shall we now decree upon a decree?
Rava responds
Not a decree upon a decree — the full pattern on the father's side (Torah + rabbinic) should be mirrored on the mother's side. The structure itself requires it.
↓
Conclusion: Rav Yehuda bar Sheila's principle from Eretz Yisrael — 'For every woman forbidden by Torah, the Sages forbade her parallel on the other side' — confirms she IS forbidden as a sheniyah
