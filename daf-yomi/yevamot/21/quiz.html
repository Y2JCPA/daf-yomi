QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava, where in the Torah is the prohibition of secondary forbidden relationships (sheniyot) hinted at?
A. In 'ushmartem' (you shall protect) — Leviticus 18:30
B. In 'ha'el' (these) — Leviticus 18:27, interpreted as 'harsh' via Ezekiel 17:13
C. In Solomon's 'weighing and seeking out' — Ecclesiastes 12:9
D. In 'Avoid it, pass not by it' — Proverbs 4:15
Correct: B — Rava links 'ha'el' (these) in Lev. 18:27 to 'eilei ha'aretz' (mighty of the land) in Ezekiel 17:13, establishing that 'ha'el' connotes harshness — the Torah thereby hints that these forbidden relations extend harshly to secondary relationships.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Levi say the punishment for dishonest measures is MORE severe than for forbidden relations?
A. Because dishonest measures involve financial harm, which the Torah treats more seriously than personal sins
B. Because the Torah uses the phrase 'abomination' only for false weights, not for forbidden relations
C. Because one who transgresses forbidden relations can repent, but victims of false measures cannot be found to receive restitution
D. Because dishonest measures can be committed by accident, while forbidden relations always require intent
Correct: C — The key is the possibility of repentance: with forbidden relations, the transgressor knows the victim and can seek forgiveness. With dishonest measures, the many anonymous victims cannot be located to receive their money back — making complete repentance structurally impossible.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Rav Ashi's orchard parable teaching about secondary forbidden relationships?
A. A small transgression near a prohibition inevitably leads to a larger one
B. Guarding from the outside (distancing oneself) protects the whole orchard; guarding from inside only protects what is immediately in front
C. One must first master primary prohibitions before the secondary ones become relevant
D. The sheniyot apply only to those who live in proximity to the relatives in question
Correct: B — Rav Ashi illustrates Rabbi Oshaya's verse ('Avoid it, pass not by it'): by keeping distance from areas near a prohibition — the secondary relationships — one protects against ever reaching the Torah violation itself, just as a guard stationed outside protects the entire orchard.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What does Rav Ḥisda mean when he says 'They prohibited a daughter-in-law only due to the daughter-in-law'?
A. The Torah prohibition on one's daughter-in-law is the source for all other daughter-in-law prohibitions
B. The son's daughter-in-law (a secondary/rabbinic prohibition) was decreed to prevent people from confusing her with their own daughter-in-law (a Torah prohibition)
C. Both one's own daughter-in-law and one's son's daughter-in-law are actually forbidden by the same Torah verse
D. The decree applies only in a household where both daughter-in-laws are present simultaneously
Correct: B — Rav Ḥisda (citing Rabbi Ami) explains that the rabbinic prohibition on a son's daughter-in-law functions as a safeguard: if people see a man being careful around his son's daughter-in-law, they will not forget to be similarly careful around his own daughter-in-law — the Torah prohibition.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why did Rav exclude the 'wife of a father's maternal half-brother' from his list of four women with a conclusion, while Ze'eiri included it?
A. Rav held this relationship was a Torah prohibition, not rabbinic, making it inappropriate for his list
B. Rav was concerned she might be confused with the wife of a father's paternal half-brother (a Torah prohibition); Ze'eiri was not, because one rarely visits a mother's household vs. frequently visiting a father's household
C. Rav derived this prohibition from a different verse that implied no conclusion; Ze'eiri derived it from a verse implying a limit
D. Rav held that this prohibition had already lapsed in the Diaspora; Ze'eiri held it remained in force
Correct: B — Rav feared that listing the wife of a father's maternal half-brother alongside the wife of a father's paternal half-brother (Torah law) would cause people to confuse the two. Ze'eiri dismissed this concern: one frequently visits one's father's father's home (paternal side), making that case familiar, but rarely visits the maternal uncle's household — so the cases remain distinct in practice.
