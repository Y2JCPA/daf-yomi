Why Does Rabbi Elazar Prohibit Levirate Marriage After Divorce & Remarriage?

Mishna
If a man divorces and remarries a woman and then dies childless, she is permitted to the yavam — but Rabbi Elazar prohibits it.
Eifa
She was forbidden at one time — when divorced, she was his brother's ex-wife. But this is rejected: if so, she shouldn't even need ḥalitza.
Abaye
Rabbi Elazar is uncertain: does death or the original marriage establish levirate candidacy? Due to safek, she does ḥalitza but not yibum.
Rava
Death clearly determines candidacy. But people know about divorces and not about remarriages — they'll think the yavam married his brother's ex-wife!
Rav Ashi
It's a decree due to the case of an orphan in her father's lifetime — where everyone agrees she is forbidden to the yavam.
KEY ISSUE Four Amoraim offer four different explanations for Rabbi Elazar's prohibition — from prior forbidden status, to uncertainty, to appearances, to protective decree.

Eifa's Explanation — Rejected

Eifa
Rabbi Elazar's reason: she was forbidden at one time. When divorced, she was the brother's ex-wife — forbidden to the yavam. Yibum only overrides the prohibition when the brother dies while still married.
Sages
If she's truly a forbidden relative, she shouldn't need ḥalitza at all! But a baraita teaches that Rabbi Elazar requires ḥalitza.
Eifa
I do not know the reason for Rabbi Elazar's opinion.
LOGIC If she were a true ervah (forbidden relative), she'd be completely exempt from both yibum and ḥalitza — but Rabbi Elazar still requires ḥalitza, so Eifa's reasoning fails.

Three Amoraic Explanations for Rabbi Elazar

Abaye — Uncertainty

  • Does death establish levirate candidacy?
  • Or does the original marriage?
  • If death → she's a valid candidate
  • If marriage → she was once forbidden, so exempt
  • Due to doubt: ḥalitza yes, yibum no

Rava — Appearances

  • Death clearly determines candidacy
  • Everyone knows about divorces
  • Not everyone knows about remarriages
  • People will think the yavam took his brother's ex-wife
  • Even if remarried in the evening and he died in the morning

Rav Ashi — Decree

  • A protective gezeira (decree)
  • Based on: orphan in father's lifetime
  • Minor married by father, then divorced
  • Remarriage is only rabbinic, not Torah-level
  • Everyone agrees she's forbidden to yavam
  • Decree extends to all divorce-remarriage cases
CONCLUSION A baraita supports Rav Ashi: the Rabbis concede to Rabbi Elazar about the orphan case because 'her divorce was Torah-level but her remarriage was not.'
