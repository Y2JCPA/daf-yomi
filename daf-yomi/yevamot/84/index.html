Yevamot 84 — Permitted & Forbidden: The Status Matrix

יש מותרות לבעליהן ואסורות ליבמיהן
CORE THEME This daf establishes a comprehensive matrix of women who are permitted to their husbands but forbidden to their yevamin, and vice versa, based on the interplay of priestly status, lineage, and marital disqualification. The Gemara then probes whether this list is exhaustive by examining Egyptian converts and men with genital injuries.
3 / 9

Can Disqualified Animals Become Sacred?

Mishnah
A crossbred animal (kilayim), a tereifa, an animal born via C-section, a tumtum, or hermaphrodite (androginos) — none of these become sacred or render another animal sacred in their place.
Shmuel
They do not become sacred by way of substitution (temurah) — if one tries to substitute them for an offering, the substitute does not become sacred. And they themselves cannot render another animal sacred as a substitute.
Gemara
Learn from this that R' Eliezer does not consider a hermaphrodite a proper male — for sacrificial purposes it is disqualified, yet for the prohibition of mishkav zakhar, R' Eliezer holds one is liable to stoning.
STORY Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi recounts going to study under R' Elazar ben Shamua, where the students were 'like the roosters of Beit Bukya' — so aggressive he could learn only this one teaching about the hermaphrodite.

The Four Categories: Women, Husbands & Yevamin

CategoryExampleStatus Logic
✅ Husband / ❌ YavamCommon priest married a widow; brother is High PriestWidow permitted to ordinary priest but forbidden to Kohen Gadol
✅ Husband / ❌ YavamḤalal married a kosher woman; brother is a fit priestMarriage to ḥalal renders her a ḥalala — now forbidden to fit priests
❌ Husband / ✅ YavamHigh Priest betrothed a widow; brother is common priestOnly betrothal — she is not yet a ḥalala, so permitted to the brother
❌ Husband / ❌ YavamHigh Priest married a widow; brother is also a High Priest or common priestConsummation made her a ḥalala — forbidden to all priests
✅ Husband / ✅ YavamAll other standard marriagesNo conflicting status disqualifications
KEY PRINCIPLE The woman's status can change through marriage (e.g., becoming a ḥalala), creating asymmetry between her original eligibility and her eligibility after the husband's death.

Mamzer & Israelite Permutations

Permitted to Husband, Forbidden to Yavam

  • Israelite married an Israelite woman — brother is a mamzer
  • Mamzer married a mamzeret — brother is an Israelite
  • Each spouse was permitted to marry, but the yavam's status conflicts

Permitted to Yavam, Forbidden to Husband

  • Israelite married a mamzeret — brother is a mamzer
  • Mamzer married an Israelite woman — brother is an Israelite
  • The forbidden marriage means the yavam's status is actually the correct match
NOTE A mamzer may marry a mamzeret, and an Israelite may marry an Israelite — but cross-status marriages create the asymmetry this mishna catalogues.

Rav Pappa's Challenge: Egyptian Converts

R' Yoḥanan
A second-generation Egyptian who married a first-generation Egyptian woman — the son is considered second-generation, as status follows the mother.
Rav Pappa
If so, the mishna should have also listed: A second-generation Egyptian with two wives — one first-generation, one second-generation — and two sons. Their wives can be permitted to husbands but forbidden to yevamin, or vice versa!
Gemara
The tanna taught certain cases and omitted others. He also omitted the case of a man with crushed testicles (p'tzu'a daka), whose wife is forbidden to him but permitted to the yavam.
Gemara
But p'tzu'a daka is a lav prohibition — and the mishna already teaches lavim in detail (priest + widow, ḥalal + kosher woman). So the omission of p'tzu'a daka proves this is a genuine omission, not a principle-based exclusion.
RESOLUTION The detailed cases in the mishna were necessary to teach Rav Yehuda's ruling in the name of Rav: Daughters of fit priests are permitted to marry disqualified men, even though this disqualifies them from the priesthood.
