Yevamot 84 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Shmuel, what happens if one tries to substitute a hermaphrodite animal for a consecrated offering?
A. The substitute becomes sacred but with lesser sanctity
B. The substitute does not become sacred at all
C. The substitute becomes sacred and the original loses sanctity
D. Both animals become sacred
Correct: B — Shmuel teaches that disqualified animals (including hermaphrodites) neither become sacred through substitution nor can they render another animal sacred when someone attempts to substitute for them.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In the mishna's case of a ḥalal who married a woman fit to marry a priest, why is she forbidden to the yavam who is a fit priest?
A. Because she was always forbidden to priests
B. Because her marriage to the ḥalal rendered her a ḥalala
C. Because the yavam already has a wife
D. Because levirate marriage is forbidden with priestly women
Correct: B — The woman was originally fit to marry a priest, but her consummated marriage to a ḥalal changed her status to ḥalala — now disqualifying her from marrying a fit priest, including the yavam.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does the mishna specify that the High Priest 'betrothed' (not married) a widow in the case where she is permitted to the yavam?
A. Betrothal alone is insufficient to create a levirate bond
B. Because consummation would have rendered her a ḥalala, forbidden to all priests including the yavam
C. Because a High Priest cannot consummate marriage with a widow under any circumstances
D. To distinguish between biblical and rabbinic prohibitions
Correct: B — If the High Priest had consummated the marriage, the widow would become a ḥalala — forbidden to all priests, including the common-priest brother. Only with mere betrothal does she remain permitted to the yavam.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What financial consequences does the mishna prescribe for a woman who married a man as a secondary relative (shniyah)?
A. She receives half her ketubah payment
B. She loses her ketubah, produce rights, sustenance, and worn clothing
C. She receives her full ketubah but loses inheritance rights
D. She receives nothing and the offspring are mamzerim
Correct: B — A shniyah marriage results in loss of ketubah, produce rights, sustenance provisions, and worn clothing — but critically, the offspring are of unflawed lineage (unlike Torah-level forbidden unions that can produce mamzerim).
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does the Gemara explain why the mishna's list of permitted/forbidden categories is not exhaustive?
A. The mishna only covers priestly cases, not cases involving converts
B. The tanna taught certain cases and omitted others — proven by the omission of p'tzu'a daka (crushed testicles)
C. The mishna is actually exhaustive; Rav Pappa's objection is rejected
D. Egyptian convert cases were omitted because R' Yoḥanan's ruling was not yet accepted
Correct: B — The Gemara concludes the tanna 'taught and omitted' — the omission of p'tzu'a daka (a lav-level prohibition already detailed in the mishna for other cases) proves this is a genuine omission, and the specific cases taught were necessary to convey Rav Yehuda's ruling in Rav's name.
