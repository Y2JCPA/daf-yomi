Rabbinic Marriage: Rights of the Deaf-Mute & Minor

Rabbinic Marriage
Both minor girls and deaf-mute women marry by rabbinic law
↓
Minor Girl
✅ May eat teruma
✅ Has a ketubah
✅ Can refuse marriage
Deaf-Mute Woman
❌ May not eat teruma
❌ No ketubah required
❌ Cannot issue refusal
↓
Why the difference?
Each restriction on the deaf-mute is a gezeirah (decree) to prevent confusion with Torah-level obligations
Key Theme The Gemara systematically compares the legal status of minors and deaf-mutes in marriage, revealing how rabbinic decrees protect vulnerable individuals while maintaining halakhic integrity.

Minor vs. Deaf-Mute Wife: Teruma Rights

Minor Girl (Married to Kohen)

  • Marriage is by rabbinic law
  • May partake of teruma
  • R' Yochanan ben Gudgada testified she can be divorced with a get
  • Her marriage has a defined endpoint — she can refuse until maturity

Deaf-Mute Woman (Married to Kohen)

  • Marriage is by rabbinic law
  • May NOT partake of teruma
  • Decree: lest a deaf-mute kohen feed teruma to a competent wife
  • Concern he might feed her Torah-level teruma — not just rabbinic
Gezeirah Logic The Sages prohibited a deaf-mute wife from eating teruma even from a competent kohen, as a protective fence: if she could eat it, people might assume a deaf-mute kohen could also feed his competent wife — which is biblically prohibited.

Why No Teruma for a Deaf-Mute Kohen's Wife?

Question: A deaf-mute is like a minor — we have no obligation to prevent minors from transgressing!
↓
Redirect: The real concern is a deaf-mute kohen feeding a competent wife
↓
Objection: Let her eat rabbinic-level teruma — her marriage is also rabbinic!
↓
Answer: Decree lest he come to feed her Torah-level teruma
Layers of Decrees The Gemara reveals a chain of protective legislation: each proposed leniency is blocked by a further concern, illustrating the Sages' caution in matters of teruma.

Ketubah: Why the Asymmetry?

Gemara
Why does a minor girl get a ketubah but a deaf-mute woman does not?
Answer
If a deaf-mute woman had a ketubah, men would refrain from marrying her entirely — she can never fully consent to waive claims.
Gemara
But shouldn't a competent woman married to a deaf-mute man also get a ketubah? Otherwise women won't marry deaf-mute men!
Answer
More than a man wants to marry, a woman wants to be married. Women will still agree, even without a ketubah.
Famous Principle "יותר ממה שהאיש רוצה לישא, אשה רוצה לינשא" — This pragmatic social observation drives the halakhic asymmetry in ketubah obligations.

Rav Malkiyyu's Creative Solution

רבא אמר: מאן חכים כרב מלכיו
Practical Wisdom Rava praised Rav Malkiyyu for arranging a marriage for a deaf-mute man in his neighborhood — and writing a ketubah of 400 dinars from the man's property for the wife.

His reasoning: "If the deaf-mute needed a maidservant, we would acquire one for him. How much more so a wife — who serves both as caretaker and as spouse!"

Though no ketubah was legally required, Rav Malkiyyu provided one voluntarily to ensure proper care for both parties.

The Mind of a Deaf-Mute: Rav Ashi's Dilemma

PossibilityDescriptionHalakhic Consequence
Weak but consistent mindAlways functions at the same (low) levelCan divorce — same mental state as when he betrothed
Fluctuating competenceSometimes competent, sometimes notCannot divorce — perhaps competent at betrothal but incompetent now
R' Elazar's teruma rulingTeruma of a deaf-mute is of doubtful statusSupports the fluctuating view — his status is uncertain
תיקו The Gemara leaves this dilemma unresolved (teiku). The practical difference: if a deaf-mute has a consistent mind, he can divorce; if his competence fluctuates, he cannot — because we fear he was competent at betrothal but incompetent at divorce.
