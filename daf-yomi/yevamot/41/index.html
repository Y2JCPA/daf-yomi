⚖️ Two Cases of Forbidden Yevama

Sister of Ḥalutza

  • Rabbinic prohibition only
  • Performs ḥalitza to dissolve bond
  • May NOT consummate levirate marriage
  • Decree prevents public confusion
  • Source: Reish Lakish's interpretation of mishna

Sister of Divorcée

  • Torah-level prohibition
  • Also performs ḥalitza only
  • May NOT consummate levirate marriage
  • Possibly entirely exempt from levirate bond
  • Reish Lakish: Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi teaches Torah source

🔄 If the Betrothed Wife Dies, Does the Yevama Become Permitted Again?

Rav
If a yavam betroths his yevama's sister, the yevama becomes forbidden as his wife's sister. But if the betrothed wife dies — the yevama reverts to being fully permitted for levirate marriage! She was permitted, then forbidden, now permitted again.
Rav Hamnuna
Challenge! In the baraita: three brothers, two married to sisters, one bachelor. The bachelor does levirate marriage with one sister. Later, the second wife dies — yet the Sages say she may NOT do levirate marriage with the bachelor. Why not, by your logic?
Rava (after he left)
The baraita must follow Rabbi Elazar, who holds: once a yevama fell before an ineligible yavam, she is permanently exempt from levirate marriage — even if the obstacle is later removed.
Rava (to himself)
But perhaps Rabbi Elazar only said this where she was forbidden from the very start — not in Rav's case, where she was initially permitted, then forbidden, then became permitted again. Are these truly comparable?
Final Resolution A second baraita confirms Rabbi Elazar applies even to the case where the yavam betrothed his yevama's sister. Thus the challenging baraita follows Rabbi Elazar — not the Rabbis — and Rav's ruling stands as the Rabbis' position.

🏛️ Three Brothers / Two Sisters — Rav Hamnuna's Baraita

Setup: Brother A married to Sister 1 · Brother B married to Sister 2 · Brother C is bachelor
↓
Brother A dies childless → Sister 1 awaits levirate from Brother C (bachelor)
↓
Brother C consummates levirate marriage with Sister 1 — now married to her
↓
Brother B dies childless → Sister 2 now requires levirate from Brother C. But C is married to her sister!
↓
Rabbi Elazar
Sister 2 is permanently exempt — she fell before an ineligible yavam (C was her sister's husband). Exemption is permanent.
Rabbis / Rav's View
Rav's ruling of reversion applies only where yevama was initially permitted. Sister 2 was never initially permitted — different case entirely.

⏳ Three-Month Waiting Period — Opinions and Rules

Base Rule (Mishna): A yevama may not perform ḥalitza or consummate levirate marriage until 3 months pass from her husband's death — concern for pregnancy and viability of offspring
↓
General Rule: All women — widows, divorcées, ḥalutzot — must wait 3 months before betrothal or remarriage to distinguish between a pregnancy from the first husband and the second
↓
Rabbi Yehuda: Previously married women must wait 3 months; previously betrothed women (no intercourse) may marry immediately — except those listed as forbidden relatives
↓
Rabbi Yosei: All women may be betrothed within 3 months — but a widow must wait even for betrothal due to the mourning period she observes for her deceased husband
↓
Key Principle (Rabbi Yosei from baraita): Verse states 'if the man does not wish to take his yevama' — ḥalitza is only valid when levirate marriage was genuinely possible at that moment

💰 Sustenance Payments During the Three Months

יְבָמָה נִזּוֹנֶת מִנִּכְסֵי בַּעְלָהּ הַמֵּת
Halakha: Sustenance for the Waiting Yevama General rule: A yevama receives sustenance (mezonot) from her deceased husband's estate during the 3-month waiting period, as she remains legally bound to the yavam.

If the yavam flees: When brought to court and obligated to perform ḥalitza or levirate marriage, then flees — she continues to receive sustenance from his estate.

Minor yavam: If her yavam is a minor, she receives no sustenance payments. Since a minor cannot consummate levirate marriage, there is no actionable levirate bond — she cannot claim against his estate. The halakha follows: no sustenance.
