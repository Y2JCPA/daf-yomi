QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Reish Lakish, what is the legal difference between the prohibition of marrying the sister of one's ḥalutza versus the sister of one's divorcée?
A. Both are forbidden by Torah law
B. Sister of ḥalutza is Torah law; sister of divorcée is rabbinic
C. Sister of ḥalutza is rabbinic; sister of divorcée is Torah law
D. Both are only rabbinic prohibitions enacted by the Sages
Correct: C — Reish Lakish explains that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi taught the sister of one's divorcée is forbidden by Torah law (she is his ex-wife's sister, like a wife's sister), while the sister of one's ḥalutza is only a rabbinic decree to prevent the public from mistaking the ḥalutza's relative for the ḥalutza herself.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is Rav's ruling when a yavam betroths his yevama's sister and then the betrothed wife dies?
A. The yevama remains permanently forbidden to him
B. The yevama reverts to being permitted for levirate marriage
C. The yevama must perform ḥalitza but may not do levirate marriage
D. A new rabbinic decree permanently forbids the levirate marriage
Correct: B — Rav rules that once the obstacle (the betrothed wife) is removed, the yevama reverts to her original permitted status. She was initially permitted, became forbidden when her sister was betrothed, and is permitted again — unlike cases where she was never initially permitted.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the Gemara's final, definitive reason for why ḥalitza may not be performed within the three-month waiting period?
A. To protect the yevama's right to sustenance payments from the estate
B. To prevent the yevama from losing her right to marry a kohen
C. Rabbi Yosei's principle: ḥalitza is only valid when levirate marriage was genuinely possible
D. To allow time for the deceased husband's estate to be appraised
Correct: C — The Gemara ultimately relies on Rabbi Yosei's baraita, deriving from the verse 'if the man does not wish to take his yevama' that ḥalitza has validity only when the man could theoretically 'take' her — i.e., when levirate marriage was actually possible at that moment.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
When a yevama's yavam is a minor, does she receive sustenance payments from the deceased husband's estate?
A. Yes, she always receives sustenance regardless of the yavam's status
B. Yes, but only until the yavam reaches majority
C. No — a minor cannot consummate levirate marriage, so there is no actionable bond
D. It depends on the size of the estate and the number of heirs
Correct: C — The Gemara rules she does NOT receive sustenance from the yavam's estate when the yavam is a minor. Since a minor is legally incapable of consummating levirate marriage, no actionable levirate bond exists against his estate, so she cannot claim sustenance from it.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rabbi Yehuda's position on the three-month waiting period is that:
A. All women — whether married or betrothed — must wait three full months
B. Previously married women must wait; previously betrothed women may marry immediately
C. Only widows must wait three months; divorcées may remarry without waiting
D. Women may be betrothed immediately but must wait three months before actual marriage
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda distinguishes: women who were actually married to their previous husbands must wait three months (pregnancy concern), but women who were only betrothed — where no intercourse occurred — may marry immediately, since there is no risk of pregnancy confusion.
