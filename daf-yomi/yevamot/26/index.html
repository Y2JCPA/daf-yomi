🔗 The Levirate Bond (Zikah) — Substantial or Not?

זיקה — ככנוסה דמיא? Is the levirate bond like actual marriage?
Central Question of the Daf Yevamot 26 opens with rulings about those who enabled women to remarry (judges, agents, witnesses) and whether they may subsequently marry those women. The daf then pivots to the defining debate of this chapter: does the zikah (levirate bond) create a quasi-marital tie? The answer drives rulings about sisters who are co-yevamot, culminating in a dispute between Rav and Shmuel over complex ḥalitza chains.

👨‍⚖️ Mishna: Those Who Permitted Women to Remarry

Who are the 'agents'? A judge who ruled a husband dead; an agent who delivered a get; a witness who testified the husband died — all enabling a woman to remarry
↓
Prohibition: While their own wives were alive, they may NOT marry the women they freed — there is suspicion that bias motivated their ruling
↓
Permission: If their wives subsequently died, they may marry those women — the suspicion is retrospectively allayed
↓
Divorce question: Baraita says even divorce allows it. The Gemara distinguishes: permitted only if the quarrel with his first wife arose after — not before — the ruling
↓
Sons and brothers: Women forbidden from marrying the man who freed them due to suspicion are nevertheless permitted to his sons or brothers — the suspicion does not extend that far

🔗 Is Zikah (the Levirate Bond) Substantial?

View A (Zikah substantial)
The levirate bond IS substantial — it creates a quasi-marital tie. Therefore when two sisters are co-yevamot, marrying one is like marrying your wife's sister. That is why ḥalitza is required and yibbum forbidden.
View B (Zikah not substantial)
The levirate bond is NOT substantial. The reason sisters cannot do yibbum is a rabbinic decree: people would say he married his wife's sister. The mishna can be explained without any quasi-marital bond.
Gemara challenge
If the reason is only a rabbinic concern about appearances, why did the mishna specify FOUR brothers? Even THREE brothers (two die, leaving one) would generate the same social concern!
Response
The mishna uses the style of lo zu af zu — 'not only this but also that.' It teaches the more obvious 3-brother case implicitly, while explicitly stating the less obvious 4-brother case. No contradiction.
Key Term זיקה (Zikah) — the levirate bond that arises when a brother dies childless, creating an obligation between his widow and surviving brothers

👭 The Four Brothers / Two Sisters Scenario

Setup: 4 brothers — Brothers A & B are each married to one of two sisters
↓
Brothers A & B die childless → both sisters fall for levirate marriage before Brothers C & D
↓
Problem: Can C or D perform yibbum with either sister?
If Zikah IS substantial
C has a bond with BOTH sisters. Marrying one = marrying his wife's sister. Forbidden.
If Zikah is NOT substantial
Still forbidden by rabbinic decree — appearances of marrying wife's sister. Forbidden.
↓
Ruling in both opinions: Both sisters must receive ḥalitza only — yibbum is prohibited

⚔️ Rav vs. Shmuel on the Three-Sisters Case

Rav

  • Levirate bond is NOT substantial
  • Yet: the 'middle' sister requires ḥalitza from BOTH brothers
  • Reasoning: each ḥalitza performed while the other sister's bond existed was potentially invalid
  • To be certain of a valid release, she must do ḥalitza with each brother
  • More stringent — requires up to 6 ḥalitzot when all three appear simultaneously

Shmuel

  • Requires a full levirate marriage (not just ḥalitza) to exempt a co-wife
  • For the three-sisters case: ONE brother performing ḥalitza with each sister is sufficient
  • Does not require each sister to receive ḥalitza from both brothers
  • More lenient — a single valid ḥalitza per sister fully severs the bond
  • Left unresolved at end of daf — tension between the two positions
