Yevamot 22 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Yehuda, what case does 'a brother of any kind' in the Mishna come to include?
A. A brother born from a Canaanite maidservant
B. A brother who is a mamzer
C. A brother born from a gentile woman
D. A half-brother who converted to Judaism
Correct: B — Rav Yehuda teaches that 'any kind' adds the mamzer brother, who creates a levirate bond despite flawed lineage. Brothers born of non-Jewish mothers (maidservant or gentile woman) are explicitly excluded from the category of 'brother' entirely.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why is a kohen permitted — indeed required — to become ritually impure for a mamzer brother?
A. Because mamzer status is not a halakhic disqualification for priestly purity laws
B. Because the verse 'his kin that is near to him' includes all halakhic brothers regardless of lineage
C. Because the Rabbis issued a special decree to honor mamzerim
D. Because a mamzer's death is considered like the death of a gentile, which does not convey impurity
Correct: B — One might think a kohen need not become impure for a brother of flawed lineage. The Gemara teaches that 'his kin that is near to him' (Lev. 21:2) includes all halakhic brothers — the mamzer's legal brotherly status is complete for this purpose.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Rav Naḥman ruled that maternal half-brothers who both converted may not testify together. What is the basis for this ruling?
A. Converts retain all family relationships from before conversion for legal purposes
B. Their testimony was invalid before conversion and remains so afterward
C. A rabbinic decree preserving kinship norms, even though conversion legally dissolved their prior ties
D. The Torah explicitly disqualifies relatives who converted together
Correct: C — While conversion legally dissolves prior family ties ('a convert is like a newborn'), the Sages decreed that converted maternal half-brothers remain disqualified as co-witnesses — to uphold the appearance of proper kinship norms and prevent erosion of these standards.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to the Rabbis (first tanna), how many sin-offerings does one bring for unwittingly having relations with a half-sister who is also the daughter of his father's wife?
A. None — an unwitting violation among close relatives requires only verbal confession
B. One — for the single underlying prohibition of 'sister'
C. Two — one for 'sister' and one for 'daughter of father's wife'
D. Three — one for each verse that mentions a related prohibition
Correct: C — The Rabbis hold that when a single unwitting act violates two distinct Torah prohibitions simultaneously — both 'sister' and 'father's wife's daughter' — the violator brings two separate sin-offerings, one for each prohibition.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Beyond the dispute about sin-offerings, what broader halakhic principle does the Gemara derive from 'she is your sister' (היא אחותך)?
A. A half-sister from a raped woman is included in the prohibition
B. Prohibitions derived through kal v'chomer (a fortiori reasoning) do not incur corporal punishment
C. A convert's sister is forbidden to him by rabbinic decree
D. The prohibition applies only when both share the same father and mother
Correct: B — The Gemara derives from 'she is your sister' that Torah prohibitions derived through a fortiori reasoning (kal v'chomer) do not carry corporal punishment (lashes). Only violations of explicitly written Torah prohibitions are punishable — this is a foundational hermeneutical principle.
