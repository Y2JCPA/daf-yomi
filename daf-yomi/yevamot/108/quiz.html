QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to R' Yosei bar Yehuda, what is the most expansive scenario in which a minor's statement counts as refusal?
A. When she is sent by her husband to a shopkeeper and says she doesn't want him
B. When she is sitting in the bridal chair on the way to the wedding
C. When she is serving drinks at the wedding feast
D. When she is standing before a beit din
Correct: A — R' Yosei bar Yehuda expanded refusal to the most casual domestic context — even when sent on an errand to a shopkeeper, her statement 'I do not want him' is valid refusal.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to R' Yehuda ben Beteira, what happens if a minor girl doesn't formally refuse but marries another man?
A. She must first perform formal refusal before the new marriage is valid
B. Her new marriage itself constitutes her refusal of the first husband
C. She is considered married to both men simultaneously
D. The second marriage is void until she obtains a get from the first
Correct: B — R' Yehuda ben Beteira's innovative ruling: the act of marrying (or even becoming betrothed to) another man inherently demonstrates her refusal of the first husband. Shmuel rules this is the halakha.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does R' Elazar (citing Shmuel's praise) describe the minor as immersing and eating teruma by evening?
A. To show that teruma purification requires a full day
B. To illustrate that the marriage has absolutely no legal effect — she remains as if unmarried
C. To demonstrate that a priest's daughter loses her teruma rights upon marriage
D. To prove that immersion is required after every marital relation
Correct: B — Shmuel praised R' Elazar's consistency: since the minor's marriage is 'nothing,' a priest's daughter can rise from her husband's bed, immerse, and eat teruma by evening — as if no marriage ever took place.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rava, why does a minor's refusal of her own husband nullify his prior get, but her refusal of a different husband does not?
A. Because refusal only works against the man she is currently married to
B. Because she already proved she can resist his influence by refusing him, but might be manipulated to refuse others
C. Because a get from another man has greater legal force
D. Because refusal and get cannot apply to different men simultaneously
Correct: B — The concern is that a husband familiar with her could manipulate her into refusing a new husband to return to him. But if she already refused HIM, she proved she resists his influence — so her refusal nullifies his get. With another man's get, this proof doesn't exist.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the key principle stated in the mishna regarding a minor who left multiple husbands by different means?
A. She may return to any husband regardless of how she left
B. She may never return to any prior husband
C. Anyone she left by mi'un — she may return to; anyone she left by get — she may not return to
D. She may return only to the first husband but not subsequent ones
Correct: C — The mishna's organizing principle: mi'un retroactively erases the marriage (so she can return), while a get treats it as real (so the prohibition against remarrying a divorced wife after she married another applies).
