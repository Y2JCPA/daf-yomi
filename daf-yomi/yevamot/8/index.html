⚖️ Does a Forbidden Relative Need a Verse to Be Exempt from Yibum?

Rava
A positive mitzva does not override a prohibition entailing karet. Therefore, no verse is needed to exempt a forbidden relative from yibum — it follows from first principles!
Baraita
But the baraita states: 'I have derived nothing other than these sisters...' — implying the verse IS necessary for forbidden relatives, not merely for rival wives!
Rava (Response)
The verse is required only for the rival wife (tzara) of the forbidden relative. The forbidden relative herself needs no verse — the karet principle suffices.
Gemara
Rav Ashi confirms: the mishna teaches 'fifteen women exempt their rival wives' — not 'are exempt and exempt others,' showing the exemption of a forbidden relative is self-evident, not derived.
Core Resolution The baraita's verse-based derivation targets rival wives (tzarot), not the forbidden relatives themselves. A karet prohibition needs no extra verse — it automatically overrides a positive mitzva like yibum.

⏱️ Does the Order of Marriages Determine Yibum Status?

Deceased brother died childless — two sisters involved
Living brother is married to one; deceased was married to the other
↓
Deceased married first
Brother's wife prohibition established first. Later, living brother married her sister → wife's sister prohibition. Yibum obligation pre-exists the competing prohibition.
Living brother married first
Wife's sister prohibition established first. Later, deceased married her sister → she became a rival wife of a pre-existing forbidden relative. No yibum obligation ever formed.
↓
Conclusion: The prohibition established first is primary. Only when the brother's wife obligation precedes the wife's sister prohibition does the classic rival-wife exemption scenario arise.

🗣️ Rami bar Hama Challenges Rava's Kal V'Chomer

Rava
'With her' (עמה) teaches: where levirate marriage is applicable, the rival wife is prohibited; where it is not applicable, she is permitted.
Rami bar Hama
Why not reverse your kal v'chomer? A forbidden relative is more stringent than a rival wife — perhaps she should be permitted where yibum doesn't apply, just as the rival wife is permitted there?
Rami bar Hama
Moreover, the rival wife herself disproves the kal v'chomer: she is prohibited where yibum applies and permitted where it doesn't. The forbidden relative could be prohibited in both places!
Rav Huna bar Tahlifa
Two separate issues are embedded in a single verse — one phrase covers the place where yibum applies, the other covers where it does not. No single kal v'chomer is needed.
Key Verse (Lev. 18:18) "And you shall not take a woman to her sister... in her lifetime" — Rav Huna bar Tahlifa reads this as two independent teachings, each governing a different scenario

👯 Forbidden Relative vs. Rival Wife — Two Legal Tracks

Forbidden Relative עצמה

  • Prohibited by a karet transgression
  • Positive mitzva of yibum cannot override karet
  • No special verse needed — derived from the general principle
  • Exempt from both yibum and chalitza
  • Example: wife's sister, mother-in-law, daughter
  • Rav Ashi: mishna's phrasing confirms this — 'exempt their rival wives,' not 'are themselves exempt'

Rival Wife צרה

  • Co-wife of the forbidden relative
  • Her own marriage was entirely permitted
  • Needs a special derivation for her exempt status
  • Derived from 'with her' (עמה) — exempt only where yibum obligation exists for the co-wife
  • Where yibum does not apply, rival wife may be permitted
  • Rav Aḥa son of Beivai Mar: even her prohibition involves karet — perhaps she too needs no verse

🔄 Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's Alternative Reading of the Verse

Background: The Gemara cites Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's proof for why forbidden relatives and rival wives are prohibited from yibum — via a different verse entirely
↓
The verse (Deut. 25:5): 'Her brother-in-law will have intercourse with her' — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi reads 'יבמה' not as a command but as indicating full marital acquisition
↓
Derivation 1: Yibum creates a complete marriage — she becomes his wife in every legal sense (divorce requires a get; she inherits from him)
↓
Derivation 2: 'To be a rival wife' (לצרור) — teaches the exemption of rival wives, consistent with Rabbi Shimon's view that even simultaneous sisters are exempt
↓
Challenge: What does Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi do with the phrase 'with her' (עמה)? The Gemara answers: he needs it for the court's obligation to provide atonement — a separate halakha entirely
