🧑‍⚖️ Rabbi Yosei b. Rabbi Yehuda vs. The Rabbis — One Verse or Two?

R. Yosei b. R. Yehuda
Both exclusions — the Canaanite maidservant's child and the gentile woman's child — can be derived from a single verse. No need to expend two separate verses.
Rabbis
Two separate verses are necessary — one for the maidservant and one for the gentile woman. Each case has a unique reason you might have included it.
Gemara
If only the gentile woman were excluded, one might think the maidservant's child IS a sibling — since she has some mitzvah obligations (Shabbat, mezuzah as part of the household).
Gemara
Conversely, if only the maidservant were excluded, one might think the gentile woman's child IS a sibling — since she has no connection to mitzvot whatsoever, perhaps a special verse is needed to include her.
Conclusion Both cases require explicit exclusion — each presents a unique argument for why one might have thought the child is a halakhic sibling. Two verses, two teachings.

💡 Ravina's Deduction — The Son of Your Daughter

כִּי יָסִיר אֶת בִּנְךָ מֵאַחֲרַי — "For he will turn away your son from following Me" (Devarim 7:4)
Key Ruling R. Yochanan citing R. Shimon ben Yochai: Deuteronomy 7:4 states "For he will turn away your son from following Me." The verse uses your son — implying the child of your daughter with a gentile man is still called your son (your grandson), and is fully Jewish.

Ravina deduces: The son of a Jewish woman by a gentile father is considered Jewish — following the mother.

Challenge raised: Does Ravina then hold that if a gentile or slave had relations with a Jewish woman, the child is fully Jewish? The Gemara explores whether this is consistent with other rulings.

Note on scope: R. Shimon's verse is from the seven Canaanite nations, but the Gemara teaches it applies universally — because the reason given in the verse (turning one's heart away from God) applies to all gentile marriages.

⚖️ Gemara: Does the Mishna Prove That Impossible Betrothal Is Valid?

Initial inference: The mishna requires giving two bills of divorce to both sisters — implying both betrothals are valid, even though the man cannot consummate with either (one is certainly the sister of his wife).
↓
Proposed proof: This proves that a betrothal destined for non-consummation (ein bo kiddushin in theory) is nevertheless a valid betrothal — in line with Rava's ruling in Kiddushin.
↓
Gemara rejects the proof: The mishna deals with a case where both sisters were initially identifiable and recognizable. The betrothal was fully valid at the moment it was made — he just later forgot which sister he betrothed.
↓
Follow-up question: If so, why does the mishna state the obvious — that he must give two gets? The Gemara answers: it is stated to teach the last clause of the mishna, where a new ruling about brothers and chalitza is introduced.
↓
Bottom line: The mishna does not resolve the Abaye-Rava dispute about impossible betrothal. The case here was a valid, consummatable betrothal from the start — uncertainty arose later.
