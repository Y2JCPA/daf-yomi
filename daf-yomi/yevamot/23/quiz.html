QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Gemara (Amud A), why is a child born to a Jewish father and a Canaanite maidservant NOT considered the father's halakhic child?
A. Because the Torah explicitly names such children as excluded in a separate verse
B. Because kiddushin (betrothal) cannot legally take effect with a Canaanite maidservant
C. Because Canaanite maidservants have no obligations in any mitzvot whatsoever
D. Because the Rabbis enacted a decree to prevent confusion in genealogical records
Correct: B — The operative principle is that kiddushin cannot take effect with a Canaanite maidservant. Without a valid kiddushin, no halakhic paternal bond is formed, and the child follows the mother's status.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does Ravina conclude from the phrase 'For he will turn away your son from following Me' (Deut. 7:4)?
A. Gentile fathers' children with Jewish women are not considered Jewish at all
B. The son of your daughter by a gentile father is still called 'your son' — fully Jewish
C. You are forbidden from giving your daughter to a gentile man in all circumstances
D. The intermarriage prohibition applies only to the seven Canaanite nations
Correct: B — Ravina deduces that the Torah calls the child of a Jewish woman and gentile man 'your son' (grandson) — meaning the child is halakhically Jewish, following the Jewish mother. R. Shimon ben Yochai extends this reasoning beyond the seven nations.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
In the Amud B mishna, if a man betrothed one unknown sister and then died leaving ONE brother, what is the brother permitted and forbidden to do?
A. Perform yibbum with one sister chosen by lot; chalitza not required
B. Perform chalitza with one sister and may perform yibbum with the other sister
C. Perform chalitza with one sister only; may NOT perform yibbum with either sister
D. Do nothing — halakhic uncertainty exempts him from all levirate obligations
Correct: C — The single brother does chalitza with one sister but may not do yibbum with the other — because the other might be the sister of a woman with whom he has a levirate bond, which would be forbidden.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does the Gemara reject the proof that the mishna demonstrates 'betrothal destined for non-consummation is valid'?
A. The mishna's betrothal was valid at the time — both sisters were known, and the uncertainty arose only afterward when he forgot which he betrothed
B. By citing Abaye's ruling in Kiddushin that such betrothal is definitely valid in all cases
C. By showing that giving two bills of divorce actually proves both betrothals are invalid
D. By demonstrating that levirate obligations would not apply if the betrothal were truly uncertain
Correct: A — The Gemara explains that the mishna deals with a case where both sisters were identifiable when the betrothal occurred — it was a fully valid betrothal. The man simply forgot which sister he betrothed afterward. This does not address impossible betrothal.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does the Gemara require TWO separate verses to exclude the daughter of a Canaanite maidservant AND the daughter of a gentile woman from the 'sister' prohibition?
A. Because the Torah principle always requires two witnesses, paralleled by two verses
B. Because without the maidservant verse, her child might seem a sibling (she has mitzvah obligations); without the gentile verse, her child might seem a sibling for the opposite reason
C. Because Rabbi Yosei b. Rabbi Yehuda's view requires one verse and the Rabbis require the other
D. Because one verse applies to biblical law and the other to rabbinic enactment
Correct: B — Each exclusion has a unique internal logic: the maidservant has some mitzvah connection, so one might include her child; the gentile woman has no mitzvah connection, so one might argue differently. Both cases need explicit Scriptural exclusion for opposite reasons.
