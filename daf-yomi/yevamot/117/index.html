Does the Yavam Inherit Based on Her Testimony?

Rav Ḥisda
If a woman entered levirate marriage based on her own testimony, her yavam inherits his dead brother's property based on that same testimony.
Rav Ḥisda
Beit Shammai derived her ketubah rights from the language of the marriage contract. Shall we not also derive inheritance from the Torah itself?
Rav Ḥisda
The Torah says: "He shall succeed in the name of his dead brother" (Deut. 25:6). He succeeded in the marital relationship — so he succeeds in inheritance too.
Kal Va-Ḥomer Logic If Beit Shammai derived ketubah rights from contract language, we can certainly derive inheritance rights from Torah language — a fortiori reasoning applied to testimony.

What if she says both?
"Permit me and give me my ketubah"
↓
Unresolved (teiku)
Whether order of mention
reveals her true motive
Rav Naḥman's Principle A person's initial request reveals their true motive. If money comes first, we suspect her testimony is driven by financial interest rather than truth.

The Five Disqualified Women (Mishna)

WomanReason for Disqualification
Mother-in-law (חמותה)Hates her daughter-in-law
Daughter of mother-in-law (בת חמותה)Hates her because her mother does
Rival wife (צרתה)Natural jealousy and enmity
Wife of the yavam (יבמתה)Fears being displaced by a new wife
Stepdaughter (בת בעלה)Resents the woman who replaced her mother
Distinction These women are disqualified only for testimony about death. For delivering a get (bill of divorce), even they are trusted — because the document itself serves as proof.

Is the Father-in-Law's Daughter Also Disqualified?

Side A
The mother-in-law's daughter hates because her mother hates. The father-in-law's daughter has no such hostile mother — she should be credible.
Side B
The real reason is resentment: "She eats the food of my father's house" (girsena). This applies equally to the father-in-law's daughter!
Proof from Baraita
One baraita lists five women — implying she is not separate. But another baraita lists seven — implying she is a separate case!
Gemara
The contradicting baraitot cannot resolve this. The dilemma remains unresolved.
Underlying Question Is the disqualification based on a specific hostile relationship (through the mother-in-law), or on a general resentment toward a woman who consumes the family's resources?

Contradicting Witnesses: The Three Cases

1 vs. 1 (Already Married)

  • One witness: "He died" → she married
  • Second witness: "He didn't die"
  • Result: She NEED NOT leave her husband
  • Her permitted status stands

1 vs. 2

  • One witness: "He died" → she married
  • Two witnesses: "He didn't die"
  • Result: She MUST leave her husband
  • Majority negates the single witness

2 vs. 1

  • Two witnesses: "He died"
  • One witness: "He didn't die"
  • Result: She MAY marry ab initio
  • Two prevail over one
Ulla's Principle Wherever the Torah relies on one witness, his testimony is treated as complete proof — like two witnesses. This is why one vs. one maintains the status quo.
