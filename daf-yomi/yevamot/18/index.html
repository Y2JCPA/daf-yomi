⚖️ The Great Zikah Debate

Rav Huna
The levirate bond (zikah) is terminated when the yevama dies. Afterward, her mother is permitted to the yavam — the bond dissolved with her death.
Rav Yehuda (citing Shmuel)
The zikah is substantial. Even after the yevama dies, her mother remains forbidden — the bond created lasting prohibitions that survive her death.
Rav Huna
The baraita proves: 'If his wife dies he is permitted to take her sister' — prohibitions dissolve at death!
Gemara
That baraita speaks of an actual wife, not zikah. The language of 'sister specifically' was only to exclude the sister — even the mother is permitted in that case. The cases are not parallel.
Core Question Does the levirate bond (זִיקָה) function like a full marriage, creating lasting prohibitions — or is it merely a rabbinic waiting period that vanishes with the yevama?

🔗 Is the Zikah Substantial? — Two Paths

Brother dies childless — his widow (yevama) awaits levirate marriage or ḥalitza
↓
Rabbis: Zikah IS substantial
Rivals of the yevama are forbidden. Relatives of the yevama are forbidden. Bond persists even if she dies — per Rav Yehuda/Shmuel.
Rabbi Meir: Zikah is NOT substantial
No formal bond is created. Rivals and relatives are technically permitted. Yet: ḥalitza must still be performed.
↓
Rabbi Meir's reason for requiring ḥalitza: we do not nullify the mitzva of levirate marriage — even if the bond is weak, the mitzva must be formally discharged

🗣️ Abaye Objects — Rabba and the Gemara Respond

Abaye
Objection! If the zikah is not substantial (per Rabbi Meir), then two sisters — each from a different household — should both be fully permitted for levirate marriage!
Rabba
Rabbi Meir rules they perform ḥalitza and not levirate marriage — not because zikah prohibits it, but to avoid appearing to nullify the entire mitzva.
Abaye
But if the zikah never created a real obligation, then the mitzva never truly came into effect — so how can performing ḥalitza be 'discharging' anything?
Gemara
As Rabban Gamliel rules: we perform ḥalitza even for a minor orphan, specifically to ensure there is no appearance of nullifying the mitzva of levirate marriage — regardless of the bond's substance.
Resolution Rabbi Meir: the zikah is not legally substantial, yet ḥalitza is still required as a formal discharge — to honor the framework of the mitzva even when it cannot be fulfilled.

📊 Three Tannaitic Positions on Zikah

Rabbis

  • Zikah IS legally substantial
  • Yevama's rival wives: forbidden
  • Yevama's relatives: forbidden
  • Prohibition persists even after yevama dies
  • Third brother (coexisted): ḥalitza only

Rabbi Meir

  • Zikah is NOT legally substantial
  • Rivals and relatives: technically permitted
  • Yet: ḥalitza still required
  • Reason: cannot nullify the mitzva
  • Rabban Gamliel's principle supports this

Rabbi Shimon

  • Third brother born after levirate marriage: free to choose either widow
  • He 'did not coexist' with the first brother
  • Levirate betrothal alone: uncertain — may be like marriage
  • Rav Yosef: levirate bond alone certainly cannot forbid
  • Opinion extends even to prior mishna (Rav Oshaya)

👶 The Third Brother — Mishna Step by Step

Step 1: Brother A dies childless — his wife (Widow 1) awaits levirate marriage. Levirate bond (זיקה) attaches.
↓
Step 2: Brother B performs full levirate marriage with Widow 1, while already married to his own wife (Wife 2).
↓
Step 3: Brother C is born after Brother B completed the levirate marriage — he enters a world where Widow 1 is already Brother B's wife.
↓
Step 4: Brother B dies — both Widow 1 and Wife 2 now fall to Brother C for levirate marriage.
↓
Rabbis rule: Both must perform ḥalitza only — Wife 2 is a rival wife of the original yevama, forbidden as the wife of a brother with whom he did coexist.
↓
Rabbi Shimon rules: Brother C may enter levirate marriage with whichever he wishes — born after the bond formed, he never 'coexisted' with Brother A's marriage, so no prohibition applies.
