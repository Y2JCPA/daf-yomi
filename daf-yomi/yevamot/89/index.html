Yevamot 89: Rabbinic Authority & Torah Law

CORE THEME This daf explores the extent of rabbinic power — from eliminating a woman's ketubah to declaring children mamzerim — culminating in the fundamental question: Can the Beit Din stipulate to uproot something prohibited by Torah law? The debate plays out through cases of terumah separation, forbidden marriages, and inheritance of minor wives.

Get from the Second Husband — Does It Matter?

Woman erroneously married a second man
↓
Second man gives her a get
↓
Is she disqualified from kehunah?
No — Mishna's ruling
The get has no validity — she was never his wife. She remains permitted to a Kohen.
Implication
She does not require a get from the second man at all.
KEY DISTINCTION The Sages penalized her to receive a get in the first clause (intercourse occurred) lest people say she divorced and remarried — but not in the latter clause (betrothal only).

Why No Ketubah, Sustenance, or Profits?

Ordinary Marriage

  • Ketubah instituted so she won't be demeaned
  • Prevents hasty divorce
  • Stipulations (mezonot, worn clothes) follow ketubah
  • Husband must pay to divorce

Forbidden Marriage (This Case)

  • Sages WANT her demeaned in his eyes
  • Goal: encourage him to divorce quickly
  • No ketubah = no stipulations either
  • Must return anything already taken
CHIDDUSH Even items she already took must be returned — we do not say since she has hold of them we leave them. The court actively removes them.

Teruma from Impure Produce — Rav Ḥisda vs. Rav Natan

Mishna (Terumot 2:2)
If one separated terumah from impure produce for pure produce: unwitting = valid terumah; intentional = he has done nothing.
Rav Ḥisda
"He has done nothing" means nothing at all — even that griva returns to its untithed state. The entire act is disregarded.
Rav Natan b. R' Oshaya
"He has done nothing" means the remaining produce is not exempted — but the separated portion is itself terumah.
Rav Ḥisda (responds)
If you say it's terumah, he may be negligent and never separate again, thinking he fulfilled his obligation.
R' ELAI'S PROOF Numbers 18:32 — 'you shall bear no sin seeing you have set apart its best.' If separating inferior produce had NO effect, there would be no sin. Therefore terumah from bad for good IS valid by Torah law.

Can the Sages Uproot Torah Law? (Beit Din Matnin)

Rav Ḥisda
The Sages CAN uproot Torah law! Proof: The mishna declares the first husband's child a mamzer — yet by Torah law she is his wife and the child is legitimate!
Rav Ḥisda (cont.)
Moreover, calling him mamzer permits him to a mamzeret — the Sages actively created a new permission against Torah law!
Rabba (citing Shmuel)
The child is forbidden to a mamzeret. So the Sages did NOT truly uproot Torah law — they only added a stringency (can't marry a regular Jewess).
R' Yoḥanan (via Ravin)
Agrees: the child is forbidden to a mamzeret. He is called 'mamzer' only to teach that marrying a regular Jewish woman is prohibited.
Rav Ḥisda (counter-proof)
But a husband inherits his minor wife and becomes impure for her — yet her marriage is only rabbinic! That IS uprooting Torah law!
FUNDAMENTAL QUESTION Is there anything that by Torah law is terumah, yet due to rabbinic concern the Sages removed its sacred status and gave it the status of chullin (non-sacred)?
