5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does a get given by the second husband NOT disqualify the woman from marrying a Kohen?
A. Because she was never his wife, so the get has no halachic validity
B. Because the get was given under duress
C. Because a Kohen can marry a divorcée in special circumstances
D. Because the second marriage was only rabbinic
Correct: A — Since the second marriage had no validity (her first husband was alive), the get is meaningless — it is a bill of divorce given to a woman who is not his wife, and therefore has no effect on her status.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why did the Sages eliminate the ketubah for a woman in a forbidden marriage?
A. As a fine for violating a prohibition
B. So that she will be demeaned in his eyes and he will easily divorce her
C. Because the marriage never had legal standing
D. To prevent the second husband from profiting
Correct: B — Normally a ketubah prevents hasty divorce. Here, the Sages WANT the husband to divorce her easily since the marriage is forbidden, so they removed the financial deterrent.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Ḥisda, what happens when someone intentionally separates terumah from impure produce for pure produce?
A. The terumah is valid but he must separate again
B. The separated portion is terumah but the remaining produce is exempt
C. Nothing at all — even the separated portion returns to its untithed state
D. The terumah is valid and the remaining produce is exempt
Correct: C — Rav Ḥisda interprets 'he has done nothing' literally — the entire act is completely disregarded, and the separated griva returns to being ordinary untithed produce.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How do Shmuel and R' Yoḥanan resolve the problem that the first husband's child is called a mamzer?
A. They say the child is a full mamzer by Torah law
B. They say the child may marry neither a regular Jewess nor a mamzeret — it is a stringency only
C. They say the mishna is not authoritative
D. They say the child's status depends on whether the mother received a get
Correct: B — The child is called 'mamzer' to teach that he may not marry a regular Jewish woman, but he is also forbidden to a mamzeret. This avoids truly uprooting Torah law — it is a double stringency, not a redefinition of status.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What proof does Rav Ḥisda bring from the minor wife to argue that Sages CAN uproot Torah law?
A. A minor wife can eat terumah even though her marriage is only rabbinic
B. A minor wife can perform chalitzah
C. The husband inherits her and becomes impure for her — Torah-level effects from a rabbinic marriage
D. A minor wife's children are considered legitimate
Correct: C — Inheritance and priestly impurity are Torah-law obligations, yet they apply to a marriage that exists only by rabbinic enactment (the minor was married off by her mother/brother). This demonstrates the Sages applying Torah-level consequences through rabbinic authority.
