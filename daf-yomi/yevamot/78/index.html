Egyptian Convert Generations & Lineage Rules

Core Question: How does a 2nd-generation Egyptian convert produce permitted 3rd-generation offspring?
↓
Torah says "sons of the third generation" — each word is expounded
↓
"Sons"
Prohibition applies in later generations too — not just the first three after Sinai
"Generation"
Depends on generation count, not birth order among siblings
"To Them"
Count from the converts themselves & follow the disqualified parent
Key Principle The Torah's treatment of Egyptian converts teaches rules about generational counting, lineage tracing, and how status follows the more flawed parent after conversion.

Can Torah Legislate Cases Arising from Sin?

Gemara
How can a 2nd-generation Egyptian convert produce permitted offspring? He can marry neither a Jewess nor an Egyptian convert!
Answer
Perhaps if he transgressed and married a convert or Jewess, his seed is purified.
Gemara
A case of 'that if' (אם עבר) — the Torah does not legislate situations arising only through transgression!
Objection
But a mamzer is itself a case of 'that if' — and the Torah writes it!
Resolution
A 'that if' resulting in a prohibition, the Torah writes. A 'that if' leading to an allowance, it does not.
Principle The Torah legislates consequences of transgression only when the result is a prohibition (mamzer), not when it would create a leniency.

Why Both 'Sons' and 'Generation' Are Needed

If Only 'Sons' (בנים)

  • Might think prohibition depends on birth order
  • 1st & 2nd sons forbidden, 3rd son permitted
  • Would ignore generational status entirely

If Only 'Generation' (דור)

  • Might count generations from Mount Sinai
  • After 3 generations from Torah-giving, all Egyptians permitted
  • Would ignore individual family lineage

Both Together

  • Prohibition depends on generational status
  • Count from each convert's own family line
  • Third generation of each convert family is permitted
Baraita Deut. 23:9 — "The sons of the third generation that are born to them may enter the congregation of the Lord."

Expounding 'To Them' (להם) and 'That Are Born'

Verse says "to them" twice and "that are born"
↓
1st 'To Them'
Count from the converts themselves — they are Generation 1
2nd 'To Them'
Follow the disqualified parent — child's status follows the unfit parent
'That Are Born'
Each birth = new generation — a fetus in a converting mother is Gen 2, not Gen 1
Practical Case A pregnant Egyptian woman who converts: without 'that are born,' her fetus would be considered part of her (Gen 1). The verse teaches the fetus is a separate generation (Gen 2).

Fetus, Immersion & Interposition (Amud B)

IssueRuleReasoning
Does immersion require the fetus to be exposed to water?No — the womb is not an interposition (חציצה)It is the fetus's natural manner of growth
Rav Kahana's rule on interpositionA substance covering the entire body is an interposition even if one is not particularTorah-level interposition applies
Why doesn't this disqualify the fetus's immersion?The womb is not a foreign substance — it is the fetus's natural placeTherefore the fetus is considered to have immersed
Result A fetus present during its mother's conversion immersion is considered to have validly immersed, making it a second-generation convert.

Rabbi Yochanan's Lineage Rules (via Ravina)

📜 Among gentile nations: lineage follows the father
↓
🔄 Source: Lev. 25:45 — Canaanite father + non-Canaanite mother = Canaanite child (cannot remain in Eretz Yisrael)
↓
✡️ After conversion and marriage: follow the more flawed parent
↓
👨 Example: Male Ammonite convert + Female Egyptian convert → son follows Ammonite father (permanently barred)
↓
👩 Same couple → daughter follows Egyptian mother (treated as 2nd-generation Egyptian)
Key Distinction Before conversion: patrilineal. After conversion: the child's status follows whichever parent bears the greater disqualification.
