Can the Sages Uproot Torah Law?

Gemara
The central question: Do the Sages have the power to uproot something established by Torah law?
Proofs Attempted
Multiple cases are brought — teruma payment, impure blood, canceling a get — each attempting to prove the Sages can override Torah law.
Resolution
Each proof is deflected: Shev v'al ta'aseh (passive inaction) is different from active uprooting. Betrothal works only by rabbinic authorization, so the Sages can revoke it.
Core Principle The Sages can instruct passive inaction (shev v'al ta'aseh) even when it sidelines a Torah obligation, but they cannot command an active violation of Torah law.

Teruma Payment: Impure Produce for Eaten Teruma

A non-priest ate impure teruma belonging to a Kohen
↓
He must pay with ritually pure, non-sacred produce
↓
What if he paid with impure non-sacred produce instead?
Sumakhos / R' Meir
Unwittingly → payment valid
Intentionally → payment NOT valid
The Rabbis
Either way → payment is valid as teruma, but he must go back and pay again with pure produce
Key Clarification Rav Aḥa b. Rav Ika: The dispute is only about pure teruma that was eaten. For impure teruma, all agree even impure payment works.

Does 'Payment Is Not Payment' Uproot Torah Law?

Gemara (Challenge)
R' Meir says intentional impure payment is not valid. But by Torah law it is valid — a Kohen could even betroth a woman with it! This means the Sages uproot Torah-level ownership!
Gemara (Answer)
'Not payment' means he must pay again with pure produce — but the original food is also consecrated as teruma. Torah law is not uprooted.
Gemara (Follow-up)
If so, Sumakhos and the Rabbis say the same thing!
Rav Aḥa b. Rav Ika
The difference: did the Sages decree against unwitting sinners on account of intentional ones? Rabbis say yes; Sumakhos says no.
Principle גזרו שוגג אטו מזיד — The Rabbis penalize accidental wrongdoers due to intentional ones; Sumakhos disagrees.

Impure Blood Sprinkled on the Altar

Torah Law (via Tzitz)

  • The High Priest's frontplate (tzitz) effects acceptance
  • Covers blood, meat, and fat that became impure
  • Works whether unwitting, intentional, or accidental
  • Applies to both individual and communal offerings

Rabbinic Ruling

  • If sprinkled intentionally while impure → not accepted
  • Owner must bring another offering
  • Seems to uproot Torah law — bringing a new animal would be chulin ba'azarah!
R' Yosei bar Ḥanina's Resolution 'Not accepted' means the meat may not be eaten, but the owner does gain atonement — no new offering needed. Torah law is preserved.

The Key Distinction: Shev V'Al Ta'aseh

שאני שב ואל תעשה
שב ואל תעשה — Sit and Do Not Act The Gemara draws a critical line: the Sages can instruct a person to refrain from performing a Torah mitzvah (passive inaction). They cannot instruct a person to actively violate Torah law.

Even if refraining means a mitzvah goes unfulfilled — like eating the sacrificial meat ('And they shall eat those things with which atonement was made' — Exodus 29:33) — this is permitted because the Sages are not commanding an action, only inaction.
