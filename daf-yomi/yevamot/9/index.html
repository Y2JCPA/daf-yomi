🏛️ Sin Offerings by Sinner Type — Unwitting Idolatry

Private Individual

  • Brings a she-goat
  • If intentional: executed by stoning
  • Money is spared from destruction
  • Derived from Numbers 15:27

Nasi (Leader)

  • Brings a she-goat
  • Same offering as individual
  • Derived via gezerah shavah on mitzvot
  • Shares individual's framework

Anointed High Priest

  • Brings a she-goat
  • Explicitly derived from written verse
  • Treated like a private individual
  • Derived from Leviticus 4

Community (Tzibbur)

  • Brings a bull (olah) + goat (chatat)
  • If intentional: condemned to burning
  • Money is destroyed
  • "One law for you" equalizes the offering

⚔️ Why Does the Community Bring the Same Offering as an Individual?

Challenger
An individual who sins intentionally is stoned — but his money is spared. A community that sins intentionally is burned — and their money is destroyed. Their punishments differ! Shouldn't their sin offerings differ too?
Torah Answer
"One law for you" (Numbers 15:29) — the Torah explicitly equates the offerings of the individual and the community, overriding the difference in punishment.
Rav Ḥilkiya (Hagronya)
Strongly objects! The verse "one law for you" implies we would otherwise differentiate — but without such a verse, why would we think they differ at all? What is the verse teaching us?
Gemara
Alternatively: Without this verse the community would have no remedy — the Torah specified no distinct offering for the many. The verse is necessary to confirm they bring the same offering as an individual, lest we think no offering exists at all.
Key Verse תּוֹרָה אַחַת יִהְיֶה לָכֶם — "One law for you" (Numbers 15:29)

מַאי טַעְמָא לָא קָתָנֵי — Why does the tanna not teach this case? (Yevamot 9b)
📜 Levi's Question to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi Levi asked: The mishna lists fifteen women whose rival wives are exempt from levirate marriage. But shouldn't there be a sixteenth — the mother who was raped by his father?

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi replied: That case is disputed between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis. According to Rabbi Yehuda, since she was forbidden at the time of the brother's marriage, she could never validly come before him for levirate marriage at all. Since it is disputed, the mishna omits it from Chapter 1.

Gemara challenges: But the mishna does include cases disputed by Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbis!

Answer: Those disputes appear in Chapter 2, not Chapter 1. This chapter lists only undisputed cases. And as for Beit Shammai's opinions — they are treated as if not present, since the halakha follows Beit Hillel.
