⚖️ Are Women Commanded in Pru U'Rvu?

Tanna Kamma
Only a man is commanded in the mitzva to be fruitful and multiply — not a woman.
R. Yoḥanan ben Beroka
Both are commanded! The verse says 'God blessed them and said to them: Be fruitful and multiply' (Gen. 1:28) — male and female alike.
Gemara
Vekhivshuha ('conquer it') is written without a vav in the Torah — read as masculine singular, excluding women from this obligation.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
The halakha is not in accordance with Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka — confirmed by Rabbi Abbahu citing Rabbi Yoḥanan directly.
Practical Ruling Though women are exempt from pru u'rvu as a mitzva, they may still demand a divorce if their husband cannot father children — based on personal desire for children, not mitzva obligation.

👨👩 Men vs. Women: The Procreation Obligation

Man — Obligated

  • Commanded by Torah: פְּרוּ וּרְבוּ
  • Must divorce wife after 10 childless years
  • Cannot waive obligation
  • Source: 'fill the land and conquer it' — masculine singular
  • Bound even if wife refuses to claim divorce

Woman — Exempt

  • Not commanded in pru u'rvu (Tanna Kamma — halakha)
  • Cannot demand divorce as mitzva fulfillment
  • May demand divorce for personal desire for children
  • Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka disagrees — overruled
  • Story of Yehudit: acted on her own after learning the law

💔 Infertility, Miscarriage & Divorce Protocol

Woman married twice — no children with either husband
↓
May not marry a third husband unless he already has children
↓
If she marries a third husband and has no children for ten years...
↓
Husband claims miscarriage once
Disputed — may not interrupt ten-year count
Miscarried twice
Husband's claim acknowledged; further ruling debated
Miscarried three times
Established pattern (ḥazakah) — must divorce; she collects ketubah
↓
After ten childless years: husband must divorce and pay ketubah

🔥 Amoraic Dispute: Who Said What?

Version A
Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Halakha is NOT like Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: Halakha IS like him.
Rabbi Abbahu
I sat before Rabbi Yoḥanan and heard him say: The halakha is in accordance with Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka — women are commanded.
Rav Pappa
If Rabbi Abbahu said this, it explains why Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi turned their faces away — they had ruled against women's claim and felt rebuked.
R. Aḥa bar Ḥanina
An incident came before Rabbi Yoḥanan in Caesarea — he compelled the husband to divorce his wife seeking children. But perhaps she had a personal reason, not a mitzva claim.
Conclusion The Gemara leaves the ruling ambiguous — but practice follows that women are not mitzva-bound, while their personal claims for divorce remain valid.

🕊️ Departing from Truth for Peace — Three Sources

גָּדוֹל הַשָּׁלוֹם שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא שִׁנָּה בּוֹ מִפְּנֵי הַשָּׁלוֹם
Rabbi Elazar son of Rabbi Shimon Three teachings on silence, truth, and peace:

1. Just as it is a mitzva to say what will be heeded, so is it a mitzva to remain silent about what will not be heeded.

2. It is permitted to depart from truth for peace — Joseph's brothers said 'Your father commanded: forgive your brothers' (Gen. 50:16) even though Jacob never said this.

3. Rabbi Natan: It is a mitzva to depart from truth for peace — God told Samuel to say he came to sacrifice, not to anoint David (I Sam. 16:2).

School of Rabbi Yishmael: Even God altered truth for peace — Sarah said 'my lord is old' but God told Abraham she said 'I am old' (Gen. 18:12–13).

🌿 Yehudit's Secret — Rabbi Ḥiyya's Wife

Background: Rabbi Ḥiyya's wife Yehudit suffered terribly in childbirth. Their twins Yehuda and Ḥizkiyya were born two months apart — she endured prolonged agony.
↓
The Disguise: Yehudit disguised herself before Rabbi Ḥiyya and asked: Is a woman commanded in pru u'rvu? He answered: No.
↓
Her Action: Armed with his ruling, she drank a sterilizing potion (sam akara) to prevent further pregnancies — without informing her husband.
↓
Discovery: Rabbi Ḥiyya eventually learned what she had done. He said: 'If only you had given me one more belly full' — wishing for more children.
↓
Legal Implication: Her actions demonstrate that a woman exempt from pru u'rvu has standing to make her own reproductive choices — the law she obtained shaped her decision.
