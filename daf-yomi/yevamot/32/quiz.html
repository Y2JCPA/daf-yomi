QUESTION 1 OF 5
In the mishna about two brothers married to two sisters: after the surviving brother's own wife dies, what is the status of the first brother's widow?
A. She remains permanently exempt from levirate marriage
B. She becomes permitted to the surviving brother for levirate marriage
C. She must perform ḥalitza with a third brother first
D. She is forbidden due to the brother's-wife prohibition
Correct: B — Once the surviving brother's wife dies, the 'sister of his wife' prohibition lapses. The widow is now fully permitted to him for levirate marriage — the original exemption was contingent on his wife being alive.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rav Ashi, what happens to the wife's-sister prohibition during a levirate bond?
A. It is permanently extinguished by the brother's-wife prohibition
B. It applies simultaneously alongside the brother's-wife prohibition
C. It is suspended, and descends to take effect once the brother's-wife prohibition ends
D. It applies only if the levirate marriage is never consummated
Correct: C — Rav Ashi teaches that the wife's-sister prohibition is 'suspended' during the levirate bond. When the brother's-wife prohibition terminates (e.g., through actual levirate marriage), the wife's-sister prohibition descends and takes full effect.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What distinguishes an 'issur mosif' (expanded prohibition) from an ordinary second prohibition?
A. It applies only in Temple contexts
B. It adds new subjects or contexts beyond those covered by the original prohibition
C. It doubles the penalty rather than adding a new prohibition
D. It applies only when the first prohibition was rabbinic in origin
Correct: B — An issur mosif is a second prohibition that expands scope — adding new persons or situations not covered by the original. Rabbi Yosei holds that such an expanded prohibition does take hold and creates double liability.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In the case of the non-priest who served in the Temple on Shabbat, why does Rabbi Ḥiyya hold him liable on two counts?
A. Temple service never overrides any prohibition for a non-priest
B. The Shabbat prohibition was overridden for Temple labor; the non-priest prohibition was not — two independent violations remain
C. Non-priests are always doubly punished in Temple cases
D. He violated Shabbat twice by performing two separate acts
Correct: B — Temple service overrides the Shabbat prohibition for those authorized (priests), but it does not override the prohibition against non-priests serving. The non-priest violated both independently, yielding double liability.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Rava explain Rabbi Yosei's ruling that one is liable on two counts when consummating levirate marriage with a woman who is both a brother's wife and a wife's sister?
A. Rabbi Yosei holds two prohibitions always coexist when both conditions are met
B. The man performs two distinct acts — betrothal then consummation — each violating one prohibition
C. Rabbi Yosei sentences him as if he transgressed both, since the act traversed each prohibition in sequence
D. The wife's-sister prohibition always overrides the brother's-wife prohibition, creating a single aggravated count
Correct: C — Rava explains that Rabbi Yosei does not require simultaneous coexistence of two prohibitions. Rather, he assigns double liability conceptually — the single act of consummation effectively traversed both prohibitions, so the man is sentenced as having violated both.
