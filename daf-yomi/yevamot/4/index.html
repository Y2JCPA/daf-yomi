⚖️ The Great Juxtaposition Debate

Ben Azzai
"You shall not allow a sorceress to live" is juxtaposed with sexual immorality laws (Exod. 22:17–18). Therefore a sorceress is executed by stoning — the same death penalty as sexual immorality!
Rabbi Yehuda
Just because the Torah placed two topics next to each other, shall we take this person out to be stoned? I do not derive laws from juxtaposed verses — there must be a more explicit source.
Rabbi Yehuda
The real source: mediums and wizards were already included among sorcerers. They were singled out in a separate verse to teach they are stoned — and this ruling extends to all sorcerers.
Rav Giddel (citing Rav)
Yet Rabbi Yehuda does use juxtaposition in Deuteronomy! 'A man shall not take his father's wife and shall not uncover his father's skirt' (Deut. 23:1) — the adjacency with the rape law (Deut. 22:29) teaches that even a woman raped by his father is forbidden.
The Rule Rabbi Yehuda: Juxtaposition is not generally valid — but Deuteronomy (Mishneh Torah) is different. Moses deliberately reviewed and arranged it, making every adjacency intentional and legally binding.

🧵 Tzitzit & Shatnez — The Opening Case

לֹא תִלְבַּשׁ שַׁעַטְנֵז... גְּדִלִים תַּעֲשֶׂה לָּךְ עַל-אַרְבַּע כַּנְפוֹת כְּסוּתְךָ
The Starting Point Deuteronomy 22:11–12: "You shall not wear diverse kinds of wool and linen together. You shall make twisted fringes on the four corners of your covering." These verses are juxtaposed to teach that tzitzit (ritual fringes) override the shatnez prohibition — a wool thread on a linen garment is fully permitted when fulfilling the mitzvah of tzitzit. This is the paradigmatic example of deriving law from adjacent verses.

⚖️ Rabbi Yehuda: When Is Juxtaposition Valid?

General Torah — No Juxtaposition

  • ❌ Does NOT derive laws from adjacent verses
  • Rejects ben Azzai's sorceress argument
  • Demands explicit textual source or analogy
  • Adjacency may be coincidental or stylistic
  • The Rabbis disagree and do use juxtaposition throughout Torah

Deuteronomy — Juxtaposition Valid

  • ✅ DOES derive from adjacent verses
  • Deuteronomy = Mishneh Torah (Moses's review)
  • Every textual placement is deliberate
  • Father's wife prohibition derived from adjacency with rape law
  • Rav Yosef: Even those who normally reject juxtaposition agree about Deuteronomy

🧙 The Sorceress: What Justifies Stoning?

Ben Azzai
Juxtaposition proves it! Sorcery verse (Exod. 22:17) sits next to sexual immorality laws — those are punished by stoning. Therefore the sorceress is stoned. Adjacency teaches the law.
Rabbi Yehuda
This reasoning is dangerously weak. We stone a person based on a verse's neighborhood? Mediums and wizards — themselves a subset of sorcerers — were singled out elsewhere to be stoned. That verse teaches all sorcerers face stoning. No juxtaposition needed.
The Stakes This debate determines life and death: stoning (the most severe execution) versus other forms. Rabbi Yehuda insists the source must be explicit — proximity of topics is insufficient grounds to impose the harshest penalty.

🔗 Juxtaposition in Deuteronomy: The Father's Wife

Deut. 22:29: "The man who lay with her [the rape victim] must give the maiden's father fifty shekels…"
↓
Deut. 23:1 (immediately after): "A man shall not take his father's wife and shall not uncover his father's skirt"
↓
Clause 1
"Shall not take his father's wife" → prohibits marrying a woman who had full relations with his father
Clause 2
"Shall not uncover his father's skirt" → prohibits a yevama waiting for levirate marriage to the father
↓
Via adjacency: Even a woman raped by his father is forbidden to him. The proximity of Deut. 22:29 (rape) to Deut. 23:1 (father's wife) is deliberate in Deuteronomy — making this a valid legal derivation.
