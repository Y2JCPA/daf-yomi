⚖️ Abaye vs Rava: Property Rights of the Yevama

Abaye
The yavam's hand is weaker than the yevama's in her property. Beit Hillel holds the husband's hand equals the wife's — so the yavam, one step further removed, has an even lesser claim.
Rava
If the property was bequeathed while she was under the first husband, everyone agrees the yavam's hand is stronger. Beit Shammai: levirate betrothal makes her like a doubly uncertain betrothed woman — certain aspects like definite betrothal, others not.
Rav Pappa
The mishna's precise wording — 'property that enters and leaves with her' — supports Abaye. 'Enters' = brought in at marriage; 'leaves' = taken out at death or divorce. The formulation favors Abaye's interpretation.
The Core Tension The dispute turns on when property was bequeathed (under first husband vs. during levirate waiting period) and what status levirate betrothal (zikah) actually confers.

🏛️ Beit Hillel vs Beit Shammai: Status of Levirate Betrothal (Zikah)

Beit Hillel

  • Zikah = weak acquisition only
  • Husband's hand equals wife's hand (not stronger)
  • Yavam has weaker claim than yevama herself
  • Property bequeathed during waiting period → yevama keeps it
  • Mishna's language ('enters and leaves') supports this view per Rav Pappa

Beit Shammai

  • Zikah = partial acquisition with real effect
  • She is like a doubly uncertain betrothed woman
  • Certain aspects: treated as definitely betrothed
  • Other aspects: treated as not betrothed at all
  • Yavam's hand stronger for property from first marriage period

🔥 R. Yoḥanan vs R. Yehoshua ben Levi: Younger's Yibum or Elder's Ḥalitza?

R. Yoḥanan
Yibum by a younger brother is preferable to ḥalitza by the eldest. The primary mitzva is the act of yibum itself — better fulfilled by any brother performing the actual marriage than by the eldest doing only ḥalitza.
R. Yehoshua ben Levi
Ḥalitza by the eldest brother is preferable to yibum by a younger brother. The obligation of yibum and ḥalitza falls primarily on the eldest — his ḥalitza is therefore a more proper fulfillment of the mitzva.
Second Version (Gemara)
A more limited version: both agree yibum by any brother beats ḥalitza entirely. The dispute is only whether ḥalitza of the eldest beats ḥalitza of a younger brother — a narrower question.
Proof & Rejection Proof attempt: 'If eldest refuses, court goes to younger brothers' implies younger's yibum is valid and preferred. Rejected: the mishna only means the eldest refused both yibum and ḥalitza — not that younger's yibum is inherently preferable.

🎯 Abba Shaul: Intent Determines the Character of Yibum

כָּל הַכּוֹנֵס אֶת יְבִמְתּוֹ לְשֵׁם נוֹי, אוֹ לְשֵׁם אִישׁוּת... כְּאִלּוּ פָּגַע בְּעֶרְוָה
Abba Shaul — Critical Ruling on Motivation One who consummates levirate marriage with his yevama for the sake of her beauty, or for the sake of marital relations, or to inherit his brother's estate — it is as though he has approached a forbidden relative (ervah).

Historical consequence: Initially (when yevamin acted with proper intent) — yibum took precedence over ḥalitza. Later, seeing improper motivations, the Sages ruled ḥalitza takes precedence. Eventually, later generations returned to preferring yibum — having aligned with the Rabbis (not Abba Shaul), who hold yibum is valid even without pure intent.

The Gemara reconciles a difficult baraita: the verse 'her brother-in-law will have intercourse with her' teaches it is a mitzva initially — i.e., when intent is proper.
