📜 The Opening Law of Yevamot The Torah obligates a yavam (surviving brother) to marry his deceased childless brother's widow (yevama) or release her via ḥalitza. Yet fifteen categories of women — forbidden as incestuous relations — are exempt from both obligations. More strikingly, they exempt their rival wives (tzarot) alongside them, and this exemption cascades: even the rival wife of a rival wife is freed.

🔒 The Fifteen Forbidden Categories

Direct Descendants

  • His daughter (derived homiletically)
  • His daughter's daughter
  • His son's daughter
  • His wife's daughter (stepdaughter)
  • His wife's son's daughter
  • His wife's daughter's daughter

Ascendants & In-Laws

  • His mother-in-law (חמותו)
  • His mother-in-law's mother
  • His father-in-law's mother
  • His mother (אמו)

Siblings & Collateral

  • His maternal half-sister
  • His mother's sister (maternal aunt)
  • His wife's sister

Brothers' Wives

  • Wife of a brother he coexisted with — forbidden during brother's lifetime
  • Wife of a brother he did not coexist with — yibum obligation never arose

🔥 Why Does the Mishna List 'Daughter' First?

Gemara
The mishna begins with 'his daughter' — but the prohibition on one's mother carries a death penalty too. Why not begin there, since it is the most basic forbidden relation?
Suggestion
Perhaps the tanna ordered the list by severity of punishment — and this follows R. Shimon, who holds that burning is MORE severe than stoning. Cases incurring burning come first!
Gemara
But even so — mother-in-law should come first, since the main verse about burning punishment is written there: 'A man who marries a woman and her mother — burning' (Vayikra 20:14)!
Resolution
The prohibition on his daughter is not stated explicitly in the Torah — it is derived only through homiletical interpretation (derashah). Because of this novelty, it is especially dear to the tanna, who places it first to teach that even this non-explicit prohibition fully applies.
Principle Laws derived through creative interpretation rather than explicit text are often highlighted first — their non-obvious derivation makes them precious to the Sages.

🔗 The Cascading Chain of Rival-Wife Exemptions

A forbidden relative (e.g., yavam's daughter) was married to the deceased brother
↓
The brother also had another wife — the 'rival wife' (tzara)
↓
The brother dies childless: the forbidden relative is fully exempt from yibum and ḥalitza
↓
By extension, the rival wife is also exempt — she performs ḥalitza only (or is fully free)
↓
The rival wife herself may have her own rival wife — who is also exempt via the chain
↓
Even 100 rival wives are all exempted by the single forbidden relative in the group
↓
⚠️ Break in chain: If the forbidden relative died, was divorced, or was an aylonit before the brother's death — all rival wives lose their exemption
