QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the Gemara require three separate verses to teach that ritually impure persons may not eat teruma?
A. Each verse addresses a different category of priest (Kohen, Levi, Israelite)
B. Each verse teaches a unique halakha: sunset required; atonement-obligated persons included; immersion still required even after purification period ends
C. The verses each address a different type of ritual impurity (zav, metzora, childbirth)
D. One verse is for teruma, one for second tithe, and one for consecrated food
Correct: B — Each verse is independently necessary. Without the first (Lev. 22:4) we wouldn't know sunset is required; without the sunset verse we'd exclude atonement-obligated persons; without Lev. 12:4 we'd think completing the purification period alone suffices without immersion.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Yoḥanan say the mishna (wife of wounded priest eats teruma) can accord with Rabbi Meir's opinion?
A. Because Rabbi Meir's restriction only applies after the husband's death, not mere injury
B. Because the marriage bond remains intact after the injury, preserving her already-established teruma-eating status
C. Because a wounded priest retains full priestly status according to Rabbi Meir
D. Because she earned an independent right to teruma before the injury occurred
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan distinguishes a wounded priest from a deceased one. When a husband dies, his acquisition of his wife lapses entirely. But when he is merely wounded, the marriage persists — and her established status to eat teruma continues with it.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Shmuel, what is the halakha for a man whose testicles were crushed 'at the hand of Heaven'?
A. He is forbidden to marry any Jewish woman
B. He may marry a convert but not a born-Jewish woman
C. He is fit to marry a woman born Jewish
D. His fitness depends on whether he can still produce children
Correct: C — Shmuel rules that Heaven-caused genital injury does not incur the Deuteronomy 23:2 prohibition. Rava derives this exegetically from the word 'ish' (man) in the verse, which excludes conditions not caused by human action.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rav Huna, when is a man whose member was partially cut deemed fit to enter the congregation?
A. When at least half the length of the member remains intact
B. When the cut is shaped like a quill — closed on all sides so air cannot penetrate
C. When a physician certifies that reproductive function is preserved
D. When a hairsbreadth of the corona remains on any single side
Correct: B — Rav Huna rules that a quill-like cut (closed, sharp all around) prevents air from penetrating and the man is fit; a gutter-like cut (open channel) allows air to penetrate and the man is unfit. Mar bar Rav Ashi applied this ruling surgically, reshaping a gutter-cut member into a quill shape to render the man fit.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does the Gemara prove that 'kerut shofkha' (severed emission channel) refers specifically to genital injury?
A. A gezera shava links 'shofkha' to a verse explicitly describing the male reproductive organ
B. By comparison to mamzer in the next verse: mamzer involves no stated generational limit, indicating reproductive dysfunction — so kerut shofkha must also affect the reproductive organ, not the nose
C. The verse uses the word 'kerut' (severed), which the Rabbis interpret as referring exclusively to the genitals
D. From a baraita that explicitly defines kerut shofkha as penile amputation
Correct: B — The Gemara reasons: mamzer, juxtaposed with kerut shofkha, has no stated limit on how many generations are excluded — this implies reproductive harm, not nasal injury. Moreover, the verse says כָּרוּת (severed), not בִּשְׁפֹּךְ (discharge), ruling out a nose from which fluid flows but nothing is severed.
