📜 Why Do Three Verses Restrict Impure Persons from Teruma?

Three separate verses restrict ritually impure persons from eating teruma — are they redundant?
↓
Leviticus 22:4
"He shall not eat holy things until he be pure" — establishes that sunset is required
Leviticus 7:20 (sunset verse)
"When the sun has set" — clarifies even those requiring an atonement offering may eat teruma after sunset + immersion
Leviticus 12:4
"Until days of her purification are completed" — teaches that immersion is still required even after completion of the purification period
↓
Each verse is indispensable — no single verse could teach all three halakhot alone

🗣️ Does the Mishna Follow Rabbi Meir? Rabbi Yoḥanan vs. Rabbi Elazar

Rabbi Elazar
The mishna (wife of wounded priest eats teruma) cannot follow Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Meir holds a widow betrothed to a High Priest may NOT eat teruma — by the same logic, the wife of a wounded priest should lose her rights.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Even according to Rabbi Meir, this case is different. She had already been eating teruma on her husband's account before his injury — her established status continues as long as the marriage persists.
Rabbi Elazar
We cannot say 'she already ate, so she continues eating.' If that were true, a widow of a High Priest should also continue eating teruma after his death!
Rabbi Yoḥanan
No comparison. When a husband dies, his acquisition of her lapses entirely — they are no longer married. But when he is merely wounded, the marriage bond remains fully intact, preserving her status.
Conclusion The mishna can accord with Rabbi Meir. The wife's prior, established status grants continuity so long as the marriage itself persists.

⚔️ Petzua Dakka vs. Kerut Shofkha — Two Excluded Categories

פְּצוּעַ דַּכָּא — Petzua Dakka

  • Wounded by crushing of the testicles
  • Even one testicle wounded = disqualified
  • Includes: cut, torn, punctured, or crushed
  • Identity derived by analogy to kerut shofkha
  • Excluded from the congregation (Deuteronomy 23:2)
  • Heaven-caused wound → still fit to marry a born-Jewish woman (Shmuel)

כְּרוּת שׁוֹפְכָה — Kerut Shofkha

  • Severed 'channel of emission' — the penis
  • Proven to mean genital injury: compared to mamzer (next verse) — both lack stated generations → indicates reproductive organ
  • Not a nose injury: verse says כָּרוּת (severed), not בִּשְׁפֹּךְ (discharge without severing)
  • Excluded from the congregation (Deuteronomy 23:2)
  • A remaining hairsbreadth of corona = still fit

🔪 Rav Huna's Ruling: Quill vs. Gutter — Determining Fitness

Injury occurs to the member — partial amputation. Is he fit or unfit?
↓
Examine the shape of the cut: does it form a closed point on all sides?
↓
Cut like a quill (kulmus) — sharp, closed all around → air does not penetrate → FIT (כָּשֵׁר)
↓
Cut like a gutter — open channel on one side → air penetrates through the opening → UNFIT (פָּסוּל)
↓
Rava confirms: the air-penetration factor is the operative distinction — like a stopper in a barrel.
↓
Surgical remedy: Mar bar Rav Ashi cut back surrounding flesh on all sides until it resembled a quill → rendered the man fit to enter the congregation

🌿 Key Ruling — Shmuel on Heaven-Caused Wounds A man with crushed testicles at the hand of Heaven — i.e., from birth or through illness — is fit to marry a woman born Jewish. Rava derives this from the word אִישׁ (man) in Deuteronomy 23:2: the verse excludes one whose condition was not caused by human action. A similar baraita confirms: the prohibition of petzua dakka is juxtaposed with mamzer — just as mamzer's blemish is caused by a person (forbidden union), so too petzua dakka refers only to human-caused injury.
