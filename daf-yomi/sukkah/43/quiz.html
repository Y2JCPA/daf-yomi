QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why did the Sages prohibit taking the lulav on Shabbat, even though the first day is a Torah mitzva?
A. Lulav is muktzeh and categorically forbidden to handle on Shabbat
B. The Sages feared one might carry the lulav to an expert to learn how to wave it, violating the Torah prohibition of carrying in the public domain
C. The first day of Sukkot can never fall on Shabbat according to the Jewish calendar
D. Lulav is only a Torah mitzva inside the Temple; outside the Temple it is entirely rabbinic
Correct: B — Rabba's reason (introduced on 42b and continued here) is concern that a person, unfamiliar with the waving technique, might carry his lulav to an expert — thereby carrying four cubits in the public domain, a Torah violation.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is derived from the word לָכֶם ('for yourselves') in the verse 'And you shall take for yourselves on the first day' (Lev. 23:40)?
A. The lulav must be waved by each person personally and cannot be fulfilled by a proxy
B. A borrowed or stolen lulav does not fulfill the mitzva — the lulav must be one's own
C. Women are equally obligated in the mitzva of lulav
D. The lulav must be acquired specifically for the purpose of the mitzva
Correct: B — The baraita teaches that 'yourselves' (לָכֶם) means from your own property — thereby excluding a stolen or borrowed lulav from fulfilling the mitzva of the four species.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does the Gemara establish that the mitzva of sukkah applies at night as well as during the day?
A. A direct verse explicitly states 'you shall dwell in the sukkah by day and by night'
B. It is derived by analogy to Shabbat, which also applies at night
C. Via a verbal analogy (gezerah shavah): the word תֵּשְׁבוּ appears in both the sukkah verse (Lev. 23:42) and the Tabernacle inauguration (Lev. 8:35), which explicitly says 'day and night'
D. The Rabbis extended the mitzva to nighttime as a rabbinic stringency
Correct: C — The Gemara employs a gezerah shavah: since תֵּשְׁבוּ ('you shall reside') appears in both the sukkah commandment and the Tabernacle inauguration — which explicitly includes 'day and night' — sukkah is derived to apply both day and night.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does the aravah mitzva override Shabbat on the seventh day (in Temple times), but the lulav does not?
A. The aravah is a more severe Torah obligation than lulav and therefore takes precedence
B. Court agents supply the aravot publicly, eliminating individual carrying concerns; lulav is brought personally by each individual
C. The seventh day of Sukkot is more sacred than the first day, giving it greater power to override Shabbat
D. The aravah is so lightweight that carrying it is considered insignificant by Torah standards
Correct: B — Since court agents supply and distribute the aravot collectively, no private person needs to carry one in the public domain. With lulav, each person carries his own — creating exactly the concern that Shabbat might be violated.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What halachic conclusion is drawn from the story of the branches hidden under the altar stones by the court agents?
A. The aravah mitzva may be performed after nightfall on the seventh day
B. The aravah mitzva is fulfilled by taking and waving the branch — not merely by standing it upright at the altar sides
C. Boethusians are invalidated from participating in Temple service
D. Willow branches prepared before Shabbat retain their sanctity when used on Shabbat itself
Correct: B — When the ignoramuses found the hidden aravot and waved them, this demonstrated that the mitzva requires active taking and waving. This refutes Rav Yosef's suggestion that the mitzva might be fulfilled merely by leaning the branches against the altar sides.
