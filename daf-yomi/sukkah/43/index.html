⚖️ Lulav vs. Aravah on Shabbat

🌿 Lulav

  • Prohibited on Shabbat by rabbinic decree
  • Concern: one may carry it to an expert to learn waving
  • First day is Torah law even in outlying areas — yet still prohibited
  • Calendar uncertainty: we don't know which day is truly the first
  • Solution (Temple era): bring lulav to Temple Mount on Friday

🌾 Aravah (Day 7)

  • Seventh day overrides Shabbat in Temple era by Torah law
  • Court agents supply the branches — no individual carrying concern
  • Today: calendar uncertainty prevents overriding Shabbat
  • Additional debate: is the mitzva by taking/waving or standing upright?
  • Hidden branches story resolves: fulfilled by taking and waving

🤔 Why Doesn't Lulav Override Shabbat on the First Day?

Questioner
Since the first day of lulav is a Torah mitzva even in the outlying areas, why not take the lulav when the first day falls on Shabbat?
Gemara
The Sages decreed against it: one might carry the lulav to an expert to learn how to wave it — violating the Torah prohibition of carrying four cubits in the public domain.
Questioner
Then at least today, people of Eretz Yisrael who know the calendar precisely should take the lulav on the first day when it falls on Shabbat!
Gemara
We do not know exactly when the month was established in earlier generations. Since we observe two days of Yom Tov due to doubt, we cannot be certain which is truly the first day.
Questioner
But when bar Hedya came from Eretz Yisrael, he testified that the people there do know the calendar precisely!
Gemara
Since we in the diaspora have adopted their practice of two days, we do not distinguish — and we cannot override Shabbat based on the calendar of Eretz Yisrael alone.
Temple Era Solution All people would bring their lulavim to the Temple Mount on Friday. If Shabbat turned out to be the first day, each person retrieved his lulav there and waved it — without any carrying in the public domain.

📜 Deriving First-Day Lulav Outside the Temple

Verse (Lev. 23:40): "And you shall take for yourselves on the first day the fruit of a beautiful tree, branches of palm trees..."
↓
לָכֶם — "For yourselves"
From your own property — a borrowed or stolen lulav does not fulfill the mitzva
בַּיּוֹם — "On the day"
Even on Shabbat — Torah mitzva applies every day of the Festival including Shabbat (R. Eliezer)
הָרִאשׁוֹן — "The first day"
Only the first day applies outside the Temple by Torah law; days 2–7 in the outlying areas are rabbinic
↓
Conclusion: The first day carries a Torah-level obligation everywhere — not only in the Temple — which is why it potentially overrides Shabbat

🏕️ Sukkah Applies at Night

בַּסֻּכֹּת תֵּשְׁבוּ שִׁבְעַת יָמִים — ויקרא כג:מב | וּפֶתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד תֵּשְׁבוּ יוֹמָם וָלַיְלָה שִׁבְעַת יָמִים — ויקרא ח:לה
גְּזֵרָה שָׁוָה — Verbal Analogy The Torah commands: "You shall reside in sukkot seven days" (Lev. 23:42). The same root תֵּשְׁבוּ — "you shall reside" — appears in the command regarding the inauguration of the Tabernacle: "At the door of the Tent of Meeting you shall reside day and night seven days" (Lev. 8:35). Just as the Tabernacle inauguration explicitly included both day and night, so too the mitzva of sukkah applies both day and night — including the obligation to sleep in the sukkah.

🪨 The Hidden Branches — Story of the Boethusians

The situation: The seventh day of Sukkot (Hoshana Rabbah) fell on Shabbat. The aravah mitzva overrides Shabbat by Torah law — but the Boethusians (a sect rejecting the Oral Torah) wanted to prevent it.
↓
Boethusian sabotage: The night before, they hid large stones beneath the willow branches piled at the altar, hoping to prevent the people from being able to take them on Shabbat morning.
↓
Counter-measure by court agents: On Friday, the court agents brought fresh aravot to the Temple and concealed them under the altar stones — hidden in advance so they could be retrieved on Shabbat without violating it.
↓
The ignoramuses intervene: The next morning, unlearned people who identified with the Sages discovered the hidden branches. Not knowing the halachic details, they pulled them out from under the stones and waved them enthusiastically.
↓
Halachic conclusion: The incident demonstrates that the aravah mitzva is fulfilled by taking and waving — not by simply standing it upright at the altar's sides (contra Rav Yosef's suggestion). The people waved the aravot and thereby fulfilled the mitzva.
