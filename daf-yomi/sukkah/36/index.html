🍋 Etrog Fitness: What Disqualifies?

UNFIT Etrog

  • Entirely peeled — skin fully removed
  • Pierced completely through (any size hole)
  • Tafuaḥ — bloated or externally rotted
  • Saruaḥ — decayed from within
  • Pickled or boiled
  • Cushite (black) etrog
  • White or speckled etrog
  • Shaped to look like another species
  • Unripe (Rabbi Akiva — lacks hadar)

FIT Etrog

  • Partially peeled — Rava permits
  • Partial hole (not through) if small
  • Molded into shape of etrog species
  • Partially eaten — remainder valid (R. Ḥanina)
  • Unripe — Rabbis permit (dispute R. Akiva)
  • Natural-color Cushite — Abaye's ruling

🔍 Pierced Etrog: Fitness Rules (Ulla bar Ḥanina)

Etrog was pierced — is it fit?
↓
Hole goes completely through the body
Unfit regardless of size — any complete perforation disqualifies
Hole does NOT go all the way through
Unfit only if large enough to extract a pomegranate seed; otherwise fit
↓
Principle: Complete penetration triggers a stricter standard than a partial cavity

🌱 Unripe Etrog: Do Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Shimon Agree?

Rabba
Rabbi Akiva (unripe etrog is unfit) and Rabbi Shimon (unripe produce is exempt from tithes) share the same principle — both hold that an unripe fruit lacks the legal status of fruit.
Abaye
Not necessarily! Rabbi Akiva's ruling is specific to etrog: the Torah requires hadar (beauty), which an unripe etrog lacks. Rabbi Shimon's tithe exemption may have an entirely different rationale.
Abaye
Alternatively: Rabbi Shimon derives the tithe exemption from 'year by year' — unripe produce has not completed its annual cycle. That logic has nothing to do with the etrog beauty requirement.
Conclusion (Gemara) Their opinions do NOT necessarily coincide. Each ruling stands on independent reasoning.

🔬 Rava's Dilemma: Tereifa-Like Signs in an Etrog

סָרוּחַ — putrid, decayed from within
The Question Rava asked: If tereifa-like signs appear in an etrog — e.g., its internal tissue dissolves to liquid (like a lung whose contents can be poured like a pitcher) — is it fit or unfit? The tension: In animal law, a lung whose tissue liquefied is not a disqualifying tereifa sign (since the organ still functions). Does the same logic apply to an etrog's dissolved interior? Resolution from baraita: A saruaḥ etrog — one that rotted internally — is unfit. Internal dissolution disqualifies an etrog even when analogous decay would not disqualify an animal organ.

🌿 Binding the Lulav: Rules and Reasoning

Mishna dispute: Rabbi Yehuda — bind the lulav only with one of its own species (the four species). Rabbi Meir — even a string or cord is valid; cites the Temple practice of using gold rings.
↓
Rava rules: One may bind the lulav with palm-tree fibers or even a piece of the date-palm trunk — both count as the lulav's own species, satisfying Rabbi Yehuda.
↓
Rava's rationale for Rabbi Yehuda: A lulav requires binding as part of the mitzva act itself. Therefore the binding must be from a valid species — the binding is not mere packaging but part of fulfilling the commandment.
↓
Rava's scriptural source: 'You shall reside in sukkot for seven days' (Lev. 23:42) — a kal va-chomer from sukkah materials to the four species implies palm fibers belong to the lulav species.
↓
Rabbis' objection: A kal va-chomer inference that begins stringently but concludes leniently is not a valid a fortiori argument — the reasoning is formally rejected, though the ruling may stand on other grounds.
