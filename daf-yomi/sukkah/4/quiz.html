QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why is the nullification of cushions and blankets (to reduce sukka height) considered invalid by the Gemara?
A. Soft furnishings can never serve as valid schach material
B. One will inevitably use them during Yom Tov, making the nullification void from the start
C. Only a rabbi can perform a valid halakhic nullification
D. Verbal nullification is never recognized in sukka law
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that the nullification is rendered batel (void) because the person will need the cushions and blankets during the festival. Since the intent to treat them as 'mere dirt' cannot be sustained through Yom Tov, the nullification is considered irrelevant from the outset.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Rava rejects Abaye's suggestion that drooping palm fronds can fix a too-short sukka. What is his reasoning?
A. Drooping fronds are classified as invalid schach (pesulei schach)
B. Fronds drooping within 10 handbreadths of the ground create a seruḥa — a sagging residence no one would dwell in
C. The principle of measuring only fronds within a boundary only applies upward, not downward
D. A sukka cannot use the same fronds to establish both its walls and its height
Correct: B — Rava invokes the principle that a seruḥa (a cramped, sagging, cave-like roof) is not a proper dwelling. When fronds droop to within 10 handbreadths of the ground, the sukka becomes an uncomfortably low sagging structure — not a dirat kavod (dignified dwelling).
QUESTION 3 OF 5
A platform is built in the center of a sukka that is more than 20 cubits high. When is the sukka fit?
A. Only if the platform covers more than half the floor area
B. If the distance from the platform's edge to the wall is less than 4 cubits in every direction
C. If the platform is at least 10 handbreadths high itself
D. Always, because the original sukka walls remain intact
Correct: B — The Gemara teaches that dofen akuma (curved wall) allows the existing walls to 'bend' horizontally to the platform edge — but only if the platform is within 4 cubits of the wall. For a center platform, this must hold in all directions. The novel ruling: dofen akuma can apply from multiple directions simultaneously.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is Rav Huna's interpretation of the dispute between Rabbi Ya'akov and the Rabbis about four posts?
A. The dispute applies only when the posts are made of a specific wood type
B. The dispute applies only when the posts are placed at the edge of a roof, where dofen akuma from the house walls below helps Rabbi Ya'akov
C. The dispute applies only in a public domain, not a private domain
D. The dispute applies only on Shabbat, not on Yom Tov
Correct: B — Rav Huna claims Rabbi Ya'akov only deems it fit when the posts are at the edge of a roof, because then dofen akuma from the pre-existing house walls below can supply the missing partitions. The Gemara refutes this: a baraita shows Rabbi Ya'akov deems fit even posts in the ground, where no house walls exist.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
From what source do Rav, Rabbi Ḥanina, Rabbi Yoḥanan, and Rav Ḥaviva derive the minimum sukka height of 10 handbreadths?
A. From the height of the Temple entrance chamber, which was 10 cubits
B. From the Ark: its body measured 9 handbreadths (1½ cubits at 6 handbreadths/cubit) plus 1 handbreadth for the kapporet cover = 10 handbreadths — the space where the Shekhinah spoke
C. From the average height of a person seated on the ground, rounded to 10 handbreadths
D. From the walls of the Tabernacle courtyard, which were 5 cubits = 30 handbreadths, divided by 3
Correct: B — The Ark of the Covenant was 1½ cubits high. Using the Temple cubit of 6 handbreadths, this equals 9 handbreadths for the Ark body, plus 1 handbreadth for the golden kapporet (cover) = 10 total handbreadths. The Divine Presence spoke from above the kapporet, establishing 10 handbreadths as the minimum space for sacred dwelling — and therefore for a sukka.
