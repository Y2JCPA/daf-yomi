QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Zevid's revised answer in the name of Rava, why does lulav merit a 7-day rabbinic commemoration but the willow branch does not?
A. Lulav is more aesthetically important than the willow branch
B. Lulav is explicitly written in the Torah, while the willow's derivation is only by inference
C. The willow was only practiced in the Temple, so it cannot be commemorated outside
D. The willow branch mitzva was discontinued after the Babylonian exile
Correct: B — Rav Zevid revised Rava's answer: since lulav is explicitly written in the Torah, we institute a 7-day commemoration. Since the willow's basis is only derived by inference (not explicitly written), it does not receive the same extension.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does the Gemara reconcile Rabbi Yoḥanan's seemingly contradictory statements — attributing the willow mitzva to Sinai, to the prophets, and to the Sages?
A. Rabbi Yoḥanan changed his mind over time and the Gemara records all three opinions
B. There were three different Amoraim all named Rabbi Yoḥanan
C. The three statements refer to different aspects: Temple willow (Sinai), taking the willow (Prophets), circling the altar (Sages)
D. Rabbi Yoḥanan was uncertain and intentionally stated all possibilities
Correct: C — The Gemara distinguishes three referents: the willow mitzva performed in the Temple is halakha from Sinai; taking the willow branch (outside the Temple) is a prophetic ordinance; the custom of circling the altar is a later rabbinic enactment.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rabbi Ami, why can one NOT fulfill the mitzva of the willow branch by lifting the willow that is bound to the lulav?
A. The bound willow becomes halachically invalid once tied to the lulav
B. A willow must be green and fresh; a bound willow dries out too quickly
C. The willow mitzva must be taken by itself as a separate act — fulfilling it simultaneously with the lulav is invalid
D. The minimum measure of the willow is lost when it is tied to the larger lulav bundle
Correct: C — Rabbi Ami ruled that the willow branch mitzva requires being taken by itself as a distinct act. Lifting the lulav bundle (with the willow bound to it) does not constitute a separate fulfillment of the willow mitzva.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What did Aivu observe when a man brought a willow branch before Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok to fulfill the mitzva?
A. Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok recited a special blessing and then circled the table
B. Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok rejected it because it lacked the minimum measure
C. Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok took the willow, waved it, and did NOT recite any blessing
D. Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok examined it carefully and declared it valid without waving it
Correct: C — Aivu testified that Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok took the willow branch, waved it, but recited no blessing (bracha) over it — establishing that waving is performed for this mitzva but no benediction is required.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the halakhic limit that Aivu transmitted in the name of Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok regarding travel on Shabbat eve?
A. One may not begin any journey after midday on Shabbat eve
B. One may not travel more than one parasang on Shabbat eve
C. One should not walk more than three parasangs on Shabbat eve without reaching one's place of rest
D. One must complete all travel by sunset on the eve of Shabbat
Correct: C — Aivu transmitted in the name of Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok that a person should not walk more than three parasangs (parsaot) on Shabbat eve — one must arrive at one's Shabbat destination before exceeding this distance. Rav Kahana adds this applies even when traveling home.
