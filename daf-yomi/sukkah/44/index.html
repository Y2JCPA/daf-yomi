⚖️ What Is the Source of the Willow Branch Mitzva?

Rabbi Yoḥanan (view A)
The willow branch is an ordinance of the prophets — Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi instituted it in the Temple.
Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi
The willow branch is a halakha transmitted to Moses from Sinai — it originates at Har Sinai itself.
Gemara
But Rabbi Yoḥanan himself said elsewhere it is a halakha from Sinai! And elsewhere he told the Babylonians it was merely their own rabbinic enactment!
Resolution
Three distinct referents: Sinai → willow in the Temple; Prophets → taking the willow; Sages → circling the altar with it.
Core Tension Rabbi Yoḥanan appears to give three contradictory sources for the willow — the Gemara resolves these as referring to three distinct aspects of the same mitzva.

🌿 Abaye Challenges Rava: Why No 7-Day Willow Commemoration?

Abaye
What is different about lulav that we perform it seven days in commemoration of the Temple — but we do not do the same for the willow branch?
Rav Zevid citing Rava (v.1)
Lulav is a Torah mitzva, so we commemorate it 7 days. Willow is a Rabbinic mitzva, so we do not extend it.
Gemara
But according to Abba Shaul, willow is derived from the Torah — the plural form aravot ha-naḥal (ערבות הנחל) implies two!
Rav Zevid citing Rava (v.2)
Revised: Lulav is explicitly written in the Torah. Willow's derivation is only by inference — not explicit enough to warrant 7-day commemoration in the Diaspora.
Principle Only a mitzva with an explicit Torah text merits a 7-day rabbinic commemoration in honor of the Temple — inference alone is insufficient.

🏛️ Blemished Priests and the Willow Branch Mitzva

Reish Lakish: Priests with physical blemishes (ba'alei mumim) may enter between the Entrance Hall and the Altar to fulfill the willow branch mitzva
↓
Problem: Blemished priests are normally prohibited from this area — what permits the exception here?
↓
Implication of Reish Lakish
The willow mitzva overrides the normal restriction on blemished priests — its fulfillment requires their presence in this space
Rabbi Yoḥanan objects
Who says the mitzva requires circling the altar? Perhaps fulfillment is by standing branches upright around it — no entry needed!

📚 Three Legal Sources for the Willow Branch Mitzva

Halakha from Sinai

  • Authority: Rabbi Yoḥanan citing Rabbi Neḥunya of Beit Ḥortan
  • Referent: Willow branch in the Temple
  • Listed alongside: water libation, ten saplings
  • These three are all הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי

Ordinance of the Prophets

  • Authority: Rabbi Yoḥanan / Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi
  • Referent: Taking the willow branch (outside Temple)
  • Instituted by Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi
  • Prophetic authority — below Torah, above Rabbinic

Rabbinic Enactment

  • Authority: Rabbi Yoḥanan (to Babylonian Sages)
  • Referent: Circling the altar with it
  • 'Yours' — meaning: instituted by the Sages
  • Babylonian practice is later custom, not biblical

📏 Rabbi Ami's Three Rulings on the Willow Branch

RulingThe LawKey Detail
Minimum measure requiredRav Naḥman: three branches of moist leaves; Rav Sheshet: even one leaf and one branchThe Gemara wonders: Rav Sheshet's view seems far too minimal — how can one branch suffice?
Must be taken aloneThe willow mitzva is fulfilled only when taken by itself — not together with the lulavTwo distinct mitzvot require two distinct acts of taking
Bound willow insufficientA willow bound to the lulav does NOT fulfill the willow branch mitzvaLifting the lulav bundle does not count as fulfilling the willow mitzva
Exception: second liftIf he first lifted the lulav, then removed the willow and lifted it separately — this DOES fulfill the obligationThe intentional, separate lift counts as a distinct act of taking

🧑‍🏫 Aivu's Halakhot from Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok

Willow waving — no blessing: A man brought a willow branch before Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok. He took it, waved it, and recited no blessing — teaching that waving is performed but no bracha is said
↓
Sabbatical Year hoeing: A man asked about hoeing olive groves in the Shemitah year. The Gemara debates: the Torah says the land must 'rest and lie fallow' (Exodus 23:11) — hoeing is prohibited; yet a special permit may apply to olive trees
↓
Shabbat eve walking limit: A person should not walk more than three parasangs [פַּרְסָאוֹת] on Shabbat eve — one must reach one's place of Shabbat rest before exceeding this distance
↓
Rav Kahana adds: This applies even when traveling to one's own house — all the more so to an inn, which requires extra preparation time before Shabbat
↓
Mishna resumes: When the first day of Sukkot falls on Shabbat — how is the lulav mitzva performed in the Temple? Attendants arrange lulavim on the bench in the Temple courtyard on erev Shabbat

📋 Sukkah 44 — Summary Map

The Question (Amud A): Why does lulav merit 7-day rabbinic commemoration of the Temple, but the willow branch does not?
↓
Lulav: Explicitly written in the Torah → 7-day commemoration granted
Willow: Only inferred (or rabbinic) → no 7-day commemoration
↓
Deeper Dispute: Rabbi Yoḥanan vs. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi — is the willow branch a prophetic ordinance or a halakha from Sinai?
↓
Resolution: Three distinct referents — Temple willow = Sinai | Taking the willow = Prophets | Circling the altar = Sages
↓
Practical Rulings (Amud B): Rabbi Ami — willow needs a measure, taken alone, bound willow invalid. Aivu — wave without blessing; no 3+ parasangs on Shabbat eve
