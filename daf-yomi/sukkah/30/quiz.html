QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why is a stolen lulav unfit for fulfilling the mitzva of the Four Species?
A. It lacks the required beauty (hadar) as specified in the Torah
B. It is a mitzva haba b'aveira — a commandment fulfilled through the act of transgression
C. The owner's despair renders it ownerless and thus ritually unfit
D. The Torah requires a lulav tied with specific knots only the owner may tie
Correct: B — The Gemara teaches that a stolen lulav is unfit because it is מִצְוָה הַבָּאָה בַּעֲבֵרָה — a commandment fulfilled through transgression (theft), which renders the entire fulfillment void, as derived from Malachi 1:13.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the core dispute between Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Yitzchak about a stolen lulav?
A. Rabbi Ami says it is fit after despair; Rabbi Yitzchak says it remains unfit
B. Rabbi Ami says it is unfit all seven days; Rabbi Yitzchak (citing Shmuel) says only the first day
C. Rabbi Ami says a dry lulav is fit; Rabbi Yitzchak disagrees
D. Rabbi Ami permits borrowing; Rabbi Yitzchak requires outright ownership always
Correct: B — Rabbi Ami holds the stolen lulav is unfit all seven days due to mitzva haba b'aveira. Rabbi Yitzchak bar Naḥmani citing Shmuel holds the halakha applies only to Day 1, when לָכֶם ('yours') is a Torah requirement.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
In Rabbi Shimon ben Yohai's parable, why did the king insist on paying the toll himself?
A. To reward the toll collectors for their diligent service
B. So travelers would not say the king evades his own tolls
C. To demonstrate that the treasury ultimately returned all funds
D. To set an example of generosity for future generations
Correct: B — The king paid lest travelers say he passes through without paying. This illustrates Isaiah 61:8 — God 'hates robbery in a burnt-offering' lest Israel conclude that God uses stolen goods for His own glory.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why did Rav Huna tell merchants to let gentiles cut myrtle branches rather than cutting them personally?
A. Cutting by Jews before Yom Tov would violate a rabbinic prohibition
B. Gentiles have a special right to harvest from untended trees
C. Land cannot be stolen — myrtle on Jewish land remains the owner's even after a gentile takes it, so the gentile must sever it to effect proper acquisition
D. The myrtle must be cut with a specific blade sanctioned by the Sages
Correct: C — Since קַרְקַע אֵינָהּ נִגְזֶלֶת — land cannot be stolen — myrtle attached to Jewish-owned land remains the owner's property even after a gentile takes possession. Having the gentile cut it effects a clean initial severance and acquisition.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Rava explain the Mishnah's inclusion of 'a stolen lulav is unfit' according to his reading?
A. The Mishnah is teaching a new law unique to Day 2 and beyond
B. The Mishnah uses the style of 'it was not necessary' — the real novelty is that a borrowed lulav is valid from Day 2 onward
C. The Mishnah lists stolen and dry together to show they share the same disqualification reason
D. The Mishnah rules that a stolen lulav becomes fit once the owner has despaired
Correct: B — Rava explains using the didactic style of לא נצרכא ('it was not necessary'): It was obvious a stolen lulav is unfit on Day 1. The Mishnah's real novelty is the implication that a borrowed lulav is perfectly valid from Day 2 onward.
