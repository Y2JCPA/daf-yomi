⚖️ When Is a Stolen Lulav Unfit?

Rabbi Ami
A stolen lulav is unfit all seven days of Sukkot — because it is a mitzva haba b'aveira, a commandment fulfilled through the act of transgression.
Rabbi Yitzchak (citing Shmuel)
The stolen lulav is unfit only on the first day, when the Torah requires לָכֶם — 'yours.' From the second day onward, even a borrowed lulav suffices.
Rava
The Mishnah uses the didactic style of לא נצרכא — 'it was not necessary.' Obviously stolen is unfit on day one; the novelty is that a borrowed lulav is valid from day two.
Core Principle מִצְוָה הַבָּאָה בַּעֲבֵרָה — A commandment fulfilled through a transgression is not a fulfillment at all.

🚫 Mitzva Haba B'Aveira — The Core Rule

מַלְאָכִי א:יג — וַהֲבֵאתֶם גָּזוּל וְאֶת-הַפִּסֵּחַ וְאֶת-הַחוֹלֶה
Foundational Principle A mitzva haba b'aveira — a commandment fulfilled by means of a transgression — is entirely void. The verse in Malachi (1:13) condemns bringing stolen animals as offerings: "You have brought that which was stolen, the lame, and the sick." Waving a stolen lulav on Sukkot constitutes no fulfillment of the mitzva. The same principle disqualifies a stolen animal from being offered on the altar.

👑 The King & Toll Collectors — Rabbi Shimon ben Yohai's Parable

Rabbi Yochanan in name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yohai:
"I the Lord love justice, I hate robbery in a burnt-offering"
(Isaiah 61:8)
↓
A king passed through his own toll gate and commanded his servants: "Pay the toll collectors on my behalf."
↓
His servants were puzzled: Does not all the toll belong to the king already? Why should he pay?
↓
The King's Reasoning
Travelers will see me pass without paying and say: The king evades the toll! I must model proper conduct.
The Divine Lesson
God says: Israel will see and conclude that God uses robbery for His own honor. Therefore — I hate robbery in a burnt-offering.

🌿 Comparing Types of Unfit Lulav

Stolen Lulav — גָּזוּל

  • Unfit due to mitzva haba b'aveira
  • Owner's despair does not automatically permit use
  • Unfit on Day 1 per all opinions
  • Rabbi Ami: unfit all 7 days
  • Rabbi Yitzchak: unfit only Day 1

Dry Lulav — יָבֵשׁ

  • Unfit due to lack of הָדָר (beauty/splendor)
  • Requirement derived from Leviticus 23:40
  • Applies to all seven days of Sukkot
  • Both defects cited together by Rabbi Ami
  • No dispute about which day this applies

Borrowed Lulav — שָׁאוּל

  • Valid from Day 2 onward
  • Day 1 requires לָכֶם — 'yours' (Lev. 23:40)
  • Mishnah implies borrowed is fit by contrast with stolen
  • A gift for duration of Sukkot is valid even Day 1
  • Rava uses לא נצרכא to explain Mishnah's structure

🏡 Rav Huna's Ruling on Myrtle from Gentiles

Rav Huna
To merchants selling the Four Species: When you purchase myrtle from gentiles, let the gentiles cut the branches — do not cut them yourselves from the tree.
Questioner
Why? Even if a Jew originally stole the myrtle and sold it to a gentile, wouldn't the owner's despair (yiush) plus the change of possession (shinui reshut) to the Jewish buyer permit it?
Rav Huna (Response)
Land cannot be stolen — קרקע אינה נגזלת. Real property stays with its original owner even after despair. Myrtle on Jewish-owned land remains theirs. The gentile must sever it to effect a proper initial acquisition.
Gemara's Challenge
Even after gentiles cut the myrtle, there is despair in their hands plus change of possession through purchase. And if binding the lulav constitutes a 'physical change,' it is a reversible change — which does not effect acquisition!
Key Legal Concept קַרְקַע אֵינָהּ נִגְזֶלֶת — Land cannot be stolen. Ownership of produce attached to land follows the landowner, not the possessor.
