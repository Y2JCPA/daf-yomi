🏛️ What Disqualifies an Altar?

Rabbis: Four Requirements

  • 🔼 Ramp — the ascent to the altar
  • 📐 Square shape — all four sides equal
  • 🔔 Horns — all four corners
  • 🪨 Base (yesod) — the foundation ledge
  • Missing or damaged = service invalid

Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda Adds

  • ➕ Surrounding ledge (sovev) also required
  • Damage = same as never built
  • Service performed without it = disqualified
  • Stricter standard than the Rabbis
  • Both agree: structural completeness is essential

Rabba bar bar Ḥana citing Rabbi Yoḥanan The shittin (drainpipes beneath the altar) were created during the six days of Creation, as hinted in the verse from Song of Songs. Rabbi Yosei adds: they are hollow and descend to the very depths of the earth. Once every seventy years, young priests descended there to gather the congealed wine accumulated over generations of libations.

🍷 Libations Poured into the Shittin — Consecrated Status?

Ravina
Via verbal analogy (gezeirah shavah) on kedushah: just as libations are poured in sanctity, so must their eventual burning be in sanctity — they retain consecrated status.
Rabbis
Even wine that spills outside the drainpipes retains consecrated status if it collects between the ramp and altar — it was part of the libation act.
Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok
The gap between the ramp and altar was where priests collected congealed wine — this wine had already left the altar's domain of sanctity.
Rav Pappa
Shekhar implies drinking, satiation, and intoxication — all three aspects of libation. The space must be filled completely, saturating the depths below.
Core Question Once wine libations are poured into the shittin, can one derive benefit from them (me'ilah)? The Gemara explores whether consecrated status persists after the act of pouring.
