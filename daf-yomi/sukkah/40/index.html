🌿 When Is an Etrog Tithed?

Rabban Gamliel
An etrog is like a vegetable — tithed at the time of its picking. If picked in Year 3, poor man's tithe applies even if it ripened in Year 2.
R' Yosei (citing Avtolemos)
An etrog follows tree rules for tithing (by flowering year), but vegetable rules for Shemitah (by picking year). The tanna of the mishna adopts this split view.
Key Insight The etrog is unique: it follows tree law for one purpose and vegetable law for another. The mishna's distinction between lulav and etrog relies on Avtolemos's testimony.

🚫 Shemitah Produce — Permitted vs. Forbidden Uses

Permitted ✓

  • Eating as food (לאכלה)
  • Normal human consumption
  • Purchasing food with Shemitah money
  • Laundering (R' Yosei — universal need)

Forbidden ✗

  • Soaking flax or materials
  • Laundering clothes (first tanna)
  • Using as a medical remedy (melugma)
  • Sprinkling wine for pleasant fragrance
  • Anointing for pleasure (not necessity)

Disputed 🔥

  • Firewood — benefit comes after consumption
  • First tanna: forbidden (not 'for food')
  • R' Yosei: permitted (like laundering)
  • Rava: undesignated wood assumed for charcoal → not Shemitah-sanctified

🔥 The Firewood Debate — Benefit vs. Consumption

לאכלה — ולא לרפואה, ולא להדלקה, ולא לכביסה
Tanna'im Dispute Core Question: Is firewood subject to Shemitah sanctity?

The verse says Shemitah produce is "for you for food" (Lev. 25:6) — normally meaning items whose benefit coincides with their consumption.

First Tanna: Firewood is forbidden — heat/light come after the wood is burned (benefit subsequent to consumption). Therefore it fails the 'for food' standard.

Rabbi Yosei: Firewood permitted — just as laundering (benefit simultaneous with use) is allowed, Shemitah wood may be burned. The 'for food' exclusion only removes remedies.

Rava's Resolution: Undesignated wood is presumed destined for charcoal, so its benefit is always subsequent — it is not Shemitah-sanctified at all.

💰 Deconsecrating Shemitah Produce

Rabbi Elazar
Shemitah produce is deconsecrated only by actual purchase. Merely declaring its sanctity transferred to money — without a real transaction — is ineffective. The produce remains forbidden.
Rabbi Yochanan
Purchase does deconsecrate Shemitah produce. The sanctity passes to the purchased item. This parallels second tithe redemption laws, derived from juxtaposing Jubilee (Lev. 25:13) to the verse on selling.
Scriptural Source Both derive from the juxtaposition: 'In this Jubilee year you shall return every man to his possession' (Lev. 25:13) adjacent to 'If you sell to your neighbor' (Lev. 25:14) — but each applies the analogy differently.

🔄 The Chain of Shemitah Sanctity Transfers

Step 1: Original Shemitah produce (e.g., fruit) carries kedushat shevi'it from the outset
↓
Step 2: One purchases meat with Shemitah produce → Both the produce and the meat now carry Shemitah sanctity simultaneously
↓
Step 3: One then purchases fish with that meat → The fish acquires sanctity; the meat loses its sanctity
↓
Constant Rule: The original Shemitah produce always retains its own sanctity — it is never released from kedushat shevi'it
↓
Bi'ur obligation: Must remove Shemitah items (produce + sanctified purchases) once that species is no longer available in the field
