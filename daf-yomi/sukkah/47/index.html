🍂 Shemini Atzeret: Extension of Sukkot or Independent Festival?

View A
The 8th day has the same status as the 7th day of Sukkot — we recite the blessing 'to reside in the sukka' and continue its observance.
View B
The 8th day is a festival entirely unto itself — we do NOT recite the sukka blessing; sitting in it is mere custom, not obligation.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
We DO recite Shehecheyanu on the 8th day — it is a distinct festival separate from the seven days of Sukkot, requiring its own blessing of time.
Rav Sheshet
We do NOT recite Shehecheyanu on the 8th day — once recited on the first Festival day, no further blessing of time is needed on subsequent days.
Core Tension Is שמיני עצרת (Shemini Atzeret) a continuation of Sukkot or an entirely independent festival with its own obligations and blessings? The answer determines whether one recites לישב בסוכה and שהחיינו.

🏕️ The Sukka Blessing Dispute on the 8th Day

Tanna Kamma
8th day = 7th day status: Recite 'to reside in the sukka' on the 8th day — it is still part of Sukkot's observance.
Opposing View
8th day = independent festival: No sukka blessing is recited — yet we sit in the sukka out of doubt, without a blessing.
Gemara (Alternative Version)
Revised dispute: Everyone agrees no sukka blessing is recited. The argument is only about whether one is obligated to sit in the sukka at all on the 8th day.
Gemara (Suggestion)
Possible harmony: Perhaps all agree no blessing is said — once one blesses on the first Festival day, no further sukka blessing is required on any subsequent day.
The Dilemma Blessing the sukka on the 8th day implies it's still Sukkot; omitting the blessing implies it's a new festival. Sitting without a blessing threads this ambiguity.

📜 Five Amoraic Proofs: The 8th Day Is Its Own Festival

Rabbi Levi bar Ḥama

  • Three distinctions from the 7 days:
  • 🌿 Lulav — not taken on 8th day
  • 🏕️ Sukka — no obligation to sit
  • 🔥 Sacrifices — entirely different offerings
  • → Therefore its own Shehecheyanu

Rav Pappa

  • Unique sacrificial language:
  • 7 days: 'offerings made by fire'
  • 8th day: 'a burnt-offering, an offering made by fire'
  • Distinct Torah phrasing = distinct festival

Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak

  • Distinct verse (Numbers 29:35):
  • 'On the 8th day — a solemn assembly'
  • Other days: 'And on the Nth day…'
  • עֲצֶרֶת marks total independence

Rav Ashi

  • Meal-offering formula differs:
  • 8th day: 'as per the regulation' (כְּמִשְׁפָּט)
  • Other days: exact numbers specified
  • Generic formula signals a standalone day

Ravina

  • Distinct even from the day before it:
  • 8th day ≠ 7th day of Sukkot
  • Contrast: 7th of Passover = 6th of Passover
  • Passover's 7th has no new distinctions → no separate blessing

⏰ Should We Recite Shehecheyanu on Shemini Atzeret?

Shemini Atzeret arrives
שמיני עצרת — the 8th day of Sukkot
↓
Rabbi Yoḥanan / Rav Naḥman: YES
8th day is a distinct festival — recite שהחיינו (Shehecheyanu)
Rav Sheshet: NO
8th day is linked to Sukkot — first-day Shehecheyanu covers all days including the 8th
↓
Baraita cited: The 8th day requires distinct mention in blessings (Birkat Hamazon, Amida) — suggesting independent status
↓
Gemara qualifies: The baraita's 'blessing' refers to Grace after Meals and Amida, not necessarily Shehecheyanu — so baraita is not decisive proof
↓
Final ruling: Rav Naḥman's position is upheld — recite Shehecheyanu on Shemini Atzeret. A baraita was taught in agreement with him.

⚖️ Why the 7th Day of Passover Needs No Shehecheyanu

Challenge: If the 8th day of Sukkot gets Shehecheyanu for being 'distinct,' why doesn't the 7th day of Passover? It's distinct in matza — the Master said matza is only obligatory on the first night of Passover!
↓
Ravina's answer: The 8th day of Sukkot is distinct even from the very day immediately before it — the 7th day. Different laws: no lulav, no sukka, different sacrifices. This is a sharp break.
↓
By contrast: The 7th day of Passover is not distinct from the 6th day of Passover — both share identical laws and obligations. No discontinuity, no new blessing.
↓
Rav Pappa adds: The 8th day uses unique Torah language for its sacrificial offerings. The 7th day of Passover uses the same formula as all other Passover days — no literary distinction in the Torah.
↓
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak: The 8th day is called עֲצֶרֶת (solemn assembly) — a designation of complete independence. The 7th day of Passover receives no such special designation in the Torah.
↓
Conclusion: The 8th day of Sukkot is uniquely distinct — both from the entire festival of Sukkot AND from the 7th day specifically — justifying its own Shehecheyanu blessing.
