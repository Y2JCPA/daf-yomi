☀️🌙 Eclipse Omens: Sun vs. Moon

Solar Eclipse

  • Bad omen for the entire world
  • Nations calculate by the sun
  • Red sun → war is coming
  • Black sun → famine is coming
  • Direction of eclipse signals where trouble originates

Lunar Eclipse

  • Bad omen for the Jewish people
  • Israel calculates its calendar by the moon
  • Eclipse strikes at the very basis of their reckoning
  • A sign of divine rebuke specifically for Israel
  • When Israel does God's will — they need not fear

🫙 Rava's Rules: Vessels in the Sukka

Stays in the Sukka

  • Drinking cups — usually clean
  • Salt shaker
  • Items that stay clean between uses
  • Vessels not likely to generate odors or mess

Removed After Use

  • Eating vessels — taken out after meals
  • Earthenware jug used for drawing water
  • Wicker basket (shaḥil) for liquids
  • Anything that becomes soiled or malodorous

🌧️ Wind-Blown Splinters — Must We Stay or May We Leave?

Rav Yosef
The wind has blown splinters from the roofing onto my food. Vacate my vessels from here — I will eat inside the house!
Abaye
But didn't we learn that one is not burdened to return to the sukka immediately? You already have grounds to remain inside.
Rav Yosef
That leniency applies when rain chased one out. Here splinters rendered the sukka itself temporarily unfit — different logic applies.
Halakha One who exits the sukka due to rain need not immediately return once rain stops — he may finish his meal first. The same rule applies if he fell asleep outside: he need not return until he awakens.

🌿 New Mishna: The Disqualified Lulav

לולב הגזול והיבש — פסול | "וּלְקַחְתֶּם לָכֶם" — משלכם
Mishna (Daf 29b) A stolen lulav or a completely dry lulav is unfit for the mitzva of the Four Species. A lulav from a tree worshipped as idolatry (asheira) or from a city incited to idolatry is also unfit — for items condemned to burning cannot fulfill a mitzva. Gemara's question: Is a stolen lulav unfit even on the first day of Sukkot, or only thereafter? Answer: The Mishna rules unequivocally — even on the first day. The Torah says "for yourselves" (Leviticus 23:40), meaning it must actually be yours. A stolen lulav belongs to another person, not to you.
