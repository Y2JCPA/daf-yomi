🌾 What Does 'From Your Threshing Floor' Mean?

Gemara
Perhaps the verse allows items placed on the threshing floor — stalks with grain attached, or vines with grapes still on them — as valid skhakh?
Rabbi Yirmeya
Why not roof a sukka with wine?! Say it refers to congealed wine from Senir (Mt. Hermon) — solid like a cake of figs. The verse is far too ambiguous to derive clear limits.
Rav Ashi
'From your threshing floor' = an item that comes from the threshing floor (straw, grape stems after processing), but not the grain/grapes themselves. Liquids like wine are excluded entirely.
Rav Ashi's Resolution Use products derived from the threshing floor/winepress — but not the raw grain, the fruit, or any liquid. The verse draws a distinction between the produce and its agricultural byproducts.

📦 Tied Bundles vs. Untied Materials (Mishna)

❌ Tied Bundles — Unfit

  • Bundles of straw tied with rope
  • Bundles of wood
  • Bundles of twigs
  • Unfit in their bundled form
  • Rabbinic decree: looks like a storehouse
  • May also violate 'prepare it, not from what's prepared'

✓ Untied — Fit

  • The same straw, wood, or twigs after untying
  • Valid skhakh — material itself is kosher vegetation
  • Problem was the bundle form, not the substance
  • Untying removes both the storehouse concern and any 'prepared' issue
  • Simply spread loose atop the sukka frame
  • The mishna explicitly teaches: untying renders them fit

🏚️ Why Are Bundles Prohibited? Two Reasons, One Unknown Match

Rabbi Ya'akov
Rabbi Yoḥanan gave me two reasons for two similar sukka halakhot — but I cannot remember which reason applies to which case!
Rabbi Yirmeya
We can figure it out! Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba cited Rabbi Yoḥanan: bundles are prohibited as a decree of the storehouse — lest one use his grain storehouse as a sukka without building one properly.
Rav Ashi
This is difficult! Surely bundles also violate the principle תעשה ולא מן העשוי — 'prepare it, not from what is already prepared.' Why only cite the storehouse decree?
Gemara
Rabbi Yoḥanan can answer: the language of the mishnayot differs. 'One may not roof' (bundles) = rabbinic prohibition only. 'It is not a sukka' (grain stack) = invalid even by Torah law. Each reason fits its case.
Two Distinct Reasons 1. Decree of the storehouse — rabbinic; the sukka is valid by Torah law but prohibited
2. 'Prepare it, not from what's prepared' — biblical invalidity; using a grain stack untouched as skhakh

🕍 Rav Ḥisda's Proof from Nehemiah 8:15

צְאוּ הָהָר וְהָבִיאוּ עֲלֵי זַיִת וַעֲלֵי עֵץ שֶׁמֶן וַעֲלֵי הֲדַס וַעֲלֵי תְמָרִים וַעֲלֵי עֵץ עָבֹת לַעֲשֹׂת סֻכֹּת
נְחֶמְיָה ח:טו "Go forth to the mount and fetch olive branches, and branches of wild olive, and myrtle branches, and palm branches, and boughs of a dense-leaved tree to make sukkot"

Rav Ḥisda derives that valid skhakh must be natural, detached vegetation — just as these branches are. The verse also lists both myrtle branches (הֲדַס) and boughs of a dense-leaved tree (עֵץ עָבֹת). Aren't those the same species?

Rav Ḥisda answers: 'Myrtle branches' = a wild myrtle whose leaves don't cover the stem — invalid for the Four Species but valid for skhakh. 'Dense-leaved tree' = a proper hadas with leaves covering the stem — valid for both. Both may be used as skhakh.
