QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Ashi, what does 'from your threshing floor' (Deut 16:13) teach about valid skhakh?
A. Items physically placed on the threshing floor, including grain stalks, may be used
B. Only grain straw is valid; all other vegetation is excluded
C. Items that come from the threshing floor process (e.g., straw), but not grain itself or liquids
D. Wine is valid skhakh if it has congealed into a solid form
Correct: C — Rav Ashi distinguishes 'from' (מן) the threshing floor — meaning products derived after threshing — from items directly 'on' the floor (the grain itself). Congealed wine is excluded because the verse says 'from your winepress,' not the wine itself.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yoḥanan (as cited by Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba), why did the Sages prohibit tied bundles of straw as skhakh?
A. Because tied bundles are susceptible to ritual impurity
B. As a decree lest one use a grain storehouse as a sukka without building one properly
C. Because tying violates the biblical principle of 'prepare it, not from what's prepared'
D. Because bundles create too much shade, disqualifying the sukka
Correct: B — This is a rabbinic decree of the storehouse (גזירת אוצר): if bundles were permitted as skhakh, people might designate their existing grain storehouse as a sukka, which would not constitute a proper sukka construction.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the legal status of concave arrow shafts (with a hollow socket) as skhakh, and why?
A. Fit, because all wood is valid skhakh material
B. Fit, but only after they are untied from bundles
C. Unfit, because concave wooden receptacles are susceptible to ritual impurity
D. Unfit, under the rabbinic decree of the storehouse
Correct: C — Concave arrow shafts form a hollow receptacle. Wooden receptacles are susceptible to ritual impurity, and materials susceptible to impurity are disqualified from use as skhakh. Convex (solid) arrow shafts are fit since they are not receptacles.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does Nehemiah 8:15 list both 'myrtle branches' (הֲדַס) and 'boughs of a dense-leaved tree' (עֵץ עָבֹת)? What does Rav Ḥisda say?
A. They are entirely different species used together for the Four Species
B. 'Myrtle branches' refers to a wild myrtle (invalid for Four Species) and 'dense-leaved tree' refers to a proper hadas; both are valid for skhakh
C. The verse uses double language to emphasize that only hadas may be used as skhakh
D. The two terms refer to the same species — the repetition is stylistic
Correct: B — Rav Ḥisda explains that 'myrtle branches' means a wild myrtle whose leaves do not fully cover the stem — it fails the standard for the Four Species (hadas) but is still valid vegetation for skhakh. 'Dense-leaved tree' refers to a proper hadas. Both may be used as skhakh.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rabba bar bar Ḥana in Rabbi Yoḥanan's name rules that bundles of combed flax are unfit for skhakh. What if the same flax is spread out loosely?
A. Still unfit — flax can never be used as skhakh under any form
B. Still unfit — flax falls under the storehouse decree regardless of form
C. Fit — once spread loosely it is like any other valid plant material, no longer susceptible to impurity
D. Fit — but only if it was never combed in the first place
Correct: C — Combed flax in bundle form is susceptible to ritual impurity (as raw material for spinning), making it unfit. Once spread loose, it loses its status as a prepared bundle or susceptible item and becomes valid skhakh like any other detached vegetation.
