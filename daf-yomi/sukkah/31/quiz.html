Sukkah 31 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Naḥman, what specific scenario is the subject of the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis regarding a stolen sukka?
A. One who builds a sukka entirely from stolen lumber
B. One who forcibly evicts another person from their sukka and takes their place
C. One who builds a sukka on public land without permission
D. One who rents a sukka but refuses to pay
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman narrows the dispute: it concerns one who assaults another and forcibly evicts them from their sukka. In this case Rabbi Eliezer rules it unfit (mitzva through transgression) while the Rabbis say fit (land cannot be stolen; the robber is like a borrower). Stolen wood, by contrast, creates a valid sukka — all agree.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
The Gemara proves Rav Naḥman's interpretation by a textual argument. What is that argument?
A. The baraita uses the word 'robbery,' which applies only to persons, not objects
B. The stolen sukka is juxtaposed with a sukka in the public domain, showing both involve land-based wrongdoing
C. Rabbi Eliezer explicitly says 'one who takes another's place' in the baraita
D. The Tosefta distinguishes between wood and a complete structure
Correct: B — The Gemara argues from juxtaposition: the stolen sukka is placed next to the sukka in the public domain. Just as the public-domain sukka involves usurping land (not stealing materials), so too the stolen sukka refers to seizing another's place — not to building with stolen wood.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
When is the owner of a stolen beam entitled to receive it back intact rather than just monetary compensation?
A. Immediately upon making a formal claim to the court
B. Only if the beam was not yet attached with cement
C. After the seven days of the Sukkot Festival have ended
D. Never — the Rabbinic ordinance permanently transfers ownership
Correct: C — During the Festival, the Sages instituted an ordinance (based on the general 'beam ordinance') that the robber need not dismantle the sukka — the owner receives monetary value. After the Festival ends, the beam must be returned intact. Exception: if attached with cement, the owner receives money even post-Festival.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rabbi Yehuda interpret the word hadar in the verse 'the fruit of a beautiful (hadar) tree' (Lev. 23:40)?
A. The fruit must be golden-yellow and unblemished
B. The fruit must be of a majestic, impressive size
C. The fruit dwells (hadar) on its tree from year to year without withering
D. The tree itself must be aesthetically pleasing and symmetrical
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehuda derives hadar from the root meaning 'to dwell' (dirah): a fruit that remains on its tree year-round without falling or withering at season's end, as the etrog does. This is a botanical characteristic, not an aesthetic beauty standard — so Rabbi Yehuda requires no beauty for lulav or etrog.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rav Huna teaches about a lulav whose top was affected. Under what condition does he rule it is still fit?
A. When the top was severed but then reattached with binding
B. When the top split apart but was not completely severed from the lulav
C. When the top was bent downward but remained fully intact
D. When the top dried out but retained its original shape
Correct: B — Rav Huna rules: the lulav is unfit only when its top was completely severed. If the top merely split (cracked apart) but the pieces remain attached to the lulav, it is still fit for use in fulfilling the mitzva.
