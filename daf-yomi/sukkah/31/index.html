🏠 Stolen Sukka: May One Fulfill the Mitzva?

Rabbi Eliezer
A stolen sukka is unfit. A mitzva that comes through transgression (mitzva haba'ah b'aveira) cannot be fulfilled — the robber may not use it.
Rabbis
A person may fulfill their obligation in another person's sukka. Since land cannot be stolen, the robber is merely an unauthorized borrower — the sukka is valid.
Rav Naḥman
The dispute is specifically about forcible eviction from another's sukka. If one steals wood and builds — everyone agrees the sukka is valid, since the wood legally belongs to the robber by Rabbinic ordinance.
Key Distinction Stealing a sukka (land) vs. stealing wood to build one: Rabbi Eliezer disqualifies the evicted sukka; the Rabbis hold land cannot be stolen. Stolen building materials create a valid sukka once incorporated.

🪵 The Stolen Beam — What Must Be Returned?

One steals a beam and incorporates it into a sukka's roof
↓
⏳ During the 7 Festival Days
Robber need not dismantle the sukka — Rabbinic ordinance: owner receives monetary value only
📅 After the Festival Ends
Beam must be returned intact to its owner; the ordinance no longer protects the robber
↓
Exception: if beam was attached with cement — owner receives only monetary value even after the Festival

🌿 Dry Lulav: Must It Be Beautiful?

Rabbis
A dry lulav is unfit. We compare lulav to etrog via Biblical juxtaposition — just as an etrog requires hadar (beauty), so does the lulav. Dry = not beautiful.
Rabbi Yehuda
A dry lulav is fit. I do not derive a beauty requirement for the lulav from the etrog. Each species stands on its own legal footing.
Rava
This dispute is only about the lulav. But regarding the etrog, everyone requires beauty — even Rabbi Yehuda agrees hadar applies there.
Gemara
Refutation! An old etrog — Rabbis: unfit; Rabbi Yehuda: fit. So Rabbi Yehuda does NOT require beauty even in the etrog — Rava's position is conclusively rejected.
Resolution Rabbi Yehuda interprets hadar (Lev. 23:40) as 'a fruit that dwells (hadar) on its tree from year to year' — a botanical fact about the etrog, not a beauty requirement at all.

⚖️ The Exilarch's Stolen Sukka

סוכה גזולה — תקנת חכמים: שמין אותו ונותן דמים
Incident An old woman appeared before Rav Naḥman crying: 'The Exilarch and all the Sages of his household are sitting in a stolen sukka!' She claimed the Exilarch's servants had stolen her wood to build his sukka. Rav Naḥman dismissed her complaints. Ravina later clarified: by the Rabbinic ordinance (instituted to prevent disruption of the Festival), the stolen beam belongs to the robber — the owner receives only monetary compensation. The sukka stands.

🍋 Etrog Measures & Rulings: Rabbi Meir vs. Rabbi Yehuda

Rabbi Meir

  • Minimum size: walnut-bulk
  • Leek-green etrog: fit
  • Large etrog: fit if two fit in one hand
  • Emphasizes flexibility in size standards

Rabbi Yehuda

  • Minimum size: egg-bulk
  • Leek-green etrog: unfit (not ripe-looking)
  • Large etrog: must hold two in two hands
  • Hadar = dwells on tree year-round, not beauty

Rabbi Yosei (on large etrog)

  • Fit even if one etrog fills two hands
  • Most lenient position on maximum size
  • Holds the practical handhold test as determinative
  • Disagrees with Rabbi Yehuda's two-etrog standard
