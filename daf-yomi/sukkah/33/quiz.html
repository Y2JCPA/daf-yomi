QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Ḥisda, if most leaves on a myrtle branch dried out but three moist branches remain, when is the branch fit?
A. When the moist leaves are anywhere on the branch
B. When the moist leaves are at the top of each of the three branches
C. When the moist leaves are at the bottom of each branch
D. Only when all three moist branches are grouped on the same side
Correct: B — Rav Ḥisda rules that the moist leaves must be specifically at the top of each of the three branches, since the branch must retain its dense-leaved appearance at the tip to be considered a valid hadas.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What halakhic precedent does Rav Pappa cite to argue there is no permanent disqualification with mitzvot?
A. A lulav whose severed top later regrew
B. Wind covering then uncovering sacrificial blood, requiring re-covering
C. A stolen lulav that was returned before Sukkot
D. An asheira tree that was uprooted and replanted
Correct: B — Rav Pappa derives the principle from the blood-covering case: wind that covers blood exempts the owner, but if the wind then uncovers it, he must cover it again — showing that a prior exemption does not permanently discharge the obligation.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
From where does Rabbi Yehuda derive that lulav requires binding by Torah law?
A. A verse in Leviticus explicitly mentioning binding the four species
B. A gezerah shavah linking 'taking' by lulav to 'taking' by the Paschal lamb
C. The principle that all four species must form a single unit to be valid
D. A verse in Nehemiah describing how Ezra bound the species together
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda employs a gezerah shavah: the word 'taking' (לְקִיחָה) appears both in the lulav verse and in the context of the Paschal lamb. Just as the Paschal lamb required binding its bundle, so too the lulav requires binding.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does Rav Ashi permit picking myrtle berries on the Festival to render the branch fit for the mitzva?
A. Because berries are ownerless produce and may always be taken
B. Because the lulav was already designated before the Festival began
C. Because he picks the berries for eating — fixing the branch is an unintentional result, permitted by Rabbi Shimon's principle
D. Because Beit Hillel permits all acts of mitzva preparation on the Festival
Correct: C — Rav Ashi applies Rabbi Shimon's principle that an unintentional act is permitted. Since the person's intent is to eat the berries (a permitted act), the incidental consequence of rendering the branch fit is not a prohibited act of 'preparing a vessel.'
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What are the two identifying features of the kosher aravah (willow) given in the baraita on 33b?
A. It must have red berries and smooth-edged leaves
B. It grows by a brook, OR its leaf is elongated like a brook
C. It must grow in Israel and have a bitter taste
D. Its bark must be white and its leaves must be broad
Correct: B — The baraita interprets 'aravei nachal' in two ways: (1) literally — willows that grow by the brook, and (2) as a description — a tree whose leaf is elongated like a brook (long and narrow). Either feature identifies the correct species.
