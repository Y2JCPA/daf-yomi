🤲 Does a Cloth Interpose When Holding the Lulav?

Rabba
One must NOT take the lulav with a cloth (sudara) around the hand. The Torah says u'lekahtem — a complete, direct taking. A cloth interposes and the mitzva is unfulfilled.
Rava
Taking through another object IS valid! We derive from the red heifer: hyssop tied to a string — the string is legally part of the hyssop. So too, holding via cloth counts as taking.
Rabba (rebuttal)
That proof fails. The string was tied to the hyssop and became like it. But a cloth is merely wrapped around the hand — it never becomes part of the lulav. The interposition stands.
Rava (reply)
Regardless, an addition whose purpose is to beautify the mitzva does not interpose. A decorative covering enhances the mitzva and cannot disqualify the taking.
⚖️ Practical Ruling The halacha follows that a cloth around the hand interposes and invalidates taking. However, decorative bindings on the lulav itself do not interpose since they serve to beautify the mitzva.

👃 Smelling the Myrtle vs. the Etrog During the Mitzva

🌿 Myrtle (Hadas)

  • Smelling during mitzva: PROHIBITED
  • Myrtle has no food use — it is naturally designated for its fragrance
  • Taking it for mitzva already 'dedicates' it to that smell-function
  • Smelling it uses it for its primary, natural purpose without extra basis
  • When attached to tree on Shabbat: PERMITTED
  • Attached myrtle is not muktzeh; its scent may be enjoyed freely

🍋 Etrog (Citron)

  • Smelling during mitzva: PERMITTED
  • Etrog is primarily designated for eating, not smell
  • The mitzva does not exhaust its smell — that is incidental
  • Smelling is an unintended bonus, not its core designation
  • When attached to tree on Shabbat: PROHIBITED
  • Rabbinically forbidden — smelling attached fruit may lead to picking (uvdin d'chol)

🔗 Laws of Binding and Handling the Lulav

Basic binding rule (Rabbi Meir): One may bind the lulav with any cord — it need not be tied with its own species.
↓
Jerusalem custom: Prominent residents bound their lulavim with gold rings — this is hiddur mitzva (beautification), not a violation.
↓
Rabba — leave a handgrip: When binding the lulav for the Exilarch, leave a bare section with neither binding nor decoration so the hand can grip directly.
↓
Rava disagrees: Unnecessary — decorative additions whose purpose is to beautify the mitzva do not constitute an interposition (ḥatzitza).
↓
Rabba — do not insert lulav into the binding: After tying hadas and aravah, inserting the lulav risks detaching leaves that would interpose between species.
↓
Rabba — do not trim while bound: Cutting the lulav to shorten it while inside the binding may detach leaves, which would interpose between the three species.
