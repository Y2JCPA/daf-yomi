QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Eliezer, how many meals must one eat in the sukka during the seven days of Sukkot, and how is this derived?
A. Seven meals — one per day, from 'seven days shall you dwell in sukkot'
B. Fourteen meals — one by day and one by night each day, from 'In sukkot shall you reside' compared to permanent residence
C. As many meals as one normally eats at home, with no fixed number
D. At least three meals, like Shabbat, derived by analogy
Correct: B — Rabbi Eliezer derives from 'In sukkot shall you reside' that one must eat one meal by day and one by night for each of the seven days, totaling fourteen meals — paralleling the regularity of meals in a permanent home.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How do the Rabbis explain why there is an obligation to eat in the sukka on the first night, if their general principle is that the sukka resembles a permanent home with no fixed meal requirement?
A. It is a rabbinic ordinance with no biblical basis
B. A verbal analogy (gezerah shavah): 'fifteenth day' links Sukkot to Passover, making the first night like the first night of Passover which requires matza
C. The first night is Yom Tov and therefore carries its own special biblical stringency
D. It is derived from 'all homeborn in Israel shall dwell in sukkot' which emphasizes the beginning
Correct: B — Rabbi Yoḥanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak teaches a gezerah shavah: the phrase 'fifteenth day' appears in both Sukkot (Lev. 23:34) and Passover. Just as Passover first night requires eating matza ('in the evening you shall eat matzot'), the first night of Sukkot requires eating in the sukka.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the fundamental dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis regarding using another person's sukka?
A. Both agree one may use another's sukka as long as permission is granted
B. Rabbi Eliezer: cannot fulfill the obligation in another's sukka; Rabbis: may use another's sukka, with only a stolen sukka being invalid
C. Rabbi Eliezer: may use another's sukka on Chol HaMoed only; Rabbis: prohibited always
D. Both agree one cannot use another's sukka, deriving this from 'for yourself'
Correct: B — Rabbi Eliezer extends 'for yourself' to require ownership of the sukka, parallel to lulav on the first day. The Rabbis use 'for yourself' only to exclude stolen sukkot, while 'all homeborn in Israel shall dwell in sukkot' implies any valid sukka fulfills the obligation.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
When the steward of King Agrippas asked Rabbi Eliezer whether he — accustomed to one meal a day — could fulfill his sukka obligation with one meal, what did Rabbi Eliezer rule?
A. Yes — personal eating habits determine one's obligation in the sukka
B. Yes — but only on weekdays; on Yom Tov two meals are required
C. No — he must add an extra meal; personal custom does not reduce the halakhic requirement
D. No ruling was given; Rabbi Eliezer deflected the question
Correct: C — Rabbi Eliezer ruled that the steward must add an extra meal for the sukka. Personal eating habits cannot reduce the halakhic obligation — the requirement of day and night meals applies regardless of one's normal customs.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
When Rabbi Elai visited Rabbi Eliezer on the first day of Sukkot, how does the Gemara reconcile Rabbi Eliezer's apparent rebuke with Rabbi Yitzḥak's teaching?
A. The Gemara concludes Rabbi Eliezer was simply wrong and Rabbi Yitzḥak's view is authoritative
B. Rabbi Eliezer's praise of staying home was only for Torah scholars; Rabbi Yitzḥak's ruling applies to everyone else
C. Rabbi Eliezer's rebuke applied only to those without a pressing reason; Rabbi Yitzḥak teaches that greeting one's teacher is itself an obligatory reason to travel on the Festival
D. The two teachings apply to different festivals — Rabbi Eliezer spoke of Sukkot, Rabbi Yitzḥak of Passover
Correct: C — Rabbi Yitzḥak derives from the Shunamite woman's husband's question — 'Why are you going to him today? It is neither New Moon nor Shabbat' — that visiting a prophet/teacher on Shabbat and Yom Tov is obligatory. Thus Rabbi Elai's visit was itself a mitzvah, resolving the tension with Rabbi Eliezer's general praise of staying home.
