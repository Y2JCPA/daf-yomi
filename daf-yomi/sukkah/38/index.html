🌿 The Lulav-Meal Dilemma

Mishna: A traveler who forgot his lulav must interrupt his meal and take it at his table
↓
Apparent contradiction: When Shema time arrives during a meal, one does not interrupt — why is lulav different?
↓
Rava: Lulav = Torah law → must interrupt; Shema in that context = Rabbinic → need not interrupt
↓
Rav Safra: If there is no later opportunity that day → interrupt; if time remains → finish meal first
↓
Rabbi Zeira: Interrupting is a mitzva, but if he did not, taking it in the afternoon is also valid
3 / 9

⚔️ Three Sages Resolve the Same Contradiction

Questioner
The mishna requires interrupting a meal for lulav — but elsewhere we rule NOT to interrupt a meal for Shema recitation. Contradiction!
Rava
No difficulty. Lulav is a Torah-law mitzva — one MUST interrupt. The Shema case may be Rabbinic — no need to interrupt a meal for a Rabbinic obligation.
Rav Safra
No difficulty. Interrupt only when there is NO later opportunity that day. Where time still remains to take the lulav, one may finish eating first.
Rabbi Zeira
No difficulty. The mishna teaches it is a mitzva to interrupt — but if he did not, he takes it in the afternoon. The mishna describes ideal behavior, not absolute obligation.
Halakhic Method Three Amoraim resolve the same contradiction via three different distinctions: (1) Torah vs. Rabbinic law, (2) availability of future opportunity, (3) mitzva-level vs. absolute requirement.

🎵 Hallel: Who Leads and How Does One Respond?

Adult Male Leads Hallel

  • Congregation responds: Halleluya after opening
  • Leader: 'Give praise, servants of God' → Congregation: Halleluya
  • Leader: 'Lord, please save us' → Congregation repeats the same
  • Congregation fulfills obligation through active response
  • May repeat a verse (e.g., 'Lord, please grant success') twice

Slave / Woman / Minor Leads

  • Adult male repeats word for word after them
  • This signals the listener's low Torah knowledge
  • Sages: 'May a curse come upon him' who needs this
  • Still fulfills obligation through word-for-word repetition
  • Reflects a real but humiliating situation the Sages acknowledged

👂 Shome'a K'oneh — The Hearing Principle

שׁוֹמֵעַ כְּעוֹנֶה — One who hears is as one who responds/recites
Core Halakhic Rule Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi said in the name of Bar Kappara: One who hears a passage recited holds the same halakhic status as one who recites it himself.

Source: When King Josiah heard the Torah scroll read by Shaphan the scribe, it is written that Josiah 'heard' — and the verse praises him for humbling himself upon hearing. He did not re-read it himself; hearing alone was sufficient.

Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov: The verse explicitly credits Josiah's hearing as the act of fulfillment — so we cannot say he read it again afterward.
