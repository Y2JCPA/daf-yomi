🏛️ Debating the Third Wall

Shmuel b. Levi
Position the third wall — at least 1 handbreadth wide — at the end of one of the standing walls, like a diagonal line closing a narrowing field
R. Yehoshua ben Levi
Use an expansive handbreadth (tefach shovet) — measured with fingers spread apart, slightly larger — to establish the third side
Rav Yehuda
An alleyway sukka (two parallel walls) is fit — position the handbreadth wall adjacent to either standing wall in whichever direction you choose
Rava
The one-handbreadth wall is valid only if it has the form of a doorway — split it in two and place each half flanking an open space
Rav Ashi observes Rav Kahana Rav Kahana used both the expansive handbreadth AND the form of a doorway — adopting the strictest combined approach. Rav Ashi questioned whether both were truly necessary.

🔀 Two Sukka Configurations Compared

Adjacent Walls (L-shape / ל)

  • Two walls meet at a corner
  • Third wall: expansive handbreadth may suffice
  • Rava: still requires form of a doorway
  • Corner provides structural anchor
  • More common; relatively more lenient

Parallel Walls (Alleyway / מבוי)

  • Two walls are parallel, open on two ends
  • One handbreadth alone is insufficient
  • Requires a board WITH form of a doorway
  • No corner — harder to render valid
  • Stricter standard; less common configuration

🏠 Abaye's 8 Tannaim — All Require Dirat Keva (Permanent Dwelling)

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi — Sukka lacking 4×4 cubits area is unfit; a permanent home has a minimum usable area
↓
Rabbi Yoshiya — Walls must block sunlight like permanent walls; derived from vesakkota (Exodus 40:3) — a partition, not merely a cover
↓
Rabbi Yehuda — Permits a sukka taller than 20 cubits, reflecting a permanent, tall structure perspective
↓
Rabbi Shimon — Requires three full standard walls plus a fourth of one handbreadth (stricter than Tanna Kamma)
↓
Rabban Gamliel — A sukka atop a wagon or boat is unfit; a permanent residence cannot be mobile
↓
Beit Shammai — If one's head and body are inside but the table is in the house, the sukka is unfit; too cramped to be a dwelling
↓
Rabbi Eliezer — A sloped lean-to sukka (walls and roof merged) is unfit; distinct roof and walls are required for a true dwelling
↓
Aḥerim — A circular sukka like a dovecote is unfit; a permanent residence requires corners

⭕ Is a Round Sukka Valid? Rabbi Yochanan's Ruling

Round sukka — shaped like a furnace or kiln (no corners)
↓
Measure the total circumference
↓
Circumference ≥ 24 cubits
Valid — fits 24 seated people (1 cubit each); inner area satisfies Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's 4×4 cubit minimum
Circumference < 24 cubits
Invalid — insufficient area; fails Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's standard of a habitable dwelling space
↓
Gemara tension: 24-cubit circumference circle has area ≈ 45.8 sq. cubits, but the largest inscribed square ≈ 11.5 sq. cubits — less than the required 16. The Gemara leaves this calculation as a pending difficulty (תיקו).
