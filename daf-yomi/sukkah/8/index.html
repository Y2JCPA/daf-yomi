📐 Two Geometric Relationships at the Heart of Daf 8

Circle Inscribed in Square

  • Circle sits inside the square
  • Circumference = ¾ of square's perimeter
  • Example: perimeter 16 → circumference 12
  • Follows from π ≈ 3 (diameter = side of square)

Square Inscribed in Circle

  • Square sits inside the circle
  • Circumference = 1½ × square's perimeter
  • Diagonal of square = 1⅖ × side (≈ √2)
  • A 4×4 square requires ~16.8-cubit circle

📐 How Large Must a Circular Sukka Be? The Geometric Chain

Starting premise: A circular sukka must hold 24 seated people, each occupying 1 cubit
↓
24 people × 1 cubit = 24-cubit outer circumference
But how does this give us the sukka's interior size?
↓
Inner Circle Method (Rav Asi)
Outer ring = 24 cubits → men sit outside sukka → inner circle = 18 cubits → diameter = 6 cubits
Sages of Caesarea
Circle inscribed in square = ¾ perimeter → circle circumscribed by square = 1½ perimeter → work backwards from required square
↓
Conclusion: Inner circle diameter = 6 cubits → inscribed square side = 6 ÷ 1⅖ ≈ 4 cubits ✓
The minimum 4×4 sukka is confirmed!

🪑 Debating the 24-Cubit Circumference of Rabbi Yoḥanan

Mar Keshisha
A man sits and occupies 1 cubit — so 24 people need 24 cubits of circumference. But that yields a diameter of 8, far exceeding the 4×4 minimum. How does Rabbi Yoḥanan's calculation work?
Gemara
Perhaps Rabbi Yoḥanan was simply not precise — the result is slightly off but we accept it as a stringency, requiring a larger sukka than necessary.
Rav Ashi
We can say a Sage was imprecise when it results in a stringency — but Rabbi Yoḥanan's figure would be a LENIENCY (permitting a smaller sukka). We cannot be imprecise in the lenient direction!
Rav Asi
Resolution: The 24 people sit OUTSIDE the sukka surrounding it. Their space is not part of the interior. The 24-cubit ring is the outer boundary, giving an inner circle of 18 cubits and a 4-cubit interior square.
Ruling Principle A Sage may be assumed imprecise when it results in a stringency — but NOT when it results in a leniency.

🏚️ The Craftsmen's Double Booth — Inner, Outer, and Mezuza

Setup: A potter builds two booths — an inner booth for living quarters and an outer booth for selling and displaying wares
↓
Sukka law: Rabbi Levi rules in the name of Rabbi Meir that the inner booth is valid for fulfilling the mitzva of sukka if it has proper schach (roofing)
↓
Mezuza question: Is the outer booth required to have a mezuza? It seems to function as a 'gatehouse' for the inner booth — and gatehouses of dwellings normally require mezuza!
↓
Answer: The outer booth is exempt from mezuza — because even the inner booth it serves is itself exempt (it is temporary, like a sukka, and temporary dwellings are exempt)
↓
Rule derived: A gatehouse serving an exempt structure is itself also exempt from mezuza

✅ The One Condition That Makes Any Booth a Valid Sukka

וּבִלְבַד שֶׁתְּהֵא עֲשׂוּיָה לְצֵל — provided that it was made for shade
Rav Ḥisda's Decisive Rule Any booth — whether built by gentiles, women, animals, Samaritans, shepherds, guards, gardeners, or tax-collectors — is valid for fulfilling the mitzva of sukka, provided one condition is met:

The booth was established to provide shade from its roofing — in the manner characteristic of a sukka.

Intent and function matter. A structure built for shade via its schach qualifies regardless of who built it or why.
