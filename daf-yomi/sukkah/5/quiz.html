QUESTION 1 OF 5
Rabbi Yosei teaches that the Divine Presence never descended below ten handbreadths. When the Torah says 'God descended onto Mount Sinai,' how does the Gemara reconcile this?
A. The verse is metaphorical and God never literally descended
B. God descended but remained ten handbreadths above the mountaintop
C. The ten-handbreadth rule applies only in the Temple, not on mountains
D. Mount Sinai was itself ten handbreadths above the ground
Correct: B — The Gemara resolves the contradiction by teaching that even when God 'descended' onto Sinai, He remained ten handbreadths above the mountaintop itself — the inviolable boundary between the divine and earthly realms was never crossed.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why is the thickness of the Ark cover NOT derived from the frontplate (tzitz), even though the tzitz is a Tabernacle object?
A. The tzitz's dimensions are unknown and unspecified in the Torah
B. The tzitz belonged to the High Priest, not to the Tabernacle itself
C. One may only derive a vessel's dimension from another vessel, not from an ornament
D. The tzitz was made of gold foil, too thin to compare to the solid Ark cover
Correct: C — The Gemara establishes the rule: one derives the dimension of a vessel from the dimension of a vessel — the tzitz is classified as an ornament (worn on the forehead), not a Tabernacle vessel, so it cannot provide the source dimension for the Ark cover.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What does Rabbi Abbahu say the face of a cherub (keruv) resembles, and how does he prove it from Ezekiel?
A. An eagle's face — Ezekiel saw an eagle in his first vision
B. A lion's face — associated with royalty and divine glory
C. A child's face — in Babylonian Aramaic 'keravya' means child, and Ezekiel uses 'cherub' and 'man' interchangeably across his two visions
D. An ox's face — representing strength and service
Correct: C — Rabbi Abbahu derives from the Aramaic etymology (keruv ≈ keravya = child) and from Ezekiel: in Ez. 1:10 the creature had 'the face of a man,' while in Ez. 10:14 the same creature is described with 'the face of a cherub.' Since Ezekiel uses both terms for the same face, a cherub's face = a youthful human (child's) face.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rabbi Meir, how is the minimum 10-handbreadth height of a sukka derived from the Tabernacle?
A. The Tabernacle entrance was exactly 10 handbreadths wide, setting the minimum sukka width and height
B. Moses was 10 handbreadths tall, and a sukka must accommodate a full person
C. Using a 6-handbreadth cubit, the Ark + Ark cover total 10 hb, and the cherubs' wings form a divine canopy above, establishing 10 hb as the sacred roofed-space minimum
D. The shewbread table stood 10 handbreadths high, and all sacred enclosures must match this
Correct: C — Rabbi Meir uses a 6-handbreadth cubit: the Ark (9 hb) + Ark cover (1 hb) = 10 hb. The cherubs (one-third of the Tabernacle's 60 hb = 20 hb tall) stood atop the Ark cover with wings spread above, forming the divine canopy. This 10-handbreadth space beneath the wing-canopy becomes the template for a valid sukka.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rav teaches that halakhic measures are 'halakha l'Moshe miSinai.' How does the Gemara handle the objection that these measures are actually written in the Torah (Deut. 8:8)?
A. The Gemara rejects Rav's position and concludes measures are purely biblical
B. The Gemara distinguishes: biblical measures apply to food, while oral measures apply to ritual objects
C. The Gemara accepts that the verses are merely asmakhtot — textual supports found after the fact; the measures themselves were transmitted orally at Sinai
D. The Gemara limits Rav's ruling to only the olive-bulk measure, not all measures
Correct: C — The Gemara resolves the tension by explaining that the scriptural verses (listing wheat, barley, figs, etc.) are asmakhtot — Scriptural anchors identified after the fact to support oral traditions. The actual measures were transmitted orally to Moses at Sinai, with the Sages later finding textual pegs for them in the Written Torah.
