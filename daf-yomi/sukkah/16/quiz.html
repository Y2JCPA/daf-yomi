Sukkah 16 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
Rabbi Ami bar Tavyomei rules that roofing a sukka with worn, incomplete vessels renders it unfit. What are these 'worn vessels' according to Abaye?
A. Cracked clay pots too small to hold olives
B. Small torn cloth remnants that were once functional garments
C. Old wooden beams from dismantled furniture
D. Reed bundles whose knots have come undone
Correct: B — Abaye identifies the worn vessels as small cloths — torn remnants of fabric. Even though diminished, they retain vessel status and cannot serve as valid sechach.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Rav Huna reconciles the mishna (grain stack = not a sukka) with the baraita (grain stack = valid sukka). What is the key distinction?
A. The mishna refers to barley; the baraita to wheat
B. The mishna applies to a covered stack; the baraita to an open one
C. The mishna applies when no 7×7 handbreadth space exists; the baraita when the space does exist
D. The baraita reflects a minority opinion that is practically rejected
Correct: C — Rav Huna explains that the mishna's ruling applies when the hollowed space lacks the minimum 7×7 handbreadth area. The baraita's validity applies only when that minimum sukka-sized space is present.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to the mishna on this daf, when is a wall lowered from above (from the sechach downward) considered a valid sukka wall?
A. When it is made of wood rather than fabric or reeds
B. When its lower edge is less than 3 handbreadths above the ground
C. When it covers at least two full sides of the sukka
D. When it has been designated as a wall before the holiday begins
Correct: B — The mishna rules that a wall hanging down from above is valid only if its lower edge comes within 3 handbreadths of the ground. The lavud principle then treats the gap as closed, making it a complete wall.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
The Gemara concludes that Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei do NOT hold in accordance with each other. Why not?
A. They disagree about the definition of lavud and how it applies
B. Each is lenient about suspended partitions only within their own specific legal domain
C. Rabbi Yehuda rejects suspended partitions entirely; only Rabbi Yosei accepts them
D. They lived in different eras and their rulings address different geographic regions
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda is lenient only for the cistern eruv (a rabbinic rule), and Rabbi Yosei only for sukka (a positive mitzva). Neither extends their leniency to the other's domain — especially not to Shabbat carrying, a more severe prohibition.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rav Ḥisda citing Avimi teaches that a mat only 4 handbreadths wide can serve as a full 10-handbreadth sukka wall. How?
A. Two such mats are placed side by side, together reaching over 8 handbreadths
B. It is suspended so the gap above and below are each less than 3 handbreadths, applying lavud twice
C. The mat is folded in half vertically, doubling its effective legal height
D. It is tied to a post that adds the remaining handbreadths
Correct: B — Suspended in the middle of the 10-handbreadth space, the mat leaves less than 3 handbreadths above and below. Lavud joins it to the top and to the ground simultaneously — applied twice — making it legally equivalent to a full 10-handbreadth wall.
