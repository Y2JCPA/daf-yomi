🧱 Suspended Partition: Valid Wall or Not?

Rabbi Yosei
A suspended partition (מחיצה תלויה) — one that does not reach the ground — is valid and permits carrying on Shabbat.
The Rabbis
A suspended partition is not valid. A wall must reach the ground to function as a partition for Shabbat domains.
Mishna (Wall from Above)
If a wall lowered from above leaves its lower edge 3+ handbreadths above the ground, the sukka is unfit — animals can pass through.
Rabbi Yosei (Sukka)
Even a suspended wall is valid for a sukka — a positive mitzva. The lavud principle closes gaps under 3 handbreadths, rendering it a complete wall.
Core Question Does a suspended wall (לחי תלויה) function as a valid partition — for sukka walls, Shabbat eruvin, or both?

🤔 Do Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei Align? The Gemara's Verdict

Rabba bar bar Ḥana (R. Yoḥanan)
Rabbi Yehuda's ruling (cistern wall suffices as partition) follows Rabbi Yosei's view — that a suspended partition permits carrying.
Gemara
Not so! The two sages do NOT align. Each holds a narrow leniency in their own domain only — they cannot be combined.
Rabbi Yehuda
I am lenient about the cistern only because eruvin are rabbinic. I would NOT extend this leniency to Shabbat carrying generally.
Rabbi Yosei
I permit a suspended wall for a sukka (positive mitzva) only. For Shabbat carrying between courtyards — a graver prohibition — I agree walls must reach the ground.
Conclusion Neither sage endorses a general principle of suspended partitions. Their leniencies are context-specific and cannot be combined into a broader rule.

📏 Lavud Applied Twice: Mat as a Full Sukka Wall (Rav Ḥisda / Avimi)

The Problem: A mat is only 4 handbreadths + a bit wide. The sukka wall must be 10 handbreadths tall. How can it serve as a full wall?
↓
Step 1 — Suspend the mat: Hang the mat in the middle of the 10-handbreadth vertical space — touching neither sechach above nor ground below.
↓
Step 2 — Lavud above: The gap between the mat's top edge and the sechach is less than 3 handbreadths → lavud joins it to the roof.
↓
Step 3 — Lavud below: The gap between the mat's bottom edge and the ground is less than 3 handbreadths → lavud joins it to the ground.
↓
Result: The mat is legally treated as spanning the full 10 handbreadths — a valid sukka wall! Lavud is applied twice in one structure.
↓
Baraita objection: A mat must be 7 handbreadths (not just 4). Gemara answers: 7 handbreadths assumes lavud is applied once; Avimi teaches it can be applied twice.

🚿 Cistern Between Two Courtyards: Partition Placement

Beit Shammai

  • Partition may be placed below the waterline
  • Underwater partition still legally divides the cistern
  • More lenient about where the partition is situated

Beit Hillel

  • Partition must be placed above the waterline
  • Visible, above-water partition required to divide cistern
  • More stringent — the partition must be functionally visible

Rabbi Yehuda

  • The existing wall between courtyards itself serves as the partition
  • No special partition needed — the shared wall suffices
  • Most lenient view; Gemara asks: does this follow Rabbi Yosei?
