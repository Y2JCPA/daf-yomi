Sukkah 32 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava, which direction of lulav curvature renders it unfit?
A. Curved backward toward the spine
B. Curved forward away from the spine
C. Curved in any direction whatsoever
D. Curvature never disqualifies a lulav
Correct: B — Rava rules that forward curvature (away from the spine, like a sickle) renders the lulav unfit because it no longer presents as a unified unit. Backward curvature toward the spine is fit — it still looks bound together.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rabbi Parnakh in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan, how long must the spine (not including leaves) of the lulav be?
A. Three handbreadths
B. Four handbreadths
C. Five handbreadths
D. One full cubit
Correct: B — Rabbi Parnakh said in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan that the spine must be four handbreadths, so that it extends at least one handbreadth above the myrtle branch when the species are bound together.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does Abaye reject oleander as the identity of the 'dense-leaved tree' commanded in Leviticus 23:40?
A. Oleander's leaves do not cover the wood of the branch
B. Oleander does not grow in the Land of Israel
C. Oleander is poisonous, contradicting 'its ways are ways of pleasantness'
D. Oleander leaves do not grow in clusters of three
Correct: C — Abaye invokes the hermeneutical principle that Torah's commandments must be pleasant (Proverbs 3:17). Since oleander is toxic and harmful, requiring Jews to handle it would violate this principle, so it cannot be the intended species.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the halakha if the majority of a myrtle's leaves fall off?
A. Always unfit — the majority of leaves must be present
B. Fit, provided the dense-leaf (meshulash) pattern remains intact
C. Fit unconditionally, since any myrtle branch qualifies
D. Depends solely on whether the branch is still green
Correct: B — The baraita rules that majority leaf-loss does not automatically disqualify — as long as the meshulash (three-from-one-base) pattern is still present. Once that pattern is lost, the branch is unfit as a hadas.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rav Pappa raised a dilemma about the tiyomet (central twin-leaf). How is it resolved?
A. A split tiyomet is definitively fit, since it is still attached
B. One version of Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement resolves it: a split tiyomet equals removal and is unfit
C. Rava rules that splitting is permitted if done unintentionally
D. The dilemma is fully unresolved (teiku) with no resolution cited
Correct: B — Rav Pappa asked whether a split tiyomet equals a removed tiyomet (which is unfit per Rabbi Yoḥanan). A second version of Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement explicitly rules that a split tiyomet is like a removed one — unfit — resolving the dilemma.
