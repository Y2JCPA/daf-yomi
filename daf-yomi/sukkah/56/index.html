🕯️ Which Blessing First on Sukkot Night?

Rav
Recite sukka first, then zman. The obligation of the day takes precedence — we are entering the mitzvah of sukka at this very moment.
Rabba bar bar Ḥana
Recite zman first, then sukka. Zman applies to the entire Festival; sukka is only one specific obligation nested within it.
Rav Naḥman bar Rav Ḥisda
Final ruling: The halakha does not follow Rav. One recites zman first, then sukka — in accordance with Rabba bar bar Ḥana.
Mishna Context The dispute is triggered by the Shavuot/Shabbat case where the distribution priest says simultaneously: 'Here is matza, here is leavened bread' — raising questions about how to handle concurrent, distinct obligations.

🏛️ Beit Shammai vs. Beit Hillel: Kiddush Order

Beit Shammai

  • Sanctification of the day first, then wine
  • The day causes the wine to be brought — it is prior in importance
  • The day is already sanctified before the wine arrives at table
  • Parallel: Rav's view — the day's central obligation comes first

Beit Hillel

  • Wine first, then sanctification of the day
  • Wine is the vehicle for kiddush — it occasions the recitation
  • One cannot recite kiddush without wine already in hand
  • Parallel: Rabba bar bar Ḥana's view — the broader/enabling item comes first

⚖️ Does the Rav/Rabba Dispute Mirror Shammai/Hillel?

Does Rav follow Beit Shammai (day first) and Rabba follow Beit Hillel (wine/enabling item first)?
↓
Rav's rebuttal
Even Beit Hillel agrees wine is first only because wine enables kiddush. For sukka, the day's mitzvah is the enabling factor — so Rav fits Hillel too.
Rabba's rebuttal
Even Beit Shammai's rule is specific to kiddush where day precedes wine. For sukka vs. zman, that logic does not apply — Rabba fits Shammai too.
↓
Neither disputant is necessarily aligned with Shammai or Hillel — the parallel breaks down
↓
Proof from mishna: The distribution priest lists matza and leavened bread without a clear order — suggesting zman (general) precedes sukka (specific)

אוי לָרָשָׁע אוֹי לִשְׁכֵנוֹ — טוֹב לַצַּדִּיק טוֹב לִשְׁכֵנוֹ
🚫 Two Traditions: Why Was Bilga Penalized? Version 1 — Miriam's Apostasy: Miriam, daughter of a Bilga priest, apostatized and married a Greek soldier. During the persecutions she entered the Temple, struck the altar with a sandal, and cried: 'Wolf, wolf! You consumed Israel's money yet did not stand by them in their hour of need!'

Version 2 — The Watch Tarried: Members of Bilga's watch were late arriving to Temple service. The preceding watch (Yeshevav) had to serve in their place.

The Penalties: (1) Bilga's ring was permanently sealed shut; (2) they always divide the shewbread in the south — a position of lesser honor.

Abaye's maxim: Woe to the wicked, woe to his neighbor — an entire watch bears the shame of its members. The Gemara closes: Good for the righteous, good for his neighbor.
