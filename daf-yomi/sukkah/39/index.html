🍋 Acquiring Etrog from an Am Ha'aretz in Shemita

You need a lulav + etrog from an am ha'aretz during Shemita
↓
The Problem
Cannot purchase Shemita produce from an am ha'aretz — he may misuse the sanctified money
The Solution
Seller gifts the etrog alongside the lulav sale — no direct Shemita transaction for the etrog
↓
Rav Huna: Fold the etrog's cost into the lulav's purchase price — transaction framed as lulav only
↓
Core reason: Shemita money is sacred — must be spent on food consumed properly, not handed to one who may misuse it

🍋 Why Is a Special Workaround Needed?

Questioner
If the am ha'aretz refuses to give the etrog as a gift, how can the buyer legally obtain it during Shemita?
Rav Huna
He incorporates the cost of the etrog into the purchase price of the lulav — paying a single inflated sum for the lulav that covers both items.
Questioner
But if he has already paid the market price for the lulav, why is a workaround needed at all? Why not simply pay separately for the etrog?
Gemara
It is necessary because one may not transfer Shemita money directly to an am ha'aretz — lest he spend those sanctified funds improperly. The workaround preserves the form of the mitzvah while protecting Shemita sanctity.
Core Principle Shemita money carries inherent sanctity (kedushat shevi'it) — it must flow toward food consumed in the proper manner, not to one who may squander it.

🌳 Etrog Follows Tree Law in Three Ways

🚫 Orla

  • Fruit of a tree is forbidden the first 3 years after planting
  • Etrog follows tree rules — not vegetable rules — for orla
  • Practically: an etrog from a newly planted tree is prohibited

🍇 Fourth-Year Produce

  • In the fourth year, fruit becomes neta revai
  • Must be redeemed or eaten in Jerusalem (like vineyard fruit)
  • Etrog in year four carries this sanctity — not treated as field crop

🌱 Shemita Determination

  • Shemita year is determined by when it is picked, not when it begins to grow
  • Both Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Eliezer agree on this for Shemita
  • Contrast: tithing year is disputed — they argue about that separately

🌾 Rav Sheshet: Objection and Self-Resolution

שביעית ט:א — רוטא ועולשין וירקות שדה...
Talmudic Dialectic Objection (Rav Sheshet): The baraita permits transferring Shemita money to an am ha'aretz for produce worth up to three meals from an ownerless field. But Mishnah Shevi'it 9:1 lists plants like rue and sorrel that are excluded from Shemita restrictions entirely — suggesting no limit applies to them at all. Contradiction?

Resolution (Rav Sheshet himself): The Sages taught the three-meal rule specifically for food gathered for one's basic sustenance [man] — plants people gather and eat immediately in the field. These are a distinct category from regular Shemita produce purchased and brought home. No contradiction: different categories, different rules.

Key Distinction: Field sustenance plants (eaten on the spot) vs. Shemita produce purchased for home consumption.
