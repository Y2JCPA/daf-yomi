QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why must the etrog be given as a gift rather than sold directly by the am ha'aretz during Shemita?
A. Because the etrog is too holy to be assigned a monetary price during Shemita
B. Because one may not transfer Shemita money to an am ha'aretz lest he misuse those sanctified funds
C. Because Shemita produce cannot legally change hands under any circumstance
D. Because an am ha'aretz is not trusted to grow etrogim with proper Shemita observance
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that Shemita money carries sanctity (kedushat shevi'it) and cannot be given to an am ha'aretz who may spend it improperly rather than on food consumed at home in the appropriate manner.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What practical solution did Rav Huna offer for acquiring an etrog from an am ha'aretz when a gift is not possible?
A. Purchase the etrog after Shemita ends and backdate the acquisition
B. Have a Torah-observant Jew serve as intermediary buyer
C. Incorporate the etrog's cost into the purchase price of the lulav, buying both under one lulav transaction
D. Trade a non-Shemita item of equal value directly for the etrog
Correct: C — Rav Huna taught that one should pay an inflated price for the lulav that covers the etrog's value as well, so no separate Shemita-money transaction occurs for the etrog itself.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Abaye, to which specific Hallel blessing does the rule 'obligation depends on custom' apply?
A. The blessing recited before beginning Hallel
B. The blessing recited over the lulav during Hallel
C. The blessing recited at the conclusion of Hallel
D. The blessing recited over the Hallel at the Passover Seder
Correct: C — Abaye specifies that the custom-dependent ruling applies to the concluding blessing of Hallel. The opening blessing has broader obligation; it is the concluding blessing whose requirement varies by community custom.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In how many ways does the Gemara establish that an etrog follows the law of a tree rather than a vegetable?
A. One way: only its Shemita year follows tree law
B. Two ways: orla and Shemita determination
C. Three ways: orla, fourth-year produce (neta revai), and Shemita determined by picking date
D. Four ways: including maaser (tithes) as well
Correct: C — The mishna explicitly states three ways the etrog is like a tree: (1) orla — forbidden during first three years; (2) fourth-year produce — neta revai sanctity; (3) Shemita year determined by when the fruit is picked, not when it began growing.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How did Rav Sheshet handle the apparent contradiction between the baraita's three-meal rule and Mishnah Shevi'it 9:1?
A. He ruled the baraita was a minority opinion and rejected it
B. He showed the Mishnah applied only outside the Land of Israel
C. He raised the contradiction himself and then resolved it: sustenance plants gathered immediately are a distinct category from regular Shemita produce
D. He deferred the question to a later amora without resolving it
Correct: C — In a display of talmudic dialectic, Rav Sheshet both posed and answered his own objection. He resolved it by distinguishing plants people gather for immediate sustenance in the field (a separate category) from regular Shemita produce bought and brought home, to which the three-meal rule applies.
