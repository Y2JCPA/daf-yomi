Sukkah 14 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Pappa's clarification of Shmuel, what is the ruling when boards have exactly four handbreadths in their width?
A. Everyone agrees the sukka is fit
B. Everyone agrees the sukka is unfit
C. Rabbi Meir says unfit; Rabbi Yehuda says fit — they dispute here
D. It depends on whether the boards are cedar or another wood
Correct: B — Rav Pappa: boards ≥ 4 handbreadths = unfit for all opinions (per Shmuel); boards < 3 handbreadths = fit for all; boards between 3 and 4 are the actual disputed range.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Rabbi Elazar's aggadic teaching compares the prayers of the righteous to a pitchfork (eter). What is the basis of this comparison?
A. A pitchfork has many tines, like the many words of a prayer
B. Just as a pitchfork turns grain from one side to another, prayer turns divine judgment toward mercy
C. Isaac used a pitchfork in the fields when he prayed for his barren wife
D. A pitchfork is used at harvest time, when prayers for sustenance are most powerful
Correct: B — The word va-yetar (entreated) shares its root with eter (pitchfork). Rabbi Elazar teaches that just as a pitchfork flips grain, prayer flips God's attribute of strict judgment to the attribute of mercy.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the halakha according to the Mishna when one places a board of four handbreadths width on top of the sukka?
A. The sukka is entirely unfit and one cannot fulfill the obligation there at all
B. The sukka is fit, and one fulfills the obligation throughout — including sleeping beneath the board
C. The sukka is fit and one fulfills the obligation, provided one does not sleep beneath the board itself
D. The sukka is fit only if the board is placed at the edge, not the center
Correct: C — The Mishna rules the sukka is fit and one fulfills the obligation, but must not sleep beneath the board. The space under the 4-handbreadth board is treated like a house interior (ohel), not valid skhakh.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What did Rav Naḥman rule when asked by Rav Ḥisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna about turning a 4-handbreadth board on its side?
A. The sukka remains unfit — the board's inherent dimensions cannot be circumvented
B. The sukka is fit — the board in its current orientation no longer presents 4 handbreadths
C. The sukka is fit only if the board is turned before construction begins
D. It depends on whether the side exposed is smooth or has protruding edges
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman ruled the sukka is fit, agreeing with Rav Huna. Rav Huna remarked that Rav Naḥman had confirmed his opinion. A baraita also supports this: a board presenting only 3 handbreadths (when turned) renders the sukka fit.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the position of the Rabbis (opposing Rabbi Yosei) regarding grain handles after they have been trampled (according to Rabbi Yochanan's reading of besasan)?
A. The handles remain susceptible to ritual impurity as long as the grain exists
B. The handles become ritually pure because trampling severs their functional connection to the grain
C. The handles are pure only if the grain was already tithed
D. The handles remain impure but only convey impurity to liquids, not solids
Correct: B — According to Rabbi Yochanan (besasan = trampled), the Rabbis hold that trampling destroys the handles' function, making them ritually pure. Rabbi Yosei disagrees, reasoning they can regain handle-function when the sukka is later dismantled.
