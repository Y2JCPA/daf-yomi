🐘 Animals as Sukka Walls: The Central Dispute

Rabbi Meir
Unfit. One may neither establish an eruv using a living creature, nor use one as a partition. Decree: lest the animal die (Abaye) or flee (Rava), leaving the sukka without a valid wall — perhaps unnoticed.
Rabbi Yehuda
Fit. The wall is valid now. We do not issue decrees against uncommon occurrences like death. A living creature may serve as a sukka partition.
Key Distinction Rabbi Meir agrees that a sukka built entirely atop a camel is fit — the dispute only applies when the animal itself serves as one of the walls.

🐘 Using an Animal as a Sukka Wall

Rabbi Meir
Unfit. A living creature may not be used as a mechitza (partition) for sukka, eruv, or similar halakhot. Rabbinic decree due to concern the wall will spontaneously become invalid.
Rabbi Yehuda
Fit. The animal forms a valid wall right now. Uncommon contingencies (death, flight) do not generate Rabbinic decrees.
Gemara's Question What exactly is Rabbi Meir's concern? Two amora'im disagree: Abaye says it is a decree lest the animal die; Rava says it is a decree lest the animal flee.

💀 Abaye vs. Rava: Why Does Rabbi Meir Disqualify Animal Walls?

Abaye: Decree Lest It Die

  • Animal dies → carcass contracts
  • Gap of >3 handbreadths opens between wall and schach
  • Lavud (≤3 tefachim = connected) no longer applies
  • Wall falls below 10 handbreadths — invalid
  • Owner may not notice in time to repair it
  • Objection: Death is uncommon — why decree against it?

Rava: Decree Lest It Flee

  • Animal flees → wall disappears entirely
  • More dramatic than death — clearly noticeable
  • Case: elephant with a rider — flight would be noticed immediately
  • If noticeable, owner would fix it; no need to decree
  • Objection: Then why not also worry about death?
  • Answer: Death is uncommon; Sages do not decree for it

📜 Abaye Resolves a Cross-Tractate Contradiction

Abaye's position: Rabbi Meir is concerned about potential death; Rabbi Yehuda is not concerned.
↓
Contradiction #1 (Yevamot): If a daughter of an Israelite married a priest and her husband went overseas — she continues to eat teruma, implying we do not fear the husband's death. This mishna seems to reflect Rabbi Meir's leniency.
↓
Contradiction #2 (Gittin): One leaving home gives his wife a conditional bill of divorce: 'if I do not return within 12 months, this is your get.' The get takes effect, apparently fearing he may die — implying that mishna is stringent about potential death.
↓
Abaye resolves: The two mishnayot reflect different tannaim. The lenient one (Yevamot) follows Rabbi Meir — not concerned about death. The stringent one (Gittin) follows Rabbi Yehuda — concerned about death.
↓
Confirmation: A baraita about purchasing wine from Samaritans provides additional evidence that these two tannaim hold consistent, opposing positions across tractates.
