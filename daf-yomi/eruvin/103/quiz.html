QUESTION 1 OF 5
How does Rabbi Elazar resolve the contradiction between the two mishnayot about wart removal on Shabbat?
A. One mishna refers to removal by hand, the other to removal with an instrument
B. One mishna refers to a moist wart, the other to a dry wart
C. One mishna follows Rabbi Eliezer, the other follows the Rabbis
D. One mishna is about a priest's wart, the other about an animal's wart
Correct: B — Rabbi Elazar holds that both mishnayot discuss removal by hand. The permissive mishna refers to a dry wart — only a rabbinic prohibition, which the Temple overrides. The restrictive mishna refers to a moist wart — a Torah prohibition that cannot be set aside.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does the baraita specify that a priest's wart should be removed with the teeth rather than by hand or instrument?
A. Using the teeth is more precise and causes less bleeding
B. Hands are ritually unfit once involved in other activities on Shabbat
C. By hand is rabbinic (Temple overrides); by instrument is a Torah violation; teeth provide the minimally invasive Torah-level act required under Rabbi Eliezer's ruling
D. The teeth method avoids the prohibition of carrying on Shabbat
Correct: C — Removal by hand is only a rabbinic prohibition (the Temple routinely overrides this). Removal by instrument is a Torah prohibition. The baraita specifies teeth as a novel, minimized method of Torah-level action — consistent with Rabbi Eliezer allowing mitzva preparations to override Shabbat, but doing so as minimally as possible.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rav Yosef respond to Rav Safra's challenge about lowering the Paschal lamb into the oven near nightfall?
A. That case involves only a rabbinic prohibition, not a Torah prohibition
B. Priests are vigilant in Temple matters and will not accidentally continue after nightfall — no preventive decree was needed
C. The Paschal lamb is a communal offering and communal needs always override Shabbat
D. The oven is within the Temple courtyard, which constitutes a private domain
Correct: B — Rav Yosef explains that we are not concerned priests will forget themselves and continue roasting after nightfall, because priests are constantly engaged in and attentive to the Temple service. Therefore no protective rabbinic decree was necessary in their case.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rav Yehuda son of Rabbi Ḥiyya, why is wrapping an injured finger with a small sash prohibited while a reed is permitted?
A. A sash interposes between the priest's hand and the holy vessels
B. A sash constitutes an extra garment, and priests are forbidden from adding to the prescribed priestly vestments
C. A sash cannot be tied on Shabbat due to the prohibition of making knots
D. A sash is too thick and prevents proper grip of the Temple vessels
Correct: B — Rav Yehuda holds that a small sash is prohibited because it is considered a garment — and priests are forbidden from wearing any garment beyond their prescribed vestments during Temple service. A reed does not have this status.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the core dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis regarding the harp string that snapped in the middle?
A. Whether tying any knot is permitted on Shabbat within the Temple
B. Whether a bow-tie counts as a forbidden knot under Torah law
C. Whether a string snapping in the middle is comparable to snapping at the end, which has an established leniency for retying
D. Whether musical instruments may be played in the Temple on Shabbat at all
Correct: C — The Gemara explains that a string snapping at the end has an established leniency allowing it to be retied during Temple service. Rabbi Shimon holds that a string snapping in the middle is not comparable and cannot benefit from that leniency; the Rabbis disagree and permit it.
