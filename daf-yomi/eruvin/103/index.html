⚖️ When Does Temple Service Override Shabbat?

Mishna (Permissive)
A wart disqualifying a priest may be removed on Shabbat — Temple mitzva needs override the prohibition.
Mishna (Restrictive)
When Passover eve falls on Shabbat, cutting a wart off the Paschal lamb is prohibited — it does not override Shabbat.
Gemara Resolution
Three approaches proposed: (1) moist vs. dry wart; (2) by hand vs. with instrument; (3) rabbinic vs. Torah-level prohibition. Rava: permissive mishna follows Rabbi Eliezer.
Core Principle Temple service overrides rabbinic prohibitions unconditionally. Torah prohibitions require either Rabbi Eliezer's broad rule or a creative minimization of the act (e.g., using teeth instead of an instrument).

🔍 Two Amoraim, Two Resolutions — Wart Contradiction

Rabbi Elazar

  • Both mishnayot discuss removal by hand
  • Permissive mishna: dry wart — only a rabbinic prohibition
  • Restrictive mishna: moist wart — Torah-level prohibition
  • Temple service overrides rabbinic only; Torah prohibition stands
  • Parallel to: carrying the animal (rabbinic) vs. slaughtering (Torah)

Rabbi Yosei ben Ḥanina

  • Both mishnayot discuss the same wart type (moist)
  • Permissive mishna: removal by hand — rabbinic level
  • Restrictive mishna: removal with instrument — Torah level
  • Temple overrides the rabbinic; Torah prohibition cannot be set aside
  • Why not also distinguish moist/dry? — The mishna juxtaposes with other Torah cases

🌊 Abaye and Rav Safra — Challenges to Rav Yosef's Rule

Rav Yosef's Rule: Transgressing a rabbinic decree for a mitzva in the Temple is permitted; laypeople may not do so outside the Temple
↓
Rav Safra's Challenge 1: A scroll rolling into the public domain — the mishna permits rolling it back. That involves a Torah prohibition!
↓
Abaye's Response: That mishna involves a karmelit (4 handbreadths wide, less than 10 high) — only a rabbinic domain. No contradiction.
↓
Rav Safra's Challenge 2: Lowering the Paschal lamb into the oven just before nightfall — one might forget and continue after nightfall, a Torah violation!
↓
Abaye is silent. Rav Yosef later answers: Priests are vigilant — they are constantly engaged in service and will not forget. No preventive decree was necessary.

🎵 Harp String Dispute — End vs. Middle of String

Baraita (R. Shimon)
Even tying a bow will not restore the harp's sound. He transgresses Shabbat without fulfilling the mitzva properly. Tying is prohibited entirely.
Mishna (Rabbis)
Tying a bow after the fact is permitted. Preparations for a mitzva that could not have been performed before Shabbat are allowed.
Gemara (Third Resolution)
Both refer to a string that snapped in the middle. Rabbi Shimon: this differs from snapping at the end — no established leniency applies. The Rabbis: it is similar enough to override.
R. Shimon ben Elazar Adds Even if tied, the harp will not sound properly — he transgresses without fulfilling the mitzva. This is an independent reason to prohibit tying, beyond the Shabbat dispute.

🏛️ Rava's Resolution — The Baraita of Removing Wart with Teeth

Setup: Two mishnayot contradict each other on whether a priest's wart may be removed on Shabbat
↓
Rava's Answer: The permissive mishna follows Rabbi Eliezer — preparations for a mitzva override all Shabbat prohibitions, even Torah-level ones
↓
Baraita Evidence: A fellow priest may cut a disqualifying wart off with his teeth on Shabbat — an unusual and specific method
↓
Why teeth specifically? By hand = rabbinic prohibition (Temple always overrides). By instrument = Torah prohibition. Teeth = minimized violation, avoiding the stricter category
↓
Whose opinion? Must be Rabbi Eliezer — the Rabbis simply permit by hand; only Rabbi Eliezer, who allows Torah overrides, needs to minimize via the novel method of teeth
↓
Conclusion: Rabbi Eliezer's broad principle holds, but even he requires using the least invasive possible method when overriding a Torah prohibition for mitzva needs
