QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why did Rav Mesharshiya rule that an alleyway opening into a single-owner's backyard is prohibited for carrying?
A. A single owner has exclusive rights and can bar neighbors from an eiruv
B. A single owner might add buildings, altering the configuration and invalidating the partition
C. Single owners are not obligated to participate in an eiruv chatzeirot
D. The halakha requires at least two residents for a shared domain to be permitted
Correct: B — Rav Mesharshiya held that a single individual might decide to add buildings to his backyard, changing the spatial arrangement and invalidating the current boundary. Many owners, by contrast, are unlikely to reach agreement on such changes, making the configuration stable.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
When the alleyway opening to the sea on one side and a refuse heap on the other was brought before Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, what did he do?
A. He ruled it was fully permitted since both are valid natural partitions
B. He ruled it was prohibited since neither partition is reliable enough
C. He did not issue any ruling on the matter
D. He permitted it only on Shabbat but not Yom Tov
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi declined to rule. The Gemara explains the partitions were technically valid, but he was cautious — particularly about the impermanence of a private refuse heap. The Rabbis of his generation subsequently ruled the practice prohibited.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rava, how is a centipede-shaped alleyway (long main alley with branches on both sides) rendered permitted for carrying?
A. A single lechi at the main entrance is sufficient for all the branches
B. Every branch requires its own complete eiruv chatzeirot
C. Doorway openings (tzurat hapetaḥ) for branches on one side; a lechi or cross beam for the other side
D. The main alleyway needs a cross beam and each branch needs a lechi
Correct: C — Rava ruled that all small alleyways on one side receive tzurat hapetaḥ (doorway-shaped openings), while the branches on the other side are permitted by a lechi (side post) or cross beam. Abaye's more lenient view — that a single lechi at the main entrance suffices — was rejected by Rava.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
The Talmudic dispute about whether one may carry in the space beneath the cross beam ultimately depends on which fundamental question?
A. Whether the cross beam must span the full width of the alleyway without gaps
B. Whether the cross beam functions as a conspicuous marker or as a legal partition
C. Whether the cross beam must be made of wood or may be of any material
D. Whether the cross beam must be at least ten handbreadths above the ground
Correct: B — If the cross beam is a conspicuous marker (היכר), it signals the boundary and the space beneath it is permitted. If it is a partition (מחיצה), treated as though it descends to the ground, only the inner side is valid and the space directly beneath and beyond is forbidden.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Rava ruled that placing a cross beam on pegs inserted outside the alleyway walls (facing the public domain) is prohibited for carrying within the alleyway. What is his reason?
A. Pegs are not sturdy enough to support a halakhically valid cross beam
B. A cross beam placed on outer pegs is technically in the public domain
C. The cross beam must be positioned directly atop the alleyway walls, not on extensions beyond them
D. Such a cross beam would be too far above the ground to serve as a marker
Correct: C — Rava held that a valid cross beam must rest directly on top of the alleyway walls. Pegs inserted outside the walls extend the beam's position beyond the walls' plane, disqualifying it. Even the lenient opinion (which permits the area beneath the beam) cannot help here, since the beam itself is not properly situated.
