🚶 Set Out and Was Turned Back — What Is Required?

Rabba
Everyone agrees he must verbally declare his residence at the end of his limit — otherwise we'd assume he returned home by choice. The dispute is only whether he must also physically set out.
Rav Yosef
Everyone agrees he must physically set out — that requirement is obvious and not disputed. Where they argue is whether a verbal declaration of residence is also required.
Ulla
If he set out and was persuaded to return, he is simultaneously considered returned to his city and set out on his way — he retains his extended Shabbat limit to the destination.
Gemara
Rav Natan bar Oshaya waited until Rav Yehuda descended one step before speaking — proving that physical departure is required, and per Rabbi Yosei b. Rabbi Yehuda, even a single step suffices.
Core Insight An involuntary return does not cancel intention. Only a person's own choice to return voids his established residence — another's interference cannot undo his legal status as 'one who set out.'

🔄 Set Out on Shabbat Eve — Then Turned Back

Person departs toward another city on Shabbat eve, intending to spend Shabbat there
↓
Another person turns him back
He is legally returned AND legally set out — may travel to the destination on Shabbat. His intention is preserved.
He returns on his own initiative
He changed his mind — loses extended limit entirely. Treated as an ordinary resident of his original city.
↓
Key principle: The act of another cannot cancel his stated intention. Only his own free choice to return voids his established residence.

📦 Four Cubits at the Eiruv Deposit Location?

Rami bar Ḥama
Dilemma: When one deposits an eiruv, does he receive four cubits at the eiruv's location plus 2000 beyond — as if physically present there? Or only 2000 cubits from that point, without the four-cubit bonus?
Rava
Proof from the mishna: the Sages permitted eiruv with bread to be lenient with the wealthy, sparing them exertion. This implies foot-residence is intrinsically superior — suggesting it grants four cubits that bread-eiruv may not.
Gemara
Rejected: Even if eiruv-deposit gives no four cubits, bread-eiruv is still preferable because it spares the wealthy person physical exertion. The mishna proves nothing about whether four cubits are granted at the deposit site.
תֵּיקוּ — Unresolved The question of whether depositing an eiruv grants four cubits at the deposit location remains unresolved. Rami bar Ḥama's dilemma stands without a definitive ruling.

🫏 Rabbi Meir: The Donkey Driver and the Camel Driver

The Donkey Driver

  • Walks behind the donkey
  • Cannot go faster than the animal
  • Restricted from advancing freely
  • Symbolizes: blocked on one side

Who Negated Residence Without Eiruv

  • Could have established eiruv — did not
  • Cancelled his original residence
  • Made no new declaration of residence
  • Stuck in place: limited in all directions

The Camel Driver

  • Walks in front of the camel
  • Must not outpace the animal from behind
  • Restricted from pulling too far ahead
  • Symbolizes: blocked on the other side

👣 One Foot In, One Foot Out — Two Versions

אִם תָּשִׁיב מִשַּׁבָּת רַגְלֶךָ — Isaiah 58:13
Rabbi Ḥanina Version A: One foot inside the limit, one beyond — may NOT reenter. The word רַגְלֶךָ (raglekha) is plural: both feet must be inside.

Version B: One foot inside, one beyond — may reenter, since he has not yet fully departed.

Resolution: Version A = opinion of the Sages (strict). Version B = opinion of Aḥerim, who attributes the person to whichever location is more lenient. The baraita citing the lenient view was taught in accordance with Aḥerim.
