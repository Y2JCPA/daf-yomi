📐 Where Is the Migrash?

Ravina
The migrash is one quarter of the boundary — distributed along the sides of the city. The corners are not part of it.
Rav Ashi
Wrong — the migrash belongs only to the corners. The sides receive no open space at all. It is one quarter of the corners.
Ravina
But Numbers 35:4 says the open space goes 'around' the city — that implies all sides, not just corners!
Rav Ashi
'Around' can mean corners specifically. Just as the burnt-offering's altar uses 'around' for specific sides, here too corners qualify as 'around.'
Core Question Numbers 35:4 mandates 1000 cubits of open space (migrash) for Levite cities. Is that space placed along the sides of the city, or only at the four corners?

📊 The Geometry Problem — One Third or One Quarter?

Extended boundary of a Levite city = 24 million square cubits total
↓
Four sides
4 sides × (1000 × 1000) = 4 million sq. cubits
Four corners
4 corners × (1000 × 1000) = 4 million sq. cubits
↓
Total migrash = 8 million sq. cubits = one third of the boundary — but the ruling says one quarter?
↓
Resolution: 'One quarter' refers to the linear dimension, not the area. The 1000-cubit migrash strip is one quarter of the 4000-cubit side of the full 4000×4000 boundary square.

🪢 Karpef Per City: Rav Huna vs. Chiyya bar Rav

Rav Huna
Each city independently receives its own karpef. Two adjacent cities get two karpefot between them — one from each side — totaling 141⅓ cubits of combined buffer.
Chiyya bar Rav
Two adjacent cities share only one karpef between them. The karpef principle applies to the gap itself, not to each city independently.
Gemara
The mishna says Rabbi Meir grants each city its own karpef — this is a conclusive refutation of Chiyya bar Rav!
Chiyya bar Rav
The mishna's later clause — requiring each city to have 70+ cubits vacant on its side — actually supports my reading differently. I hold that clause follows Rabbi Meir, not the Rabbis.
Practical Difference Rav Huna: each of two adjacent cities contributes ~70⅔ cubits → 141⅓ needed between them. Chiyya bar Rav: only 70⅔ cubits total needed in the gap.

🏘️ Three Villages — Two Arrangements

Triangle Formation (Mishna)

  • Middle village flanked by two outer ones in a triangle
  • If outer villages are within 141⅓ cubits of each other
  • The middle village combines all three into a single Shabbat unit
  • Shabbat limit measured from the combined outer edge

Row Formation (Gemara Discussion)

  • Rabba (via Rav Idi, Rabbi Ḥanina): 'aligned in a row' means triangle shape, not literal straight line
  • Middle village can be up to 2000 cubits from each outer village (Abaye)
  • Two outer villages cannot exceed 4000 cubits from each other
  • Comparison: bow-shaped city rule limits maximum span

🏙️ Akistefon & Ardeshir — A Real-World Ruling

Rav Safra's question to Rava: The cities of Akistefon and Ardeshir each measure their Shabbat limit from the far end of the other city. Is this valid?
↓
The issue: Two cities can share a combined Shabbat unit only if there is no more than 70⅔ cubits (one karpef) separating them.
↓
Rava's response: He went out and physically showed Rav Safra submerged foundation stones of a connecting wall in the Tigris River.
↓
These foundations were within 70⅔ cubits of the far city — proving the cities were once halakhically linked by a shared boundary wall.
↓
Result: The practice was fully validated. Akistefon and Ardeshir halakhically form one combined Shabbat city.
