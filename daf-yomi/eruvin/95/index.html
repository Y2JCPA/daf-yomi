🏗️ The 'Edge Descends and Seals' Principle

גּוּד אַסִּיק מְחִיצְתָּא
Core Legal Fiction (גּוּד אַסִּיק מְחִיצְתָּא) A structural element — cross beam, roof edge, or upper story — is treated as if it descends vertically to form a partition at ground level. Daf 95 tests this principle in three arenas: a ruin breached into public domain, a sukka on a roofed portico, and an upper story over reshut ha-rabbim. Abaye and Rav accept it broadly; Rava and Shmuel restrict it. The daf closes with a detailed mishna on rescuing phylacteries on Shabbat by donning them in the normal manner.

⚖️ Sukka on a Roofed Portico: Valid or Invalid?

Abaye
Valid. The edge of the roof descends and seals — we treat the perimeter of the roof as though it continues down to form walls. The portico is therefore enclosed, and the sukka built within it is valid.
Rava
Invalid. The edge of a roof does not descend and seal. Without actual physical partitions, the portico remains open on its sides — the sukka lacks the required walls and is unfit.
Gemara
This dispute mirrors Rav vs. Shmuel on whether gud asik applies to roofs. According to Shmuel, even Abaye concedes the sukka is invalid. Their dispute only exists according to Rav, who accepts the descending-edge principle for roofs.
Underlying Framework Rav: edge of roof descends and seals. Shmuel: it does not. Abaye aligns with Rav; Rava aligns with Shmuel's restriction. Abaye's position is thus internally consistent with Rav, not a new dispute.

🔒 Breached Courtyard: Rabbi Yosei vs Rabbi Yehuda

Rabbi Yehuda
If the courtyard was breached before Shabbat carrying is forbidden that day. But if breached during Shabbat, since carrying was permitted at Shabbat's onset, it remains permitted — do not retroactively prohibit.
Rabbi Yosei
Symmetry: If they are prohibited in the future (once the breach is known), they are likewise prohibited on that very Shabbat. And conversely — if permitted that Shabbat, they remain permitted in the future.
Rav Sheshet / Rabbi Yoḥanan
Rabbi Yosei intends to prohibit even on that same Shabbat. A baraita confirms: 'Just as they are prohibited in the future, so too on that very Shabbat.'
Rav Anan (citing Shmuel directly)
Shmuel rules like Rabbi Yehuda only when the breach opens into a karmelit (Rabbinic domain). Where the breach faces a full Torah-level public domain, even Shmuel is stringent.
Halakhic Bottom Line Rav Ḥiyya bar Yosef: halakha follows Rabbi Yosei. Shmuel (clarified by Rav Anan): halakha follows Rabbi Yehuda — but only for a breach into a karmelit, not a full reshut ha-rabbim.

📿 Finding Phylacteries on Shabbat: The Full Protocol

Situation: Tefillin are found outside town on Shabbat. Carrying them directly is prohibited, but leaving them risks loss or desecration.
↓
Standard method (Tanna Kamma): Don one pair at a time in the normal manner of wearing — head tefillin on head, arm tefillin on arm — walk into town, remove them, return outside, don the next pair. Repeat until all are retrieved.
↓
Rabban Gamliel's extension: Two pairs may be worn simultaneously — there is room on the head for two head-tefillin and on the arm for two arm-tefillin (per Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak). This doubles rescue efficiency.
↓
If tied in bundles: They cannot be donned normally. One must sit and guard them until Shabbat ends, then carry them in. No compromise method is available.
↓
Time of danger: Personal safety overrides. One covers the phylacteries and proceeds — one need not remain with them at personal risk.
↓
Rabbi Shimon's pass-along method: Hand them to a person less than four cubits away, who passes them to the next, and so on — even through a hundred people — until the phylacteries reach the outermost courtyard of the city. (The same method applies for a newborn found in a field on Shabbat.)

🤔 Tanna Kamma vs Rabban Gamliel — How Many Pairs?

Tanna Kamma — One Pair Per Trip

  • Only one pair may be carried at a time
  • View 1: Shabbat IS a time for tefillin — wearing a second pair while the first is on violates bal tosif (adding to a mitzva)
  • View 2: Shabbat is a time for tefillin, and mitzva fulfillment requires intent — donning the second pair with rescue intent still triggers bal tosif
  • Either way: strict cap of one pair per entry into town

Rabban Gamliel — Two Pairs Permitted

  • Two pairs may be worn simultaneously
  • View 1: Shabbat is NOT a time for tefillin — donning a second pair is not 'adding to a mitzva' since the day itself is exempt from tefillin entirely
  • View 2: Shabbat IS a time, but intent is not required — the second pair fulfills nothing (no intent), so bal tosif does not apply
  • Physical basis: Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak — there is room on both head and arm for two pairs

🌉 Rabbi Yehuda: Upper Story, Bridge, and Open Alleyway

Upper story built atop two houses on opposite sides of a reshut ha-rabbim, or bridge resting on walls over a public thoroughfare
↓
Rabbi Yehuda permits carrying beneath them. Why?
↓
Rabba's Reading
By Torah law, two partitions suffice to create a private domain. The two supporting walls form a legal enclosure — no additional markers needed.
Rav Ashi's Reading (from 'furthermore')
Rabbi Yehuda's reason is gud asik — edge of roof descends and seals. The mishna's use of ve-od ('furthermore') for the alleyway ruling signals a distinct principle, proving the upper-story leniency rests on something else entirely.
↓
Furthermore (ve-od): Rabbi Yehuda permits an open-ended alleyway without side post or cross beam — the Rabbis prohibit
↓
If reason = two partitions
No need for 'furthermore' — the alleyway (two side-walls) would follow automatically from the same principle. The word is superfluous.
If reason = gud asik (Rav Ashi)
'Furthermore' is necessary — the alleyway ruling introduces a separate, additional leniency (open ends sealed by gud asik) distinct from the upper-story case.
