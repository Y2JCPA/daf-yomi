⚖️ Abaye vs. Rava: Is the Space Between Side Posts a Karmelit?

Rabbi Zakkai
Taught before Rabbi Yoḥanan: The area between the side posts and beneath the cross beam is a karmelit — carrying is forbidden there.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
"Exit and teach this outside!" — This ruling is entirely wrong and should not be taught.
Abaye
Rabbi Yoḥanan only rejected the ruling about the area between the side posts. The area beneath the cross beam is indeed a karmelit — it is an enclosed, undefined space.
Rava
Rabbi Yoḥanan rejected both. Proof from Rav Dimi: a place less than 4×4 handbreadths is exempt from all domain laws — mekom petur, not karmelit.
Core Dispute Does the space beneath a cross beam and between side posts constitute a karmelit (Abaye) or a mekom petur / permitted entrance (Rava)? The answer shapes the entire structure of alleyway eruvin law.

📐 Cross Beam: Drawn Away or Suspended — Lavud Analysis

Cross beam (קורה) is misplaced — drawn away from walls or suspended above ground level
↓
Less than 3 handbreadths from wall
Valid — lavud applies; beam is treated as if attached to the wall
Drawn away from both sides
Rav Ashi: even if distanced on both sides, lavud still applies — we do not require attachment from both directions
Two bent pegs on wall-tops
Rav Ashi's scenario: pegs hold beam away from walls and above ground simultaneously — both conditions met at once

🗣️ Rava vs. Abaye: Proof-Texts on the Side-Post Space

Rava
Proof from Rav Dimi (citing Rabbi Yoḥanan): a space less than 4×4 handbreadths is a mekom petur — exempt, not karmelit. The area between side posts is similarly permitted.
Abaye
Reject: Rav Dimi's case involves a space at least 3 handbreadths high — it stands apart as its own domain. The side-post space has no such height, so it is a karmelit.
Abaye
My proof: Rav (via Rav Ḥama bar Guria) — a doorway between a courtyard and a karmelit requires a side post. If the entrance were simply permitted, why would a side post be needed?
Rava
Counter: That ruling applies only when the alleyway opens to a karmelit. The entrance inherits karmelit status from the domain it faces — it is not intrinsically a karmelit.
Domain Inheritance An alleyway entrance opening to a karmelit becomes karmelit; opening to a public domain does not make it public — karmelit 'finds its own type' in undefined spaces.

👁️ Side Post Visibility: Inside vs. Outside the Alleyway

Visible from Inside Only

  • Post protrudes inward from the alleyway wall
  • Flush with the exterior of the wall
  • Valid side post — all agree
  • Clearly marks the alleyway entrance from within
  • Serves the functional purpose of defining the opening

Visible from Outside Only

  • Post protrudes outward from the alleyway wall
  • Flush with the interior of the wall
  • Not a valid side post — Rabbi Yoḥanan
  • Does not mark the entrance from within
  • Rabbi Ḥiyya: valid; Rabbi Yoḥanan: invalid — amoraic dispute

Rabbi Ḥiyya's Wall Case

  • Wall at entrance: one side recessed, other protruding
  • Creates a natural 'side post' shape in the wall
  • Rabbi Ḥiyya: taught this as valid
  • Rabbi Yoḥanan: heard this ruling and rejects it
  • Gemara: Rabbi Ḥiyya would not teach what he holds invalid

🏠 Breached Small Courtyard Within a Large Courtyard — Lavud Analysis

Setup: A small courtyard sits inside a large courtyard. The small courtyard's wall is breached by more than 10 cubits — rendering it unusable for carrying.
↓
Rabbi Zeira's key point: The walls of the small courtyard protrude into the large one — they are not flush with the large courtyard's walls. This matters for lavud.
↓
Question raised: Why doesn't lavud close the breach? If the broken wall's edges are near the large courtyard's side walls, shouldn't they be treated as joined?
↓
Ravina's resolution: The small courtyard walls are 2 handbreadths from one side of the large courtyard and 4 handbreadths from the other. Since 4 > 3, lavud cannot apply from both sides.
↓
Remaining question (unresolved): Why not apply lavud from just the one side with only 2 handbreadths? The Gemara ends on this open difficulty — the daf ends mid-analysis.
