🏛️ Shared Domains: Ruins, Roofs & Courtyards

Rav Huna
The ruin between two courtyards belongs to the courtyard that established an eiruv — only its residents may carry there.
Ḥiyya bar Rav
The ruin is accessible to both courtyards — since it resembles a domain open to all, neither courtyard restricts the other.
Mishna
Large roof adjacent to small: the large is permitted, the small is forbidden. Same for large/small courtyards.
Abaye
We have found a partition that causes prohibition! The wall between large and small courtyard forbids kilayim that would otherwise be permitted without it.
Core Principle A large domain asymmetrically 'extends into' an adjacent small domain — the small is subsumed within the large, with halakhic consequences for kilayim, gittin, prayer, minyan, and excrement.

🏚️ The Ruin Between Two Courtyards

Setup
Two courtyards share a ruin between them. One courtyard established an eiruv; the other did not. Who may carry in the ruin?
Rav Huna
The ruin is conferred to the eiruv courtyard. Its residents may carry there; the other courtyard's residents may not — and their presence prohibits even the eiruv courtyard from using it freely.
Ḥiyya bar Rav
The ruin belongs to the eiruv courtyard, and it is permitted for residents of both to carry there — a ruin is not like a courtyard, since there is no concern of bringing vessels out from houses into it.
Gemara
Why distinguish a courtyard from a ruin? In a courtyard, vessels stored in houses are also protected there — so we worry people will carry from house → courtyard → ruin. This concern does not apply to a ruin directly.
Two Versions The Gemara records two versions of this dispute — the positions of Rav Huna and Ḥiyya bar Rav are swapped between versions. Both versions turn on who is lenient and who is strict regarding the shared ruin.

⚖️ Rav vs Shmuel — Why Repeat Roofs and Courtyards?

Rav

  • The repetition teaches: roofs are like courtyards
  • Just as a courtyard's partitions are made for it, so too a roof's walls (the house walls below) are considered made for the roof above
  • Therefore: roofs have valid halakhic partitions and carrying is permitted on the large roof
  • Novel teaching: We extend the concept of 'walls made for the space' upward to rooftops

Shmuel

  • The repetition teaches: courtyards are like roofs
  • Just as a roof is not a place where multitudes regularly tread, so too a courtyard should be treated similarly for leniency
  • Therefore: the lenient rule for roofs (low foot-traffic = private-like) applies to courtyards too
  • Novel teaching: Shared foot traffic — not just partitions — determines a domain's status

🚧 Abaye's Challenge: מחיצה האוסרת — A Partition That Causes Prohibition

Abaye
We have found a partition that causes prohibition! Without the wall between large and small courtyard, they would be one domain — kilayim distance rules would apply uniformly. The wall creates the 'extension' relationship that forbids sowing in the small!
Rabbi Zeira
We already knew partitions can cause prohibition! If a large courtyard is breached into a small one, carrying is permitted in the large. But if you add partitions to even the protrusions — carrying in the large becomes forbidden!
Abaye
Those are incomparable! Adding walls there is effectively the removal of partitions — you eliminate the protrusions that allowed independent carrying. It is not a case of new walls creating prohibition; it is dismantling what permitted it.
Rava
And didn't we find yet another case of a partition that causes prohibition? (The daf continues with further examples…)
מחיצה האוסרת — Partition That Prohibits Normally, partitions define and permit carrying within a domain. This daf introduces the counter-intuitive concept that a partition can itself generate a prohibition — by establishing the spatial relationship (large over small) that triggers kilayim, Shema, or eiruv restrictions.
