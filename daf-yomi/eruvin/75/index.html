⚖️ The 'Foot' Dispute: Rabbi Akiva vs. the Rabbis

Rabbis
The foot of one who is permitted in his own place does not render it prohibited to carry in the other courtyard. If the inner courtyard has its own eiruv, its residents' passage through the outer does not restrict the outer.
Rabbi Akiva
Even the foot of one who is permitted in his own place does render it prohibited in the other courtyard. The inner residents' right of passage through the outer restricts carrying there — even with separate eiruvin.
Rav Dimi
Citing Rabbi Yannai: The case where only the outer makes an eiruv follows Rabbi Akiva. He teaches via 'not only this, but also that' — first the simpler case, then the stricter. But this version was later rejected.
Ravin
Rabbi Yannai actually described three separate tannaitic disputes in this matter. Rav Dimi's version was mistaken. The mishna reflects a single consistent opinion read as incomplete (חסורי מחסרא).
Core Question Does the right of passage through a courtyard restrict carrying there, even if that person has full eiruv rights in their own courtyard?

📜 Ravin's Three Disputes (Rabbi Yannai's Corrected Version)

Dispute 1 — First Tanna (standard view): The foot of one permitted in his place does not render it prohibited elsewhere. The foot of one prohibited in his place does render it prohibited in the other courtyard.
↓
Dispute 2 — Rabbi Akiva: Even the foot of one permitted in his own courtyard does render it prohibited in the adjoining one. Right of passage is itself the restricting factor — permission is no protection.
↓
Dispute 3 — Third Tanna: Even the foot of one prohibited in his own place does not render it prohibited in the other courtyard. Each courtyard is judged entirely independently.
↓
Gemara's Resolution: The entire mishna follows Rabbi Akiva and is read as חסורי מחסרא (incomplete) — the full teaching includes all sub-cases under his unified stringent view.
↓
Consequence for Individuals: A single resident has no partner to create shared-domain conflict, so individual courtyards require no eiruv at all — by all opinions.

🔢 Ten Houses Nested Within Each Other

10 houses nested sequentially: the innermost resident passes through all 9 outer houses to reach the courtyard.
Who must contribute to the eiruv?
↓
Shmuel
Only the innermost contributes for all others.
Each outer house = a 'gatehouse' for the one it contains. A gatehouse of an individual is exempt from eiruv.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Even the outermost must contribute.
The outer house is a gatehouse relative to the inner ones — but it is still a dwelling with its own resident and obligations.
↓
Underlying Principle: Does 'gatehouse of an individual' (בית שער של יחיד) cascade inward, exempting each successive house? Shmuel: yes. Rabbi Yoḥanan: no — each dwelling stands independently.

👤 Who Counts? Individuals, Gentiles, and Exceptions

Individual in Each Courtyard

  • One person per courtyard → no eiruv required
  • No partners = no shared domain conflict
  • Mishna explicitly exempts this case
  • Rav Beivai corrects Rav Yosef: the ruling about 3-in-inner + 1-in-outer came from Rav Adda bar Ahava, not Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi — Rav Yosef forgot due to illness

Gentile in Inner Courtyard

  • Rabbi Elazar: a gentile counts as 'many' (כרבים)
  • Gentile's passage through outer courtyard renders carrying there prohibited
  • Unlike a lone Jewish resident who can be discounted
  • Why? A gentile who rented his domain would publicize it — silence means no rental, so no workaround is possible

Shmuel's Specific Exception

  • Residents permitted unless: 2 people in inner + 1 in outer
  • The outer single resident cannot be ignored when the inner has multiple
  • Rav Naḥman (citing Rabba bar Avuh citing Rav): two courtyards with three houses between them — middle house serves as valid eiruv location for both
