Eruvin 75 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Mishna, if only the INNER courtyard established an eiruv and the outer did not, what is the result?
A. Both courtyards are prohibited for carrying
B. The inner courtyard is permitted and the outer is prohibited
C. Both courtyards are permitted
D. The outer is permitted and the inner is prohibited
Correct: B — When only the inner establishes an eiruv, the inner residents have secured their own domain. But the outer has no eiruv and no arrangement — it remains prohibited. The inner's eiruv does not extend outward.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is Rabbi Akiva's distinctive ruling when each courtyard has established its OWN separate eiruv?
A. Both courtyards are fully permitted — separate eiruvin are sufficient
B. The outer courtyard is still prohibited because the inner residents' foot of passage restricts it
C. The inner courtyard loses its eiruv status because of the outer
D. Neither eiruv is valid; they must combine into one
Correct: B — Rabbi Akiva holds that even the foot of one who is permitted in his own place renders it prohibited in the adjoining courtyard. The inner residents' right of passage through the outer restricts carrying there, regardless of their own eiruv.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Yehuda citing Rav, what is the meaning of 'one place' (eḥad) when the mishna says both courtyards placed their eiruv in one place?
A. The inner courtyard, since it is the single shared destination
B. A neutral third location outside both courtyards
C. The outer courtyard, which is designated (meyuḥad) — sharing a root with eḥad
D. The house of whichever resident forgot to contribute
Correct: C — Rav explains that 'one place' (eḥad) refers to the outer courtyard, which is the place designated (מְיֻחָד, meyuḥad) for the eiruv. The words eḥad and meyuḥad share the same Hebrew root (א-ח-ד).
QUESTION 4 OF 5
In the case of ten houses nested one within the other, what does Shmuel rule about eiruv contributions?
A. All ten residents must each contribute individually
B. Only the outermost resident contributes on behalf of everyone
C. Only the innermost resident contributes for all the rest
D. The five innermost contribute; the five outermost are exempt as gatehouses
Correct: C — Shmuel holds that each outer house functions as a gatehouse (בית שער) for the house within it, and a gatehouse of an individual is exempt from eiruv. This exemption cascades outward, leaving only the innermost resident obligated.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What does Rabbi Elazar rule about a gentile living in the inner courtyard, and why?
A. A gentile is like an individual — the outer courtyard remains exempt from eiruv
B. A gentile is irrelevant to eiruv law; only Jewish residents count
C. A gentile is like many people — the gentile's presence renders carrying in the outer courtyard prohibited
D. A gentile must personally sign the eiruv document to permit carrying
Correct: C — Rabbi Elazar rules that a gentile counts as 'many' (כרבים). A typical gentile who rented out his domain would publicize it; since he hasn't, everyone assumes no rental was made — preventing the usual workaround — and his presence restricts the outer courtyard.
