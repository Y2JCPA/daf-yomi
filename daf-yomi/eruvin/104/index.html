🧂 Salt on the Ramp: A Two-Pronged Dilemma

Rav Aḥa b. Rava
If he NEGATES the salt as part of the ramp — he adds to the Temple structure! The verse says the Temple must not be modified or added to.
Rav Aḥa b. Rava
But if he does NOT negate it — it interposes between the priests' feet and the altar, disqualifying the service entirely!
Gemara (resolution)
He does NOT negate it — and scatters it only between one limb and the next, when no actual service is being performed, so there is no interposition issue.
Gemara (challenge)
But isn't bringing limbs up the ramp itself a service? The verse says 'the priest shall offer it whole and make it smoke upon the altar'!
Gemara (final answer)
The salt is scattered in the gap between limbs — precisely when no limb is being carried. There is no service occurring at that moment, so no disqualification.
Conclusion Salt is permitted: it is not negated (no addition to Temple), and it is scattered between services (no interposition during an active service).

💧 Drawing Water by Wheel: From Temple to Meḥoza

Mishna: One may draw water from the Cistern of the Exiles and the Great Cistern by wheel (גַּלְגַּל) on Shabbat — but this is permitted only in the Temple.
↓
Question: Why is the wheel prohibited outside the Temple — is it because of the sound it produces (like a musical instrument)?
↓
Answer: No — it is a rabbinic decree lest one draw water for his garden or ruin (agricultural/construction labor, a Torah prohibition).
↓
Ameimar permitted wheel-drawing in Meḥoza: the city was surrounded by water, so there was no concern of drawing for agricultural purposes.
↓
However: When Ameimar saw people soaking flax in the drawn water — a prohibited purpose — he immediately revoked his permission.
↓
Lesson: Rabbinic leniencies are context-sensitive; a permission granted for one reason is revoked when that reason no longer holds or is being abused.

🎵 Producing Sounds on Shabbat: Rabba vs. Abaye

Ulla
May his bones be blasted! [A man is knocking on a door on Shabbat — producing a sound!]
Rabba
The Sages prohibited only a pleasant, musical sound on Shabbat — not the rasping knock of a door.
Abaye
Objection from a baraita: water drips are permitted only for an ill person — implying all sounds are generally forbidden!
Rabba
That case is an ill person who is awake and wants to sleep — the melodious dripping soothes him. It proves nothing about ordinary sounds.
Abaye
Second objection: A field-guard who shouts at birds is prohibited from doing so on Shabbat — proves any sound-making is forbidden!
Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov
That prohibition has nothing to do with sound! It is a decree lest he take the animal and carry it four cubits in a public domain — a Torah-level labor.
Ruling Only pleasant, melodious sounds are rabbinically forbidden on Shabbat. Rasping, knocking, and incidental sounds are permitted.

⚖️ Temple vs. Outside: Shabbat Activities Compared

In the Temple — Permitted

  • Scatter salt on the altar ramp (for safety)
  • Draw water by wheel from Temple cisterns
  • Draw water from the Heker Well on Festivals
  • Remove a sheretz (creeping animal carcass) from sacred areas
  • Reed used to bandage a priest's finger during service

Outside the Temple — Prohibited

  • Draw water by wheel (rabbinic decree: lest used for garden/ruin)
  • Scatter straw in courtyard in certain ways (debate)
  • Draw water by wheel in Meḥoza once misuse was observed
  • Women playing with nuts: decree lest they level holes in ground
  • Women playing with apples: also forbidden (leveling concern)

Rabbi Shimon's Limiting Principle

  • Wherever Sages permitted something — they gave you only 'from your own'
  • Means: only rabbinic prohibitions are waived for Temple service
  • Torah-level prohibitions are never overridden by such leniencies
  • The permission is bounded by the exact decree originally enacted

🦎 Removing a Sheretz from the Temple on Shabbat

A שֶׁרֶץ (sheretz) — carcass of a creeping animal — is found in the Temple on Shabbat
↓
It conveys ritual impurity — remaining there defiles the Temple; it must be removed
↓
Weekday
Remove from anywhere in the Temple — no Shabbat concern
Shabbat — Dispute
From where exactly must it be removed?
↓
Rabbi Shimon ben Nannas
Remove from area between the Entrance Hall and the Altar — priests congregate there
Rabbi Akiva
Remove from any place one incurs karet for entering in impurity (all sacred areas)
↓
Shmuel's ruling: Bringing an object defiled by sheretz into the Temple → liable. But bringing the sheretz itself → exempt, as it cannot make itself more impure
