🔍 Two Major Controversies of Eruvin 10

Question
Does a lechi (side post) count as valid if it protrudes on the outside of the alleyway wall but appears flush with the wall from the inside?
Final Ruling
Yes — it IS a valid lechi. Despite conflicting deductive proofs, Rabbi Ḥiyya's explicit baraita is authoritative.
Rav Dimi
In the leather-seat case, 2 small fingers of leather on each side plus 2 small fingers of hole = one handbreadth. Each side must independently have standing > breached.
Ravin
2 large fingers (thumbs) on each side plus 2 large fingers of hole = one handbreadth. One may combine the standing segments from both sides of the breach.
Abaye's Resolution They genuinely disagree. Rav Dimi requires standing > breached on each side independently; Ravin permits combining both sides. Their dispute centers on the case where breached exactly equals standing on each side.

🏛️ Is a Lechi Flush-from-Inside Valid?

Rabbi Zeira & Ravina
A lechi that protrudes outside but is flush with the wall inside IS valid. The courtyard baraita (10½ cubits) supports this reading.
Gemara Rejects
The comparison collapses either way! If such a lechi IS valid, the baraita is problematic: 10½ cubits either needs no reduction at all, or cannot be fixed by a single board.
Deduction
Conclude from the baraita that such a lechi is NOT valid — yet the Gemara immediately notes Rabbi Ḥiyya taught a baraita stating it IS valid.
Rav Yosef / Abaye
Abaye reminded Rav Yosef (who had forgotten due to illness): You yourself taught us three halakhot from Rav Huna — including that a lechi extending beyond the wall is fully valid.
Three Halakhot from Rav Huna (1) The area between the inner and outer lechi edges is prohibited; (2) lavud applies if the gap is ≤ 3 handbreadths; (3) a lechi visible outside but flush inside is a valid lechi.

📐 Reducing a Wide Alleyway Entrance

The Rule: An alleyway entrance wider than 10 cubits is not valid for carrying and must be reduced
↓
Levi's Baraita (20 cubits): Stick a reed at the center of the entrance — this creates two sub-alleyways, each under 10 cubits wide
↓
Shmuel citing Levi: Stand a board 10 handbreadths high × 4 cubits long lengthwise down the middle, forming two valid alleyways
↓
Rav Yehuda (15 cubits): Distance 2 cubits from one wall, then place a single board — the remaining opening is under 10 cubits
↓
Concern raised: Won't people abandon the main 10-cubit entrance and use the 2-cubit side entrance, negating the larger opening?
↓
Answer: No — unlike Rabbi Ami & Asi's side-breach case, the small side entrance here does not shorten one's path. People will not preferentially use it.

⚖️ Rav Dimi vs. Ravin — The Bathroom Seat Leather

Rav Dimi

  • Leather seat of a bathroom stool
  • 2 small fingers of leather on each side
  • 2 small fingers of hole in the center
  • Total = 6 small fingers = 1 handbreadth
  • Hole ≤ ⅓ of combined width
  • Each side must independently have standing > breached

Ravin

  • Same leather seat scenario
  • 2 large fingers (thumbs) on each side
  • 2 large fingers of hole in the center
  • Total = 6 large fingers = 1 handbreadth
  • Permits a larger hole
  • May combine standing segments from both sides of breach

Abaye's Proof

  • They genuinely disagree in principle
  • If same fingers: Ravin should say 1⅓ each side
  • If same principle: Dimi should say ⅔ each side
  • Neither matches — so they dispute the rule itself
  • Rav Dimi: breached must be < standing per side
  • Ravin: when breached = standing per side, it is breached

🔢 Standing vs. Breached — Core Principle

Tosefta (Kelim): The leather covering of a bathroom stool and its hole join together to complete a handbreadth for tumah purposes
↓
Question: How large can the hole be and still combine with the leather to form a valid handbreadth?
↓
Rav Dimi
2 small fingers each side + 2 small-finger hole = 6
Standing must exceed breached on each side independently
Ravin
2 large fingers each side + 2 large-finger hole = 6
Permits combining standing from both sides; larger hole allowed
↓
Abaye's Conclusion: The dispute is about a case where breached equals standing on each side — Rav Dimi rules 'standing'; Ravin rules 'breached'

🚪 Tzurat HaPetach — The Doorway-Shaped Entrance

צורת הפתח — שני זקיפים ומשקוף על גביהן — כשרה אפילו ברוחב יתר מעשר
Mishna Rule If the entrance to an alleyway is constructed in the form of a doorway — two upright posts (zekifim) with a horizontal beam (mashkof) spanning across the top — it is halachically valid even if the opening exceeds ten cubits in width. The structural form itself constitutes a complete halachic entrance, overriding the normal 10-cubit limitation. No additional reduction is required. This concludes the mishna's framework: width alone is not the only factor; halachic form matters.
