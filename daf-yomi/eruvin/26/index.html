⚖️ Ceding Rights: How Generous Is the Renunciation?

Rabbi Eliezer
One who cedes his share in the courtyard is presumed to also cede rights over his house — he renounces in a generous, expansive manner.
The Rabbis
Ceding courtyard rights does not extend to the house. One does not usually remove himself entirely from house and courtyard like a guest.
Rav Sheshet
Per Rabbi Eliezer: renouncing to one resident covers all — no need to renounce individually to each person in the courtyard.
Abaye
Once a person has revealed his wishes explicitly — broad or narrow — both sides agree: we follow his express statement.
Central Tension Does renouncing eiruv rights carry an expansive presumption (R. Eliezer), or must each act of renunciation be explicit and individual (Rabbis)?

🏙️ New Town vs Old Town — Shabbat Limits

New Town (חֲדָשָׁה)

  • Wall built first, residents arrived afterward
  • Shabbat limit measured from the wall itself
  • The entire walled area counts as the town boundary
  • Ravina raises this baraita against Rava's orchard ruling

Old Town (יְשָׁנָה)

  • Residents settled first, wall built later
  • Shabbat limit measured from the settled area
  • People define the town — the wall follows habitation
  • Standard case: measured from actual inhabited zone

🚧 Rava's Orchard — Three Challenges on Shabbat

Rava
The orchard is enclosed for residence due to the banqueting pavilion within it — the reed enclosure is therefore unnecessary.
Ravina
Challenge from baraita: A new town (wall before residents) is measured from the wall — suggesting that the purpose of enclosure matters for status.
Rav Pappa
Rav Asi ruled: Temporary screens built by architects for sun protection are not considered valid eiruv partitions.
Rav Huna b. R. Yehoshua
Rav Huna ruled: A partition made solely for resting objects alongside it — not for enclosure — is not a valid partition.
Exilarch's Rebuke (Jeremiah 4:22) "They are wise to do evil, but to do good they have no knowledge" — Rav Pappa and Rav Huna removed the reeds on Friday, dismantling Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana's valid eiruv arrangement.

וְשָׁמַעְתִּי מֵרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר — "And I heard from Rabbi Eliezer" (Rabbi Elai's repeated formula, 26a–b)
🌿 Two Rulings of Rabbi Eliezer via Rabbi Elai 1. Arkablin for Maror (Passover): One may fulfill the obligation to eat bitter herbs with arkablin — a prickly, thorny plant (likely wild artichoke or Scolymus). Valid despite being an unusual choice.

2. Torah Scholar Who Falls Ill: Based on Isaiah going to Hezekiah (II Kings 20:4) — one establishes an academy at the entrance to the scholar's home so he may teach from his sickbed.

⚠️ Caution: The Gemara warns this may provoke Satan — better to visit individually than as a large group gathering conspicuously.
