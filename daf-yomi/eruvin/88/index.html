🪣 Balcony Hole: Drawing Water vs. Pouring Waste

Rabba bar Rav Huna
Partition leniencies for a balcony hole apply only to drawing water up. Pouring waste water down through the hole is prohibited — that water will certainly flow into the public domain.
Rav Sheizvi (objection)
What distinguishes the balcony hole from a courtyard pit? The Sages permit pouring waste into a courtyard pit — why not through the balcony hole?
Gemara (answer)
Courtyard water may be absorbed into the ground — there is uncertainty it will flow out. Balcony water poured through the hole will certainly reach the public domain below.
Some Say (alternate version)
Don't say only drawing water is permitted while pouring is prohibited. Rather, pouring waste water through the hole is also permitted — just as drawing water is, so too pouring waste.
Two Versions Rabba bar Rav Huna's ruling exists in two contradictory forms: the first restricts the balcony hole to drawing only; the second permits pouring waste water through it as well.

📐 Why Does 4×4 Cubits Exempt a Courtyard?

Rabba — Sprinkling Theory

  • A person uses 2 se'a of water per day
  • A 4×4 courtyard is large enough to sprinkle water across
  • The water stays in the yard and is useful — it does not flow out
  • Less than 4×4 = not fit for sprinkling → water exits
  • Long narrow court (e.g., 1×16 cubits): same area → permitted per Rabba

Rabbi Zeira — Absorption Theory

  • In a 4×4 cubit courtyard, waste water is absorbed into the ground
  • Less than 4×4 = insufficient area for absorption → water flows out
  • Long narrow court (1×16 cubits): wrong shape for absorption → prohibited per Rabbi Zeira
  • Explains Mishna: courtyard + portico combine for the required width
  • Unattributed Mishna follows Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov per this view

🏛️ Returning Stolen Shabbat Domains

דין גזל נוהג בשבת — חורבה חוזרת לבעליה
Ruling of Rabbi Ḥiyya & Rabbi Oshaya The halakha of returning stolen property (גזל) applies to Shabbat domains. An abandoned ruin retains its original owner's domain status and must be returned — neighbors may not simply annex it into their own courtyard domain for Shabbat purposes.

Result: Even if a ruin appears ownerless, it counts as an independent domain and two-balcony residents sharing its airspace cannot ignore it. Domain boundaries are property rights — they do not dissolve on Shabbat.
