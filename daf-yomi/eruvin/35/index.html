🪵 Wooden Cupboard: May It Be Broken Open on Shabbat?

First Tanna
A wooden cupboard is a utensil — it may be broken open on Shabbat to retrieve the eiruv. Utensils may be handled freely.
Rabbi Eliezer
A wooden cupboard is like a building — it may not be broken open. The eiruv remains inaccessible and is therefore invalid.
Rabba & Rav Yosef
Their dispute parallels the tanna'im on zav impurity: is a carriage/crate/cupboard a utensil? If a utensil — R. Neḥemya and R. Shimon say it contracts impurity; if not — it does not.
Abaye
Refutation of the zav analogy: all agree direct force of a zav causes impurity whether it is a tent or utensil. The real dispute is different — it concerns a lock tied with a leather strap.
Abaye & Rava
The lock requires a knife to cut. First Tanna: follows R. Yosei — all utensils may be moved on Shabbat, so take the knife and cut. R. Eliezer: follows R. Neḥemya — utensils only for their designated purpose.
Underlying Principle The debate reduces to: may one use a knife (muktzeh) on Shabbat to cut a strap in order to access one's eiruv? R. Yosei permits moving any utensil; R. Neḥemya restricts to designated use only.

❓ Doubtful Eiruv: Two Positions

Rabbi Meir & Rabbi Yehuda — Stringent

  • Doubtful eiruv is INVALID
  • Person belongs nowhere — like one prodded from behind and in front
  • "Donkey driver from behind, camel driver from front"
  • Shabbat limits = Torah law (d'oraita)
  • Doubt in Torah law → rule strictly

Rabbi Yosei & Rabbi Shimon — Lenient

  • Doubtful eiruv is VALID
  • Uncertainty alone does not uproot the eiruv
  • Avtolemos testified: five Elders ruled leniently
  • Rabbinic enactment → lenient in doubt
  • Follow the permissive ruling in rabbinic uncertainty

📜 Are Shabbat Limits Torah Law? — Gemara's Investigation

תחומין דאורייתא — ספק בדרבנן לקולא
The Contradiction in Rabbi Meir's Position Challenge: R. Meir rules strictly on doubtful eiruv (implying d'oraita), yet another mishna permits piercing mountains when measuring Shabbat boundaries — a leniency inconsistent with Torah-level law.

Rav Naḥman (citing Rabba bar Avuh): We may NOT pierce mountains for Torah-level measurements. So why does the mishna allow it?

Resolution: The lenient measurement ruling belongs to R. Meir's teacher, not R. Meir himself. R. Meir consistently holds Shabbat limits are d'oraita and rules strictly.

Further complexity: Rabba and Rav Yosef add a new category — doubt arising from conflicting testimony (two sets of witnesses disagreeing), not factual uncertainty. This may follow different rules entirely.
