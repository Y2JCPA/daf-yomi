QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava, what is the minimum requirement for a dragging door/mat/lattice to be used on Shabbat?
A. It must have a functioning hinge currently attached
B. It must have once had a hinge and be suspended even a hairbreadth above the ground
C. It must be made of wood, not reeds or rushes
D. It must be permanently tied to the doorpost with a knot
Correct: B — Rava holds that prior hinge-use establishes the object's door-status, and any suspension above the ground — even a hairbreadth — demonstrates it is not being newly built. Together these conditions permit its use on Shabbat.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Rabbi Meir rules that one may not stand in a private domain and open a door in the public domain. What workaround does he permit?
A. Asking a non-Jew to open the door instead
B. Opening the door with one's foot rather than a key
C. Erecting a partition ten handbreadths high around the door and standing inside it
D. Placing the key on an indoor shelf before Shabbat begins
Correct: C — Rabbi Meir permits erecting a partition of at least 10 handbreadths around the door, creating a private-domain enclosure around the lock and eliminating the cross-domain transfer concern.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rav Pappa explain why the Rabbis cited the Jerusalem poultry dealers as a counter-example to Rabbi Meir?
A. Jerusalem had eruv walls making the entire city a single private domain
B. Jerusalem was a karmelit before its walls were breached, so the poultry dealers faced only a rabbinic concern, not Rabbi Meir's Torah-level public domain concern
C. The poultry dealers had special rabbinic permission to handle keys for commercial purposes
D. Rabbi Meir's ruling applied only outside Jerusalem's city limits
Correct: B — Rav Pappa explains that pre-breach Jerusalem was a karmelit, not a full reshut ha-rabbim. The poultry dealers' case therefore involved only a rabbinic domain concern — not the Torah-level public domain issue Rabbi Meir was ruling on. The cases are incomparable.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
On what point does the Gemara say all tanna'im agree regarding the bolt with a gelustera knob?
A. Any bolt with a decorative knob automatically has vessel status and may be used
B. A bolt that can be maneuvered by the rope attaching it to the door is considered part of the door and may be used
C. The gelustera knob must be at least four handbreadths wide to qualify as a vessel
D. All agree the bolt is prohibited regardless of circumstances
Correct: C — The Gemara clarifies that when the bolt can be moved using its attached rope, everyone agrees it is part of the door itself and may be used on Shabbat. The dispute between Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Eliezer applies only when this rope-maneuverability is absent.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What does the Gemara infer from the word 'similarly' (וכן) introducing the clause about stores in the baraita on 101b?
A. That stores must also have a gatehouse to permit opening on Shabbat
B. That the threshold mentioned must be a karmelit, since otherwise any key transfer would be forbidden regardless of lock height
C. That stores in Jerusalem were exempt from the lock-height rules
D. That Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis agree about stores but disagree only about garden gates
Correct: B — The word 'similarly' ties the stores case to the prior garden-gate case where the threshold was a karmelit. If the threshold were a full public domain, key transfer would be forbidden at any lock height. The inference confirms the threshold has karmelit status.
