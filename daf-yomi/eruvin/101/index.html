⚖️ Abaye vs Rava — What Makes Suspension Permit the Door?

Abaye
The baraita permits only doors that have an actual hinge. A hinge marks it as a proper door — closing it therefore does not resemble building.
Rava
The baraita permits even doors that once had a hinge but no longer do. Being suspended above the ground — even a hairbreadth — demonstrates it is not being newly constructed.
Abaye
The second baraita ('held above the ground even a hairbreadth') also refers to a hinged door — it merely specifies the minimum required suspension height for a hinged door.
Rava
No — the second baraita specifically teaches: even without a current hinge, mere suspension above the ground suffices if the door once had a hinge. Prior hinge-use classifies it as a door.
מחלוקת Both agree that a door resting freely on the ground is prohibited. The dispute: does permissibility require an active hinge (Abaye), or does prior hinge-use plus any suspension suffice (Rava)?

🏙️ Why Did Jerusalem's Example Counter Rabbi Meir?

Rav Pappa

  • Jerusalem was a karmelit before its walls were breached
  • Locked doors prevented it from qualifying as a true public domain (רשות הרבים)
  • The poultry dealers' case involved a karmelit — only a rabbinic concern, not the Torah-level issue Rabbi Meir addressed
  • Therefore the cases are not comparable: Rabbi Meir spoke of true reshut ha-rabbim

Rava

  • The Rabbis' case is a separate topic — not a rebuttal to Rabbi Meir
  • The final clause of the mishna addresses garden gates with adjacent open space
  • It discusses a different scenario involving karmelit thresholds and varying lock heights
  • The Rabbis were not contradicting Rabbi Meir but raising a new halakhic context

🔩 The Gelustera Bolt — Vessel or Door Component?

The object: A door-bolt (בריח) with a thick knob — גלוסטרא (gelustera) — at its end, capable of serving as a useful implement beyond merely securing the door
↓
The halakhic question: If the bolt qualifies as a vessel (כלי), it may be used to close the door on Shabbat. If it is mere construction material, using it constitutes a building-like act and is prohibited.
↓
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi (Rebbe): Permitted — the gelustera knob grants it vessel status. Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda: Prohibited — the knob does not sufficiently transform it into a vessel.
↓
Conflicting incidents: Rabbi Eliezer says Rabban Gamliel came to the Tiberias synagogue and prohibited the bolt. Rabbi Yosei says Rabban Gamliel came and permitted it — the traditions are reversed.
↓
Gemara's clarification: If the bolt can be maneuvered by the rope attaching it to the door, all agree it is considered part of the door and may be used. The tanna'im dispute applies only when it cannot be so moved.
