Eruvin 76 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
In Raḥava's test, why did neither resident acquire his eiruv?
A. The houses were too far from each courtyard to serve as valid eiruv locations
B. An eiruv in a gatehouse is invalid, and each middle house functions as a gatehouse to one courtyard
C. Both residents needed to place their eiruv together in the same location
D. The eiruv was placed after Shabbat had already begun
Correct: B — The Sages ruled that each middle house serves as a gatehouse to one of the courtyards — and an eiruv placed in a gatehouse, porch, or balcony (beit sha'ar, achsadra, mirpeset) is invalid
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to the Mishna, what TWO conditions must a window between courtyards meet to allow a combined eiruv?
A. Must be round and at least 3 handbreadths in circumference
B. Must be at least 4×4 handbreadths AND within 10 handbreadths of the ground
C. Must be at least 10×10 handbreadths and positioned at ground level
D. Must be visible from both courtyards and permanently open
Correct: B — The Mishna requires the window to be at least four by four handbreadths in size AND positioned within ten handbreadths of the ground — both conditions are needed
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What circumference did Rabbi Yoḥanan require for a circular window to be valid?
A. 12 handbreadths
B. 16 handbreadths
C. 24 handbreadths
D. 32 handbreadths
Correct: C — Rabbi Yoḥanan required a circumference of 24 handbreadths (with 2 and a bit within 10 handbreadths of the ground), so that an inscribed square inside the circle would measure the required 4×4 handbreadths
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Whose opinion does Rabbi Yoḥanan's circular window calculation follow, according to the Gemara?
A. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who requires lavud for gaps under 4 handbreadths
B. Rav Naḥman, who rules stringently on window measurements
C. The Sages (or judges) of Caesarea, who hold a specific ratio for circle-square geometry
D. Abaye, who derived the measurement from the laws of sukkah
Correct: C — The Gemara explains that Rabbi Yoḥanan ruled in accordance with the Sages of Caesarea (some say: judges of Caesarea), who held that a circle inscribed in a square is smaller by one quarter — requiring a larger circumference
QUESTION 5 OF 5
If a wall between two courtyards is breached, when may the residents establish ONE combined eiruv?
A. Only if the entire wall collapses
B. If the breach is up to ten cubits wide (like a standard entrance)
C. If the breach is more than ten cubits wide
D. Only if both courtyards were already sharing an eiruv before the breach
Correct: B — The Mishna rules that a breach up to ten cubits is treated like an entrance (פֶּתַח), permitting one combined eiruv. More than ten cubits requires two separate eiruvin
