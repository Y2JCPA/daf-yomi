🤔 Raḥava Tests the Sages: Eiruv in the Middle House

Raḥava
Two courtyards, two houses between them — resident of Courtyard A places eiruv in the far house; resident of Courtyard B does the same from his side. Do both acquire their eiruv?
Sages
Neither acquires! Whichever way you look at it, each house is a gatehouse — and an eiruv placed in a gatehouse, porch, or balcony is invalid (eiruvo einenu eiruv).
Raḥava
Compare to Rava's ruling: one agent establishes eiruv for two people, completes the first at twilight (doubt day/night) — the first is valid. Why isn't one valid here similarly?
Sages
Not comparable! In Rava's case the uncertainty is invisible — no one sees exactly when each was placed. Here, the house openly and visibly serves both courtyards — the conflict is obvious and disqualifies both eiruvin.
📜 Ruling Neither eiruv is acquired — the middle house cannot simultaneously be a valid placement site for both courtyards

🪟 Window Between Courtyards: Valid vs. Invalid

✅ Valid Window

  • At least 4×4 handbreadths in size
  • Within 10 handbreadths of the ground
  • Even partially within 10 handbreadths suffices
  • Residents may establish ONE joint eiruv
  • Or TWO separate eiruvin — their choice

❌ Invalid Window

  • Less than 4×4 handbreadths in size
  • Entirely above 10 handbreadths from the ground
  • Must establish TWO separate eiruvin
  • Cannot combine into one eiruv
  • Treated as two fully separate domains

⭕ Rabbi Yoḥanan: The Circular Window

חַלּוֹן עָגוֹל — צָרִיךְ שֶׁיְּהֵא בְּהֶקֵּפוֹ עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה טְפָחִים
Geometric Ruling A circular window must have a circumference of 24 handbreadths, with at least 2 and a bit of them within 10 handbreadths of the ground.

Why 24? So that when you inscribe a square inside the circle, that square measures at least 4×4 handbreadths — the minimum valid window size.

Base ratio used: any circle with circumference of 3 = diameter of 1 (i.e., π ≈ 3)

The Gemara questions this: shouldn't a circumference of ~16–18 suffice? Rabbi Yoḥanan follows the Sages of Caesarea, who hold that inscribing a square inside a circle requires a larger circle than naive ratios suggest.

ClaimReasoningImplied Circumference Needed
π ≈ 3 (base rule)Circumference ÷ 3 = diameter; diameter of 8 → inscribed square ≈ 5.6~24 (Rabbi Yoḥanan)
Square is larger than circle by ¼Circle circumscribed by square: circle is ¼ smaller than square~16 should suffice
Diagonal of 4×4 squareDiagonal = 5⅗ handbreadths = circle diameter needed; circumference = ~17~17 handbreadths
Sages of CaesareaSquare inscribed in circle is smaller by ¼; circle needs to be larger by a third~24 handbreadths
Final rulingRabbi Yoḥanan follows Sages of Caesarea — generous ratio accounts for inscribed square geometry24 handbreadths required

🧱 Wall Between Two Courtyards (Mishna)

Wall between two courtyards
10 handbreadths high, 4 handbreadths wide
↓
Default: two separate eiruvin required — cannot establish one joint eiruv
↓
Wall < 4 wide?
Rav: Air of both domains controls it — top of wall is shared airspace between the two courtyards
Produce on top?
Each side may reach up and eat — but may not lower produce down into their domain
Wall breached?
Up to 10 cubits: may combine (like an entrance). More than 10 cubits: must maintain two eiruvin
