QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava, what does Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's phrase 'six handbreadths' for kilayim cubits come to EXCLUDE?
A. The standard sukka cubit of five handbreadths
B. The altar's special cubit of seven handbreadths (cubit + handbreadth per Ezekiel)
C. The Tabernacle cubit used by R. Yehuda
D. The medium cubit favored by R. Meir
Correct: B — Rava explains that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel specifies 'six handbreadths' to exclude the altar's cubit described in Ezekiel (43:13): 'the cubit is a cubit and a handbreadth' — i.e., seven handbreadths. He is not disputing the first tanna about kilayim vs. sukka cubits.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Which of the Seven Species establishes the minimum measure of food for liability when carrying on Shabbat?
A. Wheat — a half-loaf's bulk
B. Barley — a barley-grain bulk
C. Pomegranate — a pomegranate-bulk
D. Fig — a dried fig-bulk (k'grogeret)
Correct: D — The fig alludes to the measure of a dried fig-bulk (k'grogeret). One who carries food on Shabbat is only liable if they carry at least this amount of food fit for human consumption.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yitzḥak, when does an interposition invalidate a ritual immersion by TORAH LAW?
A. Whenever it covers any part of the body, regardless of size
B. Only when it covers a minority of the body and one is particular about it
C. Only when it covers the majority of the body AND one is particular about removing it
D. Whenever it covers the majority of the body, even if one doesn't care about it
Correct: C — By Torah law, two conditions must both be met: the interposition must cover the majority of the body, AND the person must be 'particular' (care about having it removed). When only one condition is met, any invalidity is rabbinic or from the Sinai tradition.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does the Gemara derive the 10-handbreadth minimum height for a valid halachic partition?
A. From the ten curtains of the Mishkan's Tabernacle
B. From the Holy Ark (9 handbreadths tall) plus its cover (1 handbreadth), where the Shekhinah rested
C. From the walls of the Temple Courtyard, measured at ten handbreadths
D. From Ezekiel's description of the altar base and surrounding ledge
Correct: B — The Ark was 1.5 cubits tall. Using a 6-handbreadth cubit: 1.5 × 6 = 9 handbreadths for the Ark body, plus 1 handbreadth for the cover = 10 handbreadths total. Since the Shekhinah spoke from between the cherubim atop the cover, 10 handbreadths is the minimum for a meaningful partition.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
When the alleyway korah is too high and one raises the ground beneath it, what is the underlying dispute about the minimum width of the platform?
A. Whether 4 cubits or 2 cubits is a proper space for an alleyway
B. Whether the korah must be made of wood or can be any material
C. Whether the space beneath the korah is 'used' — if yes, 4×4 handbreadths; if no, 1 handbreadth suffices
D. Whether the platform must be accessible from both sides of the alleyway
Correct: C — The dispute turns on how the korah functions: if we 'use' the space under it (treating the korah as the top of a wall), the platform needs a meaningful 4×4 handbreadth area. If the korah is a purely symbolic marker and its airspace is not used, only 1 handbreadth of width is needed to render the alleyway valid.
