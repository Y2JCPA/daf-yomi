🌳 Rabba vs. Rav Sheshet: Roots That Rise and Bend Back Down

Rabba
Permitted. Anything within 3 handbreadths of the ground is considered as the ground itself. These roots, though they rose above, bend back down into that zone — they revert to ground status.
Rav Sheshet
Prohibited. Since these roots come from above — they originate as part of the tree above ground — they retain the status of the tree regardless of how far they ultimately descend.
Point of Agreement Ascending roots (rising upward): prohibited by all. Roots that come straight down to ground level: permitted by all. The dispute is only about roots that rise AND then bend back down.

🌿 Walking on Grass on Shabbat — Contradicting Baraitot

Baraita (permits)
Permitted to walk on grass on Shabbat. No melachah is violated.
Baraita (prohibits)
Prohibited — "He who hastens with his feet sins" (Proverbs 19:2). Even walking can involve transgression.
Four-Part Resolution 1. Dry grass (permit) vs. green grass (prohibit) 2. Winter (permit) vs. summer when seeds present (prohibit) 3. With shoes (permit) vs. barefoot (prohibit) 4. Plain sole (permit) vs. spiked sole that catches seeds (prohibit).

Final ruling: Since we follow Rabbi Shimon — no liability for an unintentional prohibited act performed during a permitted act — walking on grass is now generally permitted.

🌲 Climbing Trees on Shabbat: Green vs. Dry

Green Tree

  • Prohibited to climb — rabbinic decree
  • Concern: may snap a branch (detaching = melacha)
  • Cannot climb up Friday and remain on Shabbat
  • If climbed unwittingly — may descend (per one baraita)
  • Dispute: penalize unwitting offender due to intentional one?

Dry Tree (sends new shoots)

  • Still prohibited — not truly 'dead'
  • Produces new growth when cut; has vitality
  • 'You call this dry?' — not considered dry at all
  • Included in the climbing prohibition

Dry Tree (no new shoots ever)

  • Winter: permitted (no fruit to pick or dislodge)
  • Summer: concern that old fruit will fall off
  • Rav prohibited even stripped/branch-bare tree at Apsetaya
  • Gemara: Rav was extra-strict as a fence, not strict law

💑 Conjugal Relations — Halakhic and Ethical Principles

Rami bar Ḥama / Rav Asi: A man may not force his wife — derived from "he who hastens with his feet sins" (Proverbs 19:2).
↓
Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: One who forces his wife will have unworthy children — as written: "Without knowledge the soul is not good" (Proverbs 19:2).
↓
Rava: One who wants all children to be male should have relations and repeat the act — but only when the wife consents and desires.
↓
Rav Shmuel bar Naḥmani / Rabbi Yoḥanan: A woman who demands the conjugal mitzva merits sons of surpassing greatness — even greater than Moses's generation.
↓
Proof from Leah (Genesis 30:16): "You must come in to me" — her reward was Issachar, the tribe of Torah scholars.
↓
Resolution with Eve's curse: A woman should not voice her desire explicitly (tzniut). Rather, she should make herself pleasing to him — and he will understand. This is the praiseworthy 'demand.'
