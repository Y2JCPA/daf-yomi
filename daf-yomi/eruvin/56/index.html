🌅 Orienting the Eruv: Rabbi Yosei vs. Rav Mesharshiya

Rabbi Yosei
If you cannot identify the cardinal directions by constellations, use the seasons: observe where the sun rises in Nisan (spring equinox) or sets in Tishrei (fall equinox). These mark true east and west, establishing all four directions.
Rav Mesharshiya
Rabbi Yosei's method has no validity! The sun never rises precisely at the northeast corner in summer nor at the southeast corner in winter — so the sun's path cannot reliably indicate the true four cardinal directions.
Rabbi Yosei (scriptural support)
As Scripture states (Kohelet 1:5–6): 'The sun rises and the sun sets… it goes toward the south and turns about to the north; the wind goes to the south and turns to the north.' The celestial movements mark the four directions.
Stakes Without knowing true north, south, east, and west, you cannot properly square a city or draw a valid eruv boundary — the entire halachic measurement depends on accurate orientation.

📏 Step-by-Step: Squaring a City's Eruv Boundary

Establish orientation: Determine the four cardinal directions using constellations or the equinox sunrise/sunset (Rabbi Yosei's method)
↓
Identify the widest points: Find the city's outermost edges in all four directions, including any irregular protrusions
↓
Square the city: Draw lines connecting the extreme points to form a rectangle or square aligned to north-south-east-west — this becomes the 'city' for eruv purposes
↓
Measure outward from sides: Add 2000 cubits perpendicular from each of the four sides of the squared city — do not measure diagonally from the corners
↓
Square the outer boundary: Connect the outer measurement points to form a larger square — the projecting corners add ~400 extra cubits each, protecting residents at the city's edges

⚪ Why Squaring Beats Diagonal Measurement

❌ Diagonal from Corner

  • Measure exactly 2000 cubits from each corner at a 45° diagonal
  • The boundary curves inward between corners
  • Residents near city edges lose hundreds of cubits
  • A 2000-cubit round city loses its full corner projection
  • Geometrically unfair — shorter reach at the corners

✓ Squaring the Boundary

  • Project a square corner at each of the four corners
  • City gains ~400 cubits in each corner projection
  • For a round city of 2000-cubit diameter: +800 cubits total (two opposite corners)
  • Every resident receives the full 2000-cubit Shabbat range
  • Abaye confirms the math works precisely for a 2000-cubit round city

🏘️ Levite Cities: The ¼ Geometry Proof

״וּמִגְרְשֵׁי הֶעָרִים אֲשֶׁר תִּתְּנוּ לַלְוִיִּם מִקִּיר הָעִיר וָחוּצָה אֶלֶף אַמָּה סָבִיב״ — במדבר לה:ד
Rava citing Bar Adda the Surveyor (בַּר אַדָּא אַמְּצָרָא) Setup: Each Levite city had a round city surrounded by 1000 cubits of migrash (open space), and the verse calls this open space a specific fraction of the total boundary.

The calculation: The squared outer boundary = 32 million sq. cubits. The open space ring = 12 million sq. cubits. But since the open space is circular and the outer boundary is square, subtract ¼ (the geometric difference between a circle and its enclosing square): 12M × ¾ = 9 million sq. cubits.

The ratio: City area (4M) + total boundary (32M) = 36M. And 9/36 = ¼ exactly. The Torah's proportions are geometrically precise.

Abaye confirms the same ¼ ratio holds for a city of 1000 × 1000 cubits.
