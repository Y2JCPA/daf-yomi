📜 Rabbi Yoḥanan's Hermeneutical Principle

אֵין לְמֵידִין מִן הַכְּלָלוֹת, וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר בּוֹ חוּץ
Core Rule One may not learn from general statements (כּוֹל) in a mishna, even where the mishna itself says 'except.' Every stated 'all' has further unnamed exceptions. Three proofs cited: • Mishna on women and time-bound commandments — matza, rejoicing on Yom Tov, and assembly (הקהל) are exceptions in both directions • Mishna on zav defilement — 'anything carried upon a zav is impure' has unaddressed edge cases • Mishna on eiruv foods — 'all foods, except water and salt' still excludes more than stated

🧂 Water & Salt: Two Contexts, One Parallel Exclusion

Eiruv (עֵרוּב)

  • All foods may establish an eiruv
  • Excluded: water or salt alone
  • Water + salt together? → Disputed
  • Rabbi Eliezer: Together they do qualify for eiruv
  • Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina: Together they do not qualify

Second Tithe (מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי)

  • Second-tithe money may purchase all food
  • Excluded: water or salt alone
  • Water + salt mixed? → Same dispute
  • Rav Yosef: Only permitted if oil is added
  • Abaye: Then derive permissibility from oil alone!

⚖️ Resolving Rabbi Yitzḥak's Ruling on the Brine Mixture

Rabbi Yitzḥak
Water and salt mixed together may be purchased with second-tithe money.
Baraita Objection
Rabbi Yehuda ben Gadish testified: 'In Father's house they would salt meat in brine' — implying brine cannot normally be bought with second-tithe money.
Rav Yosef
Rabbi Yitzḥak spoke only of a case where oil was added to the mixture. Water and salt alone remain excluded from second-tithe purchase.
Abaye
If so, the permissibility should be derived from the oil alone — why mention the water and salt at all?
Gemara
The case is where water and salt were paid for by being included in the price of the oil. A forbidden item included in payment for a permitted item is allowed — as ben Bag Bag teaches from Deut. 14:26.
Principle An item that cannot itself be purchased with second-tithe money may be acquired by including it in the payment for a permitted item (based on Deut. 14:26).

🐂 Ben Bag Bag and the 'On Oxen' Problem (Deut. 14:26)

Deuteronomy 14:26
"You shall bestow the money on… cattle, sheep, wine, strong drink, and on oxen"
— What does each word uniquely teach?
↓
Ben Bag Bag: Each word permits buying that item together with its accessory using second-tithe money: sheep + fleece, wine + jug, ox + hide
↓
Rabbi Yoḥanan's Challenge: 'On oxen' is entirely superfluous! If a sheep may be bought with its fleece, certainly an ox may be bought with its hide — a fortiori!
↓
Each Word IS Necessary
Strong drink → dried figs (novel food type)
Wine → jug (preservation, not attached)
Sheep → fleece (attached but detachable)
Each case adds something the previous doesn't cover
'On Oxen' Still Superfluous
Once sheep+fleece is permitted, ox+hide follows automatically. Rabbi Yoḥanan: 'Whoever explains this to me, I will carry his clothes to the bathhouse!' — no satisfactory answer found

📋 Eruvin 27 — Summary Map

Rabbi Yoḥanan's Rule: Every כּוֹל ('all') in a mishna has exceptions beyond those stated — even with an explicit 'except' clause
↓
Applied to Eiruv & Second Tithe: Water and salt alone are excluded; mixtures require oil (Rav Yosef); included items may piggyback on permitted ones (ben Bag Bag)
↓
Ben Bag Bag / 'On Oxen': Accessories (hide, fleece, jug) may be purchased alongside their item; Rabbi Yoḥanan finds 'on oxen' unexplainably redundant
Interpretation Methods: Ribbui/miyut includes fish brine broadly; klal/prat restricts to earth-grown produce — Abaye: fish excluded since they don't come from the ground
