QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does a balcony under 10 handbreadths high not restrict courtyard residents even when a 4-handbreadth partition is present?
A. A 4-handbreadth partition always creates an independent domain regardless of height
B. Under 10 handbreadths the balcony is considered part of the courtyard, not an independent domain
C. The balcony residents are presumed to have made a joint eiruv by default
D. Shmuel ruled that ladders eliminate any height requirement for partitions
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that a balcony under 10 handbreadths is not a domain of its own — it is halakhically part of the courtyard. The partition works only via lavud, not as a full domain separator.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is Shmuel's ruling about a wall that has been lined with ladders?
A. It loses partition status if the ladders span more than 10 cubits
B. Ladders are treated as doorways and invalidate the partition entirely
C. The wall retains its partition status even if ladders line it for more than 10 cubits
D. Ladders are valid only if each one is less than 4 handbreadths wide
Correct: C — Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel: a wall lined with ladders — even over more than 10 cubits — still has the status of a partition. The ladders do not constitute a prohibited opening that would invalidate it.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How many times did Abaye retract his ruling while attempting to establish an eiruv for Kakunya?
A. Two
B. Three
C. Four
D. Five
Correct: D — The Gemara records five successive retractions by Abaye, driven by the mishna's exclusion rule, the Meḥoza precedent, the roof connection problem, the windows alternative, and finally the Pumbedita storehouse case.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the halakhic consequence of placing one's eiruv even one cubit outside the city boundary?
A. The eiruv is invalid since it must be placed inside the city
B. He gains a full 2000 cubits in both directions from the eiruv location
C. What he gains in distance toward his eiruv he loses on the opposite side of the city
D. The eiruv is treated as if it were placed within the city outskirts
Correct: C — The mishna rules: what he gains on one side he loses on the other. His 2000 cubits are now measured from the eiruv, not the city edge — creating a directional shift with no net gain.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rabbi Idi citing Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, what happens when one's 2000-cubit Shabbat limit terminates in the middle of a city?
A. The person may only walk to the exact point where the 2000 cubits ended
B. The person receives the entire city and may walk through all of it
C. The person must remain outside the city and count it within the 2000 cubits
D. The person may enter the city but must count every cubit walked within it
Correct: B — Rabbi Idi: if the 2000-cubit measure terminated anywhere within the city, the person receives the entire city — treated as only 4 cubits, a rabbinic gift allowing full traversal of the city.
