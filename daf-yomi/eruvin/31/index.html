⚖️ Rabbi Yehuda vs. Sages: Eiruv with Inaccessible Items

Rabbi Yehuda
One may establish an eiruv for a pure priest using teruma resting on a grave — because mitzvot were not given for personal benefit, the item's practical usability is irrelevant.
Sages
One may not establish an eiruv with items from which benefit is prohibited. The food must be practically accessible to constitute a legal 'residence.'
Rava
Mitzvot were not given for benefit is a universal principle — my ruling (re: Shofar) applies even where the Sages would agree, so it is not a minority view.
Rav Yosef
One may establish eiruv only for the sake of a mitzva — all agree on this requirement. The dispute is only whether physical inaccessibility alone disqualifies the eiruv.
Resolution Three universal principles stand: eiruv must serve a mitzva purpose; mitzvot yield no personal benefit; the tannaitic dispute is narrowly about whether an item's physical inaccessibility invalidates the eiruv.

🪦 Teruma on a Grave: The Q&A Chain

Can a pure priest use teruma resting on a grave to establish an eiruv?
↓
Problem 1: Placing teruma on a grave defiles it — impure teruma cannot be eaten by anyone!
↓
Solution: The teruma was suspended above the grave in a carriage, crate, or cupboard — never touching it, remaining pure.
↓
Problem 2: How can the priest retrieve it? Using a flat board to slide it — but won't the board cover the grave (a handbreadth over a corpse imparts impurity)?
↓
Solution: He holds the flat utensil from its edge, not directly over the grave — avoiding the impurity decree. Flat utensils (no receptacle) also do not contract impurity themselves.
↓
Why do the Sages then forbid it? The priest cannot practically access or eat the food — and the Sages require the eiruv food to be genuinely usable.

⚖️ Mitzvot: For Personal Benefit or Not?

Question
Since Rabbi Yehuda allows eiruv with inaccessible items, does he hold that mitzvot may be used for personal benefit?
Gemara
No — Rabbi Yehuda holds mitzvot are NOT for personal benefit. He allows the eiruv precisely because the priest gains no personal enjoyment from the inaccessible teruma.
Challenge
Then Rava's ruling ('mitzvot not for benefit') aligns only with Rabbi Yehuda — a minority view. Should we apply Rava's ruling broadly?
Rava
My ruling was stated regarding Shofar on Rosh Hashana — a context where even the Sages agree. The tannaitic dispute does not affect my ruling at all.
Challenge
Similarly, Rav Yosef's rule ('eiruv only for mitzva') appears to be only one side of a tannaitic dispute!
Rav Yosef
All agree eiruv requires a mitzva purpose and that mitzvot yield no personal benefit. The dispute is only whether inaccessibility disqualifies — Sages say yes, Rabbi Yehuda says no.
Key Insight Both Rava and Rav Yosef defend their rulings as universally accepted — the tannaitic dispute is far narrower than it first appeared.

🌾 Which Foods Are Valid for Eiruv?

✅ Valid Eiruv Foods

  • Demai — doubtfully tithed produce (potentially edible: owner may declare ownerless)
  • First tithe from which teruma gedola has already been separated
  • Second tithe that has been properly redeemed with minted coins
  • Consecrated food redeemed with money (not with land)
  • Demai fed to the poor — Beit Hillel permits feeding demai to the impoverished

❌ Invalid Eiruv Foods

  • Tevel — untithed produce; prohibited entirely and cannot be made edible by ownerless declaration
  • First tithe from which teruma gedola has not been separated
  • Second tithe that has not been redeemed
  • Consecrated food not yet redeemed
  • Second tithe redeemed with asimon — unminted coin is invalid; Torah requires 'money'

📜 Demai — How Doubt Creates Validity

The Problem: Demai (produce from an am ha'aretz who may not have tithed) is forbidden to eat — so how can it qualify as eiruv food?
↓
The Key Principle: If he wishes, he can declare his property ownerless — making himself legally poor and thus permitted to eat demai. Therefore the food is potentially edible.
↓
Social Leniency: One may also feed demai to soldiers and to the poor who suffer hardship. Beit Hillel explicitly permits feeding demai to the impoverished — the halakha follows Beit Hillel.
↓
Contrast with Tevel: Tevel (certainly untithed produce) cannot be made edible by any declaration — the tithes must actually be separated. No legal fiction can render it permissible.
↓
Eiruv Criterion: The standard is potential edibility — demai clears this bar through the ownerless-property mechanism. Tevel and unredeemed sacred foods do not, and are absolutely invalid.
