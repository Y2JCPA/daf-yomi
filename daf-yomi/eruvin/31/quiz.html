QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why can demai (doubtfully tithed produce) be used to establish an eiruv even though it is normally forbidden to eat?
A. Demai is considered fully tithed by Rabbinic decree for eiruv purposes
B. If the owner declares his property ownerless he becomes poor and may eat demai — making it potentially edible
C. The Sages waived the prohibition on demai specifically in the context of eiruv
D. Demai is permitted on Shabbat and therefore always qualifies for eiruv
Correct: B — The Gemara explains: since he could declare his property ownerless and thereby become poor (eligible to eat demai), the food is considered potentially edible and therefore valid for eiruv.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the core dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Sages regarding eiruv with teruma resting on a grave?
A. Rabbi Yehuda forbids it; the Sages permit it in cases of great need
B. They disagree about whether a grave imparts impurity to hovering objects
C. Rabbi Yehuda permits it because physical inaccessibility doesn't matter; the Sages require that the food be practically accessible
D. Rabbi Yehuda holds mitzvot may yield personal benefit; the Sages disagree
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehuda holds that an eiruv establishes a halakhic residence through the mitzva act itself — physical inaccessibility is irrelevant. The Sages require the food to be genuinely usable to count as 'residence.'
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does the Gemara defend Rava's principle ('mitzvot were not given for benefit') against the charge that it follows only Rabbi Yehuda's minority view?
A. Rava explicitly stated he sides with Rabbi Yehuda over the Sages
B. Rava stated his ruling regarding Shofar on Rosh Hashana — a context where even the Sages would agree
C. The Gemara concedes Rava follows only Rabbi Yehuda but notes his ruling is still authoritative
D. Rava derived his principle from a Biblical verse independent of the tannaitic dispute
Correct: B — The Gemara defends Rava by pointing out his ruling concerned Shofar — a case outside the scope of the tannaitic dispute — so his principle stands as universally accepted.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rav Ḥisda, who is 'one who does not accept the principle of eiruv' in the mishna?
A. A Sadducee who rejects Rabbinic authority on all matters
B. A gentile resident living in the courtyard
C. A Kuti (Samaritan), who does not accept Rabbinic eiruv laws
D. A Torah scholar who personally holds eiruv is unnecessary
Correct: C — Rav Ḥisda identifies this person as a Kuti — a Samaritan who accepts the Written Torah but rejects Rabbinic enactments, including the laws of eiruv.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Reish Lakish (cited by Rabbi Abbahu), what is the halakha when a Levite takes first tithe while the grain is still on the stalks — before harvesting is complete?
A. The first tithe is invalid since the Levite took it prematurely
B. The Levite is exempt from separating teruma gedola, since the owner's obligation had not yet crystallized
C. The Levite must still separate teruma gedola as normal
D. The produce becomes tevel and must be fully re-tithed
Correct: B — Reish Lakish holds that first tithe taken while grain is still on the stalks is exempt from teruma gedola — the verse 'when you take from Israel' implies the obligation applies only after the harvest is complete and the grain is piled.
