📿 Two Pairs of Tefillin on Shabbat — The Dispute

First Tanna
One who dons two pairs of tefillin on Shabbat is liable — even without intent to violate bal tosif, the act itself constitutes prohibited addition to the mitzva.
Rabbi Meir
One is only liable if he intends to add to the mitzva. Without intent, wearing two pairs does not violate the prohibition of bal tosif.
Gemara
But if so — according to Rabbi Meir, shouldn't one be forbidden from wearing even one pair on Shabbat? He might inadvertently violate bal tosif!
Gemara
Similarly: one who sleeps in the sukkah on Shemini Atzeret would be flogged for adding to 'seven days' — yet this seems unreasonable. The argument collapses on itself.
Core Tension Does bal tosif require intent? Is Shabbat even a valid time for tefillin? These two interlocked questions drive the entire first half of the daf.

🔍 Narrowing Down the Tanna — Shabbat and Tefillin

Attempt 1: Rabbi Akiva
He holds the verse 'observe it' refers only to the Paschal lamb — so phylacteries have no Shabbat prohibition
↓
Baraita: Rabbi Akiva himself says 'I might have thought one dons tefillin on Shabbat… but Shabbat is itself a sign — tefillin are not worn' → Rabbi Akiva holds Shabbat is NOT a time for tefillin
↓
Attempt 2: Rabbi Natan
Baraita: one awake at night may keep or remove tefillin — implies night is a valid time for tefillin
↓
Rejected: night being fit does not equal Shabbat being fit — Rabbi Akiva himself could hold night is fit but Shabbat is not
↓
Attempt 3: Michal bat Kushi
She donned tefillin and the Sages did not protest against her behavior
↓
Rejected: Perhaps the Sages' silence follows Rabbi Yosei — women may optionally perform time-bound positive mitzvot (like hand-laying on sacrifices). Jonah's wife ascending to Jerusalem for pilgrimage also went unchallenged.

🧵 Found Sky-Blue Material in the Marketplace — Fit or Unfit?

Strips of Dyed Wool

  • Wide strips of sky-blue fabric
  • Unfit for tzitzit (ritual fringes)
  • Reason: dyed with intent for a cloak
  • Dyeing was not לִשְׁמָהּ — not for the sake of tzitzit
  • Work performed for the wrong purpose invalidates use

Twisted Threads

  • Twisted multi-strand threads
  • Fit for tzitzit
  • A person does not exert himself twisting threads for mere ornamentation
  • Twisting implies they were made for tzitzit purpose
  • Rava challenges: people do exert themselves making amulets shaped like tefillin — dispute between tannaim

Cut Short Strings

  • Short strings cut from a larger piece
  • Fit for tzitzit
  • Presumed cut from an actual tzitzit garment
  • Short length indicates removal from use, not preparation for a cloak
  • Rabbi Zeira sends son Ahava to teach this ruling to the Sages

👩 Michal bat Kushi — Women and Tefillin

מִיכַל בַּת כּוּשִׁי הָיְתָה מַנַּחַת תְּפִלִּין וְלֹא מִיחוּ בָּהּ חֲכָמִים
Historical Precedent Michal, daughter of Kushi (King Saul's daughter) would don tefillin on Shabbat, and the Sages did not protest against her behavior. The Gemara uses this as evidence that the tanna in question holds Shabbat is a fit time for tefillin. However, this proof is challenged: perhaps the Sages' silence reflects Rabbi Yosei's position — that women may optionally perform time-bound positive commandments (analogous to laying hands on sacrifices before slaughter). The parallel case of Jonah's wife ascending to Jerusalem for the Festival pilgrimage without being rebuked strengthens this reading — women may volunteer for mitzvot they are not obligated in, and silence proves nothing about Shabbat's status. Ultimately, Michal's baraita cannot resolve the question on its own.
