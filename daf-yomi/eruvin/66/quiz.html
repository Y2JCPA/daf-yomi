Eruvin 66 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
How does the Gemara resolve the apparent contradiction in Rabbi Yoḥanan's statements about renting from a gentile?
A. Renting is only valid before Shabbat, not during Shabbat
B. His comparison to eiruv was meant as a leniency — the same rules (e.g., less than a perutah) apply
C. The two statements refer to different types of gentile ownership
D. Renting supersedes eiruv and is always preferred
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that Rabbi Yoḥanan's comparison was a leniency: just as eiruv needs no minimum payment, neither does rental from a gentile. One agent may also act on behalf of the whole courtyard in both cases.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Shmuel, what two conditions must both be true for bitul reshut (renunciation of rights) to be valid?
A. The residents must share a wall and both be Jewish
B. They must render each other prohibited from carrying AND be capable of establishing an eiruv together
C. They must have forgotten the eiruv and have no gentile neighbors
D. They must be in adjoining courtyards and the eiruv must be in a central location
Correct: B — Shmuel requires both conditions: residents render each other prohibited from carrying, and they could have established an eiruv together. If only one condition is met, renunciation is unavailable.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why did Rav Yosef say 'I have not heard this halakha' — and how did Abaye respond?
A. Rav Yosef was being modest; Abaye confirmed the halakha was new
B. Rav Yosef had forgotten his own teaching due to illness; Abaye reminded him he had said it himself
C. Rav Yosef disagreed with Shmuel; Abaye defended Shmuel's ruling
D. Rav Yosef was testing Abaye to see if he remembered the halakha
Correct: B — Rav Yosef suffered from an illness that affected his memory. Abaye reminded him that he himself had taught these rulings in the name of Shmuel — Rav Yosef had forgotten his own transmitted traditions.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rava, in which specific scenario can residents of inner and outer courtyards successfully use renunciation?
A. Eiruv in the inner courtyard; outer resident forgot
B. Eiruv in the outer courtyard; inner resident forgot
C. No eiruv was established at all
D. Eiruv in both courtyards; one resident from each forgot
Correct: B — Rava rules that only when the eiruv is in the outer courtyard and an inner courtyard resident forgot can renunciation work — the inner resident renounces to the outer, and the outer eiruv then covers everyone.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why can't the forgotten outer-courtyard resident renounce rights when the eiruv is also in the outer courtyard?
A. An outer resident can only renounce to another outer resident, not inward
B. No valid recipient exists — inner renouncing outward leaves other outer residents still prohibiting; outer renouncing inward doesn't remove the outer prohibition
C. Renunciation requires both parties to have an eiruv
D. The outer courtyard's eiruv automatically covers any forgotten residents
Correct: B — Rava explains: if the outer resident renounces to the inner, the inner courtyard becomes like a house opening to the outer — but the outer still has a non-participating resident. If an inner resident renounces outward, the other outer residents still render it prohibited. There is no party whose receiving the renunciation actually fixes the problem.
