🔄 Rav vs. Shmuel: Renunciation of Courtyard Rights

Rav
If two people in a courtyard forgot to make an eruv, one may renounce his rights in favor of the other — and carrying is then permitted for both.
Shmuel
There is no renunciation of rights from one courtyard to another — bitul reshut does not work across courtyard boundaries.
Ravina
If both positions have merit, why not let Person A renounce to B, and then B renounce back to A so water can be passed between courtyards?
Rava
One may not renounce in favor of another and then receive a renunciation back — lest the Sages' words become a subject of mockery!
Core Rule Bitul reshut (renunciation of domain) is one-directional: once you renounce, you cannot receive a renunciation back from the same person on the same Shabbat.

📜 Rabbinic Decree: Action vs. Non-Action Rav Yosef challenges Rabba's analogy: sprinkling purification water (a non-action rabbinic decree) should not be equated with telling a gentile to heat water on Shabbat. Telling a gentile involves no direct act by the Jew — making it a shvut d'shvut, a doubly-rabbinic prohibition. Such a leniency may apply in pressing circumstances like circumcision preparation.

↓
Resident requests a share and is given some
Shituf is valid — all are genuine co-owners

Shituf is invalid — he was never truly included

👶 Baby's Circumcision Water Spilled on Shabbat

Situation: Warm water prepared for a baby's brit milah (circumcision) on Shabbat spills before the ceremony.
↓
Rava's approach: Ask the mother — if her condition is precarious, water may be heated on her behalf under pikuach nefesh.
↓
Rav Mesharshiya objects: The mother is eating dates and appears healthy — heating water seems unnecessary.
↓
Rava responds: She may be eating out of ravenous hunger, not robust health — her condition could still be dangerously weak.
↓
Second incident: Rava himself had water, but his courtyard had no joint eruv with the baby's adjacent courtyard.
↓
Resolution: Rava renounced his rights to his own courtyard, enabling the water to be legally transferred to where it was needed.

⚖️ Sadducee in the Alleyway — What Is His Status?

Tanna Kamma
A Sadducee who lives in an alleyway renders carrying prohibited for the other residents — his legal status is like that of a gentile who never renounced his rights.
Rabban Gamliel
A Sadducee's status is NOT like a gentile — he is a Jew who violates rabbinic law, but his domain can be incorporated into an eruv.
Rabbi Yehuda
Rabban Gamliel warned: 'Hurry and bring in or take out whatever you need before that loathsome person prohibits you once Shabbat falls!'
Gemara
The mishna is incomplete — it omits that the Sadducee's status is itself a tannaitic dispute. Rabban Gamliel disputes the ruling that he is like a gentile.
Historical Context Sadducees rejected the Oral Torah. Rabban Gamliel's leniency reflects that a Sadducee remains halachically Jewish — not legally equivalent to a non-Jew — even if his beliefs are heretical.

🔄 Tannaitic Roots of the Rav–Shmuel Dispute

Rabbi Meir → Rav

  • One who renounces his rights does NOT fully remove himself from his domain
  • Renunciation is partial — some ownership remains
  • Therefore: A renounces to B, then B can renounce back to A
  • Rav: bitul reshut works flexibly — one may renounce in favor of another resident
  • The renouncer retains a residual claim even after bitul

Rabbis (Ḥakhamim) → Shmuel

  • One who renounces his rights DOES fully remove himself from his domain
  • Renunciation is complete — like a full legal transfer
  • Therefore: Once A renounces to B, A has no domain left to receive back
  • Shmuel: no renunciation across separate courtyard boundaries
  • Back-and-forth renunciation makes the institution a mockery
