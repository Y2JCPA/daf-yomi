🏙️ Large City vs. Small City: Who Can Walk Where?

Mishna
Residents of a large city may walk through an entire small city — even portions beyond 2,000 cubits from their home, because a city counts as merely four cubits.
Mishna
Residents of a small city may not walk through an entire large city — their 2,000-cubit limit terminates inside it before they reach its far end.
Rav Naḥman
One who reads the second clause as 'small city may walk through large city' also does not err — the case is where one places his eiruv inside the large city, making it his base.
Gemara
The mishna is incomplete — it must be supplemented: when the eiruv is placed within the large city, even a small-city resident traverses all of it plus 2,000 cubits beyond.
Core Principle The eiruv's placement determines the starting point of the 2,000-cubit measurement. A city — inhabited or fit for habitation — counts as only four cubits, granting full traversal plus 2,000 beyond.

🕳️ Cave Eiruv — Rabbi Akiva vs. the Rabbis

Rabbi Akiva
One who places his eiruv in a cave has only 2,000 cubits from the place of his eiruv — the cave's full area is counted in the measurement, eating into his range.
Rabbis
When does Rabbi Akiva's rule apply? Only when the cave has no residents. If it has residents it is treated as four cubits — walk through all of it plus 2,000 beyond.
Rav Yehuda (Shmuel)
A desolate city with standing walls — even if currently uninhabited — is treated like four cubits per the Rabbis, since it is fit for habitation (suitable, not merely occupied).
Rava
The halakha follows the Rabbis. Nobody rules stringently like Rabbi Akiva about eiruv placement. A habitable city or cave is treated as four cubits, full stop.
Decisive Criterion Fitness for habitation — not current occupancy — determines whether a space is treated as 'four cubits' or measured by actual dimensions.

⚔️ The Geder & Ḥamtan Controversy

Geography: Geder sits atop a slope; Ḥamtan at the bottom. Normally their Shabbat limits do not overlap.
↓
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's ruling: Permitted residents of Geder to descend to Ḥamtan on Shabbat by merging their eruv limits.
↓
Rav Safra's explanation: Geder was bow-shaped — its empty arc was treated as filled with houses, extending its Shabbat limit to reach Ḥamtan.
↓
Rav Dimi's alternative: The ruling had nothing to do with Shabbat limits — Geder residents would assault Ḥamtan residents, and the ordinance addressed this social conflict.
↓
Why only on Shabbat? Drunkenness is common on Shabbat (people eat and drink freely) — violence was most likely then.
↓
Popular saying: 'A dog not in its place will not bark for seven years.' Aggressors become timid in foreign territory — so Geder visitors in Ḥamtan would not assault.

🏔️ Barrier Requirements: Standard vs. Ravine-Edge City

Standard Eiruv Partition

  • Minimum height: 4 handbreadths (tefaḥim)
  • Serves a symbolic legal function
  • Marks boundary without physically deterring entry
  • Sufficient for most Shabbat limit and eiruv contexts
  • People are not frightened by the area itself

City on Edge of Ravine

  • Minimum height: 4 cubits (amot) — far higher!
  • Use of the ravine edge is frightening
  • Barrier must physically deter people from using the area
  • Only then is the Shabbat limit measured from ravine edge
  • Source: Rav Yosef citing baraita about Geder/Ḥamtan
