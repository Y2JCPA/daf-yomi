QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Rabbis, if a resident of a large city places his eiruv inside a small city, how much may he traverse on Shabbat?
A. Only 2,000 cubits from the exact spot of his eiruv
B. The entire small city plus 2,000 cubits beyond its far end
C. Only the half of the small city closest to his home
D. He may not traverse the small city at all without a communal eiruv
Correct: B — The Rabbis hold that an inhabited city counts as merely four cubits. Placing one's eiruv inside grants traversal of the entire city plus 2,000 cubits beyond — this is the core rule of the mishna.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why does a city located on the edge of a ravine require a barrier four cubits tall, rather than the usual four handbreadths?
A. Because the ravine is classified as public domain (reshut ha-rabbim)
B. Because use of the ravine edge is frightening and the barrier must physically deter entry
C. Because the city lacks walls on its other three sides
D. Because ravines are near water, requiring stricter standards
Correct: B — Rav Yosef explains: normally partitions serve a symbolic function (4 handbreadths suffices), but where the terrain is frightening, the barrier must actively prevent use — requiring 4 full cubits of height.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav Dimi, why did Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi institute his ordinance regarding Geder and Ḥamtan specifically for Shabbat and not weekdays?
A. Because the road between them was only passable on Shabbat
B. Because only Shabbat limits, not weekday travel, were the halakhic issue
C. Because drunkenness is common on Shabbat, making violence most likely on that day
D. Because the city gates of Ḥamtan were locked on weekdays
Correct: C — Rav Dimi reinterprets the whole ordinance: it was about preventing violence from Geder residents against Ḥamtan residents. Shabbat — when people eat and drink freely — was when this aggression was most likely.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What does the Gemara mean by the saying 'A dog not in its place will not bark for seven years' in the context of Geder and Ḥamtan?
A. Animals become muktzeh and tame when taken outside their domain on Shabbat
B. An aggressor is timid and non-threatening when he is away from his home territory
C. One who leaves his city loses his Shabbat limit for seven days
D. Gentiles who move into a Jewish area become peaceful neighbors over time
Correct: B — The Gemara uses this proverb to explain why Geder residents visiting Ḥamtan would not assault the locals — people who are aggressive at home lose their boldness when in foreign territory.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, when does a gentile residing in a courtyard actually prohibit the Jewish residents from carrying?
A. Always — a gentile's mere presence prohibits carrying in the shared space
B. Only on Yom Tov, but not on regular Shabbat
C. Only when there are also two Jews in the courtyard who would otherwise prohibit each other
D. Only if the gentile actively refuses to rent his portion to the Jews
Correct: C — Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov holds that a lone gentile does not trigger the prohibition. There must also be two Jews present who would prohibit one another from carrying — the gentile then compounds an already-existing problem.
