⚖️ Death in the Caravan on Shabbat

Rav Huna
Shabbat determines the status. The space allocated at the onset of Shabbat governs the entire day — even if a caravan member dies mid-Shabbat, the original allocation remains in effect for the survivors.
Rabbi Yitzchak
The current population determines the status. Once a member dies, the survivors may only carry within space proportional to their reduced number — we recalculate based on who remains.
Gemara
We cannot simply map this dispute onto Rabbi Yehuda vs. Rabbi Yosei — each sage could defend their ruling even within the opposing tanna's framework, by pointing to unique features of their case.
Core Question Does halakhic status freeze at Shabbat onset, or does it respond to real-time conditions throughout the day?

⚔️ The Four Military Camp Exemptions

🪵 Wood Gathering

  • May take dry wood from any location
  • Rooted in Joshua's 10 stipulations
  • Extends even to wood owners care about
  • 3 distinctions from Joshua's ordinance

🙌 Hand Washing

  • Exempt from first waters (pre-meal)
  • Final waters remain obligatory
  • Sodomite salt can blind — one grain per kor
  • Danger of blindness cannot be waived

🌾 Demai Tithes

  • Exempt from separating doubtful tithes
  • Parallel to feeding demai to the poor
  • Or to quartered soldiers (akhsanya)
  • Leniency when ownership is uncertain

🏕️ Eiruv

  • Exempt from joining of houses (chatzeirot)
  • NOT exempt from Shabbat limit (techum)
  • Techum is Torah-level — cannot be waived
  • Rabbi Ḥiyya: flogged for violating techum

🪦 Met Mitzva — A Corpse Acquires Its Place

מֵת מִצְוָה — כָּל מֵת שֶׁאֵין לוֹ קוֹבְרִים
מֵת מִצְוָה קוֹנֶה מְקוֹמוֹ A soldier killed in battle is buried where he fell — even when others are present and available to tend to his burial. The Gemara challenges: a baraita permits moving a corpse off a main street to avoid obstructing passage. Rav Beivai resolves the contradiction: that baraita describes a corpse stretched across a public path reaching both sides — it must be moved specifically to prevent priests from becoming ritually impure by passing over it. In all other cases, the rule holds: a met mitzva acquires its place and is buried there.

🪵 Passin — Posts Around a Well in the Public Domain

Problem: A well in the public domain qualifies as a private domain (≥4×4 handbreadths wide, ≥10 deep) — carrying water on Shabbat requires a valid enclosure
↓
Rabbinic leniency: Four deyomadin (double posts arranged as corners) suffice — they appear as eight single posts forming a symbolic enclosure
↓
Dimensions: Height ≥ 10 handbreadths · Width ≥ 6 handbreadths · Thickness — even a minimal amount
↓
Maximum gap (Tanna Kamma): Space for two teams of three oxen to enter and exit simultaneously while tied together
↓
Rabbi Yehuda allows more: Gap for two teams of four oxen each — measured tied, one team entering as the other exits
↓
Minimum enclosure size: A cow must fit with its head and majority of body inside the posts while drinking from the well
