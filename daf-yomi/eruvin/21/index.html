🐄 What Is the Purpose of the Upright Boards?

Rav Anan
If so, what purpose do the boards serve? The cow cannot drink directly from the bucket anyway — it must be poured into a trough first!
Gemara
How can he ask what purpose they serve? They allow people to draw water from wells on Shabbat — the enclosed area becomes a private domain!
Rav Anan
Then what is the point of requiring the enclosure be large enough for the cow's head and most of its body? If it's for people, size doesn't matter that way!
Abaye
We deal here with a trough that stands in the public domain. The boards enclose that trough — creating a private domain — so cattle may drink from it on Shabbat.
Resolution The size requirement serves the animal drinking from a public-domain trough. The boards transform it into a private enclosure — permitted on Shabbat.

🌊 Where Do Eruv Leniencies NOT Apply?

Which leniency is being considered?
↓
🏘️ Huts Extending City Limits
Not in Babylonia
Floods regularly sweep huts away → huts are not considered permanent dwellings → cannot extend Shabbat boundary
⛲ Upright Boards Around Wells
Not in Babylonia
Rivers and canals make water plentiful → wells are not essential → the leniency has no purpose there
↓
What about outside Eretz Yisrael generally?
↓
Version: Rav Yirmeya bar Abba
Neither leniency applies in Babylonia nor outside Eretz Yisrael generally — geography matters fundamentally
Practical Ruling (Marei bar Huna)
Ruins of towns within 70⅔ cubits of each other can extend Shabbat boundaries — ruins are more stable than huts

📚 Words of Torah vs. Words of the Sages

Words of Torah

  • The "old" mitzvot — given at Sinai
  • Explicitly written in the Torah scroll
  • The foundation of all Jewish law
  • Example: core Shabbat prohibitions

Words of the Sages

  • The "new" mitzvot — rabbinic enactments
  • Cannot be fully committed to writing: "of making many books there is no end" (Eccl. 12:12)
  • Dearer to God than words of Torah itself
  • Example: eruv of courtyards; ritual handwashing (Solomon's enactments)

🤲 Rabbi Akiva in Prison: Washing Hands Over Drinking

Setting: Rabbi Akiva is imprisoned; Rabbi Yehoshua HaGarsi serves as his attendant, bringing water daily
↓
The Crisis: The prison guard pours out half the water — leaving barely enough to drink, certainly not enough for ritual handwashing as well
↓
Rabbi Akiva's Choice: Told of the small amount, he says: "Give me water so that I may wash my hands." Rabbi Yehoshua protests — it won't suffice for drinking!
↓
His Ruling: "What can I do? One who eats with unwashed hands is liable to death. I would rather die of thirst than transgress the words of my colleagues."
↓
The Sages' Reaction: "If in his old age and weakened state he is so meticulous — how much more so was he in his youth!" A supreme model of commitment to rabbinic law
