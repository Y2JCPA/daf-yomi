🤝 Rav Naḥman vs. Rav Sheshet: Agency Presumption

Rav Naḥman
We rely on the presumption that an agent fulfills his agency only for rabbinic law — not for Torah-level prohibitions.
Rav Sheshet
We rely on agency presumption for both Torah law and rabbinic law — the agent is trusted regardless of severity.
Rav Naḥman
Each of your proofs has an alternative explanation — the omer case works because the court never delays past midday, not due to agency trust.
Rav Sheshet
Proof from the fig tree: a gatherer eats a ḥaver's figs without tithing — we trust the owner's agency even regarding Torah-level tithes.
Core Question Does שליח עושה שליחותו (an agent fulfills his mission) apply under Torah law, or only under rabbinic law?

🌾 The Omer Proof — Reading the Evidence

Mishna: Once the omer is offered, new-crop grain is immediately permitted throughout the land
↓
Those far from Jerusalem may eat from midday onward — the omer must have been offered by then via court agents
↓
Does this prove we rely on agency presumption even for Torah law?
↓
Rav Sheshet
Yes — proves agency presumption applies to Torah prohibitions (new grain = Torah-level)
Rav Naḥman
No — the court is trusted because it never delays the omer past midday; that is the specific reason, not the general agency rule

🍇 Fig Tree Baraita — Who Tithed the Figs?

Baraita (1st case)
One who gathers figs from an am ha'aretz's tree must tithe them as demai — doubtful produce, possibly untithed.
Baraita (2nd case)
One who gathers from a ḥaver's tree may eat freely — the ḥaver certainly already separated tithes from other produce on its behalf.
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
My view is stronger: even if ḥaverim tithe from non-adjacent produce (a minor irregularity), we still trust them not to release untithed produce.
Rav Ḥanina Ḥoza'a
Legal presumption: a ḥaver never releases untithed produce from his possession — so Rav Naḥman can explain the fig-tree case without invoking agency at all.
Naḥman's Counter Each proof Rav Sheshet raises has its own local presumption — no general agency rule for Torah law is needed.

👥 Am Ha'aretz vs. Ḥaver — Tithing Obligations

Am Ha'aretz

  • Suspected of not separating tithes properly
  • Figs from his tree → gatherer must tithe as demai
  • His word on tithing status is unreliable
  • Cannot be trusted to act as tithing agent

Ḥaver

  • Committed to scrupulous halachic observance
  • Figs from his tree → may be eaten without tithing
  • Presumed never to release untithed produce (Rav Ḥanina Ḥoza'a)
  • May tithe retroactively from non-adjacent produce (Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi)

📍 Gemara: Why Does a Tree in the Public Domain Matter?

Problem posed by Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba, Rabbi Asi, and Rava bar Natan: Where does the mishna's tree stand — private or public domain?
↓
Private domain rejected: If the tree is in a private domain, height doesn't matter — the whole space is one unified domain.
↓
Public domain assumed: The tree stands in the public domain. The person placed his eiruv intending to establish Shabbat residence on the ground below, not in the tree.
↓
Above 10 handbreadths = unreachable: Climbing a tree is rabbinically forbidden on Shabbat → eiruv cannot be retrieved → it fails.
↓
Rava's limitation: This ruling applies only to a tree beyond the city's outskirts (more than 70⅔ cubits away). A tree within city limits is treated as part of the town.
↓
Rav Naḥman's affirmation: Shmuel in Babylonia reached the same conclusion independently — the Sages of Eretz Yisrael were surprised Babylonian scholars had resolved this.
