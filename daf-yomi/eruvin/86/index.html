🪣 Cistern Between Two Courtyards — Where Does the Partition Go?

Beit Shammai
The partition must be placed below — actually near the water, down in the airspace of the cistern.
Beit Hillel
The partition must be placed above — near the rim of the cistern, higher than the water's surface.
Rav Huna
Both positions rely on lavud: gaps under 3 handbreadths are treated as closed. Neither partition needs to touch the water itself.
Rav Yehuda
Below actually means below the waterline — and the reed tops must be visible at least one handbreadth above the surface so the partition is recognizable.
Alternative A crossbeam (koreh) of 4 handbreadths wide laid across the cistern also permits drawing water — the Sages established that a bucket will not drift past a beam of that width.

💰 Honoring the Wealthy Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Rabbi Akiva both honored wealthy individuals. Rava bar Mari anchors this in a verse: "May he be enthroned before God forever" (Ps. 61:8) — read as praise for those who use their wealth generously.

Rabbi Yishmael ben Rabbi Yosei illustrated this dramatically: "Bonyas, father of this man, has a thousand ships at sea and a thousand towns on land — make way for him!"

The honor shown to wealth reflects divine blessing made visible in the world.

🏠 Who Renders the Courtyard Prohibited When Absent?

Tanna Kamma

  • Both Jew and gentile who leave for Shabbat render the courtyard prohibited
  • Legal presence persists even in physical absence
  • Other residents cannot use shared courtyard without eruv

Rabbi Yosei

  • Gentile: renders it prohibited — may return at any time
  • Jew: does NOT render it prohibited
  • A Jew does not travel on Shabbat, so return is impossible

Rabbi Shimon

  • Even a Jew in the same town does NOT render it prohibited
  • Example: stayed at his daughter's house for Shabbat
  • He has mentally removed himself from the courtyard

👨‍👧 Rav's Nuance on Rabbi Shimon's Ruling

Rav
The halakha follows Rabbi Shimon — one who leaves for Shabbat does not render the courtyard prohibited.
Gemara
This applies only when he went to his daughter's house. A father is at ease at his daughter's home and will not return unexpectedly.
Gemara (limiting)
But if he went to his son's house — no. A father may feel unwelcome or out of place and return home mid-Shabbat.
Social Logic Halachic concern tracks social dynamics: the certainty that someone will not return depends on whether they are genuinely comfortable where they went.

📏 How to Permit Drawing from a Shared Cistern

Cistern situated between two courtyards
Drawing water is prohibited — may draw from the other domain
↓
Option A: Mechitza
Partition (10×4 tefachim) dividing the cistern
• Beit Shammai: placed below waterline
• Beit Hillel: placed above, near rim
• Rav Yehuda: reed tops visible 1 tefach above surface
Option B: Koreh
Crossbeam 4 tefachim wide across the cistern
Bucket is restrained — Sages established it won't drift to the other side
Rabbi Yehuda's View
The courtyard wall above the cistern already suffices
Wall above = wall below; no interior partition needed
↓
Each courtyard draws water from its own half on Shabbat ✓

🧱 The Tzippori Incident — Suspended Partitions on Shabbat

StageDetail
ProblemPeople forgot to bring a Torah scroll to the synagogue before Shabbat
ComplicationThe scroll was in a separate domain — carrying it was prohibited
First Report"They spread sheets on Shabbat" as partitions to create a permitted domain
Gemara's ObjectionOne may not erect a temporary tent (ohel) on Shabbat — this would be forbidden!
ClarificationThey did not spread sheets — they found sheets already spread on pillars
They Simply UsedPre-existing suspended partitions to permit carrying the scroll to shul
AuthorityNot based on Rabbi Yosei (who is strict) — relies on a more lenient opinion permitting mechitza ha-tluya
