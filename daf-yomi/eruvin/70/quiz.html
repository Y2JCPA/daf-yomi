QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabba, when a resident who forgot to make an eiruv wishes to renounce his rights in a courtyard shared with multiple residents, what must he do?
A. Renounce to any single resident — this covers everyone
B. Renounce to each and every individual resident
C. Renounce to the majority of residents only
D. Renunciation is invalid when more than two residents are present
Correct: B — Rabba holds that the resident must renounce his rights individually to each and every resident of the courtyard. Renouncing to one alone is insufficient according to his view.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Which renunciation combination does the baraita explicitly PROHIBIT?
A. Two residents without eiruv renouncing to two residents with eiruv
B. Two residents with eiruv renouncing to one resident without eiruv
C. One resident with eiruv renouncing to one resident without eiruv
D. One resident without eiruv renouncing to one resident with eiruv
Correct: C — The baraita explicitly prohibits one who established an eiruv from renouncing rights in favor of one who did not — the person with eiruv cannot transfer permission to one who never established it.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is Rav Naḥman's reasoning that an heir may renounce inherited domain rights on Shabbat?
A. The heir is treated as a new resident who can make his own eiruv arrangements
B. The heir is considered 'like his father's foot,' substituting for him in all respects
C. Renunciation on Shabbat is always permitted for anyone who acquires property
D. The Rabbis enacted a special leniency for heirs to prevent Shabbat violations
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman holds that an heir is considered 'like his father's foot' (רגל אביו) — he steps entirely into his father's halakhic position, and since the father could have renounced, so can the heir.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
The baraita states 'this is the principle' regarding things permitted at Shabbat's outset. What additional case does this phrase include?
A. A gentile who dies in the courtyard on Shabbat
B. A convert who dies childless, leaving an ownerless domain
C. An alleyway whose lechi or korah fell on Shabbat
D. Two residents who both forgot to join the eiruv
Correct: C — The Gemara explains that 'this is the principle' for the permissive rule includes an alleyway whose lechi (side post) or korah (cross beam) fell on Shabbat — it remains permitted since it was permitted at Shabbat's outset.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Abaye interpret the baraita's phrase 'two residents who did not make an eiruv may renounce in favor of two residents who did'?
A. They must renounce to both of the two simultaneously in a joint declaration
B. They renounce to one of the two, which counts as renouncing to both
C. The phrase is superfluous — Abaye rejects this clause entirely
D. They may each renounce to a different one of the two residents
Correct: B — Abaye interprets 'in favor of two' to mean in favor of one of the two — renouncing to one is as effective as renouncing to both. This is consistent with his overall position that renouncing to one resident suffices.
