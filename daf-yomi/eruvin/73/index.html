🏠 What Is One's 'Place' for Eiruv?

Core Question: What establishes a person's place of residence for eiruv purposes?
↓
Rav
Where he eats his bread
Shmuel
Where he sleeps
↓
Shepherds objection: They sleep in the field but make eiruv in the city — seems to favor Shmuel!
↓
Resolution: We are 'witnesses' — it is evident that if bread were brought to the field they would prefer it there. The eating location remains determinative; sleeping in the field is circumstantial.

🍞 Rav vs. Shmuel: What Defines One's Place?

Rav
A person's place is where he eats his bread. The meal location is the primary indicator of one's residence for eiruv purposes.
Shmuel
A person's place is where he sleeps. The sleeping location reflects true and consistent residence.
Gemara
Objection! Shepherds, fig watchmen, produce guardsmen — they sleep in the field yet their eiruv is valid in the city. This supports Shmuel, not Rav!
Rav (Defense)
We are witnesses that if bread were brought to the field they would prefer it there. Their true eating place is the city — they sleep in the field only out of necessity.
Abaye
Rav Yosef, you yourself told us this halakha from Rav! You said it in connection with brothers who eat at their father's table — they are considered entirely part of his household.
Key Principle The place where one regularly eats establishes eiruv residence (Rav). Where circumstances make it evident one truly belongs elsewhere, we look to that underlying reality.

👩‍👦 Five Wives vs. Five Slaves — Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava

Five Wives (חמש נשים)

  • Each wife lives in her own quarters in the courtyard
  • Each receives a food portion from her husband
  • Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava: PROHIBITED — they must each contribute separately
  • Reason: A wife is legally significant in her own right
  • She is not completely subsumed under her husband's identity
  • Each woman must participate in the courtyard eiruv independently

Five Slaves (חמשה עבדים)

  • Each slave lives in his own quarters in the courtyard
  • Each receives a food portion from his master
  • Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava: PERMITTED — master covers them all
  • Reason: A slave necessarily follows his master at all times
  • Proof: 'Daniel was in the gate of the king' (Daniel 2:49)
  • Wherever the master resides, his slave is considered to reside there too

🏘️ Five Courtyards Opening into an Alleyway — Mishna Outcomes

Setup: Five courtyards each established an eiruv within their courtyard. They may also merge the alleyway (שיתוף מבואות).
↓
Eiruv only (no shituf)
✅ Permitted in courtyards
❌ Prohibited in alleyway
Shituf only (no eiruv)
❌ Prohibited in courtyards
✅ Permitted in alleyway
Both eiruv + shituf
✅ Permitted in courtyards
✅ Permitted in alleyway
↓
If someone forgot: Forgot eiruv but did shituf → permitted in alleyway, prohibited in courtyard. Forgot shituf but did eiruv → permitted in courtyard, prohibited in alleyway.
↓
Gemara: This mishna follows Rabbi Meir who requires both eiruv AND shituf. The Rabbis require only shituf for the alleyway.

📖 Rav's Interpretation of the Five Courtyards Mishna

Standard reading: Five courtyards open into each other and also into the alleyway — they form a connected chain of courtyards.
↓
Rav's teaching (Rav Yehuda, Rav Kahana): Rav taught that each courtyard opens separately and independently into the alleyway — they do NOT open into each other.
↓
Reason (Rav Yosef to Abaye): Rav holds that a valid alleyway merging must be brought in and taken out through the entrance of the alleyway. Courtyards opening into each other would bypass the alleyway entrance.
↓
Second reason (Rav's ruling at end of daf): An alleyway cannot be rendered permitted for carrying through a side post and cross beam alone — a proper shituf is also required.
↓
Practical upshot: Each courtyard made its own internal eiruv, then all five together performed shituf via the alleyway's entrance — satisfying Rabbi Meir's dual requirement.
