⚖️ Exempt Domain Between Two Reshuyot: Rav vs Rabbi Yoḥanan

Rav
Even a hair's breadth of carrying across an exempt domain situated between two reshuyot is prohibited.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
An exempt domain between two reshuyot is truly exempt — one may carry through it freely.
Gemara
Rav's strict ruling applies to rabbinic domains, where the Sages reinforced their decrees even more than Torah law requires.
Gemara
Rabbi Yoḥanan's leniency applies to Torah-level domains — an exempt space between them is fully permitted.
Resolution No real dispute: Rav addresses rabbinic domains (stricter), Rabbi Yoḥanan addresses Torah-level domains (standard exempt-domain rules apply).

🧱 Using the Top of the Wall: What Is and Isn't Permitted

Wall Top ≥ 4×4 Tefaḥim

  • ✅ Residents may ascend from either side and eat produce on top
  • ❌ May not raise food from the courtyard up to the top
  • Raising would draw the wall-top into one courtyard's domain
  • Ascending is passive use; raising is active transfer

Wall Top < 4×4 Tefaḥim

  • ❌ May not ascend — no usable independent surface
  • ❌ May not raise food to the top
  • Wall top lacks standing as its own domain
  • No 4×4 = no independent place = no permitted use

Rabbi Yoḥanan's Broader Ruling

  • ✅ Residents may raise food up to wall top and eat there
  • ✅ Residents may lower food back down to their own courtyard
  • Each side uses only its own side of the wall
  • The wall top acts as neutral ground between courtyards

📐 Asymmetric Wall or Ditch — Who Does It Belong To?

A wall or ditch separates two courtyards — one side is 10 tefaḥim high/deep; the other side is level with the ground
↓
🧱 Wall (Rav Naḥman via Rav Huna, Rabba)
Higher courtyard: wall is a full 10-tefaḥ barrier on its side. Lower courtyard: steps up to the wall top with no effort.
🕳️ Ditch (Rav Sheizvi via Rav Naḥman)
Deep side: 10-tefaḥim ditch on one courtyard's border. Shallow side: the other courtyard is level with the ditch floor.
↓
✅ Ruling: the wall/ditch belongs exclusively to the courtyard with easier access (the higher courtyard for a wall; the shallower side for a ditch)
↓
Why both cases? Wall items risk falling (worry); ditch items are protected (no worry). Each case is necessary — neither can be derived from the other.

🪜 Egyptian Ladder vs Tyrian Ladder — The Muktzeh Principle

Baraita
An Egyptian ladder (small) does not diminish the height of a wall between courtyards.
Baraita
A Tyrian ladder (large) does effectively diminish the height of the wall.
Rav Aḥa bar Adda (via Rav Hamnuna, Rav)
The Egyptian ladder is an occasional-use item — it is set-aside (muktzeh) on Shabbat. A muktzeh item cannot diminish a wall.
Gemara Objection
A Tyrian ladder is also not muktzeh — it may be handled on Shabbat. So why does it diminish the wall?
Gemara Resolution
A Tyrian ladder is so heavy that its weight establishes it as a structural part of the wall — it is treated as a permanent fixture regardless of muktzeh status.
Core Principle An item that may be moved on Shabbat does not diminish a wall — it could be removed, restoring the wall's full height. Only truly fixed items count.

🪜 Abaye's Ladder Rule — Permitting Both Courtyards

Setup: A wall 10 tefaḥim high separates two courtyards; neither side may currently use it.
↓
Place a ladder 4 tefaḥim wide against the wall on Side A (Courtyard 1).
↓
Place a ladder 4 tefaḥim wide against the wall on Side B (Courtyard 2).
↓
⚠️ Key condition (wall < 4 tefaḥim wide): The two ladders must be close enough to each other — not greatly distanced.
↓
✅ Result: Both courtyards are permitted — each ladder diminishes the wall for its own side, and proximity links them.
↓
Exception — wall ≥ 4 tefaḥim wide: Even greatly distanced ladders permit both courtyards. The wide wall-top itself serves as the connecting usable surface.
↓
Rav Naḥman / Rabba bar Avuh: Ladder with disconnected rungs — if the bottom rung is ≥4 tefaḥim wide, it diminishes; if not, only the rung itself counts toward height reduction.
