QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rava, when is a traveler's declaration of residence beneath a distant tree valid for eruv teḥumin?
A. Only if he physically walks to the tree before Shabbat begins
B. Only if he could have reached the tree before Shabbat, even if he does not actually go
C. Any time he can see the tree from where he stands
D. Only if another person accompanies him to identify the tree
Correct: B — Rava holds that actually traveling to the tree is not required — it is sufficient that he could have reached it before Shabbat. Abaye's objection from the mishna's phrase 'grew dark while traveling' is resolved: that phrase means he cannot return home, not that he cannot reach the tree.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
How does Rav Ḥisda derive the 2000-cubit Shabbat limit from the Torah?
A. From the verse 'Let no man go out of his place' — place = 2000 cubits in each direction
B. Via a gezera shava between 'place' in Exodus 16:29 and 'place' in the Levite city measurements of Numbers 35:5
C. From the phrase 'outside the borders of the city of refuge' — each side is 2000 cubits
D. It is a Rabbinic enactment (מִדְרַבָּנַן) with no biblical source
Correct: B — Rav Ḥisda uses a verbal analogy (gezera shava): 'place' in Exodus 16:29 corresponds to 'place' in the Levite city law (Numbers 35:5), where the open space is 1000 cubits per side. Combined with the 4-cubit personal space, this yields the 2000-cubit Shabbat limit.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What do the Rabbis learn from the word 'this' (זֶה) in Numbers 35:5 — 'This shall be the open space of the cities'?
A. That the open space applies only to these specific Levite cities, not to Shabbat limits
B. That the measurement must be taken in a circular radius
C. That the 2000-cubit Shabbat boundary is measured as a square, like the Levite open space
D. That each person must measure individually and cannot share an eruv
Correct: C — The Rabbis derive from the demonstrative 'this' that the square shape of the Levite open space is the model for all Shabbat boundary measurements. Rav Ḥananya adds: 'Like this measure shall be the calculations for all who require calculations' — establishing the square-tablet standard.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the core disagreement between Rav Naḥman and Rav Ḥisda about the Meir–Yehuda dispute?
A. Rav Naḥman says the dispute is about whether bread must be placed before Shabbat; Rav Ḥisda says it is about the size of the bread
B. Rav Naḥman says they dispute the case of 'my residence is here'; Rav Ḥisda says they dispute the case of 'my residence is in such-and-such place'
C. Rav Naḥman says only a pauper may use foot-declaration; Rav Ḥisda says only a wealthy person must use bread
D. Rav Naḥman says the halakha follows Rabbi Meir; Rav Ḥisda says it follows Rabbi Yehuda
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman holds that Meir and Yehuda argue specifically about someone who says 'My residence is at my current location' — the in-place declaration. Rav Ḥisda holds their dispute is about 'My residence is in such-and-such distant place.' A baraita is cited in support of Rav Naḥman.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How did Rabba enable Rav Yosef to establish eruv at a palm tree Rav Yosef did not know?
A. He carried Rav Yosef's eruv bread to the tree on his behalf before Shabbat
B. He cited Rabbi Yosei: when two people travel together, one knowing a landmark can establish joint residence for both
C. He declared that the tree was within the city limits, so no eruv was needed for either of them
D. He sent a student ahead to place bread at the tree before nightfall
Correct: B — Rabba cited a baraita in the name of Rabbi Yosei: if two people travel together and one knows a landmark, that person's knowledge can serve as the basis for both to declare residence there. Rabba cited Rabbi Yosei specifically because Rav Yosef would readily accept that authority.
