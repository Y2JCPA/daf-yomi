🔩 Types of Bolts on Shabbat

↓

Permitted in Temple; permitted outside too (Shmuel / R. Yehuda)

↓

🔩 Bolts: Permitted or Prohibited?

Rav Yehuda (Shmuel)
Halakha follows Rabbi Yehuda: a bolt that drags along the ground is permitted even outside the Temple. Similarly, a bolt pressed into the threshold is also permitted outside.
Rava
The leniency for a dragging bolt applies only if it is tied to the door itself — not to the wall or doorpost next to it.
Rav Avya
I saw someone in Neharde'a tying a bolt to a door with only a reed — that is inadequate fastening, and the door may not be locked with it on Shabbat.
Abaye
If the bolt has a handle, it is a proper utensil in its own right. Even without being tied to the door, it is permitted — like a pestle used to secure a door.
Core Distinction The halakha turns on whether the bolt is a vessel (part of the door system) or an unattached object being 'built into' the building when placed.

⛺ Ohel Arai: Temporary Tents on Shabbat

Is this action creating a tent or adding to one that already exists?
↓
Adding to existing tent
Permitted — e.g., unrolling a mat over boat arches that already form a tent structure
Creating a new tent
Prohibited — making ohel arai from scratch is forbidden on Shabbat
↓
Rav Huna's rams: leave 1 tefach covered overnight → unroll fully by day. Each step is 'adding,' not creating.
↓
Curtain for privacy = temporary wall, not a tent → may spread or dismantle freely on Shabbat (Rav, citing Rabbi Ḥiyya)

👰 Bridal Canopy (Chuppah) — When Is It a Tent?

Permitted

  • Spreading out the canopy on Shabbat
  • Dismantling the canopy on Shabbat
  • Horizontal top is less than 1 tefach wide
  • Incline extends less than 1 tefach from center to side
  • Width is less than 1 tefach within 3 tefachim of the top

Prohibited

  • Horizontal top is 1 tefach or more — it is a real tent
  • Width is 1 tefach within 3 tefachim of the top — treated as the top itself
  • Incline extends 1 tefach or more from center — inclines of tents are like tents

Felt Hat (Sayna)

  • Wearing a stiff felt hat is permitted on Shabbat
  • Concern is not that it forms a tent over the head
  • Concern was: might one carry it in public if it falls?
  • Since it fits the head, there is no such concern — it is permitted

🩹 Replacing a Detached Bandage on Shabbat

Mishna: In the Temple one may restore a detached bandage to a wound; outside the Temple it is prohibited (rabbinic decree against spreading ointment).
↓
Baraita / Rabbi Yehuda: If the bandage slipped down, push it up; if it slipped up, push it down. Do not clean the bandage — spreading ointment is a sub-category of the prohibited labor of smoothing.
↓
Rav Ḥisda's Limitation: The leniency (per Rabbi Yehuda) applies only when the bandage fell onto a utensil — not if it fell directly onto the ground, which all agree is prohibited.
↓
Mar bar Rav Ashi: Saw his father replace a bandage that fell onto a pillow. Challenged him — doesn't Rav Ḥisda's rule apply only to utensils?
↓
Rav Ashi's Reply: I do not accept Rav Ḥisda's limitation. The dispute between the Sages applies even if the bandage fell on the ground — and the halakha follows Rabbi Yehuda: it may be replaced.
