🔑 Core Question: What Makes an Eiruv Accessible?

Could one theoretically substitute potential access for actual placement?
Since he could bring the eiruv there — why not treat it as if he did?
↓
Theoretical Argument
Potential access should suffice — if he could bring it, treat it as brought
Rabbi Zeira's Ruling
No — decree due to Festival after Shabbat. Must actually bring it.
↓
Three case studies push the limits:
Tall Cupboard • Pit • Locked Cupboard with Lost Key

🏛️ The Tall Cupboard Dispute

Rav bar Shabba
Objection! If the eiruv is above 10 handbreadths, it's invalid — but couldn't one tilt the cupboard to bring it within 10 handbreadths? Potential access should validate it!
Rava
The cupboard is extremely tall. Tilting it slightly to bring the top within 10 handbreadths would cause it to topple and shatter. Potential access does NOT exist here.
Gemara
But if the cupboard has a window and one has a rope — couldn't he lower the food out through the window?
Rava
We are dealing with a cupboard that has no window — or no rope is at hand. Without a practical means of retrieval, the eiruv remains invalid.
Key Principle Potential access is irrelevant; only genuine practical accessibility of the eiruv counts on Shabbat

🕳️ Eiruv in a Pit — Domain Analysis

Mishna: Eiruv placed in a pit, even 100 cubits deep — valid eiruv
↓
Where is the pit located?
Private Domain
Obvious ruling — private domain extends downward without limit. No novelty.
Public Domain
Problem: eiruv is in private domain (pit), person is in public domain — invalid placement!
✅ Karmelit
He intends to reside above the pit in the karmelit. The pit itself is private domain. Eiruv is valid!
↓
The mishna's novel teaching: a pit within a karmelit functions as a private domain, and the eiruv placed within it is accessible to one standing above it in the karmelit

🌾 Reed Eiruv: Four Dimensions of the Dispute

Detached Reed — Inserted in Ground

  • ✅ Valid eiruv even 100 cubits high
  • Can climb reed or pull it down to access food
  • Ravina: detachment requirement prevents using a tree on Shabbat
  • Both Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Rabbi Yehuda agree on validity

Still-Attached Reed (Growing)

  • ❌ Invalid eiruv per the Mishna
  • Forbidden to use trees or plants on Shabbat
  • Cannot shake, bend, or climb an attached plant
  • Hard reeds = classified as trees; bending is forbidden

Soft Reed vs Hard Reed — Rav Naḥman's Distinction

  • Soft marsh reeds: may be bent and pressed down on Shabbat
  • Hard reeds: treated like trees — prohibited to bend
  • Rav Naḥman permitted students to compress soft reeds for seats
  • Proof from cassia baraita: hard = tree, soft = herb

🔒 Lost Key — Is the Eiruv Still Valid?

Mishna: Eiruv in a cupboard, cupboard locked, key was lost → eiruv is still valid
↓
❓ Challenge: He is in one place and his eiruv is in another — how is he considered to have access to it?
↓
Rav & Shmuel: The mishna speaks of a cupboard built from unmortared bricks. One may break through such a wall on a Festival to access food (Rabbi Meir).
↓
Rabbi Zeira's limitation: This leniency applies only on Yom Tov (Festival), not on Shabbat — so the ruling is more restricted than it appears
↓
Tosefta — Rabbi Eliezer's distinction: Key lost in a city → valid eiruv (people may find and return it). Key lost in an open field → invalid (no one to find it).
↓
Final rule: accessibility must be realistic and not merely theoretical — even a lost key in a city implies likely retrieval
