⚖️ Does One Courtyard Suffice for a Side Post?

Rav
Rabbi Shimon's unified-domain leniency applies only when residents did not establish a separate eiruv per courtyard. With an eiruv, standard rules apply — and the alleyway needs multiple courtyards with multiple houses.
Shmuel
Rabbi Shimon's rule applies whether or not residents established an eiruv. All domains — roofs, enclosures, courtyards — remain unified in every case. Even a single courtyard with a single house may suffice.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Agrees with Shmuel: the unified domain applies in both cases. Even a vineyard path combined with one courtyard can allow a side post or cross beam to render carrying permitted.
Core Question The mishna says an alleyway must have houses and courtyards opening into it — implying plural. But does this bar leniency entirely, or can Rabbi Shimon's broad domain theory override it?

⚖️ Rabbi Shimon's Scope: Rav vs. Shmuel vs. Rabbi Yoḥanan

Rav
Rabbi Shimon's unified domain applies only without an eiruv. Residents may then carry vessels that rested in their courtyard at Shabbat onset — but not vessels from other courtyards. With an eiruv, normal divisions apply.
Shmuel
Rabbi Shimon's unified domain applies with or without an eiruv. The unification of roofs, enclosures, and courtyards is inherent — not contingent on eiruv status.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Agrees with Shmuel entirely. Rabbi Yoḥanan consistently follows Rabbi Shimon's broad position — even a vineyard path paired with one courtyard suffices for the side post's leniency.
Mishna: Rabbi Shimon's Rule גגות וקרפיפות וחצרות — Roofs, enclosures, and courtyards are all one domain regarding vessels present at Shabbat onset, even if later moved between these areas.

🤫 Rabbi Elazar's Objection — and Shmuel's Silence

אין לנו אלא לשון משנתנו — We have only the wording of our mishna
The Challenge Rav Beruna publicly recited Shmuel's ruling: even a single house with a single courtyard suffices for a side post to permit carrying in an alleyway.

Rabbi Elazar challenged him sharply: "Did the Master himself not say — 'We have only the wording of our mishna'?" The mishna compares an alleyway to its courtyards as a courtyard is to its houses — the plural implies multiple courtyards and multiple houses are required!

Shmuel heard this and fell silent. The Gemara then asks: did silence mean he accepted the objection and retracted — or did he simply not respond?

🪟 Gentile Neighbor: Windows vs. Doors — Rav's Ruling

Alleyway Case (Rav's Ruling)

  • One side of alleyway: gentile resident
  • Other side: Jewish resident
  • Jew's house connected to other Jewish houses via windows only — no shared doors
  • Rav rules: permitted — window connections suffice to unify the Jewish houses
  • The gentile's side does not block carrying on the Jewish side

Courtyard Case (Abaye Asks Rav Yosef)

  • One side of courtyard: gentile
  • Other side: Jew whose space connects to others only via windows
  • Abaye asks: Does Rav's ruling extend to courtyards too?
  • Answer: Yes — Rav's ruling applies equally to alleyways and courtyards
  • Abaye's insight: Rav requires houses + courtyards in an alleyway precisely because without them, window-connections alone cannot define a valid domain
