🏠 What Unifies a Space? Partitions, Sleep, and Eiruv Obligations

Rabbi Meir
Whether using wine or bread, you always need BOTH an eiruv (joining of courtyards) AND a shituf (merging of alleyways). Neither alone suffices.
The Rabbis
With bread: EITHER an eiruv alone (which covers both courtyard and alley) OR a shituf alone suffices. The two are not always required together.
Rav Naḥman
The Shammai–Hillel dispute about five groups applies only to a mesifas (peg partition). With a sturdy 10-handbreadth partition, all agree: each group needs its own eiruv.
Gemara (Sleep Rule)
Brothers eating at their father's table but sleeping in separate houses require separate eiruvin — sleep location, not eating location, defines eiruv residency.
Core Question Does shared space (a hall, a table, a courtyard) create legal eiruv unity — or must physical separation and sleep location always divide obligations?

🍷 Wine vs Bread: Rabbi Meir vs the Rabbis

Rabbi Meir
Wine: both eiruv + shituf required.
Bread: same — both required. One never replaces the other.
The Rabbis
Wine: agrees — both required.
Bread: EITHER the eiruv alone (permits both spaces) OR the shituf alone is sufficient.
Rav Giddel / Rav
Clarifies the Rabbis' position: 'Either' means eiruv with bread renders carrying permitted both in the courtyard and the alley; OR shituf alone covers the alley.
Rav Yehuda / Rav Huna
Halakha follows Rabbi Meir. Rav Huna qualifies: no formal ruling was issued, but custom follows him — if asked, instruct to do both.
Practical Takeaway Establish a bread eiruv for the courtyard AND a separate shituf for the alleyway. Custom is stringent per Rabbi Meir.

🏛️ Five Groups in One Hall — Beit Shammai vs Beit Hillel

Beit Shammai — Stringent

  • Each of the five groups must contribute separately to the eiruv
  • Even a mesifas (peg-partition) creates enough division
  • Physical separation — however slight — demands separate eiruvin
  • Concedes: if eiruv comes to them (placed in their hall), one eiruv suffices

Beit Hillel — Lenient

  • One shared eiruv suffices for all groups in the hall
  • A mesifas alone does NOT mandate separate contributions
  • Concedes: separate rooms or upper stories DO require individual eiruvin
  • Functional unity (shared hall, shared courtyard) enables a single eiruv

🛏️ Sleep = Residence: Brothers and the Eiruv Rule

The Scenario: Brothers eat all their meals at their father's table but sleep in their own separate houses within the same courtyard
↓
The Derivation: Learn from the mishna — one's place of sleep establishes eiruv residence, not one's place of eating
↓
Default Rule: Each brother must contribute separately to the eiruv. If one forgot, he renders the others prohibited from carrying
↓
Exception — Eiruv Comes to Them: If the eiruv is placed in the father's house, or if there is no other resident in the courtyard except the father, the brothers' shared eating unifies them — one eiruv suffices
↓
Non-Residences: A gatehouse, porch, or balcony in a neighbor's courtyard does NOT render the owner prohibited — these structures are not considered residences for eiruv purposes
↓
Ben Nappaḥa Precedent: Rabbi Yehuda reported that a man with houses in five courtyards came before the Sages — they ruled that only the primary place of residence creates eiruv obligations in the other courtyards
