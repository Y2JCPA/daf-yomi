QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Meir, when establishing an eiruv with bread in a shared courtyard/alley, what is required?
A. Bread eiruv alone suffices for both the courtyard and the alley
B. Both an eiruv (courtyard) and a shituf (alley) are always required
C. A shituf alone is sufficient when bread is used instead of wine
D. Only a shituf is needed — the courtyard eiruv is optional
Correct: B — Rabbi Meir holds that both an eiruv (joining of courtyards) and a shituf (merging of alleyways) are required regardless of whether bread or wine is used. This is also the accepted custom.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Rav Naḥman limits the Beit Shammai–Beit Hillel dispute about five groups in a hall to which specific situation?
A. Partitions that reach the ceiling of the hall
B. Any partition at least ten handbreadths high
C. A mesifas — a partition of pegs or a flimsy divider
D. Upper stories and separate rooms only
Correct: C — Rav Naḥman rules that the Shammai/Hillel dispute applies only to a mesifas (partition of pegs). With a sturdy ten-handbreadth partition, all agree a separate eiruv contribution is required from each group.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the ruling of Rabbi Yehuda the Keen (הַסַּבָּר) that the Gemara accepts as halakha?
A. Beit Shammai and Hillel disputed only cases with ceiling-height partitions
B. Partitions not reaching the ceiling always require separate eiruvin
C. Shammai and Hillel agreed about ceiling-height partitions (separate required); they disputed only partitions not reaching the ceiling
D. The dispute applies equally to all partition types and heights
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehuda the Keen taught that where partitions reach the ceiling, all agree a separate eiruv is required. The Shammai/Hillel dispute is specifically about partitions that do NOT reach the ceiling. Rav Naḥman rules the halakha follows this view.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Brothers eat all meals at their father's table but sleep in separate houses in the same courtyard. What does the Gemara rule?
A. Shared meals unify them — one eiruv contribution covers all
B. Since they share a courtyard, one eiruv always suffices
C. Each brother must contribute a separate eiruv because sleep determines residence
D. Only the eldest brother must contribute; others are included automatically
Correct: C — The Gemara derives that sleep location — not eating location — determines one's eiruv residence. Since each brother sleeps in his own house, each must contribute separately to the eiruv.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Under what condition do the brothers NOT need separate eiruv contributions, despite sleeping in separate houses?
A. When there are fewer than five brothers total
B. When the eiruv is placed in their father's house, or when there is no other resident in the courtyard besides the father
C. When they use wine instead of bread for the eiruv
D. When the courtyard has a shared gate that all brothers use
Correct: B — The mishna rules that when the eiruv 'comes to them' (is placed in the father's house where they eat) or when there is no other resident in the courtyard besides the father, the brothers' shared eating unifies them — one eiruv suffices for all.
