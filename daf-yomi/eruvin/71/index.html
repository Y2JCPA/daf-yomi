⚖️ Can One Renounce Rights (Bitul Reshut) on Shabbat?

Beit Shammai
Renunciation of domain = acquisition of domain. Acquisition is forbidden on Shabbat — therefore renunciation is also forbidden.
Beit Hillel
Renunciation is not equivalent to acquisition. One may renounce rights on Shabbat, thereby restoring permission to carry for neighbors.
Rav Naḥman
One who acquires a convert's property renders carrying prohibited — but bitul reshut is effective to restore permission.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
The baraitot implying renunciation cannot work follow Beit Shammai. The accepted halakha follows Beit Hillel.
Core Dispute Does renunciation of a Shabbat domain constitute a forbidden act of acquisition, or is it a distinct and permitted act?

⚖️ Beit Shammai vs. Beit Hillel — And the Amoraim Who Explain Them

Beit Shammai
Renunciation of domain = acquisition of domain.
Acquisition forbidden on Shabbat → renunciation also forbidden.
No bitul reshut on Shabbat.
Beit Hillel
Renunciation ≠ acquisition.
Bitul reshut is permitted on Shabbat and restores carrying rights to all neighbors.
Ulla
Beit Hillel permit renunciation because it is like selecting the high-quality ones — a category of permitted Shabbat activity.
Abaye
Rejects Ulla: When a gentile dies on Shabbat, what relevance is 'turning toward high-quality ones'? The real dispute is simply whether renunciation equals acquisition.
Conclusion Abaye's framing prevails: Beit Shammai equates renunciation with acquisition (forbidden); Beit Hillel denies this equation (permitted).

🍷 Mishna: Homeowner Partners With Both Neighbors — Eiruv Required?

Wine + Wine (same item)

  • Homeowner shares wine with neighbor A AND wine with neighbor B
  • Rav: Exempt only if wine is in one shared vessel
  • Abaye: Even separate vessels — wine+wine can be mixed, treated as unified
  • Result (Rabbis): No eiruv needed — all are one household

Wine + Oil (different items)

  • Homeowner shares wine with neighbor A AND oil with neighbor B
  • Cannot be mixed — not a unified substance
  • Rabbis: Eiruv IS required — not one partnership
  • Rabbi Shimon: No eiruv needed even here — any shared partnership with the homeowner suffices

🛤️ Eiruv and Shittuf — Rabbi Meir's Dual Requirement

Baraita: One must establish an eiruv between courtyards AND a shittuf (merging) for the alleyway — even though either might suffice alone
↓
Why both? So that the laws of eiruv will not be forgotten by children — if only shittuf were used, future generations would never learn about eiruv
↓
Rabbi Meir: One may NOT rely on shittuf alone to permit carrying in courtyards — a proper eiruv between courtyards is required
↓
Rabbis: One may rely on the shittuf of the alleyway even to permit carrying in the courtyards that open into it
↓
Rav Yehuda citing Rav: Halakha follows Rabbi Meir. Two separate rulings issued because each case has unique leniencies that might have led to a different conclusion
