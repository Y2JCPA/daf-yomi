🌿 Which Foods May Establish an Eiruv?

עירוב — shared food establishes shared Shabbat domain
Core Question of Daf 28 Rav permits humble, edible plants — cress, purslane, sweet clover — for establishing a Shabbat eiruv. But contradictions arise: Rav first forbids then permits green grain; debates surround sweet clover's health risks. The daf further examines blessings over unusual foods, the legal status of heart of palm, and — memorably — a young school child whose teaching becomes normative halakha.

🌿 Eiruv-Eligible vs. Ineligible Foods

✅ Valid for Eiruv

  • Cress (גרגיר)
  • Purslane (רגילה)
  • Sweet clover — for those with many children
  • Median clover — superior quality, universally safe
  • Dodder (כשות)
  • Garden-variety green grain (Babylonian custom)
  • Salt of glasswort (the ash, not the plant itself)
  • Moist beans
  • Raw beets (Rav Hamnuna's ruling)

❌ Invalid or Disputed

  • Green grain — Rav's pre-Babylonian ruling
  • Unripe dates from palms that eventually ripen
  • Sweet clover — for those without children (harms reproduction)
  • Glasswort itself (inedible)
  • Raw beet — 'kills a healthy person' (Rav Ḥisda's concern)

📜 Does Fowl Fall Under Second-Tithe Purchases?

Tanna I (Includes Fowl)
In a klal, prat, klal structure, the final generalization is primary. We include anything that resembles the detail, even loosely — so fowl is permitted with second-tithe money.
Tanna II (Excludes Fowl)
The first generalization is primary. Like a simple klal u'prat, we include only what strictly resembles the detail — produce of the ground. Fowl does not grow from the ground.
The Exegetical Principle at Stake Both tannaim apply כלל ופרט וכלל (generalization-detail-generalization). Their dispute: does the first or last generalization govern the scope of inclusion?

🌾 Rav's Evolving Ruling on Green Grain

Rav (pre-Babylonia): Green grain may NOT be used for eiruv
↓
Contradiction: Rav also said dodder and green grain MAY be used for eiruv!
↓
Pre-Babylonia Ruling
People didn't eat green grain as food → ineligible for eiruv
Post-Babylonia Ruling
Babylonians ate garden grain regularly → now valid food → eligible for eiruv
↓
Final resolution: Garden-variety green grain — valid for eiruv. Custom determines food status.

🧒 The Young School Child's Teaching (28b)

Rabbi Zeira, exhausted from study, sits at the entrance of Rav Yehuda bar Ami's academy
↓
His goal: stand up for the Sages as they pass — earning reward for kavod haTorah without active learning
↓
A young child exits the study hall. Rabbi Zeira asks: "What did your teacher teach today?"
↓
Child answers: Dodder → borei pri ha'adama; Green grain → shehakol nihyeh bidvaro
↓
The Gemara rules: The halakha follows the young school child. Reason: dodder is fully ripe; green grain is not — an unripe food merits only the generic blessing
