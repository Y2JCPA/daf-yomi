Eruvin 28 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why did Rav reverse his ruling and permit green grain for eiruv?
A. A contradicting Torah verse was brought to his attention
B. He arrived in Babylonia and observed that people ate garden grain as common food
C. A baraita overruled his earlier position
D. He ruled leniently due to widespread famine
Correct: B — When Rav came to Babylonia, he saw that the local population regularly ate garden-variety green grain. Since food eligibility for eiruv depends on whether people actually eat it, the Babylonian custom made it valid.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What blessing did the young school child say should be recited over dodder (כשות)?
A. שהכל נהיה בדברו
B. בורא פרי העץ
C. בורא פרי האדמה
D. המוציא לחם מן הארץ
Correct: C — The child taught that dodder receives 'borei pri ha'adama' because it is fully ripened produce. The Gemara explicitly rules that the halakha follows this child.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rava, what is the practical halakhic difference between Rabbi Yehuda and the first Tanna regarding heart of palm?
A. Whether heart of palm may be purchased with second-tithe money
B. Whether heart of palm is susceptible to ritual impurity
C. The blessing recited before eating heart of palm
D. Whether heart of palm requires tithing
Correct: C — Rava argues the dispute is about the blessing: Rav Yehuda says 'borei pri ha'adama' (it is edible food and counts as a fruit), while Rav says 'shehakol' (it is not the palm tree's intended fruit).
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What does the Tanna who INCLUDES fowl under second-tithe purchases hold about klal-prat-klal exegesis?
A. The first generalization governs; only produce of the ground is included
B. The final generalization is primary; we include whatever resembles the detail
C. Both generalizations cancel each other out
D. The detail alone determines what is included
Correct: B — This Tanna holds that in a klal-prat-klal structure, the final generalization is primary, broadening inclusion to anything resembling the detail — including fowl, which is eaten like produce.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Zeira sit at the entrance of the academy when exhausted, rather than going home to rest?
A. To guard the academy from unwanted visitors
B. To stand before the Sages as they pass and earn reward for honoring Torah scholars
C. To review his learning in a quieter environment
D. To wait for students who had questions for him
Correct: B — Rabbi Zeira says explicitly: 'When the Sages go in and out, I shall stand up before them and receive reward.' Honoring Torah scholars is itself a mitzvah, so even physical exhaustion could be productive time.
