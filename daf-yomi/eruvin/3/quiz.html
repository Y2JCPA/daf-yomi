5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Ḥama son of Rabba bar Avuh, what physical object does 'amaltera' refer to?
A. Cedar poles protruding from alleyway walls
B. Decorative wood carvings shaped like birds' nests
C. A stone lintel spanning the alleyway entrance
D. A hanging curtain used as a partition
Correct: B — Rav Ḥama says amaltera = decorative carvings shaped like birds' nests. Rav Dimi (coming from Eretz Yisrael) says it refers to cedar poles. Both explanations ground the ruling in the cornice's eye-catching quality.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
In the first version of Rabba's ruling (about a structure partially above 20 cubits), what is the outcome for an alleyway cross beam?
A. Unfit — the beam blows in the wind if thinned
B. Fit — we mentally 'thin' the portion above 20 cubits
C. Fit — but only if the beam is made of cedar
D. Unfit — the entire beam must be below 20 cubits
Correct: B — In Version 1, the alleyway is fit because we apply the principle of 'thinning' — we disregard the portion above 20 cubits. The sukka, however, is unfit in this version because removing the upper portion would leave insufficient shade.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How many handbreadths make up the 'small cubit' used for sukka height and alleyway cross beam measurements?
A. Four handbreadths
B. Five handbreadths
C. Six handbreadths
D. Seven handbreadths
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman (per Abaye) says the cubit for sukka height and alleyway height is five handbreadths. This makes the 20-cubit maximum physically lower, applying the height restriction more stringently.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Rava from Parzakya explain why sukka roofing (Version 2) is more lenient than an alleyway cross beam regarding partial height?
A. A sukka has Torah-law status so the Sages were lenient with it
B. A sukka is erected for the individual who personally ensures the roofing is valid
C. An alleyway must accommodate many people so standards are higher
D. Cedar poles used in alleyways are more durable than sukka roofing material
Correct: B — Rava from Parzakya: a sukka is generally built for an individual, so he 'casts responsibility on himself' — he is personally reminded to check that the roofing is valid. An alleyway serves many and may be overlooked, requiring stricter standards.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rava citing Rav Naḥman, what is the general rule about cubit measurements used by the Sages?
A. All Rabbinic cubits are five handbreadths, except kilayim which uses six
B. All Rabbinic cubits are six handbreadths, except sukka and alleyway which use five
C. All Rabbinic cubits are six handbreadths with absolutely no exceptions
D. The cubit size is always determined by the local custom of the community
Correct: B — Rava (citing Rav Naḥman) states: all cubits mentioned by the Sages are large cubits of six handbreadths — except those connected with sukka and alleyway, which are small cubits of five handbreadths. The five-handbreadth cubit creates a stricter height ceiling in those two domains.
