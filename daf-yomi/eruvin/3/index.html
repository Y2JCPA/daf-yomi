🏛️ What Is an Amaltera?

Rav Ḥama
An amaltera refers to decorative wood carvings shaped like birds' nests — ornate woodwork above the entrance that attracts the eye.
Rav Dimi
An amaltera refers to cedar poles (arzei) protruding from the walls at the alleyway entrance.
Gemara
The one who says cedar poles would a fortiori permit carvings — carvings are even more eye-catching. But the reverse is not true.
Gemara
Cedar poles attract attention due to their great value; carvings attract due to beauty. Both serve the purpose: conspicuousness compensates for being above 20 cubits.
Core Principle The 20-cubit limit is about conspicuousness — if a marker is truly eye-catching, it remains valid even at a greater height.

🔄 Two Contradictory Versions of Rabba

Version 1 — Rabba

  • Alleyway cross beam: ✅ FIT
  • 'Thin' the section above 20 cubits
  • Sukka roofing: ❌ UNFIT
  • Thinning would leave less shade than sun
  • Ravina: Sukka is Torah law → Sages were strict
  • Rava (Parzakya): Individual responsibility for sukka

Version 2 — Rav Adda bar Mattana

  • Alleyway cross beam: ❌ UNFIT
  • Thinned beam becomes unstable in wind
  • Sukka roofing: ✅ FIT
  • 'Thin' the section above 20 cubits
  • Ravina: Alleyway is Rabbinic → Sages were lenient
  • Rava (Parzakya): Individual erects/monitors own sukka

🌿 Kilayim: Vineyard Measurement Rules

Clearing in a vineyard (middle section stripped of vines)
Beit Shammai: 24 cubits  |  Beit Hillel: 16 cubits
↓
If clearing is less than 16 cubits → one may not sow foreign seeds there
↓
Perimeter of a vineyard (vacant area between vines and surrounding fence)
Beit Shammai: 16 cubits  |  Beit Hillel: 12 cubits
↓
These measurements use large cubits of 6 handbreadths per Rav Naḥman — ensuring greater physical separation

Resolution of internal tension: Rav Naḥman follows Rabbis who hold densely-planted vines still form a valid vineyard — so only the large-cubit stringency applies here

✅ Rava's Final Rule on Cubit Sizes

כל אמות שאמרו חכמים של ששה טפחים, חוץ מסוכה ומבוי שהן של חמישה
Rava citing Rav Naḥman All cubits mentioned by the Sages are large cubits of six handbreadths — EXCEPT those used in connection with sukka and alleyway, which are small cubits of five handbreadths.

Why smaller for sukka and alleyway? Because the 20-cubit maximum height is a stringency — using a smaller cubit makes 20 cubits physically lower, thus applying the limit more strictly. In all other contexts (minimums, clearances), the large cubit yields the stringent result.
