🔥 Shared Space: Who Wins?

Mishna
A mound ≥10 handbreadths high — its use belongs to the balcony. Lower than ten — its use belongs to the courtyard.
Objection
If the lower mound belongs only to the courtyard, why can't the balcony still use it? This is no different from residents on two sides of a wall!
Resolution
"To the courtyard" means ALSO to the courtyard — both balcony and courtyard residents can reach it. Since two domains share it, carrying is prohibited for both.
Proof
The mishna confirms: when the mound is near the balcony it belongs to the balcony; when far, it belongs to the courtyard. Proximity determines access, not exclusive ownership!
Core Principle When two domains can both access a space, neither holds exclusive rights — Shabbat carrying is prohibited for both parties.

🪣 The Cistern Dilemma: Can the Balcony Reach It?

Problem: Cistern water sits inside a pit — how can its surface be ≥10 handbreadths high for the balcony's use?
↓
Rav Yitzḥak b. Rav Yehuda: Cistern is full of water — draw from the top near the balcony!
↓
Objection
As water is drawn, the level drops — the distance exceeds 10 handbreadths!
Response
Since permitted when full, remains permitted even when partially emptied
↓
Counter: Then prohibit it when empty and permit when full — why extend the leniency?
↓
Abaye: Cistern filled with produce — the upper layer is accessible to the balcony!

🌾 Abaye's Brilliant Solution

בור מלא פירות של טבל — שאינו יכול לעשרן בשבת
Untithed Produce (Tevel) Abaye explains: the cistern is filled with untithed produce (tevel). Since one may not tithe produce on Shabbat, it cannot be removed during Shabbat. Therefore, the pile height remains constant throughout the entire day — the top layer stays within reach of the balcony. The mishna's parallel treatment of cistern and rock supports this reading: just as only the rock's upper surface matters, so too only the upper portion of the cistern contents is relevant. The language of the mishna is thus precise in grouping them together.

🪨 Rock vs. 🪣 Cistern — Why State Both Cases?

🪨 Rock

  • Flat surface roughly level with the balcony floor
  • Residents use the top surface directly — no depth issue
  • Height is fixed — no risk of diminishing
  • Case needed to teach: near the balcony vs. far (proximity matters)
  • Straightforward access once height requirement is met

🪣 Cistern

  • Contents sit inside a pit — level fluctuates
  • Abaye: must be filled with untithed produce
  • Tevel cannot be removed on Shabbat → height stays constant
  • Case needed to teach the novel solution of tevel
  • Teaches that 'use' of the upper layer suffices even when bulk is lower

🏊 Two Balconies Over Water: One Partition for Both?

Setup: Two balconies extend over a body of water, one above the other. The upper balcony cuts a hole and erects a partition — may both draw water on Shabbat?
↓
Rav Adda bar Ahava: The lower balcony residents climb up to the upper balcony by ladder to draw water. Since they are physically in the upper balcony when drawing, the upper partition covers their act of drawing.
↓
Abaye: Uses the literary style of 'there is no need' — the mishna says: even if only the upper erected a partition and not the lower, both are permitted.
↓
Why? The two balconies are within ten handbreadths of each other. Their proximity makes them a single unit — the upper partition covers both.
↓
Parallel (Shmuel): A roof adjacent to a public domain requires a fixed ladder from the courtyard to the roof — establishing regular, intentional access — before the courtyard residents may carry there.
