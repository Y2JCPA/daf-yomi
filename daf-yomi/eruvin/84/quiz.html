QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does Abaye specify that the cistern must be filled with untithed produce (tevel) rather than ordinary produce?
A. Tevel is heavier and stays compressed, making the pile more stable
B. Since tevel cannot be tithed on Shabbat, it cannot be removed — the pile height remains constant all day
C. Tevel is ownerless and belongs to neither the balcony nor the courtyard
D. The Rabbis declared that any produce in a cistern counts as part of the balcony's domain
Correct: B — Since one may not tithe tevel on Shabbat, the produce cannot be removed. The pile height remains constant throughout Shabbat, keeping the top layer within the balcony's reach the entire day — solving the problem of diminishing access.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
The Gemara reinterprets 'its use belongs to the courtyard' as 'also to the courtyard.' What halakhic consequence follows from this reading?
A. The courtyard has priority over the balcony for all mounds below 10 handbreadths
B. The balcony may use the lower mound only on weekdays, not on Shabbat
C. Since both the courtyard and the balcony can access the mound, carrying is prohibited for both parties
D. Only an eruv chatzeirot can resolve the shared access and restore carrying rights
Correct: C — By reading 'to the courtyard' as 'also to the courtyard,' the Gemara concludes that both domains share access to the lower mound. When two domains share a space, neither holds exclusive rights and carrying is prohibited for both — mirroring the rule of a jointly used area.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rav Naḥman neutralize the proof from the baraita about the 'prohibited ledge between this and the other'?
A. He argues the baraita is a minority opinion and not normative halakha
B. He explains the baraita describes a wall of 19 handbreadths with a protruding ledge — below 10 belongs to the lower residents, above 10 to the upper, and the ledge between is prohibited because both sides can reach it
C. He claims the baraita only applies to stone walls, not wooden partitions
D. He reads the baraita as referring to a public domain rather than a private courtyard
Correct: B — Rav Naḥman explains the baraita describes a specific structure: a 19-handbreadth wall with a protruding ledge. The lower zone (below 10) belongs to courtyard residents, the upper zone (above 10) to upper-story residents, and the ledge at the junction is prohibited because it is reachable from both sides — no general proof about Rav vs. Shmuel's dispute can be drawn from this.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Abaye, why are lower balcony residents permitted to draw water even when only the upper balcony erected a partition?
A. The lower balcony automatically inherits any partition built by a neighbor above
B. The two balconies are within ten handbreadths of each other, making them a single unit — the upper partition covers both
C. The lower balcony paid a share of the partition's construction cost
D. A partition over water always applies to all balconies regardless of their height difference
Correct: B — Abaye's ruling applies when the two balconies are within ten handbreadths of each other. Their proximity makes them effectively one unit, so the upper balcony's partition extends coverage to the lower one as well. Abaye frames this using the 'there is no need' literary style — the main ruling covers both cases.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What condition does Shmuel require before residents of a courtyard may carry on a roof that is adjacent to a public domain?
A. The roof must be enclosed by walls on at least three sides
B. The courtyard residents must merge their eruv with the public domain
C. There must be a fixed (permanent) ladder connecting the courtyard to the roof
D. The roof must be lower than ten handbreadths above the courtyard floor
Correct: C — Shmuel rules that a fixed, permanent ladder — not a movable one — must connect the courtyard to the roof. This establishes the roof as regularly and intentionally accessible to courtyard residents, giving them a defined right of use that permits carrying there on Shabbat.
