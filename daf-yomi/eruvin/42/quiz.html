QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Gemara's final resolution, what does it mean that produce was 'returned to its original place' (per Rabbi Neḥemya)?
A. It was returned intentionally by a Jew before nightfall
B. It was returned unwittingly — not through intentional action
C. It was returned by a gentile on the person's behalf
D. It was brought back within 4 cubits of the original location
Correct: B — The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction in the baraita by reading 'returned to its place' as meaning it was returned unwittingly. If returned intentionally, the first tanna forbids eating it even after return; Rabbi Neḥemya permits it.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
When gentiles surround a valley with a partition on Shabbat, what does Rav Huna permit the person inside?
A. Walking and carrying freely throughout the entire enclosed area
B. Walking 2,000 cubits and carrying freely within those 2,000 cubits
C. Walking 2,000 cubits but carrying only 4 cubits, like a karmelit
D. Only 4 cubits of walking since the partition was built on Shabbat
Correct: C — Rav Huna agrees with Shmuel that walking extends to 2,000 cubits. However, he holds that carrying is restricted to only 4 cubits — like a karmelit — because the area resembles a partition breached entirely, open toward a forbidden zone.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
On what case do both Rav and Shmuel agree that the halakha follows Rabban Gamliel?
A. A pen (דיר) on Shabbat
B. A stable (סהר) on Shabbat
C. A boat (ספינה) on Shabbat
D. A valley surrounded by a gentile partition
Correct: C — While Rav follows Rabban Gamliel for pen, stable, and boat, Shmuel only agrees regarding the boat. Both Rav and Shmuel thus rule that a person on a boat on Shabbat may move freely throughout it, in accordance with Rabban Gamliel.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is Rabba's explanation for why a person on a boat on Shabbat may move freely throughout the boat?
A. The boat's walls are not halachic mechitzot, so techum does not apply on water
B. The boat continuously moves the person, so he cannot stay in any fixed 4 cubits
C. The person acquired residence within the boat's partitions before Shabbat began
D. A boat is a private domain (reshut hayachid) where Shabbat limits do not apply
Correct: C — Rabba explains that since the person acquired his Shabbat residence within the partitions of the boat while it was still day (before Shabbat), the entire boat is considered as if it is his personal 4-cubit domain, allowing full movement within it.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the practical halakhic difference between Rabba's and Rabbi Zeira's explanations for the boat exception?
A. Whether the exception applies on rivers versus open seas
B. Whether one may carry objects across the entire length of the boat
C. What applies if the walls of the boat are breached after Shabbat begins
D. Whether a person who boarded the boat on Shabbat may move freely
Correct: C — If the boat's walls are breached after Shabbat begins: per Rabba, the person is now restricted (he is no longer enclosed within valid partitions); per Rabbi Zeira, he remains permitted to move freely (the continuous-motion argument still applies even without intact walls).
