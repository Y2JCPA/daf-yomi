📏 Cross Beam Width: One Handbreadth or More?

Mishna
A cross beam (korah) must be one handbreadth wide to render an alleyway valid.
Gemara Challenge
But a standard small brick is 1½ handbreadths wide — how can one handbreadth suffice to hold it?
Resolution
The beam receives the brick for one handbreadth. The remaining ½ handbreadth is filled with plaster — a small amount on each side — and the brick stands in place.
Rabba bar Rav Huna
The beam must be sturdy enough to hold a brick. But its supports need not be sturdy enough to hold both beam and brick — that is too strict.
Rav Sheshet
A mat draped over a beam but raised 3 handbreadths from the ground is neither a valid beam nor a valid partition — it fails on both counts.
Key Principle The beam need only receive the brick — halakhic fictions (plaster, lavud) can bridge small physical gaps.

🔵 When Are Two Partial Cross Beams Valid?

Two beams project from opposite walls but do not meet in the middle
↓
Gap < 3 handbreadths
Valid — principle of lavud: objects within 3 handbreadths are considered joined
Gap = 4 handbreadths
Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: Must bring another beam — lavud only applies up to 3
Two narrow beams side by side
If combined width ≥ 1 handbreadth: valid, even if each alone is insufficient
↓
Two narrow beams stacked vertically (Rabbi Yosei son of R. Yehuda): visualize the upper as lower and vice versa — if combined they hold a brick, the beam is valid

🔵 Cross Beam Shape & Material Rules

Beam ConditionHalakhic RuleNovel Teaching / Reason
Made of straw or reedsConsidered as if made of metalEven materials whose own kind are not sturdy qualify — we say: consider it as if it were sturdy
Curved beam (inside alleyway)Considered as if it were straightIf the curved portion exits the alleyway, it does not count toward the required width
Round beamConsidered as if it were squareNeeded to teach: circumference = 3 × diameter (π ≈ 3), so a round beam of circumference 3 = diameter 1
4-handbreadth wide beamValid even without structural sturdinessRav: width alone (≥ 4 handbreadths) renders the alleyway fit, independent of load-bearing capacity

🌊 Solomon's Sea: Deriving π ≈ 3 and 150 Ritual Baths

The verse (I Kings 7:23): Solomon's Sea — 10 cubits from brim to brim, and 30 cubits its circumference
↓
Therefore: circumference = 3 × diameter → the Talmud uses π ≈ 3 as its working ratio for all halakhic calculations
↓
Objection: The brim adds to the outer diameter — making the outer circumference larger than 30. Isn't the ratio off?
↓
Rav Pappa: The brim was thin as a lily petal — negligible. And the circumference was measured from the inside, matching the inner diameter.
↓
Volume challenge: 10×10×5 cubits (square) = 500 cubic cubits = ~166 ritual baths. But a round vessel holds ¼ less → 375 cubic cubits = ~125 ritual baths. Neither matches the stated 150.
↓
Rami bar Yeḥezkel resolves it: Bottom 3 cubits were square (300 cu. cubits = 100 ritual baths) + top 2 cubits were round (150 cu. cubits = 50 ritual baths) = exactly 150 ritual baths

🚪 Side Post (Lechi) Requirements — Where They Agree and Differ

All Agree

  • Height: minimum 10 handbreadths
  • Thickness: any amount whatsoever
  • Must be placed at alleyway entrance
  • Cannot be raised 3+ handbreadths off the ground (Rava)
  • Cannot be distanced 3+ handbreadths from the wall (Rava)
  • A side post placed mid-alleyway permits only the inner half

Rabbi Yosei (Strict)

  • Width: minimum 3 handbreadths
  • Must be a substantial, visible post
  • Halakha: NOT in accordance with Rabbi Yosei
  • Shmuel: his ruling is rejected for side posts

Majority / Common Practice

  • Width: any minimal amount suffices
  • Abaye (to Rava bar Rav Ḥanan): "Go out and see what the people do"
  • Popular practice: rely on minimal-width side posts
  • Practical halakha follows common custom here
