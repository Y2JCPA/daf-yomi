⚔️ Who Is an Apostate? The Great Dispute

Rav Huna
An apostate Jew is one who desecrates Shabbat in public — he is no longer considered a Jew in any legal sense.
Rav Naḥman
In accordance with whose opinion? If the Rabbis — they say one sin does not make a full apostate, unless it is idolatry!
Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak
Rav Huna spoke specifically about eiruvin: a public Shabbat violator may not renounce domain rights, since he is treated like a gentile for this purpose.
Rav Ashi
Rav Huna's statement IS general — a public Shabbat desecrator is a full apostate, as the baraita on Leviticus 1:2 confirms, excluding him even from bringing sacrifices.
Core Question Is public Shabbat desecration enough to define someone as a full apostate in all law — or only for eiruvin?

🏠 Mishna Cases: Renouncing Rights After Forgetting the Eiruv

One or more residents forgot to join the eiruv before Shabbat
↓
Forgetful resident renounces to neighbors
His house is prohibited to him; courtyard is permitted for all others
Neighbors renounce their rights to him
He may carry into courtyard; they are prohibited from the courtyard
Two forgot; others renounce to both of them
They prohibit each other — the two share the courtyard but each house remains private
↓
A single resident can both give and receive rights. Two residents may give rights together but cannot simultaneously receive from each other.

🕯️ When May One Renounce Domain Rights?

Beit Shammai

  • Only while it is still day (before Shabbat begins)
  • Renunciation must occur before nightfall
  • After Shabbat starts — it is too late
  • Renunciation is like a formal legal act: requires daytime validity

Beit Hillel

  • Valid even after nightfall once Shabbat has begun
  • Renunciation is a mere verbal declaration — no time restriction
  • Practical: the need arises on Shabbat when eiruv is discovered missing
  • Lenient ruling — halakha follows Beit Hillel

📿 The Coral Ring Story — Shame vs. Brazenness

A certain man went out wearing a coral ring into the public domain on Shabbat — a prohibited act of carrying
↓
He noticed Rabbi Yehuda Nesia approaching and quickly covered the ring — showing deference to rabbinic authority
↓
Although he was actively desecrating Shabbat, he still felt shame — indicating some residual connection to Torah values
↓
The Gemara uses this to illustrate the distinction: a brazen-faced apostate shows no shame whatsoever — openly and defiantly rejecting Torah
↓
Only such a brazenly brazen apostate — not every sinner — is barred from performing bitul reshut for his neighbors
