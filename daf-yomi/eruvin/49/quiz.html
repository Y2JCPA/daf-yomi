QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Shmuel, why must an eiruv use bread rather than a coin (ma'a)?
A. The Torah explicitly requires food for all forms of eiruv
B. A coin cannot create a legal kinyan under rabbinic law
C. A ma'a is often unavailable on Shabbat eve as people spend their money on Shabbat needs
D. Money is muktzeh and cannot be handled on Shabbat
Correct: C — Shmuel explains that he would theoretically permit a coin, but it is rabbinically forbidden as a precaution — people often spend all available cash before Shabbat, so coins may be unavailable. Bread, however, is always accessible.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What is the key practical difference between Shmuel (acquisition) and Rabba (residence) regarding a minor collecting the eiruv?
A. According to Shmuel a minor may collect it; according to Rabba a minor may not
B. Both Shmuel and Rabba agree a minor may collect the eiruv
C. According to Shmuel a minor may not collect it; according to Rabba a minor may
D. Both Shmuel and Rabba agree a minor may not collect the eiruv
Correct: C — Under Shmuel's acquisition theory, a minor cannot legally effect a kinyan on behalf of others, invalidating the eiruv. Under Rabba's residence theory, depositing the food simply establishes symbolic residence — an act a minor can perform.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel rules that one who is 'particular' about his eiruv has an invalid eiruv. What does this mean?
A. He placed his food in a separate utensil from everyone else
B. He insists that others may not eat from the food he contributed
C. He established his eiruv in a different house than the others
D. He contributed less than the required minimum amount of food
Correct: B — Being 'particular' means insisting that others cannot eat from one's contributed portion. This defeats the purpose of an eiruv, which by definition means 'joining' — the food must be communally shared to symbolize merged residence.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to the mishna, what is the result when a traveler declares: 'My residence is at the trunk of the tree'?
A. The declaration is invalid because he has not yet reached the tree
B. He may walk only to the tree and no further
C. He may walk 2,000 cubits to the tree and an additional 2,000 cubits beyond it
D. He acquires the tree itself as his Shabbat domain
Correct: C — By specifying the trunk of the tree as his residence, the traveler establishes his Shabbat base there. He may walk up to 2,000 cubits to reach the tree and then an additional 2,000 cubits in any direction — potentially reaching his home.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What does the Gemara mean by applying the 'Sodom principle' to an alleyway eiruv dispute?
A. One may destroy a neighbor's eiruv if it was improperly constructed
B. Courts impose strict penalties on those who violate Shabbat in walled cities
C. We compel a person to waive legal rights when asserting them causes harm to others while providing no real benefit to himself
D. A person must always share his eiruv food with the poor before Shabbat
Correct: C — The principle of כופין על מידת סדום (we compel against Sodom-like behavior) holds that when one person can greatly benefit from another's concession, and that other person loses nothing by conceding, we legally compel the concession. Sodom was infamous for withholding benefit from others at no cost to themselves.
