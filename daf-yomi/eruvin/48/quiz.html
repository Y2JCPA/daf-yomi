QUESTION 1 OF 5
What was the true reason Rabbi Yosei son of Rabbi Ḥanina laughed at Rabbi Ḥiyya's baraita?
A. Because flowing water has no fixed residence and requires no partition at all
B. Because the Sages were lenient regarding water and even a minimal partition suffices
C. Because an iron partition is weaker than wood when submerged in water
D. Because the ditch belonged to only one side and required no division
Correct: B — The Gemara concludes that Rabbi Yosei laughed because the Sages established broad leniency for water — even a suspended partition suffices — making Rabbi Ḥiyya's iron partition requirement entirely unnecessary.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
When Rabbi Tavla asked Rav whether a suspended partition permits carrying in a ruin, what did Rav answer?
A. Yes — a suspended partition is valid in any enclosed area
B. Only if the ruin has two intact walls remaining
C. A suspended partition is valid only for water, not for ruins
D. No — a suspended partition is never valid according to halacha
Correct: C — Rav ruled that the Sages' leniency applies specifically to water. A suspended partition permits carrying only across water, not in ruins or other dry enclosures.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
From which verse does the baraita derive the four-cubit Shabbat limit?
A. Numbers 35:5 — the Levitical city boundaries
B. Leviticus 23:3 — 'A Shabbat of complete rest'
C. Exodus 16:29 — 'Remain every man in his place'
D. Deuteronomy 5:14 — 'You shall not do any work'
Correct: C — The baraita interprets 'Remain every man in his place' (Exodus 16:29): 'his place' equals the space a person occupies, derived as four cubits — the basis for the minimum Shabbat limit.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to Rav Pappa, how are the four Shabbat cubits measured for each person?
A. By the standard Temple cubit of six handbreadths
B. By a fixed royal cubit regardless of body size
C. By the average cubit of the local community
D. By each individual's own forearm length
Correct: D — Rav Pappa ruled that each person's four cubits are measured by their own forearm (אמה של אותו האדם). This is why it was not listed among the mishna's standardized measures — it is inherently personal.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rav Sheshet, why does placing eiruvin from two outer courtyards into the middle courtyard fail to unite all three as one?
A. Because the middle courtyard has too many residents to join
B. Because each outer courtyard placed its eiruv in a different house within the middle courtyard
C. Because the middle courtyard's residents did not formally consent
D. Because an eiruv placed in a middle courtyard is always invalid
Correct: B — Rav Sheshet explains that each outer courtyard deposited its eiruv in a separate house inside the middle courtyard. Beit Hillel's leniency about multiple utensils applies only within one house — two separate houses do not combine.
