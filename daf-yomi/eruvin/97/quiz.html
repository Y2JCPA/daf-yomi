Eruvin 97 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yehuda, why may one NOT carry new tefillin into town on Shabbat?
A. New tefillin have not yet been inspected for kashrut
B. They lack a permanent knot and appear to be an unfinished object
C. New tefillin are too valuable to risk in public
D. One might come to write the owner's name on them
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehuda holds that new tefillin without a permanent knot do not yet have the status of a completed, purposeful object — carrying them resembles carrying an unfinished item.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does Abaye teach about a bow knot in the context of the tefillin debate?
A. A bow knot is fully permitted on Shabbat and is a valid solution
B. A bow is only rabbinically prohibited on Shabbat
C. A bow is a full-fledged knot, prohibited by Torah law on Shabbat
D. A bow may be used only if the knot is tied before Shabbat
Correct: C — Abaye explains that Rabbi Yehuda himself holds that a bow has the same Torah-level prohibition as a permanent knot — it therefore cannot serve as a permitted workaround.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to Rav (as taught by Rav Ḥisda), when purchasing a large lot of tefillin from one non-expert seller, how many must one examine?
A. One of the arm and one of the head
B. Two of the arm and two of the head
C. Three of the arm and three of the head
D. Every single pair must be examined individually
Correct: B — Rav rules that examining two arm tefillin and two head tefillin establishes sufficient presumptive status for the entire lot purchased from a single seller.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why does Rabbi Shimon prefer passing tefillin from person to person in a time of danger, rather than one person carrying them less than 4 cubits at a time?
A. It is more dignified for the tefillin to be actively held
B. Each carrier receives a mitzvah for holding them
C. One person may forget and unwittingly carry them a full four cubits
D. Passing prevents the tefillin from getting damaged
Correct: C — Rabbi Shimon's concern is practical: if one person carries the tefillin intermittently, they may lose track and inadvertently carry them four cubits in the public domain, violating Shabbat.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In the Mishna at the end of Daf 97b, when a scroll rolls off a roof toward the public domain, when is one permitted to roll it back?
A. Only if the entire scroll remains physically on the roof
B. Any time, since retrieving Torah is always permitted
C. As long as the scroll is still above ten handbreadths from the ground
D. Only if it rolled less than four cubits from the reader
Correct: C — The Mishna teaches that above ten handbreadths the scroll is still considered in its own domain and may be retrieved. Once it descends below ten tefachim into the public domain, one must avert their eyes.
