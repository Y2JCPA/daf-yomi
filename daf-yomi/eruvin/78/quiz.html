QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rav Naḥman citing Rabba bar Avuh, a wall between two courtyards that is twenty handbreadths high requires how many projections to permit its use?
A. One projection in the middle
B. Two projections — one near the ground, one near the top
C. Three projections evenly spaced
D. No projections, only a ladder
Correct: B — A nineteen-handbreadth wall requires only one projection in the middle with a ladder on each side. A twenty-handbreadth wall requires two projections — one within ten handbreadths of the ground and one within ten handbreadths of the top.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua holds that a ladder of only seven handbreadths and a bit suffices against a ten-handbreadth wall. What is his reasoning?
A. The ladder need only reach the halfway point of the wall
B. Standing the ladder upright brings it within three handbreadths of the top, and lavud bridges the gap
C. A seven-handbreadth ladder is considered the standard size in Babylonia
D. The wall is assumed to lean toward the ladder, reducing its effective height
Correct: B — When stood upright against the wall, a seven-handbreadth-plus ladder reaches within three handbreadths of the top of a ten-handbreadth wall. The halakhic principle of lavud treats a gap of less than three handbreadths as closed, so the ladder is considered to reach the top.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Why does Rav Ashi rule that even a stake three handbreadths high does NOT diminish the usable area of a ten-handbreadth pillar?
A. Because three handbreadths is less than the minimum size for a private domain
B. Because the stake is absorbed into the pillar by the principle of lavud
C. Because one can hang items on the stake, so the pillar retains functional utility
D. Because the pillar is in the public domain and its measurements are calculated differently
Correct: C — Rav Ashi reasons that although objects can no longer rest flat on the top of the pillar, a person can still hang items on the stake. Since the pillar retains this alternative utility, it is not considered diminished and remains a valid private domain.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Rabbi Yoḥanan's ruling (as transmitted by Ravin) about trees and asheira trees used as ladders — what is the rule?
A. Both a tree and an asheira are valid passages because physical access is what matters
B. Neither a tree nor an asheira is valid because both involve prohibitions
C. A tree is a valid passage; an asheira is not valid
D. An asheira is a valid passage; a tree is not valid
Correct: C — Rabbi Yoḥanan rules: when a Shabbat prohibition causes something to be forbidden (like climbing a tree), it is paradoxically considered a valid opening — since people will not climb it on Shabbat, the wall is treated as closed. But when a non-Shabbat prohibition (like benefiting from an asheira) forbids it, it is NOT considered a valid opening.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
In the Mishna about a ditch between courtyards, Abaye explains why hay does not seal the ditch even though it is a valid partition. What is his distinction?
A. Hay is too light — it can blow away in the wind and therefore cannot serve any halakhic purpose
B. Hay serves as a partition dividing the space, but does not constitute a fill that annuls the ditch itself
C. Hay is muktzeh on Shabbat and therefore cannot be used in any eiruv-related context
D. Hay is only valid as a partition if it is at least ten handbreadths high, not just wide
Correct: B — Abaye distinguishes between two functions: (1) acting as a partition that divides space — here hay is valid; and (2) filling/sealing a ditch so it is treated as solid ground — here hay fails because it is temporary and removable. Only dirt or pebbles truly annul the ditch.
