⚖️ Rav Huna vs. The Baraita: Two Courtyards Between Well-Boards

Rav Huna
Two courtyards that interpose between boards — it is prohibited to carry, even if residents made an eiruv together. Decree: lest people say eiruv is effective between well-boards.
Baraita
A courtyard that interposes between boards — it is permitted to carry from inside the courtyard to the area between the boards, and vice versa.
Gemara
Is this a refutation of Rav Huna? No — Rav Huna can say the baraita speaks of one courtyard; he prohibited only two courtyards.
Core Principle Well-boards (pasei bira'ot) create a valid rabbinic partition — but multiple courtyards raise concern that people will misapply eiruv rules to the well enclosure itself.

🤔 Abaye's Questions to Rabba: Courtyards and Well-Boards

Abaye
One courtyard opens between well-boards — is it permitted to carry between the courtyard and the board enclosure?
Rabba
It is permitted — the courtyard and the board area are treated as one shared domain.
Abaye
What if two adjacent courtyards interpose between the boards?
Rabba
It is prohibited — two separate courtyards without an eiruv between them cannot both carry into a shared well enclosure.
Rav Huna goes further Even if the two courtyards made an eiruv together — still prohibited. A rabbinic decree prevents people from mistakenly thinking eiruv applies to well-board areas.

💧 Water Dries on Shabbat — Does the Partition Remain Valid?

Well-boards surrounding a well on Shabbat — carrying is permitted
↓
Water remains
Partition is valid — carrying remains permitted throughout Shabbat
Water dries up on Shabbat
Partition becomes invalid — boards exist only because of the water (Rabba)
↓
Rain falls on same Shabbat replacing the water — is the original permission restored? (Ravin's dilemma)
↓
Abaye: No. Rain refilling the well is treated as a partition erected on Shabbat. Baraita: any partition erected on Shabbat — even unwittingly — is not a valid partition.

🚧 Partitions Erected on Shabbat — Intent Determines Severity

Pre-existing Partition

  • Boards set up before Shabbat began
  • Fully valid — carrying permitted inside
  • Standard case of well-boards
  • Water must also be present at start of Shabbat

Unwitting / Accidental

  • Partition arose without deliberate Shabbat intent
  • Invalid — baraita rules it is not a valid partition
  • No carrying permitted inside on Shabbat
  • Treated as a stringency (Rav Naḥman)

Intentional

  • Person deliberately erected partition on Shabbat
  • Sages imposed a penalty — more severe than unwitting
  • Rav Naḥman: prohibited by Torah law to throw objects into it
  • Stringency applies only where intentional — unwitting case follows regular rules

📜 For Whom Were Well-Boards Originally Permitted?

פסי בירות — לעולי רגלים הותרו, לצורך בהמתם
Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Adda vs. Baraita Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Adda: Upright boards surrounding wells were only permitted for Festival pilgrims — the large crowds traveling to Jerusalem who needed water for their animals en route.

Baraita: Boards were permitted only for cattle — to allow animals to drink from public wells without violating Shabbat carrying restrictions.

Gemara's Resolution: Both are correct — the boards were permitted for Festival pilgrims specifically so they could water their cattle. The leniency was designed for large-scale pilgrim travel, combining both conditions.
