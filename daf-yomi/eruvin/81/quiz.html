QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why did Rabba bar bar Ḥana say the mishna repeats which foods may be used for an eiruv?
A. To teach that wine and oil may also be used, not only bread
B. To exclude Rabbi Yehoshua's view that only a whole loaf — not a broken loaf — is valid
C. To include the laws of merging alleyways with joining courtyards
D. To respond to Abaye's objection from a baraita
Correct: B — Rabba bar bar Ḥana said the repetition targets Rabbi Yehoshua's position that only a whole loaf is valid — the mishna teaches that even a broken loaf and other foods may be used.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why is a broken loaf generally unfit for use as an eiruv?
A. It does not meet the minimum shiur (measure) of food required
B. It is rabbinically forbidden as a form of degrading Shabbat
C. It causes enmity — neighbors feel insulted receiving a broken piece while others bring whole loaves
D. It cannot be placed in the eiruv container without crumbling
Correct: C — Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul citing Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: the reason is enmity (איבה). When one participant deposits a broken loaf and others whole loaves, it generates bad feeling among neighbors.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the difference between a baker's ḥalla (1/48) and a householder's ḥalla (1/24) regarding eiruv validity?
A. Both render the loaf invalid; the only difference is who separates the portion
B. A loaf missing baker's ḥalla (1/48) is still valid for eiruv; missing householder's ḥalla (1/24) renders it invalid
C. A loaf missing householder's ḥalla is valid; baker's ḥalla renders it invalid
D. The Gemara rules both measurements make the loaf invalid for eiruv
Correct: B — The baker removes only 1/48 — a small amount — so the loaf remains largely intact and is valid for eiruv. The householder removes 1/24, a larger fraction, leaving the loaf too diminished to be used.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How did Rav Naḥman explain the basis for Rabbi Eliezer's ruling that giving money to a grocer acquires eiruv food?
A. Rabbi Eliezer held that money always acquires movable goods for eiruv purposes specifically
B. Rabbi Eliezer derived it from the laws of betrothal, where a coin effects acquisition
C. Rabbi Eliezer modeled it on the four annual pre-holiday market days when the Sages allow money to effect acquisition
D. Rabbi Eliezer followed the minority Sages who held meshikha (pulling) is never required
Correct: C — Rav Naḥman citing Rabba bar Abbahu: Rabbi Eliezer based his ruling on Erev Pesaḥ, Shavuot, Rosh HaShana, and Sukkot — four times when paying money suffices to acquire festival animals, since everyone benefits from buying meat on those days.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yehuda, which type of eiruv may be established for a person without their knowledge?
A. Eiruv teḥumin only — joining Shabbat boundaries — since it extends movement options
B. Both eiruv teḥumin and eiruv ḥatzeirot require explicit consent in all cases
C. Eiruv ḥatzeirot (joining courtyards for carrying) — it only grants benefit and never restricts, so consent is not required
D. Neither type may be established without consent; Rabbi Yehuda's ruling concerns a different law entirely
Correct: C — Rabbi Yehuda distinguishes: eiruv teḥumin limits where one may walk on Shabbat, so consent is required. Eiruv ḥatzeirot only permits carrying and imposes no restriction — we may confer benefit on a person in their absence (זכין לאדם שלא בפניו).
