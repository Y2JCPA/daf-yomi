⚔️ Nannas vs. Akiva: Must Impurity Be Fully Removed?

R' Shimon b. Nannas
The handoff from priests (inner Sanctuary) to Levites (courtyard) proves there is no obligation to continue removing impurity once it leaves the Sanctuary — the task is complete.
R' Akiva
Priests carried impurity out only because Levites cannot enter the Sanctuary. In the courtyard, where Levites can serve, the obligation remains — impurity must be fully removed.
📖 Shared Source — II Chronicles 29:16 "The priests went into the inner part of the House of God to cleanse it, and brought out all the impurity into the courtyard; and the Levites received it, to carry it out abroad." Both sages read this verse — and reach opposite conclusions.

🔵 The Dilemma: Impure Priest vs. Blemished Priest

Rav (via Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi)
The ritually impure priest should enter. Impurity carries a communal exception — tumah hutrah b'tzibur — making his disqualification less absolute. His status is situational, not permanent.
Implied Counter-Position
The blemished priest should enter. Though his blemish is permanent, a blemished priest retains certain Temple roles. The impure priest is barred from all service with no exceptions.
⚖️ Unresolved The Gemara presents this as an open dilemma (תֵּיקוּ). Both priests are disqualified — but the nature of each disqualification differs fundamentally.

📜 Rav Kahana's Baraita — The Limiting Power of אַךְ

אַךְ אֶל-הַפָּרֹכֶת לֹא יָבֹא וְאֶל-הַמִּזְבֵּחַ לֹא יִגַּשׁ כִּי-מוּם בּוֹ — ויקרא כא:כג
🔍 Rav Kahana (a priest) teaches: Leviticus 21:23 states: "אַךְ אֶל-הַפָּרֹכֶת לֹא יָבֹא — Only he shall not go in unto the veil."

The word אַךְ (akh) is a term of exclusion and limitation. It signals that while the mitzva is ideally performed by unblemished priests, the prohibition is not absolute — if no unblemished priests are available, blemished priests may enter to perform necessary repairs. The Torah itself built in this flexibility.

🔓 Rabbi Shimon's Two Interlocking Principles

Principle 1: Spatial Scope

  • "Whatever the Sages permitted — they gave you only from your own"
  • The area they permitted was already legally yours
  • Entering it violates nothing — it was never truly forbidden
  • Example: Eruv extends the 2,000-amot zone that belongs to you
  • You are not borrowing someone else's domain — you own it

Principle 2: Legal Depth

  • "They permitted only rabbinic prohibitions — never Torah ones"
  • Forming a bow-knot on Shabbat: only rabbinically forbidden → permitted
  • A full knot: Torah prohibition → the Sages cannot override
  • Rabbinic authority operates within its own layer of law
  • The Torah's boundary is a ceiling no rabbinic permit can pierce
