🍞 Eiruv Techumim: Rules, Measures, and Dependencies

עירובין פ״ב
Core Themes of Daf 82 The daf opens by testing Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's principle that Rabbi Yehuda's במה דברים אמורים ('when is this so?') always signals explanation rather than dispute — raising and resolving counterexamples. It then turns to practical eiruv techumim law: who may establish an eiruv for dependents (minors, servants, spouses), at what age a child ceases to follow his mother's eiruv, and what quantity of food constitutes the required two-meal measure, reconciling the views of Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Meir, Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, and Rabbi Shimon through Rav Ḥisda's grocer-markup calculation.

📜 Does Rabbi Yehuda's 'When' Always Explain?

R. Yehoshua ben Levi
Whenever Rabbi Yehuda says במה דברים אמורים — 'when is this so?' — he is explaining the previous Sages' opinion, not disputing it.
Gemara Objection
But the mishna about dice players: Rabbi Yehuda says 'when' — and a baraita shows this is a genuine dispute, disqualifying gamblers even when they have another occupation!
Gemara Resolution
That baraita does not express the Rabbis' view — it is Rabbi Yehuda citing Rabbi Tarfon, who ruled that a doubtful nazirite never fully accepts his vow. Rabbi Yehuda's own 'when' still signals explanation.
Rava
The underlying logic: Rabbi Tarfon held that one in doubt about a vow does not submit to it — Rabbi Yehuda extends this to games of chance: such a person never fully commits, so the winnings are legally dubious.
Takeaway במה דברים אמורים in Rabbi Yehuda's mouth = clarification of the prior ruling — unless he explicitly attributes a position to a named dissenting source.

🤝 Who May Establish an Eiruv on Behalf of Others?

A person wishes to establish eiruv techumim for a dependent
↓
Minor children / Canaanite servants
May establish with or without their knowledge — they lack independent legal standing
Adult children / Hebrew servants / Wife
May establish only with their knowledge — they are legally autonomous persons
↓
What if the dependent also establishes their own eiruv in a different direction?
↓
Adult children / Hebrew servants
Rava: Their separate eiruv = implicit protest — no stronger objection exists; they follow their own eiruv
Wife
She may explicitly object: 'I do not wish to go where you designated' — her eiruv or her words override the husband's

⚖️ Four Opinions on the Two-Meal Measure for Eiruv

Rabbi Meir

  • Food for two standard meals
  • Rav: Two ikaryata (farmers' loaves)
  • Based on a typical Shabbat meal portion

Rabbi Yehuda

  • Food for two standard meals
  • Rav Adda bar Ahava: two standard-baked loaves
  • Rav Yosef aligns with this view

R. Yochanan ben Beroka

  • A loaf worth one pundeyon (1/48 of a sela)
  • When the market rate is 4 se'a wheat per sela
  • Rav Ḥisda: deduct ½ for grocer's markup to reconcile

Rabbi Shimon

  • ⅔ of a loaf baked three loaves to a kav
  • = 2/9 kav per meal × 2 meals = 4/9 kav total
  • Tosefta: their statements are close to identical

🧮 Rav Ḥisda Reconciles Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka and Rabbi Shimon

Rabbi Shimon's measure: ⅔ of a loaf from 3-per-kav baking → each loaf = ⅓ kav → two meals = 4/9 kav → 9 meals per kav
↓
Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka's measure: loaf worth 1 pundeyon when 4 se'a = 1 sela → raw calculation = 8 meals per kav
↓
First attempt — deduct ⅓ for grocer's profit: Grocer buys cheap, sells for ⅓ markup. Adjusted calculation still leaves the two opinions unequal. This does not work.
↓
Second attempt — deduct ½ for grocer's profit: Grocer doubles cost (100% markup = ½ of sale price is profit). After adjustment, both opinions yield the same measure. ✓
↓
Remaining gap (9 vs. 8 meals/kav): Resolved — R. Yochanan speaks of a homeowner buying at retail price; R. Shimon speaks of a baker selling at a lower bulk price. Different market contexts.
