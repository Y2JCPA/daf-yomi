📏 Three Se'a Measures Compared

Wilderness Se'a

  • 144 egg-bulks
  • Moses' original standard
  • Basis of all Torah measurements
  • Eifa = 3 se'ah = 432 eggs
  • Referenced: omer = 1/10 eifa

Jerusalem Se'a

  • 173 egg-bulks
  • Enlarged from wilderness measure
  • Baraita: 'larger by one-sixth'
  • Gemara reconciles the discrepancy
  • Temple-era priestly standard

Tzippori Se'a

  • 207 egg-bulks
  • Enlarged from Jerusalem measure
  • Larger than wilderness by ~1/3
  • Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's measured se'ah
  • Used to calculate ḥalla portion

⬇️ Lowering vs. Throwing: Access Rights Dispute

Rav
A place accessible only by lowering from one courtyard, and only by throwing from the other — it is prohibited for both. Neither has truly convenient access; they have equal competing claims and thus block each other.
Shmuel
The area follows the courtyard whose residents can lower objects down to it. Lowering is more controlled and convenient than throwing — the dominant access determines ownership.
Gemara
The Mishna supports judging by relative convenience: the balcony controls areas ≥10 handbreadths (reachable by stepping) while the courtyard controls those below (only reachable by throwing up). The more accessible side wins.
Core Principle In eiruv disputes, access rights flow to the side with meaningfully more convenient use — not merely theoretical access.

עֹמֶר לַגֻּלְגֹּלֶת — An omer per head (Shemot 16:16)
🌿 Ethical Teaching: The Manna as Life Wisdom From the omer of manna — the exact daily portion the Torah prescribed — the Sages derived a lasting lesson:

One who eats roughly an omer's worth daily is healthy and blessed.
One who eats more is a glutton. One who eats less will suffer illness.

The Torah's ideal measure of sustenance is neither excess nor deprivation — it is the precise, balanced portion that God provided in the wilderness.
