🏛️ Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel & the Narrow Alleyway

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel
Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not disagree about an alleyway less than 4 cubits wide — both agree it requires nothing at all to permit carrying.
Rav Ashi
This means: it doesn't need a side post + cross beam (Beit Shammai's requirement), nor two side posts (Rabbi Eliezer's requirement). The narrow width itself exempts it entirely.
Rav Aḥlei
More precisely: up to 4 handbreadths wide — nothing required. From 4 handbreadths to 4 cubits — a single side post or cross beam suffices. This is the true threshold.
Key Threshold 4 handbreadths is the minimum for an alleyway to carry any Shabbat relevance at all. Below that, the space is halachically negligible — no partition is required.

🔲 Lechi (Side Post) vs. Korah (Cross Beam) — Two Views

לֶחִי — Lechi (Side Post)

  • Rav Yehuda & Rabba: functions as a true partition
  • Completes the wall — creates private domain
  • Throwing from public domain into alleyway with lechi: liable
  • Rabbi Eliezer requires two side posts for a courtyard breach
  • Must be ≥ 4 handbreadths wide for a courtyard upright board

קוֹרָה — Korah (Cross Beam)

  • Rav Yehuda & Rabba: functions only as a conspicuous marker
  • Does not create a full partition — merely signals the boundary
  • Throwing into alleyway with korah alone: exempt
  • Rava: a korah-only alleyway is not a fully enclosed domain
  • Rabbeinu Ḥananel: this distinction governs liability, not basic permission

🌊 The Water Leniency — Narrow Inlet of the Sea

הקלו חכמים בדבר המים — Eruvin 12b
Rabbinic Leniency for Water Cases A narrow sea inlet (mechiẓat hayam) that breaches a courtyard wall is governed by more lenient standards than an ordinary breach. The inlet itself is a karmelit, so one may not draw water from it on Shabbat — but carrying within the courtyard may remain permitted if remnants of the wall stand on each side.

Abaye confirms the principle explicitly: "We know that the Sages lessened requirements in cases involving water." Rabbi Tavla's dilemma about suspended partitions over ruins further illustrates this special category. The leniency reflects a deliberate rabbinic policy, not mere accident.
