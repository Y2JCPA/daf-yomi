🤝 The Great Possession Debate: Rav vs. Shmuel

Rav
It is not necessary to confer possession of the food used in merging the alleyway to all the residents — informing them suffices.
Shmuel
It is necessary to formally confer possession of the food to all residents of the alleyway.
Rav Naḥman
Tradition holds: for all three — joining of Shabbat boundaries, joining of courtyards, and merging of alleyways — possession must be conferred.
Ruling Rav Naḥman rules stringently across all eiruv types, resolving disputes in favor of zikui (formal acquisition).

🏊 The Bathhouse Incident — Rabbi Oshaya's Daughter-in-Law

Mother-in-law establishes eiruv for daughter-in-law before Shabbat — using her own food
↓
Daughter-in-law goes to the bathhouse beyond the Shabbat boundary; eiruv validity disputed
↓
Case brought before Rabbi Ḥiyya for a ruling
↓
Rabbi Ḥiyya's Ruling
Invalid — daughter-in-law may not return on Shabbat; possession was not conferred
Rabbi Yishmael bar Yosei
"Babylonian! Are you so stringent with regard to an eiruv?" — publicly challenges the ruling
↓

🔍 Abaye vs. Rav Yosef — What Did Rav Naḥman Not Know?

Rav Yosef
Why did Rav Naḥman raise a dilemma? He should have heard Shmuel's ruling via Rav Naḥman bar Rav Adda: joining of cooked foods requires conferring possession!
Abaye
Obviously he had not heard that tradition. And even if he had — Shmuel himself said possession is not required for joining of Shabbat boundaries, yet Rav Naḥman overruled Shmuel there. He may disagree here too.
Abaye (rejoinder)
How can you compare? For merging alleyways and joining Shabbat boundaries, Rav and Shmuel dispute — so Rav Naḥman teaches the stringent rule. For joining of cooked foods, all agree possession is required. Rav Naḥman would follow the consensus.
Takeaway When amora'im dispute, Rav Naḥman sides with stringency. Where all agree, no dilemma exists.

👩 Can a Wife Establish Eiruv Without Her Husband?

Baraita — General Rule

  • Women who join courtyards or merge alleyways without their husbands' knowledge — eiruv is invalid
  • A wife cannot unilaterally impose eiruv obligations on her household

Shmuel's Exception (via Reish Lakish / Rabbi Ḥanina)

  • A man's wife may establish eiruv without his knowledge
  • Applies when the husband's non-participation would prohibit his neighbors from carrying on Shabbat
  • The wife acts to benefit the community, not to defy her husband

Shmuel's Corroborating Ruling

  • If a resident was accustomed to joining the merging, neighbors may enter his house to collect his contribution
  • If he was not accustomed, they may not — confirming that coercion is limited
  • These two rulings of Shmuel are internally consistent

🌳 Asheira Wood for Shabbat Partitions

מִצְווֹת לָאו לֵיהָנוֹת נִיתְּנוּ — Mitzvot were not given for personal benefit
Disputed Halakha Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi: One may build a lechi (side post) from asheira wood — the wood of an idolatrous tree.

Reish Lakish: One may build a korah (cross beam) from asheira wood — an even stricter position.

Logical extension: One who permits the cross beam (a more significant structure) certainly permits the side post. One who permits only the side post does not necessarily permit the cross beam.

Shared reasoning: Mitzvot are not subject to the prohibition of benefit — using forbidden material for a mitzva-purpose structure is permitted, since one does not derive personal pleasure from it.
