⚖️ The Airspace Prohibition Debate

Shmuel
One person does render it prohibited for another by way of the air. Shared airspace alone creates a mutual Shabbat prohibition — a ledge adjustment is required.
Rav
One person does not render it prohibited for another by way of the air alone. Physical proximity or direct access is required before any prohibition arises.
Abaye
Rav Yehuda's ruling requiring a minimal ledge adjustment must follow Shmuel — if he followed Rav, no adjustment would be required at all.
Rava
Rav Dimi's leniency about an exempt domain between a public and private domain is distinct — the Sages did not apply the airspace prohibition there. Shmuel's position remains intact.
The Core Question If two parties share access to the same airspace — a cistern, ruin, or water source — does mere shared air create a mutual prohibition on Shabbat use, or is physical proximity required?

🌊 Cistern Between Two Courtyards

A cistern lies between two courtyards, 4 handbreadths from each courtyard wall
↓
Each resident wishes to draw water through their window using a ledge
↓
Shmuel's Ruling
Each must extend a minimal ledge — shared airspace creates mutual prohibition requiring an adjustment
Rav's Ruling
No mutual prohibition through air — each resident draws freely without concern for the other party

🏚️ Three Ruins — Rav's Position Challenged and Clarified

Rav (via Rav Naḥman)
Two houses with three ruins between them: each resident may use only the ruin adjacent to his house. The middle ruin is forbidden to both.
Rabbi Elazar
But if you forbid the middle ruin, you hold that airspace creates prohibition! Then each resident should only use one adjacent ruin — why do you permit them two ruins each?
Rav
You misunderstand the case. The ruins are not in a straight line — they are arranged as a tripod (triangle). Each side ruin is adjacent only to one house. The middle ruin faces both, hence forbidden to both.
Rav Beruna
I transmitted this ruling in Rav's name. Rabbi Elazar's question led me to bring him directly to Rav to clarify — confirming the triangular arrangement resolves the apparent contradiction.
Key Insight The triangular arrangement means no ruin lies in a straight airspace path shared by both houses. Rav's position — that airspace alone does not create prohibition — remains fully consistent.

📦 Valid vs. Invalid Eiruv Deposit Locations (Mishna)

❌ Invalid — Eiruv Not Effective

  • Gatehouse (שער) — not a fully private enclosed space
  • Portico/אכסדרה — open or incomplete walls, exposed
  • Balcony (מרפסת) — semi-public, not guarded
  • Residing in these places does NOT render the courtyard prohibited
  • → If eiruv is deposited here, it has no legal effect

✅ Valid — Eiruv Is Effective

  • Hay shed (בית התבן) — properly enclosed structure
  • Cowshed (בית הבקר) — guarded private space
  • Woodshed (בית העצים) — secured storage area
  • Storehouse (בית האוצרות) — private and protected
  • Residing here does render the courtyard prohibited for others

🍞 Bread on Table as a Shabbat Eiruv

Situation: A group dines together on Shabbat eve — Shabbat begins while they are still eating at the table
↓
Rav Yehuda (citing Shmuel): They may rely on the bread already on the table as a valid eiruv of courtyards — no separate deposit is required
↓
Rabba's Distinction: Dining in a house → bread counts as eiruv of courtyards. Dining in a courtyard → bread counts as merging of alleyways (שיתוף מבואות)
↓
Baraita Support (cited by Abaye): Eiruvin of courtyards are deposited in a courtyard; mergings of alleyways are placed in an alleyway — location determines the legal function of the food
↓
Conclusion: The two versions are not in conflict — the bread's location (house vs. courtyard) determines whether it functions as eiruv or shittuf. Both are valid in their respective contexts.

📋 Eruvin 85 — Summary Map

Core Dispute:
Does shared airspace alone create mutual Shabbat prohibition between two parties?
↓
Shmuel: Yes
Air creates prohibition → minimal ledge required for cistern; ladder for low roof
Rav: No
Air alone insufficient → ruins in tripod; balconies only when ≤4 handbreadths apart
↓
Mishna (85b): Valid eiruv must be in a guarded, inhabited space — hay shed, cowshed, storehouse. Gatehouse, portico, and balcony are invalid.
