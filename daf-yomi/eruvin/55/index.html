🌌 Torah Is Not in Heaven — Three Interpretations

Avdimi bar Ḥama
If Torah were in heaven you would need to ascend for it; if beyond the sea you would cross for it. The point: exert every effort to acquire Torah — no distance is too great.
Rava
"Not in heaven" — Torah is not found in one who raises his mind above it like the heavens, thinking he needs no teacher. "Not beyond the sea" — not in one who is arrogant as the vast, boundless sea.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
"Not in heaven" — not found among the haughty. "Not beyond the sea" — not found among merchants and traders who are perpetually traveling and have no time to sit and study.
Core Lesson Torah demands both humility and dedicated, unhurried time. The arrogant and the perpetually distracted cannot truly acquire it.

🏹 Bow-Shaped City: The 4,000-Cubit Rule & Karpef

City shaped like a bow — two inhabited sections with a gap between them
↓
How large is the gap between the two sections?
↓
Gap < 4,000 cubits
Treat as one city. Fill in the imaginary interior; measure 2,000 cubits from the outer edge of the bow.
Gap ≥ 4,000 cubits
Two separate cities. Each gets its own karpef (~70 cubits). Combined buffer ≈ 141⅓ cubits.
↓
Rav Huna: A karpef is added to each city — the same rule applies to breached city walls and to two adjacent cities

🏛️ Which Structures Are Included in City Boundaries?

✅ Included in the City

  • House with two walls and a roof
  • Cave with a structure at its entrance
  • Tomb attached to a dwelling (residence present)
  • Synagogue with a caretaker's residence
  • Portico / covered colonnade
  • Seaside house used to store a ship's utensils

❌ Not Included in the City

  • Tomb breached on both sides — open all the way through
  • Bridge without any attached residence
  • Grave without a residence
  • Synagogue without a caretaker's residence
  • Cave without a structure at its entrance
  • Pure settlement of huts (temporary dwellings)

Rav Huna's Ruling on Scholars & Cities Any city that does not have vegetables, a Torah scholar is not permitted to dwell there.

Vegetables support physical health, and a Torah scholar must maintain his body to sustain continuous learning. This reflects the broader principle that bodily well-being is a prerequisite for Torah study.

The Gemara further connects this to the contrast between settled city-dwellers and hut-dwellers — stable, healthy conditions produce stable Torah life.
