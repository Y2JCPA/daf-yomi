⚖️ Rav & Shmuel: A Daf of Great Disputes

Rav
me'abberin is spelled with an alef — from ever (limb), meaning we add extensions limb by limb to the city's core
Shmuel
me'abberin is spelled with an ayin — from ibur (pregnant), the city expands outward as if with child
Rav
Machpelah Cave has two rooms side by side, one farther in than the other
Shmuel
Machpelah Cave has two rooms stacked, one atop the other — that is the meaning of 'double'
Rav
The 'new king' (Exodus 1:8) was actually a new king — the verse says so explicitly
Shmuel
He was the same old king with new decrees — the verse does not say a new man arose
Resolution The people of Judea, consulted as arbiters of precise language, reported: both spellings — alef and ayin — are valid traditions. Both are well-founded.

🗣️ Judea vs. Galilee: The Power of Precise Speech

People of Judea ✓

  • Precise and careful in language
  • Created mnemonics for their learning
  • Studied from one consistent teacher
  • Pre-disclosed which tractate would be studied (ge'onim)
  • Torah knowledge endured for them
  • Example: Said 'I have a cloak to sell' — only the right buyer understood
  • David's approach: disclosed tractate in advance (Ps. 119:74)

People of Galilee ✗

  • Imprecise, confusing pronunciation
  • No mnemonics for studies
  • Studied from many teachers — inconsistent approach
  • Did not pre-disclose tractate
  • Torah did not endure for them
  • Example: 'Amar' confused with 'ḥamar'; 'ḥelba/liba' mix-up; 'Mari/Mari' confusion
  • Saul's approach: did not disclose tractate (I Sam. 14:47)

👦 Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya: Humbled Three Times

The Woman at the Meal
Rabbi Yehoshua didn't eat beans. She said: 'Withdraw your hand from bread to leave room for beans.' When he left food on his plate, she corrected him again: 'Leave a remainder in the plate — not in the pot [ilpas]!' She knew the halacha better than he anticipated.
↓
The Young Girl in the Field
Rabbi Yehoshua walked through a field on a trodden path. She said: 'My Rabbi, isn't this a field?' He replied: 'There is already a trodden path here.' She shot back: 'Bandits like you have trodden it!' — pointing out that he himself was the one making the path.
↓
The Young Boy at the Crossroads
The boy said: 'This path is short and long; that path is long and short.' Rabbi Yehoshua took the 'short' path — and hit walled gardens. Returning, the boy reminded him: 'Did I not say it was also long?' Rabbi Yehoshua kissed him on the head.
↓
Rabbi Yehoshua's Conclusion
"Happy are you, O Israel, for you are all wise — from your greatest to your least!"
Even a towering Sage can be taught by a woman, a young girl, or a child. Wisdom has no fixed address.

🧠 Enigmatic Speech of the Sages

כָּל יָמַי לֹא נִצַּחְתַּנִי אָדָם חוּץ מֵאִשָּׁה וְתִינוֹק וְתִינוֹקֶת
Cleverness in Language Rabbi Yosei bar Asyan: 'Prepare an ox in judgment on a poor mountain' = Cook me a calf on a small stove (syllable-by-syllable Aramaic/Hebrew encoding)

Rabbi Abbahu: 'Make coals like an etrog; beat the golden ones; bring two speakers-in-the-dark' = Prepare the hearth, spread the glowing coals, bring two roosters

About Rabbi Elai: 'He rejoiced with the latter Aharonic girl; she is lively and from a village' = Either he married a young priestly woman, or he is immersed in a tractate from Seder Kodashim

Berurya rebukes Rabbi Yosei HaGelili: 'Foolish Galilean! Did not the Sages say — do not engage in lengthy speech with a woman? Just say: to Lod, which way?'
