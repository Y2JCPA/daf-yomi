אֵלּוּ הֵן הַמְמֻנִּין שֶׁהָיוּ בַמִּקְדָּשׁ — These are the appointed officers who served in the Temple
📜 Mishnah: The Fifteen Officers These fifteen individuals served as permanent officers in the Temple: each held a specialized hereditary role critical to daily Temple function. The Mishnah preserves their names as a monument to their service — זֵכֶר לְמִקְדָּשׁ — a memory of the Temple.

Gemara
Why did the House of Avtinas refuse to teach their incense formula? The smoke always rose straight upward — a miracle — and they feared that if others learned the technique, they might use it improperly.
Sages
Nevertheless, the Sages criticized them! They should have taught the formula rather than hoarding sacred knowledge for family prestige and economic control.
Gemara
But they replied: Our ancestors' tradition teaches this skill was never to be shared — לֹא לָמְדוּ — lest it be used for idol worship outside the Temple.
Resolution
Despite the reproach, they are praised for one thing: no woman of their household ever wore perfume, so none would suspect them of diverting Temple incense for personal use.
⚖️ Principle Sacred expertise carries both privilege and responsibility — hoarding it is suspect, yet their personal scrupulousness vindicated their integrity.

🎭 Hugras ben Levi and the Art of Song

Hugras ben Levi
Appointed over the Temple song (שיר)
↓
He knew a special vocal technique:
he would put his thumb in his mouth
producing an extraordinary trill (נֳעִימָה)
↓
Praised
His skill beautified the Temple service and moved listeners to devotion
Criticized
He refused to teach others, keeping the art within his family lineage
↓
Sages preserved his name as a warning:
sacred gifts must be shared with Israel

🚪 Elazar over the Curtains — Parochet Sanctity

The Parochet (פָּרוֹכֶת) — the curtain separating the Kodesh from Kodesh HaKodashim — was of immense sanctity and value
↓
Elazar was the officer responsible for the curtains: their weaving, maintenance, immersion when impure, and proper storage
↓
A curtain that became tameh (ritually impure) was immersed in a special mikveh within the Temple courts
↓
Debate: Does a sanctified curtain contract impurity? — Since it has no owner who could become impure from it, some held it cannot be made impure at all
↓
Conclusion: Temple vessels designated for public use can still contract impurity — sanctity does not provide blanket exemption from tumah law
