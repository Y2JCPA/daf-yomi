🗄️ The 13 Shofar Chests of the Temple

New Shekalim

  • Contributions for the current year's half-shekel
  • Used for communal daily offerings (tamid)
  • Labeled: שקלים חדשים
  • First priority for terumat haLishkah

Old Shekalim

  • Contributions from prior year not yet applied
  • Designated for surplus communal needs
  • Labeled: שקלים ישנים
  • Subject to debate on their use

Freewill Bird Offerings

  • Coins designated for turtle-doves or pigeons
  • Used as voluntary burnt-offerings
  • Labeled: קינין
  • Individual, not communal, in character

Wood Offering

  • Donations for firewood on the altar
  • Priestly families contributed on set days
  • Labeled: עצים
  • Surplus used for Temple maintenance

📜 Mishnah Background The Mishnah (Shekalim 6:1) records 13 shofar-shaped chests (shofarot) in the Temple courtyard. Each was inscribed with its designated purpose, ensuring donated funds reached their proper use. The wider end faced downward to prevent theft — coins could be inserted but not removed by hand.

⚖️ Ownership of Motar (Surplus Funds)

R' Yochanan
Surplus from a freewill offering (motar nedavah) belongs to the public — it falls into the Temple treasury for communal sacrifices, since the donor had no specific obligation.
Reish Lakish
Surplus from a freewill offering reverts to the individual donor — he designated funds for a specific voluntary act, and once fulfilled, the remainder is his.
R' Yochanan
Proof: We learn that surplus from obligatory sin-offerings goes to the donor. By contrast, nedavah surplus — having no personal obligation behind it — must go to the public fund.
Reish Lakish
The distinction is different: the donor's intention at the time of pledging governs the surplus. If he pledged for a specific act, whatever remains tracks his original intent.
Resolution The Yerushalmi concludes that motar nedavah goes to public nedavah funds (voluntary communal burnt-offerings), while motar of obligatory offerings returns to the owner — a middle path honoring both positions.

🔄 Flow of Terumat HaLishkah

Adar arrives
Proclamation: bring your half-shekalim to the Temple
↓
Lishkah sealed
Treasury chamber is locked until the appointed day
↓
Three terumot per year
One before Pesach, one before Shavuot, one before Sukkot
↓
Coins present
Treasurer scoops three korim of coins as terumah — sanctified for communal sacrifices
Coins insufficient
Chamber supplemented from prior-year shekalim (yeshanot) or Temple endowment funds

📋 How Surplus Communal Funds Were Spent

Step 1 — Terumah taken: The appointed treasurer scoops three measures from the lishkah; these coins are now hekdesh (sanctified Temple property).
↓
Step 2 — Communal sacrifices purchased: The daily tamid, Musaf offerings of Shabbat and Yom Tov, and the Omer are all bought from this terumah.
↓
Step 3 — First surplus (motar hatefumah): After all communal sacrifices are covered, remaining sanctified coins go toward golden vessels and Temple repair.
↓
Step 4 — Second surplus (motar nedavah): If freewill-offering chests overflow, the excess funds purchase communal voluntary burnt-offerings (olot nedavah) burned on Shabbat.
↓
Step 5 — Accountability: The gizbarim publicly declare all expenditures; the Mishnah emphasizes transparency to avoid suspicion of misappropriation.
