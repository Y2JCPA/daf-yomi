🏛️ Mishnah: Found Money in Jerusalem

מָצָא מָעוֹת בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם לִפְנֵי סוֹחֲרֵי בְהֵמָה — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי
Core Rule One who finds money before the animal dealers in Jerusalem presumes it is Maaser Sheni (second tithe) and must treat it accordingly — redeeming it onto another coin before spending. The animal market was specifically where pilgrims spent their Maaser Sheni to purchase offerings and festival meals.

⚖️ Temple Mount Found Money — Chullin or Sacred?

Rabbi Yochanan
Money found on the Temple Mount is presumed chullin — pilgrims bring ordinary coins for personal expenses and incidental purchases when visiting. We follow the majority of passersby.
Reish Lakish
But donations are made throughout the Temple precinct! Should we not presume that coins found there are lost shekalim or dedicated Temple funds? The very sanctity of the place argues otherwise.
Rabbi Yochanan
The Temple Mount functions as a public thoroughfare — the overwhelming majority who traverse it carry personal money. We follow acharei rabbim: presumption is chullin. Donations are made at specific collection points, not scattered throughout.
Ruling Halakha follows Rabbi Yochanan: the Temple Mount is treated like a general public area for purposes of found money. The paradox is that the most sacred location yields the least sacred presumption — because rov (majority) overrides geography.

📅 Festival Timing Changes Everything

Close to a Pilgrimage Festival

  • Found money in Jerusalem → presumed Maaser Sheni
  • Massive influx of pilgrims carrying tithe funds
  • Majority shifts: most coins are now second-tithe money
  • Applies to all three festivals: Pesach, Shavuot, Sukkot
  • Finder must redeem it onto a coin before use

Outside Festival Season

  • Found money in Jerusalem → presumed chullin
  • Fewer pilgrims; ordinary residents predominate
  • Most coins in circulation are non-sacred
  • Finder may keep and use as ordinary property
  • No redemption required

💰 Surplus Shekel Funds — From Coin to Golden Vessel

Collection: All Jewish men contribute a half-shekel annually; funds accumulate in the Temple treasury chamber.
↓
Three Withdrawals: The treasurer makes terumah (withdrawal) before each of the three pilgrimage festivals to fund communal offerings for the whole year.
↓
Communal Sacrifices Purchased: The withdrawn funds pay for the tamid (daily offering), Musaf sacrifices, and other obligatory communal korbanot.
↓
Surplus Identified: After all communal offerings are funded, any remaining balance constitutes surplus (motar) belonging to the community.
↓
Temple Beautification: Surplus is used to purchase golden vessels — menorah fittings, altar overlays, and sacred implements — so every Jew shares in the Temple's splendor.
