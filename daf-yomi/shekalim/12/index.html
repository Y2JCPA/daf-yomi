⚖️ What Is Done with Motar Terumas HaLishkah?

R. Akiva
The leftover funds (motar) are used to purchase nedavah — communal free-will burnt offerings placed on the altar when it would otherwise stand idle
R. Ḥanina Sgan HaKohanim
The motar is used to purchase Temple service vessels — the sacred surplus should maintain the instruments of worship, not add more sacrifices
Ben Azzai
The leftover funds are scattered on the floor of the Temple courtyard and become chullin — ordinary funds absorbed back into general Temple upkeep
Core Tension Does surplus sacred money flow back to the altar (maximizing active sanctity), to Temple infrastructure, or does it lose its designated status entirely?

🔴 Parah Adumah: Funded by Shekalim or Not?

Yes — From Public Shekels

  • The red heifer purifies all Israel collectively
  • It serves a communal need — all benefit from tahara
  • Like tamid and musaf, it is a national obligation
  • Therefore the public treasury must fund it

No — Separate Funding

  • Parah adumah is not an altar sacrifice
  • Its unique status sets it apart from communal offerings
  • It has its own funding mechanism or comes from motar
  • Equating it with tamid conflates two different categories

🏛️ How the Seal System Prevented Fraud

A person arrives at the Temple needing libations (wine, oil, flour) for their sacrifice
↓
They pay money to Officer #1 and receive a stamped seal matching their offering type (egel, zachar, gedi, or ḥote)
↓
They bring only the seal (not money) to Officer #2, who dispenses the actual wine, flour, and oil
↓
At end of each day, the two officers reconcile their records — seals issued must match goods dispensed
↓
Any discrepancy triggers investigation; the separation of money-collection from goods-distribution prevents any single person from committing fraud

📜 The Nedavah Principle — The Altar Must Not Stand Idle

מוֹתַר תְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה — נְדָבָה (Shekalim 4:2)
Halakhic Principle The Sages teach that it is a disgrace for the outer altar to have no offering burning upon it. When the motar — leftover shekalim funds — accumulates, R. Akiva rules it must purchase nedavah burnt offerings. These communal free-will offerings have no specific owner; they belong to all Israel. This ensures the surplus of Israel's sacred giving returns directly to its highest purpose: constant fire on the altar.
