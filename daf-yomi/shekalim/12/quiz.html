Shekalim 12 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
What is 'motar terumas halishkah'?
A. The initial funds placed into the Temple chamber at the start of the year
B. Leftover funds remaining after the annual separation has covered all Temple needs
C. The fine paid by those who delayed giving their half-shekel
D. The surplus libations left over after daily sacrifices
Correct: B — Motar means 'leftover' or 'surplus.' After the terumas halishkah funds the year's public sacrifices, any remainder is the motar — its disposition is the subject of a three-way dispute.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does R. Akiva rule should be done with the motar?
A. Returned proportionally to those who contributed shekels
B. Used to purchase new Temple service vessels
C. Used to purchase nedavah — communal free-will burnt offerings
D. Stored as a reserve for the following year's separation
Correct: C — R. Akiva rules the motar buys nedavah offerings to ensure the altar never stands idle. The surplus of Israel's giving goes directly back to the altar — its highest sacred use.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Which seal was used when purchasing libations for a bull (par) offering?
A. Zachar
B. Egel
C. Gedi
D. Ḥote
Correct: B — Egel means 'calf' and was the seal for bull libations — the largest quantity (3 esronim of flour, half a hin of wine). Each animal type had its own seal to ensure correct libation amounts.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Why did the Temple use two separate officers in the seal system rather than one?
A. To honor the two tablets of the Torah by doubling every function
B. Because one officer handled morning sacrifices and the other afternoon
C. To separate money collection from goods dispensing, preventing fraud
D. Because priests could not handle money directly
Correct: C — The genius of the seal system is its separation of roles: Officer #1 takes money and issues seals; Officer #2 sees only seals and dispenses goods. They reconcile at day's end — no single person can misappropriate funds.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the core debate regarding the parah adumah (red heifer) and shekalim?
A. Whether the red heifer must be purchased before or after Pesach
B. Whether shekalim public funds may be used to purchase the red heifer
C. Whether women are obligated to contribute a half-shekel toward the red heifer
D. Whether the red heifer's ashes may be stored in the Temple chamber
Correct: B — The parah adumah purifies all Israel from corpse-impurity — a communal need. The question is whether this qualifies it for shekalim funding (like communal sacrifices) or whether its non-altar status places it outside the shekalim mandate.
