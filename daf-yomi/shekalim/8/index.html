🗓️ The Three Annual Collections

Before Pesach: First terumah taken — shekels arrived from nearby regions; baskets marked אלף (א)
↓
Before Shavuot: Second terumah — shekels arrived from more distant regions; baskets marked בית (ב)
↓
Before Sukkot: Third terumah — shekels arrived from diaspora communities; baskets marked גימל (ג)

All public offerings must be purchased only from funds already in the lishkah — ensuring proper consecration

👔 Rules for Entering the Chamber

לא יכנס לא בפרגוד חפות ולא במנעל ולא בסנדל ולא בתפילין ולא בקמיע — ומדבר עמהם משיכנס ועד שיצא
📜 Mishnah Rule The treasurer who enters to collect may not wear a hemmed garment, shoes, sandals, tefillin, or an amulet — lest he conceal coins in them. He enters while speaking aloud, so no one suspects he pocketed funds. If he later became poor, none could say it was divine punishment for theft; if he became rich, none could say he stole from the chamber.

💰 What the Shekel Fund Purchases

Daily Offerings

  • תמידין — daily burnt offerings (morning & evening)
  • מוספין — additional Shabbat and Yom Tov offerings
  • מנחת חביתין — High Priest's daily meal offering
  • Libations of wine, oil, and fine flour

Temple Purification

  • פרה אדומה — Red Heifer preparation
  • שעיר המשתלח — Scapegoat on Yom Kippur
  • לשון של זהורית — Crimson thread for purity rites
  • Water libation (ניסוך המים) on Sukkot

Surplus Usage

  • Temple vessels and ornamental repair
  • Golden altar (מזבח הזהב) refinishing
  • Water channel (אמת המים) construction
  • City walls and towers of Jerusalem

⚖️ Dispute: How Is Surplus Allocated?

Tanna Kamma
Surplus from the shekel collection goes to Temple vessels and ornaments — the holiness of the surplus follows the nature of the original public donation.
Rabbi Yishmael
Surplus funds revert to the donors — shekels were given for specific offerings; once those are covered, the excess belongs to those who gave it.
Rabbi Akiva
Surplus from the terumah goes to נסכים (libations), since libations are an ongoing communal obligation funded from the same public shekel pool.
Rabbi Chananiah סגן הכהנים
Surplus from the incense collection specifically refurbishes the golden altar — the dedicated vessel for incense — preserving the thematic link between surplus and source.
Ruling The accepted halacha: surplus from the public shekel donation remains communal property and is applied to Temple infrastructure, never returned to individual donors.
