🏛️ The Thirteen Shofarot — Temple Collection Boxes

New Shekalim

  • Current-year half-shekel contributions
  • Funds communal public sacrifices
  • Opened three times per year
  • Labeled שקלים חדשים

Old Shekalim

  • Previous-year arrears
  • For those who hadn't yet paid
  • Covers outstanding obligations
  • Labeled שקלים ישנים

Freewill Offerings

  • Bird offerings (קינין)
  • Voluntary burnt offerings (עולות)
  • Wood for the altar fires
  • Frankincense donations

Specific Pledges

  • Gold for the Kapporet cover
  • Half-shekel exact amounts
  • Surplus goes to Temple treasury
  • Designated by donor's vow

🔐 The Chamber of Secrets — Tzedakah with Dignity

Lishkat Chassha'im (לשכת חשאים) — a hidden chamber maintained within the Temple precincts
↓
God-fearing, wealthy donors would deposit money anonymously — no public recognition sought
↓
Poor people from distinguished families would draw funds from this chamber in secret
↓
Goal: Protect the dignity of the poor — no shame, no public exposure, no recorded names
↓
Etymology: chashah (חשה) — meaning silence; the chamber was defined by what was not said
↓
Rabbinic ideal: the giver does not know the recipient; the recipient does not know the giver — highest form of tzedakah

💰 Debate: What Happens to Surplus Shekalim Funds?

R' Akiva
Surplus from the terumah collection becomes nedavah — freewill communal burnt offerings (olot) burned on the altar
R' Yishmael
Surplus shekalim retains its original designation and should not be reallocated to a different purpose
R' Eliezer
Surplus funds are used for nesachim — libations of wine accompanying the communal sacrifices throughout the year
Gemara
Practical halachah: surplus is directed to public needs — wine, oil, flour for communal meal-offerings. It never reverts to private hands
Core Principle Shekalim designated for the public must serve the public — sacred funds given communally remain communal forever

🚪 Nicanor's Gates — A Miracle of the Sea

שערי נקנור — יומא לח א; שקלים פרק ג משנה ד
Story: Yerushalmi Shekalim Nicanor traveled to Alexandria, Egypt to commission magnificent bronze doors for the Temple. On the return voyage a terrible storm threatened to sink the ship. The sailors threw one door overboard to lighten the load — the storm did not stop. As they prepared to throw the second door, Nicanor embraced it crying: 'Throw me in together with it!' The storm immediately ceased.

When the ship arrived at Akko, the first door miraculously surfaced from beneath the hull. In commemoration of this miracle, Nicanor's Gates (שערי נקנור) were left in their natural bronze while every other Temple gate was plated in gold — their plainness itself a monument to divine intervention.

💎 Voluntary Temple Improvements by Benefactors

Wealthy donors and foreign royalty sought to beautify and improve the Temple beyond their required obligations
↓
Ben Kattin
Added 12 spigots to the laver (כיור) so 12 priests could wash simultaneously; invented a mechanical wheel to submerge the laver overnight keeping its water valid
King Monbaz
Melted down his own gold and silver vessels to fashion new handles for the Yom Kippur service implements — donated fresh ones each year
Queen Helena
Donated a gold candlestick (מנורה of gold) above the Temple entrance — its morning reflection announced the time for reciting Shema
↓
Mishnah: נזכרו לשבח — Their memory was preserved for eternal praise, unlike those who acted for personal glory
