🔔 The Thirteen Shofar Chests (Shofarot)

New Shekalim 🆕

  • Current year's half-shekel donations
  • Placed in first shofar chest
  • Used immediately for communal offerings
  • Most sacred — first priority

Old Shekalim 📅

  • Donations from the prior year
  • Placed in second shofar chest
  • Used for voluntary burnt offerings
  • Handled after new shekalim

Bird Offerings 🕊️

  • Funds designated for turtle-doves or young pigeons
  • Used for olot (burnt) or chatat (sin offerings)
  • Separate chest to preserve donor intent
  • Priests allocate between offering types

Wood, Frankincense & Gold 🌿

  • Three separate chests for Temple materials
  • Wood for the altar fire
  • Frankincense for the incense
  • Gold for the Kapporet cover

🤫 Who Benefits from the Chamber of Secret Donations?

Mishnah
The Lishkat Chashaim (לשכת חשאים) exists so that God-fearing donors can give secretly — and poor people from distinguished families are supported from it privately, so they are not shamed.
R' Yehoshua b. Levi
In Jerusalem there were three such chambers. The merit of giving in this way is doubled — the donor avoids pride, and the recipient avoids shame. This is the highest form of tzedakah.
Gemara
How often was the chamber checked? The officers would distribute its funds three times a year — before Pesach, before Shavuot, and before Sukkot — so that the needy would have funds for the festivals.
Tanna Kamma
If funds remained after distributing to the poor of distinguished families, the surplus itself could go toward communal Temple needs — nothing in the Temple treasury was ever wasted.
📜 Key Principle The Temple economy was designed so that every shekel had a purpose — surplus cascaded from one category to the next, always serving the public good.

🔥 How Surplus Incense Funds Are Handled

Step 1 — Annual Incense Purchase: The appointed treasurer purchases the ketoret (קטורת) incense for the entire year from communal shekalim funds.
↓
Step 2 — Assess Leftover Funds: After the incense is purchased, any remaining designated incense money is counted. This is the motar ketoret.
↓
Step 3 — Use for Next Year: The Mishnah rules that motar ketoret is used to purchase incense for the following year — it stays in the incense category.
↓
Step 4 — Compensate the Preparer: The craftsman who pounds and blends the incense is paid from incense funds. His wage comes from this designated pool, not general treasury.
↓
Step 5 — Dispute Over Deep Surplus: If after compensation and next-year purchase there is still excess — it becomes motar and flows into the voluntary burnt-offering fund (olot nedavah).
↓
Step 6 — Public Benefit Preserved: The Talmud emphasizes that donated money never simply disappears — it always circles back to benefit the public through Temple offerings.

⚖️ The Principle of Motar — Cascading Temple Surplus

מותר תרומת הלשכה — חולין לנדבה; מותר הקטרת — קונים בה קטרת; מותר הקטרת — מה שנשתייר הולך לנדבה (שקלים פ"ד)
🏛️ Core Halakha The Mishnah and Gemara on this daf establish a cascading rule for all Temple surpluses (motar): surplus from a specific fund first returns to replenish that same fund for the following year; if still surplus remains, it flows into the general public olot nedavah (voluntary burnt offerings). This ensures the ongoing sanctity of the Temple service and that donors' intentions are maximally honored — specific before general, year-specific before perpetual. The principle — אין מותר הקדש הולך לאיבוד — 'no Temple surplus is ever lost' — undergirds the entire economic structure of Temple finance.
