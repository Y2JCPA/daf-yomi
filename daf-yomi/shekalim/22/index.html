🥩 Found Meat in Jerusalem: Permitted or Forbidden?

R' Yehuda
Meat found in the butcher market of Jerusalem is presumed shelamim — these animals were slaughtered for the altar. It is forbidden outside the Temple courts!
Sages
We follow rov — the majority: most animals slaughtered in Jerusalem are chullin, not consecrated offerings. The meat is permitted.
R' Yehuda
But proximity to the Temple mount establishes a chazakah of holiness. That presumptive status overrides mere statistics!
Sages
Rov is a stronger halakhic mechanism than chazakah. When the majority is clear, we follow it — even near the Temple.
Ruling The law follows the Sages: in the market, follow rov — the meat is chullin and permitted.

🗝️ Lishkat HaRazim — The Chamber of Secrets

לִשְׁכַּת חֲשָׁאִים הָיְתָה בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ — יִרְאֵי חֵטְא נוֹתְנִין לְתוֹכָהּ בְּחָשַׁאי, וַעֲנִיֵּי בְנֵי טוֹבִים מִתְפַּרְנְסִין מִתּוֹכָהּ בְּחָשַׁאי
Temple Institution In the Temple stood a special chamber called Lishkat HaRazim (the Chamber of Secrets). God-fearing wealthy donors would secretly deposit funds within it. Impoverished people from distinguished families — who could not bear the shame of public charity — would secretly withdraw what they needed. The chamber preserved the dignity of both giver and receiver.

🔍 How to Rule on a Doubtful Item Found Near the Temple

Step 1: Identify the location — Temple Mount, Jerusalem proper, or city market? Location is the first filter.
↓
Step 2: Check for a prior status (chazakah) — Was the item previously consecrated, dedicated, or formally designated for Temple use?
↓
Step 3: Calculate rov (majority) — What category do most similar items in this location belong to? How large is the majority?
↓
Step 4: Weigh the two principles — Rov generally prevails in market contexts; chazakah prevails when a formal prior status is legally established.
↓
Step 5: Apply the ruling — Permit or restrict the item. For Temple matters, lean toward stringency when rov and chazakah are roughly equal.
