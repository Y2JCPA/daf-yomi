🪙 The Kalbon Surcharge: Who Pays Extra?

Mishnah
One who pays his own shekel and his fellow's shekel with a single sela owes one kalbon — only one surcharge for the overage.
R' Meir
If one pays for himself and a deaf-mute, minor, or imbecile, he owes two kalbonot — one for himself and one for the dependent, since they cannot act for themselves.
Sages
One who pays for his sons and household owes only one kalbon — they are considered an extension of his person for the purpose of this payment.
Gemara (Yerushalmi)
Why the distinction? A dependent who lacks da'at (legal cognizance) cannot join the payer's intent — their portion stands separately and incurs its own surcharge.
What is a Kalbon? The kalbon (כַּלְבּוֹן) is a small surcharge — roughly a grain of silver — paid when exchanging a larger coin (sela = 2 shekels) for the exact half-shekel. It compensates for the moneychanger's service and ensures the Temple receives exactly the right amount.

📅 Adar 15 — The Tables Are Set On the 15th of Adar, moneychangers set up tables throughout the land to facilitate exchange of larger coins into half-shekels. On the 25th of Adar, tables were set up inside the Temple courtyard itself — at this point, social pressure and even pledging (achrayut) was applied to ensure payment.

👥 Who Is Obligated vs. Exempt from Shekalim

Obligated

  • Adult Israelite men
  • Converts (גֵּרִים)
  • Freed slaves
  • Women who wish to give voluntarily (accepted)
  • Priests — debated, but many say obligated

Exempt

  • Minors (קְטַנִּים) — not obligated
  • Deaf-mutes (חֵרֵשׁ) — lacks legal cognizance
  • Imbeciles (שׁוֹטֶה)
  • Non-Jewish residents (גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב)
  • Slaves not yet freed

🏛️ Are Priests Required to Give Shekalim?

Ben Bukhri
A priest who gives his shekel commits no sin — it is permitted but not required. Priests atone through their Temple service, which is their contribution.
Rabban Yochanan b. Zakkai
You are wrong! A priest who does not give is sinning — the shekel obligation applies to all Israel, priests included, lest they consume the communal offerings without having contributed to them.
Gemara
The Yerushalmi harmonizes: priests in practice refrained from giving, as a form of peaceful assertion of their special status — but halacha is with Rabban Yochanan b. Zakkai that they are obligated.
Historical note Priests claimed that since they eat from the altar, they should not also contribute to it — they would be 'eating from what they themselves sanctified,' creating a conflict of interest. Sages rejected this reasoning.
