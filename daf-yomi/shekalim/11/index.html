⚖️ Individual Surplus vs. Communal Surplus

Individual Motar (מותר יחיד)

  • Leftover from a private vow or donation
  • Belongs to the individual donor's estate
  • Heirs may claim unused surplus
  • Example: surplus from a private bird offering
  • Must be used for its designated purpose

Communal Motar (מותר ציבור)

  • Leftover from communal Temple collections
  • Reverts to the Temple treasury (הקדש)
  • Used for communal Temple needs
  • Example: surplus from the half-shekel fund
  • No individual can claim ownership

שלשה עשר שופרות היו במקדש
📜 The Thirteen Shofar Chests The Mishnah teaches that there were thirteen shofar-shaped collection chests (שופרות) in the Temple. Each was designated for a specific type of donation — new shekalim, old shekalim, bird offerings, wood, frankincense, gold for the kapporet, and various voluntary offerings. The shofar shape (wide at top, narrow at bottom) prevented people from reaching in to take money back.

🗣️ Surplus from Bird Offerings — Who Benefits?

R' Yochanan
Surplus funds from a private bird offering (motar kinnim) go to the Temple treasury — they were consecrated for that purpose, and all leftovers belong to hekdesh.
Reish Lakish
No — surplus from a private obligation returns to the donor's estate. The individual dedicated only what was needed; the excess retains its private character and does not become Temple property.
R' Yochanan
But the person consecrated this money for the offering! Once dedicated, all connected funds are hekdesh — even the surplus cannot simply revert.
Resolution The Yerushalmi distinguishes: if the donor said 'these coins are for my offering,' surplus goes to hekdesh. If the donor said 'I will bring an offering from these,' only what is used is consecrated, and surplus is unconsecrated.

🔍 Found Coins in Jerusalem — Determining Status

Coins found in Jerusalem — what is their status?
↓
Found near the Temple Mount
Presumed to be maaser sheni (second tithe money) — must be redeemed and spent in Jerusalem on food
Found in markets of Jerusalem
Presumed to be ordinary (chullin) money — most money in markets is unconsecrated
Found in the Temple courtyard
If found during Passover — assume maaser sheni; rest of year — assume Temple funds (hekdesh)

💰 What Happens to Temple Surplus Funds (Motar)?

Step 1 — Identify the source: Was the money donated for a specific obligatory purpose, or as a voluntary gift?
↓
Step 2 — Determine designation: Did the donor consecrate the object (e.g., 'this animal') or the obligation (e.g., 'I must bring an offering')?
↓
Step 3 — Apply the rule: Obligatory surplus (motar chovat) goes to communal Temple needs — nedavah (voluntary) offerings.
↓
Step 4 — Nedavah surplus: Surplus from voluntary offerings is used for communal burnt offerings (olot tzibur) on the altar.
↓
Step 5 — Final accounting: Any residual surplus at year's end contributes to Temple infrastructure: water channels, city walls, and towers of Jerusalem.
