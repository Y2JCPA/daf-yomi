Blood Stain Attribution Framework

Blood stain found on a woman's body or garment
↓
Can Attribute to External Source
Passed through butcher market, handled animals, killed a louse, has a wound → Pure
Cannot Attribute
No plausible external source for the blood → Impure
Core Principle R' Akiva: The Sages did not institute blood stain impurity to be stringent — but rather to be lenient. By Torah law only sensed blood flow causes impurity, not stains.

Why Does Shmuel Specify 'the Ground'?

Shmuel
If she examined the ground, sat on it, and found blood — she is pure even if she didn't sense emission.
Rav Ashi
Shmuel follows R' Neḥemya: items not susceptible to tumah (like the ground) cannot become impure from stains.
Rav Yirmeya
No — Shmuel means she is pure by rabbinic law specifically because lack of sensation indicates the blood is not from her body.
Gemara
If Rav Yirmeya is right, why say 'the ground'? A cloak should also be pure!
Gemara
Shmuel uses lo tzirikha style: a cloak is obvious (hard to examine), so he teaches the chiddush — even the ground, which is easy to examine well, she is still pure.
Key Dispute Rav Ashi: Shmuel's ruling is limited to ground (not susceptible to tumah). Rav Yirmeya: It applies broadly to any surface when she didn't sense emission.

Inner Side of the Leg — How Far?

Stain found on inner side of woman's leg
↓
Students of R' Yannai: Inner side extends to the mekom ḥavak — the joint/ligament at the kneecap
↓
Is the joint itself inner or outer?
↓
Rav Ketina: The joint itself is considered inner side → she is impure
R' Yirmeya's Dilemmas What if the stain is shaped like a bracelet? A straight line? A series of drops? Along the width of the thigh? — Left unresolved (teiku).

Three Robes — Can She Attribute the Stain?

Can Attribute

  • Passed through a marketplace of butchers
  • Had contact with animal blood
  • Even if stain is on the lowest robe (closest to skin)
  • External source is plausible → Pure

Cannot Attribute

  • No butcher market or blood contact
  • No plausible external source
  • Even if stain is on the uppermost robe
  • Must assume blood is from her body → Impure
Baraita The determining factor is not which robe the stain is on, but whether there is a plausible external attribution.

Mishna: Sources a Woman May Attribute Blood To

🐄 Slaughtered a domesticated animal, wild animal, or bird
↓
👗 Was occupied removing blood stains from garments
↓
👥 Sat alongside others removing blood stains
↓
🐛 Killed a louse — up to size of a split bean (R' Ḥanina ben Antigonus)
↓
🩹 Has a wound that can reopen and bleed, even if scabbed over
↓
👨‍👩‍👦 Her husband or son nearby has a wound
R' Akiva's Teaching By Torah law, only sensed blood flow ("her issue in her flesh shall be blood") renders impure — not a stain. Stain impurity is rabbinic, so the Sages ruled leniently wherever attribution is possible.
