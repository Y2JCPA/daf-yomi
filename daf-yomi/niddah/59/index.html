Stain Slightly Larger Than a Gris — With Squashed Louse

R' Chanina
She is temei'ah (impure). A stain larger than a gris cannot be attributed to a louse — even if a louse is visibly squashed on it.
R' Yannai
She is tehorah (pure). The visible squashed louse clearly accounts for the excess beyond the gris. Subtract the louse's blood → the remainder is only a gris, which can be attributed to a second louse.
R' Yirmiya
New dilemma: What if instead of a squashed louse, she was occupied with a staining item the size of a gris (e.g., bird blood or eye salve)?
R' Yirmiya
Perhaps even R' Chanina would be lenient here (she was actively handling the item), or perhaps even R' Yannai would be strict (no visible proof like a squashed louse)?
Key Distinction R' Yannai requires visible evidence (a squashed louse on the stain) to subtract from the total. R' Yirmiya asks: does being occupied with a staining substance provide equivalent certainty?

Can a Large Stain Be Split by an External Source?

Stain found: 2 gris + slightly more
↓
Can she 'cast' the bird blood into the middle of the stain, splitting it into two sub-gris halves?
↓
You might think
Each side < gris → Tehorah
Baraita rules
No! We do not split stains this way → Temei'ah
Baraita If she was occupied with a small item, she may not attribute a large stain to it. One cannot assume external blood landed in the middle of a stain to divide it into permissible segments.

Rava's Ruling: Cross-Type Attribution

אמר רבא: נמצא עליה מין אחד — תולה בו אפילו כמה מינין
Rava If one type of stain is found on a woman (e.g., sycamore sap or eye salve), she may attribute other stains of different colors to external sources as well. Reasoning: just as one unknown substance sprayed on her, so too could others have.

This differs from a woman who was occupied with a specific red item — she cannot attribute a black stain to it, because the known source doesn't match.

Mishna: Blood Found in Urine — R' Meir vs R' Yosei

R' Meir

  • Standing → Temei'ah (urine may return to uterus and bring blood)
  • Sitting with steady stream → Tehorah (urine flows without returning)
  • Distinguishes based on posture and flow mechanics

R' Yosei

  • Standing or sitting → Tehorah
  • Blood in urine is attributed to a wound, not the uterus
  • Halakha follows R' Yosei (Shmuel / R' Abba)
Halakha Shmuel ruled the halakha follows R' Yosei: a woman who finds blood in her urine is tehorah regardless of posture. R' Abba similarly ruled this way in practice.
