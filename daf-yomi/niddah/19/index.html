Niddah 19: The Spectrum of Blood

חמשה דמים טמאים באשה
CORE QUESTION The Mishna establishes five definitive colors of ritually impure blood in a woman, each defined by a precise visual standard. The Gemara then explores the scriptural basis for the existence of pure blood and debates whether green blood carries any halakhic significance — as niddah blood, as a liquid for food susceptibility, or neither.

The Five Colors of Impure Blood

ColorVisual Standard
🔴 Red (אדום)Blood flowing from a wound
⚫ Black (שחור)Dark as ḥeret (dried ink) — deeper is impure, lighter is pure
🟠 Crocus (כרכום)The brightest part of the crocus/saffron flower
🟤 Water on Red EarthEarth from Beit Kerem Valley, inundated until water pools on surface
🍷 Diluted WineTwo parts water, one part wine — specifically Sharon wine
PRECISION Each color is defined by a specific real-world comparison — not abstract descriptions but concrete, reproducible standards.

Two Additional Colors?

Beit Shammai
Blood the color of fenugreek water (מי תלתן) and roast meat drippings (מראה בשר צלי) are also ritually impure.
Beit Hillel
Blood of those colors is ritually pure.
First Tanna
The five listed colors are definitely impure. Other colors like fenugreek water are uncertain — left in abeyance.
Beit Hillel
No — those additional colors are entirely pure, not even uncertain.
KEY DISTINCTION The dispute between the First Tanna and Beit Hillel is whether borderline colors are uncertain (ספק) or completely pure.

The Green Blood Debate

A woman sees green blood (דם ירוק)
↓
Akavya ben Mahalalel
Impure — green is faded red blood (originally red, color faded)
Rabbis
Pure — green blood is entirely pure, not even uncertain
HOW DOES AKAVYA RECONCILE? R' Ḥanina explains: just as black blood is really red that faded dark, green blood is red that faded in the other direction — it was initially red but its color turned green.

Rabbi Meir vs. The Rabbis on Green Blood

Rabbi Meir

  • Green blood should at minimum render food susceptible to ritual impurity
  • Derives from verbal analogy: שלחיך (Song of Songs 4:13) ↔ שולח (Job 5:10)
  • Menstrual blood is compared to water — both render food susceptible
  • R' Yoḥanan says: Rabbi Meir actually accepted Akavya's view that green blood is impure as niddah blood

The Rabbis

  • Green blood does NOT render food susceptible to impurity
  • Blood that renders food susceptible must be like 'blood of the slain' (Numbers 23:24)
  • Green blood is not the type that flows at time of death
  • Reject verbal analogy — no tradition that it is an accepted derivation
RABBI YOSEI Green blood is neither impure as niddah blood (contra Akavya) nor a liquid for food susceptibility (contra R' Meir). It has no halakhic status at all.

Three Interpretations of Rabbi Meir's View

📋 Interpretation 1: Green stain on clothing = pure; green blood seen directly = impure as niddah
↓
❌ Rejected: If so, why did R' Meir mention food susceptibility instead of niddah impurity?
↓
📋 Interpretation 2: Green blood alone = pure; red blood followed by green = impure (like fluids of a zav/zava)
↓
❌ Rejected: Rabbis counter that impure fluids must be 'gathered and expelled' like saliva — green blood is not
↓
📋 Interpretation 3 (Final): Green blood should at least render food susceptible via verbal analogy from Song of Songs
↓
⚖️ Rabbis respond: Only blood like 'blood of the slain' renders food susceptible; verbal analogy not accepted
PRINCIPLE A person may derive an a fortiori inference independently, but a verbal analogy (גזירה שוה) must be received by tradition — one cannot construct it on one's own.
