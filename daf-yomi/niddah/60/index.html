Attributing Blood Stains on a Loaned Garment

A woman lends her garment and finds a blood stain upon its return
↓
To whom was the garment lent?
Gentile Woman
Can she attribute the stain?
Depends on the gentile's status — age and bleeding history
Jewish Woman (Zava)
Dispute: R' Shimon b. Gamliel permits attribution;
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi does not
Non-susceptible Item
R' Neḥemya: No impurity from stains on items not susceptible to tumah
DAF THEME When a blood stain is found on a loaned garment, whose status is "ruined"? The Gemara explores when a woman may attribute the stain to the borrower, sparing herself from impurity.

Must the Gentile Woman Have Already Bled?

Rav
The mishna's gentile woman is like a niddah — she must have already experienced a menstrual flow to attribute the stain to her.
Rav Sheshet
Rav was dozing when he said that! A baraita says she can attribute the stain to any gentile woman, regardless of bleeding history.
Rav Sheshet
Even Rabbi Meir, who is stricter, only requires the gentile be fit to see blood — not that she actually has!
Rava
You misread the baraita! Rabbi Meir is actually the lenient opinion. The first tanna requires the gentile to have already bled; Rabbi Meir says being of age suffices.
KEY PROOF A second baraita states the first tanna forbids attribution entirely — proving he is more stringent, and Rabbi Meir is the lenient voice permitting attribution to any age-appropriate gentile.

Lending to a Lesser Zava: Two Views

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel

  • She MAY attribute the stain to the borrower
  • Applies to a שומרת יום כנגד יום on her second day (her uterus is considered 'open')
  • Also applies to a woman counting 7 clean days who hasn't immersed
  • Result: Lender is pure ✓ — borrower must restart counting

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi

  • She may NOT attribute the stain to the borrower
  • Both women are deemed impure
  • Both statuses are 'ruined'
  • More stringent approach to protect both women's counting
AGREEMENT Both agree she MAY attribute the stain to: (1) a day-for-day observer on her FIRST day, (2) a woman after childbirth in her period of דם טוהר (blood of purity), and (3) a virgin whose blood is assumed to be hymenal.

When Can She Attribute the Stain? (Cases of Agreement)

Borrower's StatusCan Attribute?Reason
שומרת יום כנגד יום — Day 1✅ Yes (both agree)Her status is already impure; attribution doesn't worsen it

Counting 7 clean days (no mikveh)⚖️ DisputeRSBG: yes; Rabbi: no — would ruin her count

PRINCIPLE Attribution is permitted when it does not worsen the borrower's status. The dispute centers on borderline cases where attribution would restart a counting process.

Rabbi Neḥemya: Stains on Non-Susceptible Items

Blood stain found on an item
↓
Is the item susceptible to ritual impurity (מקבל טומאה)?
Yes
The woman is ritually impure due to the stain (Rabbis)
No
R' Neḥemya: She is pure — 'clean she shall sit upon the ground' (Isaiah 3:26)
↓
Extensions of R' Neḥemya's rule:
R' Ḥanina
Exterior of an inverted earthenware vessel — pure (no decree resembling interior)
Abaye
Small rags less than 3×3 fingerbreadths — pure (not useful for rich or poor)
HALAKHA Rav Ḥiyya bar Rav Mattana rules in the name of Rav: The halakha follows Rabbi Neḥemya. Rav Naḥman challenges this with an incident where the Sages ruled strictly.
7 / 9

Does the Halakha Follow Rabbi Neḥemya?

Rav Ḥiyya bar Rav Mattana
Rav rules: The halakha follows Rabbi Neḥemya — stains on non-susceptible items do not cause impurity.
Rav Naḥman
But there was an incident where blood was found on an olive leaf and on the edge of a bathtub — the Sages deemed both women impure!
Rav Naḥman
An olive leaf is not susceptible to impurity — this ruling contradicts Rabbi Neḥemya!
Rav Ḥiyya bar Rav Mattana
It is a dispute among Tannaim — the incident follows the Rabbis, but the halakha still follows Rabbi Neḥemya.
INCIDENT The Gemara details an incident of two women grinding at a hand mill: blood found beneath the inner woman renders both impure (the outer one pushes inward); blood beneath the outer woman renders only her impure.
