Blood Found in a Piece of Tissue

First Tanna (R' Eliezer)
It is the manner of a woman to see menstrual blood in a discharged piece of tissue. The term "in her flesh" applies — she is impure.
Rabbis
It is not the manner of a woman to see blood in a piece of tissue. The blood is not considered menstrual — she is pure.
Rava
Everyone agrees it's not the normal manner. The real dispute: Can the woman be pure but the source (uterus) be impure?
R' Eliezer (per Rava)
Woman is pure (not a niddah), but the blood is impure since it passed through the source. She contracts first-degree impurity on contact.
Rabbis (per Rava)
The woman is entirely pure, and the source location is also pure. Food she touches remains pure.
PRINCIPLE Rava reframes the dispute: not about the nature of tissue discharge, but whether the uterine source can independently transmit impurity.

Semen Extracted with a Sliver of Wood

Rabba asks Rav Huna:
A man extracts semen with a sliver of wood — is he impure?
↓
Reading 1: "From whom"
Semen must emerge by itself from his flesh — extraction = pure
Reading 2: "From whom"
Semen must merely emerge outside his body — even by extraction = impure
↓
Rav Huna: Semen is impure only when sufficient to block the tip of the penis — this amount cannot be extracted with a sliver of wood → pure
SOURCE "A man from whom the flow of semen emerges" (Leviticus 22:4) — the word "from whom" drives the entire inquiry.

Does Seminal Emission Negate a Zav's Clean Days?

Rabba
If impurity comes only from touching the semen (not emission itself), why does a seminal emission negate the count of seven clean days? Touching impurity doesn't negate a zav's count!
Rav Huna
A zav's seminal emission always contains bits of ziva (tzaḥtzoḥei ziva) — that's why it negates the count.
Rabba
But if it contains ziva, it should negate the entire seven-day count! Yet the baraita teaches it negates only one day!
Rav Huna
It's a Torah edict: pure ziva negates all seven days; bits of ziva mixed with semen negate only one day.
VERSE "This is the law of the zav, and of one from whom the flow of semen emerges, so that he is impure by it" (Lev. 15:32) — "by it" limits negation to one day.

The Dissolution Test for Unusual Discharges

👩 Woman discharges unusual items (shell-like, hair-like, dry substances)
↓
🏛️ Sages consult with doctors to understand the medical cause
↓
💧 Cast the item into lukewarm water (Reish Lakish: specifically lukewarm)
↓
⏳ Soak — possibly up to 24 hours (unresolved for blood items)
↓
✅ If it dissolves → it is blood → woman is impure
↓
❌ If it does not dissolve → it is a distinct entity → woman is pure
HISTORICAL NOTE R' Elazar son of R' Tzadok brought two real cases from Tivin to the Sages in Yavne — women discharging red shell-like and hair-like items — establishing this practical test.

Dissolution Test: Two Methods

Sages' Method

  • Cast item into lukewarm water
  • Item must dissolve on its own
  • May require soaking up to 24 hours (like carcass test)
  • Passive process — no manual intervention

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel

  • Woman mashes the item with saliva
  • Presses with fingers on a fingernail
  • Active manual process
  • Difference: mashing vs. self-dissolving
RAVINA The practical difference: does an item count as blood if it can be mashed by pressing, or only if it dissolves by itself in water?
