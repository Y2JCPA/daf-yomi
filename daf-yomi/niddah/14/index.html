Sleeping on One's Back (Aparkeid)

R' Yehoshua b. Levi
One who sleeps lying on his back is cursed — it may lead to seminal emission
Rav Yosef
One lying on his back may not recite Shema — implying that sleeping is permitted?
Gemara
Sleeping on back is forbidden, but leaning slightly to the side is permitted. For Shema, even leaning is prohibited.
Objection
But R' Yochanan would lean slightly and recite Shema!
Gemara
R' Yochanan was corpulent (שמן) — he could only lean slightly, so this was sufficient for him
Principle Physical circumstances can create different halakhic standards for different individuals

R' Zeira: Why Not Suspect Louse Blood?

Blood found on husband's cloth — could it be from a squashed louse?
↓
Version 1: Baduk (Examined)
The area is considered examined — no louse could be present there
Version 2: Daḥuk (Narrow)
The area is too narrow for a louse to enter
↓
Practical difference: A squashed louse is found near blood on the cloth
↓
Baduk
Louse came from elsewhere → blood is definitely hers → impure
Daḥuk
Louse may have entered this time → uncertain impurity
Nafka Mina When a dead louse is found alongside blood, the two versions yield different rulings

R' Ḥiyya vs. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: Cloth in the Box

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
A woman who examined with an unchecked cloth, placed it in a box, and found blood the next day — she is definitely impure
R' Ḥiyya
Don't you require the stain to be larger than a split bean (גריס) to exclude louse blood?
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi
Indeed (aval), that is correct
R' Ḥiyya
Then you are treating this as a stain (uncertain) — if it were definite, size wouldn't matter!
Rabbi (rebuttal)
The size requirement only excludes louse blood. Once excluded, it is certainly from her body
Analysis The Gemara concludes R' Ḥiyya challenged his teacher in old age — in youth, he too would have ruled definitely impure
