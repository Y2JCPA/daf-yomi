The Eleventh Day: Where Impurity Meets Its Limits

Beit Shammai
A woman who sees blood on the 11th day of ziva and immerses that evening transmits impurity to mishkav u'moshav AND is liable for a sin offering
Beit Hillel
She transmits impurity to mishkav u'moshav by decree, but is exempt from bringing a sin offering
Beit Shammai
Apply your method equally! If you decree impurity, decree the offering too!
Beit Hillel
No — extending the decree to offerings would be a leniency, permitting a non-sacred animal into the Temple courtyard!
Core Principle The 12th day cannot combine as a day of ziva — so the 11th day's bleeding cannot create a zavah gedolah, limiting the scope of impurity decrees.

Woman Sees Blood on the 11th Day of Ziva

Woman experiences bleeding on day 11 of her ziva period
↓
She immerses that evening (night after day 11) and has intercourse
↓
Beit Shammai
Impure: transmits to mishkav u'moshav
Liable for sin offering
Beit Hillel
Impure: transmits to mishkav u'moshav (decree)
Exempt from sin offering
Why Exempt? Since day 12 is unfit for ziva, even three consecutive days (11-12-13) would not create a zavah gedolah — so the 11th day carries reduced severity.

The Great 'Apply Your Method Equally' Exchange

Beit Shammai
What's different about the 11th day vs. mid-ziva days? If you equate them for impurity, equate them for the offering too!
Beit Hillel
Mid-ziva: the next day combines with it as a ziva day. The 11th day? Day 12 does not combine — that's the difference.
Beit Shammai
Then be consistent — if they're not equal for offerings, don't equate them for impurity either!
Beit Hillel
We decreed impurity as a stringency. But requiring an offering would be a leniency — permitting a non-sacred animal into the Temple!
Beit Hillel
And from your own words you are refuted! You say if she immersed on day 12 and then saw blood — impure but exempt from offering. Apply YOUR method equally!
Key Logic Beit Hillel distinguish between decrees that add stringency (permitted) and those that would introduce leniency (forbidden — bringing a non-sacred animal to the Temple).

Zav's First Discharge: Moving It & Sitting On Items

Beit Shammai

  • One who moves the first discharge — status is contingent
  • If zav sees a second discharge → retroactively impure
  • If no second discharge → treated like seminal emission (pure)
  • Items of mishkav u'moshav between sightings — also contingent

Beit Hillel

  • One who moves the first discharge — pure
  • Treated as seminal emission from the start
  • Does not transmit impurity to one who moves it
  • Items of mishkav u'moshav between sightings — pure
Context This baraita about the zav is brought to challenge Rav Huna's position that Beit Shammai consider a shomeret yom k'neged yom definitely impure by decree.

Rav Huna's Resolution: Frequency Determines the Decree

🔍 Challenge: Baraita says Beit Shammai treat zav's status as contingent — but Rav Huna says Beit Shammai hold shomeret yom is definitely impure!
↓
🔄 Resolution: Reinterpret — 'like a shomeret yom' means like one who has intercourse with a shomeret yom after immersion
↓
❓ But why is she definitely impure while he (who has intercourse) has only contingent status?
↓
🩸 Answer: She frequently sees blood — so the Sages decreed her definitely impure
↓
👤 He does not frequently see blood — so the decree on him is lighter (contingent only)
↓
🛏️ Similarly: she transmits to mishkav u'moshav (she sits/lies frequently) but not to her partner (intercourse is less frequent)
Principle The scope of rabbinic decrees is calibrated to the frequency of the activity — more common actions receive stricter treatment.

↓
Why exempt?

Frequency principle:
She → definitely impure (commonly sees blood)
He → contingent only (less frequent)
Mishkav → impure (constant contact)
