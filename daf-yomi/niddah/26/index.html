Sandal Fetus: Birth Order Dispute

Rav Pappa
The sandal fetus and twin are not side by side — the twin's head presses into the sandal's abdomen at half its height.
Rav Pappa
Head-first birth: The twin pushes the sandal out first → sandal is the bekhor for inheritance.
Rav Pappa
Feet-first birth: The twin emerges first → the mother brings an offering for the sandal.
Rav Huna bar Taḥlifa (Rava)
They lie precisely side by side. Reverse Rav Pappa's cases!
Rav Huna bar Taḥlifa (Rava)
Feet-first: The living twin hangs on; the lifeless sandal slides out first → bekhor for inheritance.
Rav Huna bar Taḥlifa (Rava)
Head-first: The living twin's head emerges first = birth. The sandal needs majority of body → twin is born first.
Core Issue Both agree one exits first — they disagree on the mechanism: position in the womb vs. life force affecting speed of emergence.

Sandal Fetus: Bekhorot vs. Karetot

Bekhorot (Firstborn)

  • Sandal fetus born first → twin is firstborn for inheritance
  • NOT firstborn for pidyon haben (redemption)
  • Case: Sandal exits womb before twin
  • Or: Caesarean delivery + regular sandal birth

Karetot (Offerings)

  • Mother brings a birth offering for the sandal
  • Case: Full offspring by caesarean, sandal through the womb
  • No offering for caesarean birth (acc. to Rabbis)
  • R' Shimon: Gentile converts then delivers sandal → brings offering
R' Yirmeya's Case Even R' Shimon (who requires an offering for caesarean birth) agrees the Karetot mishna is needed: a woman who gave birth as a gentile, converted, then delivered the sandal — she brings an offering only for the sandal.

Afterbirth in the House: Impurity Dispute

Tanna Kamma (Mishna)
If there is a שליא (afterbirth) in a house, the house is tamei with tumat ohel (corpse-tent impurity).
Tanna Kamma (Mishna)
The afterbirth is not itself an offspring — but there is no afterbirth without an offspring. The offspring disintegrated inside it.
R' Shimon
The house is NOT tamei. The offspring disintegrated before emerging, turning to blood.
R' Shimon
That blood was batel b'rov (nullified by the majority) of blood that accompanied the miscarriage.
Principle The Tanna Kamma holds: once an offspring existed, the afterbirth transmits corpse impurity. R' Shimon holds: the offspring dissolved and was nullified before birth.

Attributing Afterbirth to a Prior Birth

Woman gives birth, then later discharges an afterbirth (שליא)
↓
Non-Viable Offspring
Within 3 days → attributed to that birth
After 3 days → concern for a second offspring
Viable Offspring
Up to 10 days → attributed to that birth
No concern for another offspring
↓
Rav's principle: An offspring does not remain in the womb after a viable birth — but a non-viable birth is different
Resolution Rav Kahana reconciles Rav's two seemingly contradictory statements: the 3-day window applies only to non-viable births, where a second fetus may linger. After a viable birth, no fetus remains.
