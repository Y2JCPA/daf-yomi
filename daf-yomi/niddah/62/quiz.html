QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why does the Gemara reject the identification of borit as sulfur?
A. Sulfur is too expensive to use as a cleaning agent
B. Sulfur has no root and cannot be subject to Sabbatical Year laws, yet the baraita lists borit among Sabbatical Year items
C. Sulfur would damage the fabric of the garment
D. Sulfur was not available in the Land of Israel
Correct: B — The baraita lists borit among plants subject to Sabbatical Year laws. Since anything without a root is not subject to these laws, and sulfur is a mineral without a root, borit cannot be sulfur — it must be a type of ice plant.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to R' Zeira, if one applies the seven substances twice and the stain disappears the second time, what is the status of items handled AFTER the second washing?
A. Pure, because the first test already cleared them
B. Impure, because by repeating the test he showed he was particular and the stain proved to be blood
C. It depends on whether the stain faded or fully disappeared
D. Pure, because the second application is considered extra-stringent
Correct: B — R' Zeira teaches that by repeating the washing, the owner demonstrated his concern (particularity) about the stain. When the stain then disappeared, it proved to be blood — making items handled after the second washing impure.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the fundamental disagreement between R' Yochanan and Reish Lakish regarding absorbed impure liquids?
A. Whether the oven needs to be new or used
B. Whether absorbed substances impart impurity before they emerge — and whether this differs by severity of the impurity
C. Whether chamber pots can become pure through immersion
D. Whether a zav's liquids are impure at all
Correct: B — R' Yochanan holds that absorbed substances don't impart impurity until they emerge, regardless of severity. Reish Lakish distinguishes: lesser impurity requires emergence (heating), but greater Torah-level impurity (like a zav's urine) imparts impurity even while absorbed.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does Reish Lakish dismiss R' Yochanan's proof from R' Ḥiyya's baraita about nullifying certain menstrual blood?
A. He says R' Ḥiyya was speaking hypothetically
B. He argues R' Ḥiyya's teaching contradicts the Mishna — if R' Yehuda HaNasi didn't teach it, R' Ḥiyya couldn't have learned it
C. He claims the baraita was a later interpolation
D. He says it only applies to old, dried blood
Correct: B — Reish Lakish argues that as R' Yehuda HaNasi's student, R' Ḥiyya could not have taught something contradicting the Mishna. Therefore, this teaching attributed to R' Ḥiyya must be erroneous and cannot serve as proof.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why can't applying soap (tzafon) alone determine whether a stain is blood?
A. Soap is too weak to remove blood
B. Soap causes both blood AND dye to disappear, so its removal of a stain is inconclusive
C. Soap was not included in the original Mishnaic list
D. Soap only works on fresh stains
Correct: B — The Gemara explains that soap removes dye as well as blood. Therefore, a stain disappearing under soap alone does not prove it was blood. The seven-substance test is needed because those specific substances remove blood but not dye.
