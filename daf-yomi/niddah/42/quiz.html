QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to the Gemara, why does Rava say a woman will certainly discharge semen over three days?
A. Because all semen naturally dissolves in three days
B. Because she turns from side to side in bed
C. Because the Torah mandates a three-day waiting period
D. Because the body temperature causes discharge
Correct: B — The Gemara resolves the contradiction between Rava and R' Shimon by explaining that Rava refers to a woman who turns in bed, which causes certain discharge, while R' Shimon refers to one who remains still.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What are the three practical differences (nafka mina) between classifying semen discharge as 'emission' vs. 'contact'?
A. Negating 7 clean days, minimum amount, and impurity while inside the body
B. Duration of impurity, immersion requirements, and terumah restrictions
C. Whether she counts as a zavah, niddah, or yoledet
D. Whether she needs one immersion or two, and the waiting period
Correct: A — The Gemara explicitly lists three differences: whether discharge negates the 7 clean days count, whether any amount renders impure, and whether she is impure while the semen is still inside her body.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
In the story of the Shabbat circumcision, why did Rava ultimately rule the baby should NOT be circumcised on Shabbat?
A. Because the baby was ill and circumcision had to be delayed
B. Because the baby's head emerged before Shabbat, making it born on Friday — so Shabbat would be day 9, not day 8
C. Because circumcision never overrides Shabbat
D. Because the father hadn't properly prepared the circumcision tools
Correct: B — Since the baby's cry was heard before Shabbat, Rava concluded the head had already emerged (הוציא ראשו), making Friday the birthday. The 8th day would be the following Friday, and a delayed circumcision does not override Shabbat.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What is the dispute between Abaye and Rava regarding the vaginal space?
A. Whether it is considered internal or external for niddah purposes
B. Whether it is 'encapsulated' (no impurity) or 'concealed' (impure by carrying)
C. Whether blood found there is from menstruation or from a wound
D. Whether items placed there require immersion or not
Correct: B — Abaye holds the vaginal space is 'encapsulated' (בלוע) so an olive-bulk of carcass there doesn't impart impurity. Rava holds it is 'concealed' (בית הסתרים) — not impure by contact, but impure by carrying.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Why does R' Yirmeya distinguish between the impurity of a swallowed bird carcass and childbirth blood inside the body?
A. Bird carcass impurity is rabbinic while blood impurity is biblical
B. A swallowed bird carcass has no external contact-impurity equivalent, but blood does — so blood should only be impure outside
C. Bird carcasses are always impure but blood is only sometimes impure
D. The bird carcass dissolves inside the body while blood does not
Correct: B — R' Yirmeya notes that touching an unslaughtered bird carcass doesn't cause impurity — swallowing is the only route. But blood has an external impurity form (contact), so there's no reason to impose internal impurity; it should only be impure when it emerges.
