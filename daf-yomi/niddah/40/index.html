Disqualified Offerings on the Altar: Ascend or Descend?

Tanna Kamma
Three exclusions teach that offerings slaughtered at night, whose blood spilled, or whose blood left the courtyard — if they ascended the altar, they must descend.
R' Shimon
'This is the law of the burnt offering' — the Torah included all items that ascend upon the altar. Even disqualified offerings: if they ascended, they shall not descend.
R' Shimon
But 'This' limits the rule — animals involved in bestiality, idol worship, tereifa, and similar inherent disqualifications must descend.
Core Principle The dispute centers on whether procedural disqualifications (how the offering was handled) differ from inherent disqualifications (what the animal is) regarding the altar's capacity to 'accept' them.

The Governing Verse

זאת תורת העולה
Torah Source "Zot torat ha'olah" — "This is the law of the burnt offering"

Rabbi Shimon derives from this verse a sweeping principle: the Torah included in one law all items that ascend upon the altar. Even disqualified offerings — if they ascended, they shall not descend.

The word "This" (zot) then serves to exclude certain categories of disqualified animals.

Three Cases That Must Descend (Tanna Kamma)

Slaughtered at Night

  • Offering slaughtered outside proper time
  • Night is not a valid time for slaughter
  • If placed on altar — must be removed

Blood Spilled

  • Blood spilled before sprinkling (zerikah)
  • Cannot fulfill the essential blood service
  • If placed on altar — must be removed

Blood Taken Outside

  • Blood left beyond the curtains (kela'im)
  • Exited the Tabernacle courtyard
  • If placed on altar — must be removed
Key Term Kela'im (קלעים) — the curtains surrounding the courtyard of the Tabernacle in the wilderness, equivalent to the Temple courtyard walls.

R' Shimon's Excluded Cases (MUST Descend)

↓

↓
🤕 Tereifa — animal with fatal wound/defect
↓
✂️ Animal born by caesarean section (yotzei dofen)
Common Thread These are all inherent disqualifications — the problem is in the animal itself, not in how the service was performed. The word 'Zot' (This) excludes them.
