↓

Burial locations, firstborn status, grave markings — with corroborating evidence

Why Don't Samaritans Bury Stillborns?

Gemara
What verse did the Samaritans (Kutim) rely on to not bury stillborn children?
Gemara
From Deuteronomy 19:14: 'You shall not remove your fellow's boundary marker' — the Sages derived a prohibition on selling ancestral burial grounds.
Samaritan Logic
One who has an inheritance (nachalah) has a boundary (gevul) — i.e., a burial place. A stillborn who never inherits land has no burial obligation.
Mishna
Yet the Samaritans are deemed credible to state where they did or did not bury stillborns, and that place is considered pure.
PRINCIPLE The Samaritans' mistaken derivation from 'boundary marker' led them to exempt stillborns from burial — yet their testimony about locations remains accepted under certain conditions.

Samaritan Credibility: Accepted vs. Not Accepted

✅ Credible

  • Burial of stillborn children (with corroboration)
  • Firstborn animal status (when shearing/working it)
  • Grave markings (based on Ezekiel 39:15)
  • They are knowledgeable about embryo formation
  • They are knowledgeable about murky uterine discharge

❌ Not Credible

  • Overhanging boughs (sechachot) — tree hanging over ground
  • Protrusions (peraot) — stones jutting from a fence
  • Beit haperas — field where bones were dispersed by plowing
  • These require knowledge of rabbinic impurity they don't accept
  • Impurity could be transmitted without visible markers
KEY DISTINCTION Samaritans are meticulous about Torah law and biblical practices (like grave marking from Ezekiel), but cannot be trusted regarding impurity rules that depend on rabbinic enactments they don't observe.

Shmuel's Principle: Sensation (Hargasha) Required

Shmuel
If a woman checked the ground, sat on it, and found blood — she is pure. From Leviticus 15:19: 'In her flesh' teaches she must sense the emission.
Gemara
But Shmuel already uses 'in her flesh' to teach that blood inside the body is like blood outside!
Shmuel
If the verse only meant that, it could say 'in the flesh.' The extra 'her' teaches the sensation requirement — two halakhot from one phrase.
Gemara
But Shmuel also needs 'in her flesh' to exclude blood in a gestational sac or amorphous tissue!
Shmuel
We derive all three halakhot from the plain meaning of the single term — the verse is rich enough to support multiple conclusions.
THREE FROM ONE From 'in her flesh' (בבשרה): (1) blood inside = blood outside, (2) must touch her flesh — not in a sac/tissue, (3) she must sense the emission.
