Deriving Mikveh Law from Sota

Rabbis
Just as a sota is uncertain yet treated as definitely forbidden — so too a mikveh of uncertain measure: teruma must be burned
R' Shimon
Just as a sota in the public domain is considered pure — so too an item immersed in a deficient mikveh in the public domain is pure
Rabbis
The sota's public domain exception is because proper seclusion (yichud) is impossible there. But a mikveh can lack water anywhere — domain is irrelevant!
R' Shimon
I derive the end of impurity (immersion) from the beginning of impurity (contact): uncertain contact in public domain = pure, so uncertain immersion in public domain = pure
Key Rebuttal The Rabbis counter R' Shimon: at the beginning of impurity the person has a chezkat tahara (presumption of purity), but at immersion the person has a chezkat tum'ah (presumption of impurity) — we don't remove that status based on uncertainty.

Why Public Domain Doesn't Help (According to the Rabbis)

Mikveh found to be lacking water
↓
Is the mikveh in a public or private domain?
Public Domain
Two weakening factors:
1. Mikveh is currently lacking
2. Item has chezkat tum'ah
→ Still definitely impure
Private Domain
Standard uncertainty + chezkat tum'ah
→ Definitely impure
Rabbis' Principle When two factors weaken the case for purity — current deficiency plus presumptive impurity — the item is treated as definitely impure regardless of domain.

R' Shimon's Mikveh Outcomes by Domain

Public Domain Mikveh

  • Derived from sota in public domain = pure
  • Also derived from beginning of impurity: uncertain contact in public = pure
  • Item immersed there is considered PURE
  • Teruma that touches it may be consumed

Private Domain Mikveh

  • Not 'definitely impure' like sota — no raglayim la'davar (basis for certainty)
  • Sota has a warning + seclusion; mikveh has no such basis
  • Status is SUSPENDED (תלוי)
  • Teruma neither consumed nor burned
R' Shimon's Logic Unlike sota where the husband's warning provides raglayim la'davar (a basis for the matter), the mikveh's uncertainty has no such foundation — so private domain gets suspended status, not definite impurity.

Shammai vs. Hillel: The Basket Challenge

Shammai
A woman who discovers menstrual blood — her time is sufficient (דיה שעתה). She is impure only from the moment of discovery, not retroactively.
Hillel
Don't you concede: a basket with pure items in one corner, and a sheretz found in another corner — the pure items are retroactively impure?
Shammai
Yes, but the basket has a base — the sheretz could have been hidden underneath. A woman has no base — if blood existed earlier, it would have flowed out!
Hillel
Even so, you should enact a safeguard and render her retroactively impure, as the Sages do throughout the Torah!
Shammai
If you do that, you will cause Jewish women to be derelict in the mitzva of pru u'rvu — they will fear intimacy lest they be already impure!
Hillel's Basket (משל הסל) The sheretz in the basket is the classic analogy: just as impurity can be hidden in a basket and discovered later, shouldn't undiscovered menstrual blood render a woman retroactively impure?

Three Versions of Shammai's Reasoning

VersionReasoningImplication for Contraceptive Cloth (מוך)
1. Chezkat TaharaA woman retains her presumptive status of purityContradicted by barrel/mikveh/alleyway cases
2. She Would Sense ItA woman feels the emission of blood (מרגשת)With a cloth: she still didn't sense it → not retroactively impure
3. It Would Flow OutIf blood existed, it would have already emergedWith a cloth: blood may have been blocked → retroactively impure (per Abaye)
Practical Difference Abaye says: the distinction between versions 2 and 3 emerges with a contraceptive cloth (מוך). According to 'she would sense it,' the cloth doesn't affect sensation. According to 'it would flow out,' the cloth blocks flow — so she may be retroactively impure.
