Niddah 49: Hierarchical Halakhic Principles

KEY THEME The Mishnah teaches a series of asymmetric pairs: holes that allow liquid entry certainly allow exit (but not vice versa), limbs with a nail certainly have bone (but not vice versa), and vessels susceptible to zav treading are susceptible to corpse impurity (but not vice versa). The Gemara explores the practical tests and halakhic consequences of each distinction.

Two Types of Holes in Earthenware Vessels

Hole Allows Liquid Entry

  • Larger hole — water can flow IN
  • Vessel loses its vessel status entirely
  • Unfit for mei ḥatat (purification waters)
  • Also disqualified as a gastera (usable shard)
  • No longer susceptible to tumah at all

Hole Allows Only Exit

  • Smaller hole — liquid seeps OUT only
  • Still considered a vessel for some purposes
  • Still fit for mei ḥatat (purification waters)
  • Disqualified as a gastera (shard)
  • Mar Zutra: no one patches a leaky shard
RAV ASI The measure for losing tumah susceptibility is a hole large enough for liquid to enter. The shard standard (exit only) applies specifically to gastera disqualification.

How Do You Test a Vessel's Hole?

Tanna Kamma
Place the broken pot into a tub filled with water. If water enters the pot — the hole allows entry. If not — it only allows exit.
R' Yehuda
Turn the pot upside down in an empty tub, then cover it with water. This avoids the force of submerging it directly.
Ulla
The dispute is about whether liquid entering with difficulty (through force) counts as true 'entry.' R' Yehuda says it does not.
R' Yosei
Don't use fire to test — fire may seal even a large hole. Use hot ash instead, which gives a more reliable result.
ALTERNATIVE TEST Fill the vessel with liquid. If it drips one drop after another continuously, the hole is large enough to allow entry.

Testing Methods for Vessel Holes

🏺 Is the vessel's hole large enough to allow liquid entry?
↓
Tanna Kamma's Test
Place pot right-side up into a tub of water
R' Yehuda's Test
Place pot upside down in empty tub, then add water
R' Yosei's Test
Fill pot, place on hot ash (not fire!)
↓
Water Enters
❌ Hole allows entry — no longer a vessel
Water Does Not Enter
✅ Hole allows only exit — still a vessel for mei ḥatat
KEY DISPUTE Ulla explains: R' Yehuda rejects the Tanna Kamma's method because submerging the pot uses force, and he holds that liquid entering with difficulty does not count.

Zav Treading vs. Corpse Impurity

Susceptible to Both

  • Items designated for sitting or lying
  • Vessels meant for human use/contact
  • If susceptible to midras (treading) → automatically susceptible to met (corpse) tumah

Corpse Tumah Only

  • Measuring vessels (se'ah, tarkav)
  • Not designated for sitting
  • If zav sits on them: 'Stand up so we can do our work!'
  • Susceptible to corpse tumah but NOT midras
LEVITICUS 15:6 "Any object whereon the zav sits" — the verse requires the item be designated for sitting. A measuring vessel someone happens to sit on is excluded.
