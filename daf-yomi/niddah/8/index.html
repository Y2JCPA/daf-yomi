Shmuel's Principle: Where Does Halakha Follow R' Eliezer?

Shmuel: Halakha follows R' Eliezer in four cases
↓
Does this mean four cases total across all of Mishna?
Initial Reading
Only four cases anywhere
Gemara's Conclusion
Four cases within Seder Teharot; many more in other orders
KEY QUESTION How do we prove Shmuel meant only Seder Teharot? By finding cases outside Teharot where halakha also follows R' Eliezer.

Scope of Shmuel's Principle

Shmuel
The halakha is in accordance with Rabbi Eliezer in four cases.
Gemara
He meant four cases within Seder Teharot only. In other orders, there are many instances where halakha follows R' Eliezer.
Proof
Challah case: R' Eliezer says a basket combines loaves for challah obligation — and Shmuel rules halakha follows him! This is in Seder Zera'im, not Teharot.
Gemara
This proves Shmuel's 'four cases' limitation applies only to Seder Teharot.
PRINCIPLE Shmuel's four-case limit applies narrowly to Seder Teharot; R' Eliezer's rulings are accepted far more broadly.

Levirate Marriage with Minor Wife

A man dies childless. His brother (yavam) has a minor wife and the deceased had an adult wife
↓
Yavam had intercourse with the minor, then with the adult
↓
What is the status of each wife?
Rabbis
Minor is disqualified (uncertain bond); adult is also prohibited
R' Elazar
Court instructs minor to refuse (mi'un) → retroactive annulment → yavam may marry the adult
MI'UN A minor girl married off by her mother or brothers can 'refuse' the marriage, dissolving it retroactively — a powerful halakhic tool.

Is Tree Sap Considered Fruit?

Rabbi Eliezer

  • Sap of a tree = its fruit
  • Cheese curdled with orlah sap is prohibited
  • Applies even to fruit-bearing trees
  • Balsam sap has Sabbatical Year sanctity

Rabbis (R' Yehoshua)

  • Sap of leaves/roots ≠ fruit → permitted
  • Sap of unripe figs = fruit → prohibited
  • Distinction: sap of fruit vs. sap of tree
  • Debate whether they concede for non-fruit-bearing trees
RESOLUTION ATTEMPTS The Gemara tries two approaches: (1) Rabbis agree sap of fruit is fruit, just not sap of the tree; (2) Rabbis agree for non-fruit-bearing trees whose sap IS their fruit.
