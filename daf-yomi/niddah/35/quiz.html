QUESTION 1 OF 5
What analogy does Rav Yehuda of Diskarta use to challenge Rava's kal vachomer about ziva imparting impurity?
A. The red heifer — it purifies others but becomes impure itself
B. The scapegoat — it causes impurity to the dispatcher but is itself pure
C. The sotah waters — they test the woman but are themselves pure
D. The parah adumah ashes — they purify the impure but contaminate the pure
Correct: B — Rav Yehuda of Diskarta argues that the scapegoat proves something can cause impurity to others (the dispatcher becomes tamei) while itself remaining pure, since a living animal cannot become impure. This undermines Rava's inference.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
According to Rav Huna, why does the first sighting of ziva impart impurity even when caused by ones (accident)?
A. Because the Torah explicitly states it regarding ziva
B. Because the verse compares the first sighting of ziva to a seminal emission, which imparts impurity even if accidental
C. Because Beit Hillel ruled strictly on all first sightings
D. Because a zav who saw once is already considered a full-fledged zav
Correct: B — Rav Huna derives from Leviticus 15:32 that the first sighting of ziva is compared to a seminal emission (keri). Just as keri imparts tum'ah even if it occurs due to ones, so too the first sighting of ziva.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
What is the purpose of examining a zav after his FIRST sighting, according to the Gemara's defense of Rav Huna?
A. To determine if he has ritual impurity
B. To determine if he will be obligated to bring an offering if he sees two more discharges
C. To verify he is not also a metzora
D. To establish whether he needs to count seven clean days
Correct: B — The Gemara defends Rav Huna by explaining that the examination after the first sighting is not about impurity (he is tamei regardless) but about whether this sighting counts toward the three needed to obligate a korban.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
How does the Gemara reconcile the mishna (where Beit Shammai concede about a yoledet b'ziva) with the baraita (where they disagree)?
A. The mishna is R' Eliezer's view and the baraita is the Sages'
B. The mishna discusses a woman who didn't count seven clean days; the baraita discusses one who counted but didn't immerse
C. The mishna is about dam tohar and the baraita about dam tum'ah
D. The mishna is about the first seven days and the baraita about the remaining thirty-three
Correct: B — The mishna, where Beit Shammai concede, discusses a yoledet b'ziva who did NOT yet count seven clean days — even Beit Shammai agree her blood is impure moist or dry. The baraita, where they disagree, discusses one who counted seven clean days but didn't immerse.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rav Huna's physical description, how does the discharge of ziva differ from semen?
A. Ziva is dark red and thick; semen is clear and thin
B. Ziva is viscous like a fertilized egg white; semen is runny like barley dough water
C. Ziva is runny like barley dough water from flaccid flesh; semen is viscous from erect flesh
D. Ziva comes from living flesh and is warm; semen comes from dead flesh and is cold
Correct: C — Rav Huna teaches that ziva resembles water of barley dough, comes from 'dead flesh' (flaccid), and is runny like the white of an unfertilized egg. Semen comes from 'living flesh' (erect) and is viscous like the white of a fertilized egg.
