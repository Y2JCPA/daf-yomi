⚖️ Beit Shammai vs. Beit Hillel: Festival Offerings

Beit Shammai
One may bring peace-offerings on a Festival (shared by owners & priests), but NOT burnt-offerings — which are entirely consumed on the altar and bring no benefit to owners.
Beit Hillel
One may bring both peace-offerings AND burnt-offerings on a Festival. Semikha (hand-laying) is also permitted on peace-offerings.
Beit Shammai
If Shavuot falls on Friday, Festival offerings cannot push off Shabbat — the day of slaughter is deferred to Sunday.
Beit Hillel
If Shavuot falls on Friday, there is no special makeup day — all offerings are brought on the Festival itself (Friday).
Historical Record When the day of slaughter fell on Sunday, the High Priest deliberately wore regular (not festive) clothes and eulogies were permitted — a public rejection of the Sadducean view that Shavuot always falls on Sunday.

📜 Mishna: What Is Permitted on a Festival?

Beit Shammai

  • ✅ Peace-offerings (שלמים) — permitted
  • ❌ Burnt-offerings (עולות) — prohibited
  • ❌ Semikha (hand-laying) on peace-offerings — prohibited
  • 📅 Shavuot on Friday → slaughter day is Sunday
  • Reasoning: burnt-offerings give no benefit to the owner

Beit Hillel

  • ✅ Peace-offerings (שלמים) — permitted
  • ✅ Burnt-offerings (עולות) — permitted
  • ✅ Semikha (hand-laying) — permitted
  • 📅 Shavuot on Friday → no separate slaughter day
  • Reasoning: Festival joy includes all obligatory offerings

🔄 Deriving Shavuot's 7-Day Redress Period

Question (R. Elazar b. R. Oshaya): From where do we learn that missed Shavuot offerings can be made up during the days following the Festival?
↓
The verse lists Shavuot alongside Passover and Sukkot: "Three times a year" — implying shared redress rules.
↓
Option 1 — Compare to Sukkot: 8-day redress (7 days + Shemini Atzeret). But Shemini Atzeret is an independent festival (with its own הלכות) — the analogy is suspect.
↓
Option 2 — Compare to Passover: 7-day redress. Passover has no appended independent festival — the analogy is cleaner and more certain.
↓
Principle applied: תָּפַסְתָּ מְרוּבֶּה לֹא תָּפַסְתָּ — prefer the smaller, more certain derivation → 7 days for Shavuot.

✊ Grasped Many / Grasped Few

תָּפַסְתָּ מְרוּבֶּה — לֹא תָּפַסְתָּ | תָּפַסְתָּ מוּעָט — תָּפַסְתָּ
כְּלָל פַּרְשָׁנִי — Hermeneutic Principle When an analogy can yield two different results — a larger number (8 days, like Sukkot) or a smaller number (7 days, like Passover) — the Talmud favors the smaller, more certain conclusion.

The logic: a broader claim is harder to establish with certainty; a narrower claim is more defensible.

This principle recurs throughout the Talmud whenever competing analogies pull in different numerical directions.
