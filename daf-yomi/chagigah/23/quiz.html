QUESTION 1 OF 5
What caused the Sages to enact the prohibition against carrying purification water alongside an impure sandal?
A. A Sadducee deliberately contaminated a barrel of purification water
B. A sandal strap made impure by a zav fell off an old man's foot into a barrel of purification water on a boat in the Jordan
C. Rabbi Eliezer issued a ruling that zav-sandals defile by proximity without contact
D. Impure sandals were discovered stored near the Temple courtyard
Correct: B — Rav taught: the incident involved someone transporting purification water on the Jordan; an old man helping him had a zav-defiled sandal strap snap off, land on the barrel's mouth, and fall inside — rendering the water impure and triggering the enactment.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why do vessels 'completed in purity' by an am ha'aretz still require immersion for kodesh?
A. The am ha'aretz's hands are always halakhically impure and defile at the moment of completion
B. Any vessel manufactured outside a חבר's home requires immersion by Mosaic law
C. Rabba bar Sheila (citing Shmuel) explains: the Sages were concerned that the am ha'aretz's spittle may have fallen on the vessel after its completion
D. Rabbi Eliezer holds that all newly completed vessels require immersion regardless of who made them
Correct: C — The puzzle: before completion it cannot contract impurity; the am ha'aretz's touch at completion would make it impure outright. The answer: it was completed in purity, but we fear spittle fell afterward — hence immersion as a Rabbinic safeguard.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rabbi Yehoshua explain why the reed tube used in the red heifer rite must wait for sunset after immersion?
A. Reed is especially porous and absorbs impurity more deeply, requiring extended purification
B. Requiring sunset for the tube is an act of recognition demonstrating rejection of the Sadducees, who mistakenly required sunset-immersion for the priest who burned the red heifer
C. The Torah explicitly requires awaiting sunset for all vessels used in purification rituals
D. It was a precaution in case the cutter accidentally touched a corpse while harvesting the reed
Correct: B — Rabbi Yehoshua: requiring the tube to wait for sunset (even though it was not impure) mimics the sunset requirement — thereby publicly affirming that the Sages reject the Sadducee position that the priest burning the heifer needed to wait for sunset.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What does Abaye mean when he says the Sages 'made no innovations in the impurity laws of the red heifer'?
A. The Sages had no authority to add any stringencies to the red heifer procedure whatsoever
B. The Sages only followed existing biblical rules and never added any stringencies
C. The Sages did not create new categories of impurity — e.g., they did not declare a zav's spade susceptible to seat-impurity (midras) — but adding stringencies within existing categories is permitted
D. Abaye agrees innovations were made but argues they were authorized by a prophetic ruling
Correct: C — Abaye clarifies: 'no innovations' means no new impurity categories were invented (like making a zav's spade a midras object). The Sages could add stringencies within the existing framework — such as treating the tube as if defiled by a creeping animal — without that counting as a forbidden innovation.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What is the source that foods in a single vessel combine to reach the minimum measure (shiur) for sacrificial food (kodesh), but not for teruma?
A. A baraita from the school of Rabbi Ishmael derives it from laws of tithes
B. Rabbi Ḥanin cites a biblical verse as the source for combination applying to kodesh
C. It is derived from teruma by a kal va'ḥomer: if teruma combines, surely kodesh does
D. Rabbi Akiva derives it from the laws of frankincense and coals listed in Tractate Eduyyot
Correct: B — Rabbi Ḥanin identifies a verse as the Torah's source for the principle that kodesh foods within a vessel combine for a shiur. This makes it a biblical derivation unique to kodesh — which is why teruma, lacking such a verse, does not have the same rule.
