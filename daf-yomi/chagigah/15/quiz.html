QUESTION 1 OF 5
Why did Elisha ben Avuya conclude there were 'two powers in heaven,' leading to his heresy?
A. He read Greek philosophical texts arguing for dual divine principles
B. He saw Metatron seated in heaven, an honor he believed reserved only for God
C. A heavenly voice told him directly that a second power existed
D. He misread the verse about the spirit hovering over the waters of creation
Correct: B — Acher saw Metatron sitting in heaven. Since sitting in God's presence is normally forbidden, he wrongly concluded Metatron must be a second divine power. In truth, Metatron had special permission to sit while recording Israel's merits. Metatron was struck with 60 rods of fire precisely to prevent this misunderstanding.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What was Ben Zoma contemplating so deeply that he failed to stand before Rabbi Yehoshua?
A. The four Sages who entered the Pardes and their different fates
B. How many angels surrounded God's throne in the heavenly chambers
C. The precise distance between the upper and lower waters at the moment of creation
D. The nature of the heavenly voice that excluded Acher from repentance
Correct: C — Ben Zoma was absorbed in the verse 'the spirit of God hovered over the face of the waters' (Bereishit 1:2). He was calculating that the upper and lower waters were separated by only a hairsbreadth — like a craftsman's tool between planks, or a dove hovering over its nest. Rabbi Yehoshua sorrowfully concluded: 'Ben Zoma is gone' — his mind had departed through excessive mystical contemplation.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does Rava resolve the contradiction between 'learn from any teacher' and 'the teacher must be like an angel'?
A. One verse applies before the teacher sins; the other applies after he sins
B. One refers to halachic learning; the other refers to aggadic learning
C. Only someone as exceptionally wise as Rabbi Meir can safely extract Torah from a flawed teacher without absorbing his errors
D. The permissive view applies only in the absence of other qualified teachers
Correct: C — Rava explains that Rabbi Meir could do what others cannot — he was wise enough to 'eat the date and discard the peel,' extracting Torah wisdom from Acher while remaining unaffected by his heresy. Ordinary students, however, risk absorbing the teacher's flaws along with the Torah content and should not attempt this.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
What was Acher's unique posthumous verdict, and what created this paradoxical situation?
A. He was immediately sent to Gehenna but given a shortened sentence due to Rabbi Meir's merit
B. He was given a full portion in the World-to-Come because he taught Rabbi Meir Torah
C. He was neither judged for punishment nor admitted to the World-to-Come — Torah protected him from judgment but his sins barred him from reward
D. His verdict was suspended until the end of days when all souls will be judged together
Correct: C — Acher's case was unprecedented: his Torah learning shielded him from being punished (he could not be 'judged' for his sins), yet his grave sins excluded him from the World-to-Come. Rabbi Yoḥanan eventually declared that he himself could bring Acher into the World-to-Come through the power of Torah's merit — and he did so.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
What does the Gemara identify as the root cause embedded in Acher from the beginning that led to his downfall?
A. He was jealous of Rabbi Akiva's ability to ascend and descend safely from the Pardes
B. He had studied too many mystical texts without proper rabbinic supervision
C. Greek tunes never ceased from his mouth and Greek philosophical books were found in his clothing
D. He refused to accept the authority of the Sanhedrin after a personal dispute
Correct: C — The Gemara explicitly states that 'Greek tunes never ceased from his mouth' and that when Acher rose from the study hall, Greek books would fall from his lap. This indicates a deep, longstanding dual loyalty to Hellenistic culture and philosophy that coexisted with — and ultimately undermined — his Torah commitment.
