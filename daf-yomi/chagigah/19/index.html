⚖️ Does Immersion for Non-Sacred Food Require Intent?

Rabba
One who immersed for non-sacred food is still prohibited from eating tithes — this implies intention IS required even for non-sacred food, otherwise why would the mishna specify his intent?
Rav Naḥman
The mishna means: even though he achieves purity for non-sacred food, he remains prohibited from tithes. Intention is NOT required for non-sacred food at all.
Rabba
The mishna also says: one who immersed without intending to assume purity status is as if he has not immersed — this proves intention IS required!
Rav Naḥman
That clause means only as if he has not immersed for tithes. For non-sacred food, which needs no intention, he is fully purified regardless.
Resolution Non-sacred food (chulin) does NOT require intention for immersion. Proof from Makhshirin: fruit wetted by water unintentionally still becomes susceptible to impurity, showing chulin-level acts need no kavvanah.

🌊 When Does a Wave Count as Valid Immersion?

A wave from the sea breaks — can one immerse in it or use it for purification?
↓
Edge of the Wave
Valid immersion ✓
Water already detached and settled — counts as a valid body of water for purification
Arc (Bow) of the Wave
Invalid immersion ✗
One may not immerse in air — the arching water is still airborne, not a settled body
↓
Why state that a detached wave is valid?
Rabbinic decree: to prevent people from thinking they may immerse in 40 se'a of flowing rainwater — which is invalid

🦶 Rabbi Yehuda vs. Rabbis: Foot Still in the Water

Baraita
One ascending from immersion with one foot still in the water, who had intended only for a minor matter — he may now intend for a major matter as well.
Rabbi Yehuda
As long as a foot remains in the water, the immersion is still in progress — he may upgrade his intention to assume purity for higher purposes.
Rav Naḥman bar Avuh (v.1)
The dispute between Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda applies only to rabbinic-level purity standards. For Torah-level impurity, even Rabbi Yehuda requires full immersion without the foot-extension rule.
Rav Naḥman bar Avuh (v.2)
Other version: the dispute applies to standard immersion from impurity. For higher rabbinic purity standards, all agree the immersion is complete upon ascending — no foot allowance.
Tanna Identified Rabbi Pedat: the rule that foot-in-water = still immersing is the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, sourced from Mikvaot.

✨ Rabbi Elazar: Intention After Ascending

טָבַל וְלֹא הִכְוִּין — עָלָה, יַחְזֹר לְכָל דָּבָר שֶׁיִּרְצֶה
Rabbi Elazar's Ruling If one immersed without any particular intention and then ascended from the mikveh, he may thereafter assume a presumptive status of ritual purity (chezkat taharah) for any purpose he desires — even tithes or terumah — without immersing again.

This applies once he has fully left the water. While still ascending (foot in water), he may direct his intent mid-process per Rabbi Yehuda.

📊 Five Ascending Levels of Ritual Purity

Chulin (Non-Sacred)

  • Lowest standard of purity
  • No intention required for immersion
  • Am ha'aretz garments render chulin impure
  • Proof from Makhshirin: unintentional contact with water counts

Maaser (Tithes)

  • Requires intention for immersion
  • Garments of chulin-eaters render maaser impure
  • One level above chulin
  • Core of Rabba–Rav Naḥman dispute

Terumah (Priestly Portion)

  • Higher standard than maaser
  • Garments of maaser-eaters render terumah impure
  • Priests must maintain this purity
  • Stricter handling requirements

Kodesh (Sacrificial Food)

  • Near-highest standard
  • Chulin prepared at this level = treated as kodesh (Rav Mari)
  • Garments of terumah-eaters render kodesh impure
  • Immersion of hands required

Mei Chatat (Purification Water)

  • Absolute highest standard
  • Red heifer purification water
  • Garments of kodesh-eaters render it impure
  • Most stringent purity requirements of all
