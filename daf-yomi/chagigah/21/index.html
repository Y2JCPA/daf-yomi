🏺 Teruma vs. Sacrificial Food: Purity Standards

Teruma (תְּרוּמָה)

  • May immerse one vessel inside another
  • Lighter standard of purity
  • Interposition concern is relaxed
  • Outer vessel does not invalidate immersion
  • Knots on handles: permitted

Kodashim (קָדָשִׁים)

  • May NOT immerse one vessel inside another
  • Strictest standard of purity required
  • Any interposition invalidates immersion
  • Outer vessel blocks water from inner vessel
  • Knots tighten in water — real blockage

👤 Who Is Restricted From Sacred Foods? The mishna opens with two individuals in a liminal purity state: Onen (אוֹנֵן) — An acute mourner on the day a close relative dies, even if he had no contact with the body, is barred from eating sacrificial food. His personal distress places him outside the ritual community temporarily. Machsar Kipurim (מְחֻסַּר כִּפּוּרִים) — One who has immersed in a mikveh but still needs to bring an atonement offering (e.g., a zav or metzora) remains in a liminal state. Immersion alone is insufficient — the offering must be brought before full access to kodashim is restored.

🪣 Immersing One Vessel Inside Another

Teruma ✓ Permitted

  • Outer vessel does not create meaningful chatzitza
  • Water naturally flows around and through
  • Rabbinic purity standard is satisfied
  • The practical concern is minimal
  • Mishna explicitly permits this

Kodashim ✗ Forbidden

  • Outer vessel creates a chatzitza (interposition)
  • Water cannot fully penetrate to the inner vessel
  • Stricter Torah-level standard required
  • Even partial blockage invalidates immersion
  • Mishna explicitly forbids this

💧 The Chain of Reasoning: Why Does It Matter?

Question: Why is vessel-in-vessel forbidden for kodashim but permitted for teruma?
↓
Core Concern: Chatzitza — the outer vessel interposes, blocking water from fully surrounding the inner vessel
↓
First Clause: Vessel-in-Vessel
Water flows around the outer vessel but cannot fully penetrate — the natural gap between vessels constitutes an interposition for kodashim
Latter Clause: Knotted Handles
Knots tighten further when submerged in water — creating an even clearer interposition that blocks water from entering
↓
Gemara's Resolution: Both clauses are necessary to teach independently — each case might not have been learned from the other alone

⚖️ What Is the Real Reason for the First Clause?

Rabbi Ila
Both the first clause (vessel-in-vessel) and the latter clause (knotted handles) share the same reason: chatzitza (interposition). The mishna groups them together as part of the ten kodashim stringencies precisely because they stem from the same principle.
Rava
Since the latter clause explicitly states that its reason is interposition, this implies by inference that the first clause must operate on a DIFFERENT reason — otherwise the mishna would have stated the reason once and covered both!
Rabbi Ila
Both cases must still be taught independently because a reader might not derive one from the other. Sharing the same underlying reason (chatzitza) does not make either clause redundant — each is a novel application.
Rava
The very structure of the mishna — placing the reason explicitly only with the latter clause — is structural evidence that the first clause rests on a distinct, unstated principle separate from interposition.
📌 The Core Dispute Do both mishna clauses flow from one shared reason (chatzitza), or does the first clause rest on a separate, distinct principle? This shapes how we read the mishna's architecture and the nature of kodashim stringencies.
