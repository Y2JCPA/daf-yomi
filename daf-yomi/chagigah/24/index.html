🤲 Does One Impure Hand Defile Another Person's Hand?

Reish Lakish
An impure hand only renders one's own other hand impure — it does not render impure the hand of another person.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
An impure hand renders another hand impure whether his own or another person's — provided it is the same hand that made contact.
Abaye
Objection: Even a dry impure hand renders its counterpart impure — this is noteworthy only if the second hand is impure even without touching the food directly.
Reish Lakish (retraction)
Rabbi Yona cited Reish Lakish's own later teaching: The decree applies whether his own hand or another's — Reish Lakish ultimately retracted to agree with Rabbi Yoḥanan.
📜 Derivation Rabbi Yoḥanan learns from the mishna's phrase 'one renders its counterpart impure' — implying any counterpart, not only one's own hand.

🆚 Teruma vs. Sacrificial Food: Stringencies on This Daf

Teruma תְּרוּמָה

  • One impure hand does NOT render the other hand impure
  • Dry (unwetted) foods may be eaten with impure hands
  • Acute mourner (onen) need not immerse before eating
  • No fourth-degree impurity disqualification
  • Amei ha'aretz trusted during winepress/oil-press season

Sacrificial Food קָדָשִׁים

  • One impure hand DOES render the other hand impure
  • Dry foods may NOT be eaten even with impure hands
  • Acute mourner MUST immerse before eating kodashim
  • Fourth-degree impurity disqualifies the food
  • Amei ha'aretz NEVER trusted — not even in season

🏺 Rabbi Akiva's Testimony: Vessel Combining

עֵדוּת רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא — Testimony of Rabbi Akiva
כְּלִי מְצָרֵף — A Vessel Combines Basic Torah law: A vessel combines its contents to create susceptibility to impurity — already established halakha.

What Rabbi Akiva adds: Even leftover meal-offering flour (which does not inherently require a vessel) is combined by a vessel through Rabbinic decree.

Dispute on derivation:
• Rabbi Ḥanin: Vessel-combining power is derived directly from the Torah.
• Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba (citing Rabbi Yoḥanan): The mishna represents only a Rabbinic ruling — the combining is not Torah-level for these cases.

Note: Incense and frankincense always require a vessel — no Rabbinic extension needed there.

🔄 When Does an Impure Hand Defile the Other Hand? (Kodashim)

Step 1: One hand becomes impure (e.g., touches an impure object) — it is now a sheni l'tuma for kodashim purposes.
↓
Step 2: The impure hand touches the other hand — the second hand is now rendered impure by Rabbinic decree.
↓
Step 3 (dry hand case): Even a dry impure hand defiles its counterpart — dryness does not prevent the transfer for kodashim.
↓
Step 4: The second hand now disqualifies any kodashim it touches — but does NOT render the food impure to a further degree (dispute among tanna'im).
↓
Conclusion (Rabbi Yoḥanan / final ruling): This applies whether the second hand is one's own or another person's hand — Reish Lakish retracted his limiting opinion.
