📚 What Counts as Abandoning Torah Study?

Rav
One who abandons the study of halacha entirely — leaves the house of study. The verse (Zechariah 8:10) says: "No peace to him that went out or came in due to the adversary."
Shmuel
One who leaves Talmud (with its reasoning) to learn only Mishna (bare rulings without explanation). Talmud trains the mind; Mishna alone does not.
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Even one who leaves Mishna to study only Aggadah (narrative/homily) has abandoned the essential discipline of Torah study.
Implied Hierarchy Talmud > Mishna > Aggadah — each sage defines abandonment at a different level of the ladder

📜 Competing Sources for Dissolution of Vows (הפרת נדרים)

Rabbi Eliezer
"When a man shall clearly utter a vow" (Lev. 27:2) — the word 'clearly' implies a Sage can clarify and thereby dissolve. Refuted: perhaps 'clearly' has another meaning per Rabbi Tarfon.
Rabbi Yehoshua
"In My wrath I swore" (Ps. 95:11) — God retracted an oath made in anger, modeling dissolution. Refuted: perhaps the verse means God did not retract it.
Rabbi Yitzḥak
"Whoever is of a willing heart, let him bring" (Ex. 35:5) — only if willing must one fulfill. Refuted: Rav Giddel offers an alternate reading of this verse.
Shmuel
Best source: "He shall not nullify his word" (Num. 30:3) — he cannot, but a Sage can. No refutation exists. 'One spicy pepper > a basketful of squash!' (Rava)
Rava's Verdict Shmuel's proof from Numbers 30:3 is the only irrefutable source — a single decisive verse outweighs multiple weaker ones

✂️ Is This Shabbat Labor a Torah Violation?

Labor performed on Shabbat
↓
Was it intended?
Planned, deliberate act with desired outcome
Was it unintended?
Performed without intent, or result was unintended (davar she-eino mitkavven)
Was it destructive?
The act destroyed rather than built or created
↓
❌ Liable
Torah prohibition violated — this is the 'mountain' of Shabbat law that dwarfs its thin textual base
✓ Exempt (Rabbi Shimon)
Not liable even if result occurred — unintended labor is not biblically prohibited
✓ Exempt
Destructive acts do not constitute creative labor — Torah prohibits only melekhet maḥshevet (planned creative work)

🕯️ Reconstructing the Source for Festival Peace-Offerings

Problem: The Mishna says Festival peace-offerings are 'mountains on a hair' — but ḥagigah offerings are mentioned in the Torah. Why weak support?
↓
Rav Pappa's clarification: The specific ruling is that a Festival peace-offering brought on Shabbat overrides Shabbat restrictions — this particular rule has no clear verse.
↓
Verbal Analogy (גזירה שוה): 'Wilderness' in Exodus 5:1 (Festival context) is linked to 'wilderness' in Numbers 9:3 (Passover offering) — teaching that Festival sacrifices override Shabbat, just as Passover does.
↓
Abaye's challenge: But the Festival verse says yaḥogu (celebrate) — perhaps it means merely celebrate, with no required offering at all?
↓
Why still 'a hair'? The Gemara concludes: one does not normally derive Torah law from a verse in the Prophets (Nevi'im), and the verbal analogy itself derives from oral tradition — both very thin supports.
