Chagigah 27 Quiz

5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Eliezer, why are the golden and bronze altars not susceptible to impurity?
A. Because they are coated with precious metal
B. Because they are considered like the ground
C. Because fire constantly purifies them
D. Because the High Priest sanctified them annually
Correct: B — Rabbi Eliezer holds that structures attached to and fixed in the ground share the ground's legal status — just as the earth itself cannot become impure, neither can large fixed structures like the altars.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does the Gemara derive from the verse in Ezekiel that begins with 'altar' and ends with 'table'?
A. That the altar must be constructed from wood
B. That only wooden tables can be used in the Temple
C. That eating at one's table in purity atones like the altar
D. That the table and altar must be the same dimensions
Correct: C — Both Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish derive that the shift from 'altar' to 'table' in a single verse teaches that the dining table replaces the altar's atoning function — especially relevant after the Temple's destruction.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
According to the Rabbis, why are the altars exempt from impurity? And what does the Gemara say about their reasoning?
A. Because they are like the ground; the Gemara fully accepts this as an independent ruling
B. Because they are coated; the Gemara says the coating is legally significant
C. Because they are coated; but the Gemara says the coating is nullified and the Rabbis likely agree with Rabbi Eliezer
D. Because fire purifies them; the Gemara cites this as the primary reason
Correct: C — The Rabbis cite the coating, but the Gemara clarifies that a coating is generally subordinate to the main material and legally nullified. Therefore the Rabbis likely meant to challenge Rabbi Eliezer's logic while ultimately agreeing that 'like the ground' is the correct reason.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
From what creature does Rabbi Abbahu (citing Rabbi Elazar) derive that Gehenna's fire cannot harm Torah scholars?
A. The phoenix, which is reborn from its own ashes
B. The salamander, which is born of fire
C. The leviathan, which lives in the depths
D. The ox, whose hide is fire-resistant
Correct: B — The salamander (salamandra) is a creature generated from fire after it burns for many years. Anointing with salamander blood protects against fire. By a fortiori: Torah scholars who are immersed in Torah's fire are certainly protected from Gehenna.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Reish Lakish says the fire of Gehenna cannot harm the sinners of Israel. What analogy does he use?
A. The salamander anointed with blood
B. The golden altar whose thin coating was never damaged by fire
C. The three youths cast into Nebuchadnezzar's furnace
D. The burning bush that was not consumed
Correct: B — The golden altar had only a dinar-thickness of gold coating, yet years of fire service never harmed it. Israel's sinners are 'coated' — filled with mitzvot like a pomegranate — so by a fortiori argument, Gehenna cannot consume them either.
