⚖️ The Core Dispute: What Is Re'iyah?

Rabbi Yochanan
Re'iyah = appearing in the Temple courtyard. One may appear as often as he likes — no offering is required for subsequent visits during the Festival.
Reish Lakish
Re'iyah = appearing with a burnt-offering. Every time one comes to the Temple on a Festival, he must bring an olat re'iyah.
Resolution All agree: on the first (main) day of the Festival, appearance must be accompanied by an offering. The dispute concerns subsequent visits on the remaining Festival days.

⚔️ Proofs and Counter-Proofs

Reish Lakish
Proof (Ex. 23:15): 'None shall appear before Me empty-handed' — one must bring an offering every time he appears at the Temple.
Rabbi Yochanan
Counter (Ex. 23:17): 'All your males shall appear [יֵרָאֶה]' — the verse is read passively: shall be seen. The obligation is the appearance itself, not an offering.
Reish Lakish
From baraita: R. Yose b. R. Yehuda — pilgrims must appear with animal offerings; logic derives these must be burnt-offerings, for your table should not be full while the altar is empty.
Rabbi Yochanan
From Proverbs 25:17: 'Let your foot be seldom in your neighbor's house.' Extra visits to the Temple do not require extra offerings — this proves appearance alone suffices.
R. Levi Resolves the Contradiction 'Let your foot be seldom' = sin-offerings and guilt-offerings (avoid sin). 'I will come with burnt-offerings' (Ps. 66:13) = welcome voluntary offerings. No contradiction.

Subsequent Festival days

Second visit on any day (after initial appearance that day)?

All agree: One enters, appears, and exits — no offering required for a repeat appearance on the same day

🔥 Festival Offerings: Yom Tov vs. Chol HaMoed

Yom Tov (Festival Day Itself)

  • Burnt-offerings of vows/gifts — may NOT be sacrificed
  • Beit Shammai: Peace-offerings may come, but no laying of hands (semicha)
  • Beit Hillel: Both peace-offerings AND laying of hands are permitted
  • Olat re'iyah (burnt-offering of appearance) — obligatory on the first day
  • Shlamei simcha (peace-offerings of rejoicing) — may come from second-tithe money

Chol HaMoed (Intermediate Days)

  • Burnt-offerings of vows/gifts — MAY be sacrificed
  • Must come from non-sacred (chullin) property, not second tithe
  • Peace-offerings of rejoicing — may come from second-tithe money
  • Voluntary vows and gifts donated during the year fulfill the simcha obligation
  • Animal tithes (ma'aser beheimah) also count toward the rejoicing obligation

🧠 The Logical Derivation: Why a Burnt-offering?

Step 1 — Command: 'None shall appear before Me empty-handed' (Ex. 23:15). One must bring an animal offering when appearing at the Temple.
↓
Step 2 — Which offering? The baraita asks: does 'empty-handed' mean burnt-offerings (olot) or peace-offerings (shelamim)?
↓
Step 3 — Kal Va-Chomer (lenient to strict): If peace-offerings — which you eat — are required, how much more so burnt-offerings, which are entirely consumed by God on the altar!
↓
Step 4 — The Table Analogy: Your table should not be full of meat while your Master's table (the altar) is empty. You must bring a burnt-offering.
↓
Conclusion: The offering of re'iyah is specifically an olah (burnt-offering) — entirely consumed on the altar, honoring God before yourself.
