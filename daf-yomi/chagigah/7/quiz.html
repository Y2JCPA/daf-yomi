QUESTION 1 OF 5
What is the core dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish regarding the mitzva of re'iyah?
A. Whether women are also obligated to appear at the Temple
B. Whether re'iyah means appearing in the courtyard (face) or appearing with a burnt-offering
C. Whether re'iyah applies on Yom Kippur as well as the pilgrimage Festivals
D. Whether one may send a proxy to fulfill re'iyah on his behalf
Correct: B — Rabbi Yochanan holds re'iyah = showing one's face in the Temple courtyard (no offering needed for extra visits). Reish Lakish holds it means appearing with a burnt-offering — obligatory each time.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
On which occasion do all authorities — including both Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish — agree that an offering must accompany one's Temple appearance?
A. Any visit during Chol HaMoed
B. The final day (Shemini Atzeret / Shemini HaChag)
C. The first (main) day of the pilgrimage Festival
D. Every Shabbat during the Festival week
Correct: C — The Gemara concludes that on the first day of the Festival, everyone agrees one must bring an offering (olat re'iyah). The dispute is only about subsequent appearances on the remaining Festival days.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How does the baraita prove that the 'appearance offering' must specifically be a burnt-offering (olah) rather than a peace-offering (shlamim)?
A. A gezeirah shavah (verbal analogy) links the word 'appear' to the laws of burnt-offerings
B. A kal va-chomer: if peace-offerings you eat are required, surely burnt-offerings for God alone are required too
C. An explicit verse in Deuteronomy mandates burnt-offerings at every Temple appearance
D. The High Priest's daily burnt-offering serves as a model for all pilgrims
Correct: B — The baraita uses kal va-chomer: peace-offerings (which the pilgrim eats) are obligatory — how much more must burnt-offerings (entirely for God) be brought. The 'table analogy' reinforces: your table should not be full while the altar is empty.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
According to the mishna (Daf 7b), from what type of property must burnt-offerings sacrificed on Chol HaMoed come?
A. Second-tithe (ma'aser sheni) money
B. Temple treasury (hekdesh) funds
C. Terumah set aside for the Kohanim
D. Non-sacred (chullin) property
Correct: D — The mishna rules that burnt-offerings on Chol HaMoed must come from non-sacred (chullin) property. By contrast, peace-offerings of rejoicing (simcha) may be purchased with second-tithe money.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
How does Rabbi Levi resolve the apparent contradiction between 'Let your foot be seldom in your neighbor's house' (Prov. 25:17) and 'I will come into Your house with burnt-offerings' (Ps. 66:13)?
A. Proverbs refers to human neighbors; Psalms refers to God — no real contradiction exists
B. Proverbs refers to weekday visits; Psalms refers to Festival obligations only
C. 'Be seldom' refers to sin-offerings and guilt-offerings (avoid sin); 'burnt-offerings' refers to welcome voluntary offerings
D. The two verses describe different historical periods — pre- and post-Temple
Correct: C — Rabbi Levi explains: 'Be seldom in your neighbor's house' alludes to sin-offerings and guilt-offerings — ideally, visit God's house seldom in that capacity (i.e., sin less). 'I will come with burnt-offerings' refers to the desired, voluntary offerings one should bring with joy.
