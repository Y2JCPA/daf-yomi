QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Ulla, why does Beit Hillel permit using second-tithe funds for Festival peace-offerings?
A. Second-tithe funds are legally equivalent to non-sacred property for all Festival purposes
B. The Festival peace-offering has a voluntary dimension, which permits use of second-tithe money
C. The verse 'after the measure' explicitly includes second-tithe funds as a valid source
D. Peace-offerings are entirely voluntary and thus tithe funds are always permitted for them
Correct: B — Ulla explains Beit Hillel's reasoning: the Festival peace-offering contains a voluntary aspect (one chooses the animal). Unlike a purely obligatory offering — which must come only from non-sacred property — this voluntary element opens the door to second-tithe funds.
QUESTION 2 OF 5
What does Ḥizkiya permit when combining second-tithe and non-sacred funds?
A. Pooling tithe coins with non-sacred coins to purchase a single animal together
B. Using tithe money to supplement the cost of one animal as long as non-sacred covers the minimum
C. Bringing one animal from non-sacred funds and buying a separate additional animal with tithe money
D. Using tithe money first and non-sacred money to cover any shortfall
Correct: C — Ḥizkiya holds 'animal with animal' — one brings one animal purchased entirely from non-sacred funds, then uses second-tithe money to purchase a completely separate second animal. He does not allow mixing money sources within the cost of a single animal.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
How do priests fulfill their mitzva of rejoicing (שמחה) on the Festival?
A. By eating the Festival peace-offerings of Israelites, from which they receive a portion
B. By bringing their own peace-offerings purchased from second-tithe funds
C. Through sin-offerings, guilt-offerings, firstborn offerings, and the breast and thigh of peace-offerings
D. Through bird-offerings and meal-offerings specifically designated for priestly rejoicing
Correct: C — Priests do not receive a share of a layperson's peace-offering. They fulfill rejoicing through their own designated portions: sin-offerings (חטאות), guilt-offerings (אשמות), firstborn offerings (בכורות), and the breast and thigh of peace-offerings. Bird and meal-offerings do not count.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
The word 'missat' (מִסַּת) in Deuteronomy 16:10 is interpreted to mean non-sacred property. What verse provides the proof?
A. The Passover verse about calculating household size for the lamb
B. 'And you shall rejoice in your feast' (Deuteronomy 16:14)
C. 'And King Ahasuerus laid a tribute [mas] upon the land' (Esther 10:1)
D. The verse about marking every tenth animal with red dye (sikrata)
Correct: C — The Gemara links 'missat' to the word 'mas' (tribute/tax) from Esther 10:1 — 'King Ahasuerus laid a mas upon the land.' Just as royal tribute refers to ordinary money, 'missat' in Deuteronomy means the obligatory portion must come from non-sacred funds.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yoḥanan (as reported by Ravin), what is the ruling if one explicitly states his intention when setting aside Festival animals?
A. He must sacrifice all of them on the first day or they become invalid
B. He may sacrifice the remaining animals on any of the remaining days of the Festival
C. He may only carry over animals to the following day, not to later days
D. His stated intention is irrelevant — the same rules apply as when he did not specify
Correct: B — Ravin reported that Rabbi Yoḥanan said: if one explicitly states his intention (designating the animals for 'this Festival'), he may return and sacrifice the remaining animals on subsequent Festival days. The dispute between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish concerns only the unspecified case.
