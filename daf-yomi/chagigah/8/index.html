🔑 Central Questions of Daf 8

Can second-tithe funds pay for Festival peace-offerings?
↓
Beit Shammai
No — obligatory offerings must come from non-sacred property only
Beit Hillel
Yes — peace-offerings may be funded from second-tithe
↓
How exactly may one combine second-tithe with non-sacred funds?
↓
Ḥizkiya
Combine animal with animal — buy one animal from non-sacred funds, use tithe to buy a separate second animal
Rabbi Yoḥanan
Combine money with money — supplement one animal's cost with tithe funds, as long as non-sacred covers 'the first eating'

⚖️ Second Tithe for Festival Peace-Offerings

Beit Shammai
Festival peace-offerings are obligatory. Any obligatory matter may only come from non-sacred property — second-tithe money is forbidden for this purpose.
Beit Hillel
Festival peace-offerings may be brought even from second-tithe funds. The peace-offering has a voluntary dimension (one chooses the animal), and that permits tithe money.
Ulla
Beit Hillel's rationale: the voluntary aspect of the peace-offering opens the door to second-tithe funds — unlike a purely obligatory offering.
Rav Ashi
An additional reason not to use animal tithes: one might come to separate tithes on the Festival itself, which is forbidden due to the red dye (sikrata) used to mark every tenth animal.
Source "After the measure [missat]" (Deut. 16:10) — 'missat' is linked to royal tribute (mas, Esther 10:1), establishing that the obligatory portion must come from non-sacred property.

🔀 Ḥizkiya vs. Rabbi Yoḥanan: How to Combine

Ḥizkiya

  • Combine animal with animal
  • Bring one animal purchased entirely from non-sacred funds
  • Use second-tithe money to purchase a separate additional animal
  • Cannot mix tithe and non-sacred money toward a single animal's cost
  • Baraita support: 'after the measure' implies each animal is funded from one source

Rabbi Yoḥanan

  • Combine money with money
  • Purchase one animal partly from non-sacred funds, partly from tithe
  • Non-sacred portion must cover at minimum 'the first eating' (the required measure)
  • Remainder of the animal's cost may come from second-tithe funds
  • Baraita support: non-sacred need only cover the minimal obligatory measure

🥩 How Priests Fulfill the Obligation of Rejoicing

וְשָׂמַחְתָּ בְּחַגֶּךָ — "And you shall rejoice in your feast" (Devarim 16:14)
Halakha Priests do not receive a portion of a layperson's peace-offering, so they cannot rejoice through it the way Israelites do.

Instead, priests fulfill שמחה (rejoicing) via portions they do receive:

• חטאות / Sin-offerings — priestly share
• אשמות / Guilt-offerings — priestly share
• בכורות / Firstborn offerings
• Breast and thigh of peace-offerings (their designated portion)

Not fulfilled by: bird-offerings or meal-offerings — only offerings from which the Festival peace-offering itself may come (cattle and sheep) count toward the mitzva of rejoicing.

👨‍👩‍👧 Many Eaters, Little Property — What to Do

Situation A: Many household members to feed, but little property → bring many peace-offerings (meat for household) and few burnt-offerings
↓
Situation B: Much property but few eaters → bring many burnt-offerings and few peace-offerings (no waste from uneaten meat)
↓
Situation C: Both property and household are small → the minimum established by the Sages applies: one silver ma'a for the burnt-offering and two silver ma'a for the peace-offering
↓
Rav Ḥisda: The person with many eaters and little property may draw on second-tithe funds to purchase the additional peace-offerings needed to feed his household
↓
Rav Sheshet objects: One may only combine animal-with-animal — one cannot pool multiple coins of tithe money to buy even one animal!
↓
Gemara resolves: Via the principle of 'the first eating' — the non-sacred portion must cover the minimum required measure; tithe funds may supplement beyond that

📅 Remaining Festival Animals — May They Be Sacrificed Later?

One sets aside 10 animals for Festival peace-offerings; only 5 are sacrificed on Day 1
↓
Did he specify his intentions when designating the animals?
Did NOT specify
Rabbi Yoḥanan: May not sacrifice the remaining 5 on later days — since he had time and eaters yet still did not offer them, we infer he silently relinquished his intention

Ulla / Reish Lakish: May sacrifice them on later Festival days — the entire Festival is the designated time
DID specify explicitly
All agree (per Ravin citing Rabbi Yoḥanan): He may return and sacrifice the remaining animals on any of the remaining days of the Festival
↓
Conclusion: The dispute between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish concerns only the unspecified case; where intentions are stated, all permit sacrificing on subsequent days
