🏛️ Who Must Appear at the Temple?

"All your males shall appear" (Exodus 23:17)
↓
Obligated ✅
Adult Jewish males — whole, sighted, able to walk, ritually pure
Exempt ❌
Women · Slaves · Tumtum · Hermaphrodite · Lame · Blind · Sick · Old · Impure · Uncircumcised
↓
Each exclusion requires a Scriptural source or halakhic reasoning — the Gemara probes every one

📋 Six Categories Exempt from Re'iyah

Women & Slaves

  • Women: derived from 'your males' (Ex. 23:17)
  • Slaves: equated with women in time-bound mitzvot
  • Half-slave/half-free: Ravina adds — verse needed for this case

Tumtum & Hermaphrodite

  • Hermaphrodite: has masculine aspect — might have been obligated
  • Tumtum: doubtful sex status — Abaye: testicles visible but penis covered
  • Both excluded by 'your males' — neither is fully male

Lame, Blind & Infirm

  • Lame: 'regalim' (occasions) alludes to raglayim (feet) — must be able to walk
  • Blind in one eye: exempt per R. Yochanan ben Dehavai
  • Sick · Old · Shoeless (cannot enter Temple barefoot)

Ritually Compromised

  • Impure: 'and there you shall come' — excludes those who cannot enter
  • Uncircumcised: per Rabbi Akiva's amplification
  • Mekametz (scrimper of dog feces): cannot enter Temple precincts

⚖️ Does Each Exemption Really Need a Verse?

Gemara
Why does a verse exempt women? Re'iyah is a positive time-bound mitzva — women are already exempt from those!
Answer
Without the verse, we might derive via gezera shava: 'appearance' at re'iyah equals 'appearance' at Hakhel — and women ARE obligated at Hakhel!
Gemara
Granted the hermaphrodite (androginos) needs a verse — it has an aspect of masculinity. But why does a tumtum need one? A doubtful case should simply be exempt by default!
Abaye
The verse addresses a specific tumtum: one whose testicles are visible externally but whose penis is covered. That person might seem obligated — the verse excludes him.
Gemara
'All your males' — the mishna says minors are exempt, yet the baraita says they are included. Contradiction!
Abaye
No contradiction. The baraita includes a minor who can hold his father's hand and walk to Jerusalem. The mishna exempts one too young to make the journey.
Aḥerim (Sages) 'All your males' actually comes to teach that a mekametz — one who gathers dog feces for tanners — is exempt, because he cannot enter the Temple courtyard due to the foul odor on his hands

👁️ R. Yochanan ben Dehavai: The One-Eyed Man

Exodus 23:17: "All your males shall appear [yera'e] before the Lord God"
↓
The verb yera'e (shall appear) is identical in spelling to yireh (shall see) — the verse implies a double meaning
↓
Verbal analogy: Just as God 'sees' the one who comes, so too the one who comes must be able to fully 'see' God's presence
↓
Fully sighted
Obligated — he sees and is seen
Blind in one eye
Exempt — his sight is incomplete; he cannot 'see' as God sees him

👼 The Angel of Death and the Wrong Miriam

מִרְיָם מְגַדְּלַת שְׂעָרוֹת נָשִׁים — Miriam, raiser of women's hair
Aggadah — Rav Beivai bar Abaye Rav Yosef asked: Can one die before one's appointed time? The Gemara answers: Yes.

Rav Beivai bar Abaye had frequent encounters with the Angel of Death. Once, the Angel sent his agent to bring Miriam the raiser of women's hair. The agent accidentally seized a different woman named Miriam. When told of his error, the Angel replied: "Since you have already brought her, let her be counted among the number of deceased."

This teaches that some are swept away before their time — not through sin, but through the accidents of heavenly administration.
