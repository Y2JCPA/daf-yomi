5 questions · Self-graded · Test your learning!
QUESTION 1 OF 5
According to Rabbi Yishmael, what determines whether a garment woven in purity remains pure?
A. The garment must be immersed in a mikveh before use regardless
B. The weaver must have maintained focused intent (kavanah) to guard its purity throughout the weaving
C. Only a garment woven by a ḥaver can be considered pure
D. The garment is pure only if a second person witnessed the weaving
Correct: B — Rabbi Yishmael declared both weavers' garments impure when the women admitted their minds wandered, confirming the Sages' rule: focused intent = pure; distracted mind = impure (even if no impurity actually occurred).
QUESTION 2 OF 5
Why did Rabbi Oshaya object to Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok's ruling about the switched garments?
A. Women are not obligated to maintain purity of garments
B. The bathhouse is inherently an impure domain
C. By the same logic, accidentally grabbing the wrong loaf of bread from a basket would also render it impure — an obviously absurd result
D. The wives of ḥaverim are not trusted to report accurately on purity matters
Correct: C — Rabbi Oshaya used a reductio ad absurdum: if merely receiving the wrong item by mistake causes impurity, the same would apply to accidentally pulling barley bread instead of wheat from a basket — clearly an unacceptable outcome.
QUESTION 3 OF 5
Which of the following is a stringency that applies to sacrificial food (kodesh) but NOT to teruma?
A. The food must be eaten within the walls of Jerusalem
B. A tav'ul yom (one who immersed but awaits sunset) may not handle it
C. Only a kohen gadol may decide its purity status
D. It must be salted and rinsed before consumption
Correct: B — The Mishna teaches that a tav'ul yom may handle teruma but is disqualified from handling kodesh — one of multiple extra stringencies that apply exclusively to sacrificial food.
QUESTION 4 OF 5
Rabbi Yoḥanan's principle about guarding purity establishes that:
A. Only a Torah scholar can be trusted to reliably guard ritual purity
B. Objects in transit through a public domain are always presumed impure
C. It is presumed that a person does not guard that which is currently in another's hand
D. Guarding requires physical barriers; mental intent alone is insufficient
Correct: C — Rabbi Yoḥanan established a legal presumption (ḥazakah): when an object is physically in someone else's possession, the original owner is presumed incapable of maintaining the focused guarding necessary to preserve its purity.
QUESTION 5 OF 5
Regarding vessels used with sacrificial food, the Mishna rules that:
A. Only vessels fashioned by a kohen are valid for kodesh
B. Earthenware vessels may never be used for kodesh under any circumstances
C. Vessels fashioned and completed in a state of purity still require immersion before use with kodesh, but not for teruma
D. Vessels must be immersed seven times for kodesh, mirroring the purification of a metzora
Correct: C — The Mishna explicitly lists this as a stringency of kodesh: even a vessel whose entire manufacturing process occurred in purity must undergo tevila (immersion) before contact with sacrificial food — no such requirement applies to teruma.
